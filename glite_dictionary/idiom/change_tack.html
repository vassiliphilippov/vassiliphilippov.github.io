to change tack

1. VERB

/ tʃeɪndʒ tæk / (AmE), / tʃeɪndʒ tæk / (BrE)
to change one's approach or course of action, especially in order to deal with a problem or situation more effectively
change approach or strategy

Examples

After several unsuccessful attempts, the manager decided to change tack and try a new marketing strategy.
If we don't see better results soon, we may have to change tack entirely.
The politician was losing support, so she chose to change tack and address new issues.

Synset Examples

A change of tack was necessary for better results.
The team decided to change tack after receiving feedback.
They had to change tack when the plan failed.
We should change tack if we want to succeed.
When sales dropped, they decided to change tack.
The team had to change tack after the feedback.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to change course - to take a different direction or approach
  • Antonym: stay the course - to continue with the same approach or strategy

Etymology

Originally a nautical term referring to changing the direction of a sailing vessel by moving the sails to take advantage of the wind.
