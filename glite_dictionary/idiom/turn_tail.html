to turn tail

1. VERB

/ tɝn teɪl / (AmE), / tɜːn teɪl / (BrE)
to run away suddenly, especially in fear or defeat
to flee suddenly in fear or defeat

Examples

When the dog barked, the thief turned tail and fled down the street.
Rather than face the consequences, he chose to turn tail and disappear.
Most of the enemy soldiers turned tail when they saw the reinforcements arrive.

Synset Examples

If you see this man, run!
The burglars escaped before the police showed up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to flee - to run away from danger or something frightening
  • Antonym: stand one's ground - to refuse to run away or back down, especially in the face of danger or opposition

Etymology

Originally from the image of an animal turning its tail to flee; 'tail' here refers to the tail of an animal.

2. VERB

/ tɝn teɪl / (AmE), / tɜːn teɪl / (BrE)
to flee or retreat quickly, usually out of fear or alarm
flee or retreat quickly

Examples

As soon as they heard the siren, the thieves turned tail and disappeared into the alley.
The fox turned tail when it spotted the hunter approaching with a rifle.
Faced with overwhelming opposition, the politicians had to turn tail and abandon their plans.

Synset Examples

Seeing the bear, the hikers decided to turn tail.
He had no choice but to turn tail and escape.
The dog turned tail and ran when it saw the thunder.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to run away - to leave a place or person secretly and suddenly
  • Antonym: stand one's ground - to refuse to retreat or back down

Etymology

From the notion of an animal turning its tail to run away, attested since the 16th century.
