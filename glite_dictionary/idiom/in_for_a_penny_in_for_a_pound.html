in for a penny, in for a pound

1. X

/ ɪn fəɹ ə ˈpɛni, ɪn fəɹ ə ˈpaʊnd / (AmE), / ɪn fə ɹ ə ˈpɛni, ɪn fə ɹ ə ˈpaʊnd / (BrE)
used to express the idea that once you have started doing something, especially something risky or difficult, you should fully commit yourself to it regardless of the cost or consequences.
fully committed once involved
british

Examples

They had already spent weeks on the plan, so it was in for a penny, in for a pound from then on.
When the meeting went overtime, he thought, 'in for a penny, in for a pound,' and volunteered for the next task too.
If she was going to confront her boss, she might as well be in for a penny, in for a pound and address all her concerns.

Synset Examples

Since he's already committed, he's in for a penny, in for a pound.
She figured if she's going to help, she’s in for a penny, in for a pound.
Once he started the project, he thought he was in for a penny, in for a pound.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: all in - completely committed to an action or decision
  • Synonym: go the whole hog - do something completely or thoroughly

Etymology

From British currency, where a penny is a small coin and a pound is a large amount of money, indicating full commitment once some investment is made.
