to see a man about

1. VERB

/ si ə mæn əˈbaʊt / (AmE), / siː ə mæn əˈbaʊt / (BrE)
(informal) Used to indicate that one is leaving to undertake some private or undisclosed business, often as an excuse to avoid stating the real reason.
leave for an undisclosed reason
informal

Examples

Excuse me, I need to see a man about something important.
He said he was going to see a man about a dog and left the meeting early.
Whenever she wanted to leave discreetly, she claimed she had to see a man about something.

Synset Examples

I have to see a man about some business.
He left to see a man about a car deal.
He slipped away to see a man about a dog.
Excuse me, I need to see a man about a dog.
I need to run; I have to see a man about a horse.
Whenever he disappears, he claims to see a man about a horse.
He said he had to see a man about a horse.

Etymology

Originated in late 19th century Britain, often appearing as 'see a man about a dog (or horse)', used as a polite or humorous excuse for leaving.
