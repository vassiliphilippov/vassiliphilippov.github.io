to pull in one's horns

1. VERB

/ pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔrnz / (AmE), / pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔːnz / (BrE)
to retreat or withdraw from an earlier commitment or position; to become less aggressive or assertive in one's actions or plans
retreat from a prior commitment

Examples

After the budget cuts, the company had to pull in its horns and limit new projects.
Facing mounting opposition, the politician decided to pull in her horns and withdraw the proposal.
When their profits dropped, the restaurant owners pulled in their horns and postponed the renovations.

Synset Examples

We'll have to crawfish out from meeting with him
He backed out of his earlier promise
The aggressive investment company pulled in its horns

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to back out - to withdraw from a commitment or agreement
  • Synonym: retreat - to move back or withdraw from a position

Etymology

From the habit of animals such as snails or bulls withdrawing their horns when threatened; first recorded in the 18th century.

2. VERB

/ pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔrnz / (AmE), / pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔːnz / (BrE)
to restrain oneself or become less aggressive, cautious, or less assertive, especially after being ambitious or bold
behave more cautiously; restrain oneself

Examples

After the market crash, investors began to pull in their horns and take fewer risks.
He realized he needed to pull in his horns after overreaching at work.
As the criticism mounted, the politician tried to pull in her horns and adopt a more conciliatory tone.

Synset Examples

She decided to pull in her horns and save more.
It's time to pull in your horns and cut expenses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: draw in one's horns - to behave more cautiously; restrain oneself

Etymology

From the behavior of animals (such as snails or bulls) retracting their horns as a defensive behavior, figuratively adopted in English since the 19th century.

3. VERB

/ pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔrnz / (AmE), / pʊl ɪn wʌnz hɔːnz / (BrE)
to reduce one's ambitions or lower one's expectations, typically in response to difficulties or setbacks
reduce ambitions or expectations

Examples

After the market crash, investors had to pull in their horns and reconsider their strategies.
With mounting debts, the company decided it was time to pull in its horns and focus on core services.
When she realized the job was harder than expected, she had to pull in her horns and set more realistic goals.

Synset Examples

After losing money, he had to pull in his horns.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scale back - to reduce the size or scope of something
  • Synonym: to cut back - to reduce something, especially expenses or ambitions

Etymology

Refers to the defensive behavior of animals (such as snails) that retract their horns or projections when threatened.
