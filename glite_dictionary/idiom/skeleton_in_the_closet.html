skeleton in the closet

1. NOUN

/ ˈskɛlətən ɪn ðə ˈklɑːsət / (AmE), / ˈskɛlɪtən ɪn ðə ˈklɒzɪt / (BrE)
A secret source of shame or embarrassment, often from the past, which someone keeps hidden.
hidden shameful secret

Examples

Every politician seems to have a skeleton in the closet that could damage their reputation if revealed.
She was afraid her skeleton in the closet would be discovered during the background check.
The company's financial scandal became public, exposing a major skeleton in the closet.

Synset Examples

there must be a skeleton somewhere in that family's closet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dirty secret - an embarrassing fact about someone or something kept hidden
  • Antonym: open secret - a fact that is supposed to be secret but is actually widely known

Etymology

From the metaphorical idea of hiding a skeleton, representing a dark secret, in one's closet. Popularized in 19th-century English.

2. NOUN

/ ˈskɛlətən ɪn ðə ˈklɑzət / (AmE), / ˈskɛlɪtən ɪn ðə ˈklɒzɪt / (BrE)
a hidden secret or shameful fact that someone keeps concealed, especially one that could cause embarrassment if revealed
secret or shameful fact kept hidden

Examples

Every politician seems to have a skeleton in the closet that could damage their reputation.
She was terrified that her skeleton in the closet would come out during the interview.
Admitting to a skeleton in the closet can be frightening, but it is often a relief.

Synset Examples

Her past is a skeleton in the closet she rarely discusses.

He finally revealed his skeleton in the closet to his friends.
Her childhood was a skeleton in the cupboard she kept hidden.
Every family has a skeleton in the cupboard they avoid.
The scandal was a skeleton in the cupboard for the politician.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secret - something that is kept or meant to be kept unknown or unseen by others
  • Antonym: open secret - information that is supposed to be secret but is widely known

Etymology

From the practice of concealing a dead body (skeleton) out of shame or fear of scandal; first recorded in 19th-century English.
