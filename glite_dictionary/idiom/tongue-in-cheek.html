tongue-in-cheek

1. ADJ

/ ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃik / (AmE), / ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃiːk / (BrE)
intended to be humorous or ironic, not meant to be taken seriously
humorous or ironic, not serious

Examples

His comments about quitting his job were clearly tongue-in-cheek and not meant to be taken seriously.
The article offered some tongue-in-cheek suggestions for how to survive a zombie apocalypse.
She gave a tongue-in-cheek response to the reporter's intrusive question.

Synset Examples

a bantering tone
facetious remarks
tongue-in-cheek advice

Related Terms

  • Synonym: facetious - treating serious issues with deliberately inappropriate humor
  • Synonym: sarcastic - marked by or given to using irony to mock or convey contempt

Etymology

From the phrase 'with one's tongue in one's cheek', originally describing a facial expression suggesting suppressed mirth.

2. ADJ

/ ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃik / (AmE), / ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃiːk / (BrE)
intended as a joke or not meant to be taken seriously; ironic or sarcastic in tone
intended as a joke; not serious

Examples

Her tongue-in-cheek remarks often left people wondering if she was serious or joking.
The article took a tongue-in-cheek look at modern dating trends.
He gave a tongue-in-cheek apology that made everyone laugh.

Synset Examples

His comments were tongue-in-cheek, intended to be humorous.
Their tongue-in-cheek approach made the meeting enjoyable.
She made a tongue-in-cheek remark about the weather.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sarcastic - using irony to mock or convey contempt
  • Antonym: sincere - genuine or free from pretense

Etymology

Refers to a facial gesture of pushing one's tongue into the cheek to signal insincerity or jest, dating from the 18th century.
