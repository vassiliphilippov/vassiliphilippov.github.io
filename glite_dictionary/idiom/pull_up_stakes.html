to pull up stakes

1. VERB

/ pʊl ʌp steɪks / (AmE), / pʊl ʌp steɪks / (BrE)
to leave one's home, job, or place of association, often in order to relocate or move on to something new
to leave and move elsewhere

Examples

After losing his job, Mark decided to pull up stakes and move to a new city.
The family finally pulled up stakes after living there for more than three decades.
She is planning to pull up stakes and travel around the world for a year.

Synset Examples

She wants to leave
The teenager left home
She left her position with the Red Cross
He left the Senate after two terms
after 20 years with the same company, she pulled up stakes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leave - to go away from a place or person
  • Antonym: to settle down - to begin living a stable or permanent life in one place

Etymology

From the practice of pulling up tent stakes when moving camp.

2. VERB

/ pʊl ʌp steɪks / (AmE), / pʊl ʌp steɪks / (BrE)
to leave one's home or place of residence in order to relocate, especially suddenly or permanently
leave and relocate

Examples

After living in Chicago for twenty years, they decided to pull up stakes and try life on the coast.
When the factory closed, many workers had no choice but to pull up stakes.
We're thinking of pulling up stakes next spring and moving closer to family.

Synset Examples

He had to pull up stakes for a new job opportunity.
After years in the city, it's time to pull up stakes.
They decided to pull up stakes and move to a warmer climate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to move house - to change one's place of residence
  • Antonym: to settle down - to establish a permanent home

Etymology

Originally from the practice of pulling up tent stakes before moving camp in early American settlements.
