be-all and end-all

1. NOUN

/ ˈbi ˌɔl ənd ˈɛnd ˌɔl / (AmE), / ˈbiː ˌɔːl ən ˈɛnd ˌɔːl / (BrE)
the most important part or ultimate aim of something; the essential or supreme factor
supreme or most important element

Examples

For her, winning this award was the be-all and end-all of her career.
Many people mistakenly treat money as the be-all and end-all in life.
Academic success is important, but it shouldn't be seen as the be-all and end-all.

Synset Examples

profit is the be-all and end-all of business

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hallmark - a distinctive feature, especially one of excellence
  • Synonym: ultimate - the best achievable or imaginable of its kind
  • Antonym: detail - a minor or less important part or feature

Etymology

Popularized by Shakespeare, first used in 'Macbeth' (1605–1606).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbiˌɔl ənd ˈɛndˌɔl / (AmE), / ˈbiːˌɔːl ənd ˈendˌɔːl / (BrE)
the most important aspect or factor; the thing that is considered to be the ultimate or most essential.
the most important thing

Examples

Some people view financial success as the be-all and end-all of life.
While winning is fun, it's not the be-all and end-all of participating in sports.
Education should not be seen as the be-all and end-all of a person's worth.

Synset Examples

Winning the award was not the be-all and end-all for her.
For him, success in business is the be-all and end-all.
Don't treat grades as the be-all and end-all of education.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: essence - the intrinsic nature or indispensable quality of something
  • Antonym: triviality - something that is not important or significant

Etymology

Originates from Shakespeare's Macbeth (1606), where 'be-all and end-all' denoted something ultimate or final.
