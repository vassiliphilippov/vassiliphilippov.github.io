neck and neck

1. ADV

/ nɛk ənd nɛk / (AmE), / nɛk ənd nɛk / (BrE)
so evenly matched in a race, competition, or comparison that it is very difficult to say who will win or which is better
evenly matched; too close to call

Examples

The two candidates have been running neck and neck in the polls for weeks.
As the marathon reached its final stretch, the leading runners were neck and neck.
Sales figures for both companies are neck and neck this quarter.

Synset Examples

the horses ran neck and neck
he won nip and tuck

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nip and tuck - so close in a competition that the outcome is uncertain

Etymology

From the image of horses racing with their necks level; first recorded in the 19th century.

2. ADJ

/ nɛk ənd nɛk / (AmE), / nɛk ənd nɛk / (BrE)
so close in a race or competition that it is impossible to say who will win; exactly even or almost even in progress or achievement
very closely matched (in competition)

Examples

The two runners were neck and neck as they approached the finish line.
Going into the last quarter, the teams remained neck and neck in points.
The companies are neck and neck in the race to develop new technology.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nip and tuck - a situation where competitors are so close that the outcome is uncertain

3. ADJ

/ nɛk ənd nɛk / (AmE), / nɛk ənd nɛk / (BrE)
(of competitors) level with each other in a race or competition; so close in progress that it is not clear who will win.
very close in a competition or race

Examples

The election remained neck and neck until the final votes were counted.
The two swimmers were neck and neck for most of the race.
It's still neck and neck between the teams as halftime approaches.

Synset Examples

The two candidates are neck and neck in the polls.
Their scores were neck and neck throughout the competition.
The race was neck and neck until the final lap.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: even - equal in progress, score, or results
  • Antonym: ahead - in front in a competition or race
  • Antonym: behind - behind in a competition or race

Etymology

Originally from horse racing, where horses are so close their necks are aligned.
