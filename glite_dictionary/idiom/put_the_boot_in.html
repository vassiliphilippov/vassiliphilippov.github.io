to put the boot in

1. VERB

/ pʊt ðə buːt ɪn / (AmE), / pʊt ðə buːt ɪn / (BrE)
to attack or criticize someone, especially when they are already weak, vulnerable, or in a difficult situation
attack or criticize someone already vulnerable
british

Examples

Despite her apology, the critics continued to put the boot in during the interview.
When their team was losing badly, the fans really put the boot in with boos and jeers.
It's unfair to put the boot in now that he's already facing so much trouble.

Synset Examples

Don’t put the boot in; he’s already struggling.
When he was down, they really put the boot in.
She put the boot in during the argument.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kick a man when he's down - to attack or criticize someone who is already in trouble

Etymology

British English idiom, originally referring to kicking someone when they are down; 'boot' refers to footwear.
