Mason-Dixon line

1. NOUN

/ ˈmeɪsən ˈdɪksən laɪn / (AmE), / ˈmeɪsən ˈdɪksən laɪn / (BrE)
a boundary between Maryland and Pennsylvania in the United States, historically considered the symbolic dividing line between the northern and southern states, especially before the American Civil War
boundary between North and South in the US
american

Examples

The Mason-Dixon line was considered the cultural boundary between the North and the South.
Many escaped slaves crossed the Mason-Dixon line in their journey to freedom.
Even today, some people refer to the Mason-Dixon line when talking about differences between northern and southern states.

Etymology

Named after English surveyors Charles Mason and Jeremiah Dixon, who marked the line in the 1760s.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmeɪsən ˈdɪksən laɪn / (AmE), / ˈmeɪsən ˈdɪksən laɪn / (BrE)
The boundary between Maryland and Pennsylvania in the eastern United States, historically regarded as the dividing line between the North and South, especially before the American Civil War.
boundary between North and South U.S.
american

Examples

Many considered the Mason-Dixon line to be the unofficial border between the North and the South.
As tensions grew over slavery, the Mason-Dixon line took on symbolic meaning across the United States.
The historic Mason-Dixon line still appears on some modern maps of Pennsylvania and Maryland.

Etymology

Named after English surveyors Charles Mason and Jeremiah Dixon, who mapped the border in the 1760s.
