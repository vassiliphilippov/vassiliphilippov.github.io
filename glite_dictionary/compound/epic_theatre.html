epic theatre

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛpɪk ˈθiətɚ / (AmE), / ˈɛpɪk ˈθɪətə / (BrE)
A form of didactic drama that presents a series of loosely connected scenes, often breaking the illusion and addressing the audience directly to provide analysis, argument, or documentation.
form of drama breaking theatrical illusion

Examples

Many directors have experimented with epic theatre to challenge their audiences' expectations.
The influence of epic theatre is evident in several modern plays that directly address social issues.
Brecht's use of epic theatre techniques was revolutionary in twentieth-century drama.

Synset Examples

Epic theatre is now most often associated with the dramatic theory and practice evolved by the playwright-director Bertolt Brecht in Germany from the 1920s onward.

Etymology

From German 'episches Theater', introduced by playwright Bertolt Brecht.
