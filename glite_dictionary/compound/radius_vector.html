radius vector

1. NOUN

/ ˈreɪdiəs ˈvɛktər / (AmE), / ˈreɪdɪəs ˈvɛktə / (BrE)
a line drawn from a rotating object, such as a satellite, to the center of the body around which it revolves.
line from satellite to center of rotation

Examples

The position of the planet at any moment can be specified by its radius vector.
As the satellite moves, its radius vector changes direction but not length in a circular orbit.
Astronomers calculate the radius vector to determine how close an object is to the center of the Earth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vector - a quantity having direction as well as magnitude

Etymology

From Latin radius (ray, spoke) and vector (carrier).

2. NOUN

/ ˈreɪdiəs ˈvɛktɚ / (AmE), / ˈreɪdiəs ˈvɛktə / (BrE)
a straight line joining a point in space to the origin of a polar coordinate system.
line from origin to a point (math)

Examples

The position of the planet can be described by its radius vector from the sun.
In polar coordinates, the radius vector gives both the distance and direction from the origin.
As the object moves, its radius vector sweeps out an area in the plane.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vector - a quantity with both magnitude and direction

Etymology

From Latin 'radius' (ray, spoke) and Latin 'vector' (carrier, bearer).
