formal logic

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrməl ˈlɑdʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməl ˈlɒdʒɪk / (BrE)
the branch of logic that studies the formal structure of statements and arguments, focusing on their form rather than content to determine validity and consistency
branch of logic dealing with abstract form of arguments

Examples

She studied formal logic to better understand the underlying structure of mathematical arguments.
The rules of formal logic help distinguish between valid and invalid reasoning.
Courses in formal logic are fundamental for philosophy and computer science students.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: informal logic - logic that focuses on natural language arguments rather than formal structures

Etymology

From Latin 'formalis' (of form) and Greek 'logike' (reasoning, thought).
