optic axis

1. NOUN

/ ˈɑptɪk ˈæksɪs / (AmE), / ˈɒptɪk ˈæksɪs / (BrE)
in a doubly refracting crystal, the direction along which light does not undergo double refraction; a line in the crystal where light travels without splitting into two rays.
direction in crystal with no double refraction

Examples

Light passing along the optic axis of a calcite crystal will not be split into two rays.
Mineralogists often determine the number and direction of optic axes to classify crystals.
The optic axis is a fundamental concept in the study of birefringence.

Synset Examples

a crystal may have either one or two optic axes

Etymology

From 'optic', relating to sight or light, and 'axis', from Latin axis (axle, pivot).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɑptɪk ˈæksɪs / (AmE), / ˈɒptɪk ˈæksɪs / (BrE)
A straight line that passes through the center of curvature of a lens or the eye along which light passes without being refracted or reflected.
line through center of lens or eye

Examples

Light passing along the optic axis travels through the lens without changing direction.
The optic axis of the eye determines the direction in which we see most clearly.
Precise alignment of the optic axis is crucial in designing optical instruments.

Synset Examples

in a normal eye the optic axis is the direction in which objects are seen most distinctly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: principal axis - the main axis of symmetry of an optical element

Etymology

From Greek 'optikos' (of or for sight) and Latin 'axis' (axle, axis).
