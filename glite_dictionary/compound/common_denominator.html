common denominator

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmən dɪˈnɑːmɪneɪtər / (AmE), / ˈkɒmən dɪˈnɒmɪneɪtə / (BrE)
A feature or attribute shared by all members of a group or category.
shared feature among a group

Examples

Trust seems to be the common denominator in all successful teams.
The one common denominator among the candidates was their passion for education.
A desire for freedom is a common denominator in many revolutionary movements.

Etymology

From mathematical terminology: the denominator that is common to two or more fractions, extended figuratively to mean any shared trait.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑmən dɪˈnɑməˌneɪtər / (AmE), / ˈkɒmən dɪˈnɒmɪneɪtə / (BrE)
an integer that is a common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions, used to enable addition, subtraction, or comparison of those fractions
integer multiple of denominators (math)

Examples

To add fractions, you must first find a common denominator.
The teacher explained how to calculate the common denominator for 1/2 and 1/3.
A common denominator simplifies the process of comparing different fractions.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: lowest common denominator - the smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions

Etymology

From Latin 'communis' (common) and 'denominator' (from 'denominare', to name).

3. NOUN

/ ˌkɑmən dɪˈnɑmɪˌneɪtər / (AmE), / ˌkɒmən dɪˈnɒmɪˌneɪtə / (BrE)
a feature or quality that is shared by all the members of a group or set of things
shared trait or factor

Examples

The one common denominator among all the candidates is their dedication to community service.
Lack of trust was the common denominator in each failed project we analyzed.
Across the different countries, education appeared as a common denominator influencing progress.

Synset Examples

From mathematics, where it refers to a common multiple of the denominators of fractions; sense extended figuratively in the 20th century.
