ergative-absolutive language

1. NOUN

/ ɜrˌɡætɪv ˈæbsəˌluːtɪv ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈɜːɡətɪv ˌæbsəˈluːtɪv ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (BrE)
A language in which the direct object of a transitive verb and the subject of an intransitive verb are treated the same way, usually marked with the absolutive case, while the subject of a transitive verb is marked differently with the ergative case.
language with ergative-absolutive case marking

Examples

Basque is a well-known example of an ergative-absolutive language studied by linguists.
In an ergative-absolutive language, the object and intransitive subject share the same grammatical marking.
Many indigenous languages of Australia display patterns consistent with an ergative-absolutive language.

Synset Examples

An ergative language maintains a syntactic or morphological equivalence (such as the same word order or grammatical case) for the object of a transitive verb and the single core argument of an intransitive verb, while treating the agent of a transitive verb differently.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominative-accusative language - language where subjects of both transitive and intransitive verbs are treated alike (opposite case system)

Etymology

From 'ergative' and 'absolutive,' both from linguistic terminology relating to grammatical cases.
