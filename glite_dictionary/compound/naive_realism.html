naive realism

1. NOUN

/ naɪˈiv ˈriəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / naɪˈiːv ˈrɪə.lɪz.əm / (BrE)
the philosophical doctrine that physical objects exist independently of perception and continue to exist even when not being observed
belief that objects exist independently of perception

Examples

Many philosophers challenge naive realism by arguing that reality is constructed by perception.
In introductory philosophy courses, students are often exposed to the concept of naive realism.
Debates about the nature of existence frequently mention naive realism as a starting point.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealism - the philosophical doctrine that reality is fundamentally mental or immaterial

Etymology

From French 'naïf' (natural, unaffected) and late Latin 'realismus' (regarding things as real).
