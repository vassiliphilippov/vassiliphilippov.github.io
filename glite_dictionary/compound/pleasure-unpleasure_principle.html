pleasure-unpleasure principle

1. NOUN

/ ˈplɛʒər ənˈplɛʒər ˈprɪnsəpəl / (AmE), / ˈplɛʒə ənˈplɛʒə ˈprɪnsɪpl / (BrE)
In psychoanalysis, the principle by which the id seeks immediate gratification of desires and attempts to avoid unpleasure, without distinguishing fantasy from reality.
psychoanalytic concept about seeking gratification

Examples

According to Freud, the pleasure-unpleasure principle dominates the mental life of the infant.
The pleasure-unpleasure principle explains why young children often struggle to delay gratification.
In psychoanalysis, the pleasure-unpleasure principle is contrasted with the reality principle as the mind develops.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reality principle - psychoanalytic principle counteracting the pleasure principle by delaying gratification

Etymology

From 'pleasure' and 'unpleasure', with 'principle' from Latin 'principium' (beginning, foundation). Term popularized by Sigmund Freud.
