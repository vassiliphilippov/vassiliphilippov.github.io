trade wind

1. NOUN

/ treɪd wɪnd / (AmE), / treɪd wɪnd / (BrE)
a steady wind that blows from east to west in the Earth's equatorial regions, north and south of the equator
steady east-to-west wind near the equator

Examples

Early sailors relied on the trade wind to cross the Atlantic Ocean.
The trade wind brings warm, humid air to the Caribbean islands.
Without the steady trade wind, the ship's progress slowed considerably.

Synset Examples

they rode the trade winds going west

Etymology

From the obsolete sense of 'trade' meaning 'track, path' or 'course', referring to the regular course of these winds.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtreɪd ˌwɪnd / (AmE), / ˈtreɪd ˌwɪnd / (BrE)
a steady wind blowing from the east toward the west in the tropics, especially just north or south of the equator
steady wind in the tropics blowing east to west

Examples

Early explorers relied on the trade winds to cross the Atlantic Ocean safely.
The Caribbean's warm climate is partly due to the steady trade winds that blow across the islands.
Sailboats moved swiftly as they caught the trade winds near the equator.

Synset Examples

they rode the trade winds going west

Related Terms

  • Antonym: westerly - a wind blowing from the west

Etymology

From the obsolete sense of 'trade' meaning 'course; track', referring to the regular course of the winds.
