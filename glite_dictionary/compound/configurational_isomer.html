configurational isomer

1. NOUN

/ kənˌfɪɡjəˈreɪʃənl ˈaɪsəmɚ / (AmE), / kənˌfɪɡjʊˈreɪʃənl ˈaɪsəmə / (BrE)
a molecule that is a type of stereoisomer which can only be converted into its counterpart by breaking and reforming covalent bonds at the stereocenter
type of stereoisomer requiring bond breaking to interconvert
medical

Examples

The two compounds are configurational isomers and cannot be interconverted without breaking covalent bonds.
A key property of a configurational isomer is its stability against simple rotation about bonds.
Chemists distinguish between configurational isomers such as cis-trans and enantiomers based on their stereochemistry.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stereoisomer - a compound with the same molecular formula and connectivity, but different spatial arrangement of atoms
  • Antonym: conformational isomer - a type of stereoisomer that can be interconverted by rotation about single bonds

Etymology

From configuration, ultimately from Latin 'configurare' (to shape), and isomer, from Greek 'isos' (equal) + 'meros' (part).
