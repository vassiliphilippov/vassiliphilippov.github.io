Archimedes' principle

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑrkəˈmidiz ˈprɪnsəpəl / (AmE), / ˌɑːkɪˈmiːdiːz ˈprɪnsəpl / (BrE)
the scientific principle stating that a body immersed in a fluid is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the body
principle in physics about buoyancy

Examples

We used Archimedes' principle to calculate the volume of the irregular object.
Submarines rely on Archimedes' principle to control their buoyancy under water.
In the laboratory, students demonstrated Archimedes' principle using a beaker, water, and a metal block.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buoyancy - upward force exerted by a fluid that opposes the weight of an immersed object

Etymology

Named after Archimedes (c. 287–212 BC), ancient Greek mathematician and engineer, who first stated the principle.
