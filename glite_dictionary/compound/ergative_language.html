ergative language

1. NOUN

/ ˈɜːrɡətɪv ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈɜːɡətɪv ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (BrE)
A language in which the grammatical subject of an intransitive verb is treated like the object of a transitive verb, and differently from the subject of a transitive verb, often marked by an ergative case.
language with ergative grammatical alignment

Examples

Basque is a well-known example of an ergative language.
In an ergative language, the roles of arguments are marked differently than in English.
Linguists often compare English to an ergative language to illustrate differences in grammatical structure.

Synset Examples

An ergative language maintains a syntactic or morphological equivalence (such as the same word order or grammatical case) for the object of a transitive verb and the single core argument of an intransitive verb, while treating the agent of a transitive verb differently.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: accusative language - language in which the subject of transitive and intransitive verbs is treated the same

Etymology

From Greek ergatēs (worker), relating to the agent in linguistic cases.
