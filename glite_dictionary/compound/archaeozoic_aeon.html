Archaeozoic aeon

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑːrkioʊˈzoʊɪk ˈiːɑn / (AmE), / ˌɑːkiəˈzəʊɪk ˈiːɒn / (BrE)
The geological aeon lasting from about 3,800 million to 2,500 million years ago, during which Earth's crust formed and the earliest unicellular life appeared.
ancient geological aeon, 3.8–2.5 billion years ago

Examples

The Archaeozoic aeon marks a period when Earth's crust first solidified and the oldest known rocks formed.
During the Archaeozoic aeon, simple unicellular organisms were the only forms of life on the planet.
Geologists study the Archaeozoic aeon to learn about the origins of Earth's continental crust.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Precambrian - Earliest part of Earth's history, encompassing the Archaeozoic aeon
  • Degree Higher: Proterozoic - The aeon following the Archaeozoic, marked by more complex life

Etymology

From Greek 'arkhaios' (ancient) + 'zoē' (life), referring to the ancient life forms of that era.
