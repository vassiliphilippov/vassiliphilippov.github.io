shutter telegraph chain

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃʌt̬ɚ ˈtɛl.ɪˌɡræf tʃeɪn / (AmE), / ˈʃʌ.tə ˈtel.ɪˌɡrɑːf tʃeɪn / (BrE)
a historical system for transmitting information over long distances using a series of towers with pivoting shutters, where messages are encoded by the arrangement of mechanical elements and relayed visually from one tower to the next
system of visual signal towers for communication

Examples

In the early nineteenth century, the British navy used a shutter telegraph chain to send urgent messages along the coast.
The operation of a shutter telegraph chain required skilled operators to decode and relay signals quickly.
With the invention of the electric telegraph, the shutter telegraph chain became obsolete.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: semaphore line - visual signaling system using pivoted arms instead of shutters

Etymology

shutter: from Middle English 'shutour', from 'shut'; telegraph: from French 'télégraphe', from Greek 'tele' (far) + 'graphein' (to write).
