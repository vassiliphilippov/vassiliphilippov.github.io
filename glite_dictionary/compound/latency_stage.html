latency stage

1. NOUN

/ ˈleɪtənsi steɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈleɪtənsi steɪdʒ / (BrE)
In psychoanalysis, the period in a child's development, from about age 5 or 6 until puberty, during which sexual impulses are repressed and redirected into socially acceptable activities.
psychoanalytic stage of childhood development
medical

Examples

Freud believed the latency stage was essential for a child's social and cognitive growth.
During the latency stage, children often focus on schoolwork, friendships, and hobbies.
Teachers notice that students in the latency stage tend to channel their energy into learning and socializing.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: phallic stage - the preceding stage in Freudian psychosexual development, before the latency stage
  • Degree Higher: genital stage - the subsequent stage in Freudian psychosexual development, following the latency stage

Etymology

From 'latency' (from Latin latentia, 'lying hidden'), denoting hidden or inactive processes, and 'stage' (from Old French estage, meaning 'dwelling' or 'stage'). Term popularized by Sigmund Freud in psychoanalytic theory.
