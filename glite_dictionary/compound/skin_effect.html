skin effect

1. NOUN

/ skɪn ɪˈfɛkt / (AmE), / skɪn ɪˈfɛkt / (BrE)
The tendency of high-frequency alternating current to flow near the surface of a conductor, rather than evenly throughout its cross section.
distribution of AC near conductor surface

Examples

Engineers must consider the skin effect when designing high-frequency power cables.
At extremely high frequencies, the skin effect significantly increases the resistance of conductors.
Because of the skin effect, thick wires do not always conduct more high-frequency current than thin ones.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eddy current - an electric current induced within conductors by a changing magnetic field
  • Synonym: proximity effect - the tendency of current to distribute in a conductor due to the presence of a nearby conductor carrying current

Etymology

From 'skin', referring to the outer layer, and 'effect', denoting the phenomenon. The term reflects that current concentrates at the 'skin' or surface of the conductor.
