Truman doctrine

1. NOUN

/ ˈtruː.mən ˈdɑːk.trɪn / (AmE), / ˈtruː.mən ˈdɒk.trɪn / (BrE)
The policy stated by U.S. President Harry S. Truman promising economic and military aid to countries threatened by communism or totalitarian ideologies, first articulated in 1947.
U.S. policy aiding countries against communism (1947)

Examples

The Truman doctrine marked a significant shift in American foreign policy during the early Cold War era.
Many historians argue that the Truman doctrine laid the groundwork for U.S. involvement in conflicts like the Korean War.
Under the Truman doctrine, the United States provided support to Greece and Turkey to prevent them from falling under Soviet influence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Marshall Plan - U.S. program of economic aid to rebuild Europe after WWII

Etymology

Named after U.S. President Harry S. Truman (1884–1972), who proposed the policy in 1947.
