immediate constituent

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈmiːdiət kənˈstɪtʃuənt / (AmE), / ɪˈmiːdiət kənˈstɪtjʊənt / (BrE)
a part of a sentence or phrase identified as one of the major components in the first stage of syntactic analysis, such as the subject and predicate
major part in sentence structure analysis

Examples

The sentence was broken down into its two immediate constituents: the subject and the predicate.
Linguists use the concept of immediate constituent to analyze how words group together in a sentence.
By examining each immediate constituent, the structure of the sentence becomes clearer.

Etymology

From 'immediate' (Latin immediatus, 'without anything between') and 'constituent' (Latin constituere, 'to set up'). Used in linguistic theory since the early 20th century.
