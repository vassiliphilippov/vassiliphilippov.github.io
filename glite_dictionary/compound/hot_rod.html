hot rod

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɑt ˌrɑd / (AmE), / ˈhɒt ˌrɒd / (BrE)
a car that has been specially modified to go faster, especially by increasing its engine power or reducing its weight
car modified for speed

Examples

He spent years restoring his grandfather’s hot rod in the garage.
Many enthusiasts gather each summer to showcase their customized hot rods.
The car show was filled with brightly painted hot rods roaring down the street.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: muscle car - a powerful car with a large engine, especially from the US in the 1960s and 70s

Etymology

From 'hot' (intense) + 'rod' (slang for car or engine component), first used in 20th-century American English car culture.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɑt ˌrɑd / (AmE), / ˈhɒt ˌrɒd / (BrE)
A car that has been specially modified to increase its speed and acceleration, often associated with a distinctive style.
car modified for speed
informal american

Examples

His hot rod drew admiring looks at the car show.
In the 1950s, teenagers often built their own hot rods from old vehicles.
She spent months restoring her grandfather's vintage hot rod.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: muscle car - a high-powered car, especially from the 1960s and 1970s, designed for straight-line speed
  • Degree Lower: classic car - an old, usually collectible car, valued for its age and condition

Etymology

From 'hot' (meaning fast) and 'rod' (slang for car), originating in American car culture of the 1940s.

A car that has been specially modified for speed and performance, often featuring custom parts and design.
car modified for high speed
informal american

Examples

His father spent years building his own hot rod from parts collected at junkyards.
Dozens of vintage hot rods lined up along Main Street during the festival.
She joined a club for hot rod enthusiasts who meet every Saturday.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: custom car - car modified for appearance or performance
  • Synonym: muscle car - high-power American car, esp. from 1960s-70s

Etymology

From 'hot' (meaning energetic or exciting) and 'rod', slang for automobile; originated in American car culture of the 1930s-40s.
