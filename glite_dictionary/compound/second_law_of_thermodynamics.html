second law of thermodynamics

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɛkənd lɔ əv ˌθɝmoʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (AmE), / ˈsɛkənd lɔː əv ˌθɜːməʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (BrE)
the principle in physics stating that energy spontaneously spreads from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration, resulting in an increase in entropy in any spontaneous process
law stating entropy increases in spontaneous processes

Examples

The second law of thermodynamics explains why heat flows from hot objects to cold ones.
According to the second law of thermodynamics, no machine can be 100% efficient.
The concept of entropy is central to the second law of thermodynamics.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: first law of thermodynamics - the law of energy conservation in thermodynamics
  • Degree Higher: third law of thermodynamics - the law relating to entropy at absolute zero temperature

Etymology

From Greek 'thermē' (heat) and 'dynamis' (power); term formalized in 19th-century physics.
