perpendicular style

1. NOUN

/ pərˌpɛndɪˈkjulər staɪl / (AmE), / pɜːˌpɛndɪˈkjʊlə staɪl / (BrE)
a style of English Gothic architecture from the 14th and 15th centuries, noted for strong vertical lines, four-centered arches (Tudor arches), and fan vaulting.
English Gothic architectural style with vertical lines

Examples

The chapel was built in the perpendicular style, featuring tall windows with intricate tracery.
Historians admire the perpendicular style for its striking vertical emphasis and elegant vaults.
King's College Chapel in Cambridge is a celebrated example of the perpendicular style in architecture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Gothic architecture - a style of architecture that flourished in Europe during the high and late medieval period

Etymology

So called for its emphasis on perpendicular (vertical) lines. 'Style' from Latin 'stilus' (writing instrument, manner).
