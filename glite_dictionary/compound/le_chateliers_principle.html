Le Chatelier's principle

1. NOUN

/ lə ʃæˈtɛl.i.eɪz ˈprɪn.sə.pəl / (AmE), / lə ʃæˈtɛl.i.eɪz ˈprɪn.sɪ.pl̩ / (BrE)
the principle stating that if a change is applied to a system in equilibrium, the system will adjust to counteract the change and reach a new equilibrium
principle predicting system response to change in equilibrium

Examples

According to Le Chatelier's principle, increasing the pressure shifts the equilibrium toward the side with fewer gas molecules.
Chemists often use Le Chatelier's principle to predict the effect of changing temperature on a reaction.
Le Chatelier's principle explains why adding more reactant shifts the balance toward producing more product.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equilibrium - a state in which opposing forces or influences are balanced

Etymology

Named after French chemist Henri Le Chatelier (1850–1936), who formulated the principle.
