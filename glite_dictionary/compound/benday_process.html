Benday process

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɛn.deɪ ˌprɑː.sɛs / (AmE), / ˈbɛn.deɪ ˌprəʊ.ses / (BrE)
a printing technique that uses small dots or patterns to create shading, texture, or tone in printed images
printing technique using dots for shading

Examples

Many early comic books relied on the Benday process to produce colorful images with limited printing technology.
The artist used the Benday process to create subtle shading in the background of the illustration.
Learning about the Benday process reveals how vintage advertisements achieved their distinctive look.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: halftone - a technique that simulates continuous tone by using dots of varying size or spacing
  • Synonym: screen tone - adhesive patterns used to create shading effects in illustrations

Etymology

Named after Benjamin Day (1838–1916), the American illustrator who invented the process.
