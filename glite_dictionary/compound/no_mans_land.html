no man's land

1. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊ ˌmænz lænd / (AmE), / ˈnəʊ ˌmanz land / (BrE)
land that is unowned, uninhabited, or undesirable; especially, the area between opposing armies' front lines or any region not controlled by any party
unowned or uninhabited land; disputed area

Examples

The area between the two borders was a no man's land filled with debris and abandoned buildings.
During the war, soldiers avoided crossing the no man's land between the trenches.
After the factory closed, the old industrial district became a sort of no man's land.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wasteland - an area that is unused, barren, or ruined

Etymology

First used during World War I to describe the land between enemy trenches.

2. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊ ˌmænz ˌlænd / (AmE), / ˈnəʊ ˌmænz ˌlænd / (BrE)
an area of land between the positions of opposing armies, especially one that is unoccupied or under dispute
unoccupied area between enemy lines

Examples

During the First World War, soldiers rarely dared to cross no man's land under the threat of enemy fire.
After the ceasefire, the border became a no man's land where neither side dared to enter.
The area between the two fences was treated as no man's land and was heavily mined.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: battlefield - an area where a battle is fought between armies

Etymology

Originates from the Middle English 'nomanneslond,' originally denoting a piece of unowned or disputed land.

3. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊ ˌmænz ˌlænd / (AmE), / ˈnəʊ ˌmænz ˌlænd / (BrE)
an area, situation, or state that is ambiguous or undefined, lying between two clearly defined positions or conditions
ambiguous area between two states or categories

Examples

After the merger, some employees found themselves in a no man's land between departments, unsure of their roles.
The debate falls into a no man's land where law and ethics do not clearly align.
Digital privacy is a no man's land as regulations struggle to keep up with technology.
