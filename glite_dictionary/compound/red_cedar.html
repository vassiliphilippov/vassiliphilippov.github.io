red cedar

1. NOUN

/ rɛd ˈsiːdɚ / (AmE), / rɛd ˈsiːdə / (BrE)
a tall tree of the Pacific coast of North America with foliage resembling cypress and cinnamon-red bark, known for its aromatic wood
tall aromatic tree of the Pacific Northwest

Examples

The furniture was made from beautiful red cedar harvested in the Pacific Northwest.
A mature red cedar can tower over 60 meters, providing valuable timber and habitat.
Gardeners often plant red cedar for its striking color and resistance to decay.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: western red cedar - a related species also known as Thuja plicata

2. NOUN

/ ɹɛd ˈsiːdɚ / (AmE), / ɹɛd ˈsiːdə / (BrE)
A large, valuable evergreen tree of the cypress family native to northwestern North America, known for its aromatic, reddish wood.
large evergreen tree with aromatic red wood

Examples

Many Native American tribes used red cedar for building totem poles and canoes.
The furniture was crafted from solid red cedar wood, giving it a pleasant aroma.
In the Pacific Northwest, red cedar trees can grow to impressive heights.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: western red cedar - a specific species, also known as Thuja plicata, native to the Pacific Northwest

3. NOUN

/ ˌrɛd ˈsiːdər / (AmE), / ˌrɛd ˈsiːdə / (BrE)
A small evergreen tree of the juniper family, native to eastern North America, with a conical shape, reddish aromatic wood, and needle-like leaves.
a juniper tree native to eastern North America

Examples

The aromatic wood of the red cedar is often used to make chests and closets.
Many birds nest in the dense foliage of the red cedar tree.
We planted a row of red cedars along the eastern fence for privacy and shade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eastern red cedar - another name for 'red cedar', a juniper species of eastern North America
  • Antonym: western red cedar - a different species of cedar native to western North America

Etymology

From 'red' (referring to the color of the wood) + 'cedar,' though it is actually a juniper.
