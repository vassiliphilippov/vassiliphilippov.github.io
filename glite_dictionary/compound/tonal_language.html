tonal language

1. NOUN

/ ˈtoʊnəl ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈtəʊnəl ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ / (BrE)
A language in which variations in pitch or tone are used to distinguish word meanings.
language where tone differentiates meaning

Examples

Mandarin Chinese is a well-known example of a tonal language where the tone can change a word's meaning.
Linguists often compare tonal languages to non-tonal languages to study how pitch affects communication.
Learning a tonal language can be challenging for speakers of languages without tonal distinctions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: non-tonal language - a language in which pitch does not distinguish word meanings

Etymology

From 'tone' (from Latin 'tonus', meaning 'sound' or 'pitch') + 'language'.
