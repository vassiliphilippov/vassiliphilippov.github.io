Conservative Judaism

1. PROPN

/ kənˌsɝːvətɪv ˈdʒuːdiɪzəm / (AmE), / kənˌsɜːvətɪv ˈdʒuːdiɪzəm / (BrE)
A major branch of Judaism that seeks to conserve Jewish traditions while allowing for adaption of some religious laws to fit modern circumstances.
branch of Judaism balancing tradition and modernity

Examples

Many American Jews identify with Conservative Judaism as a way to balance tradition and modern life.
The rabbi explained the core beliefs of Conservative Judaism during the seminar.
Services at her synagogue follow the practices of Conservative Judaism rather than Reform or Orthodox branches.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Reform Judaism - a branch of Judaism advocating more liberal adaptation of Jewish laws and traditions
  • Degree Higher: Orthodox Judaism - a branch of Judaism strictly adhering to traditional laws and customs

2. NOUN

/ kənˈsɝːvətɪv ˈdʒuːdiɪzəm / (AmE), / kənˈsɜːvətɪv ˈdʒuːdiɪzəm / (BrE)
A branch of Judaism that seeks to conserve Jewish traditions while allowing for modernization and critical scholarship, positioned between Orthodox and Reform Judaism.
branch of Judaism balancing tradition and change

Examples

Many American Jews identify with Conservative Judaism because it balances tradition and modern values.
The synagogue follows the customs of Conservative Judaism in its religious services and educational programs.
Conservative Judaism emerged in the 19th century as a reaction to the changes within Reform Judaism.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Reform Judaism - A liberal branch of Judaism emphasizing change and adaptation
  • Degree Higher: Orthodox Judaism - A traditionalist branch of Judaism adhering strictly to traditional beliefs and practices
