indirect discourse

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.dəˈrɛkt ˈdɪs.kɔrs / (AmE), / ˌɪn.dɪˈrɛkt ˈdɪs.kɔːs / (BrE)
a form of reported speech in which what someone has said is expressed without quoting their exact words, often making appropriate changes to pronouns and verb tenses
reported speech not using exact words

Examples

In English, indirect discourse often requires changing verb tenses and pronouns.
Teachers ask students to convert direct speech into indirect discourse for grammar exercises.
The novel frequently shifts between direct and indirect discourse to show different points of view.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: direct speech - the exact words spoken by someone, quoted as said
  • Synonym: indirect speech - another term for indirect discourse, often used in grammar

Etymology

Formed from the words 'indirect' (from Latin 'indirectus') and 'discourse' (from Latin 'discursus', running to and fro, conversation).
