judicial activism

1. NOUN

/ dʒuˈdɪʃəl ˈæktɪvɪzəm / (AmE), / dʒuˈdɪʃəl ˈæktɪvɪzəm / (BrE)
The practice or philosophy of allowing current social, political, and national needs to influence judicial decisions, particularly in constitutional interpretation.
allowing current needs to influence judicial rulings
legal

Examples

The Supreme Court was criticized for its judicial activism in overturning longstanding precedents.
Many legal scholars debate whether judicial activism undermines or strengthens democracy.
Accusations of judicial activism often arise during controversial court cases involving constitutional rights.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: judicial restraint - the principle of limiting judicial power, favoring narrow interpretations of the law

2. NOUN

/ dʒuˈdɪʃəl ˈæktɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / dʒuˈdɪʃəl ˈæktɪvɪzəm / (BrE)
the practice by courts of making rulings that significantly influence public policy, often based on personal or political considerations rather than strict interpretation of the law
courts influencing policy through rulings
legal

Examples

The court was accused of judicial activism after overturning the long-standing law.
Many scholars debate whether judicial activism enhances or undermines democracy.
Public reaction to judicial activism can vary depending on the outcome of the case.

Synset Examples

Judicial activism often stirs public debate on rights.
Critics of judicial activism argue it undermines democracy.
Judicial activism can lead to significant legal changes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: judicial restraint - philosophy favoring limited court intervention in policy

Etymology

From 'judicial', relating to courts or judges, and 'activism', from Latin 'activus' (active).
