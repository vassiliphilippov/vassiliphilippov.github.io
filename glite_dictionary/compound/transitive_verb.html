transitive verb

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrænzətɪv vɜrb / (AmE), / ˈtrænsətɪv vɜːb / (BrE)
A verb that requires both a subject and a direct object to form a complete sentence.
verb requiring a direct object

Examples

In the sentence 'She reads books', 'reads' is a transitive verb because it takes an object.
When learning English, it's important to distinguish between transitive verbs and intransitive verbs.
The teacher asked the class to give examples of a transitive verb in a sentence.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intransitive verb - a verb that does not take a direct object

Etymology

From Latin 'transitivus' (passing over) + 'verb' from Latin 'verbum'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrænzɪtɪv vɝb / (AmE), / ˈtranzɪtɪv vɜːb / (BrE)
A verb that requires an object to complete its meaning; a verb that must take a direct object.
verb requiring a direct object

Examples

In the sentence 'She kicked the ball,' 'kicked' is a transitive verb.
English learners often have trouble identifying whether a word is a transitive verb or not.
'Eat' can be a transitive verb when used in phrases like 'eat an apple.'

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intransitive verb - verb that does not take a direct object

Etymology

From Latin 'transitivus' (passing over), referring to action passing from subject to object.
