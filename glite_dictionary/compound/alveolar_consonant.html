alveolar consonant

1. NOUN

/ ælˈviːələr ˈkɑːnsənənt / (AmE), / alˈviːələ ˈkɒnsənənt / (BrE)
A consonant sound produced by placing the tip or blade of the tongue close to or against the alveolar ridge (the bony ridge behind the upper front teeth).
consonant articulated near the alveolar ridge

Examples

In English, the alveolar consonant /t/ appears in the word 'time'.
The teacher demonstrated how to produce the alveolar consonant by touching the tongue to the ridge behind the teeth.
Sounds like /d/ and /n/ are also classic examples of alveolar consonants.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: dental consonant - a consonant articulated with the tongue against the teeth
  • Degree Higher: postalveolar consonant - a consonant articulated slightly behind the alveolar ridge

Etymology

From Latin alveolus (small cavity) and consonant.
