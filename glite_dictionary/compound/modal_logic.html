modal logic

1. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊ.dəl ˈlɑː.dʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈməʊ.dəl ˈlɒ.dʒɪk / (BrE)
a branch of logic concerned with the study of necessity and possibility, often using modal operators such as 'necessarily' and 'possibly'
logic dealing with necessity and possibility

Examples

She specialized in modal logic during her graduate studies in philosophy.
The concept of possibility is a central concern in modal logic.
Advances in modal logic have influenced the development of computer science.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: predicate logic - logic dealing with predicates and quantifiers

Etymology

From 'modal', relating to mode or form, and 'logic', from Greek 'logikē' (art of reasoning).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊdəl ˈlɑːdʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈməʊdəl ˈlɒdʒɪk / (BrE)
a system of logic that deals with concepts such as necessity and possibility, used especially in philosophy and mathematics
system of logic dealing with necessity and possibility

Examples

Philosophers often use modal logic to analyze statements about what could or must be true.
In modal logic, the concepts of necessity and possibility play a central role.
She wrote her dissertation on the application of modal logic to computer science.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: classical logic - the traditional system of logic without modal operators
  • Degree Higher: deontic logic - a type of modal logic concerned with obligation and permission

Etymology

From 'modal', relating to mode (from Latin 'modus', measure or manner), and 'logic'.
