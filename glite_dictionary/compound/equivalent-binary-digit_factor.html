equivalent-binary-digit factor

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈkwɪvələnt ˈbaɪnɛri ˈdɪdʒɪt ˈfæktər / (AmE), / ɪˈkwɪvələnt ˈbʌɪnəri ˈdɪdʒɪt ˈfaktə / (BrE)
the average number of binary digits required to represent a single digit of another base in a non-binary numeration system; used to relate the efficiency of different base systems.
average binary digits per non-binary digit

Examples

The equivalent-binary-digit factor helps determine how much storage different numeral systems require in binary form.
When comparing decimal and binary, the equivalent-binary-digit factor shows why computers prefer binary.
A hexadecimal digit has a higher equivalent-binary-digit factor than a decimal digit, but it's still efficient for programming.
