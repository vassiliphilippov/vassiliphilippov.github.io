reverse osmosis

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈvɝs ɑsˈmoʊsɪs / (AmE), / rɪˈvɜːs ɒzˈməʊsɪs / (BrE)
a process in which a solvent, such as water, passes through a semipermeable membrane from a more concentrated solution into a less concentrated one, typically to purify the solvent, by applying pressure greater than the osmotic pressure
the purification of water by forcing it through a membrane

Examples

Many households use reverse osmosis systems to purify their drinking water.
The laboratory relies on reverse osmosis to remove impurities from samples.
Desalination plants often employ reverse osmosis to produce fresh water from seawater.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: osmosis - the natural movement of a solvent through a semipermeable membrane into a solution of higher concentration

Etymology

From 'reverse' (opposite direction) and 'osmosis' (from Greek ōsmos, 'push, thrust').
