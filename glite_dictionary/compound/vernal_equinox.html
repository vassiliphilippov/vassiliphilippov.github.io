vernal equinox

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɜrnəl ˈiːkwənɑks / (AmE), / ˈvɜːnəl ˈiːkwɪnɒks / (BrE)
the time in spring, usually around March 21 in the northern hemisphere, when day and night are of approximately equal length because the sun crosses the celestial equator moving northward.
time in spring when day and night are equal

Examples

The vernal equinox marks the beginning of spring in the northern hemisphere.
Many cultures hold festivals to celebrate the vernal equinox each year.
On the vernal equinox, daylight and darkness are nearly equal in duration.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: autumnal equinox - the date in autumn when day and night are of equal length

Etymology

From Latin 'ver' (spring) + 'aequinoctium' (equal night).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɜrnəl ˈikwəˌnɑks / (AmE), / ˈvɜːnəl ˈiːkwɪnɒks / (BrE)
the point in the sky where the Sun crosses the celestial equator moving northward, marking the beginning of spring in the Northern Hemisphere; occurs in the constellation of Pisces
the astronomical start of spring; Sun crosses celestial equator

Examples

Ancient cultures often held festivals to celebrate the vernal equinox.
The vernal equinox marks the point when day and night are nearly equal in length.
On the vernal equinox, the Sun rises exactly in the east and sets exactly in the west.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: autumnal equinox - the point where the Sun crosses the celestial equator moving southward; start of autumn

Etymology

From Latin 'vernal' (of the spring) and 'equinox' (equal night).
