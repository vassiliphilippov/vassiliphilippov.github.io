white mangrove

1. NOUN

/ waɪt ˈmæŋˌɡroʊv / (AmE), / waɪt ˈmæŋˌɡrəʊv / (BrE)
a shrub or moderately large tree (Laguncularia racemosa) that grows in brackish water along the coasts of western Africa and tropical America, important locally as a source of tannin
coastal shrub or tree of West Africa and tropical America

Examples

Researchers studied the ecological role of the white mangrove in restoring coastal habitats.
Tannin extracted from the white mangrove is used locally for various purposes.
The white mangrove can be distinguished from other mangroves by its rounded leaves and habitat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mangrove - a tropical tree or shrub that grows in tidal coastal swamps

2. NOUN

/ waɪt ˈmæŋɡroʊv / (AmE), / waɪt ˈmæŋɡrəʊv / (BrE)
a small to medium-sized tropical tree growing in brackish water, especially along the shores of the southwestern Pacific, known for its ecological role in coastal environments
a tropical brackish-water tree

Examples

The white mangrove thrives along the edge of saltwater lagoons and estuaries.
Scientists are studying the white mangrove for its role in stabilizing coastal ecosystems.
Fishermen often find juvenile fish sheltering among the roots of the white mangrove.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mangrove - a tropical tree or shrub growing in coastal saline or brackish water
