Euclid's second axiom

1. NOUN

/ ˈjuːklɪdz ˈsɛkənd ˈæksiəm / (AmE), / ˈjuːklɪdz ˈsɛkənd ˈæksiəm / (BrE)
the postulate stating that any finite straight line can be extended continuously without limit in a straight line
Euclidean geometry axiom about extending lines

Examples

According to Euclid's second axiom, a straight line can be extended without any limit in either direction.
The teacher explained Euclid's second axiom as a foundation for constructing infinite lines in geometry.
Understanding Euclid's second axiom is essential to grasp the concept of unbounded lines in classical geometry.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Euclid's first axiom - the postulate that a straight line can be drawn joining any two points
  • Degree Higher: Euclid's third axiom - the postulate that a circle can be drawn with any centre and radius

Etymology

Named after Euclid, the ancient Greek mathematician; 'axiom' from Greek axiōma (that which is thought worthy).
