bunyip aristocracy

1. NOUN

/ ˈbʌn.jɪp ˌɛr.ɪˈstɑː.krə.si / (AmE), / ˈbʌn.jɪp ˌær.ɪsˈtɒk.rə.si / (BrE)
an Australian satirical term for people who claim aristocratic status or act as though they are part of an elite, often implying they are pretentious or self-important
pretentious or self-styled elite in Australia (satirical)
british

Examples

Many reject the idea of a bunyip aristocracy in a country known for its egalitarian values.
The satirical speech ridiculed politicians who saw themselves as part of a bunyip aristocracy.
Historians often reference the debate over establishing a bunyip aristocracy in colonial Australia.

Synset Examples

The town's legends spoke of a bunyip aristocracy ruling the wetlands.
He joked about the bunyip aristocracy that supposedly controlled the river.
The story featured a bunyip aristocracy that governed the swamp.

Etymology

Australian English; originally coined in 1853 by politician and journalist Daniel Deniehy as satire, combining 'bunyip' (a creature from Australian Aboriginal mythology) with 'aristocracy'.
