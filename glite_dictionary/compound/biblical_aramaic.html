Biblical Aramaic

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɪb.lɪ.kəl ˌær.əˈmeɪ.ɪk / (AmE), / ˈbɪb.lɪ.kəl ˌær.əˈmeɪ.ɪk / (BrE)
the form of Aramaic spoken and written in parts of the Hebrew Bible and during the time of the New Testament, chiefly in Palestine
form of Aramaic used in parts of the Hebrew Bible

Examples

Many scholars study Biblical Aramaic to better understand ancient religious texts.
Parts of the books of Daniel and Ezra are written in Biblical Aramaic.
He took a specialized course in Biblical Aramaic during his theology studies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Aramaic - a Semitic language historically spoken in the Near East

Etymology

Aramaic: from Aram, an ancient region; 'Biblical' relates to its use in biblical texts.

2. NOUN

/ bɪˈblɪkəl ˌɛə.rəˈmeɪ.ɪk / (AmE), / bɪˈblɪk.əl ˌær.əˈmeɪ.ɪk / (BrE)
the variety of the Aramaic language used in certain parts of the Hebrew Bible and spoken in ancient Palestine around the time of the New Testament
the form of Aramaic in some Hebrew Bible texts

Examples

Several chapters of the Book of Daniel are written in Biblical Aramaic rather than Hebrew.
Scholars study Biblical Aramaic to better understand certain Old Testament passages.
Differences between Biblical Aramaic and later Aramaic dialects are significant for linguistic research.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Aramaic - a Northwest Semitic language historically spoken in the Near East

Etymology

Aramaic from the ancient region of Aram; 'Biblical' refers to its use in the Bible.
