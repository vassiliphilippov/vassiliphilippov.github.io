intransitive verb

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtrænzətɪv vɜrb / (AmE), / ɪnˈtrænsətɪv vɜːb / (BrE)
A verb that does not take a direct object.
verb without a direct object

Examples

Some verbs, like 'sleep', are classified as intransitive verbs because they do not take an object.
The teacher explained that 'arrive' is an intransitive verb in the sentence, 'She arrived late.'
It's important to know whether a verb is transitive or an intransitive verb when learning English grammar.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transitive verb - verb that requires a direct object

Etymology

From Latin 'intransitivus' (not passing over), from 'in-' (not) + 'transire' (to go across).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtrænzətɪv vɜːrb / (AmE), / ɪnˈtrænzɪtɪv vɜːb / (BrE)
A verb that does not take a direct object; it denotes an action or state that does not require an object.
verb not taking a direct object

Examples

The word 'arrive' is an intransitive verb because it does not take a direct object.
Run is an intransitive verb in the sentence 'He runs quickly.'
Students often confuse intransitive verbs with transitive ones in grammar exercises.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transitive verb - a verb that takes a direct object

Etymology

From Latin 'intransitivus', meaning 'not passing over,' referring to the verb's action not extending to an object.
