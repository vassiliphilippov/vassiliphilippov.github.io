punctuated equilibrium

1. NOUN

/ ˈpʌŋktʃuˌeɪtɪd ˌikwəˈlɪbriəm / (AmE), / ˈpʌŋktjʊeɪtɪd ˌiːkwɪˈlɪbrɪəm / (BrE)
the evolutionary theory that suggests species experience rapid bursts of change interspersed with long periods of stability, rather than gradual, continuous evolution
evolutionary theory of sudden change and stability

Examples

Some paleontologists support punctuated equilibrium as the best explanation for the gaps in the fossil record.
The textbook provides a clear contrast between gradualism and punctuated equilibrium.
Gould and Eldredge proposed punctuated equilibrium to explain patterns seen in evolutionary history.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: gradualism - the theory that evolution proceeds mainly by gradual changes

Etymology

From 'punctuated' (interrupted at intervals) and 'equilibrium' (state of balance), coined in the 1970s by paleontologists Stephen Jay Gould and Niles Eldredge.
