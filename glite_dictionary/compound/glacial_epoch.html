glacial epoch

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡleɪʃəl ˈɛpək / (AmE), / ˈɡleɪsɪəl ˈiːpɒk / (BrE)
A period in Earth's history characterized by extensive glaciation, especially one occurring from about two million to eleven thousand years ago, marked by large ice sheets covering significant parts of the Northern Hemisphere.
period of extensive glaciation in Earth's history

Examples

During the last glacial epoch, much of northern Europe was covered in thick ice sheets.
Scientists study sediment samples to understand climate changes during different glacial epochs.
The migration patterns of ancient humans were greatly influenced by the conditions of the glacial epoch.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ice age - a period of long-term reduction in Earth's temperature resulting in glaciers expanding

Etymology

From Latin 'glacies' (ice) and Greek 'epokhē' (a fixed point in time).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡleɪʃəl ˈɛpək / (AmE), / ˈɡleɪsiəl ˈiːpɒk / (BrE)
A period of time in Earth's history during which glaciers covered large areas of the surface.
period with widespread glaciation

Examples

The Pleistocene is perhaps the best-known glacial epoch in Earth's history.
During a glacial epoch, sea levels drop as water becomes locked in ice sheets.
Many species adapted to survive the harsh conditions of the last glacial epoch.

Synset Examples

the most recent ice age was during the Pleistocene

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ice age - a period of prolonged low temperatures when glaciers advanced

Etymology

From Latin 'glacies' (ice) and Greek 'epokhē' (a fixed point in time, epoch).
