conning tower

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑnɪŋ ˌtaʊər / (AmE), / ˈkɒnɪŋ ˌtaʊə / (BrE)
A raised platform or structure on a submarine, typically housing navigation equipment and providing access for entering and exiting the vessel.
raised structure on a submarine

Examples

The captain stood in the conning tower, scanning the horizon for any sign of danger.
Crew members climbed up through the conning tower to reach the deck when the submarine surfaced.
Some modern submarines have replaced the traditional conning tower with more streamlined structures.

Etymology

From 'con' (to direct the steering of a ship) + 'tower'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑnɪŋ ˌtaʊər / (AmE), / ˈkɒnɪŋ ˌtaʊə / (BrE)
an armored structure on a warship, above the deck, from which the ship is navigated and commanded, usually serving as the pilothouse in battle situations
armored pilothouse on a warship

Examples

The submarine's conning tower rose above the surface as it prepared to make radio contact.
During the battle, officers commanded the ship from the armored conning tower.
After the war, many old warships had their conning towers removed during refits.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bridge - the area on a ship from which it is steered, commanded, or controlled

Etymology

From 'con', meaning to direct the steering of a ship, plus 'tower'.
