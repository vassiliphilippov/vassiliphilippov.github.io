rope-a-dope

1. NOUN

/ ˌroʊp ə ˈdoʊp / (AmE), / ˌrəʊp ə ˈdəʊp / (BrE)
A boxing tactic in which a fighter pretends to be trapped against the ropes, luring an opponent into expending energy by throwing punches.
boxing tactic of feigning vulnerability on the ropes

Examples

Muhammad Ali famously used the rope-a-dope to tire out George Foreman in the Rumble in the Jungle.
Politicians sometimes use a verbal rope-a-dope, letting opponents exhaust themselves with criticism.
The fighter relied on his rope-a-dope technique to outlast his stronger rival.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterpunch - a punch made in reaction to an opponent's attack

Etymology

Coined in the 1970s during Muhammad Ali's boxing matches, combining 'rope' and rhyme with 'dope.'

2. NOUN

/ ˈroʊp ə ˌdoʊp / (AmE), / ˈrəʊp ə ˌdəʊp / (BrE)
a boxing strategy in which a person pretends to be vulnerable or passive to lure an opponent into expending energy, often used figuratively to mean outsmarting or misleading someone by appearing weak or inactive
strategy of luring and tiring out opponents

Examples

The politician's rope-a-dope tactic left his rivals exhausted and off balance.
During the championship fight, the boxer employed a rope-a-dope to tire his opponent.
She used a rope-a-dope in negotiations, allowing the other side to reveal their strategy first.

Synset Examples

His rope-a-dope approach caught everyone off guard during the debate.
He used a rope-a-dope strategy to outlast his opponent.
In the meeting, she deployed a rope-a-dope tactic to gather information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feint - a deceptive or pretended blow, thrust, or other movement

Etymology

Popularized by boxer Muhammad Ali in the 1970s, combining 'rope' and (to be) 'a dope'.
