time constant

1. NOUN

/ taɪm ˈkɑnstənt / (AmE), / taɪm ˈkɒnstənt / (BrE)
(electronics) The time required for a current or voltage in a circuit to rise or fall exponentially by about 63% of its total change.
electronics: time to reach 63% change in circuit

Examples

The capacitor's time constant determines how quickly it charges and discharges in the circuit.
Engineers calculated the time constant to optimize the performance of the signal filter.
A longer time constant means the voltage will change more slowly in response to input.

2. NOUN

/ taɪm ˈkɑnstənt / (AmE), / taɪm ˈkɒnstənt / (BrE)
The time required for a physical quantity, such as current or voltage in a circuit, to change significantly (about 63%) from its initial value, especially defined as the ratio of the inductance (in henries) to the resistance (in ohms) in an electrical circuit.
circuit parameter measuring rate of change

Examples

The time constant of this RL circuit determines how quickly the current reaches its steady value.
A larger inductor or a smaller resistor will increase the time constant of the circuit.
Understanding the time constant helps engineers design filters and control response speeds in electronics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decay constant - the rate at which a quantity decreases over time
