dead reckoning

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɛd ˈrɛkənɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌdɛd ˈrɛkənɪŋ / (BrE)
The process of determining one's position by estimating the direction and distance traveled from a known point, without using celestial observations or external references.
navigation by estimated position

Examples

Sailors relied on dead reckoning when clouds obscured the stars for several days.
Early pilots used dead reckoning to navigate across long stretches of ocean.
Without GPS, hikers sometimes use dead reckoning to retrace their path in the wilderness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: celestial navigation - navigation using observations of the sun, moon, stars, or planets

Etymology

From the phrase 'dead reckon' (to calculate exactly), with 'dead' used as an intensifier; 'reckoning' from Old English 'gerecenian' (to recount, relate).

2. NOUN

/ dɛd ˈrɛkənɪŋ / (AmE), / dɛd ˈrɛkənɪŋ / (BrE)
the process of calculating one's position or an outcome by estimating rather than using precise information, especially based on previously known positions or facts
estimation without precise data

Examples

Without a map, the hikers used dead reckoning to find their way back to camp.
His prediction of the election result was just dead reckoning, with little real data to support it.
Pilots often rely on dead reckoning when their navigation instruments fail.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: estimate - a rough calculation or judgment
  • Synonym: guesswork - the process or result of making a guess

Etymology

Originally a nautical term; 'dead' here is from 'deduced', and 'reckoning' means calculation.
