fair-trade act

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɛr treɪd ækt / (AmE), / ˈfeə treɪd ækt / (BrE)
a former state law in the United States that allowed manufacturers to set minimum retail prices for their products, aimed at preventing price-cutting; repealed federally in 1975
former U.S. law on minimum retail pricing
legal american

Examples

Many retailers were restricted in their pricing strategies due to the fair-trade act until its repeal in 1975.
The fair-trade act allowed manufacturers to enforce minimum prices for branded goods across different stores.
Legal scholars often debate the effects the fair-trade act had on competition in the U.S. market.
