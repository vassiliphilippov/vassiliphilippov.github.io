absolute music

1. NOUN

/ ˈæbsəˌlut ˈmjuːzɪk / (AmE), / ˈabsəluːt ˈmjuːzɪk / (BrE)
Instrumental music that is not explicitly about anything and does not refer to a text, story, or external idea, but exists purely for its musical form and structure.
music with no programmatic or narrative content

Examples

Beethoven's symphonies are often considered prime examples of absolute music.
Many composers of the Romantic era preferred absolute music to program music.
The debate between absolute music and program music shaped much of nineteenth-century musical criticism.

Synset Examples

There were several champions of absolute music, people that believed that music had the ability to transcend the boundaries of human limitations (language, time, space, etc.)

Related Terms

  • Antonym: program music - music intended to evoke images or convey a story

Etymology

From the philosophical use of 'absolute' meaning 'existing independently of anything else'; 'music' from Greek 'mousikē'.
