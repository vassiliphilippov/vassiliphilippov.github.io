iambic senarius

1. NOUN

/ aɪˌæmbɪk səˈnɛəriəs / (AmE), / aɪˌæmbɪk sɪˈnɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
A line of verse consisting of six iambic feet, commonly used in classical Latin comedy, which allows for various rhythmic variations.
six-foot iambic verse line in Latin comedy

Examples

The comedies of Plautus are written predominately in iambic senarius to match everyday speech.
Latin playwrights used iambic senarius as the standard meter for conversational passages.
Understanding iambic senarius is essential for students studying classical Latin drama.

Synset Examples

Variations in iambic senarius are due to resolution (replacing a long with two shorts) and anceps (when a syllable may be either long or short).

Etymology

From Latin: 'iambicus' (relating to iambs) + 'senarius' (containing six each), indicating a six-iamb line.
