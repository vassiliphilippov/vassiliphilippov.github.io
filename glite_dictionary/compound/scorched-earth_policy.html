scorched-earth policy

1. NOUN

/ skɔrtʃt ˈɜrθ ˌpɑləsi / (AmE), / skɔːtʃt ˈɜːθ ˌpɒləsi / (BrE)
A strategy in which a company or military deliberately destroys assets or resources to prevent them from falling into enemy or hostile hands, especially as a defense against takeover or invasion.
deliberate destruction to deny enemy assets

Examples

The company adopted a scorched-earth policy to fend off the hostile takeover bid.
During the war, the retreating army employed a scorched-earth policy to deny resources to the enemy.
Some investors criticized management for using a scorched-earth policy that hurt shareholders.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poison pill - a defensive strategy used by companies to avoid hostile takeovers

Etymology

From the military practice of destroying land and resources to prevent use by advancing enemies; 'scorched earth' refers to land burned to deny agricultural use.

2. NOUN

/ skɔrtʃt ˈɜrθ ˈpɑləsi / (AmE), / skɔːtʃt ˈɜːθ ˈpɒlɪsi / (BrE)
A military or political strategy involving the deliberate destruction of resources or infrastructure, so they cannot be used by an enemy or opponent.
strategy of deliberate resource destruction

Examples

The general adopted a scorched-earth policy to slow down the invading forces.
The company’s scorched-earth policy during negotiations shocked its competitors and partners.
In history, a scorched-earth policy has often been used as a last resort during retreats.

Synset Examples

The general employed a scorched-earth policy during the retreat.
Her scorched-earth policy left no room for negotiation.
They adopted a scorched-earth policy to ensure victory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorched earth - the deliberate destruction of resources to prevent enemy use

Etymology

From the literal military practice of burning crops and property to prevent their use by advancing enemies.
