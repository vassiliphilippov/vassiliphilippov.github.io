Missouri Compromise

1. NOUN

/ mɪˈzʊr.i kəmˈprɑː.maɪz / (AmE), / mɪˈzʊə.ri kəmˈprɒ.maɪz / (BrE)
an agreement passed in 1820 in the United States admitting Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state, and regulating slavery in the western territories
U.S. 1820 agreement on slavery and territories

Examples

The Missouri Compromise temporarily eased tensions between the North and South over the issue of slavery.
Historians often cite the Missouri Compromise as a pivotal moment in antebellum American politics.
Many politicians debated fiercely before the Missouri Compromise was finally enacted in 1820.

Etymology

Named after the U.S. state of Missouri and 'compromise,' from Late Latin compromissum (a mutual promise).
