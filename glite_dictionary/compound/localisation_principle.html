localisation principle

1. NOUN

/ loʊˌkæləˈzeɪʃən ˈprɪnsəpəl / (AmE), / ləʊˌkælɪˈzeɪʃən ˈprɪnsɪpəl / (BrE)
(physiology) The theory that specific functions are associated with specific, relatively distinct locations or areas within parts or organs of the body, especially the brain.
theory linking functions to specific body parts or areas
medical

Examples

The localisation principle suggests that language ability is largely situated in specific regions of the brain.
Early neurologists used the localisation principle to map out brain functions after head injuries.
Modern neuroscience has challenged the strict version of the localisation principle, arguing for more distributed processing.
