corrosive sublimate

1. NOUN

/ kəˈroʊsɪv ˈsʌblɪˌmeɪt / (AmE), / kəˈrəʊsɪv ˈsʌblɪmeɪt / (BrE)
a white, poisonous, soluble crystalline compound of mercury (mercuric chloride), formerly used as a pesticide, antiseptic, or wood preservative.
poisonous mercury compound, used as antiseptic, pesticide

Examples

Laboratory safety protocols strictly prohibit the use of corrosive sublimate due to its high toxicity.
In the nineteenth century, corrosive sublimate was commonly used to disinfect surgical instruments.
Wooden artifacts were once preserved using corrosive sublimate, but safer chemicals are now preferred.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mercuric chloride - alternative chemical name for corrosive sublimate

Etymology

From Latin 'sublimatus' (raised on high, lofty), in reference to the process of sublimation; 'corrosive' referring to its destructive chemical properties.
