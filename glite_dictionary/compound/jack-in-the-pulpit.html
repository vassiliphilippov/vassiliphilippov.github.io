jack-in-the-pulpit

1. NOUN

/ ˌdʒæk ɪn ðə ˈpʌlpɪt / (AmE), / ˌdʒæk ɪn ðə ˈpʌlpɪt / (BrE)
A North American woodland plant (Arisaema triphyllum) with a hooded green and purple flower and cluster of bright red berries.
North American woodland plant with hooded flower

Examples

We spotted a jack-in-the-pulpit growing near the edge of the forest trail.
The unique shape of the jack-in-the-pulpit makes it easy to identify in spring.
Many gardeners appreciate the red berries of the jack-in-the-pulpit in late summer.

Etymology

Named for its resemblance to a preacher (Jack) in a church pulpit.

2. NOUN

/ ˌdʒæk ɪn ðə ˈpʌlpɪt / (AmE), / ˌdʒak ɪn ðə ˈpʌlpɪt / (BrE)
A woodland plant of the arum family, typically having a hooded green or purple spathe and a spadix, and appearing in early spring.
woodland plant with hooded spathe

Examples

Every spring, the jack-in-the-pulpit appears in shaded woodlands, its striped hood unfurling among the leaves.
We identified a jack-in-the-pulpit by its unique flower structure during our nature walk.
Gardeners appreciate the jack-in-the-pulpit for its striking appearance in shady areas.

Etymology

American English, from the fancied resemblance of the spadix ("Jack") in its spathe ("pulpit") to a preacher in a church pulpit.
