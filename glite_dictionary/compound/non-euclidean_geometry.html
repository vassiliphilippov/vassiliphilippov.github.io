non-Euclidean geometry

1. NOUN

/ nɑn juˈklɪdiən dʒiˈɑmətri / (AmE), / nɒn juːˈklɪdiən dʒiˈɒmətri / (BrE)
A branch of geometry based on axioms or postulates that differ from those of Euclidean geometry, leading to geometric systems where, for example, parallel lines may behave differently.
branch of geometry with non-Euclidean axioms

Examples

Einstein's theory of general relativity relies on non-Euclidean geometry to describe the structure of space-time.
In non-Euclidean geometry, the sum of the angles in a triangle may not equal 180 degrees.
Studying non-Euclidean geometry can deepen your understanding of mathematical concepts beyond classical geometry.

Synset Examples

non-Euclidean geometries discard or replace one or more of the Euclidean axioms

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Euclidean geometry - geometry based on Euclid's postulates

Etymology

From 'non-' (not) + 'Euclidean', after Euclid, the ancient Greek mathematician, whose axioms define classical geometry.
