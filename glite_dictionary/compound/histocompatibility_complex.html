histocompatibility complex

1. NOUN

/ ˌhɪstoʊkəmˌpætəˈbɪlɪti ˈkɑmplɛks / (AmE), / ˌhɪstəʊkəmˌpætəˈbɪlɪti ˈkɒmplɛks / (BrE)
a group of genes on chromosome 6 in humans that code for cell-surface proteins involved in the immune response, determining compatibility for organ transplantation
gene group important in immune response
medical

Examples

The histocompatibility complex determines whether a donated organ will be accepted or rejected by the recipient’s immune system.
Researchers study the histocompatibility complex to better understand autoimmune diseases.
Genetic variation within the histocompatibility complex can influence susceptibility to infections.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: major histocompatibility complex - the main set of genes playing this role in humans and other vertebrates

Etymology

From 'histocompatible' (from Greek 'histos' meaning tissue) + 'complex' (set of related genes).
