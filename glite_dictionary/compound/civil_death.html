civil death

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪvəl dɛθ / (AmE), / ˈsɪvəl dɛθ / (BrE)
the loss or cancellation of a person's civil rights, especially as a result of conviction for a serious crime
loss of civil rights by legal action
legal

Examples

After the conviction, the prisoner effectively suffered civil death, losing the right to vote and own property.
Historically, bankruptcy could result in civil death, stripping individuals of key legal protections.
Some legal scholars debate whether certain modern penalties amount to a form of civil death.

Etymology

From Latin 'civilis' (relating to citizens) and 'death', metaphorically indicating the end of civil existence.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪvəl dɛθ / (AmE), / ˈsɪvəl dɛθ / (BrE)
The legal status of a person who is alive but has been deprived of all or most civil rights, especially as a result of conviction or life imprisonment.
legal loss of civil rights
legal

Examples

After his conviction, the prisoner was subjected to civil death and could not participate in any legal or political process.
In some jurisdictions, life imprisonment can effectively result in civil death for the incarcerated person.
Advocates argue that civil death is a harsh punishment that leaves individuals without any means to participate in society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disenfranchisement - the act of depriving someone of the right to vote or other rights
  • Antonym: civil rights - rights protecting an individual's freedom and ensuring equality before the law

Etymology

From Latin 'civilis' (relating to a citizen) plus English 'death'; refers metaphorically to the loss of legal personhood.
