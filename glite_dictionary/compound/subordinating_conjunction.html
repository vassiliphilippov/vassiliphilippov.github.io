subordinating conjunction

1. NOUN

/ səˌbɔːrdəˌneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋk.ʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔː.dɪ.neɪ.tɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋk.ʃən / (BrE)
a conjunction that introduces a dependent clause, connecting it to a main clause
a conjunction that introduces a dependent clause

Examples

‘Because’ is a common subordinating conjunction in English grammar.
The sentence uses a subordinating conjunction to link the dependent clause to the main clause.
Words like ‘after’ and ‘although’ are classified as subordinating conjunctions.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: conjunction - a word used to connect clauses or sentences

Etymology

From 'subordinate' (from Latin subordinatus 'subjected, subordinated') + 'conjunction' (from Latin conjunctio 'joining together').

2. NOUN

/ səˌbɔrdəˌneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔːdɪneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
a conjunction that introduces a subordinate clause, making it dependent on a main clause
conjunction introducing a subordinate clause

Examples

Words like 'because' and 'although' are common subordinating conjunctions in English.
When teaching grammar, understanding the role of a subordinating conjunction can help students write complex sentences.
In the sentence 'She went inside because it was raining,' 'because' is the subordinating conjunction.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: coordinating conjunction - a conjunction that joins words or clauses of equal importance

Etymology

From 'subordinate' (from Latin sub- ‘under’ + ordinare ‘to arrange’) and 'conjunction' (from Latin conjunctio ‘joining together’).
