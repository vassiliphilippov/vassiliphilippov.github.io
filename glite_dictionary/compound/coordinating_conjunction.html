coordinating conjunction

1. NOUN

/ koʊˌɔrdɪˈneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / kəʊˌɔːdɪˈneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
a conjunction that joins words, phrases, or clauses of equal grammatical rank, such as 'and', 'but', or 'or'
a conjunction joining equal grammatical elements

Examples

You should use a coordinating conjunction to join two independent clauses.
In the sentence 'I wanted to go, but it was raining,' 'but' is a coordinating conjunction.
'Or' is often used as a coordinating conjunction to present alternatives.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subordinating conjunction - a conjunction that joins a dependent clause to a main clause

Etymology

Formed from 'coordinate' (from Latin co- + ordinare, to arrange) and 'conjunction' (from Latin coniunctio, joining together).

2. NOUN

/ koʊˌɔːrdəˌneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / kəʊˌɔːdɪˌneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
a word, such as 'and', 'but', or 'or', that joins two words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance in a sentence
word joining equal elements (and, but, or)

Examples

Use a coordinating conjunction like 'and' to connect the two clauses.
When joining main clauses, a coordinating conjunction such as 'but' is used.
He brought snacks and drinks, linking the items with a coordinating conjunction.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subordinating conjunction - a conjunction that introduces a subordinate clause (e.g., although, because)

Etymology

From Latin 'coordinare' (to arrange together) and 'conjunctio' (joining).
