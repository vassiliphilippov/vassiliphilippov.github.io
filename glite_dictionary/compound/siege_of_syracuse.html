Siege of Syracuse

1. PROPN

/ ˈsiːdʒ əv səˈraɪkjuːs / (AmE), / ˈsiːdʒ əv sɪˈraɪkjuːs / (BrE)
the prolonged military siege of the city of Syracuse by Athenian forces from 415 to 413 BC, ending in defeat for the Athenians
Athenian siege of Syracuse, 415–413 BC

Examples

The Siege of Syracuse was a turning point in the Peloponnesian War.
Many historians analyze the failure of the Athenians at the Siege of Syracuse as a crucial moment in Greek history.
The Siege of Syracuse demonstrated the strategic importance of naval power in ancient warfare.

2. NOUN

/ sidʒ əv səˈraɪkjus / (AmE), / siːdʒ əv səˈrʌɪkjuːz / (BrE)
the Roman siege of the city of Syracuse (214–212 BC) during the Second Punic War, which resulted in the city's capture and the death of Archimedes.
Roman siege of Syracuse, 214–212 BC

Examples

The Siege of Syracuse is remembered for the ingenious defenses designed by Archimedes.
During the Siege of Syracuse, Roman forces blockaded the city for two years.
Many historians study the Siege of Syracuse to understand ancient military technology.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: siege - a military operation in which forces surround a place to compel surrender
