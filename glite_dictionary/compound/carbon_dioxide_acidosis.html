carbon dioxide acidosis

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑrbən daɪˌɑksaɪd əˈsɪdoʊsɪs / (AmE), / ˌkɑːbən dʌiˌɒksaɪd əˈsɪdəʊsɪs / (BrE)
A form of acidosis caused by reduced gas exchange in the lungs, leading to excess carbon dioxide in the blood and increased acidity.
acidosis caused by excess carbon dioxide
medical

Examples

Patients with severe emphysema are at risk of carbon dioxide acidosis due to poor gas exchange.
The doctor explained that carbon dioxide acidosis can lead to confusion and lethargy in patients.
Rapid breathing is sometimes a response to carbon dioxide acidosis as the body tries to expel excess CO2.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: respiratory acidosis - acidosis due to failure of ventilation leading to CO2 retention
  • Antonym: metabolic acidosis - acidosis caused by underlying metabolic processes rather than respiratory failure

Etymology

From 'carbon dioxide' and 'acidosis', with 'acidosis' from acid + -osis (denoting a medical condition).
