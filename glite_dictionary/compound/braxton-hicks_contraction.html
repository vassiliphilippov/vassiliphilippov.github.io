Braxton-Hicks contraction

1. NOUN

/ ˈbrækstən ˌhɪks kənˈtrækʃən / (AmE), / ˈbrækstən ˌhɪks kənˈtrækʃən / (BrE)
a painless tightening of the uterus that occurs intermittently throughout pregnancy, often increasing in frequency as pregnancy progresses, but not indicating the onset of true labor
intermittent painless uterine contraction during pregnancy
medical

Examples

Many pregnant women experience Braxton-Hicks contractions in the third trimester, mistaking them for true labor.
The doctor explained that Braxton-Hicks contractions are usually harmless and help prepare the body for childbirth.
If your Braxton-Hicks contractions become regular or painful, you should consult your healthcare provider.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: labor contraction - painful, rhythmic uterine contractions signaling the onset of labor

Etymology

Named after John Braxton Hicks (1823–1897), English doctor who first described the contractions.
