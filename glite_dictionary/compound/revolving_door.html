revolving door

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈvɑlvɪŋ dɔɹ / (AmE), / rɪˈvɒlvɪŋ dɔː / (BrE)
an organization or institution where there is frequent turnover of personnel or membership
organization with frequent staff turnover

Examples

The company has become a revolving door, with employees constantly coming and going.
After a year, the department was known as a revolving door because so many staff members had left.
When management changed, the hospital turned into a revolving door for nurses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: turnover - rate at which employees leave and are replaced

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈvɑlvɪŋ dɔr / (AmE), / rɪˈvɒlvɪŋ dɔː / (BrE)
A door made of several panels arranged around a central axis which rotate to allow people to enter or leave a building while minimizing the exchange of air.
rotating entrance door with panels

Examples

The hotel lobby featured a grand revolving door that spun smoothly as guests arrived.
She got briefly stuck in the revolving door during the morning rush.
Revolving doors are often used in office buildings to save energy by limiting airflow.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: swinging door - a door that opens by swinging on hinges

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈvɑlvɪŋ dɔr / (AmE), / rɪˈvɒlvɪŋ dɔː / (BrE)
a situation in which the same positions or experiences are repeatedly occupied or undergone, especially because of frequent staff turnover or recurring cycles
frequent turnover or repeated cycles

Examples

The company had a revolving door of managers, making it hard to implement long-term changes.
She was tired of being caught in a revolving door of failed relationships.
High staff turnover created a revolving door environment in the department.

Synset Examples

The company has a revolving door of employees.
She felt stuck in a revolving door of bad relationships.
The revolving door policy led to constant changes in leadership.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: turnover - rate at which employees leave and are replaced
  • Synonym: attrition - gradual reduction in staff, especially by not replacing those who leave

Etymology

From the literal 'revolving door,' a type of entrance, used metaphorically since the 20th century.
