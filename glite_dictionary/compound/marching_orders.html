marching orders

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɑrtʃɪŋ ˌɔrdɚz / (AmE), / ˈmɑːtʃɪŋ ˌɔːdəz / (BrE)
an official order given to soldiers to begin moving or leave a place
military order to move or depart

Examples

The captain gave the company their marching orders at dawn.
After the briefing, the soldiers waited for their marching orders.
When the alarm sounded, we knew our marching orders would follow soon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: command - an authoritative order
  • Degree Higher: discharge - official release from military service

Etymology

From 'march' (to walk as soldiers) and 'order' (command); originally military slang.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɑrtʃɪŋ ˈɔrdɚz / (AmE), / ˈmɑːtʃɪŋ ˈɔːdəz / (BrE)
informal notice telling someone to leave a job, place, or situation; dismissal or discharge.
notice of dismissal or discharge (informal)
informal

Examples

After the mistake at work, he finally got his marching orders.
The company cutbacks meant several senior managers received their marching orders yesterday.
She was late too many times and knew she might get her marching orders soon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: the boot - informal dismissal from a job
  • Synonym: dismissal - the act of removing someone from a job or position

Etymology

Originally military, referring to orders directing soldiers to march; later broadened as idiom for dismissal.
