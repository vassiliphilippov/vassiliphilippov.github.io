direct proof

1. NOUN

/ dəˈrɛkt ˈpruf / (AmE), / dɪˈrɛkt ˈpruːf / (BrE)
a method of proving a statement to be true by logically combining established facts, such as axioms and theorems, without making additional assumptions
mathematical proof using only established facts

Examples

The mathematician presented a direct proof of the theorem in her lecture.
A direct proof often relies on previously established results and logical deduction.
When possible, a direct proof is usually preferred for its clarity and simplicity.

Synset Examples

A proof by contraposition of the implication p ⇒ q is a direct proof of ¬q ⇒ ¬p (the contrapositive of p ⇒ q).

Related Terms

  • Antonym: indirect proof - a proof that establishes the truth by showing that its denial leads to a contradiction
