connective tissue

1. NOUN

/ kəˈnɛktɪv ˌtɪs.u / (AmE), / kəˈnɛktɪv ˌtɪs.juː / (BrE)
tissue that supports, binds, or separates other tissues and organs in the body, consisting mainly of cells such as fibroblasts and fibers like collagen
body tissue supporting or connecting organs
medical

Examples

Tendons are composed primarily of connective tissue that links muscles to bones.
Swelling can occur when connective tissue is damaged after an injury.
Some diseases, such as lupus, can directly affect the body's connective tissue.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: epithelial tissue - tissue forming the outer layer of body surfaces and lining hollow structures

2. NOUN

/ kəˈnɛk.tɪv ˈtɪʃ.uː / (AmE), / kəˈnɛk.tɪv ˈtɪs.juː / (BrE)
Something that serves to link, join, or support different parts or groups, either literally as a type of body tissue or figuratively as a unifying element.
linking or supporting element (literal or figurative)

Examples

Collaboration within teams often acts as the connective tissue holding organizations together.
Shared values can serve as the connective tissue among diverse community members.
Technology has become the connective tissue of modern communication networks.

Synset Examples

Friendship is the connective tissue that binds us together.
Healthy connective tissue is essential for joint mobility.
The manager acts as the connective tissue between departments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bond - a force or influence that unites people or groups
  • Synonym: link - a relationship or connection between things

Etymology

From Latin 'connectere' (to bind together) + 'tissue' (structure of cells).
