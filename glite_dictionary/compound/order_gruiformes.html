order Gruiformes

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔrdɚ ɡruːɪˈfɔːrmiːz / (AmE), / ˈɔːdə ɡruːɪˈfɔːmiːz / (BrE)
A group of inland marsh-dwelling birds, such as cranes, rails, and bustards, characterized by long legs, necks, and bills for wading in water to search for food.
a bird order including cranes, rails, and bustards

Examples

The wetland reserve is home to several species within the order Gruiformes, including rails and cranes.
Many birds in the order Gruiformes rely on marshes and wetlands for nesting and feeding.
Ornithologists are studying population declines in the order Gruiformes to better understand environmental changes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crane - a large, long-legged, long-necked wading bird
  • Synonym: rail - a small, slender marsh bird in the order Gruiformes
  • Synonym: bustard - a large terrestrial bird also in the order Gruiformes

Etymology

From Latin 'gruis' (crane) + Greek 'formes' (forms), referring to the shape of cranes.
