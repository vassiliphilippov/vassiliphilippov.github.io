sour mash

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊɚ mæʃ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊə mæʃ / (BrE)
A mash made with a portion of previously fermented mash to ensure optimal acidity for yeast fermentation, used in the production of some whiskeys.
acidic mash used in whiskey making

Examples

The distillery prides itself on using the sour mash process to enhance flavor and consistency.
Bourbon made from sour mash has a distinct, rich character appreciated by connoisseurs.
To maintain quality, a portion of the old sour mash is always used in the next batch.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: sweet mash - mash made without adding previously fermented mash, used in some whiskey production

Etymology

From 'sour' (referring to increased acidity) and 'mash' (the mixture of grains and water in brewing/distilling).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊɚ mæʃ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊə mæʃ / (BrE)
whiskey made using a fermentation process in which part of a previous batch is used to start the fermentation of a new batch, giving a distinctive sour flavor.
whiskey made using a souring process

Examples

Many American bourbons are produced using the sour mash method to ensure a consistent flavor.
The distillery is famous for its traditional sour mash whiskeys.
He prefers the smooth taste of sour mash bourbon over other styles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whiskey - a distilled alcoholic beverage made from a fermented grain mash
