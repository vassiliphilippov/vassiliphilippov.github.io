atomic theory

1. NOUN

/ əˈtɑmɪk ˈθiəri / (AmE), / əˈtɒmɪk ˈθɪəri / (BrE)
A scientific theory stating that all matter is made up of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms.
theory that matter is made of atoms

Examples

John Dalton developed the modern atomic theory in the early nineteenth century.
The atomic theory helps explain chemical reactions and the conservation of mass.
Ancient philosophers like Democritus proposed an early form of atomic theory.

Synset Examples

the ancient Greek philosophers Democritus and Epicurus held atomic theories of the universe

Related Terms

  • Synonym: molecular theory - theory that matter is composed of molecules

Etymology

atomic from Greek atomos (indivisible); theory from Greek theōria (contemplation, speculation).

2. NOUN

/ əˈtɑm.ɪk ˈθɪr.i / (AmE), / əˈtɒm.ɪk ˈθɪə.ri / (BrE)
The scientific theory that all matter is composed of atoms, and that the atom is the basic unit of matter.
scientific theory about atoms

Examples

Dalton's atomic theory transformed the study of chemistry in the 19th century.
Modern atomic theory explains chemical bonding and reactions at the molecular level.
She is researching the early history of atomic theory in her physics class.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atomic model - a physical model representing the structure of the atom
  • Degree Lower: molecular theory - theory concerning the structure of molecules

Etymology

From 'atomic' (from Greek 'atomos', indivisible) and 'theory' (from Greek 'theoria', speculation).
