Jerusalem artichoke

1. NOUN

/ dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːrtɪˌtʃoʊk / (AmE), / dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːtɪˌtʃəʊk / (BrE)
a tall North American perennial plant of the sunflower family, cultivated for its edible, knobby tubers
edible tuber-producing plant

Examples

Roasted Jerusalem artichoke has a sweet, nutty flavor that pairs well with thyme.
The farmer grew rows of Jerusalem artichoke for their nutritious tubers.
She added sliced Jerusalem artichoke to the soup to give it a unique texture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sunflower - a tall plant of the daisy family with large yellow flowers, of which Jerusalem artichoke is a species

Etymology

'Jerusalem' is a corruption of Italian 'girasole' (sunflower); not related to the city.

2. NOUN

/ dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːrtʃɪˌkoʊk / (AmE), / dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːtɪˌtʃəʊk / (BrE)
A North American sunflower (Helianthus tuberosus) grown for its edible, knobby tubers, which can be eaten raw, boiled, or fried.
edible tuber of a sunflower plant

Examples

She added slices of Jerusalem artichoke to the salad for a sweet, nutty flavor.
The chef recommended roasting Jerusalem artichoke with olive oil and herbs.
Have you ever tried Jerusalem artichoke soup on a cold winter's day?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sunchoke - an alternative name for the Jerusalem artichoke

Etymology

The name is a corruption of Italian 'girasole articiocco' ('sunflower artichoke'); not related to Jerusalem.

3. NOUN

/ dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːrtɪˌtʃoʊk / (AmE), / dʒəˈruːsələm ˈɑːtɪˌtʃəʊk / (BrE)
a North American sunflower (Helianthus tuberosus) cultivated for its edible, knobby tubers, which are eaten as a vegetable
edible tuber; type of sunflower

Examples

Roasted Jerusalem artichoke makes a delicious and nutty side dish for winter meals.
The farmer harvested several kilos of Jerusalem artichokes from the field.
Have you ever tried making soup with Jerusalem artichoke as the main ingredient?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sunchoke - another name for the Jerusalem artichoke

Etymology

Named for a corruption of Italian 'girasole' (sunflower) and 'artichoke', due to a similar flavor.
