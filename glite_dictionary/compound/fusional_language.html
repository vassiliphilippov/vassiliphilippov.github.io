fusional language

1. NOUN

/ ˈfjuʒənəl ˈlæŋwɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfjuːʒənəl ˈlæŋgwɪdʒ / (BrE)
a language in which single morphemes can express several grammatical meanings (such as tense, number, and case), characteristic of certain synthetic languages.
language with morphemes showing multiple grammatical meanings

Examples

Spanish is an example of a fusional language, where verb endings encode both tense and subject.
In a fusional language, a single suffix might indicate case, number, and gender at the same time.
Many Indo-European languages, like Russian and Latin, are considered fusional languages.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: agglutinative language - language in which morphemes are joined together but each usually expresses a single meaning
  • Antonym: analytic language - language largely relying on separate words rather than inflection for grammatical relationships

Etymology

From 'fusion' (joining elements), referring to the merging of grammatical information in word forms.
