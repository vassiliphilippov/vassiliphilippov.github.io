pathetic fallacy

1. NOUN

/ pəˈθɛtɪk ˈfæləsi / (AmE), / pəˈθɛtɪk ˈfaləsi / (BrE)
the literary device or fallacy of attributing human feelings or responses to inanimate objects, especially in nature
ascribing human emotions to things or nature

Examples

Many poets use the pathetic fallacy to make the weather reflect a character's mood.
Describing the clouds as 'angry' is an example of the pathetic fallacy.
The novel is rich in imagery, including frequent uses of the pathetic fallacy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: personification - attributing human characteristics to non-human things in general

Etymology

Coined by John Ruskin in 1856, from Greek pathētikos (capable of feeling) and fallacy (mistaken belief).
