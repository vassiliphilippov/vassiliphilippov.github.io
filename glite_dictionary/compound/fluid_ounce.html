fluid ounce

1. NOUN

/ ˌfluːɪd ˈaʊns / (AmE), / ˌfluːɪd ˈaʊns / (BrE)
a unit of volume for liquids, equal to 1⁄20 of a British pint (about 28.4 millilitres) or 1⁄16 of an American pint (about 29.6 millilitres)
unit of liquid volume (approx. 28–30 ml)

Examples

The recipe calls for three fluid ounces of milk.
He drank a full fluid ounce of cough syrup as directed.
British and American fluid ounces are not exactly the same in volume.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ounce - unit of weight or volume
  • Antonym: millilitre - metric unit of liquid volume

Etymology

From fluid (liquid) + ounce (from Old French *unce*, from Latin *uncia*, a twelfth part).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfluːɪd aʊns / (AmE), / ˈfluːɪd aʊns / (BrE)
A unit of capacity or volume in the U.S. customary system, equal to 1.804 cubic inches or approximately 29.57 milliliters.
U.S. volume unit (about 29.57 ml)

Examples

The recipe calls for eight fluid ounces of milk.
A standard soda can holds twelve fluid ounces.
He measured out exactly four fluid ounces for the experiment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ounce - unit of weight (about 28.35 grams) or volume

Etymology

From 'fluid' (liquid) and 'ounce' (a weight and volume unit from Latin 'uncia', twelfth part).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfluɪd aʊns / (AmE), / ˈfluːɪd aʊns / (BrE)
A unit of liquid capacity in the US customary system, equal to 1/16 of a US pint or about 29.573 milliliters.
US liquid volume unit, 1/16 of a US pint

Examples

The recipe calls for one fluid ounce of lemon juice.
Each bottle contains twelve fluid ounces of water.
He measured exactly two fluid ounces of syrup for the cocktail.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ounce - a unit of weight or mass (about 28.35 grams)

Etymology

From 'fluid' (liquid) + 'ounce', from Old French 'unce', from Latin 'uncia' (twelfth part).
