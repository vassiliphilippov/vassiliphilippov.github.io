ablative absolute

1. NOUN

/ ˈæblətɪv ˈæbsəˌluːt / (AmE), / ˈablətɪv ˈabsəluːt / (BrE)
(in Latin grammar) a construction consisting of a noun and participle both in the ablative case, forming a phrase that modifies the sentence as a whole and is grammatically independent of the rest of the sentence.
Latin grammatical construction; noun+modifier phrase

Examples

The Roman historian frequently used the ablative absolute to provide background information at the start of a sentence.
Understanding the ablative absolute is essential for accurately translating Latin passages.
She explained how the ablative absolute differs from a clause in English.

Etymology

From Latin 'ablativus absolutus', meaning 'loosened from the ablative', referring to its grammatically independent nature.
