Old English

1. NOUN

/ oʊld ˈɪŋɡlɪʃ / (AmE), / əʊld ˈɪŋɡlɪʃ / (BrE)
The form of the English language spoken and written in parts of England and southern Scotland before about AD 1100.
early form of the English language (before 1100)

Examples

He studied Old English literature during his university course.
Many modern English words have their roots in Old English.
It can be difficult to read texts written in Old English without special training.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Middle English - form of English spoken from about 1100 to 1500

Etymology

Refers to the earliest stage of the English language, also called Anglo-Saxon.

2. NOUN

/ oʊld ˈɪŋɡlɪʃ / (AmE), / əʊld ˈɪŋɡlɪʃ / (BrE)
the earliest form of the English language, spoken and written in parts of what is now England and southern Scotland from about the mid-5th century to the mid-12th century
early form of the English language

Examples

Many medieval texts were originally written in Old English.
Students often find Old English challenging because of its unfamiliar grammar and vocabulary.
The epic poem Beowulf was composed in Old English.

Synset Examples

They studied Old English to better understand Shakespeare.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Middle English - the English language as spoken after Old English until about 1500

Etymology

Refers to the English language as it existed in England before roughly 1150; from 'old' + 'English'.
