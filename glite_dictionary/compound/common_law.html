common law

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑm.ən lɔ / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.ən lɔː / (BrE)
A system of law based on judicial precedents and customs rather than written statutes, originating in England and used in many English-speaking countries.
legal system based on precedents, not statutes
legal

Examples

Many countries that were once British colonies still follow the principles of common law.
Under common law, court decisions have significant authority in shaping future legal outcomes.
The U.S. legal system is influenced by both statutes and common law precedents.

Synset Examples

common law originated in the unwritten laws of England and was later applied in the United States

Related Terms

  • Antonym: statutory law - laws enacted by a legislative body

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑmən lɔ / (AmE), / ˈkɒmən lɔː / (BrE)
The body of law developed from judicial decisions and customs, rather than from statutes, especially as practiced in England and countries influenced by its legal system.
body of law based on judicial precedents
legal

Examples

The principles of common law have evolved over centuries of court rulings.
Many countries with a British heritage have legal systems based on common law.
Under common law, decisions by higher courts are binding on lower courts.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: statute law - law made by legislative enactment

Etymology

From Middle English 'comoun lawe', translating Norman French 'la comune ley'.
