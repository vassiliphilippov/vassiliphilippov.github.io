King's shilling

1. NOUN

/ kɪŋz ˈʃɪlɪŋ / (AmE), / kɪŋz ˈʃɪlɪŋ / (BrE)
a historical term referring to the money given to a recruit as a token of their enlistment in the British army, often used metaphorically to symbolize acceptance of military service and loyalty to the Crown
token of enlistment in British army
british archaic

Examples

In those days, taking the King's shilling meant a binding commitment to serve.
Many young men found themselves in the army after accepting the King's shilling in a local tavern.
Once you had the King's shilling in your pocket, turning back was no longer an option.

Synset Examples

Joining the army meant accepting the King's shilling.
He was loyal, having taken the King's shilling.
Once you accept the King's shilling, you owe loyalty.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Queen's shilling - the equivalent term used during the reign of a queen, referring to the same enlistment practice

Etymology

From the practice in Britain of giving a shilling to new army recruits as a symbolic payment of enlistment, dating from the 18th century.
