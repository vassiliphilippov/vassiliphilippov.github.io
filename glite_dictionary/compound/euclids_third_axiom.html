Euclid's third axiom

1. NOUN

/ ˈjuːklɪdz θɜrd ˈæksiəm / (AmE), / ˈjuːklɪdz θɜːd ˈæksiəm / (BrE)
the geometric axiom stating that a circle with any radius can be drawn around any given point
geometric axiom about circles

Examples

According to Euclid's third axiom, you can draw a circle of any size at any location.
When proving the theorem, we relied on Euclid's third axiom to construct the required circle.
In geometry class, the teacher explained Euclid's third axiom using a compass and a piece of paper.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Euclid's postulates - the set of five fundamental axioms of plane geometry proposed by Euclid
  • Degree Lower: Euclid's first axiom - things which are equal to the same thing are equal to one another
  • Degree Higher: Euclid's fifth axiom - the parallel postulate in Euclidean geometry

Etymology

Named after Euclid, the ancient Greek mathematician who formulated the fundamental axioms of geometry.
