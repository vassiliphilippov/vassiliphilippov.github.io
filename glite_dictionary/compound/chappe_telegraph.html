Chappe telegraph

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃæp təˈlɛɡɹæf / (AmE), / ˈʃap tɪˈliːɡrɑːf / (BrE)
A signalling device consisting of a tower with pivoting shutters used for sending visual messages over a distance, invented in the late 18th century by Claude Chappe.
early visual signalling device with pivoting shutters

Examples

The Chappe telegraph revolutionized long-distance communication in France during the late 18th century.
Many towers from the original Chappe telegraph network can still be found across the French countryside.
Before the electric telegraph, armies relied on the Chappe telegraph for rapid transmission of important messages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: optical telegraph - a general term for any visual signalling system using movable parts, often towers

Etymology

Named after Claude Chappe (1763–1805), the French inventor of the system.
