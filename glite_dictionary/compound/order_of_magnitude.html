order of magnitude

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔrdər əv ˈmæɡnɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈɔːdə əv ˈmæɡnɪˌtjuːd / (BrE)
a class in a system of classification determined by size, amount, or quantity, typically measured as powers of ten; two values are of the same order of magnitude if their ratio is less than ten.
difference in scale, typically by power of ten

Examples

The budget for the new project is an order of magnitude higher than last year’s.
Scientists found energy levels differing by several orders of magnitude.
The accuracy of this measurement is within one order of magnitude of the true value.

Etymology

From Latin 'ordo' (order) and 'magnitudo' (greatness, size).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɔrdɚ əv ˈmæɡnɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈɔːdə əv ˈmæɡnɪˌtjuːd / (BrE)
a level or scale of size or quantity, especially as measured by powers of ten; an approximate measure of how much greater or smaller something is compared to something else, often expressed as being ten times larger or smaller
level or scale of size or quantity

Examples

The energy released by a supernova is several orders of magnitude greater than that of our sun.
Our budget is on the same order of magnitude as last year's, so spending limits remain similar.
Switching to LED bulbs reduced our electricity usage by an order of magnitude.

Synset Examples

it was on the order of a mile
an explosion of a low order of magnitude

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scale - a graduated range of values forming a standard system for measuring or grading something

Etymology

From 'order' (Latin 'ordo', rank, row) and 'magnitude' (Latin 'magnitudo', greatness), used in mathematics and sciences to denote powers of ten or general scaling.
