cathode ray

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæθoʊd ˌreɪ / (AmE), / ˈkaθəʊd ˌreɪ / (BrE)
a beam of electrons emitted from the cathode in a vacuum tube or discharge tube, often used in early electronic devices such as television and oscilloscopes
beam of electrons from a cathode in a tube

Examples

The cathode ray was fundamental to the invention of early television screens.
Scientists studied the properties of the cathode ray to better understand the nature of electrons.
A cathode ray tube was used to display waveforms in old oscilloscopes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: anode ray - stream of positively charged particles in a discharge tube

Etymology

From 'cathode', coined in the 19th century from Greek 'kathodos' (way down), and 'ray' meaning a beam.
