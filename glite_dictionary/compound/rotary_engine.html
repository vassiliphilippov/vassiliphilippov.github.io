rotary engine

1. NOUN

/ ˈroʊˌtɛri ˈɛn.dʒɪn / (AmE), / ˈrəʊ.tər.i ˈɛn.dʒɪn / (BrE)
an internal-combustion engine in which the cylinders are arranged radially around a central crankcase, and either the cylinders rotate around a fixed crankshaft or the crankshaft rotates within stationary cylinders
internal-combustion engine with cylinders arranged radially

Examples

The aircraft was powered by a rotary engine, which was common during World War I.
Mazda is famous for developing a type of rotary engine known as the Wankel engine.
The museum displayed a restored early rotary engine used in vintage airplanes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: internal-combustion engine - engine powered by burning fuel within

2. NOUN

/ ˈroʊˌtɛri ˈɛn.dʒɪn / (AmE), / ˈrəʊ.tər.i ˈen.dʒɪn / (BrE)
an internal-combustion engine in which power is transmitted directly to rotating components, often used in some aircraft and vehicles
type of internal-combustion engine with rotating parts

Examples

The Mazda RX-7 was renowned for its unique rotary engine design.
Early airplanes often used a rotary engine for a lightweight power source.
Engineers studied the pros and cons of the rotary engine compared to traditional piston engines.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: piston engine - an engine in which pistons move up and down in cylinders
  • Synonym: Wankel engine - a specific type of rotary engine using an eccentric rotary mechanism

Etymology

From Latin 'rota' meaning 'wheel' and engine from Latin 'ingenium' (invention).
