classical haemophilia

1. NOUN

/ ˈklæsɪkəl hiːˌmoʊˈfɪliə / (AmE), / ˈklɑːsɪkəl hɪˌməʊˈfɪlɪə / (BrE)
A form of hemophilia caused by a congenital deficiency of clotting factor VIII, occurring almost exclusively in men.
hereditary bleeding disorder (factor VIII deficiency)
medical

Examples

Patients with classical haemophilia require regular infusions of clotting factor VIII to prevent bleeding episodes.
Genetic testing can confirm a diagnosis of classical haemophilia in families with a history of the disorder.
Because classical haemophilia is X-linked, it rarely affects women but is more common in men.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hemophilia - general term for inherited bleeding disorders impairing blood clotting

Etymology

From 'haemophilia', coined from Greek 'haima' (blood) + 'philia' (love, tendency); 'classical' denotes the original or most typical form.
