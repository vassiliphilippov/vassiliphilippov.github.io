daisy cutter

1. NOUN

/ ˈdeɪzi ˌkʌtər / (AmE), / ˈdeɪzi ˌkʌtə / (BrE)
A batted or served ball that travels very low, skimming just above the ground.
ball skimming close to the ground (in sports)

Examples

The batsman hit a daisy cutter that sped past the fielder.
Her serve turned into a daisy cutter, bouncing just once before hitting the fence.
Spectators gasped as the ball rolled like a daisy cutter across the outfield.

Etymology

Originally a military term for a type of bomb, later applied metaphorically in sports for a ball that 'cuts the daisies' by staying close to the ground.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdeɪzi ˌkʌtər / (AmE), / ˈdeɪzi ˌkʌtə / (BrE)
a bomb designed to break into many small, high-velocity fragments, most effective against troops and vehicles
type of bomb with fragmenting casing

Examples

The military deployed a daisy cutter to clear a landing zone in the dense forest.
A daisy cutter releases deadly fragments over a wide area, making it effective against enemy convoys.
Reporters described the devastating impact of the daisy cutter on the battlefield.

Etymology

Originally slang in the US military, referring to bombs that create a large blast and clear vegetation like a mowing 'daisy cutter'.
