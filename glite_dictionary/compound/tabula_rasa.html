tabula rasa

1. NOUN

/ ˌtæbjələ ˈrɑsə / (AmE), / ˌtabjʊlə ˈrɑːsə / (BrE)
the idea of the mind in its original state, unmarked by experience or learning; a blank slate, especially as a philosophical concept.
the mind as a blank slate; unmarked by experience

Examples

John Locke believed that each child is born as a tabula rasa, ready to be shaped by experience.
The concept of tabula rasa is often discussed in theories of human development.
She approached her new life as a tabula rasa, free from her past.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blank slate - a mind not yet influenced by experience; direct English synonym

Etymology

From Latin 'tabula rasa' (scraped tablet), used by John Locke to describe the mind at birth.

2. NOUN

/ ˌtæbjələ ˈrɑsə / (AmE), / ˌtabjʊlə ˈrɑːzə / (BrE)
the idea of a mind or situation in a state of complete blankness, free from preconceptions or existing content, providing a fresh start
a blank slate; fresh start

Examples

With each new year, she viewed her goals as a tabula rasa, ready to write a new story.
After the company merger, the organization treated its culture as a tabula rasa to avoid old conflicts.
The abandoned building was seen by artists as a tabula rasa for creative expression.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blank slate - a person or thing regarded as uninfluenced by prior knowledge or experience

Etymology

From Latin, meaning 'scraped tablet', originally referring to a wax tablet with the writing erased.
