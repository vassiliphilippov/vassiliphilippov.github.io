Fechner's law

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɛk.nɚz lɔ / (AmE), / ˈfɛk.nəz lɔː / (BrE)
the scientific principle that the strength of a subjective sensation increases in proportion to the logarithm of the stimulus intensity
law relating sensation magnitude to stimulus intensity

Examples

Fechner's law is fundamental to understanding how humans perceive differences in brightness and sound.
According to Fechner's law, each equal step in sensation corresponds to a multiplicative increase in stimulus.
Modern psychophysical experiments still reference Fechner's law when analyzing sensory thresholds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Weber's law - principle stating stimulus difference needed to detect a change grows with magnitude of initial stimulus

Etymology

Named after Gustav Theodor Fechner (1801–1887), German philosopher and physicist.
