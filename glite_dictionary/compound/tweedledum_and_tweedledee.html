Tweedledum and Tweedledee

1. NOUN

/ ˌtwiːdəlˈdʌm ənd ˌtwiːdəlˈdiː / (AmE), / ˌtwiːdldʌm ənd ˌtwiːdldəˈdiː / (BrE)
any two people who are very similar to each other or hard to distinguish
two people who are hard to tell apart

Examples

The two politicians are like Tweedledum and Tweedledee; it's hard to tell them apart.
Some critics argue that the rival products are just Tweedledum and Tweedledee.
Whenever they enter a room together, they're called Tweedledum and Tweedledee because of their striking similarity.

Etymology

First popularized by Lewis Carroll in 'Through the Looking-Glass' (1871), originally from a poem by John Byrom (1725).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtwiːdəlˈdʌm ənd ˌtwiːdəlˈdi / (AmE), / ˌtwiːdəlˈdʌm ənd ˌtwiːdəlˈdiː / (BrE)
two people who are very alike or indistinguishable, especially in their opinions, actions, or appearance
two very similar or indistinguishable people

Examples

The two candidates were so similar that voters saw them as Tweedledum and Tweedledee.
Whenever they disagreed, it was hard to tell who was Tweedledum and Tweedledee.
The managers' ideas were nearly identical—real Tweedledum and Tweedledee.

Synset Examples

Their conflicting views made them look like Tweedledum and Tweedledee.
The two friends argued like Tweedledum and Tweedledee.
In the debate, they were just Tweedledum and Tweedledee.

Etymology

From characters created by Lewis Carroll in 'Through the Looking-Glass', originally from a nursery rhyme by John Byrom.
