Fermi-Dirac statistics

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɜːrmi daɪˈræk stəˈtɪstɪks / (AmE), / ˌfɜːmi dɪˈræk stəˈtɪstɪks / (BrE)
(in physics) the statistical distribution describing the behavior of particles, such as fermions, which obey the Pauli exclusion principle and whose wave function changes sign when two particles are exchanged.
physics: statistics for particles obeying Pauli exclusion

Examples

Electrons in a metal are well described by Fermi-Dirac statistics at low temperatures.
The behavior of neutrons in a nucleus follows the rules of Fermi-Dirac statistics.
Unlike bosons, fermions are governed by Fermi-Dirac statistics and cannot occupy the same state.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Bose-Einstein statistics - statistical laws for particles not restricted by Pauli exclusion (bosons)
  • Synonym: Pauli exclusion principle - the quantum mechanical principle stating that no two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state

Etymology

Coined from the names of Italian physicist Enrico Fermi and English physicist Paul Dirac.
