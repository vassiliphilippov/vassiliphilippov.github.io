long-neck clam

1. NOUN

/ lɔŋ nɛk klæm / (AmE), / lɒŋ nɛk klæm / (BrE)
A type of clam with an elongated siphon, commonly steamed in its shell and eaten as seafood.
clam with a long siphon, eaten as seafood

Examples

We enjoyed a delicious plate of long-neck clams steamed with garlic and butter.
Many seafood lovers prefer long-neck clams for making chowder.
The restaurant serves fresh long-neck clams straight from the local bay.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steamer clam - another name for the long-neck clam
  • Synonym: soft-shell clam - a related species of clam with a thin shell and long siphon

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɔŋ nɛk klæm / (AmE), / ˈlɒŋ nɛk klam / (BrE)
an edible clam with a thin, oval-shaped shell, found in coastal areas of the United States and Europe
an edible clam species with an elongated neck

Examples

Fishermen gathered long-neck clams from the tidal flats early in the morning.
The chef prepared a delicious chowder using fresh long-neck clams.
Populations of long-neck clam have declined in some coastal regions due to overharvesting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soft-shell clam - an edible clam with a thin brittle shell, often called 'steamers' in the US
  • Antonym: quahog - a hard-shell edible clam
