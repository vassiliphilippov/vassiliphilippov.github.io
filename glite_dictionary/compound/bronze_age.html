Bronze Age

1. NOUN

/ brɑnz eɪdʒ / (AmE), / brɒnz eɪdʒ / (BrE)
the period in ancient history between the Stone Age and the Iron Age, characterized by the use of bronze tools and weapons
ancient era marked by use of bronze

Examples

Many ancient civilizations flourished during the Bronze Age.
Archaeologists discovered artifacts from the Bronze Age at the dig site.
The transition from the Stone Age to the Bronze Age marked significant technological advancement.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Iron Age - historical period following the Bronze Age, marked by use of iron
  • Degree Lower: Stone Age - prehistoric period before the Bronze Age, marked by stone tool use

Etymology

From 'bronze', referring to the copper and tin alloy, and 'age', meaning a historical period.

2. NOUN

/ brɑːnz eɪdʒ / (AmE), / brɒnz eɪdʒ / (BrE)
(In classical mythology) The third age of the world, marked by war, violence, and decline in morality, following the Golden and Silver Ages.
mythological third age marked by violence

Examples

According to Hesiod, humanity descended into the Bronze Age, a time defined by endless strife and warfare.
In the Bronze Age of myth, people were hard and strong but quick to anger.
Poets often reference the Bronze Age to illustrate an era when violence corrupted society.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Silver Age - the second age of the world in classical mythology, preceding the Bronze Age
  • Degree Higher: Iron Age - the fourth and last age of the world in classical mythology, marked by complete decline

Etymology

From Greek mythology—corresponds to Greek 'χάλκεον γένος' (khalkeon genos, bronze race/age); bronze as a symbol of strength and warlike qualities.
