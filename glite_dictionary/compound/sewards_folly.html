Seward's Folly

1. NOUN

/ ˈsuːərdz ˈfɑːli / (AmE), / ˈsjuːədz ˈfɒli / (BrE)
the 1867 purchase of Alaska from Russia by the United States, negotiated by Secretary of State William H. Seward, originally considered foolish by many Americans
the U.S. purchase of Alaska from Russia in 1867

Examples

Many Americans originally mocked the Alaska purchase as Seward's Folly, believing the land was worthless.
Historians now view Seward's Folly as a visionary decision that greatly benefited the United States.
In 1867, the U.S. expanded its territory through Seward's Folly, acquiring Alaska from Russia.

Etymology

Named after U.S. Secretary of State William H. Seward, with "folly" referring to the purchase being seen as a foolish act at the time.
