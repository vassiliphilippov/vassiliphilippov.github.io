structural linguistics

1. NOUN

/ ˌstrʌktʃərəl lɪŋˈɡwɪstɪks / (AmE), / ˌstrʌktʃərəl lɪŋˈɡwɪstɪks / (BrE)
an approach to linguistics that analyzes language as a system of interrelated formal structures, rather than focusing on meaning or historical development
study of language's formal structures
medical

Examples

Structural linguistics emerged in the early 20th century as a new way to analyze languages.
Many foundational theories in structural linguistics come from the work of Ferdinand de Saussure.
Her research applies methods from structural linguistics to understand syntactic patterns in English.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: generative linguistics - a theory of linguistics focusing on the innate structure of language in the mind
  • Synonym: descriptive linguistics - the study of language by describing its current structure

Etymology

From 'structural' (from Latin 'structura', meaning 'a fitting together, building') + 'linguistics' (from Latin 'lingua', 'tongue, language').
