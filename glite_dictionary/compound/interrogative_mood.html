interrogative mood

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.təˈrɑː.ɡə.tɪv muːd / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈrɒ.ɡə.tɪv muːd / (BrE)
the grammatical mood used for asking questions, typically signaled by question words or intonation
grammatical mood for asking questions

Examples

In English, the interrogative mood is often marked by the use of auxiliary verbs at the beginning of a question.
Learning about the interrogative mood helps students form questions more accurately in foreign languages.
The interrogative mood can also be indicated by a rising intonation in spoken speech.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: imperative mood - the grammatical mood expressing commands or requests
  • Antonym: declarative mood - the grammatical mood used for making statements

Etymology

From Latin 'interrogativus' (pertaining to asking questions) and Middle English 'mood' (grammatical form expressing attitude).
