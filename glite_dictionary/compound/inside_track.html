inside track

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.saɪd træk / (AmE), / ˈɪn.saɪd træk / (BrE)
the inner side of a curved racecourse, especially where the distance is shortest
inner side of a racecourse curve

Examples

She started the race in lane one, closest to the inside track.
The inside track often gives runners an advantage on curved sections of the course.
During the final lap, he surged ahead along the inside track to win the race.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌsaɪd træɪk / (AmE), / ˌɪn.saɪd ˈtræk / (BrE)
an advantage or favorable position in a competition or situation, giving someone a better chance of success than others
advantageous position in a competition

Examples

Her experience in the company gave her the inside track during the promotion process.
Investors with the inside track can anticipate market movements before others.
Because of his close friendship with the mayor, he had the inside track on the contract.

Synset Examples

the boss's son had the inside track for that job

Related Terms

  • Synonym: edge - an advantage over others
  • Antonym: disadvantage - an unfavorable or less advantageous position

Etymology

From horse racing, where the inner track covers a shorter distance, giving an advantage.

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌsaɪd ˈtræk / (AmE), / ˌɪn.saɪd ˈtræk / (BrE)
an advantage or privileged position, often because of special knowledge or access, giving a better chance of success than others
advantage or privileged position

Examples

Because her uncle works there, she has the inside track for the internship.
Anyone with the inside track will hear about the new opportunities before others do.
He hoped his connections would give him the inside track in the election.

Synset Examples

He secured the inside track for the promotion.
She has the inside track on the job openings.
They have the inside track on local politics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advantage - a condition or circumstance that puts one in a favorable or superior position
  • Synonym: edge - an advantage over others
  • Antonym: disadvantage - an unfavorable condition that reduces the chances of success

Etymology

Originally from horse racing, where the lane closest to the inside of the track offers a shorter distance; figurative use developed later.
