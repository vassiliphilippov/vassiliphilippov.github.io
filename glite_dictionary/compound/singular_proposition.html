singular proposition

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪŋgjələr ˌprɑːpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋgjʊlə ˌprɒpəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
In logic, a statement whose subject refers to a specific individual or object, rather than to a general class.
a statement about a specific individual (logic)

Examples

The statement 'Socrates is wise' is an example of a singular proposition in philosophy.
In contrast to general statements, a singular proposition directly refers to one particular entity.
Many debates in philosophy of language focus on how singular propositions relate to the objects they mention.

Synset Examples

A singular proposition is to be contrasted with a general proposition, which is not about any particular individual, and a particularized proposition, which is about a particular individual but does not contain that individual as a constituent.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: general proposition - a proposition that refers to a general class, not a specific individual
