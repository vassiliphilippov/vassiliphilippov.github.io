magnetic induction

1. NOUN

/ mæɡˈnɛtɪk ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / mæɡˈnɛtɪk ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a material becomes magnetized, either temporarily or permanently, by exposure to a magnetic field
process of magnetizing a substance

Examples

When a piece of iron is placed near a magnet, magnetic induction causes it to behave like a magnet itself.
The principle of magnetic induction is essential in the operation of electric generators and transformers.
Scientists studied magnetic induction to understand how certain materials become permanently magnetized.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magnetization - the process of making something magnetic

Etymology

From 'magnetic' (relating to magnetism) and 'induction' (from Latin inductio, 'leading in, introduction').

2. NOUN

/ mæɡˈnɛt̬ɪk ɪnˈdʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / mæɡˈnɛtɪk ɪnˈdʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
the process or state in which a material or space develops a magnetic field, especially measured as the amount of magnetic flux passing through a unit area perpendicular to the magnetic field direction
the amount of magnetic flux per unit area
medical

Examples

The strength of the magnetic induction is measured in teslas in the International System of Units.
A change in magnetic induction can induce an electric current in a nearby conductor.
Physicists calculate magnetic induction to understand how materials respond to external fields.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magnetic flux density - the measure of the strength of a magnetic field per unit area
  • Degree Lower: magnetic susceptibility - the degree to which a material can be magnetized in an external magnetic field

Etymology

From 'magnetic' (from Greek 'magnetis') and 'induction' (from Latin 'inductio', meaning 'a leading in').
