Wood's alloy

1. NOUN

/ wʊdz ˈælɔɪ / (AmE), / wʊdz ˈælɔɪ / (BrE)
a fusible alloy consisting chiefly of bismuth, lead, tin, and cadmium, which melts at a low temperature (about 160°F or 71°C)
a low-melting-point alloy of bismuth, lead, tin, and cadmium

Examples

The technician used Wood's alloy to create a temporary safety fuse in the circuit.
Because Wood's alloy melts at a low temperature, it's ideal for making molds in delicate procedures.
Some old fire sprinkler systems relied on Wood's alloy to activate when exposed to heat.

Etymology

Named after Barnabas Wood (1819–1875), the American dentist who invented it.
