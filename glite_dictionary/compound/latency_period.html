latency period

1. NOUN

/ ˈleɪtənsi ˌpɪriəd / (AmE), / ˈleɪtənsi ˌpɪəriəd / (BrE)
(in psychoanalysis) the stage of childhood, from about age 5 or 6 until puberty, during which sexual impulses are repressed and redirected into socially acceptable activities.
stage in childhood development (psychoanalysis)
medical

Examples

During the latency period, children's energies are often directed toward school and friendships.
Freud believed that the latency period was crucial for developing social and intellectual skills.
Some psychologists now question whether the latency period is as universal as Freud once suggested.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: phallic stage - the third stage in Freud's theory (before latency period)
  • Degree Higher: genital stage - the stage following the latency period in psychoanalytic theory

Etymology

From Latin 'latens' (lying hidden), referring to hidden or suppressed impulses; 'period' from Greek 'periodos' (cycle, circuit).
