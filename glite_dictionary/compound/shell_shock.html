shell shock

1. NOUN

/ ʃɛl ʃɑk / (AmE), / ʃɛl ʃɒk / (BrE)
a psychological disturbance caused by prolonged exposure to active warfare, especially bombardment; historically associated with World War I soldiers
war-related psychological disorder

Examples

Many soldiers returned from the front lines suffering from shell shock, unable to cope with everyday life.
The effects of shell shock were misunderstood and often dismissed during the early twentieth century.
He was diagnosed with shell shock after enduring months of intense combat during the war.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: post-traumatic stress disorder - a condition of persistent mental and emotional stress as a result of injury or severe psychological shock

Etymology

Formed from 'shell,' referring to explosive artillery shells, and 'shock,' meaning traumatic impact; first used during World War I.

2. NOUN

/ ʃɛl ʃɑk / (AmE), / ʃɛl ʃɒk / (BrE)
a psychological condition caused by prolonged exposure to active warfare, especially bombardment, resulting in severe stress, anxiety, and emotional disturbance
psychological trauma from warfare
archaic medical

Examples

Many soldiers returning from the front suffered from shell shock and found it difficult to adjust to normal life.
The lasting impact of shell shock during World War I changed the way mental health was perceived in the military.
Her grandfather was diagnosed with shell shock after months of relentless bombing on the battlefield.

Synset Examples

Many veterans suffered from shell shock after the war.
The film depicted the horrors of shell shock.
He experienced shell shock, struggling with memories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: post-traumatic stress disorder - a mental health condition triggered by a terrifying event

Etymology

First used during World War I to describe the psychological effects of bombardment on soldiers.
