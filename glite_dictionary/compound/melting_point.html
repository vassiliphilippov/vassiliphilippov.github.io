melting point

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɛltɪŋ pɔɪnt / (AmE), / ˈmɛltɪŋ pɔɪnt / (BrE)
the temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid; the fixed temperature at which a substance melts
temperature at which a solid melts

Examples

The melting point of ice is 0 degrees Celsius.
Gold has a much higher melting point than aluminum.
At the melting point, a solid changes to a liquid.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: boiling point - temperature at which a liquid turns to vapor
  • Synonym: freezing point - temperature at which a liquid becomes solid

Etymology

From 'melt' (to become liquid by heat) + 'point', first attested in the 18th century in scientific contexts.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɛltɪŋ ˌpɔɪnt / (AmE), / ˈmɛltɪŋ ˌpɔɪnt / (BrE)
The temperature at which a solid substance changes to a liquid; also, a critical moment when significant change occurs.
temperature where solid becomes liquid

Examples

The melting point of iron is much higher than that of aluminum.
Scientists recorded the melting point of the new compound during the experiment.
Negotiations reached their melting point before a sudden breakthrough resolved the dispute.

Synset Examples

Frit is essential for the melting point in glassmaking.
The melting point of ice is when it turns to water.
Water's melting point is zero degrees Celsius.
Understanding is the melting point of conflict resolution.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: freezing point - temperature at which a liquid turns into a solid

Etymology

From 'melt' (Old English 'meltan', to liquefy) + 'point' (from Latin 'punctum', a mark or position).
