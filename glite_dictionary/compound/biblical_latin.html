Biblical Latin

1. NOUN

/ ˌbɪblɪkəl ˈlætɪn / (AmE), / ˌbɪblɪkəl ˈlatɪn / (BrE)
the form of Latin used in Christian writings, especially translations of the Bible, from the 3rd to the 8th centuries
form of Latin used in early Christian texts

Examples

The Vulgate is one of the most influential texts composed in Biblical Latin.
Scholars study Biblical Latin to better understand early Christian theology.
Characteristics of Biblical Latin often differ from those found in Classical Latin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Ecclesiastical Latin - the form of Latin used in the Roman Catholic Church

Etymology

From Latin ‘Biblia’ (Bible) and ‘Latinus’ (Latin).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɪb.lɪ.kəl ˈlæt.ɪn / (AmE), / ˈbɪb.lɪ.kəl ˈlæ.tɪn / (BrE)
the form of Latin used in translations and writings of the Bible, particularly between the 3rd and 8th centuries
form of Latin used in biblical texts (3rd–8th c.)

Examples

Many early Christian texts were translated into Biblical Latin for use by the Western church.
Scholars study Biblical Latin to better understand theological interpretations of scripture.
The style of Biblical Latin differs significantly from that of Classical Latin literature.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Classical Latin - the standardized form of Latin used in formal Roman literature
  • Degree Lower: Vulgar Latin - the informal spoken form of Latin used by the common people

Etymology

Biblical from Bible + Latin, referring to the era and context in which this form developed.
