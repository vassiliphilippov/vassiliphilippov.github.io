mortise joint

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɔrtəs dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪs dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
A strong joint made by inserting a projecting tenon on one piece into a corresponding mortise hole in another piece, commonly used in woodworking and carpentry.
joint in woodworking using tenon and mortise

Examples

The craftsman used a mortise joint to connect the table leg securely to the frame.
Many traditional doors are constructed with mortise joints for extra durability.
Learning how to make a mortise joint is an essential skill in woodworking.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dovetail joint - a joint in woodworking where wedge-shaped tenons fit into matching mortises

Etymology

From Middle English 'morteys', of Old French origin; related to Old French 'morteise'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɔrtɪs dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪs dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
a gliding joint formed between the distal ends of the tibia and fibula and the proximal end of the talus bone in the human ankle
ankle joint formed by tibia, fibula, and talus
medical

Examples

The surgeon examined the mortise joint to assess the extent of the ankle injury.
Arthritis in the mortise joint can cause significant pain and limited mobility.
A clear X-ray of the mortise joint helps diagnose fractures or dislocations in the ankle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ankle joint - the joint connecting the foot with the leg

Etymology

From 'mortise,' a carpentry term for a hole or recess, by analogy with the fitting of bones.
