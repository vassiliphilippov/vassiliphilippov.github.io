Maginot Line

1. NOUN

/ ˈmædʒɪˌnoʊ laɪn / (AmE), / ˈmaʒɪnəʊ laɪn / (BrE)
A line of defensive fortifications built by France along its eastern border before World War II, intended to deter invasion but bypassed and overrun by Germany in 1940. Also used figuratively to describe a defensive barrier or strategy that provides a false sense of security.
French line of WWII fortifications; false sense of security

Examples

The failure of the Maginot Line became a symbol of outdated military thinking.
Many see the firewall as a modern Maginot Line against cyber attacks—impressive but ultimately penetrable.
France invested heavily in the Maginot Line, but it did not prevent the German invasion.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Siegfried Line - German defensive fortifications facing the Maginot Line during WWII

Etymology

Named after André Maginot (1877–1932), French Minister of War, who championed its construction.
