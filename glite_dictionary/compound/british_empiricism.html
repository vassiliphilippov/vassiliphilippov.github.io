British empiricism

1. NOUN

/ ˈbrɪtɪʃ ɪmˈpɪrɪˌsɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈbrɪtɪʃ ɪmˈpɪərɪsɪzəm / (BrE)
the philosophical tradition that emphasizes knowledge derived from sensory experience, predominant in Great Britain since the 17th century
philosophical tradition emphasizing sensory experience

Examples

Many scholars credit British empiricism with shaping modern scientific thought.
John Locke, George Berkeley, and David Hume are key figures in British empiricism.
The emphasis on observation and experience is central to the philosophy of British empiricism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: empiricism - the theory that knowledge comes from sensory experience

Etymology

Empiricism from Greek 'empeiria' (experience), with reference to its major British proponents.
