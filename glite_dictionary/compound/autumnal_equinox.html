autumnal equinox

1. NOUN

/ əˈtʌmnəl ˈikwəˌnɑks / (AmE), / ɔːˈtʌmnəl ˈiːkwɪnɒks / (BrE)
the time or point at which the sun crosses the celestial equator going southward, usually occurring around September 22 or 23, marking the beginning of autumn in the northern hemisphere
astronomical event marking start of autumn

Examples

The autumnal equinox occurs each year in late September, bringing nearly equal hours of daylight and darkness.
Many cultures celebrate special festivals during the autumnal equinox to mark the change of season.
Astronomers use the autumnal equinox as a reference point for celestial coordinates.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: vernal equinox - the point or time at which the sun crosses the celestial equator going northward, marking the start of spring

Etymology

From Latin 'autumnalis' (of autumn) and 'aequinoctium' (equal night).

2. NOUN

/ əˈtʌmnəl ˈekwɪˌnɑks / (AmE), / ɔːˈtʌmnəl ˈiːkwɪnɒks / (BrE)
the time at which the sun crosses the celestial equator, resulting in day and night of approximately equal length, occurring around September 22 in the northern hemisphere
day when autumn begins; day and night are equal

Examples

The autumnal equinox marks the official start of fall in the northern hemisphere.
Many cultures celebrate festivals around the time of the autumnal equinox.
On the autumnal equinox, day and night are nearly the same length.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: vernal equinox - the equinox marking the beginning of spring, around March 21

Etymology

From Latin 'autumnalis' (of autumn) + 'equinoxium' (equality of night and day).
