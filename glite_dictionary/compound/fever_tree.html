fever tree

1. NOUN

/ ˈfiːvər triː / (AmE), / ˈfiːvə triː / (BrE)
an ornamental shrub or small tree found in swampy areas of the southwestern United States, known for its large pink or white sepals and for producing Georgia bark used as a treatment for fever
ornamental tree whose bark treats fever

Examples

The fever tree is famed for the medicinal properties of its bark.
During our hike, we stumbled upon a grove of blooming fever trees near the riverbank.
Traditional remedies sometimes use extracts from the fever tree to treat certain illnesses.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfiːvər triː / (AmE), / ˈfiːvə triː / (BrE)
a tall, fast-growing tree whose leaves contain a medicinal oil, especially noted for its bluish young leaves; often grown for timber or for its use in traditional medicine
tree with medicinal leaves; bluish young foliage

Examples

The plantation included hundreds of fever trees cultivated for their valuable medicinal oils.
Local healers harvested the leaves of the fever tree to prepare herbal remedies.
Young fever trees are recognizable by their unique bluish foliage along the riverbanks.

Etymology

So called because some species were believed to reduce fever or grow near malaria-prone wetlands.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfiːvər triː / (AmE), / ˈfiːvə triː / (BrE)
any of several trees whose leaves or bark are used to reduce fever, or that are believed to indicate areas free from fever; commonly refers to species such as the cinchona or Vachellia xanthophloea.
tree with bark or leaves used to reduce fever

Examples

The bark of the fever tree was once widely used as a remedy for malaria.
During their safari, they camped beneath a tall fever tree by the riverbank.
Some believed that the presence of a fever tree signaled healthy, malaria-free ground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cinchona - a tree whose bark yields quinine, used to treat malaria

Etymology

From the belief that some of these trees' bark or presence was associated with the absence or cure of fever (such as malaria).

4. NOUN

/ ˈfiːvər triː / (AmE), / ˈfiːvə triː / (BrE)
an African tree, especially various species of Acacia, so named because its presence was once believed to indicate areas free from malaria
African tree believed to indicate healthful regions

Examples

The fever tree grows near rivers and is easily identified by its pale bark.
Explorers once believed that the presence of a fever tree meant the area was safe from malaria.
Several species of Acacia are commonly called fever tree due to local beliefs about their health benefits.

Etymology

Named because its presence was thought to indicate areas free of fever (malaria).
