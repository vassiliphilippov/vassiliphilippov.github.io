joint-stock company

1. NOUN

/ dʒɔɪnt ˈstɑk ˌkʌmpəni / (AmE), / dʒɔɪnt ˈstɒk ˌkʌmpəni / (BrE)
A business entity whose capital is owned jointly by shareholders, who each own transferable shares and are liable for the company's debts.
business with jointly owned transferable shares

Examples

The East India Company was a famous joint-stock company chartered by the British crown.
In a joint-stock company, profits are distributed among the shareholders according to the number of shares they own.
Before the invention of modern corporations, the joint-stock company was a common business structure for large ventures.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: corporation - a company recognized by law as a single body; owners have limited liability
  • Degree Lower: partnership - a business organization owned by two or more people, without share capital

Etymology

From 'joint', meaning shared, and 'stock company', meaning an entity with share capital.
