Jehovah's Witnesses

1. NOUN

/ dʒɪˈhoʊvə(z) ˈwɪtnəsɪz / (AmE), / dʒɪˈhəʊvə(z) ˈwɪtnəsɪz / (BrE)
A Protestant Christian denomination, founded in the United States in 1884 by Charles Taze Russell, known for distinct beliefs and active evangelism.
Protestant Christian denomination; evangelistic group

Examples

My neighbors are Jehovah's Witnesses and occasionally share religious pamphlets with the community.
The beliefs of Jehovah's Witnesses differ from those of many other Christian denominations.
Jehovah's Witnesses are known for refusing blood transfusions on religious grounds.

Etymology

Named after 'Jehovah' (a form of the Hebrew name for God) and 'witness' (one who testifies), adopted officially in 1931.
