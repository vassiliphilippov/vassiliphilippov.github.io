roman type

1. NOUN

/ ˈroʊ.mən taɪp / (AmE), / ˈrəʊ.mən taɪp / (BrE)
a style of upright typeface originally modeled on the inscriptional lettering used in ancient Rome, now widely used in modern printing, distinct from italic or blackletter styles.
upright typeface style used in printing

Examples

In most books, the main body of text is set in roman type for clarity.
The designer chose roman type to evoke a sense of tradition and authority.
Unlike italic or bold fonts, roman type remains upright and easy to read.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: italic type - a style of typeface in which the letters slant to the right
  • Antonym: blackletter - a style of script or typeface used in Western Europe during the Middle Ages

Etymology

From Latin 'Romanus' (Roman), indicating the style's origin in ancient Roman inscriptions.
