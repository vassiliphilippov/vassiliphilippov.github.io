Hubble constant

1. NOUN

/ ˈhʌbəl ˈkɑnstənt / (AmE), / ˈhʌbəl ˈkɒnstənt / (BrE)
a value that expresses the current rate at which the universe is expanding, calculated as the ratio of the speed of recession of a galaxy to its distance from the observer
rate of expansion of the universe

Examples

The value of the Hubble constant is crucial for estimating the age of the universe.
Recent studies have led to debates over the exact value of the Hubble constant.
Astronomers use the Hubble constant to determine how quickly the universe is expanding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Hubble's law - the law stating that the speed at which a galaxy recedes is proportional to its distance

Etymology

Named after Edwin Hubble (1889–1953), American astronomer.
