vinifera grape

1. NOUN

/ vɪˈnɪfərə ɡreɪp / (AmE), / vɪˈnɪfərə ɡreɪp / (BrE)
A common European grape species (Vitis vinifera) cultivated in many varieties, the chief source of Old World wine and table grapes.
common European grape for wine and table use

Examples

The region’s red wines are made almost exclusively from the vinifera grape.
Many Old World vineyards cultivate the vinifera grape for both wine and eating grapes.
A true champagne must be produced only from approved varieties of the vinifera grape.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Vitis vinifera - the botanical name for the vinifera grape species
  • Antonym: concord grape - a variety of grape native to North America, different species

Etymology

From Latin vinifera, meaning 'wine-producing', from vinum (wine) + ferre (to bear).

2. NOUN

/ vəˈnɪfərə ɡreɪp / (AmE), / vɪˈnɪfərə ɡreɪp / (BrE)
A grape from a cultivated variety of the common European grapevine (Vitis vinifera), widely grown for wine and eating.
common cultivated European grape

Examples

Most fine wines are made from vinifera grapes grown in traditional vineyards.
The vinifera grape thrives in temperate climates with dry summers and mild winters.
Experts recommend vinifera grapes for their superior flavor and suitability for winemaking.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Vitis vinifera - the species of grapevine native to the Mediterranean region and Central Europe
  • Degree Lower: table grape - a grape variety grown for eating rather than making wine
  • Degree Higher: wine grape - a grape variety primarily grown for producing wine

Etymology

From Latin vinifera (wine-bearing), from 'vinum' (wine) + 'ferre' (to bear).
