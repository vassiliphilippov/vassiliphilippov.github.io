Great Schism

1. NOUN

/ ɡreɪt ˈskɪzəm / (AmE), / ɡreɪt ˈsɪzəm / (BrE)
the period from 1378 to 1417 when there were rival papal claimants in Rome and Avignon, causing division in the Roman Catholic Church
division in the Roman Catholic Church, 1378–1417

Examples

The Great Schism created lasting divisions within the Roman Catholic Church.
Historians often debate the causes and impact of the Great Schism.
During the Great Schism, rival popes claimed authority from Rome and Avignon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Western Schism - another name for the Great Schism (1378–1417)

Etymology

From Greek 'schisma' (division, split), used for major splits in the Church.
