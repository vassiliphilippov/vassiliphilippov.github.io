prime meridian

1. NOUN

/ ˌpraɪm məˈrɪdiən / (AmE), / ˌpraɪm məˈrɪdiən / (BrE)
the meridian at Greenwich, established as the international zero of longitude from which east and west are measured
the earth’s zero longitude line

Examples

The prime meridian runs through Greenwich, England, marking zero degrees longitude.
Cartographers use the prime meridian as a reference point to measure east and west.
Before the prime meridian was agreed upon, different countries used their own reference lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equator - the imaginary line dividing the earth into northern and southern hemispheres

Etymology

Prime from Latin primus (first) + meridian from Latin meridianus (midday).

2. NOUN

/ praɪm məˈrɪdiən / (AmE), / praɪm məˈrɪdiən / (BrE)
the meridian at zero degrees longitude, from which east and west distances are measured on the globe, usually passing through Greenwich, England
zero degrees longitude; Greenwich meridian

Examples

The prime meridian passes through Greenwich, England, and serves as the reference point for longitude.
Maps display all locations in relation to the prime meridian and the equator.
The time in various countries is often calculated based on their distance from the prime meridian.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Greenwich Mean Time - the mean solar time at the Royal Observatory in Greenwich

Etymology

From Latin 'primus' (first) and medieval Latin 'meridianus' (of midday, relating to meridians)
