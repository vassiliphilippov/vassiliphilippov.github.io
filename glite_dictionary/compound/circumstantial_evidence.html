circumstantial evidence

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɝːkəmˈstænʃəl ˈɛvədəns / (AmE), / ˌsɜːkəmˈstænʃəl ˈɛvɪdəns / (BrE)
Evidence that does not directly prove a fact but allows for inference about the fact in dispute based on surrounding circumstances.
evidence based on inference, not direct proof
legal

Examples

The prosecution relied heavily on circumstantial evidence to argue the defendant's guilt.
There was no direct proof, but the circumstantial evidence was enough to convince the jury.
Although circumstantial evidence can be powerful, it is often viewed with caution in court.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: direct evidence - evidence that directly proves a fact without inference

Etymology

From Latin circumstantia (surrounding condition) + evidence.
