Received Pronunciation

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈsiːvd prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsiːvd prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃn / (BrE)
the standard form of British English pronunciation, traditionally considered the prestige accent and widely used in British broadcasting and education
standard British English accent
british

Examples

The BBC once required all its announcers to speak in Received Pronunciation.
She learned to imitate Received Pronunciation for her role as a television news anchor.
Many dictionaries use Received Pronunciation as the basis for their pronunciation guides.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: King's English - the English language as spoken with standard pronunciation and grammar, associated with educated speakers
  • Degree Lower: Estuary English - an accent of English spoken in the southeast of England, combining features of Received Pronunciation and regional accents
  • Antonym: regional accent - any accent associated with a particular geographic area, differing from standard pronunciation

Etymology

From 'received' (meaning accepted as standard) and 'pronunciation'; first widely used in the 19th century to refer to the accent of educated speakers.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈsivd prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsiːvd prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the standard accent of British English, traditionally considered the most prestigious; formerly the customary accent in British broadcasting and among the educated elite
standard British English accent
british

Examples

Many English language learners strive to master Received Pronunciation to sound more like native speakers.
The newsreader's clear Received Pronunciation made the report easy to understand.
Although regional accents are now more accepted, Received Pronunciation was once standard on the BBC.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: BBC English - a term for Received Pronunciation, as used by announcers on the BBC
  • Degree Lower: estuary English - an accent of English in southeast England, considered a degree lower in prestige than RP
  • Degree Lower: Cockney - a dialect and accent of English from East London, considered a degree lower in prestige than RP

Etymology

Term first used in the late 19th century, from 'received' meaning 'generally accepted as correct'.
