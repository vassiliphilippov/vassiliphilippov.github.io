wireless telegraphy

1. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪrləs təˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / ˈwaɪəlɪs tɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
the transmission of telegraphic messages without wires, typically by radio signals such as Morse code
sending telegrams by radio signals

Examples

At the start of the 20th century, wireless telegraphy revolutionized long-distance communication across oceans.
Marconi's experiments in wireless telegraphy marked a turning point in technological history.
In the era before telephones, ships relied on wireless telegraphy to send messages to shore.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: radiotelegraphy - sending telegraph messages via radio waves

Etymology

wireless, meaning 'without wires,' combined with telegraphy, from French télégraphie, originally from Greek tele- 'far' + -graph 'writing'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪrləs təˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / ˈwaɪəlɪs tɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
the sending of telegraphic messages by radio waves instead of wires
telegraphy using radio waves, not wires

Examples

The development of wireless telegraphy revolutionized long-distance communication in the early twentieth century.
Marconi was a pioneer in the field of wireless telegraphy and helped lay the foundations for modern radio.
Ships at sea relied on wireless telegraphy to send distress signals to nearby vessels and the mainland.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: radio telegraphy - telegraphy using radio transmission instead of wires

Etymology

Formed from 'wireless' and 'telegraphy'; 'telegraphy' from Greek 'tele-' (far) + 'grapho' (write).
