Punic War

1. NOUN

/ ˈpjuːnɪk wɔr / (AmE), / ˈpjuːnɪk wɔː / (BrE)
any of the three wars fought between Carthage and Rome (264–241 BC, 218–201 BC, 149–146 BC), resulting in the destruction of Carthage and its annexation by Rome.
wars between Carthage and Rome in ancient times

Examples

The First Punic War broke out over control of Sicily.
Historians still debate the causes of the Punic War series.
Carthage was destroyed at the end of the Third Punic War.

Etymology

From Latin 'Punicus' (relating to Carthage, or the Phoenicians), referring to the people of Carthage.

2. PROPN

/ ˈpjunɪk wɔr / (AmE), / ˈpjuːnɪk wɔː / (BrE)
any of the three wars fought between Rome and Carthage between 264 and 146 BC, resulting in the destruction of Carthage and Roman dominance over the western Mediterranean.
wars between Rome and Carthage (264–146 BC)

Examples

He wrote his thesis on the economic consequences of the Punic War for Carthage.
Many students find the Punic War a fascinating topic in ancient history.
The rivalry between Rome and Carthage during the Punic War shaped the future of the Mediterranean region.

Synset Examples

The historian studied the Punic wars extensively.

Etymology

From Latin 'Punicus' (Carthaginian), referring to the people of Carthage.
