French leave

1. NOUN

/ frɛntʃ liv / (AmE), / frɛntʃ liːv / (BrE)
An abrupt and unannounced departure, especially one made without saying farewell or seeking permission.
abrupt, unannounced departure

Examples

He took French leave and slipped out of the party without anyone noticing.
The manager was annoyed to discover that two employees had gone on French leave during the busiest hour.
Rather than cause a scene, she decided on French leave and quietly exited the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leave - the act of going away or departing

Etymology

From the practice, historically attributed (sometimes pejoratively) to the French, of leaving a social event without saying goodbye.

2. NOUN

/ frɛntʃ liːv / (AmE), / frɛntʃ liːv / (BrE)
the act of leaving or departing without permission or notice, especially from work or duty
leaving without permission or notice

Examples

After the meeting started, Tom took French leave and slipped out quietly.
She was reprimanded for taking French leave without informing her supervisor.
Whenever work gets overwhelming, he is tempted to take French leave.

Synset Examples

He often took French leave to avoid long meetings.
She took French leave and went on a spontaneous trip.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to abscond - to leave hurriedly and secretly, typically to avoid detection
  • Antonym: to resign - to formally leave a position or job

Etymology

From the 18th-century English phrase, originally referring to the French custom (as perceived by the English) of leaving a social gathering without saying goodbye.
