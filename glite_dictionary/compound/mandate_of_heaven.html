Mandate of Heaven

1. NOUN

/ ˈmænˌdeɪt əv ˈhɛvən / (AmE), / ˈmæn.deɪt əv ˈhɛv.ən / (BrE)
the traditional Chinese doctrine that heaven grants emperors the right to rule based on their virtue and ability to govern justly; loss of virtue is believed to result in loss of this right
Chinese doctrine of divine right to rule

Examples

Many dynastic transitions in China were justified through the Mandate of Heaven.
Rebellions were often seen as a sign that the ruler had lost the Mandate of Heaven.
The concept of the Mandate of Heaven was central to the legitimacy of ancient Chinese emperors.

Synset Examples

According to tradition, the Mandate of Heaven justified his rule.
They claimed the uprising was against the Mandate of Heaven.
The emperor believed he ruled by the Mandate of Heaven.

Etymology

Mandate from Latin "mandatum" (command), combined with the English phrase "of Heaven"; translation of Chinese "Tianming" (天命).
