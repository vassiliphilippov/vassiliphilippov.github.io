hour angle

1. NOUN

/ ˈaʊɚ ˌæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈaʊə ˌæŋɡəl / (BrE)
the angular distance, measured along the celestial equator, from the observer's meridian to the hour circle of a celestial body
angular distance from meridian to celestial body

Examples

Astronomers calculate the hour angle of a star to determine when it will be visible.
The hour angle increases as a celestial object moves westward from the observer's meridian.
To find the local sidereal time, you can add the right ascension and hour angle of a star.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: right ascension - celestial coordinate measuring an object's position eastward along the celestial equator

2. NOUN

/ ˈaʊɚ ˌæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈaʊə ˌæŋɡəl / (BrE)
(Astronomy) The angular distance, measured westward along the celestial equator from the observer's meridian, to a celestial object; used to determine its position in the sky relative to the local time.
angular measurement of a celestial object from the meridian

Examples

Astronomers use the hour angle to determine when a star will cross the local meridian.
At midnight, the hour angle of a star with zero right ascension is zero degrees.
Knowing the hour angle allows you to calculate the position of the sun at any given time.

Etymology

Formed from "hour" and "angle," referring to the time-based angular measurement in astronomy.
