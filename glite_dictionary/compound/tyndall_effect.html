Tyndall effect

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɪndəl əˌfɛkt / (AmE), / ˈtɪndəl ɪˌfɛkt / (BrE)
the phenomenon in which light is scattered by very small particles in its path, making a beam of light visible; the scattered light is often blue
light scattering by small particles

Examples

The Tyndall effect can be observed when sunlight streams through the forest, revealing dust and water droplets in the air.
In science class, we demonstrated the Tyndall effect using a flashlight and a glass of milk.
The sky appears blue due to the Tyndall effect caused by the scattering of sunlight by atmospheric particles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scattering - the process by which particles or waves are deflected or diffused

Etymology

Named after John Tyndall (1820–1893), Irish physicist who first studied the effect.
