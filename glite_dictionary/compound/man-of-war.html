man-of-war

1. NOUN

/ ˌmæn əv ˈwɔr / (AmE), / ˌmæn əv ˈwɔː / (BrE)
a powerful warship, especially one of the type used from the 16th to the 19th centuries, equipped for combat
warship (historical)

Examples

The British navy launched a man-of-war to patrol the coastal waters.
During the 18th century, a man-of-war was the pride of any major fleet.
The museum displays a detailed model of a famous man-of-war.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warship - a ship equipped and armed for fighting

Etymology

From the phrase 'man of war', originally denoting a military man or combatant, later applied to warships in the 16th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmæn əv ˈwɔr / (AmE), / ˌmæn əv ˈwɔː / (BrE)
a large floating marine animal (Portuguese man-of-war) with a bladder-like float and long stinging tentacles, found in warm seas
stinging marine animal with a gas float

Examples

Swimmers are warned to avoid the man-of-war washed up on the shore.
The sting of a man-of-war can be extremely painful and dangerous.
A group of man-of-war drifted past the boat, their tentacles trailing through the water.

Etymology

From the resemblance of its float to the sails of a warship called 'man-of-war'.
