Seville orange

1. NOUN

/ səˈvɪl ˈɔːrɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪˈvɪl ˈɒrɪndʒ / (BrE)
a variety of bitter orange with sour or bitter fruit, often used in marmalade or as grafting stock
bitter orange used in marmalade

Examples

Traditional British marmalade is made from Seville oranges harvested in Spain.
The chef requested fresh Seville oranges for the tart to achieve the right balance of sour and sweet flavors.
Gardeners often use the hardy Seville orange tree as rootstock for other citrus varieties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bitter orange - a type of orange with bitter, inedible fruit; used for preserves and flavoring

Etymology

Named after Seville, the Spanish city where the fruit is widely cultivated.

2. NOUN

/ səˈvɪl ˈɔrɪndʒ / (AmE), / səˈvɪl ˈɒrɪndʒ / (BrE)
a variety of orange with a highly acidic, bitter flavor, used especially in making marmalade
bitter, acidic orange used in marmalade

Examples

The recipe calls for six Seville oranges to give the marmalade its characteristic tang.
Every January, markets are filled with Seville oranges perfect for homemade preserves.
Unlike sweet varieties, Seville orange juice is too sour for drinking but ideal for cooking.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bitter orange - a citrus fruit used mainly for its sour juice and rind

Etymology

Named after Seville, a city in southern Spain, where the fruit was historically cultivated and exported.
