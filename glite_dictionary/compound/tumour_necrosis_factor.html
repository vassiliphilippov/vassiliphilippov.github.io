tumour necrosis factor

1. NOUN

/ ˈtuːmɚ nəˈkroʊsɪs ˈfæktɚ / (AmE), / ˈtjuːmər nɪˈkrəʊsɪs ˈfæktə / (BrE)
a protein produced by certain white blood cells that promotes inflammation and can induce the death of tumor cells, but may also contribute to inflammatory diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis.
a cytokine involved in inflammation and cell death
medical

Examples

Tumour necrosis factor plays a key role in causing inflammation in autoimmune diseases.
Some drugs used to treat arthritis specifically block tumour necrosis factor.
Elevated levels of tumour necrosis factor can indicate ongoing severe inflammation in the body.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cytokine - a broad category of small proteins important in cell signaling

Etymology

From 'tumour', Latin 'tumor' (swelling), 'necrosis', from Greek 'nekrosis' (act of killing), and 'factor', from Latin 'facere' (to make).
