New Realism

1. NOUN

/ nu ˈrɪəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / njuː ˈrɪəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
A philosophical movement developed in the early 20th century that asserts the external world exists independently of perception and can be known directly, in contrast to idealism.
20th-century philosophical movement; world exists independently of mind

Examples

A central principle of New Realism is the rejection of the idea that objects depend on the mind for their existence.
The debate between New Realism and Idealism shaped much of early 20th-century philosophy.
In his lectures, the professor explained how New Realism opposed older forms of epistemological dualism.

Synset Examples

central feature of the new realism was a rejection of the epistemological dualism of John Locke and of older forms of realism.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealism - philosophical belief that reality is fundamentally mental or immaterial
  • Degree Lower: realism - philosophical stance that objects exist independently of perception

Etymology

Formed from 'new' and 'realism' to designate this early 20th-century philosophical school.
