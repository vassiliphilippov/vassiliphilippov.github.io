front-porch campaign

1. NOUN

/ frʌnt ˈpɔrtʃ kəmˈpeɪn / (AmE), / frʌnt ˈpɔːtʃ kəmˈpeɪn / (BrE)
a political campaign in which the candidate makes speeches and meets supporters at or near their home rather than traveling extensively
political campaign conducted from candidate's home

Examples

William McKinley's front-porch campaign is considered one of the most iconic in American political history.
Unlike modern active tours, her front-porch campaign brought voters to her home for direct conversation.
Critics argued that a front-porch campaign would not reach enough undecided voters in a national race.

Synset Examples

William McKinley's dignified front-porch campaign won him the presidency in 1896
her approach was the opposite of a passive front-porch campaign

Etymology

Based on the U.S. tradition of candidates giving speeches on their front porch, notably by President William McKinley in 1896.
