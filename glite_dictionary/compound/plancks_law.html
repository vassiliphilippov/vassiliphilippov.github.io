Planck's law

1. NOUN

/ plæŋks lɔ / (AmE), / plæŋks lɔː / (BrE)
a fundamental principle in physics stating that the energy of electromagnetic radiation is quantized and proportional to its frequency, with the proportionality constant being Planck's constant
quantum law relating energy to frequency of electromagnetic radiation

Examples

According to Planck's law, the energy of a photon is directly proportional to its frequency.
Scientists used Planck's law to accurately predict the spectrum of radiation emitted by a black body.
The development of Planck's law marked the beginning of quantum physics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quantum theory - the branch of physics explaining the nature and behavior of matter and energy on the atomic and subatomic level

Etymology

Named after German physicist Max Planck (1858–1947), who formulated the law in 1900.
