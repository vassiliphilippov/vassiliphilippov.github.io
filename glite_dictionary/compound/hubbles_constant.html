Hubble's constant

1. NOUN

/ ˈhʌbəlz ˌkɑnstənt / (AmE), / ˈhʌbəlz ˌkɒnstənt / (BrE)
the ratio of the speed at which a galaxy is receding from Earth to its distance from Earth, used as a measure of the current expansion rate of the universe
measure of universe's expansion rate

Examples

Current estimates of Hubble's constant help astronomers determine the age of the universe.
The value of Hubble's constant has been refined over decades as new data becomes available.
Discrepancies in measuring Hubble's constant have sparked debates among cosmologists.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Hubble's law - the law stating that the speed at which a galaxy moves away is proportional to its distance from the observer

Etymology

Named after American astronomer Edwin Hubble (1889–1953).
