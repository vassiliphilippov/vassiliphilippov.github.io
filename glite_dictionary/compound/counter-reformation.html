Counter-Reformation

1. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊntər ˌrɛfəˈrmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntə ˌrɛfəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
The period of reform and renewal in the Roman Catholic Church in response to the Protestant Reformation, characterized by reaffirmation of Catholic doctrine and the authority of the Pope, and often associated with the work of the Jesuits.
Catholic reform movement opposing the Reformation

Examples

During the Counter-Reformation, the Catholic Church implemented sweeping changes to address abuses and clarify doctrine.
The Jesuit order played a significant role in the spread of the Counter-Reformation across Europe.
Art and architecture flourished in Catholic countries during the Counter-Reformation as part of efforts to inspire religious devotion.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Reformation - the 16th-century religious movement to reform the Roman Catholic Church, leading to Protestant churches

Etymology

From counter- (against) and Reformation (the Protestant Reformation of the 16th century).
