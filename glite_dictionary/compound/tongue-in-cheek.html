tongue-in-cheek

1. ADV

/ tʌŋ ɪn tʃik / (AmE), / tʌŋ ɪn tʃiːk / (BrE)
in an ironic, humorous, or insincere manner; not meant to be taken seriously
ironically or humorously, not seriously

Examples

He made a tongue-in-cheek comment about becoming the next president.
Her tongue-in-cheek response made everyone laugh at the meeting.
The article offered tongue-in-cheek advice for surviving a zombie apocalypse.

Synset Examples

he spoke to her banteringly

Related Terms

  • Antonym: seriously - in a sincere, genuine, or earnest way

Etymology

From the phrase 'tongue in cheek', originally referring to a physical gesture indicating suppressed mirth or insincerity; dates from the 19th century.

2. ADV

/ ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃik / (AmE), / ˌtʌŋ ɪn ˈtʃiːk / (BrE)
in an ironic, humorous, or insincere manner; not seriously or literally meant
ironically, not seriously

Examples

His comments about quitting his job were clearly tongue-in-cheek and not to be taken seriously.
The article was written in a tongue-in-cheek style, poking fun at modern technology.
She made a tongue-in-cheek remark about becoming the next president.

Synset Examples

I meant it facetiously

Related Terms

  • Synonym: facetiously - in a manner not meant to be taken seriously; jokingly

Etymology

Refers to a gesture where one pushes the tongue into the cheek to indicate irony or insincerity; recorded from early 19th century.
