Michelson-Morley experiment

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪkəlsən ˈmɔːrli ɪkˌspɛrɪmənt / (AmE), / ˈmɪkəlsən ˈmɔːli ɪkˌspɛrɪmənt / (BrE)
a famous physics experiment conducted in 1887 by Albert A. Michelson and Edward W. Morley to detect the effect of the Earth's motion on the speed of light, whose null result was pivotal for the development of Einstein's theory of relativity
historic physics experiment on the speed of light

Examples

The Michelson-Morley experiment famously failed to detect any difference in the speed of light due to the Earth's motion.
Physics students often study the Michelson-Morley experiment to understand the origins of special relativity.
The outcome of the Michelson-Morley experiment challenged existing theories about the nature of light and space.

Etymology

Named after physicists Albert A. Michelson and Edward W. Morley, who conducted the experiment in 1887.
