postalveolar consonant

1. NOUN

/ ˌpoʊstælˈvioʊlɚ ˈkɑnsənənt / (AmE), / ˌpəʊstælˈviːələ ˈkɒnsənənt / (BrE)
(Phonetics) A consonant sound produced with the tongue near or touching the back of the alveolar ridge, just behind the teeth.
consonant pronounced just behind the alveolar ridge
medical

Examples

The English 'sh' in 'ship' is a common postalveolar consonant.
Many languages have both alveolar and postalveolar consonants in their sound inventories.
The distinction between sibilant and non-sibilant postalveolar consonants is important in phonetics.

Synset Examples

The sibilant postalveolars (i.e. fricatives and affricates) are sometimes called ‘hush consonants’ because they include the sound /ʃ/ of English ‘Shhh!’ (as distinguished from the ‘hiss’ consonant /s/, as in ‘Ssss!’).

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: alveolar consonant - a consonant produced with the tongue against the alveolar ridge
  • Degree Higher: palatal consonant - a consonant produced with the tongue against the hard palate
  • Degree Higher: retroflex consonant - a consonant pronounced with the tongue curled toward the palate

Etymology

From Latin 'post-' (after) + 'alveolus' (small cavity) and 'consonant'.
