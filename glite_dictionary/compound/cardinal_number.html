cardinal number

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrdɪnəl ˈnʌmbər / (AmE), / ˈkɑːdɪnəl ˈnʌmbə / (BrE)
a number that expresses quantity (how many) but not order; for example, one, two, or three
number indicating quantity, not order

Examples

The cardinal number of the set {2, 4, 6} is three.
Children often learn cardinal numbers before ordinal numbers in school.
In mathematics, a cardinal number represents the size of a collection.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ordinal number - number indicating position or order (e.g., first, second, third)

Etymology

From Latin cardinalis (principal, chief) and numerus (number).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrdɪnl ˈnʌmbɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɑːdɪn(ə)l ˈnʌmbə / (BrE)
A number word that expresses a countable quantity, such as one, two, or three, as opposed to indicating order.
number word expressing quantity

Examples

A cardinal number is used to show how many items are present.
The teacher asked the students to list every cardinal number up to ten.
Words like 'four' and 'seventeen' are examples of cardinal numbers.

Synset Examples

Cardinal numerals expressing numbers below 100 are single words, while those expressing higher numbers are syntactically composite.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ordinal number - a number word indicating position or order (first, second, third)

Etymology

From Latin cardinalis (principal, chief) + numerus (number).
