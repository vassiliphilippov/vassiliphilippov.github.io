adverse selection

1. NOUN

/ ædˌvɝs səˈlɛkʃən / (AmE), / ˈædvɜːs sɪˈlɛkʃən / (BrE)
A situation in which one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other, leading to an inefficient or unfavorable outcome, often used in economics and insurance.
economic term for information imbalance in transactions

Examples

Many insurance companies take steps to minimize adverse selection by thoroughly screening new applicants.
If only the buyers of used cars know their true condition, adverse selection can occur in the market.
The concept of adverse selection explains why healthy people may avoid signing up for health insurance.

Synset Examples

The insurance company investigators realized new policy-holder Louis was an adverse selection shortly after he was insured, when a slew of illnesses suddenly sent him to the hospital numerous times.

Etymology

From 'adverse' (harmful or unfavorable) and 'selection' (the process of choosing), used in economic theory.
