magnetic flux

1. NOUN

/ məgˈnɛtɪk flʌks / (AmE), / məɡˈnɛtɪk flʌks / (BrE)
the total amount of magnetic field passing through a given area, represented by lines of force around a magnet or moving charged particle
measure of magnetic field through an area

Examples

The strength of the magnetic flux determines the force exerted on nearby objects.
Physicists calculated the magnetic flux through the loop to solve the circuit problem.
Changes in magnetic flux can induce an electric current in a conductor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magnetic field - a region where magnetic forces are observable

Etymology

From 'magnetic' (from Latin magnes, 'magnet') + 'flux' (from Latin fluxus, 'flow').

2. NOUN

/ mægˈnɛtɪk flʌks / (AmE), / mæɡˈnɛtɪk flʌks / (BrE)
the total amount of magnetic field passing through a given area, used as a measure of the strength of a magnetic field
measure of magnetic field strength over an area
medical

Examples

The magnetic flux through the coil increases as the magnet moves closer.
Scientists measured the change in magnetic flux to calculate the induced voltage.
A uniform magnetic flux is essential for the experiment's accuracy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magnetic field - the space around a magnet where magnetic forces can be observed

Etymology

From 'magnetic' (relating to magnetism) and Latin 'fluxus' (flow), referring to the flow of magnetic field lines.
