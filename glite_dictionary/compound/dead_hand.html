dead hand

1. NOUN

/ dɛd hænd / (AmE), / dɛd hænd / (BrE)
Real property held inalienably, especially by a church or ecclesiastical corporation, so that it cannot be transferred or sold.
inalienable property, esp. church property
legal

Examples

The church owned vast estates under the dead hand, making them unavailable for redistribution.
Laws were passed to prevent land from remaining under the dead hand for generations.
Reformers criticized the dead hand of ecclesiastical control over so much valuable property.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'manus mortua' (dead hand), referring to property held by institutions that, unlike individuals, never 'die.'

2. NOUN

/ dɛd hænd / (AmE), / dɛd hand / (BrE)
the continuing and often restrictive influence of past actions, decisions, or institutions; something that holds progress back due to its persistent control from the past
oppressive influence of the past

Examples

The dead hand of bureaucracy stifles innovation in many organizations.
Modern law is often hindered by the dead hand of outdated statutes.
He argued that the dead hand of tradition should not dictate our current policies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: legacy - something handed down from the past
  • Synonym: grip - a firm hold; control or power over something (metaphorically)

Etymology

From the literal sense of 'dead hand' meaning ‘the effect of something no longer living’—figuratively, an influence that should have ended but still persists.
