lynch law

1. NOUN

/ lɪntʃ lɔ / (AmE), / lɪntʃ lɔː / (BrE)
the practice of punishing people, especially by hanging, without legal trial or due process, typically carried out by a mob
extrajudicial punishment by mob

Examples

The rise of lynch law in the 19th century reflected the lack of effective legal authority.
His novel depicts the terror and injustice wrought by lynch law in rural communities.
Some historians have studied the decline of lynch law after the expansion of federal oversight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vigilantism - the act of taking the law into one's own hands

Etymology

From the name of Charles Lynch, an 18th-century American who sanctioned such punishments during the American Revolution.
