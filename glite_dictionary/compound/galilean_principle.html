Galilean principle

1. NOUN

/ ˌgæləˈliːən ˈprɪnsəpəl / (AmE), / ˌɡalɪˈliːən ˈprɪnsɪpəl / (BrE)
The principle, formulated by Galileo, that the laws of motion are the same in all inertial frames of reference.
principle of motion from Galileo

Examples

The Galilean principle explains why passengers feel the same inside a moving train as when stationary.
Physicists often apply the Galilean principle when analyzing objects in uniform motion.
According to the Galilean principle, the results of mechanical experiments are identical in any inertial frame.

Synset Examples

The Galilean principle explains the motion of objects.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: principle of relativity - the concept that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames

Etymology

Named after Galileo Galilei (1564–1642), Italian physicist and astronomer.
