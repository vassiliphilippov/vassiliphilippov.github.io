vested interest

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɛstɪd ˈɪntrəst / (AmE), / ˈvɛstɪd ˈɪntrəst / (BrE)
A right or claim under the law that is secured and cannot be taken away, often with the ability to be transferred to another.
secured legal right or claim
legal

Examples

Under the will, the heir received a vested interest in the property.
Once a vested interest is created, it cannot be revoked without the beneficiary's consent.
She sold her vested interest in the trust to another party.

Etymology

From 'vested' (from Latin vestire, to clothe or endow) meaning conferred or secured, and 'interest' meaning legal right.

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɛstɪd ˈɪntrəst / (AmE), / ˈvɛstɪd ˈɪntrəst / (BrE)
a group or person with a strong personal stake in maintaining or influencing a system, activity, or decision from which they gain private benefit
group/person seeking to protect private advantage

Examples

A powerful vested interest lobbied against the new environmental regulations.
The reforms threatened the vested interests of several influential corporations.
Politicians often have to contend with vested interests when implementing major policy changes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: self-interest - regard for one's own advantage or benefit
  • Antonym: disinterestedness - lack of bias or self-interest; impartiality

Etymology

From 'vested', meaning secured in the possession of a person, and 'interest', meaning benefit or stake.
