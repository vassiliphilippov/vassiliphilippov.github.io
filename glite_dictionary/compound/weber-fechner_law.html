Weber-Fechner law

1. NOUN

/ ˈweɪbər ˈfɛknər lɔ / (AmE), / ˈvɛːbə ˈfɛknə lɔː / (BrE)
a principle stating that the perceived strength of a stimulus increases in proportion to the logarithm of the stimulus intensity
principle of stimulus perception in psychology

Examples

The Weber-Fechner law explains why a dim light must be increased significantly before it seems noticeably brighter.
Many introductory psychology textbooks discuss the Weber-Fechner law when introducing the study of sensation and perception.
Researchers applied the Weber-Fechner law to analyze how people perceive changes in sound volume.

Etymology

Named after German scientists Ernst Heinrich Weber and Gustav Theodor Fechner, who formulated the law in the 19th century.
