Pauli exclusion principle

1. NOUN

/ ˈpaʊli ɪkˈskluʒən ˈprɪnsəpəl / (AmE), / ˈpaʊli ɪkˈskluːʒən ˈprɪnsɪpl̩ / (BrE)
a principle in quantum mechanics stating that no two identical fermions (such as electrons, protons, or neutrons) in a given system can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously
quantum rule: no identical fermions in same state

Examples

The Pauli exclusion principle explains why electrons in an atom fill different energy levels.
Because of the Pauli exclusion principle, no two electrons can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
The organization of the periodic table is a direct result of the Pauli exclusion principle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quantum mechanics - branch of physics dealing with subatomic particles and their behaviors

Etymology

Named after Austrian physicist Wolfgang Pauli, who formulated the principle in 1925.
