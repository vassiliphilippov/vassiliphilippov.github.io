Democratic-Republican Party

1. NOUN

/ dɛməˌkrætɪk rɪˈpʌblɪkən ˈpɑrti / (AmE), / dɛməˌkrætɪk rɪˈpʌblɪkən ˈpɑːti / (BrE)
a former major political party in the United States in the early 19th century that opposed the Federalist Party and favored limiting the powers of the federal government through a strict interpretation of the Constitution
early 19th-century U.S. political party

Examples

Historians often discuss how the Democratic-Republican Party shaped early American politics.
Thomas Jefferson was one of the founders of the Democratic-Republican Party.
The decline of the Democratic-Republican Party led to the rise of new political factions in the United States.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Federalist Party - a political party in early U.S. history that supported a strong federal government

Etymology

Formed from 'Democratic' and 'Republican', reflecting the party's ideals. Founded by Thomas Jefferson and James Madison in the 1790s.
