coho salmon

1. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊhoʊ ˈsæmən / (AmE), / ˈkəʊhəʊ ˈsæmən / (BrE)
the flesh of a small Pacific salmon, valued for its fatty, pinkish meat, or the fish itself
a species of Pacific salmon; its pinkish flesh

Examples

The fresh coho salmon was grilled to perfection and served with a lemon-dill sauce.
Many chefs prefer using coho salmon for its tender, flavorful flesh in sushi rolls.
She bought a fillet of coho salmon from the local fish market for dinner.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sockeye salmon - a species of salmon with distinctive red flesh
  • Synonym: Chinook salmon - the largest species of Pacific salmon

Etymology

From Haida (a North American indigenous language), originally referring to the fish species.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊˌhoʊ ˈsæmən / (AmE), / ˈkəʊˌhəʊ ˈsæmən / (BrE)
a small salmon native to the northern Pacific coasts and introduced to the Great Lakes, valued for its game and food qualities
a species of salmon native to the Pacific coasts

Examples

The fishermen celebrated a good catch of coho salmon during the autumn run.
Grilled coho salmon is a popular dish in many Pacific Northwest restaurants.
Biologists monitor the coho salmon populations in the Great Lakes each year.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sockeye salmon - a species of salmon native to the northern Pacific Ocean, known for its red flesh
  • Synonym: chinook salmon - the largest species of Pacific salmon

Etymology

From Pacific Northwest Salish languages, e.g., Halkomelem *kʷəxʷə* (coho); 'salmon' from Old French *saumon*, from Latin *salmo*.
