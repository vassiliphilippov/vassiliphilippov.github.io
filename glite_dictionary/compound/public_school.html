public school

1. NOUN

/ ˈpʌblɪk skuːl / (AmE), / ˈpʌblɪk skuːl / (BrE)
In Britain, a private independent secondary school, often supported by endowment and tuition, typically attended by pupils from wealthy or influential families.
British private independent secondary school
british

Examples

He attended a renowned public school before enrolling at university.
Many British politicians are former public school students.
The traditions of the public school have influenced much of the country's elite.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: private school - A school supported by private funds rather than by the government

Etymology

In British English, the term originally referred to schools open to the paying public, as opposed to religious or local schools.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpʌblɪk skuːl / (AmE), / ˈpʌblɪk skuːl / (BrE)
In the United States, a school that is free to attend, supported by public taxes, and controlled by a local school board.
US state-funded school
american

Examples

Most children in the United States attend a public school near their homes.
The budget for the public school district was increased this year.
After moving to a new city, Mia enrolled her daughter in the local public school.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: private school - a school supported by a private organization or private individuals rather than by the government

Etymology

Compound of 'public' (from Latin 'publicus', of the people) and 'school' (from Greek 'skholē', leisure, then place of learning).
