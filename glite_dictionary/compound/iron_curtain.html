iron curtain

1. NOUN

/ ˈaɪərn ˈkɜrtən / (AmE), / ˌaɪən ˈkɜːtn̩ / (BrE)
a barrier of secrecy and censorship, especially the ideological and physical boundary dividing Eastern and Western Europe during the Cold War
Cold War boundary of secrecy and censorship

Examples

During the Cold War, the iron curtain separated families and friends living on opposite sides of Europe.
Winston Churchill's speech popularized the term iron curtain to describe Soviet domination over Eastern Europe.
Information could rarely pass through the iron curtain, due to strict government control and censorship.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: curtain - a piece of cloth hung to cover a window, stage, etc.

Etymology

From the phrase used by Winston Churchill in 1946 to describe the division between East and West in Europe after World War II.

2. NOUN

/ ˈaɪɚn ˈkɝːtn̩ / (AmE), / ˈaɪən ˈkɜːtn̩ / (BrE)
A political, military, and ideological barrier that separated the Soviet bloc and the West during the Cold War, often used more generally for any impenetrable barrier between groups or ideologies.
Cold War barrier between East and West

Examples

After the iron curtain fell, Eastern European countries began to embrace democratic reforms.
The iron curtain was a powerful symbol of political and ideological division during the twentieth century.
Many families were separated by the iron curtain for decades until new policies allowed for reunions.

Synset Examples

The iron curtain divided the country for decades.
Politicians discussed the impact of the iron curtain on Europe.
The fall of the iron curtain changed global politics.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: curtain - A piece of cloth or other material used to cover a window, stage, etc.; used metaphorically in 'iron curtain'.

Etymology

First popularized by Winston Churchill in 1946, metaphorically using 'iron' and 'curtain' to denote a solid, impenetrable divide.
