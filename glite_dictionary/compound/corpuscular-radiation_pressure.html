corpuscular-radiation pressure

1. NOUN

/ kɔrˈpʌskjələr ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən ˈprɛʃər / (AmE), / kɔːˈpʌskjʊlə ˌreɪdɪˈeɪʃən ˈprɛʃə / (BrE)
the very small pressure exerted on a surface at right angles to the direction in which wave-like energy (such as light) travels, due to the impact of particles
minute pressure from wave propagation on a surface

Examples

The corpuscular-radiation pressure on the spacecraft's solar sail is enough to gradually change its velocity.
Scientists measured the corpuscular-radiation pressure exerted by laser light reflecting off a small mirror.
Understanding corpuscular-radiation pressure is important when studying the behavior of photons in astrophysical environments.

Etymology

From Latin 'corpusculum' (a small body) and 'radiation', referring to particle-like aspect of radiation.
