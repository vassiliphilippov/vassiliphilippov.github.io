black mangrove

1. NOUN

/ blæk ˈmæŋˌɡroʊv / (AmE), / blæk ˈmæŋɡrəʊv / (BrE)
a tropical tree or shrub of the genus Avicennia, found chiefly in coastal areas and characterized by dark bark and pneumatophores, commonly found in the West Indies, Florida, and parts of Australia
a coastal tropical mangrove tree

Examples

The coast is lined with dense stands of black mangrove trees, providing habitat for many birds.
Roots of the black mangrove stick up from the mud, helping the tree get oxygen in waterlogged soil.
Scientists studied the distribution of black mangrove populations in different climate zones.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: white mangrove - a mangrove species with lighter bark, found in similar habitats
  • Antonym: red mangrove - another type of mangrove tree distinguished by its prop roots and reddish bark

2. NOUN

/ blæk ˈmæŋɡroʊv / (AmE), / blæk ˈmæŋɡrəʊv / (BrE)
a tropical tree or shrub (Avicennia germinans) of coastal regions, especially in the West Indies and southern Florida, characterized by dense thickets and numerous roots that protrude above the ground
tropical coastal mangrove tree

Examples

The dense thickets of black mangrove provide vital habitat for many fish species.
Researchers studied the unique roots of the black mangrove along the Florida coastline.
Hiking through the swamp, we passed stands of black mangrove with their distinctive aerial roots.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: white mangrove - a type of mangrove, Laguncularia racemosa, found in similar coastal regions
  • Degree Higher: red mangrove - a type of mangrove, Rhizophora mangle, that grows closest to the water
