Dalton's law

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɔltənz lɔ / (AmE), / ˈdɔːltənz lɔː / (BrE)
a scientific law stating that the pressure exerted by a mixture of gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases in the mixture
law on pressures in gas mixtures

Examples

According to Dalton's law, the total pressure in the container is 5 atmospheres.
In chemistry class, we used Dalton's law to calculate the pressure contribution of each gas.
The engineer applied Dalton's law to determine the oxygen level in the air sample.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: partial pressure - the pressure that a single gas in a mixture would exert if it occupied the whole volume alone

Etymology

Named after English chemist John Dalton (1766–1844), who formulated the law.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɔltənz lɔ / (AmE), / ˈdɔːltənz lɔː / (BrE)
A law in chemistry stating that if two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole-number ratios.
law of mass ratios in compound formation

Examples

According to Dalton's law, the ratio of oxygen to hydrogen in different oxides of hydrogen is always a simple whole number.
Chemistry students often study Dalton's law when learning about the composition of compounds.
Experiments in the early 19th century provided strong evidence in support of Dalton's law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: law of definite proportions - law stating that a chemical compound always contains the same proportion of elements by mass

Etymology

Named after English chemist John Dalton (1766–1844), who formulated the law.
