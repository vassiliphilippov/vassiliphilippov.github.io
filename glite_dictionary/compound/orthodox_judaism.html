Orthodox Judaism

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔrθədɑks ˈdʒudeɪɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈɔːθədɒks ˈdʒuːdeɪɪzəm / (BrE)
A branch of Judaism characterized by strict observance of traditional Jewish laws and customs as prescribed by the Torah and rabbinic tradition.
strictly traditional branch of Judaism

Examples

Many families who follow Orthodox Judaism observe the Sabbath with great reverence and tradition.
She grew up in a community where Orthodox Judaism shaped daily life and cultural practices.
Although his parents practiced Orthodox Judaism, he chose a more liberal branch of the faith as an adult.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Conservative Judaism - a branch of Judaism between Reform and Orthodox in observance
  • Degree Lower: Reform Judaism - a branch of Judaism with more liberal practices

Etymology

Orthodox from Greek 'orthodoxos' (having the right opinion); Judaism from Latin 'Judaismus', from Greek 'Ioudaismos'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɔrθəˌdɑks ˈdʒuːdiˌɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈɔːθədɒks ˈdʒuːdiˌɪzəm / (BrE)
A branch of Judaism that strictly observes traditional Jewish laws and practices as interpreted in the Talmud and other authoritative texts.
branch of Judaism adhering strictly to traditional law

Examples

Many communities around the world practice Orthodox Judaism and uphold centuries-old traditions.
He grew up in a family deeply committed to Orthodox Judaism.
Women following Orthodox Judaism often observe strict religious dress codes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Reform Judaism - A major Jewish denomination that advocates a more liberal approach to Jewish law and practice.
  • Synonym: Conservative Judaism - A branch of Judaism seeking a middle ground between Orthodox and Reform traditions.

Etymology

From 'orthodox' (meaning 'conforming to established doctrine') and 'Judaism,' from Latin and Greek origins.
