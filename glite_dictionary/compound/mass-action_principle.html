mass-action principle

1. NOUN

/ ˌmæs ˈæk.ʃən ˈprɪn.sə.pəl / (AmE), / ˌmæs ˈæk.ʃən ˈprɪn.sɪ.pl̩ / (BrE)
The theory in neurology that the cortex of the brain functions as a coordinated system, with large regions of neural tissue working together in all complex activities.
theory of brain function as coordinated mass
medical

Examples

The mass-action principle suggests that the brain's cortex functions as a unified whole rather than as isolated parts.
Many neuropsychologists once subscribed to the mass-action principle when explaining complex cognitive deficits after brain injury.
Research has challenged the mass-action principle, showing that certain areas of the cortex are specialized for different tasks.
