bilaterally symmetrical

1. ADJ

/ bɪˈlæt̬ɚəli sɪˈmɛtrɪkəl / (AmE), / bɪˈlætərəli sɪˈmɛtrɪkəl / (BrE)
(of an organism or structure) capable of being divided into symmetrical halves along only one longitudinal plane passing through its axis.
having symmetry on only one plane

Examples

Many animals, including humans, are bilaterally symmetrical in their body structure.
The anatomy of a fish is bilaterally symmetrical, allowing for streamlined swimming.
Butterflies are bilaterally symmetrical, with matching patterns on both wings.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: radially symmetrical - capable of division into similar halves by multiple planes passing through the central axis

Etymology

From 'bilateral' (from Latin 'bi-' meaning 'two' + 'lateralis' meaning 'side') and 'symmetrical' (from Greek 'symmetros', 'measured together').

2. ADJ

/ bɪˈlæt̬.ə.rə.li sɪˈmɛt.rɪ.kəl / (AmE), / bɪˈlæt.ər.əli sɪˈmɛt.rɪ.kəl / (BrE)
having identical parts or halves on each side of a central axis, especially in reference to biological organisms or anatomical structures
having equal halves on both sides of an axis
medical

Examples

Most mammals are bilaterally symmetrical, with matching features on each side of their bodies.
The butterfly's wings are bilaterally symmetrical, creating a mirror image from left to right.
A human face is generally bilaterally symmetrical, although minor differences do exist.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: radially symmetrical - having body parts arranged around a central axis

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two), 'latus' (side), and 'symmetrical' from Greek 'symmetros' (of like measure).
