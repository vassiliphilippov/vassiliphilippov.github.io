Socratic irony

1. NOUN

/ soʊˈkrætɪk ˈaɪrəni / (AmE), / səˈkrætɪk ˈaɪrəni / (BrE)
A method of pretending ignorance or a willingness to learn in order to expose the inconsistencies or weaknesses in another person's arguments, especially through close questioning.
pretended ignorance to expose inconsistency (philosophy)

Examples

The philosopher skillfully applied Socratic irony to reveal the flaws in the politician's reasoning.
Through Socratic irony, she led her class to recognize their own contradictions.
During the debate, he used Socratic irony to gently expose gaps in his opponent's logic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: irony - the use of words to express something other than, especially the opposite of, the literal meaning

Etymology

Named after Socrates, the ancient Greek philosopher, who used this technique in philosophical discussions.
