Parkinson's law

1. NOUN

/ ˌpɑrkɪnsənz lɔ / (AmE), / ˌpɑːkɪnsənz lɔː / (BrE)
the observation that work expands to fill the time available for its completion
principle stating work expands to fill allotted time

Examples

According to Parkinson's law, projects often take as long as the deadlines allow, regardless of the actual workload.
Many managers are familiar with Parkinson's law, noticing that employees seem to stretch their tasks to suit the time allotted.
Planning shorter, focused meetings can help counteract Parkinson's law and improve productivity.

Etymology

Named after British historian C. Northcote Parkinson, who formulated the concept in 1955.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːrkɪnsənz lɔː / (AmE), / ˈpɑːkɪnsənz lɔː / (BrE)
the principle that work expands to fill the time and resources available for its completion, often leading to unnecessary growth in staff or bureaucracy regardless of workload
phenomenon of work expanding to fill available resources

Examples

Many large companies fall victim to Parkinson's law, hiring more staff than necessary as their operations grow.
If you give yourself a week to complete a simple task, Parkinson's law suggests the task will take the entire week.
Government departments often illustrate Parkinson's law by expanding their workforce without an actual increase in work.

Etymology

Named after British historian and author C. Northcote Parkinson, who formulated the law in 1955.
