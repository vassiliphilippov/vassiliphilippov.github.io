gateway drug

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡeɪtˌweɪ drʌɡ / (AmE), / ˈɡeɪtˌweɪ drʌɡ / (BrE)
a drug, typically not highly addictive itself, whose use is thought to lead to the use of more dangerous or addictive drugs
a drug leading to use of more harmful drugs

Examples

Many experts debate whether marijuana should be classified as a gateway drug.
He warned that vaping could serve as a gateway drug for teenagers.
The concept of a gateway drug remains controversial among scientists and policymakers.

Synset Examples

one college athlete recently called beer a gateway drug for young people

Etymology

From 'gateway' (an entrance or means of access) + 'drug', referring metaphorically to a starting point for drug use progression.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡeɪtˌweɪ drʌɡ / (AmE), / ˈɡeɪtˌweɪ drʌɡ / (BrE)
a drug, often considered less harmful, that may lead to the use of more dangerous or addictive substances
drug that leads to harder substance use

Examples

Some experts argue that marijuana is often used as a gateway drug before trying harder substances.
The idea of alcohol being a gateway drug has sparked much debate among health professionals.
Parents worry that vaping may serve as a gateway drug for teenagers exploring other substances.

Synset Examples

Many believe alcohol is a gateway drug for teenagers.
Cigarettes are often considered a gateway drug for young adults.
Experts warn that marijuana can be a gateway drug.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: hard drug - a drug that is considered highly addictive or dangerous, such as heroin or cocaine
  • Degree Lower: soft drug - a drug that is considered less harmful, such as cannabis or nicotine

Etymology

From 'gateway' (meaning an entry or means of access) and 'drug'. Usage dates to the late 20th century.
