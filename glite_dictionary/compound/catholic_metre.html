catholic metre

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæθəlɪk ˈmiːtər / (AmE), / ˈkæθəlɪk ˈmiːtə / (BrE)
A former unit of length, defined as the length of a free seconds pendulum, introduced by Tito Livio Burattini in 1675; differs from the modern metre by about half a centimetre.
historical unit of length (1675)

Examples

The catholic metre was intended as a universal standard of length in the 17th century.
Tito Livio Burattini proposed the catholic metre as a scientific measure based on the seconds pendulum.
Although obsolete, the catholic metre is an important concept in the history of measurement.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: metre - the SI base unit of length (modern standard)

Etymology

From 'catholic' (meaning universal) and 'metre', from Greek 'metron' (measure).
