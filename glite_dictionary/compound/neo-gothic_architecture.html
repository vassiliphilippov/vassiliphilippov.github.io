neo-Gothic architecture

1. NOUN

/ ˌnioʊˈɡɑθɪk ˈɑrkɪˌtɛktʃɚ / (AmE), / ˌniːəʊˈɡɒθɪk ˈɑːkɪtɛktʃə / (BrE)
A style of architecture inspired by medieval Gothic design, popular especially in the 19th century, which competed with Neoclassical revivals in the United States and Great Britain.
19th-century revival of Gothic architectural style

Examples

The cathedral was built in the neo-Gothic architecture style, featuring pointed arches and elaborate stained-glass windows.
Many universities in the United States adopted neo-Gothic architecture to convey a sense of tradition and grandeur.
The city hall's neo-Gothic architecture stands out among the surrounding modern buildings.

Synset Examples

In England, the centre of the Gothic revival movement, Gothic revival was intertwined with deeply philosophical movements associated with a re-awakening of High Church or Anglo-Catholic belief (and by the Catholic convert Augustus Welby Pugin) concerned by the growth of religious nonconformism.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Gothic architecture - medieval style of architecture characterized by pointed arches and ribbed vaults
  • Antonym: Neoclassical architecture - architectural style drawing from classical antiquity

Etymology

From Greek neo- (new) and Gothic, referring to the medieval Gothic style.
