Milky Way

1. PROPN

/ ˈmɪlki weɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɪlki weɪ / (BrE)
The galaxy that contains our solar system, appearing as a diffuse band of light in the night sky formed by millions of stars.
the galaxy containing the solar system

Examples

The Milky Way is most visible on clear summer nights, far from city lights.
Astronomers continue to explore the structure and history of the Milky Way.
Many of the stars we see at night are part of the Milky Way.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: galaxy - a massive collection of stars, gas, and dust bound together by gravity

Etymology

Named for its milky appearance; from Latin via lactea, itself a translation of the Greek galaxías kýklos (milky circle).

2. PROPN

/ ˈmɪlki weɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɪlki weɪ / (BrE)
the galaxy that contains the solar system, visible from Earth as a faint band of light stretching across the night sky, composed of millions of stars
galaxy containing the solar system

Examples

The Milky Way is especially bright on clear, moonless nights far from city lights.
Astronomers estimate that the Milky Way contains more than 100 billion stars.
Our solar system is located in one of the spiral arms of the Milky Way.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Andromeda Galaxy - the nearest large galaxy to the Milky Way

Etymology

From the Latin 'via lactea', translating the Greek 'galaxias kyklos' (milky circle), a reference to its appearance in the sky.
