potter's field

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːdərz ˌfiːld / (AmE), / ˈpɒtəz ˌfiːld / (BrE)
a burial ground for unknown or poor people, especially those who cannot afford private graves
cemetery for unknown or poor people

Examples

The city buried unclaimed bodies in the potter's field on the outskirts of town.
Many immigrants who died penniless in the nineteenth century were laid to rest in a potter's field.
After weeks with no identification, his remains were placed in the municipal potter's field.

Etymology

From Biblical reference to the field bought by the priests with Judas Iscariot's returned silver, and used as a burial place for strangers, mentioned in the Gospel of Matthew.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːtərz fiːld / (AmE), / ˈpɒtəz fiːld / (BrE)
a burial place for people who are unknown, indigent, or unclaimed by relatives
burial ground for the unknown or indigent

Examples

The unidentified man was laid to rest in a potter's field outside the city.
Many cities once had a potter's field for those with no known family.
The guide pointed to the overgrown potter's field behind the old church.

Synset Examples

Many unknown souls rest in the potter's field nearby.
He was buried in a potter's field, far from his family.
The unmarked graves were found in a potter's field.

Etymology

Biblical origin: from Matthew 27:7, where a field bought by the priests with Judas’s returned thirty pieces of silver was used to bury strangers.
