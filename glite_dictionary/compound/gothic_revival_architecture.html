Gothic revival architecture

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑːθɪk rɪˈvaɪvəl ˈɑːrkɪˌtɛktʃər / (AmE), / ˈɡɒθɪk rɪˈvaɪvəl ˌɑːkɪˈtɛktʃə / (BrE)
a style of architecture that originated in the late 18th and 19th centuries, inspired by medieval Gothic design and characterized by features such as pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and ornate decoration
architectural style inspired by medieval Gothic design

Examples

Many churches and university buildings in the 19th century were designed in the Gothic revival architecture style.
The intricate tracery and pointed arches are hallmarks of Gothic revival architecture seen throughout Victorian-era London.
She wrote her thesis on how Gothic revival architecture influenced urban development in New England.

Synset Examples

In England, the centre of the Gothic revival movement, Gothic revival was intertwined with deeply philosophical movements associated with a re-awakening of High Church or Anglo-Catholic belief (and by the Catholic convert Augustus Welby Pugin) concerned by the growth of religious nonconformism.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Gothic architecture - original medieval style characterized by pointed arches and ribbed vaults
  • Antonym: Neoclassical architecture - architectural style based on classical Greek and Roman forms

Etymology

From 'Gothic', referring to the medieval architectural style, and 'revival', meaning a renewed interest or comeback.
