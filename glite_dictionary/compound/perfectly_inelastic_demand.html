perfectly inelastic demand

1. NOUN

/ pərˈfɛktli ɪnɪˈlæstɪk dɪˈmænd / (AmE), / pəˈfɛktli ɪnɪˈlæstɪk dɪˈmɑːnd / (BrE)
a situation in economics where the quantity demanded of a good remains constant regardless of changes in its price
constant demand regardless of price

Examples

For life-saving medicines like insulin, economists often cite perfectly inelastic demand because patients need them regardless of price.
When a good has perfectly inelastic demand, a price increase will not affect the quantity sold.
Government policies may target goods with perfectly inelastic demand to raise revenue through taxation.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: inelastic demand - quantity demanded changes little with price changes
  • Antonym: perfectly elastic demand - quantity demanded changes infinitely with a tiny change in price

Etymology

Formed from 'perfectly' (from Latin 'perfectus'), 'inelastic' (from in- 'not' + elastic from Greek 'elastos'), and 'demand' (from Latin 'demandare', to demand).
