Jungian psychology

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌŋiən saɪˈkɑlədʒi / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌŋiən saɪˈkɒlədʒi / (BrE)
the system of psychological theories and practices developed by Carl Jung, emphasizing concepts such as the collective unconscious, archetypes, and individuation.
psychological theories developed by Carl Jung

Examples

Many therapists incorporate elements of Jungian psychology into their practice to explore clients' dreams and unconscious motivations.
Her interest in symbolism led her to study Jungian psychology extensively.
The concept of archetypes is central to Jungian psychology and helps explain universal patterns in human behavior.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Freudian psychology - the psychological theories of Sigmund Freud

Etymology

Named after Swiss psychiatrist Carl Jung (1875–1961).
