yellow journalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈjɛloʊ ˈdʒɜrnəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈjɛləʊ ˈdʒɜːnəlɪzəm / (BrE)
Journalism that is based on sensationalism and exaggeration rather than well-researched news; reporting that aims to attract attention by presenting stories in a lurid or shocking way.
sensationalist, exaggerated journalism

Examples

The tabloid was accused of yellow journalism for exaggerating the facts of the scandal.
Many historians blame yellow journalism for inflaming public opinion before the Spanish-American War.
Some news outlets continue to rely on yellow journalism to draw in more readers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensationalism - Presentation of stories to provoke public excitement at the expense of accuracy

Etymology

Coined in the late 19th century after the 'Yellow Kid' cartoons featured in sensationalist American newspapers.

2. NOUN

/ ˈjɛloʊ ˈdʒɜrnəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈjɛləʊ ˈdʒɜːnəlɪzəm / (BrE)
journalism that uses sensationalism, exaggeration, or scandal to attract readers, often sacrificing accuracy or ethics
sensationalist style of journalism

Examples

The newspaper was accused of yellow journalism after publishing exaggerated reports about the incident.
Many critics argue that yellow journalism undermines public trust in the media.
She studied the history of yellow journalism in her journalism ethics class.

Synset Examples

He refused to engage with yellow journalism tactics.
Many believe yellow journalism misleads the public.
The article was criticized for its reliance on yellow journalism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensationalism - presentation of stories in a way intended to provoke public interest or excitement

Etymology

Originally from late 19th-century US journalism, linked to the sensationalistic 'Yellow Kid' comic in newspapers.
