French polish

1. NOUN

/ frɛntʃ ˈpɑlɪʃ / (AmE), / frentʃ ˈpɒlɪʃ / (BrE)
a type of varnish for wood, made by dissolving shellac in alcohol, used to produce a high-gloss finish
a shellac-based wood varnish

Examples

The antique table was restored carefully using French polish to bring out its shine.
He applied several coats of French polish to achieve a deep, glossy surface on the wooden cabinet.
Traditional furniture makers often use French polish to give wooden pieces a luxurious finish.

Etymology

Named for its association with French cabinet makers who popularized this finishing method in the 18th century.

2. NOUN

/ frɛntʃ ˈpɑlɪʃ / (AmE), / frɛntʃ ˈpɒlɪʃ / (BrE)
a high-gloss finish on wood, produced by applying many thin layers of shellac with a rubbing pad
a high-gloss wood finish with shellac

Examples

The antique table gleamed under a fresh French polish.
Restorers applied several coats of French polish to bring out the wood's rich color.
Over time, the French polish developed a beautiful, deep luster.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shellac - a resin used in making French polish

Etymology

Named after the technique's association with French cabinetmakers of the 18th-19th centuries.
