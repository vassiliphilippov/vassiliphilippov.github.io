variola major

1. NOUN

/ vəˌraɪələ ˈmeɪdʒər / (AmE), / vəˌraɪələ ˈmeɪdʒə / (BrE)
the more severe form of the smallpox virus, characterized by a high fatality rate of up to 25 percent
severe form of smallpox virus
medical

Examples

The fatality rate of variola major made early detection and isolation essential for controlling outbreaks.
Scientists studied variola major to develop effective smallpox vaccines.
Before its eradication, variola major caused widespread fear due to its high mortality.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: variola minor - the less severe form of the smallpox virus
  • Synonym: smallpox - an infectious disease caused by the variola virus

Etymology

From Latin 'variola' (smallpox) and Latin 'major' (greater, larger).

2. NOUN

/ vɛrˈaɪ.ələ ˈmeɪ.dʒɚ / (AmE), / vəˈrʌɪ.ələ ˈmeɪ.dʒə / (BrE)
the more severe and common form of smallpox, a highly contagious viral disease marked by fever, weakness, and skin eruptions that leave permanent scars
severe form of smallpox disease
medical

Examples

Outbreaks of variola major had devastating effects on populations before the advent of vaccination.
The last naturally occurring case of variola major was reported in the 1970s.
Treatment options were extremely limited during epidemics of variola major throughout history.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: variola minor - a milder form of smallpox
  • Synonym: smallpox - an acute contagious viral disease; also known as variola

Etymology

From Latin 'variola' (smallpox) and Latin 'major' (greater), distinguishing it from 'variola minor'.
