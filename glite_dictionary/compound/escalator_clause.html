escalator clause

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛskəˌleɪtər klɔz / (AmE), / ˈɛskəleɪtə klɔːz / (BrE)
a clause in a contract that allows for automatic adjustment of wages, prices, or benefits based on changes in economic indicators such as the cost of living index
contract clause for automatic adjustments
legal

Examples

The union negotiated an escalator clause to ensure that wages rise with inflation.
Because of the escalator clause, her rent increased every year in line with the consumer price index.
Many long-term contracts now include an escalator clause to adjust for economic fluctuations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cost-of-living allowance - an allowance to offset increases in the cost of living
  • Antonym: fixed-rate clause - a contract clause specifying that the rate will not change

Etymology

Named after the word 'escalate', meaning to increase, referring to the clause's function of causing adjustments to rise or fall.
