Krebs cycle

1. NOUN

/ krɛbz ˈsaɪ.kəl / (AmE), / krɛbz ˈsaɪ.kəl / (BrE)
a sequence of chemical reactions in living cells, particularly in mitochondria, through which the oxidation of acetyl compounds produces energy, carbon dioxide, and water; also called the citric acid cycle
biochemical energy-producing cycle in cells
medical

Examples

The Krebs cycle is a crucial part of how cells generate energy from nutrients.
Biology students must learn each step of the Krebs cycle to understand cellular respiration.
If the Krebs cycle is disrupted, the production of cellular energy can be severely affected.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: citric acid cycle - alternative name for the Krebs cycle
  • Degree Lower: glycolysis - metabolic pathway breaking down glucose before the Krebs cycle
  • Degree Higher: electron transport chain - stage following the Krebs cycle in cellular respiration

Etymology

Named after Hans Adolf Krebs, German biochemist, who described the cycle in the 1930s.
