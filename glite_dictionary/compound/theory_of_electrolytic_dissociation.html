theory of electrolytic dissociation

1. NOUN

/ ˈθiəɹi əv ɪˌlɛktɹoʊˈlɪtɪk dɪˌsoʊsiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈθɪəri əv iˌlɛktrəʊˈlɪtɪk dɪˌsəʊsiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
A scientific theory in chemistry stating that acids, bases, and salts dissociate into ions when dissolved in water, explaining electrical conductivity in aqueous solutions.
chemical theory explaining electrolytic dissociation
medical

Examples

The theory of electrolytic dissociation helped explain why salts conduct electricity when dissolved in water.
Modern chemistry classes often cover the theory of electrolytic dissociation as a foundation for understanding acids and bases.
Arrhenius received the Nobel Prize in part for developing the theory of electrolytic dissociation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Arrhenius theory - the scientific theory of electrolytic dissociation formulated by Svante Arrhenius

Etymology

From 'electrolytic' (relating to electrolysis, from Greek 'ēlektron' and 'lysis') and 'dissociation' (from Latin 'dissociare' to separate).
