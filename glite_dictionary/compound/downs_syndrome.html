Down's syndrome

1. NOUN

/ daʊnz ˈsɪnˌdroʊm / (AmE), / daʊnz ˈsɪn.drəʊm / (BrE)
A congenital disorder caused by the presence of an extra chromosome 21, typically leading to distinct facial features, short stature, and varying degrees of intellectual disability.
genetic disorder with extra chromosome 21
medical

Examples

Children with Down's syndrome may benefit from early intervention programs.
A genetic test can confirm the diagnosis of Down's syndrome during pregnancy.
Many people with Down's syndrome lead fulfilling, active lives in their communities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trisomy 21 - another name for Down's syndrome, referring to the additional chromosome 21

Etymology

Named after the English physician John Langdon Down, who first described the condition in 1866.
