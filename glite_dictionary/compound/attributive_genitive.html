attributive genitive

1. NOUN

/ əˈtrɪbjətɪv ˈdʒɛnətɪv / (AmE), / əˈtrɪbjʊtɪv ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (BrE)
A word or phrase in the genitive case that functions as an attributive adjective, typically indicating possession or close association.
genitive case used attributively

Examples

In the phrase ‘my brother's car’, ‘brother's’ is an attributive genitive.
The attributive genitive is commonly used in English to show possession or relationships.
Teachers often explain the attributive genitive as a way to combine two nouns into a descriptive phrase.

Synset Examples

an example of the attributive genitive is ‘John's’ in ‘John's mother’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possessive - indicating possession or ownership

Etymology

From Latin 'genitivus' (of or belonging to birth), combined with 'attributive', from Latin 'attribuere' (to assign).

2. NOUN

/ əˈtrɪbjətɪv ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (AmE), / əˈtrɪbjʊtɪv ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (BrE)
A word or phrase in the genitive case used as an attributive adjective to modify a noun, as in 'John's mother' where 'John's' describes whose mother.
genitive case used as an adjective

Examples

In the phrase "the girl's bicycle", attributive genitive is represented by "girl's".
The sentence "the company's policy" provides another example of an attributive genitive.
Understanding the use of the attributive genitive can clarify how possession is expressed in English.

Synset Examples

an example of the attributive genitive is ‘John's’ in ‘John's mother’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: genitive case - grammatical case typically indicating possession or close association

Etymology

'Genitive' from Latin 'genitivus', meaning 'of or belonging to a kind', and 'attributive' from Medieval Latin 'attributivus'.
