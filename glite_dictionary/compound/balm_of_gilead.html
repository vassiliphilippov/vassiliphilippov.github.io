balm of Gilead

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɑlm əv ˈɡɪliæd / (AmE), / ˈbɑːm əv ˈɡɪliəd / (BrE)
a fragrant resin obtained from certain plants, especially used in ancient times for medicinal and aromatic purposes
fragrant resin from certain plants

Examples

The ancient Egyptians prized balm of Gilead for its healing properties.
Traders once carried balm of Gilead across great distances to sell in foreign markets.
Legends speak of the balm of Gilead as a cure for all wounds.

Etymology

From Gilead, a region east of the Jordan River, mentioned in the Bible for its healing balm.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɑm əv ˈɡɪliæd / (AmE), / ˈbɑːm əv ˈɡɪliæd / (BrE)
A small evergreen tree of Africa and Asia with aromatic leaves, historically valued for its fragrant resin.
aromatic evergreen tree of Africa and Asia

Examples

The ancient Egyptians valued balm of Gilead for its powerful fragrance and medicinal properties.
When bruised, the leaves of the balm of Gilead release a strong, pleasing aroma.
Some traditional medicines use extracts from the balm of Gilead in healing ointments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: balsam - an aromatic resinous substance obtained from various plants

Etymology

From Biblical Gilead, a region east of the Jordan River noted for its healing balm.

3. NOUN

/ bɑm əv ˈɡɪliæd / (AmE), / bɑːm əv ˈɡɪliæd / (BrE)
a medium-sized fir tree of northeastern North America, whose crushed leaves emit a balsam scent; widely used for pulpwood and as Christmas trees.
a North American fir tree with balsam-scented leaves

Examples

The balm of Gilead is prized for its fragrant needles and use as Christmas trees.
Many paper mills rely on balm of Gilead trees for high-quality pulpwood.
When you crush the needles of balm of Gilead, they release a distinctive balsam aroma.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: balsam fir - a North American fir tree producing balsam resin

Etymology

Named after the biblical region of Gilead, associated with healing balms; refers here to a tree believed to produce similar resin.
