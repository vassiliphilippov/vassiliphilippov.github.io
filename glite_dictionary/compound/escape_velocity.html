escape velocity

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈskeɪp vəˈlɑːsəti / (AmE), / ɪˈskeɪp vɪˈlɒsəti / (BrE)
the minimum speed required for an object to break free from the gravitational attraction of a celestial body, such as a planet or star, without further propulsion.
minimum speed to leave a gravitational field

Examples

To leave Earth's atmosphere, a rocket must reach escape velocity.
The planet's low escape velocity means it cannot retain a thick atmosphere.
Calculating escape velocity helps scientists understand space travel requirements.

Etymology

From 'escape' + 'velocity', used in physics and astronomy since the mid-20th century.

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈskeɪp vəˈlɑːsəti / (AmE), / ɪˈskeɪp vɪˈlɒsɪti / (BrE)
the minimum speed that an object must reach to break free from a celestial body's gravitational field without further propulsion
minimum speed to escape gravity

Examples

The spacecraft needs to reach escape velocity to leave Earth's atmosphere and enter space.
Scientists calculated the escape velocity needed for a probe to depart the Moon's surface.
Achieving escape velocity is essential for any mission aiming to travel beyond our planet.

Synset Examples

Understanding escape velocity is key to space travel.
The rocket achieved escape velocity within minutes.
They calculated the escape velocity needed to leave Earth.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: terminal velocity - the constant speed an object reaches when falling through a fluid, when the force of gravity is balanced by resistance

Etymology

From 'escape' (to get free from) and 'velocity' (speed; from Latin 'velocitas').
