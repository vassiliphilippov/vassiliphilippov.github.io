Greek chorus

1. NOUN

/ ɡrik ˈkɔrəs / (AmE), / ɡriːk ˈkɔːrəs / (BrE)
(in ancient Greek drama) a group of performers who speak or sing in unison to comment on the main action of the play.
group of performers commenting in Greek drama

Examples

The Greek chorus provided the audience with background information as the tragedy unfolded.
In many productions, a modern equivalent of the Greek chorus is used to narrate the story.
Scholars often debate the exact function of the Greek chorus in classical theatre.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chorus - group of performers who comment on the action in a play

Etymology

From ancient Greek theatrical tradition; 'chorus' from Greek 'khoros' (group of singers and dancers).

2. NOUN

/ ɡrik ˈkɔɹ.əs / (AmE), / ɡriːk ˈkɔː.rəs / (BrE)
a group of performers in ancient Greek drama who comment on the main action of a play, or, by extension, a group of people who comment on or react to events as observers
group commenting in drama or as observers

Examples

The panel of experts acted as a Greek chorus, providing commentary on the unfolding debate.
During the performance, a Greek chorus narrated the hero's journey to the audience.
In the meeting, several participants became a Greek chorus, echoing the manager's concerns.

Synset Examples

In their discussions, they formed a Greek chorus, echoing each other's thoughts.
The audience felt like a Greek chorus, reacting in unison to the drama.
The critics acted like a Greek chorus, commenting on every scene.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chorus - a group of performers who comment on the action in a drama

Etymology

From Ancient Greek theatre, where a group (the chorus) narrated and commented on the play's action.
