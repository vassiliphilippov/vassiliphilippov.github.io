major premiss

1. NOUN

/ ˈmeɪdʒər ˈpriːmɪs / (AmE), / ˈmeɪdʒə ˈpriːmɪs / (BrE)
the premise in a syllogism that contains the major term, which is the predicate of the conclusion
premise in a syllogism with the major term

Examples

In a classic syllogism, the major premiss is 'All humans are mortal.'
You must first identify the major premiss before analyzing the logical structure.
The logician explained how the major premiss determines the scope of the argument.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: minor premiss - the premise of a syllogism containing the minor term (the subject of the conclusion)

Etymology

From Latin 'praemissa' (sent before) in logic, with 'major' indicating the more general or universal premise.
