serjeant-at-law

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɜrdʒənt æt ˈlɔ / (AmE), / ˌsɜːdʒənt ət ˈlɔː / (BrE)
a member of an order of barristers of the highest rank in England, formerly having exclusive rights of pleading in the Court of Common Pleas
English barrister of the highest rank (historical)
legal archaic british

Examples

The appointment of a serjeant-at-law was considered a high honor in medieval England.
Only a serjeant-at-law could plead in the Court of Common Pleas before reforms.
He wore the distinctive coif that marked him as a serjeant-at-law.

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *sergeaunt* and 'at law'; see 'sergeant' and 'law'.
