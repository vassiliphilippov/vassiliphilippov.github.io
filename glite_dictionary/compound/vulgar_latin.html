Vulgar Latin

1. NOUN

/ ˈvʌlɡər ˈlætɪn / (AmE), / ˈvʌlɡə ˈlætɪn / (BrE)
the nonclassical varieties of Latin spoken by the common people in the Roman Empire, from which the Romance languages developed
nonclassical Latin dialects of the Roman Empire

Examples

Modern Spanish and Italian both evolved from Vulgar Latin rather than from Classical Latin.
Pronunciation in Vulgar Latin differed significantly across regions of the Roman Empire.
Many everyday words in French can be traced directly to Vulgar Latin origins.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Classical Latin - the formal, literary form of Latin used in official and literary contexts
  • Degree Higher: Romance languages - languages that evolved from Vulgar Latin, including Spanish, French, and Italian

Etymology

From Latin 'vulgaris' (common) and 'Latinus' (Latin).
