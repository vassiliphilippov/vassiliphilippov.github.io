willy-nilly

1. ADV

/ ˌwɪliˈnɪli / (AmE), / ˌwɪliˈnɪli / (BrE)
Whether one likes it or not; without having a choice in the matter.
whether one wants to or not

Examples

The new policy was implemented willy-nilly, regardless of employees' opinions.
We had to evacuate willy-nilly when the alarm sounded, without time to prepare.
The town grew willy-nilly as people settled wherever they could find space.

Etymology

From the phrase 'will ye, nill ye' meaning 'whether you want to or not.'

2. ADV

/ ˌwɪliˈnɪli / (AmE), / ˌwɪliˈnɪli / (BrE)
whether one likes it or not; without choice or planning
without choice; whether one likes it or not

Examples

The company was forced to relocate willy-nilly after the building was sold.
New technologies are introduced willy-nilly, often before people are ready to adapt.
He found himself, willy-nilly, responsible for managing the entire team.

Synset Examples

Willy-nilly, he found himself involved in the project.
She had to move, willy-nilly, due to the job offer.
They had to accept the changes, willy-nilly.

Etymology

From a contraction of 'will I, nill I' (whether I want to or not), from Old English 'nyllan' (not will).
