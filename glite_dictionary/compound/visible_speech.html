visible speech

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɪzəbəl spiːtʃ / (AmE), / ˈvɪzɪbəl spiːtʃ / (BrE)
a phonetic alphabet invented by Melville Bell in the 19th century, designed to visually represent the position and movement of speech organs.
phonetic alphabet invented by Melville Bell

Examples

Linguists studied visible speech to understand how spoken sounds could be represented visually.
Melville Bell's invention of visible speech was a significant milestone in phonetics.
The teacher demonstrated visible speech symbols to show how each sound is formed.

Etymology

Coined in the 19th century by Alexander Melville Bell to describe his system for representing speech sounds visually.

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɪzəbəl spiːtʃ / (AmE), / ˈvɪzɪbəl spiːtʃ / (BrE)
A visual representation of speech sounds, especially a spectrogram showing the frequency and intensity of sounds over time.
visual representation of speech sounds

Examples

Linguists analyzed the visible speech of the recording to better understand pronunciation differences.
The teacher used visible speech software to help students see how their voices change.
By examining the visible speech, researchers could identify speech disorders more effectively.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spectrogram - a visual representation of the spectrum of frequencies in a sound signal as they vary with time
