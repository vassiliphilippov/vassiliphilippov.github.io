mutual inductance

1. NOUN

/ ˈmjuːtʃuəl ɪnˈdʌktəns / (AmE), / ˈmjuːtʃuəl ɪnˈdʌktəns / (BrE)
a measure of the ability of one electrical circuit to induce an electromotive force in another nearby circuit, expressed as the ratio of induced voltage to the rate of change of current; usually measured in henries
measure of electromagnetic induction between circuits

Examples

The mutual inductance between the two coils determines how much energy can be transferred.
Engineers calculate mutual inductance to predict the interaction between the circuits in a transformer.
Changes in the arrangement of the wires can significantly alter the mutual inductance.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-inductance - inductance of a single circuit causing an emf in itself

Etymology

From 'mutual' (shared by two or more) and 'inductance,' from Latin 'inducere' (to lead in).
