way station

1. NOUN

/ ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (BrE)
a small railway station situated between main stations, often where trains stop only if signaled or requested
minor railway station between main ones

Examples

The train halted briefly at the way station before continuing to the next major city.
She waited alone on the platform of a deserted way station late at night.
Many small towns have a way station that sees only a handful of trains each day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: station - a place where trains stop for passengers or goods

2. NOUN

/ ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (BrE)
A stopping place along a journey, often used for rest or changing transportation.
stopping place on a journey

Examples

The hikers spent the night at a way station before continuing up the mountain.
Our train stopped briefly at a small way station in the countryside.
The old barn once served as a way station for travelers riding stagecoaches.

Synset Examples

there is a stopover to change planes in Chicago

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stopover - a short stay between parts of a journey

Etymology

Formed from 'way' (travel, road) and 'station' (stopping place); first used in 19th-century American English.

3. NOUN

/ ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈweɪ ˌsteɪʃən / (BrE)
A small station or stopping place along a route, especially for travelers or vehicles, where they may pause before continuing their journey.
a stopping place along a route

Examples

The hikers rested at the way station before tackling the next stretch of their trip.
Our train made a brief stop at a small way station in the countryside.
The caravan used the desert way station to refill water and supplies.

Synset Examples

The train stopped at the way station for ten minutes.
We took a break at the way station before continuing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stopover - a short stay between parts of a journey
  • Antonym: terminus - the final stopping place on a route

Etymology

From "way" (road, path) and "station" (stopping place); first used in the U.S. in the 19th century, originally in reference to railroads.
