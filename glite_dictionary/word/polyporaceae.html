Polyporaceae

1. NOUN

/ ˌpɑːliˌpɔːˈreɪsi.iː / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪpɔːˈreɪsiː / (BrE)
a family of fungi that become corky or woody with age, often forming shelflike growths on trees
family of woody shelf fungi

Examples

The Polyporaceae are often seen growing on decaying logs in dense forests.
Some members of the Polyporaceae family are important in wood decomposition.
Mycologists study the Polyporaceae for their unique shelflike fruiting bodies.

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'poly-' (many) and 'poros' (pore), referring to the numerous pores on their fruiting bodies.

2. NOUN

/ pɑːliˌpɔːrˈeɪsi.iː / (AmE), / pɒlɪˌpɔːrəˈsiːiː / (BrE)
a family of fungi that become corky or woody as they age, often forming shelflike growths on trees
family of shelf-like woody fungi

Examples

Some fungi in the Polyporaceae family play an important role in decomposing wood in forests.
The Polyporaceae are commonly known as bracket fungi or shelf fungi due to their appearance on tree trunks.
Researchers studied the growth patterns of Polyporaceae to understand their effect on tree health.

Etymology

From New Latin, from genus Polyporus + -aceae (a suffix forming names of families).
