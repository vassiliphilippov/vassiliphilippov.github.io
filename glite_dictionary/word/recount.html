recount

1. NOUN

/ ˈriˌkaʊnt / (AmE), / ˈriːkaʊnt / (BrE)
an official second count, especially of votes in an election, typically conducted when results are very close or disputed
second count, especially of votes

Examples

After the election results were disputed, officials ordered a recount of the ballots.
The candidate requested a recount when the initial tally showed a margin of only 20 votes.
A full recount was conducted to ensure the accuracy of the close election outcome.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: count - the process of determining the total number of something

Etymology

From re- (again) + count (to enumerate).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈkaʊnt / (AmE), / rɪˈkaʊnt / (BrE)
to count something again, especially in order to check the accuracy of the count
count again, especially for accuracy

Examples

After a discrepancy was found, officials decided to recount the ballots.
The teacher asked the student to recount the books on the shelf to ensure the number was correct.
Due to the tight results, they had to recount the votes to confirm the outcome.

Synset Examples

We had to recount all the votes after an accusation of fraud was made

Related Terms

  • Synonym: count - to determine the total number of something

Etymology

From re- (again) + count, following standard prefixation in English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00803017-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈkaʊnt / (AmE), / rɪˈkaʊnt / (BrE)
to tell someone about something, especially something that you have experienced, in detail; to narrate or describe the facts of an event
to narrate or describe in detail

Examples

He recounted his adventures in the Amazon to an eager audience.
During the interview, she recounted every detail of the accident.
The witness recounts the events of that night in court.

Synset Examples

Tell what happened
The father told a story to his child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to narrate - to tell a story or describe a series of events
  • Synonym: to describe - to give an account in words of someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'reconter', based on Latin 'computare' (to calculate, reckon).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
