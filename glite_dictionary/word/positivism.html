positivism

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑs.ə.tɪˌvɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈpɒz.ɪ.tɪˌvɪz.əm / (BrE)
A quality or attitude characterized by certainty, acceptance, or confident affirmation, often involving dogmatic assertiveness.
attitude of certainty or affirmation

Examples

Her positivism helped the team face challenges with unwavering certainty.
The manager's positivism led to confident, affirmative decisions even in uncertain situations.
Some critics argue that extreme positivism can become close-minded and dismissive of valid doubts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assertiveness - quality of being self-assured and confident without being aggressive
  • Synonym: dogmatism - tendency to lay down principles as undeniably true

Etymology

From French *positivisme*, from Latin *positivus* (settled by agreement, positive).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːzətɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈpɒzɪtɪˌvɪzəm / (BrE)
A philosophical theory holding that all genuine knowledge is based on perceptual experience and empirical evidence, rather than on intuition or revelation.
philosophical view: knowledge from experience/evidence

Examples

The scientist defended positivism by insisting that only empirical evidence mattered in research.
Many philosophers criticized positivism for dismissing metaphysical speculation as meaningless.
Modern sociology was profoundly influenced by the principles of positivism in its early development.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: empiricism - the theory that all knowledge originates in experience
  • Antonym: idealism - the philosophical theory that reality is fundamentally mental, not material

Etymology

From French *positivisme*, coined by Auguste Comte, from Latin *positivus* (positively laid down).
