superposition

1. NOUN

/ ˌsuːpərpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəzɪˈʃən / (BrE)
In geology, the principle that in a sequence of stratified sedimentary rocks, the lowest layer is the oldest, with successively higher layers being progressively younger.
principle that lower strata are oldest

Examples

The law of superposition is fundamental for dating layers of sedimentary rock.
Geologists use superposition to determine the relative ages of different rock layers.
Without the principle of superposition, it would be difficult to interpret the geological history of an area.

Etymology

From Latin superpositio (a placing upon), from superponere (to place upon).

3. NOUN

/ ˌsuː.pɚ.pəˈzɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˌsuː.pə.pəˈzɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
(Geometry) The act of placing one object over another to show that the two coincide in position and shape.
placement of one object atop another to show alignment

Examples

In geometry, superposition is used to prove that two triangles are congruent by placing one over the other.
The superposition of two shapes helps students understand their similarities and differences.
To solve the problem, he used superposition to demonstrate that both angles were identical.
