wound

1. VERB

/ wund / (AmE), / wuːnd / (BrE)
to injure someone physically, especially by cutting or breaking the skin
to injure by cutting or breaking skin

Examples

He was badly wounded in the accident but managed to survive.
The sharp edge of the metal wounded her arm during the fall.
Words can wound as deeply as physical blows.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to injure - to harm or damage someone physically
  • Antonym: to heal - to become healthy again; to recover from a wound or injury

Etymology

From Old English 'wundian' (to injure), related to 'wund' (wound, injury).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00069650-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ wund / (AmE), / wuːnd / (BrE)
a feeling of intense hurt or pain caused by an emotional injury, especially to one’s pride or feelings
emotional or figurative injury

Examples

His harsh comments left a wound that was slow to heal.
Even after many years, the wound of betrayal remained fresh.
Apologizing does not always close the wound caused by a broken friendship.

Synset Examples

he feared that mentioning it might reopen the wound
deep in her breast lives the silent wound
The right reader of a good poem can tell the moment it strikes him that he has taken an immortal wound — that he will never get over it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurt - emotional or psychological pain
  • Synonym: trauma - emotional or psychological injury
  • Synonym: insult - emotionally damaging remark or action

Etymology

Old English 'wund' (wound, injury), related to Proto-Germanic forms.

5. NOUN

/ wund / (AmE), / wuːnd / (BrE)

Examples

The soldier suffered a serious wound in combat but survived thanks to prompt medical attention.

He was awarded a medal for bravery after receiving a wound while protecting his fellow soldiers.

Examples

Try not to wound someone with careless remarks.

Related Terms
