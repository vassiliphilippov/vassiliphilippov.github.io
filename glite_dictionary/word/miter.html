miter

1. NOUN

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
A tall, pointed headdress with two peaks and two hanging bands, worn by bishops and certain abbots during formal liturgical ceremonies.
bishop's ceremonial headdress

Examples

During the ceremony, the bishop placed the miter on his head as a symbol of authority.
The ornate embroidery on the miter reflected the importance of the occasion.
Several photographs captured the cardinal in full regalia, including his golden miter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mitre - alternative British spelling and form of miter

Etymology

From Greek 'mitra' (headband, turban) via Latin 'mitra'.

2. VERB

/ ˈmaɪtɚ / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
to join two pieces of material, especially wood, by cutting their ends at an angle and fitting them together to form a miter joint
to fit pieces together at an angle (miter joint)

Examples

The carpenter carefully mitered the corners of the picture frame for a seamless fit.
You need to miter the ends of the molding before attaching them.
They spent the afternoon mitering the boards for the new door frame.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: join - to connect or fasten things together

3. VERB

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
to bevel the edges of two pieces (usually wood or metal) so that they fit together at an angle, typically to form a miter joint
bevel to form a miter joint

Examples

The carpenter needed to miter the ends of the molding to create a corner joint.
To improve the frame's appearance, they decided to miter each of its corners.
She carefully measured and mitered the boards so they would fit together seamlessly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bevel - to cut or shape at an angle other than 90°

Etymology

From Old French mitre, influenced by the shape of a bishop's headdress, ultimately from Greek mitra (headband, turban).

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
the beveled surface at the end of a piece, prepared to form a miter joint with another piece
beveled end surface for a joint

5. NOUN

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
a joint made between two surfaces that are beveled, usually at a 45-degree angle, so as to form a right angle, commonly used in woodworking and carpentry
a bevelled joint forming a corner

Examples

The picture frame was constructed with perfectly cut miter joints at each corner.
He learned how to make a miter joint in his woodworking class.
A flawed miter can result in a gap at the corner of the finished product.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: butt joint - a joint made by placing two pieces of wood end to end without any special shaping

Etymology

From Old French *mitre*, from Latin *mitra* (cap, headdress), from Greek *mítra* (headband), probably influenced in English by the appearance of a mitre (headgear) resembling the angle of the joint.

6. VERB

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
to confer a miter on (a bishop), especially at the time of consecration; to make someone a bishop.
confer a miter on a bishop

Examples

The archbishop will miter the newly appointed clergyman during Sunday's service.
It is a great honor to be mitered as a bishop in the cathedral.
The ceremony to miter the candidate was attended by bishops from across the country.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consecrate - to make or declare someone sacred, especially by a religious ceremony

Etymology

Related to the noun 'miter', from Old French 'mitre', from Latin 'mitra' (headdress).
