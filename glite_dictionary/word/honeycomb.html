honeycomb

1. NOUN

/ ˈhʌniˌkoʊm / (AmE), / ˈhʌniˌkəʊm / (BrE)
a structure of hexagonal cells made by bees from wax, used to store honey and larvae
beeswax structure of hexagonal cells

Examples

The bees built a new honeycomb in the corner of the hive.
Fresh honeycomb can be eaten as a sweet treat or spread on toast.
She marveled at the perfect symmetry of the honeycomb filled with golden honey.

Etymology

From Old English 'hunigcamb', from 'hunig' (honey) + 'camb' (comb).

2. NOUN

/ ˈhʌniˌkoʊm / (AmE), / ˈhʌnɪkəʊm / (BrE)
a structure consisting of a pattern of closely spaced hexagonal cells, typically resembling the wax comb built by bees, or any framework that imitates this pattern.
structure of hexagonal cells

Examples

The lightweight panels have a honeycomb structure for added strength.
Engineers studied the honeycomb pattern to design more efficient building materials.
The artist carved a honeycomb motif into the wooden surface.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lattice - a regular arrangement of points or cells in space

Etymology

From Old English 'hunigcamb', from 'hunig' (honey) + 'camb' (comb).

3. VERB

/ ˈhʌniˌkoʊm / (AmE), / ˈhʌniˌkəʊm / (BrE)
to fill or penetrate thoroughly, especially so as to create a structure full of holes or areas; to infiltrate every part of something
penetrate or fill every part thoroughly

Examples

Corruption has honeycombed the administration at every level.
Worms had honeycombed the old piece of wood, leaving tiny holes everywhere.
The revolutionary group honeycombed the army ranks with its secret members.

Synset Examples

the revolutionaries honeycombed the organization

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to permeate - to spread or seep throughout
  • Synonym: to infiltrate - to enter or gain access to, especially gradually or stealthily

Etymology

From Old English 'hunigcamb' (honeycomb), referring originally to the structure made by bees.
