muck

1. VERB

/ mʌk / (AmE), / mʌk / (BrE)
to make something dirty with mud or filth
make dirty with mud or filth

Examples

The boys mucked their shoes tramping through the puddles.
Be careful not to muck the floor with your muddy boots.
She mucked up her dress after running through the wet grass.

Synset Examples

The child mucked up his shirt while playing ball in the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dirty - to make something dirty; to soil
  • Antonym: clean - to make something clean; to remove dirt or stains
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ mʌk / (AmE), / mʌk / (BrE)
to spread manure on land to fertilize it
spread manure on land

Examples

Every spring, the farmers muck the fields to prepare for planting.
They mucked the vegetable patch with composted manure last weekend.
Before the crops were sown, the laborers spent hours mucking the soil.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manure - animal dung used for fertilizing land
  • Synonym: to fertilize - to add nutrients to soil for plant growth
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02087334-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ mʌk / (AmE), / mʌk / (BrE)
any thick, wet, and sticky matter, especially something unpleasant or dirty
thick, sticky, often dirty matter

Examples

The boots were covered in muck after walking through the swamp.
Please wipe that muck off the kitchen floor before it dries.
Thick muck clogged the drain, making the water back up.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sludge - thick, soft, wet mud or a similar viscous mixture
  • Synonym: filth - disgusting dirt or refuse
  • Antonym: cleanliness - the state of being clean, free from dirt or impurities

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *muk*, from Old Norse *myki* (dung).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ mʌk / (AmE), / mʌk / (BrE)
to remove dirt, waste, or refuse, especially from a mine or similar place
clear dirt or waste, esp. in mining

Examples

It took all morning to muck out the old coal mine.
The workers were assigned to muck the debris from the tunnel.
After the blasting, teams entered the shaft to muck the loose rubble.

Etymology

From Middle English *muk*, of Scandinavian origin; related to Swedish *mök* (‘dirt’).

5. NOUN

/ mʌk / (AmE), / mʌk / (BrE)
wet dirt, especially consisting of manure or animal feces, often found on farms or in stables.
animal fecal matter or manure

Examples

After cleaning the stable, my boots were covered in muck.
The farmer spread the muck over the fields as fertilizer.
Heavy rains turned the barnyard into a sea of muck.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manure - animal dung used for fertilizing land
  • Degree Lower: filth - disgusting dirt or refuse

Etymology

From Middle English ‘muk’, from Old Norse ‘myki’ (dung).
