to reckon

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.ən / (BrE)
to think or believe something; to consider or regard in a particular way
to consider or believe

Examples

I reckon it's going to rain later based on those dark clouds.
Most people reckon him to be the best player on the team.
She doesn't reckon this plan will work without more support.

Synset Examples

She views this quite differently from me
I consider her to be shallow
I don't see the situation quite as negatively as you do

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to consider - to think about or regard someone or something in a particular way
  • Synonym: to deem - to regard or judge in a particular way

Etymology

From Old English *gerecenian* (to explain, recount), related to Middle Dutch *rekenen* (to count).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00692380-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈrɛkən / (AmE), / ˈrɛkən / (BrE)
to make a mathematical calculation or computation; to count or estimate numerically
to calculate or count

Examples

It took her only a moment to reckon the total cost of the groceries.
He paused to reckon the distance they had traveled so far.
The accountants must reckon every expense before closing the accounts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to calculate - to determine mathematically
  • Synonym: estimate - to roughly calculate or judge the value, number, or amount

Etymology

From Old English *recenian* (to explain, relate, recount), influenced by Old Norse *reikna* (to count, calculate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00638921-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.ən / (BrE)
to think or believe that something is likely to be true; to judge to be probable
judge to be probable

Examples

I reckon it will rain before the afternoon is over.
Many people reckon that the new law will benefit large companies.
She reckoned him to be about forty years old.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppose - to assume something to be likely or true
  • Antonym: doubt - to feel uncertain or not believe something is true

Etymology

From Old English *recenian* (explain, recount), reinforced by Old Norse *rekna* (to count, reckon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00713964-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈrɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.ən / (BrE)
to consider or take into account something, especially as a factor to be dealt with; to regard as necessary to address or handle
take into account; consider (as a factor)

Examples

We must reckon with the unpredictable effects of climate change.
The company failed to reckon with public opinion when launching the new product.
Politicians often have to reckon with opposition from various groups.

Synset Examples

Count on the monsoon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to consider - to think about carefully, especially to make a decision
  • Antonym: to ignore - to deliberately pay no attention to

Etymology

From Old English *gerecenian* (to relate, recount).

5. VERB

/ ˈrɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.ən / (BrE)
to think or believe that something is true or likely; to suppose
think or believe; suppose

Examples

I reckon it will rain later this afternoon, so bring an umbrella just in case.
Do you reckon she will accept the job offer?
Most people reckon that house prices will keep rising for the foreseeable future.

Synset Examples

I imagine she earned a lot of money with her new novel
I thought to find her in a bad state
he didn't think to find her in the kitchen
I guess she is angry at me for standing her up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppose - assume something is true without certain proof
  • Synonym: guess - form an opinion without sufficient information
  • Antonym: doubt - feel uncertain or lack belief

Etymology

From Old English *ge-rec(c)an* (to explain, relate); influenced by Old Norse *rekja* (to recount).
