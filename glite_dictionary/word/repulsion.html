repulsion

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
A strong feeling of dislike or disgust.
intense aversion or disgust

Examples

She felt a strong repulsion toward the idea of eating insects.
His cruelty filled her with immediate repulsion.
There was a sense of repulsion in the room after the shocking news was announced.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aversion - a strong feeling of dislike or unwillingness
  • Antonym: attraction - the action or power of evoking interest, pleasure, or liking

Etymology

From Latin 'repulsio' (a driving back), from 'repellere' (to drive back).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
The act of repulsing or driving back an attack; a successful defensive stand against an aggressor.
act of driving back an attack

Examples

The fort's soldiers made a determined repulsion of the enemy at dawn.
Their repulsion of the attack saved the city from destruction.
After several hours, the defenders' fierce repulsion forced the invaders to retreat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defense - the act of protecting against attack
  • Antonym: retreat - the act of withdrawing an attacking force

Etymology

From Latin 'repulsio' (a driving back), from 'repellere' (to drive back).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
the force that makes bodies or objects move away from each other when they are nearby
force causing objects to repel each other

Examples

The repulsion between the magnets prevented them from sticking together.
Electrostatic repulsion keeps similarly charged particles apart.
Scientists measured the repulsion produced by the two wires carrying similar currents.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: attraction - the force by which bodies are drawn together
  • Synonym: to repel - to drive or force something away; to cause repulsion

Etymology

From Latin 'repulsio' (driving back), from 'repellere' (to drive back).
