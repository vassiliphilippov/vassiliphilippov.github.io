lust

1. NOUN

/ lʌst / (AmE), / lʌst / (BrE)
Strong and intense sexual desire, often considered excessive or self-indulgent and traditionally classified as one of the seven deadly sins.
strong sexual desire; one of the deadly sins

Examples

He warned against the dangers of lust and other sinful urges.
The medieval church considered lust to be one of the gravest sins.
Her novel explores the conflict between love and lust in human relationships.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desire - a strong feeling of wanting something
  • Antonym: chastity - the practice of refraining from sexual activity

Etymology

From Old English 'lust' (desire, pleasure), related to Old High German 'lust'.

2. NOUN

/ lʌst / (AmE), / lʌst / (BrE)
very strong sexual desire; intense or uncontrolled craving for sexual gratification
strong sexual desire

Examples

He tried to hide his lust for his coworker, fearing it would ruin their friendship.
The novel explores themes of lust and betrayal among its main characters.
She realized that what she felt was mere lust, not love.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desire - a strong feeling of wanting to have something
  • Synonym: passion - strong and barely controllable emotion or desire
  • Antonym: love - an intense feeling of deep affection
  • Antonym: chastity - the practice of refraining from sexual activity

Etymology

From Old English lust (desire, pleasure), from Proto-Germanic *lustuz (pleasure, desire).

3. VERB

/ lʌst / (AmE), / lʌst / (BrE)
to have a strong craving or intense desire for something, especially something forbidden or unattainable
have a strong craving or desire

Examples

After months of dieting, she would lust after chocolate and sweets.
The explorer could not help but lust for adventure in unknown lands.
He found himself lusting for power, despite his earlier humility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to crave - to feel a powerful desire for something
  • Synonym: to yearn - to have an intense feeling of longing for something

Etymology

From Old English *lust* (desire, pleasure), from Proto-Germanic *lustuz* (pleasure, desire).
