cross-pollination

1. NOUN

/ krɑs ˌpɑlɪˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / krɒs ˌpɒlɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
The transfer of pollen from the anther of one flower to the stigma of another, resulting in fertilization.
pollination between different flowers

Examples

Many fruit trees require cross-pollination to produce a good harvest of apples.
Gardeners often encourage cross-pollination to improve the genetic diversity of their flowers.
Bees and other insects play a crucial role in cross-pollination between different plants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-pollination - pollination within the same flower or plant

Etymology

Formed from 'cross-' (between different individuals) and 'pollination' (from Latin *pollen*, fine flour, dust).

2. NOUN

/ krɔs ˌpɑləˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / krɒs ˌpɒlɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
The process of introducing ideas or influences from different fields, people, or sources in order to create something new or foster innovation.
mixing of diverse ideas or influences

Examples

The cross-pollination of ideas from different disciplines led to a groundbreaking invention.
The company's success comes from the cross-pollination of talent from various cultural backgrounds.
Artists benefit greatly from the cross-pollination that happens when different creative circles interact.

Synset Examples

the cross-pollination of the arts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fertilization - the action of fertilizing an egg or plant
  • Antonym: inbreeding - reproduction from closely related individuals, especially in plants or animals

Etymology

Originally from pollination in botany, from Latin 'pollinatio' (fertilization by pollen).
