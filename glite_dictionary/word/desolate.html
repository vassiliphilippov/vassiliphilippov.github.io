desolate

1. VERB

/ ˈdɛsəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɛsələt / (BrE)
to reduce the population of an area, often leaving it empty or nearly empty, especially as a result of disaster or abandonment
to make a place empty or sparsely populated

Examples

The war threatened to desolate entire villages, driving many residents away.
Repeated droughts would eventually desolate the once thriving farmland.
The pandemic began to desolate the remote province, leaving it almost deserted.

Synset Examples

The epidemic depopulated the countryside

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to depopulate - to reduce the population of

Etymology

From Latin desolatus, past participle of desolare (to leave alone, to abandon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00532382-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ ˈdɛs.ə.lət / (AmE), / ˈdɛs.əl.ət / (BrE)
feeling or showing great unhappiness or loneliness, especially due to grief or abandonment
deeply unhappy or lonely due to grief

Examples

After the tragedy, she felt utterly desolate and lost.
The abandoned puppy looked desolate as it wandered through the empty streets.
He sat in his room, desolate and overwhelmed by grief.

Synset Examples

depressed and desolate of soul
a low desolate wail

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forlorn - pitifully sad and abandoned or lonely
  • Antonym: cheerful - noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

From Latin 'desolatus' (abandoned, left alone), past participle of 'desolare' (to leave alone).

/ ˈdɛsələt / (AmE), / ˈdɛsələt / (BrE)
(of a place) empty, bleak, and providing no shelter or sustenance; deserted and inhospitable.
bleak and uninhabited (of places)

Examples

The explorers struggled to cross the desolate desert, where nothing grew and there was no shelter.
Standing on the desolate shore, she felt utterly alone.

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin *desolatus*, past participle of *desolare* (to abandon, lay waste).
