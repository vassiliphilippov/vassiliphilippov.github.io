vamp

1. NOUN

/ væmp / (AmE), / væmp / (BrE)
a sexually attractive woman who uses her charm to seduce and exploit men, often for personal gain
seductive woman who exploits men

Examples

In the classic film, she played a vamp who manipulated every man she met.
Rumors spread quickly that the new actress was a real-life vamp, always at the center of scandal.
Writers often portrayed strong, independent women as vamps in early Hollywood movies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: femme fatale - an attractive and seductive woman, especially one who brings disaster to a man

Etymology

Shortened from 'vampire' in early 20th-century slang, referencing the femme fatale archetype.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ væmp / (AmE), / væmp / (BrE)
a short, simple musical phrase or accompaniment that is repeated and often improvised, typically to fill time or provide a backing for a soloist or singer
repeated improvised musical accompaniment

Examples

The pianist played a vamp while the singer found her place in the melody.
During the jazz session, the band improvised a bluesy vamp to keep the energy up.
He asked the guitarist to vamp until the rest of the band was ready.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: riff - a short repeated phrase in popular music and jazz, typically used as an accompaniment or introduction

Etymology

From jazz and popular music, origin uncertain, possibly related to the earlier sense 'to patch up' (as in shoes) or from the French phrase 'en vamp' (for shoe upper).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ væmp / (AmE), / væmp / (BrE)
the front part of the upper section of a shoe, typically made of leather

Examples

The shoemaker replaced the worn vamp with a new piece of leather.
Water had stained the vamp of his favorite shoes.
A scuffed vamp can often be restored with polish and care.

Etymology

From Middle English *vampe*, from Old French *avantpied* (forefoot), from *avant* (before) + *pied* (foot).
