prune

1. NOUN

/ pruːn / (AmE), / pruːn / (BrE)
a dried plum, typically eaten as a snack or used in cooking
dried plum

Examples

She ate a prune with her breakfast for extra fiber.
Dried prune pieces add a sweet taste to oatmeal cookies.
The nutritionist recommended including prunes in her diet for better digestion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plum - a soft, sweet, purple or red fruit with a stone

Etymology

From Old French 'prune', from Latin 'prūnum' (plum).

2. VERB

/ pruːn / (AmE), / pruːn / (BrE)
to remove unwanted or unnecessary parts from something, especially to improve its quality or efficiency
remove unwanted parts

Examples

It's time to prune your email inbox and delete old, unnecessary messages.
The company decided to prune several underperforming products from its lineup.
She regularly prunes her to-do list to stay focused on what matters most.

Synset Examples

We had to lose weight, so we cut the sugar from our diet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trim - to cut away unwanted parts, especially from a plant or hair
  • Synonym: cut - to reduce or remove by cutting
  • Antonym: to add - to put something together with something else; the opposite of remove

3. VERB

/ pruːn / (AmE), / pruːn / (BrE)
to trim or cut back the branches or stems of a plant to improve its shape, growth, or health
trim a plant by cutting branches or stems

Examples

It is important to prune roses in early spring for better blooms.
Every year, the gardener prunes the old apple trees to encourage new growth.
She spent the afternoon pruning the hedges into neat, straight lines.

Synset Examples

dress the plants in the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trim - to make something neat or of the required size or form by cutting away irregular or unwanted parts
  • Synonym: clip - to cut or remove a small part, especially to tidy plants or hair

Etymology

From Old French 'proignier' (to cut or lop off), from Vulgar Latin 'protinare' (to cut off).
