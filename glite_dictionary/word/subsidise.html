to subsidise

1. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.sɪˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.sɪ.daɪz / (BrE)
to give financial assistance to an organization, activity, or country, often to help it continue operating or to support its interests
provide financial support

Examples

The government plans to subsidise local farmers to help increase food production.
Many countries subsidise their national airlines to maintain international routes.
The state agreed to subsidise the construction of new affordable housing units.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sponsor - to support financially, especially in business or the arts
  • Synonym: finance - to provide money for a purpose
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to stop giving support, aid, or resources

Etymology

From Latin subsidium (support, aid) via French and Medieval Latin subsīdium.

2. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.sɪˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.sɪ.daɪz / (BrE)
to support financially by providing a subsidy, especially from a government or authority
provide financial support through subsidies

Examples

The government decided to subsidise local farmers to help them compete with imports.
Many European countries subsidise public transportation to make it more affordable for citizens.
If we subsidise the industry, it could save thousands of jobs.

Synset Examples

The arts in Europe are heavily subsidized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - give assistance or help to, especially financially
  • Synonym: to subsidize - American English spelling of 'subsidise'
  • Antonym: to withdraw support - to stop providing financial or other assistance

Etymology

From Latin 'subsidium' (support, assistance), via French 'subsidier'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
