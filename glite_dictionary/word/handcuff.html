handcuff

1. NOUN

/ ˈhændˌkʌf / (AmE), / ˈhændˌkʌf / (BrE)
A metal fastening that can be locked around a person's wrist, typically used in pairs to restrain someone, especially by police.
locking restraint for wrist

Examples

The police officer quickly snapped a handcuff onto the suspect's wrist.
After searching his pockets, she removed the handcuff and let him go.
He wore a single handcuff as a prop at the costume party.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shackle - a device used to restrain the movement of a prisoner

Etymology

From 'hand' + 'cuff', reflecting the function of binding the hand at the wrist.

2. VERB

/ ˈhændˌkʌf / (AmE), / ˈhandkʌf / (BrE)
to confine or restrain someone by fastening their wrists together with handcuffs
fasten someone's wrists with handcuffs

Examples

The police handcuffed the suspect before placing him in the patrol car.
She was handcuffed to the chair during the intense interrogation scene.
Security guards handcuffed the man after he tried to escape.

Synset Examples

The police handcuffed the suspect at the scene of the crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shackle - to restrain with chains or shackles
  • Antonym: free - to release from confinement or restraint

3. VERB

/ ˈhændˌkʌf / (AmE), / ˈhændˌkʌf / (BrE)
to restrain someone by fastening their wrists together with handcuffs, typically used by police when arresting a suspect
to restrain with handcuffs

Examples

The police had to handcuff the suspect to ensure everyone's safety.
After the arrest, the officer handcuffed the man behind his back.
She watched as they handcuffed the suspect and read him his rights.

Synset Examples

The officer had to handcuff the suspect after the chase.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shackle - to restrain with chains or irons, typically the legs
  • Antonym: to unbind - to release from restraints or bonds

Etymology

From Middle English 'hand' + 'cuff', meaning a covering for the wrist or end of the sleeve; later applied to manacles.
