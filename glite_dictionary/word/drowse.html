drowse

1. VERB

/ draʊz / (AmE), / draʊz / (BrE)
to sleep lightly or for a short period, often while remaining partially aware of one's surroundings
sleep lightly; doze

Examples

She began to drowse in her chair during the long meeting.
The warm afternoon sun made him drowse by the window.
I tend to drowse on trains, even if I'm not tired.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doze - to sleep lightly and briefly, especially during the day
  • Degree Higher: slumber - to sleep

Etymology

Of uncertain origin, perhaps related to Middle English 'drusen' (to be sluggish, inactive).

2. NOUN

/ draʊz / (AmE), / draʊz / (BrE)
A light or fitful sleep; a state of being partially asleep.
a light, fitful sleep
archaic

Examples

After lunch, he fell into a drowse by the window.
The long lecture sent her into a quiet drowse.
A gentle breeze and warm sunlight lulled him into a peaceful drowse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nap - a short period of sleep, especially during the day

Etymology

Related to the verb 'drowse', likely from Middle English 'drous' (inclined to sleep).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00860527-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ draʊz / (AmE), / draʊz / (BrE)
to be half asleep or on the verge of sleeping; to fall into a light sleep
be half asleep; doze

Examples

She began to drowse as the train gently rocked back and forth.
The professor noticed a few students drowsing in the warm lecture hall.
After lunch, he often drowses at his desk for a few minutes.

Synset Examples

The students were drowsing in the 8 AM class

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doze - sleep lightly or nap, especially briefly

Etymology

From Middle English *drousen*, probably from Old Norse *drausna* (to be sluggish).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
