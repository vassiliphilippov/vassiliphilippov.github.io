to animalize

1. VERB

/ ˈæn.ə.məˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈæn.ɪ.mə.laɪz / (BrE)
to represent or portray something as an animal or in the form of an animal
represent in the form of an animal

Examples

The artist chose to animalize the characters, giving them wolf-like features.
In ancient mythology, people often animalized gods to explain natural phenomena.
The cartoon series animalizes everyday objects, turning them into talking animals.

Etymology

From Latin 'animalis' (having breath, living being) + English suffix '-ize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01684651-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈænɪməˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈænɪməlaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something brutal, unfeeling, or inhuman; to deprive of human qualities
make brutal or inhuman

Examples

The cruel conditions tended to animalize the prisoners over time.
Political propaganda sometimes aims to animalize the enemy in the public's eyes.
War can animalize even the most gentle of people.

Synset Examples

Life in the camps had brutalized him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to brutalize - make cruel, harsh, or inhuman
  • Synonym: to dehumanize - deprive of human qualities

Etymology

From French 'animaliser', from Latin 'animalis' (animal) + '-ize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00113860-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈænɪm(ə)ˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈænɪməlaɪz / (BrE)
to make or become brutal, insensitive, or lacking in human feeling or refinement
make or become brutal or insensitive

Examples

The harsh conditions seemed to animalize the prisoners, stripping them of compassion.
War can animalize even the most gentle souls, hardening their hearts.
Without guidance, cruelty and neglect will quickly animalize a child's temperament.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dehumanize - to deprive of human qualities

Etymology

From French animaliser, based on animal.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00113661-v
Synset POS: v
