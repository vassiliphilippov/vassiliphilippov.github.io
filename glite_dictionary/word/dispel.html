to dispel

1. VERB

/ dɪˈspɛl / (AmE), / dɪˈspɛl / (BrE)
to make something, such as a feeling, idea, or group, disappear or break up, especially by scattering it in different directions
make something disappear or scatter

Examples

The morning sunlight helped to dispel the thick fog surrounding the city.
She tried to dispel her worries before the important meeting.
The leader’s calm words began to dispel the fear among the crowd.

Synset Examples

She waved her hand and scattered the crowds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or separate things in different directions
  • Antonym: gather - to come together or collect into one group or place

Etymology

From Latin 'dispellere', from 'dis-' (apart) + 'pellere' (to drive).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈspɛl / (AmE), / dɪˈspɛl / (BrE)
to make something, especially a feeling or belief, disappear or go away
make something disappear

Examples

The new evidence helped to dispel many of their fears.
A good night's sleep can often dispel feelings of anxiety.
She tried to dispel the rumors with a public statement.

Synset Examples

Drive away potential burglars
drive away bad thoughts
dispel doubts
The supermarket had to turn back many disappointed customers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to banish - send something or someone away, especially officially or forcefully
  • Synonym: to disperse - to scatter or spread things or people over a wide area
  • Antonym: to summon - call for the presence of; to bring to mind or into being

Etymology

From Latin dispellere (to drive apart, scatter), from dis- (apart) + pellere (to drive).
