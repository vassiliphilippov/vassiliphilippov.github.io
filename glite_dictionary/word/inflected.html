inflected

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈflɛktɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈflɛktɪd / (BrE)
(of a word or language) showing changes in form, especially by the addition of prefixes or suffixes to indicate grammatical functions such as tense, case, or number.
showing grammatical changes in form

Examples

Latin is a highly inflected language in which word endings reveal grammatical roles.
The verb 'to be' has many inflected forms in English, such as 'am,' 'is,' and 'are.'
He could recognize the inflected endings that indicated past tense in the foreign language class.

Synset Examples

‘boys’ and ‘swam’ are inflected English words
German is an inflected language

Related Terms

  • Antonym: uninflected - (of a word or language) not showing inflection; having a fixed form

Etymology

From Latin 'inflectere' meaning 'to bend in, curve.'

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈflɛktɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈflɛktɪd / (BrE)
(of the voice) varied or altered in tone or pitch.
voice: altered in tone or pitch

Examples

Her voice was softly inflected with amusement as she told the story.
He spoke in an inflected tone that revealed his excitement.
The singer's inflected words gave the song an emotional depth.

Synset Examples

his southern Yorkshire voice was less inflected and singing than her northern one

Etymology

From Latin 'inflectere' meaning 'to bend in' or 'curve'.
