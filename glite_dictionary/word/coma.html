coma

1. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.mə / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.mə / (BrE)
a luminous cloud of gas and dust that surrounds the nucleus of a comet, forming as the comet approaches the sun
cloud of gas around a comet's nucleus

Examples

The coma of the comet became brighter as it neared the sun.
Astronomers studied the chemicals present in the coma.
A comet's coma can extend for thousands of kilometers in space.

Etymology

From Greek kōmē (hair), originally referring to the comet's 'hairy' appearance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊmə / (AmE), / ˈkəʊmə / (BrE)
(Botany) A tuft of bracts or hairs, especially one at the tip of a plant organ such as a seed or fruit.
terminal tuft of bracts or hairs in plants

Examples

The dandelion seed's coma helps it to float gracefully on the wind.
In pineapples, the coma consists of a tuft of leafy bracts at the fruit's apex.
Many seeds disperse more easily because of the silky coma attached to them.

Etymology

From Latin *coma* (hair of the head, foliage of a plant), from Greek *kōmē* (hair, foliage).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.mə / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.mə / (BrE)
a state of deep unconsciousness, usually caused by illness or injury, from which a person cannot be awakened
state of deep unconsciousness
medical

Examples

After the accident, he was in a coma for six weeks before regaining consciousness.
Doctors are closely monitoring her coma to determine if there are any signs of improvement.
A severe infection can sometimes lead to a prolonged coma if it affects the brain.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: unconsciousness - the state of not being conscious, insensible
  • Degree Higher: catatonia - a state of psychomotor immobility and behavioral abnormality

Etymology

From Greek kōma (deep sleep, trance).
