ferment

1. NOUN

/ fərˈmɛnt / (AmE), / fəˈmɛnt / (BrE)
a substance, such as an enzyme or microorganism, that causes fermentation
substance that causes fermentation

Examples

The brewer added a special ferment to the mixture to help convert sugar into alcohol.
Early scientists discovered that yeast acted as a natural ferment in bread making.
Without the right ferment, the process of making cheese would not succeed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enzyme - a protein that acts as a catalyst in living organisms

Etymology

From Latin fermentum (leaven, yeast), from fervere (to boil, seethe).

2. NOUN

/ fərˈmɛnt / (AmE), / fəˈmɛnt / (BrE)
a process in which an agent, such as yeast or bacteria, causes an organic substance to break down into simpler substances, especially the anaerobic conversion of sugar into alcohol.
anaerobic breakdown of organic substances, esp. sugar to alcohol

Examples

Yeast initiates the ferment that transforms grape juice into wine.
The natural ferment of dough gives sourdough bread its distinctive flavor.
During the ferment, sugars are converted into alcohol and carbon dioxide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fermentation - the chemical process of breaking down substances by bacteria, yeasts, or other microorganisms

Etymology

From Latin 'fermentum' (leaven, yeast), from 'fervere' (to boil).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13596636-n
Synset POS: n
