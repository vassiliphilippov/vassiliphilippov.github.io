proconsul

1. NOUN

/ proʊˈkɑn.səl / (AmE), / prəʊˈkɒn.səl / (BrE)
A governor of a province in the Roman Republic or Roman Empire, with authority equivalent to that of a consul.
Roman provincial governor of consular rank

Examples

Julius Caesar once served as a proconsul in Gaul before becoming dictator of Rome.
The Senate appointed him proconsul of Asia following his distinguished military service.
Historians often study the powers and responsibilities given to a Roman proconsul.

Etymology

From Latin 'proconsul', from 'pro' (on behalf of) and 'consul' (highest magistrate).

2. NOUN

/ proʊˈkɑːn.səl / (AmE), / prəʊˈkɒn.səl / (BrE)
an official, especially in a colony or occupied territory, who exercises significant administrative or governing authority, often on behalf of a foreign power or central government
powerful colonial or governing official

Examples

The British appointed a proconsul to oversee the administration of the newly acquired territory.
Many saw the American administrator as a modern-day proconsul, wielding authority over the occupied country.
During the colonial period, a proconsul often made decisions without local input.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: governor - official who governs a colony or territory
  • Antonym: consul - an official appointed by a government to live in a foreign city and protect its interests

Etymology

From Latin 'proconsul', meaning someone acting in place of a consul.

3. NOUN

/ proʊˈkɑːn.səl / (AmE), / prəʊˈkɒn.səl / (BrE)
a genus of extinct primates regarded as early ancestors of modern apes and humans, known from fossils found in Africa
genus of extinct apes

Examples

Fossils of proconsul have been discovered in East Africa.
The anatomy of proconsul suggests it was well adapted for life in the trees.
Scientists believe that proconsul bridges the evolutionary gap between monkeys and apes.

Etymology

From Neo-Latin Proconsul, naming the genus by analogy with the name for a Roman official.
