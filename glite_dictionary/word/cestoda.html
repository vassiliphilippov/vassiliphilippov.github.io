Cestoda

1. NOUN

/ sɛˈstoʊdə / (AmE), / sɛˈstəʊdə / (BrE)
A class of parasitic flatworms, commonly known as tapeworms, that live in the digestive tracts of vertebrates.
class of parasitic flatworms (tapeworms)

Examples

Cestoda are known for their ability to absorb nutrients directly through their body surface.
In zoology class, we studied the life cycle of Cestoda species that infect mammals.
Infections caused by Cestoda can lead to serious health problems in both livestock and humans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tapeworm - a flat, ribbon-like parasitic worm from the class Cestoda
  • Antonym: Trematoda - a class of parasitic flatworms commonly known as flukes

Etymology

From modern Latin, from Greek kēstōdēs (girdle-like), from kēstē (girdle).

2. NOUN

/ sɛsˈtoʊdə / (AmE), / sɛsˈtəʊdə / (BrE)
A class of parasitic flatworms commonly known as tapeworms, which live in the digestive tracts of vertebrates.
a class of parasitic flatworms (tapeworms)
medical

Examples

Members of the Cestoda class, such as tapeworms, often infect mammals and birds.
Research on Cestoda has led to advancements in the treatment of parasitic diseases.
The biology course included a section on the structure and lifecycle of Cestoda.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tapeworm - common name for members of the class Cestoda
  • Degree Lower: Trematoda - a class of parasitic flatworms known as flukes

Etymology

From Greek kestos (girdle) + -oda, referring to the belt-like shape of these worms.
