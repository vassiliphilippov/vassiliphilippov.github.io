tusk

1. VERB

/ tʌsk / (AmE), / tʌsk / (BrE)
to remove the tusks from an animal, especially an elephant or walrus.
remove tusks from an animal

Examples

Illicit poachers often tusk elephants for the illegal ivory trade.
Veterinarians may tusk wild boars to prevent them from injuring each other.
Efforts to tusk walruses for research purposes are carefully regulated.

2. VERB

/ tʌsk / (AmE), / tʌsk / (BrE)
(of an animal) to stab or pierce with a tusk or horn.
stab or pierce with a tusk

Examples

The wild boar tusked the farmer during the attack.
In the documentary, an elephant was shown tusking a tree to mark its territory.
The rhinoceros tusked its rival in a territorial battle.

3. NOUN

/ tʌsk / (AmE), / tʌsk / (BrE)
a long, pointed tooth that sticks out from the mouth of some animals, especially elephants, walruses, or wild pigs, used for fighting or digging
a long, pointed tooth in some animals

Examples

The elephant used its tusk to dig for water beneath the dry earth.
Poachers often target animals for their valuable tusks.
A walrus uses its tusks to pull itself out of the water and onto the ice.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fang - a long, pointed tooth, especially in animals such as snakes or dogs

Etymology

From Old English 'tusk', related to Old Norse 'tusk' (tooth).

4. NOUN

/ tʌsk / (AmE), / tʌsk / (BrE)
the hard, elongated, ivory-colored tooth projecting from the mouth of certain animals, such as elephants and walruses, and composed mainly of dentine
long, hard tooth of an elephant or walrus

Examples

The elephant protected itself with its massive tusk during the fight.
Walrus tusks are used for digging and fighting among males.
Poachers hunt elephants illegally for the valuable ivory in their tusks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ivory - the hard white substance from elephant tusks
  • Degree Lower: tooth - hard structure in the mouths of vertebrates

Etymology

From Middle English 'tusk' or 'tosk', from Old English 'tux', related to Old High German 'zahsan' (fang, tooth).
