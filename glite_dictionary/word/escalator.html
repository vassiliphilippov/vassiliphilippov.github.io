escalator

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛskəˌleɪtər / (AmE), / ˈɛskəleɪtə / (BrE)
A moving staircase powered by a motor, designed to carry people between different floors or levels.
moving staircase for people

Examples

We took the escalator up to the second floor of the shopping mall.
The escalator was out of service, so we had to use the stairs.
Children should always hold the handrail when riding the escalator.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elevator - a platform or compartment for raising and lowering people or goods
  • Synonym: stairs - a set of steps for going from one level to another

Etymology

From 'escalade', French for 'climb', combined with the English '-or' suffix. Originally a trademark.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛskəˌleɪtər / (AmE), / ˈɛskəleɪtə / (BrE)
a clause in a contract that allows for automatic adjustment of wages, prices, or benefits based on specified conditions such as changes in the cost of living
contract clause for automatic wage or price adjustment
legal

Examples

The union demanded an escalator in the contract to keep up with inflation.
Recent agreements often include an escalator to adjust salaries when the cost of living rises.
Without an escalator, employees' real wages could decline during periods of high inflation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: escalator clause - formal term for a contract provision adjusting wages or prices automatically

Etymology

From 'escalator clause', using 'escalator' metaphorically after the moving staircase, with sense of moving up automatically.
