fantastical

1. ADJ

/ fænˈtæstɪkəl / (AmE), / fænˈtæstɪkəl / (BrE)
existing only in the imagination; not real
imaginary; not real

Examples

The story was filled with fantastical creatures that could never exist in reality.
She described her fantastical dreams of flying over cities and talking to animals.
The artist's paintings are famous for their fantastical landscapes and bizarre figures.

Synset Examples

fantastic figures with bulbous heads the circumference of a bushel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imaginary - existing only in the imagination, not real
  • Antonym: realistic - representing things as they really are

Etymology

From Middle English fantastical, from fantastik + -al, influenced by fantasy.

2. ADJ

/ fænˈtæstɪkəl / (AmE), / fænˈtæstɪkəl / (BrE)
extremely strange, bizarre, or imaginative in a way that seems unreal or ludicrous.
extremely strange or imaginative

Examples

The novel's plot features several fantastical creatures that do not exist in our world.
Her room was decorated with fantastical masks and swirling patterns.
The artist's fantastical visions were brought to life in vivid colors and sharp lines.

Synset Examples

Hamlet's assumed antic disposition
fantastic Halloween costumes
a grotesque reflection in the mirror

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fantastic - extraordinarily strange or imaginative
  • Synonym: grotesque - comically or repulsively ugly or distorted
  • Antonym: realistic - representing things as they really are

Etymology

From Middle English fantastical, from Old French fantastique, from Greek phantastikos (able to imagine).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00970835-s
Synset POS: s
