preempt

1. NOUN

/ priˈɛmpt / (AmE), / priːˈɛmpt / (BrE)
(in bridge and similar card games) a high bid made to prevent opponents from bidding or to disrupt their strategy.
high bid to disrupt opponents (bridge)

Examples

He made a preempt with seven clubs, cutting off any opposing bids.
Their team's aggressive preempt forced us to pass early.
Using a preempt can be risky if your partner is not prepared.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preemptive bid - a high, blocking bid in bridge
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06750757-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ priˈɛmpt / (AmE), / priːˈɛmpt / (BrE)
to take the place of something or have precedence over it, especially by happening or being announced first
take the place of; have precedence over

Examples

The breaking news report will preempt the movie scheduled for tonight.
A sudden change in schedule preempted the team's regular practice session.
The prime minister's statement preempted questions from the press by addressing their main concerns.

Synset Examples

live broadcast of the presidential debate preempts the regular news hour
discussion of the emergency situation will preempt the lecture by the professor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to supersede - take the place of (a person or thing previously in authority or use)

Etymology

From Latin praemere (to press before), via Latin praemptus and English pre- + empt.

Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ priˈɛmpt / (AmE), / priˈɛmpt / (BrE)
to acquire or take possession of something before anyone else has a chance to do so
acquire before others can

Examples

The company tried to preempt the competition by securing exclusive rights to the new technology.
Buyers rushed to preempt the best seats before tickets officially went on sale.
She made a large down payment to preempt any other offers on the property.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to forestall - to act in advance of someone in order to prevent them from doing something
  • Synonym: to seize - to take something quickly and eagerly before anyone else can

Etymology

From Latin praeemere, from prae- (before) + emere (to buy).

4. VERB

/ priˈɛmpt / (AmE), / priːˈɛmpt / (BrE)
to acquire possession of something, especially land, by claiming a prior right or opportunity, often to obtain the right to purchase it first
obtain (land) by prior right or claim

Examples

The government reserved the right to preempt any land needed for public works projects.
Settlers rushed to preempt valuable land before it was officially opened for sale.
He hoped to preempt the property and secure first rights to purchase it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to acquire - to come into possession or ownership of something

Etymology

From Latin praeemere, from prae- (before) + emere (to buy).

5. VERB

/ priˈɛmpt / (AmE), / priˈɛmpt / (BrE)
(in bridge) to make a preemptive bid, typically at a higher level than necessary, to interfere with the opponents' bidding sequence.
make a preemptive bid in bridge

Examples

She decided to preempt with a three-diamond bid, hoping to disrupt the opponents' communication.
It can be risky to preempt on a weak hand, but sometimes it pays off.
During last night's game, he preempted at the three-level to keep his rivals out of the auction.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overcall - a bid in bridge made after the opponents have bid

Etymology

From 'preemptive', which originates from Latin 'praeemere' (to buy before).
