gangrene

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡæŋˌɡrin / (AmE), / ˈɡæŋɡriːn / (BrE)
The death and decay of body tissue, usually as a result of infection or interruption of blood supply.
death of body tissue due to lack of blood or infection
medical

Examples

If untreated, gangrene can spread quickly and become life-threatening.
Doctors tried to save the limb, but gangrene had already set in.
Poor circulation due to diabetes increases the risk of gangrene.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: necrosis - the death of most or all of the cells in an organ or tissue

Etymology

From Latin 'gangraena', from Ancient Greek 'gangraina' (an eating sore).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡæŋˌɡrin / (AmE), / ˈɡæŋɡriːn / (BrE)
Dead or decaying body tissue caused by a loss of blood supply or bacterial infection.
dead or decaying body tissue
medical

Examples

The surgeon examined the foot and confirmed that gangrene had set in.
Early treatment is crucial to prevent gangrene from spreading in the affected area.
If gangrene develops, amputation may be necessary to save the patient’s life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: necrosis - death of most or all of the cells in an organ or tissue

Etymology

From Latin 'gangraena', from Greek 'gangraina' (an eating sore, gangrene).

3. VERB

/ ˈɡæŋˌɡrin / (AmE), / ˈɡæŋɡriːn / (BrE)
(of tissue) to undergo necrosis, typically caused by infection or lack of blood supply.
(of tissue) undergo necrosis
medical

Examples

If the wound is left untreated, it might gangrene and require amputation.
Doctors warned that without prompt care, the tissue could gangrene rapidly.
When a limb ceases to receive blood flow, it may begin to gangrene.

Synset Examples

the tissue around the wound necrosed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to necrose - (of tissue) to die or become necrotic

Etymology

From Old French *gangrené*, from Latin *gangraena*, from Ancient Greek *gangraina* (an eating sore).
