couplet

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌp.lət / (AmE), / ˈkʌp.lɪt / (BrE)
A pair of similar things; two items of the same kind considered together.
pair of similar things

Examples

The museum displayed a couplet of ancient vases on the shelf.
She bought a couplet of matching candlesticks for the dining table.
Each couplet of socks was neatly folded in the drawer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pair - two items considered together or regarded as a unit

Etymology

From French 'couplet', diminutive of 'couple' (pair).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkʌp.lət / (AmE), / ˈkʌp.lɪt / (BrE)
A pair of successive lines of verse, typically rhymed and of the same length, forming a unit in poetry.
two-line unit of verse (often rhymed)

Examples

Shakespeare often used couplets to end his sonnets.
The final couplet in the poem delivers a powerful message.
Many limericks finish with a clever couplet.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: stanza - a grouped set of lines in a poem

Etymology

From French 'couplet', diminutive of 'couple', from Latin 'copula' (bond, link).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06390540-n
Synset POS: n
