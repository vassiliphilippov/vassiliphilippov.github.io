to undermine

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈmaɪn / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈmaɪn / (BrE)
to make a hollow or cavity beneath something, especially by gradually removing supporting material; to erode the base of.
hollow out or erode at the base

Examples

Over time, the river began to undermine the steep banks, creating small caves.
Continuous flooding can undermine the foundations of a bridge, threatening its stability.
Workers discovered that erosion had undermined a section of the tunnel roof.

Synset Examples

The river was caving the banks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to erode - to gradually wear away soil, rock, or land

Etymology

From Middle English undermyne, from under- (beneath) + mine (to dig), influenced by mining terminology.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01285636-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈmaɪn / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈmaɪn / (BrE)
to damage or weaken (someone or something), often gradually or secretly; or to hinder normal operations or destroy the integrity of something
weaken or hinder (especially gradually)

Examples

The spies tried to undermine the enemy's supply routes during the conflict.
Recent cyber attacks have undermined the company's computer systems, causing significant disruption.
Failure to maintain the equipment could undermine normal operations and lead to costly breakdowns.

Synset Examples

The Resistance sabotaged railroad operations during the war

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sabotage - to deliberately destroy, damage, or obstruct something, especially for political or military advantage

Etymology

From Middle English, literally 'to mine under', from under + mine (in the sense of digging beneath).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02549304-v
Synset POS: v
