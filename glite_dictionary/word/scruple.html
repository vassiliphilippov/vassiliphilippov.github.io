scruple

1. NOUN

/ ˈskruːpəl / (AmE), / ˈskruːpl / (BrE)
a unit of apothecary weight equal to 20 grains, used historically in pharmacy
unit of apothecary weight (20 grains)
archaic medical

Examples

In the old recipe, a scruple of powdered root was added to the mixture.
The pharmacist measured out exactly one scruple of the compound for the prescription.
Two scruples of the ingredient are required according to ancient apothecary standards.

Etymology

From Latin scrupulum (small weight, 1/24 of an ounce), diminutive of scrupus (small sharp stone).

2. NOUN

/ ˈskruːpəl / (AmE), / ˈskruːpəl / (BrE)
a feeling of doubt or hesitation about the morality or propriety of a course of action
moral doubt; ethical hesitation

Examples

She had no scruple about telling the truth, even if it hurt.
His scruples prevented him from accepting the bribe.
Without a scruple, he manipulated the data to fit his theory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: qualm - an uneasy feeling of doubt or worry, especially about one's own conduct
  • Synonym: compunction - a feeling of guilt or moral scruple that prevents or follows wrongdoing

Etymology

From Latin scrupulus (a small, sharp stone; anxiety, uneasiness), diminutive of scrupus (sharp stone).

/ ˈskruːpəl / (AmE), / ˈskruːpəl / (BrE)
to hesitate to do something because it may be morally wrong
hesitate on moral or ethical grounds

Examples

He would scruple to cheat, even if no one would ever find out.
Some journalists fail to scruple over using questionable sources.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

Etymology

Examples

His scruples prevented him from lying on the application form.

  • Synonym: compunction - a feeling of moral uneasiness or guilt after doing something bad
  • Degree Higher: remorse - deep regret or guilt for a wrong committed

From Latin scrupulus (a small sharp stone, anxiety, doubt), diminutive of scrupus (rough stone).
