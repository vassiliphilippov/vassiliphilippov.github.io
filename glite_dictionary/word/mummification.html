mummification

1. NOUN

/ ˌmʌmɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmʌmɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
The process of preserving a dead body by embalming and drying it and wrapping it in cloth, as practiced by ancient civilizations such as the Egyptians.
preservation of a body as a mummy

Examples

The ancient Egyptians perfected the art of mummification for their pharaohs.
Modern science has uncovered the secrets behind the mummification process.
Some cultures practiced mummification to prepare bodies for the afterlife.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embalmment - the process of preserving a body with chemicals
  • Antonym: decomposition - the process of decaying after death

Etymology

From French *mummification*, from *momifier* (to mummify), ultimately from Latin *mumia* (mummy).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmʌm.ə.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌmʌm.ɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a state in which something becomes dry, shriveled, and lifeless like a mummy, or the process of being preserved as a mummy; also used figuratively to describe something that has become rigid or lifeless
drying or preservation like a mummy; becoming lifeless

Examples

After centuries in the hot, dry climate, the body underwent natural mummification.
Critics accused the institution of bureaucratic mummification and resistance to change.
The mummification of cultural traditions can prevent societies from adapting to new circumstances.

Synset Examples

bureaucratic mummification in red tape

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desiccation - the process of drying out
  • Antonym: vitalization - the act of making something more lively or vital

Etymology

From Latin 'mummia' (mummy) + English '-fication' (making, becoming).

3. NOUN

/ ˌmʌmɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmʌmɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Pathology) Gangrene caused by arterial obstruction, in which dead tissue becomes dry, shriveled, and dark brown.
dry gangrene with shriveled dead tissue
medical

Examples

The patient's toes showed signs of mummification after prolonged arterial blockage.
In severe cases, diabetes can lead to mummification of the lower extremities.
Doctors identified mummification of the gangrenous tissue during examination.

Etymology

From Latin 'mummificare', meaning 'to embalm as a mummy.'
