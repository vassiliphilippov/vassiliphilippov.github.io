to arouse

1. VERB

/ əˈraʊz / (AmE), / əˈraʊz / (BrE)
to summon into action or bring into existence, often as if by magic or a supernatural force
summon or bring into existence, often magically

Examples

The ritual was said to arouse ancient spirits who would answer the villagers' questions.
Legends claim some shaman could arouse storms with a single chant.
The author hoped to arouse a sense of wonder in his audience, almost as if by magic.

Synset Examples

he conjured wild birds in the air
call down the spirits from the mountain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to conjure - to call upon or bring into existence by magic
  • Synonym: to evoke - to bring a feeling, memory, or image into the mind; to call forth

2. VERB

/ əˈraʊz / (AmE), / əˈraʊz / (BrE)
to cause someone to become alert, awake, or energetic
make someone alert or energetic

Examples

A loud noise can arouse even the deepest sleeper.
The teacher tried to arouse enthusiasm in the class.
Strong coffee in the morning always arouses my energy.

Synset Examples

Coffee and tea stimulate me
This herbal infusion doesn't stimulate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stimulate - to encourage activity or increased alertness

Etymology

From Old French *arouser*, probably from Vulgar Latin *arūsāre*, based on Latin *ad-* (to) + *rusus* (towards).

4. VERB

/ əˈraʊz / (AmE), / əˈraʊz / (BrE)
to cause someone to wake up or become conscious
cause to wake or become conscious

Examples

The loud thunder aroused her from a deep sleep.
Nothing could arouse him while he was in such a deep slumber.
Doctors tried to arouse the patient after the surgery.

Synset Examples

He was roused by the drunken men in the street
Please wake me at 6 AM.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wake - to stop sleeping; to become awake
  • Synonym: to awaken - to make someone stop sleeping

Etymology

From Old French 'arouser', based on the prefix 'a-' (from Latin 'ad-') and 'rouser' (to rouse).

5. VERB

/ əˈraʊz / (AmE), / əˈraʊz / (BrE)
to cause a particular feeling, emotion, or response to be felt or expressed
cause feelings or responses

Examples

The film managed to arouse strong emotions among the audience.
Her speech was intended to arouse public interest in the issue.
The news did not arouse much excitement in the community.

Synset Examples

arouse pity
raise a smile
evoke sympathy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evoke - bring or recall a feeling, memory, or image to mind
  • Synonym: to stimulate - encourage something to develop or become more active
  • Antonym: to quell - to put an end to a feeling, often by force; suppress

Etymology

From Old French *arouser*, based on the prefix *a-* (to) and *rouser* (to shake, stir up).
