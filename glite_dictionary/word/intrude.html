to intrude

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtrud / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter a place, especially private property, without permission or lawful right
enter without permission

Examples

The sign warned that anyone who intruded on the property would be prosecuted.
She felt uneasy when she noticed someone intruding into her backyard after dark.
If you intrude on private land, you may face a fine or legal action.

Synset Examples

Don't trespass on my land!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trespass - to enter someone's land or property without permission

Etymology

From Latin 'intrudere' meaning 'to thrust in', from 'in-' (in) + 'trudere' (to thrust).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtrud / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter, inquire, or interfere in a way that is unwelcome or meddlesome; to involve oneself where one is not wanted
to interfere or involve oneself without invitation

Examples

I don't mean to intrude, but may I ask what you're discussing?
Reporters continued to intrude into the celebrity's private life despite her requests for privacy.
He felt embarrassed to intrude on their conversation without being invited.

Synset Examples

This guy is always nosing around the office

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to interfere - to get involved in a situation where your involvement is not wanted
  • Synonym: to meddle - to become involved in something that is not your concern
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove oneself or cease to be involved

Etymology

From Latin 'intrudere' (to thrust in).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02173563-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈtruːd / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter or force oneself into a place or situation where one is not wanted or welcomed
enter uninvited or by force

Examples

I'm sorry to intrude, but could I ask you a quick question?
She tried not to intrude on their private conversation.
Loud noises would only intrude upon the peaceful atmosphere of the library.

Synset Examples

The colors don't intrude on the viewer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to invade - to enter a place forcibly or as an enemy
  • Synonym: interrupt - to break the continuity of something by intrusion
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove oneself from a place or situation

Etymology

From Latin 'intrudere' (to thrust in), from 'in-' (in) + 'trudere' (to thrust).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈtruːd / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter a place or situation where you are not wanted or invited, often causing a disturbance
enter uninvited or unwanted

Examples

I hope I'm not intruding, but I needed to ask you something important.
Reporters intruded on the family's privacy during a difficult time.
Please knock before you intrude into someone else's office.

Synset Examples

They intruded on our dinner party
She irrupted into our sitting room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interrupt - to stop the continuous progress of something; to break into a conversation or event
  • Synonym: to invade - to enter a place forcefully or as an aggressor
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to leave a place or situation, especially to avoid being an intruder

Etymology

From Latin intrudere (to thrust in), from in- (in) + trudere (to thrust).
