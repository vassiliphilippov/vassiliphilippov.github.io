defect

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈfɛkt / (AmE), / ˈdiːfɛkt / (BrE)
a fault or shortcoming; a lack or imperfection that impairs worth or utility
a fault or deficiency

Examples

The product was returned because it had a manufacturing defect.
His plan was well thought out, but there was one serious defect in the logic.
Honesty is important, but a lack of confidence can be a defect in leadership.

Synset Examples

that interpretation is an unfortunate defect of our lack of information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flaw - a mark, fault, or other imperfection that mars a substance or object
  • Antonym: strength - a beneficial quality or attribute

Etymology

From Latin 'defectus' (failure, lack), past participle of 'deficere' (to fail).

3. VERB

/ dɪˈfɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈfɛkt / (BrE)
to leave one’s country, organization, or cause, often to join an opposing one, especially out of dissatisfaction or disloyalty
leave for the opposing side

Examples

Three members of the team chose to defect to the rival company for better pay.
During the Cold War, some athletes would defect while competing abroad and seek asylum.
Fearing persecution, the scientist decided to defect and disclose valuable information to the other country.

Synset Examples

If soldiers deserted Hitler's army, they were shot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desert - to leave a place or person, often in a disloyal way
  • Antonym: to remain - to stay in the same place or with the same group, not leaving or changing sides

Etymology

From Latin defectus (failure, desertion), from deficere (to fail, desert).
