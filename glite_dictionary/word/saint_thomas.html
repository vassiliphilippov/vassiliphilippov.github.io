Saint Thomas

1. PROPN

/ seɪnt ˈtɑːməs / (AmE), / seɪnt ˈtɒməs / (BrE)
(1225–1274) Italian Catholic theologian and philosopher, known as Thomas Aquinas, celebrated for reconciling faith and reason and presenting philosophical arguments for the existence of God.
Italian Catholic theologian and philosopher (Thomas Aquinas)

Examples

Many philosophers study the works of Saint Thomas to understand the relationship between faith and reason.
The writings of Saint Thomas had a profound influence on medieval theology.
Aquinas, known as Saint Thomas, sought to prove the existence of God using rational arguments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Thomas Aquinas - Italian Catholic philosopher and theologian; also known as Saint Thomas

Etymology

From Latin 'Sanctus Thomas', referring to Thomas Aquinas, a medieval theologian and Doctor of the Church.

2. PROPN

/ seɪnt ˈtɑːməs / (AmE), / seɪnt ˈtɒməs / (BrE)
the Apostle who doubted Jesus's resurrection until he saw him in person; often called 'Doubting Thomas'
Apostle who doubted Jesus's resurrection

Examples

Many famous paintings depict Saint Thomas touching the wounds of Christ after the resurrection.
In Christian tradition, Saint Thomas is remembered for his initial doubt and later faith.
Because of the story of Saint Thomas, some people call a skeptic a 'Doubting Thomas'.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Doubting Thomas - a person who is habitually doubtful or skeptical

Etymology

From Thomas, a Greek form of the Aramaic 'T’oma’ meaning 'twin'; called 'Saint' indicating sainthood in Christian tradition.
