fugacity

1. NOUN

/ fjuˈɡæsɪti / (AmE), / fjʊˈɡasɪti / (BrE)
A measure of the tendency of a gas to expand, escape, or vaporize, used in thermodynamics to express deviations from ideal gas behavior.
tendency of a gas to expand or escape

Examples

The fugacity of a substance must be considered when modeling chemical reactions at high pressures.
Engineers adjusted the calculations to account for the fugacity of the gas under extreme conditions.
In non-ideal gases, fugacity differs significantly from pressure, affecting industrial processes.

Etymology

From Latin 'fugacitas' (fleeing, transience), from 'fugax' (apt to flee).

2. NOUN

/ fjuˈɡæs.ə.ti / (AmE), / fjʊˈɡæs.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
The quality of being fleeting or short-lived, especially as applied to plant parts; transience.
short-lived nature, especially of plants

Examples

The fugacity of cherry blossoms is celebrated as a symbol of life's brief beauty.
Botanists note the fugacity of certain flowers, which wither soon after blooming.
The poem draws a parallel between human ambitions and the fugacity of petals falling in spring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transience - the quality of being temporary or short-lived
  • Antonym: permanence - the state of lasting for a very long time or forever

Etymology

From Latin *fugitivus* (fleeing, transient), from *fugere* (to flee).
