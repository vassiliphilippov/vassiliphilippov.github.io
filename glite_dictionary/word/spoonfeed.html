to spoonfeed

1. VERB

/ ˈspuːnˌfiːd / (AmE), / ˈspuːnˌfiːd / (BrE)
to feed someone, especially a baby or invalid, with a spoon
feed with a spoon

Examples

The nurse had to spoonfeed the patient because he was too weak to eat by himself.
It usually takes months before a toddler stops needing to be spoonfed.
She patiently spoonfed her baby, making sure not to spill a drop.

Etymology

Formed from 'spoon' + 'feed'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01181935-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈspunˌfid / (AmE), / ˈspuːnfiːd / (BrE)
to provide information or instruction in an overly simple, undemanding way that requires little or no effort from the learner
to teach in an overly simple way

Examples

The teacher refused to spoonfeed the class, encouraging them to find answers on their own.
If you spoonfeed employees, they may never learn to solve problems independently.
The course does not spoonfeed its students, instead it expects active participation and research.

Synset Examples

This professor spoonfeeds his students

Related Terms

  • Antonym: challenge - to test someone's abilities in a demanding way
  • Antonym: encourage independent learning - to promote self-guided study and critical thinking

Etymology

Formed from 'spoon' + 'feed', originally referring to feeding infants, later used figuratively.

WordNet ID: oewn-00789538-v
