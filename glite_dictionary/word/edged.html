edged

1. ADJ

/ ɛdʒd / (AmE), / ɛdʒd / (BrE)
having a particular kind of border or edge, often specified by an adjective or noun modifier
having a specified edge or border

Examples

She handed me a gold-edged invitation to the gala.
The table was covered with a lace-edged cloth that looked quite elegant.
Many stamps in the collection were perforated-edged for easy separation.

Synset Examples

a black-edged card
rough-edged leaves
dried sweat left salt-edged patches

Related Terms

  • Antonym: smooth-edged - having a smooth edge
  • Antonym: rough-edged - having a rough edge

2. ADJ

/ ɛdʒd / (AmE), / ɛdʒd / (BrE)
(of speech or writing) harsh, biting, or hurtful in tone or character; sharply critical.
harsh or hurtful in tone

Examples

She made an edged comment that clearly upset him.
His reviews are always edged with sarcasm and criticism.
The debate was filled with edged remarks from both sides.

Synset Examples

cutting remarks
edged satire
a stinging comment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cutting - (of a remark) harsh and intended to hurt
  • Synonym: caustic - (of speech or writing) severely critical or sarcastic
  • Antonym: gentle - kind and mild in tone or character

Etymology

From the noun 'edge' (sharp border), used figuratively of language or tone.

3. ADJ

/ ˈɛdʒd / (AmE), / ˈɛdʒd / (BrE)
having a cutting edge or edges, or having edges of a specified type (often used in combination)
having a cutting edge or edges

Examples

The chef prefers to use an edged blade for precise slicing.
Be careful with that edged tool; it's extremely sharp.
The museum displayed a collection of edged weapons from the medieval period.

Synset Examples

an edged knife
a two-edged sword

Related Terms

  • Antonym: blunt - not having a sharp edge or point

Etymology

From 'edge' + -ed, denoting possession of an edge.
