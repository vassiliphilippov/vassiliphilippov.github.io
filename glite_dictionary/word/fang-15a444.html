fang

1. NOUN

/ fæŋ / (AmE), / fæŋ / (BrE)
a pointed appendage of certain insects or arthropods, often capable of injecting venom, usually evolved from the legs
venom-injecting appendage of insects/arthropods

Examples

The spider's fang pierced the prey, injecting venom instantly.
Some insects possess a specialized fang to deliver their toxic bite.
Researchers studied how the scorpion’s fang evolved from leg segments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tooth - a hard, pointed structure in the mouth used for biting or chewing

Etymology

From Old Norse 'fang', meaning 'catch' or 'grip'.

2. NOUN

/ fæŋ / (AmE), / fæŋ / (BrE)
a long, pointed tooth in the jaws of a carnivorous animal, used to seize and tear prey
long pointed tooth of a carnivorous animal

Examples

The venomous snake sank its fang into the hiker's ankle.
Wolves use their sharp fangs to grab and tear meat from their prey.
The vampire legend describes creatures with long, protruding fangs used to bite their victims.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canine tooth - a pointed tooth between the incisors and premolars

Etymology

From Old Norse *fang* (catch, grasp), related to Old English *fōn* (to seize).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ fæŋ / (AmE), / fæŋ / (BrE)
a long, hollow or grooved pointed tooth of a venomous snake, used to inject poison
tooth of a venomous snake injecting poison

Examples

The viper injected venom through its sharp fang.
A rattlesnake uses its fang to deliver a toxic bite.
Scientists examined the length of the cobra's fang under the microscope.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: tooth - hard, calcified structure in the mouth used for biting and chewing

Etymology

From Old English 'fang' (a grasp, seizure); related to Old Norse 'fanga' (to seize).
