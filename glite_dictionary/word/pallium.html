pallium

1. NOUN

/ ˈpæliəm / (AmE), / ˈpalɪəm / (BrE)
A woolen band worn by archbishops in the Roman Catholic Church, encircling the shoulders with two lappets hanging down front and back.
Roman Catholic clerical vestment

Examples

The archbishop donned the pallium during the special ceremony in Rome.
Only archbishops are entitled to wear the pallium as a symbol of their office.
The pallium is made of lamb's wool and is richly decorated for solemn occasions.

Etymology

From Latin 'pallium' meaning 'cloak' or 'mantle'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpæliəm / (AmE), / ˈpalɪəm / (BrE)
a cloak or mantle worn by men in ancient Rome, typically draped over the shoulders
ancient Roman men's cloak

Examples

The senator adjusted his pallium before stepping onto the rostrum.
In ancient Rome, the pallium was often made of wool and draped elegantly.
Archaeological finds include fragments of what was once a richly decorated pallium.

Etymology

From Latin pallium (cloak, mantle).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpæliəm / (AmE), / ˈpalɪəm / (BrE)
(Zoology) A protective layer of epidermis in mollusks or brachiopods that secretes the substance forming the shell.
epidermal layer in mollusks forming the shell
medical

Examples

The pallium is responsible for secreting the calcium carbonate that forms a clam's shell.
Damage to the pallium can impair a mollusk's ability to repair its protective shell.
Researchers studied how the pallium reacts to changes in environmental acidity.

Etymology

From Latin 'pallium' (cloak).

4. NOUN

/ ˈpæliəm / (AmE), / ˈpalɪəm / (BrE)
the layer of unmyelinated neurons, or grey matter, that forms the cortex of the cerebrum in the brain
outer grey matter layer of the brain's cortex
medical

Examples

Recent studies have focused on the development of the pallium in vertebrate brains.
Damage to the pallium can affect higher cognitive functions in mammals.
The pallium in birds is organized differently from that in mammals.

Etymology

From Latin 'pallium' (cloak), referring to its covering nature in the brain.
