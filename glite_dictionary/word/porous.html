porous

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɔrəs / (AmE), / ˈpɔːrəs / (BrE)
Allowing liquids, gases, or other substances to pass through, often by having many small holes or spaces; easily permeable.
allowing substances to pass through

Examples

The old stone walls were so porous that rainwater seeped into the basement.
Security at the border is porous, making it easy for people to cross undetected.
Certain types of rocks are more porous and can absorb water quickly.

Synset Examples

our unfenced and largely unpoliced border inevitably has been very porous

Related Terms

  • Antonym: impermeable - not allowing substances to pass through

Etymology

From Latin 'porosus', from 'porus' (pore), via late Middle English.

2. ADJ

/ ˈpɔːrəs / (AmE), / ˈpɔːrəs / (BrE)
having many small holes or spaces through which liquid or air may pass; full of pores
having many small holes or pores

Examples

The sponge is highly porous and absorbs water efficiently.
Volcanic rocks can be very porous, containing numerous cavities and channels.
The border between the two countries was too porous to prevent illegal crossings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permeable - allowing liquids or gases to pass through
  • Antonym: impermeable - not allowing liquid or gas to pass through

Etymology

From Latin 'porosus', from 'porus' (pore), via Old French and Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00330294-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ˈpɔrəs / (AmE), / ˈpɔːrəs / (BrE)
having many small holes or spaces that allow liquid or air to pass through or be absorbed
full of tiny holes; able to absorb fluids

Examples

The sponge is highly porous and can soak up a lot of water.
Because the rocks are porous, groundwater moves through them easily.
Proper running shoes have a porous material to allow your feet to breathe.

Synset Examples

the partly porous walls of our digestive system
compacting the soil to make it less porous

Related Terms

  • Antonym: impermeable - not allowing fluid to pass through

Etymology

From Latin 'porosus' (full of pores), from 'porus' (pore).

Source: WordNet
