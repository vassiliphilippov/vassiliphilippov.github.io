sergeant-at-law

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɝː.dʒənt ət ˈlɔ / (AmE), / ˌsɑː.dʒənt ət ˈlɔː / (BrE)
a former English barrister of the highest rank, entitled to plead in the Court of Common Pleas
highest-ranking English barrister (historical)
archaic legal british

Examples

During the medieval period, a sergeant-at-law was allowed to plead cases in the highest courts.
He dressed as a sergeant-at-law for the historical reenactment at the old courthouse.
Chaucer mentions a sergeant-at-law as one of the pilgrims in The Canterbury Tales.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barrister - a lawyer entitled to practice as an advocate, especially in higher courts

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *sergeaunt* (servant, official) + 'at law'.
