Yukon

1. PROPN

/ ˈjuːkɑn / (AmE), / ˈjuːkɒn / (BrE)
a territory in northwestern Canada, known for its vast wilderness and as the site of the Klondike gold rush in the 1890s
Canadian territory in the northwest

Examples

The Yukon is famous for its stunning natural landscapes and rich history.
During the late 19th century, thousands flocked to the Yukon in search of gold.
Tourists often visit the Yukon for outdoor adventures such as hiking and dog sledding.

Etymology

From Gwichʼin (Athabaskan language) 'Yu-kun-ah', meaning 'great river', originally referring to the Yukon River.

2. PROPN

/ ˈjuːkɑn / (AmE), / ˈjuːkɒn / (BrE)
A large river in northwestern North America that flows from the Yukon Territory through central Alaska to the Bering Sea.
major river in northwestern North America

Examples

The Yukon is famous for its role in the Klondike Gold Rush of the late 19th century.
Many adventurous travelers paddle down the Yukon each summer to experience its remote wilderness.
Salmon migrate thousands of miles up the Yukon to spawn every year.

Etymology

From the Gwich'in word 'Yu-kun-ah' (great river).
