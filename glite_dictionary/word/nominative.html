nominative

1. NOUN

/ ˈnɑːmənətɪv / (AmE), / ˈnɒmɪnətɪv / (BrE)
(in grammar) the case of a noun, pronoun, or adjective that is used as the subject of a verb.
grammatical case of the subject

Examples

In Latin, the nominative case is used for the subject of a sentence.
English rarely marks the nominative case except in pronouns like 'he' and 'she'.
The teacher asked the students to identify the nominative in each sentence.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: accusative - case for the direct object of a verb

Etymology

From Latin nominativus (pertaining to a name), from nominare (to name).

/ ˈnɑːmənətɪv / (AmE), / ˈnɒmɪnətɪv / (BrE)
(of a grammatical case) relating to or denoting a case that typically indicates the subject of a verb or the word identified with the subject after a linking verb.
relating to the subject case in grammar

Examples

In Latin, the nominative case marks the subject of the sentence.
The word 'she' is in the nominative form.
Students must identify the nominative noun in each sentence.

3. ADJ

/ ˈnɑmənətɪv / (AmE), / ˈnɒmɪnətɪv / (BrE)
(of a share, bond, etc.) registered in the name of a specific person, rather than being bearer or anonymous.
registered in a specific person's name (finance)

Examples

The investor preferred nominative shares because they were registered in his own name.
Only nominative bonds can be reissued if lost or stolen, as they are tied to the owner's identity.
Banks often require clients to hold securities in nominative form for greater transparency.

Synset Examples

nominative shares of stock

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bearer - a security or document that is not registered in any person's name, owned by whoever holds it

Etymology

From Latin nominativus (pertaining to naming), from nominare (to name).

(of a person or position) appointed or designated by nomination rather than by election or another process.
appointed by nomination
