coaxing

1. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊksɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkəʊksɪŋ / (BrE)
gentle persuasion or flattery intended to influence or gain favor
gentle persuasion or flattery

Examples

With a little coaxing, the child finally agreed to eat her vegetables.
It took some gentle coaxing before he revealed the real reason he was upset.
Despite all her coaxing, he refused to change his mind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persuasion - the act of convincing someone to do or believe something
  • Synonym: blandishment - flattering or pleasing statement to persuade someone
  • Antonym: threat - a statement of intention to cause harm as a means of influencing someone

Etymology

From the verb 'coax', origin uncertain, possibly from obsolete 'cocks' or 'cokes' (fool).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkoʊksɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkəʊksɪŋ / (BrE)
tending to persuade someone gently and pleasantly; intended to coax.
gently persuasive; intended to coax

Examples

With a coaxing smile, she encouraged the child to try the vegetables.
His coaxing tone made it hard to refuse his request.
She used a coaxing manner to convince her parents to let her stay out late.

Synset Examples

a coaxing and obsequious voice
her manner is quiet and ingratiatory and a little too agreeable

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persuasive - good at persuading someone to do or believe something
  • Antonym: forceful - strong and assertive (contrasting character of persuasion)

Etymology

From the verb 'coax' (late 16th century, perhaps from obsolete cox, cockscomb 'fool').
