to degrade

1. VERB

/ dɪˈɡreɪd / (AmE), / dɪˈɡreɪd / (BrE)
(of land) to be worn down or reduced in level by erosion or other natural processes.
reduce land level by erosion

Examples

Severe flooding can degrade riverbanks and alter natural landscapes.
If deforestation continues unchecked, it will degrade the soil and lower the land surface.
Years of heavy rainfall have gradually degraded the hillside.

Etymology

From Latin degradare (to reduce in rank), from de- (down) + gradus (step).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈɡreɪd / (AmE), / dɪˈɡreɪd / (BrE)
to make something worse in quality or value; to lower the grade, status, or condition of something
reduce quality or value

Examples

Air pollution can degrade the quality of life in urban areas.
If you degrade the product with cheap materials, customers will notice the decline.
Exposure to moisture will gradually degrade the electronics inside the device.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to improve - to make something better in quality or value
  • Synonym: to deteriorate - to become worse in quality or value

Etymology

From Latin 'degradare' (to reduce in rank), from 'de-' (down) + 'gradus' (step).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00208136-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈɡreɪd / (AmE), / dɪˈɡreɪd / (BrE)
to treat or describe (someone or something) in a way that causes a loss of dignity or respect; to lower in character or quality
to treat with disrespect or reduce in dignity

Examples

Nobody should degrade another person because of their background.
The manager was criticized for degrading his staff in front of customers.
She felt degraded by the unfair remarks made in the meeting.

Synset Examples

She tends to put down younger women colleagues
His critics took him down after the lecture

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to humiliate - to make someone feel ashamed or foolish by injuring their dignity
  • Antonym: to dignify - to treat with respect or add dignity

Etymology

From Latin degradare, from de- (down) + gradus (step).
