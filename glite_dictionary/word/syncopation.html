syncopation

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɪŋkəˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪŋkəˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
In phonology, the omission of sounds or letters from the middle of a word, as in 'fo'c'sle' for 'forecastle'.
loss of sounds from within a word

Examples

The syncopation of vowels is common in informal speech, creating shorter forms such as 'fam' for 'family'.
Many nautical terms, like 'fo'c'sle', are created through syncopation.
Linguists study syncopation to understand how words evolve over time.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: apocope - the omission of the final sound or syllable of a word
  • Synonym: elision - the omission of a sound or syllable when speaking

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *syncopatio*; based on Greek *synkopē* (cutting short).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɪŋkəˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪŋkəˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
A musical rhythm in which stress or accent is placed on the weak beats instead of the strong beats, especially to create a lively or dancing feel.
accenting of off-beats in music

Examples

The lively jazz piece features prominent syncopation that keeps listeners on their toes.
Dancers were drawn to the syncopation in the salsa music, which made the beat feel exciting and unpredictable.
He practiced playing with syncopation to give his drumming a more interesting and dynamic sound.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhythm - a regular, repeated pattern of sounds or movements in music

Etymology

From Medieval Latin syncopatio, from Greek synkopē (cutting up).

3. NOUN

/ ˌsɪŋ.kəˈpeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪŋ.kəˈpeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A musical effect where regular beats are displaced by accenting normally weak beats, creating unexpected rhythms.
accenting weak beats in music

Examples

The jazz pianist used syncopation to give the piece a lively, unpredictable feel.
Reggae music is known for its distinctive syncopation that sets it apart from other genres.
Learning to play with syncopation can be challenging for beginning drummers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhythm - The pattern of regular or irregular pulses in music

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'syncopatio', from Late Latin 'syncopare' (to cut short).
