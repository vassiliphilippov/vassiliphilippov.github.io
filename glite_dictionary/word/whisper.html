whisper

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɪspər / (AmE), / ˈwɪspə / (BrE)
a soft, rustling or faint noise, like the sound of silk or leaves moving in the wind
soft, rustling or faint noise

Examples

There was a whisper of silk as she entered the room.
The whisper of leaves in the evening quieted his mind.
A faint whisper of wind brushed across the lake.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rustle - a soft, muffled crackling sound like dry leaves or paper
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07407383-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪspɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɪspə / (BrE)
a very quiet way of speaking in which only the breath is used, without vibration of the vocal cords
very quiet way of speaking

Examples

The children spoke in a whisper so they wouldn't wake the baby.
I heard a faint whisper behind the curtain, but I couldn't make out the words.
She delivered the message in a hurried whisper, afraid someone might overhear.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: murmur - a low, continuous, but indistinct sound
  • Antonym: shout - a loud call or cry

Etymology

From Old English 'hwisprian' (to whisper), related to 'hwispretan' (to whistle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07145049-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈwɪspɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɪspə / (BrE)
to speak very softly, using breath rather than the vocal cords, especially so that only one person can hear you
speak very softly

Examples

She leaned over to whisper a secret in my ear.
If you whisper during the movie, others may not notice.
He whispered his answer so quietly that only his friend could hear.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: murmur - to speak softly and indistinctly
  • Antonym: shout - to speak very loudly

Etymology

From Old English 'hwisprian' (to mutter, whisper).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00917783-v
Synset POS: v
