West Saxon

1. NOUN

/ wɛst ˈsæksən / (AmE), / wɛst ˈsæksən / (BrE)
a historical dialect of Old English spoken in the region west of Sussex and south and southwest of the Thames.
historical English dialect

Examples

Many important Old English texts were written in the West Saxon dialect.
Linguists study West Saxon to understand the development of modern English.
The influence of West Saxon can still be traced in some modern English words.

Etymology

From West, referring to the region, and Saxon, the Germanic people who settled there.

2. NOUN

/ wɛst ˈsæksən / (AmE), / wɛst ˈsæksən / (BrE)
a native or inhabitant of Wessex, an ancient kingdom in southern England
inhabitant of Wessex (historic English kingdom)

Examples

During the early Middle Ages, the West Saxon kings played a major role in uniting England.
Archaeologists discovered the ancient burial site of a West Saxon near Winchester.
The language spoken by the West Saxon people greatly influenced Old English literature.

Etymology

From West, meaning 'western', and Saxon, referring to the Saxons, a Germanic people who settled in England.

3. NOUN

/ wɛst ˈsæksən / (AmE), / wɛst ˈsæksən / (BrE)
the principal literary dialect of Old English, spoken and written in southwestern England before the Norman Conquest.
literary dialect of Old English

Examples

Many extant Old English texts are written in West Saxon rather than other regional dialects.
Scholars often analyze the differences between West Saxon and Mercian dialects.
The poem Beowulf was preserved in the West Saxon literary standard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Old English - the earliest form of the English language, spoken in England until about 1150

Etymology

From West Saxon, referring to the Saxons of western England; 'West' + 'Saxon' (from Old English Seaxe).
