sticky

1. ADJ

/ ˈstɪki / (AmE), / ˈstɪki / (BrE)
having the properties of an adhesive; tending to adhere to surfaces or objects when touched
having adhesive or glue-like properties

Examples

The tape was very sticky and difficult to remove from the packaging.
After spilling the soda, the floor became sticky and uncomfortable to walk on.
A sticky label was attached to every box for easy identification.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adhesive - able to stick fast to a surface or object; sticky
  • Antonym: slippery - not sticky; tending to slide easily

Etymology

From stick + -y, dating to Middle English, meaning 'having the quality of sticking'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈstɪki / (AmE), / ˈstɪki / (BrE)
(of weather) unpleasantly hot and humid, making people feel sweaty and uncomfortable.
hot and humid (of weather)

Examples

The air was so sticky that you could feel moisture clinging to your skin.
It’s hard to sleep through these sticky summer nights with no breeze at all.
Everyone was uncomfortable in the sticky heat of the crowded subway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steamy - (of air or weather) hot and humid or full of steam

3. ADJ

/ ˈstɪki / (AmE), / ˈstɪki / (BrE)
(of skin or clothing) damp with sweat so that things tend to cling to the surface.
slightly wet and clinging with sweat

Examples

After running in the humid heat, her shirt felt sticky against her back.
He wiped his sticky forehead with the back of his hand after the workout.
The sticky sensation made her want to change out of her damp clothes immediately.

4. ADJ

/ ˈstɪki / (AmE), / ˈstɪki / (BrE)
Covered with or resembling an adhesive substance; tending to stick to things that touch it.
covered with something that sticks

Examples

Be careful, the table is sticky because someone spilled juice on it.
After peeling the label, my hands felt sticky for hours.
She wiped the sticky residue from the countertop.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tacky - slightly sticky to the touch
  • Antonym: slippery - not sticky; difficult to hold because smooth or wet

Etymology

From stick + -y, dating to the 14th century.
