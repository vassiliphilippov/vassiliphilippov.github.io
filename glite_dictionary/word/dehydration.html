dehydration

1. NOUN

/ ˌdiː.haɪˈdreɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌdiː.haɪˈdreɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of removing moisture or water from something, especially food or substances
removal of water or moisture

Examples

The dehydration of fruit preserves it for a much longer time.
Industrial processes often utilize dehydration to create powdered milk.
Sunlight causes the gradual dehydration of puddles in the hot summer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drying - the process of making something free from moisture

Etymology

From New Latin dehydratio, from de- (removal) + hydration.

2. NOUN

/ diˌhaɪˈdreɪʃən / (AmE), / diːhaɪˈdreɪʃən / (BrE)
an excessive loss of water from the body, which can cause symptoms such as thirst, dizziness, and confusion
excessive loss of body water
medical

Examples

Prolonged exposure to heat can lead to dehydration if you don't drink enough fluids.
Children and the elderly are particularly vulnerable to dehydration during a heatwave.
Athletes must be careful to avoid dehydration by drinking water before and after exercise.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rehydration - the process of restoring lost water to the body

Etymology

From French déshydratation, from dé- (removal) + hydratation (hydration).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdiːhaɪˈdreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdiːhaɪˈdreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or state of losing or removing water or moisture; extreme dryness caused by the absence of water
removal or loss of water; dryness

Examples

The severe dehydration of the soil made it impossible for crops to grow.
After exercising in the sun, he suffered symptoms of dehydration.
The dehydration process is used to preserve fruits and vegetables for long-term storage.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hydration - the process of adding water or moisture

Etymology

From French 'déshydratation', from 'dé-' (removal) + 'hydratation' (hydration)
