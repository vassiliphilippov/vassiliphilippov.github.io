Adonic

1. NOUN

/ əˈdɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / əˈdɒ.nɪk / (BrE)
A poetic line made up of a dactyl followed by a spondee or a trochee, traditionally used in laments for Adonis.
type of metrical verse line in poetry

Examples

The poet used an Adonic at the end of each stanza to evoke a mournful tone.
Scholars have traced the origin of the Adonic meter to ancient Greek laments for Adonis.
To perfect her ode, she carefully balanced the dactyl and spondee in every Adonic line.

Etymology

From Latin 'Adonicus', from Greek 'Adōnikos', relating to Adonis, a figure in Greek mythology.

2. ADJ

/ əˈdɑnɪk / (AmE), / əˈdɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of a verse or line) having a rhythm consisting of a dactyl followed by a spondee or a trochee, especially as used in classical poetry.
verse rhythm: dactyl + spondee/trochee

Examples

The poet composed several Adonic lines in her elegy.
Classical scholars often analyze the Adonic structure in ancient Greek verse.
The presence of Adonic rhythm gives the poem a mournful quality.

Synset Examples

the verse of the laments is Adonic

Etymology

From Latin 'Adonicus', from Greek 'Adōneios', referring to Adonis and the type of verse in laments for him.

3. ADJ

/ əˈdɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / əˈdɒn.ɪk / (BrE)
relating to or resembling Adonis, especially in beauty or attractiveness; also relating to a type of verse associated with Adonis.
relating to or like Adonis

Examples

The poet used Adonic imagery to describe the hero’s appearance.
His Adonic features attracted admirers wherever he went.
Certain ancient Greek verses are composed in the Adonic metre.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Adonis - a youth of remarkable beauty in Greek mythology

Etymology

From Latin Adonicus, from Adonis, a figure in Greek mythology noted for his beauty.
