Semitic

1. ADJ

/ səˈmɪtɪk / (AmE), / sɪˈmɪtɪk / (BrE)
relating to the Semitic languages, a family of languages that includes Hebrew, Arabic, Aramaic, Amharic, and others originating in the Middle East and North Africa.
relating to Semitic languages

Examples

Linguists study the structure of Semitic languages such as Arabic and Hebrew.
The ancient city provided evidence of early Semitic writing systems.
Modern Amharic is a Semitic language spoken in Ethiopia.

Synset Examples

Semitic tongues have a complicated morphology

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Indo-European - relating to the Indo-European language family

Etymology

From Latin Semiticus, from Semita (descendant of Shem in the Bible).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03127542-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ səˈmɪtɪk / (AmE), / sɪˈmɪtɪk / (BrE)
a major branch of the Afro-Asiatic language family that includes languages such as Hebrew, Arabic, and Amharic
Afro-Asiatic language family branch

Examples

Arabic and Hebrew are both members of the Semitic language group.
Scholars study the origins and development of Semitic languages across different regions.
Ethiopian languages such as Amharic also belong to the Semitic branch.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Hamito-Semitic - another name for the Afro-Asiatic language family

Etymology

From Latin 'Semiticus', from Shem (a son of Noah in the Bible), whose descendants supposedly spoke these languages.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06999554-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ səˈmɪtɪk / (AmE), / sɪˈmɪtɪk / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of the Semites, their languages, or cultures
relating to Semites or their languages

Examples

Many Semitic languages, such as Arabic and Hebrew, are spoken in the Middle East.
The museum displayed artifacts from various Semitic civilizations that flourished in ancient times.
She specialized in the study of Semitic linguistics at university.

Synset Examples

Semite peoples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Semite - a member of any of the peoples who speak a Semitic language, especially Jews and Arabs

Etymology

From French *sémitique*, from Modern Latin *Semiticus*, from Semite, referring to Shem, one of Noah's sons in the Bible.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03127361-a
Synset POS: a
