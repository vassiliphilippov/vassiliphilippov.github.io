lawlessness

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɔləsnəs / (AmE), / ˈlɔːləsnɪs / (BrE)
a situation in which laws are not obeyed or enforced, leading to disorder and chaos
absence of law and order; disorder

Examples

The collapse of the regime led to widespread lawlessness in the streets.
After the hurricane, reports of lawlessness made many residents fear for their safety.
The movie portrays a town gripped by lawlessness until a new sheriff arrives.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anarchy - absence of government or order
  • Antonym: order - a state in which laws and rules are obeyed

Etymology

From 'lawless' + '-ness', dating to Middle English.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɔləsnəs / (AmE), / ˈlɔːlɪsnəs / (BrE)
a situation or condition where laws are not obeyed and people do whatever they want, often resulting in disorder
state of lacking law and order

Examples

The country descended into lawlessness after the collapse of the central government.
Decades of corruption and weak institutions have led to widespread lawlessness in some regions.
Many citizens demanded tougher measures to restore order and end the growing lawlessness in the city.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anarchy - a state of society without government or law
  • Antonym: order - a situation in which rules are obeyed and people behave well

Etymology

From 'lawless' + '-ness', dating to Middle English.

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɔləsnəs / (AmE), / ˈlɔːləsnəs / (BrE)
A state or condition in which laws are not obeyed or cannot be enforced, resulting in disorder or chaos.
absence of law or order

Examples

Many residents left the town because of the growing lawlessness and fear for their safety.
The fall of the regime led to a period of lawlessness and rampant crime.
Journalists reported on the lawlessness that plagued the area after the disaster.

Synset Examples

Dodge City was famous for its lawlessness in the 1800s.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anarchy - absence of government and absolute freedom of the individual
  • Antonym: order - a state in which laws and rules are obeyed

Etymology

From 'lawless' (Old English 'laglēas', from 'lage' meaning 'law' and 'lēas' meaning 'without') + '-ness'.
