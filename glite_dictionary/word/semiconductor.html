semiconductor

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛm.i.kənˈdʌk.tɚ / (AmE), / ˌsɛm.i.kənˈdʌk.tə / (BrE)
A substance, such as silicon or germanium, whose electrical conductivity is between that of a conductor and an insulator, and which is widely used in electronic devices.
substance with conductivity between conductor and insulator

Examples

Silicon is the most widely used semiconductor material in modern electronics.
Researchers are developing new types of semiconductors for faster computer chips.
The electrical properties of a semiconductor can be modified by adding impurities in a process called doping.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: conductor - a substance that readily conducts electricity
  • Degree Lower: insulator - a substance that does not conduct electricity well

Etymology

From semi- (half, partly) and conductor, reflecting partial conductivity.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛm.i.kənˈdʌk.tɚ / (AmE), / ˌsɛm.i.kənˈdʌkt.ə / (BrE)
A substance, usually a solid, that has a conductivity between that of an insulator and most metals, either due to the addition of an impurity or because of temperature effects; used especially in electronic devices.
material with conductivity between insulator and conductor

Examples

Silicon is the most widely used semiconductor in the electronics industry.
Modern smartphones contain millions of tiny semiconductors that make computation possible.
A semiconductor can both conduct and insulate electricity, depending on conditions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: insulator - a material that does not conduct electricity
  • Antonym: conductor - a material that allows the flow of electrical current

Etymology

From Latin 'semi-' (half) + 'conductor', reflecting partial conductivity.
