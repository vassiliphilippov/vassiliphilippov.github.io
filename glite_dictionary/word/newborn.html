newborn

1. NOUN

/ ˈnuˌbɔrn / (AmE), / ˈnjuːˌbɔːn / (BrE)
a baby that has just been born, typically less than four weeks old
baby less than four weeks old

Examples

The newborn slept peacefully in her mother's arms.
Doctors recommend special care for a newborn in the first month of life.
They dressed the newborn in a soft, warm onesie before leaving the hospital.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: infant - a very young child or baby
  • Degree Higher: toddler - a young child who is just beginning to walk

Etymology

From Old English *nīwe* (new) + *boren* (born).

2. ADJ

/ ˈnuˌbɔrn / (AmE), / ˈnjuːˌbɔːn / (BrE)
(of a baby or animal) recently born; having come into existence only a short time ago.
recently born baby or animal

Examples

The mother gently cradled her newborn in her arms.
Hospitals provide special care for newborn babies.
The puppy was so tiny it looked like a newborn just hours old.

Synset Examples

a newborn infant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: infant - a very young child or baby
  • Antonym: adult - a fully grown person or animal

Etymology

From 'new' + 'born', meaning 'recently brought into existence.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01653626-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈnubɔrn / (AmE), / ˈnjuːbɔːn / (BrE)
Having just or recently come into existence; newly created or formed.
recently created or arisen

Examples

The country's newborn democracy faced many challenges in its first year.
He felt a newborn sense of hope after starting his new job.
With newborn confidence, she addressed the audience.

Synset Examples

with newborn fears
new-sprung nations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: new - having recently come into existence
  • Antonym: established - having existed or been in use for a long time

Etymology

From Middle English 'newborn', from 'new' + 'born'.
