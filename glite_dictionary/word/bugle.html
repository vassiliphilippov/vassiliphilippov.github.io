bugle

1. NOUN

/ ˈbjuːɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbjuːɡl̩ / (BrE)
a long, tubular glass or plastic bead sewn onto clothing as a decoration
a tubular decorative bead

Examples

The dress was embellished with silver bugles that sparkled under the lights.
She spent hours sewing bugles and sequins onto the costume for the school play.
Tiny bugle beads lined the cuffs of her vintage jacket.

Etymology

From French 'bugle' meaning 'wild ox', originally referring to ox-bone beads.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbjuːɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbjuːɡl̩ / (BrE)
A brass musical instrument without valves, typically used for military calls and fanfares.
valveless brass musical instrument

Examples

The soldier played the bugle at sunrise to wake the camp.
Each evening, he practices the bugle for the upcoming parade.
The sound of a distant bugle announced the end of the ceremony.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trumpet - a brass instrument with valves and a brighter tone

Etymology

From Middle French 'bugle', meaning 'wild ox', from Latin 'buculus' (young bull), referring to early instruments made from animal horns.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbjuːɡl̩ / (AmE), / ˈbjuːɡl̩ / (BrE)
A low-growing annual or perennial evergreen herb, especially of the genus Ajuga, native to Eurasia and often used as ground cover.
a low-growing Eurasian herb (Ajuga) used as ground cover

Examples

The bugle in the garden spreads quickly and produces pretty blue flowers in spring.
Gardeners appreciate bugle for its ability to cover bare patches with dense foliage.
After planting bugle under the trees, the area became lush and green throughout the year.

4. VERB

/ ˈbjuːɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbjuːɡl̩ / (BrE)
to play a sound or signal on a bugle, a simple brass instrument without valves
to play a bugle instrument

Examples

The soldier bugled the morning reveille at sunrise.
As the band advanced, a musician bugled a stirring tune to rally the troops.
During the ceremony, he stood aside and quietly bugled taps in memory of the fallen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trumpet - to play on a trumpet

Etymology

From French 'bugle' (a horn), ultimately from Latin 'buculus' (young bull, ox, referring to the ox horn used as an instrument).
