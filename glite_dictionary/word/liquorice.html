liquorice

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɪkərɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈlɪkərɪs / (BrE)
A plant native to southern Europe and Asia, with sweet-tasting roots used in confectionery and medicine.
plant with sweet-tasting roots

Examples

Liquorice is often used to make traditional sweets and candies enjoyed around the world.
Some herbal teas contain liquorice root for its natural sweetness and medicinal qualities.
The liquorice plant thrives in sunny, well-drained soils of the Mediterranean region.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: licorice - American English spelling of 'liquorice'

Etymology

From Old French *licoresse*, from Late Latin *liquiritia*, adapted from Greek *glykyrrhiza* (sweet root).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɪkərɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈlɪk.ər.ɪs / (BrE)
A chewy black sweet made with extract from the root of the licorice plant, often with a distinct, slightly bitter flavor.
black sweet made with licorice root
british

Examples

Many people find the taste of liquorice either delightful or unpleasant.
She bought a bag of liquorice at the sweet shop for her grandfather.
Traditional liquorice is often softer and less sweet than the modern version.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: licorice - a black candy flavored with the dried root of the licorice plant (American spelling)

Etymology

From Old French 'licoresse' via Latin 'liquiritia', originally from Greek 'glykyrrhiza' (sweet root).
