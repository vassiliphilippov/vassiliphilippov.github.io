tautology

1. NOUN

/ tɔˈtɑːlədʒi / (AmE), / tɔːˈtɒlədʒi / (BrE)
(Logic) A statement that is necessarily true, such as one that is true in all possible interpretations; a formula that cannot be false.
statement true in all cases (logic)

Examples

The proposition 'It will rain tomorrow or it will not rain tomorrow' is a classic tautology in logic.
In formal logic, a tautology is always true, regardless of the truth values of its components.
Her argument relied on a tautology, making it impossible to disprove.

Synset Examples

the statement ‘he is brave or he is not brave’ is a tautology

Related Terms

  • Antonym: contradiction - A statement that is necessarily false; logical opposite of tautology
  • Synonym: logical truth - A statement true in all possible scenarios; synonym in logic

Etymology

From Greek tautologia, from tautos ‘same’ + -logia ‘saying’.

2. NOUN

/ tɔːˈtɑːlədʒi / (AmE), / tɔːˈtɒlədʒi / (BrE)
the use of words that repeat the same idea in a way that is unnecessary, making a statement redundant
useless repetition of meaning

Examples

The phrase 'free gift' is a classic tautology often found in advertisements.
Saying 'he made it with his own hands' is a tautology because it is obvious.
Critics argue that some political promises sound like empty tautology.

Synset Examples

to say that something is ‘adequate enough’ is a tautology

Related Terms

  • Synonym: redundancy - the state of being not or no longer needed or useful; superfluity, especially in language

Etymology

From Greek tautología, from 'tauto-' (the same) and 'logia' (saying).
