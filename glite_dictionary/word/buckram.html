buckram

1. NOUN

/ ˈbʌkrəm / (AmE), / ˈbʌkrəm / (BrE)
a coarse, stiff cotton fabric, often stiffened with glue, used in bookbinding and to reinforce or stiffen clothing
stiff cotton fabric for bookbinding or clothing

Examples

The old book was bound in buckram to provide extra durability.
Her new dress had a layer of buckram to help it hold its shape.
They repaired the torn spine of the book using buckram fabric.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: muslin - a lightweight cotton cloth, often plain-woven
  • Degree Higher: canvas - a strong, coarse unbleached cloth made from hemp, flax, or cotton

Etymology

From Old French bouqueran, possibly from Arabic būqalamūn (variegated cloth).

2. ADJ

/ ˈbʌkrəm / (AmE), / ˈbʌkrəm / (BrE)
Rigidly formal or stiff in manner or style, especially in a way that seems unnatural or affected.
rigidly formal; stiff in manner (literary)
archaic

Examples

Her buckram attitude made conversation difficult at the dinner table.
The principal delivered a buckram speech that failed to engage the students.
His letters have a buckram tone, as if he were always writing to a stranger.

Synset Examples

a starchy manner
the letter was stiff and formal
his prose has a buckram quality

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stiff - formal or not relaxed in manner or appearance
  • Synonym: formal - following established conventions or rules; not relaxed
  • Antonym: relaxed - free from tension and stiffness

Etymology

From the noun 'buckram', referring to a stiff cloth used for lining; extended figuratively to describe stiffness in manner or style.

3. VERB

/ ˈbʌkrəm / (AmE), / ˈbʌkrəm / (BrE)
to stiffen (fabric or clothing) with or as with buckram, making it rigid or more structured
stiffen with buckram (as a verb)

Examples

The costume designer decided to buckram the jacket to give it a sharper silhouette.
In the past, milliners would buckram the hats to help them keep their shape.
For the period drama, the tailor expertly buckrammed all the skirts to achieve the desired fullness.

Synset Examples

buckram the skirt

Etymology

From buckram (noun), a stiff cotton or linen cloth used for stiffening garments; origin uncertain, possibly from Old French 'bucqueren'.
