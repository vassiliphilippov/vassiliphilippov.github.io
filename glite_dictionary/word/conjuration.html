conjuration

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑndʒəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒndʒəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of summoning a spirit or devil, especially by invocation or incantation, often as part of a magical ritual
summoning a spirit or devil

Examples

The ancient text describes a conjuration performed to summon a protective spirit.
Legends tell of wizards who practiced conjuration in secret chambers lit by candlelight.
She warned that attempting a conjuration could have dangerous and unforeseen consequences.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evocation - the act of calling forth a spirit or supernatural being
  • Antonym: banishment - the act of driving away a spirit or supernatural entity

Etymology

From Latin 'conjuratio' (a swearing together, conspiracy), from 'conjurare' (to swear together, conspire; later, to invoke by oath).

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːndʒəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒndʒəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
a ritual recitation of words or sounds believed to have a magical effect; the act of invoking spirits or supernatural powers through such means
ritual recitation for magical effect

Examples

The wizard began his conjuration, chanting ancient words in the flickering candlelight.
Stories of medieval conjurations often involve summoning spirits or demons for knowledge or power.
The old book described a conjuration to protect one’s home from evil influences.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incantation - a series of words said as a magic spell or charm
  • Synonym: enchantment - the use of magic or spells to cast a charm
  • Antonym: exorcism - the expulsion of an evil spirit by ritual

Etymology

From Latin 'conjurationem' (a swearing together; conspiracy, enchantment), from 'conjurare' (to swear together, conspire, invoke).

3. NOUN

/ ˌkɑndʒəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒndʒəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
an act or display that appears magical or mysterious, especially one that deceives or amazes spectators by seeming to defy ordinary explanation
act of creating illusions or tricks

Examples

The magician's conjuration left the audience spellbound, unable to explain the floating coin.
To the naive children, the escape act seemed like pure conjuration.
Despite knowing it was a trick, she was still delighted by the clever conjuration performed on stage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: illusion - a false or deceptive appearance or impression
  • Synonym: magic - the use of special powers, especially for entertainment

Etymology

From Latin 'conjuratio' (a swearing together; plot, conspiracy), via Old French 'conjuration'.
