synchronic

1. ADJ

/ sɪnˈkrɑnɪk / (AmE), / sɪŋˈkrɒnɪk / (BrE)
concerned with phenomena, especially language, as they exist at one particular period in time, without reference to their historical development
describing phenomena at a specific time

Examples

The linguist conducted a synchronic analysis of English grammar as it is used today.
A synchronic study ignores how the language has changed over the centuries.
In anthropology, a synchronic approach examines cultural practices at a specific historical moment.

Synset Examples

synchronic linguistics

Related Terms

  • Antonym: diachronic - concerned with the development and evolution of something over time

Etymology

From Greek 'synchronos' (contemporary), from 'syn-' (together) + 'chronos' (time).

2. ADJ

/ sɪnˈkrɑnɪk / (AmE), / sɪnˈkrɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of organisms, events, or phenomena) existing or occurring at the same period of geological or historical time.
occurring at the same time period

Examples

The two species are considered synchronic because they existed during the same geological period.
Fossils found in these strata are synchronic and help reconstruct past ecosystems.
The team studied synchronic populations to better understand evolutionary patterns.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: diachronic - concerned with changes over time, especially in language or history

Etymology

From Greek syn- (together) + khronos (time).

3. ADJ

/ sɪnˈkrɑːnɪk / (AmE), / sɪŋˈkrɒnɪk / (BrE)
occurring or existing at the same time or having the same phase or period
existing or occurring at the same time

Examples

The linguist performed a synchronic analysis of the English language as it exists today.
Researchers compared synchronic patterns in economic growth across different countries.
The study focused on synchronic changes in the heart's electrical activity during exercise.
