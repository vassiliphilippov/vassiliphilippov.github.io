syneresis

1. NOUN

/ sɪˈnɛrəsɪs / (AmE), / sɪˈnɛrɪsɪs / (BrE)
the contraction of two adjacent vowels into a single syllable, producing a diphthong
contraction of vowels into a diphthong

Examples

In poetry, syneresis often occurs when two vowels are pronounced together as one syllable.
The word 'poet' can undergo syneresis in rapid speech, sounding like a single syllable.
Linguists study syneresis to understand changes in language pronunciation over time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crasis - the merging of two vowels into one, especially in Greek or Latin

Etymology

From Greek synairesis (a taking or drawing together), from syn- (together) + hairein (to take).

2. NOUN

/ sɪˈnɛrəsɪs / (AmE), / sɪˈnɪərəsɪs / (BrE)
the separation of liquid from a gel caused by contraction, often observed in processes like cheese making or gelatin setting
liquid separation from a gel

Examples

During yogurt production, syneresis can be seen as liquid whey separates from the gel.
To prevent syneresis, the custard must be cooked gently and stirred constantly.
Excessive syneresis can affect the texture and appearance of cheese.

Etymology

From Greek 'synairesis' (a taking or drawing together), from 'syn-' (together) + 'hairein' (to take).
