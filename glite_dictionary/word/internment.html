internment

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtɝːnmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɜːnmənt / (BrE)
the act of confining someone, especially during wartime, in a prison, camp, or other restricted area, often without trial
the act of confining people, often without trial

Examples

The government ordered the internment of enemy aliens during the war.
Thousands were held in internment camps far from their homes.
Her grandfather shared stories about his experiences in internment during World War II.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imprisonment - the state of being held in prison
  • Antonym: release - the act of letting someone go free

Etymology

From French 'interner' (to confine), from Latin 'internus' (inward).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtɜrnmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɜːnmənt / (BrE)
the practice of confining people, especially during wartime, often without trial or due process
confinement of people during wartime

Examples

The government ordered the internment of enemy aliens during the war.
Many families were separated as a result of the mass internment in camps.
His grandfather spoke about his experiences of internment during World War II.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imprisonment - the state of being kept in prison as a punishment or while awaiting trial
  • Synonym: detention - the act of keeping someone in official custody, often as a punishment or during investigation

Etymology

From French 'interner', from Latin 'internare' (to confine within).

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtɝːnmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɜːnmənt / (BrE)
the act of placing private property in the legal custody of an officer, especially as a preventive or protective measure
placing property into legal custody
legal

Examples

The court ordered the internment of the disputed assets until the case was resolved.
Legal internment of goods may occur when ownership is unclear or contested.
Following the investigation, the internment of the car was authorized by the authorities.

Etymology

From French 'internement', from 'interner' (to intern), reflecting the sense of placing or holding; related to 'internal'.
