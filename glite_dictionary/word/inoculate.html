to inoculate

1. VERB

/ ɪˈnɑkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈnɒkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to insert a bud into another plant for the purpose of propagation
insert a bud for plant propagation

Examples

Skilled gardeners often inoculate their plants to produce new varieties.
The tree can be inoculated with a bud from a superior species to improve its fruit.
She taught the students how to inoculate roses to propagate desirable traits.

Etymology

From Latin inoculare, from in- (into) + oculus (eye, bud).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00055835-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪˈnɑk.jə.leɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈnɒk.jʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
to introduce a virus, germ, or antigen into a person or animal, typically by injection, to produce immunity against a disease
introduce a virus/germ to produce immunity
medical

Examples

Doctors now inoculate children against common childhood diseases such as measles and mumps.
Before traveling abroad, she decided to inoculate herself against hepatitis A.
The campaign aims to inoculate thousands of people in remote areas where medical access is limited.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vaccinate - to treat with a vaccine for immunity

Etymology

From Latin *inoculatus*, past participle of *inoculare* (to graft by budding), from *in-* (into) + *oculus* (eye, bud).

Synset POS: v

6. VERB

/ ɪˈnɑkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈnɒkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to introduce beneficial microorganisms or agents into (a garden) to protect plants from pests or disease
introduce agents to protect a garden

Examples

Gardeners often inoculate the soil with beneficial bacteria to prevent root diseases.
Experts recommend inoculating a garden in spring to reduce the risk of pest infestations.
After she inoculated her garden, she noticed fewer signs of aphids on the plants.

Synset Examples

She decided to inoculate her garden from pests.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vaccinate - to treat with a vaccine to produce immunity
  • Antonym: to infect - to contaminate with a disease-producing substance

Etymology

From Latin inoculare, meaning 'to graft a bud.'
