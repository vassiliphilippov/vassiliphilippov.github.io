sensitization

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛn.sə.t̬əˈzeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛn.sɪ.taɪˈzeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of making an organism sensitive to a substance, such as a serum, especially by repeated exposure or injections
making an organism sensitive to a substance
medical

Examples

The repeated injections led to sensitization of the patient's immune system.
Allergic reactions can develop after sensitization to a specific protein.
Scientists study sensitization in animals to understand immune responses.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: desensitization - the process of making an organism less sensitive to a substance

Etymology

From 'sensitize' + '-ation'; 'sensitize' from 'sensitive' + '-ize'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛn.sɪ.t̬əˈzeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛn.sɪ.tɪˈzeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process or state of becoming sensitive, especially to a stimulus or antigen
state of increased sensitivity, esp. to an antigen
medical

Examples

Repeated exposure to the allergen led to sensitization in some patients.
The sensitization of nerve cells can increase a person's response to pain.
Researchers studied the sensitization process to better understand allergic reactions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: desensitization - the process of making less sensitive, especially to an antigen

Etymology

From Latin sensus (feeling) + -ization (forming nouns), via French sensibiliser.

3. NOUN

/ ˌsɛnsətaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛnsɪtaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a person or organism becomes highly sensitive to specific events, situations, or stimuli, especially emotionally or psychologically
becoming more sensitive to a stimulus
medical

Examples

After repeated exposure to stress, sensitization to minor triggers can lead to anxiety attacks.
Some therapies aim to reduce sensitization to traumatic memories in patients with PTSD.
The experiment measured the sensitization of rats to a specific sound after several pairings with a shock.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: desensitization - the process of making someone less sensitive, especially to stimuli or triggers

Etymology

From Latin 'sensibilis' (capable of feeling) via French 'sensitisation'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13575546-n
Synset POS: n
