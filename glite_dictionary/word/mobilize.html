to mobilize

1. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to move or become active; to put into movement or motion
cause to move or become active

Examples

The leader worked to mobilize the entire community for the clean-up effort.
Researchers hope to mobilize cells in the body to fight infection more effectively.
The coach will mobilize the players as soon as practice starts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to activate - to make active; put into operation
  • Antonym: to immobilize - to prevent from moving

Etymology

From French *mobiliser*, from Latin *mobilis* (movable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00270891-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to organize and prepare people or resources for active use or action, especially in order to achieve a particular goal
prepare people or resources for action

Examples

The government decided to mobilize additional resources to deal with the crisis.
We need to mobilize all available staff to meet the tight deadline.
Environmental activists are working to mobilize public support for climate action.

Synset Examples

marshal resources

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marshal - to arrange or assemble people or things in order
  • Synonym: to deploy - to move into position for action

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile' (movable) based on Latin 'mobilis'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00271014-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to prepare and organize troops or people for active military service or action, especially in response to a threat or emergency
prepare troops for military service

Examples

The government decided to mobilize additional forces in response to the crisis.
After the declaration of war, all able-bodied men were quickly mobilized.
Nations may mobilize their armies when faced with an imminent threat.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to demobilize - to release troops from military service
  • Synonym: to deploy - to move troops or resources into position for action

Etymology

From French mobiliser, from mobile (movable), itself from Latin mobilis (movable, agile).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01100371-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to prepare and organize troops or resources for active service, especially for war
prepare for war; organize for action

Examples

The government decided to mobilize its forces after the threat became clear.
Countries often mobilize quickly in response to international crises.
If tensions escalate, the army may be mobilized within hours.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to demobilize - to disband troops or end military mobilization
  • Synonym: to deploy - to move troops or equipment into position for military action

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile' (movable) + '-iser' (-ize).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01090792-v
Synset POS: v
