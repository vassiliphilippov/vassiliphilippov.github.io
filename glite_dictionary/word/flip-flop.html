flip-flop

1. NOUN

/ ˈflɪp ˌflɑp / (AmE), / ˈflɪp ˌflɒp / (BrE)
a sudden or unexpected reversal in policy, opinion, or decision
a reversal of a previous decision

Examples

The senator's flip-flop on the issue angered many of his supporters.
After public outcry, the company made a quick flip-flop and reinstated the old policy.
Voters are often frustrated by politicians' frequent flip-flops during election season.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reversal - the act of changing to the opposite direction, position, or course of action
  • Synonym: U-turn - a sudden or dramatic change in policy or opinion

2. NOUN

/ ˈflɪp ˌflɑp / (AmE), / ˈflɪp ˌflɒp / (BrE)
an electronic circuit that can be switched between two stable states, used for storing binary information
electronic circuit with two stable states

Examples

The D-type flip-flop is commonly used in digital memory circuits.
You can design a simple counter by connecting several flip-flops in sequence.
A flip-flop changes state every time it receives a clock pulse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: latch - an electronic circuit used for storing one bit of data, similar to a flip-flop

3. VERB

/ ˈflɪpˌflɑp / (AmE), / ˈflɪp.flɒp / (BrE)
to suddenly change one's opinion, policy, or course of action to the opposite
change opinion or policy abruptly

Examples

Politicians sometimes flip-flop on issues when public opinion changes.
After much debate, the committee flip-flopped on their decision regarding the new policy.
She tends to flip-flop between supporting different candidates during election season.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vacillate - to waver between different opinions or actions
  • Synonym: waffle - to be indecisive or change one's mind repeatedly

Etymology

From the noun 'flip-flop', imitative of the sound produced, later applied figuratively to describe abrupt reversals.

5. VERB

/ ˈflɪp flɑp / (AmE), / ˈflɪp flɒp / (BrE)
(verb) to change one's opinions, decisions, or positions frequently and suddenly, especially in a way that suggests inconsistency or indecisiveness.
change opinions or decisions frequently
informal

Examples

The politician was criticized for his tendency to flip-flop on important issues.
She used to support the proposal, but now she's flip-flopped again.
It's frustrating when leaders flip-flop instead of taking a clear stance.

Synset Examples

Her opinions seem to flip-flop every week.
He tends to flip-flop between different diets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: waffle - to be indecisive or change opinions frequently

Etymology

Originally from the sound made by a sandal slapping against the foot; verb sense developed from the notion of rapid back-and-forth movement.
