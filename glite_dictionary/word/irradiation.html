irradiation

1. NOUN

/ ɪˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌrædɪˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the apparent enlargement of a bright object when viewed against a dark background, due to a visual perception effect
apparent size increase of a bright object on a dark background

Examples

The irradiation of the white disk made it appear larger than it actually was.
Scientists have studied irradiation to understand how brightness and contrast affect visual perception.
Due to irradiation, a bright light can seem to spread into the surrounding darkness.

Etymology

From Latin ‘irradiatio’, from ‘irradiāre’ meaning ‘to shine upon’.

the treatment of disease, especially cancer, by exposing the body or a part of it to radioactive substances
treatment of disease with radiation
medical

Examples

After her diagnosis, the patient received irradiation as part of her cancer treatment plan.
Advancements in irradiation techniques have reduced side effects compared to older radiation therapies.
Doctors discussed the benefits and risks of irradiation with the patient's family before starting the treatment.

Related Terms

Etymology

4. NOUN

/ ɪˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Physiology) The spread of sensory neural impulses in the cortex, often resulting in sensations being experienced beyond the initial area of stimulation.
spread of neural impulses in the cortex (physiology)
medical

Examples

The irradiation of pain in the cortex can make it difficult to locate the true origin of discomfort.
In severe cases, sensory irradiation may cause a patient to perceive touch in a much wider area than was stimulated.
Neurologists study irradiation to better understand how brain injuries affect the spread of neural signals.

Etymology

From Latin irradiatio, from irradiatus, past participle of irradiare (to shine upon).

5. NOUN

/ ɪˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
In Pavlovian conditioning, the process by which a conditioned response is triggered by a stimulus that is similar but not identical to the original conditioned stimulus.
elicitation of response by similar stimulus in conditioning

Examples

In classical conditioning, irradiation can cause a dog to salivate at tones similar to the original one.
Early psychologists studied irradiation to understand the spread of conditioned responses.
Through irradiation, children might fear objects resembling the original threatening stimulus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: generalization - the tendency to respond in the same way to different but similar stimuli in conditioning
  • Antonym: discrimination - the learned ability to distinguish between a conditioned stimulus and similar stimuli

Etymology

From Latin 'irradiatio' (a shining upon), from 'irradiāre' (to shine upon).
