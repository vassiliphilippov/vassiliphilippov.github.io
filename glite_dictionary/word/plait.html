plait

1. VERB

/ plæt / (AmE), / plɑːt / (BrE)
to weave hair, thread, or other material into a plait or braid
to braid or weave into plaits

Examples

She likes to plait her hair every morning before school.
The stylist quickly plaited the child's ponytail into a neat braid.
Villagers would plait reeds together to make strong baskets.

Synset Examples

plait hair

Related Terms

  • Synonym: braid - to weave together three or more strands, especially hair

Etymology

From Old French *pleit* (a fold), from Latin *plicare* (to fold).

2. VERB

/ plæt / (AmE), / plæt / (BrE)
to make or form something by braiding or interlacing strands, such as hair or threads
braid or interlace strands

Examples

She skillfully plaited her daughter's long hair before school each morning.
The artisan used straw to plait elaborate hats for the festival.
He watched the old woman plait a colorful basket from thin reeds.

3. NOUN

/ plæt / (AmE), / plæt / (BrE)
A length of hair that has been divided into three or more sections and twisted or braided together.
a braid or twist of hair
british

Examples

She wore her hair in a single long plait down her back.
The little girl's mother expertly tied her plaits before school.
He admired the neat plait that kept her hair out of her face.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: braid - a length of hair made by weaving three or more strands together

Etymology

From Old French *pleit* (fold), from Latin *plicatum*, past participle of *plicare* (to fold).

4. NOUN

/ plæt / (AmE), / plɑːt / (BrE)
a fold made by doubling fabric back upon itself and then pressing or stitching it into shape, often used as a decorative or structural feature in garments and textiles
a type of fabric fold or pleat

Examples

The tailor added a sharp plait to the skirt for extra fullness.
Each curtain panel was finished with a neat plait along the top edge.
You can create a decorative effect by stitching a contrasting ribbon into the plait.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pleat - a fold in cloth made by doubling the material on itself and then pressing or stitching it in place

Etymology

From Old French *pleit* (fold), from Latin *plicare* (to fold).
