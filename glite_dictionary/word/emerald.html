emerald

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛmərəld / (AmE), / ˈɛmərəld / (BrE)
a bright green color, like that of an emerald gemstone
a bright green color

Examples

The designer chose an emerald shade for the living room walls.
Her dress shimmered in a deep emerald that caught everyone's attention.
Sunlight reflected on the lake, turning the water an intense emerald.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: green - a color between blue and yellow in the spectrum; the color of grass
  • Degree Higher: forest green - a darker shade of green than emerald

Etymology

From Latin 'smaragdus' via Old French 'esmeraude' (emerald, green gem); adopted for the color by reference to the gem's hue.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛmərəld / (AmE), / ˈɛmərəld / (BrE)
a bright green precious stone, a variety of beryl, highly valued as a gemstone
bright green valuable gemstone

Examples

She wore a necklace set with a large emerald at the gala.
The jeweler examined the emerald under a magnifying glass to assess its quality.
Many royal crowns are decorated with brilliant emeralds and other precious stones.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sapphire - a precious blue gemstone, variety of corundum
  • Synonym: ruby - a precious red gemstone, variety of corundum
  • Synonym: beryl - a mineral, the base for emerald and other gems

Etymology

From Old French 'esmeraude', from Medieval Latin 'esmaralda', from Latin 'smaragdus', from Greek 'smaragdos' (green gem).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɛmərəld / (AmE), / ˈɛmərəld / (BrE)
A bright green precious stone that is a variety of beryl, valued as a gemstone when cut and polished.
bright green precious gemstone

Examples

She wore a stunning necklace set with a large emerald at the gala.
The crown was decorated with diamonds, rubies, and a brilliant emerald at its center.
He gave her an exquisite ring featuring a polished emerald surrounded by small diamonds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ruby - a precious stone that is a red variety of corundum
  • Synonym: sapphire - a precious stone that is a blue variety of corundum
  • Synonym: diamond - a precious stone consisting of a clear and typically colorless crystalline form of pure carbon

Etymology

From Old French *esmeralde*, from Latin *smaragdus*, from Ancient Greek *smaragdos* (green gem).
