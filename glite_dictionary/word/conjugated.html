conjugated

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɑndʒəˌɡeɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈkɒndʒʊɡeɪtɪd / (BrE)
(of an organic compound) containing two or more double bonds each separated by a single bond.
(chem.) with alternating double and single bonds

Examples

Benzene is an example of a conjugated hydrocarbon with alternating double and single bonds in its ring structure.
The dye molecule absorbs light because it has a conjugated system of double bonds.
Vitamin A contains a long conjugated chain, which is essential for its biological activity.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unconjugated - (of an organic compound) not having alternating double and single bonds

Etymology

From Latin conjugatus, past participle of conjugare ('to join together').

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɑndʒəˌɡeɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈkɒndʒʊɡeɪtɪd / (BrE)
joined together, especially in pairs or groups; in chemistry and biology, often referring to structures that are connected or combined.
joined together in pairs or groups

Examples

The conjugated double bonds in the molecule allow it to absorb visible light.
Some bacteria move using conjugated flagella that operate in pairs.
Many medicines are conjugated with other molecules to improve effectiveness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: connected - joined or linked together
  • Antonym: separate - not joined or connected

Etymology

From Latin 'conjugatus', past participle of 'conjugare' (to join together).

3. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːn.dʒəˌɡeɪ.tɪd / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.dʒəˌɡeɪ.tɪd / (BrE)
(of a substance, especially a compound) formed by the chemical union of two or more compounds, often resulting in new properties.
combined chemically; chemically bonded compounds
medical

Examples

Bilirubin in the body may exist in unconjugated or conjugated forms.
Insulin can be attached to other molecules, creating a conjugated hormone.
A conjugated protein consists of a protein joined with a non-protein component.

Synset Examples

Etymology
