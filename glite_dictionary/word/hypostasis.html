hypostasis

1. NOUN

/ haɪˈpɑstəsɪs / (AmE), / hɪˈpɒstəsɪs / (BrE)
(in philosophy) the underlying reality or substance, especially as opposed to attributes or that which is apparent.
underlying reality or essence (philosophy)

Examples

The philosopher spoke of the hypostasis that exists beyond mere appearances.
Classical metaphysics seeks to define the hypostasis underlying all physical phenomena.
In certain doctrines, the soul is considered the hypostasis of the self.

Etymology

From Greek hypostasis (substance, foundation), from hypo- (under) + histanai (to cause to stand).

2. NOUN

/ haɪˈpɑːstəsɪs / (AmE), / hɪˈpɒstəsɪs / (BrE)
(Genetics) The suppression of the effect of one gene by the presence of an unrelated gene.
genetic suppression by another gene
medical

Examples

In some plants, hypostasis can mask the expression of color genes, leading to unexpected flower colors.
The geneticist explained how hypostasis influences the inheritance of certain traits in mice.
Research into hypostasis has helped clarify the complex interactions between different genes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: epistasis - the interaction of genes in which one gene suppresses the expression of another

Etymology

From Greek hupostasis (substance, foundation), from hupo- (under) + stasis (standing).

3. NOUN

/ haɪˈpɑstəsɪs / (AmE), / hɪˈpɒstəsɪs / (BrE)
an accumulation of blood in an organ or body part, usually after death, causing discoloration of the tissue
accumulation of blood in an organ or area
medical

Examples

After death, hypostasis can cause a purplish discoloration in the dependent parts of the body.
Pathologists often examine areas of hypostasis to estimate the time of death.
The visible hypostasis confirmed the body's position had not changed after death.

Etymology

From Greek hypostasis (foundation, sediment, subsidence) via Late Latin.

4. NOUN

/ haɪˈpɑːstəˌsɪs / (AmE), / hʌɪˈpɒstəsɪs / (BrE)
(in Christian theology) Any of the three persons of the Trinity, especially the person of Christ in whom divine and human natures are united.
theological term: person of the Trinity

Examples

In Christian doctrine, each hypostasis of the Trinity is distinct yet shares the same essence.
The union of divine and human in Christ is known as the hypostasis or hypostatic union.
Theologians debated for centuries how many hypostases exist within the Godhead.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: person - one of the three distinct persons of the Christian Trinity

Etymology

From Greek hypostasis (substance, underlying state), from hypo- (under) + stasis (standing).
