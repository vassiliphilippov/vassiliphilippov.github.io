stockade

1. NOUN

/ stɑˈkeɪd / (AmE), / stɒˈkeɪd / (BrE)
a camp or area where prisoners, especially political prisoners or prisoners of war, are confined, often under harsh conditions
penal camp for prisoners

Examples

Many political opponents were detained in the stockade during the conflict.
The prisoners attempted to escape from the heavily guarded stockade.
Conditions in the stockade were harsh, with little food and poor sanitation.

Etymology

From Spanish *estacada* (stockade, palisade), from *estaca* (stake).

2. VERB

/ stɑːˈkeɪd / (AmE), / stɒˈkeɪd / (BrE)
to surround a place with a barrier of strong posts or stakes to fortify it against attack
fortify by surrounding with a stockade

Examples

The settlers decided to stockade their camp for protection against intruders.
During the conflict, soldiers quickly stockaded the perimeter with sharpened logs.
They spent days stockading the outpost to prepare for a possible siege.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fortify - to strengthen a place against attack, especially by building defensive works

Etymology

From French *estacade* (barrier of stakes), from Provençal *estacada*, from *estaca* (stake).

3. NOUN

/ stɑːˈkeɪd / (AmE), / stɒˈkeɪd / (BrE)
a strong fence made of close-set wooden posts or stakes, built as a defensive barrier around a place
defensive barrier of wooden posts

Examples

The soldiers quickly built a stockade to protect the camp from wild animals.
Prisoners were kept inside a stockade during the war.
The old frontier town was surrounded by a wooden stockade for security.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: palisade - a fence of stakes or iron railings forming an enclosure or defense

Etymology

From French 'stockade', from Spanish 'estacada' (a palisade), from 'estaca' (stake).
