rumble

1. NOUN

/ ˈrʌmbəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌmbəl / (BrE)
a servant's seat or a luggage compartment at the rear of a carriage, especially in older horse-drawn vehicles
servant's seat or luggage space in carriage

Examples

In the 19th century, wealthy families often had a servant ride in the rumble of their carriage.
The small trunk in the rumble was used to store the travelers' baggage.
He waved goodbye from the rumble as the carriage pulled away.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrʌmbəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌmbəl / (BrE)
a violent fight between rival gangs of young people, especially in urban areas
gang fight among adolescents
informal american

Examples

The two gangs met in the alley for a rumble late last night.
Rumors spread quickly through the school that a big rumble was about to happen.
Several people were injured during the rumble between the rival groups.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brawl - a noisy fight in a crowd or public place

3. VERB

/ ˈrʌm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌm.bəl / (BrE)
to make or produce a low, continuous, deep, resonant sound
make a low, continuous sound

Examples

Thunder began to rumble in the distance as the storm approached.
Her stomach started to rumble loudly during the quiet meeting.
The old truck's engine would often rumble to life with a deep growl.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grumble - to complain or mutter in a low voice

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound; related to Middle English 'rumbelen'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈrʌm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌm.bəl / (BrE)
a loud, low, continuous, and deep sound or noise
low, continuous deep noise

Examples

The distant rumble of thunder signaled an approaching storm.
We could feel the rumble of the train as it passed beneath our feet.
There was a low rumble coming from the furnace in the basement.

Synset Examples

they heard the rumbling of thunder

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roar - a full, deep, prolonged loud noise
  • Degree Lower: hum - a low, steady continuous sound
  • Synonym: thunder - a loud rumbling sound associated with storms

Etymology

Imitative origin, from Middle English 'rumblen', resembling the sound itself.
