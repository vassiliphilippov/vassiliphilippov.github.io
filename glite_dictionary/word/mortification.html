mortification

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɔrt̬əfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɔːtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the localized death of living tissue, often due to infection or loss of blood supply; gangrene
localized death of living tissue (medical)
medical

Examples

The surgeon noted that mortification had set in and recommended immediate amputation.
If left untreated, the infection can result in mortification of the affected limb.
Efforts to restore blood flow may prevent mortification after a severe injury.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gangrene - death and decay of body tissue, often caused by loss of blood supply

Etymology

From Latin 'mortificatio' (causing death), from 'mortificare' (to cause death).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɔrtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɔːtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
A feeling of great embarrassment, shame, or humiliation.
strong embarrassment or shame

Examples

She turned bright red in mortification when she realized her mistake in front of the entire class.
To his mortification, he slipped and fell during the important presentation.
The letter was read aloud, much to his mortification and dismay.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embarrassment - a feeling of self-consciousness, shame, or awkwardness
  • Synonym: humiliation - the action of humiliating someone or the state of being humiliated

Etymology

From Latin 'mortificatio' (a killing, a subduing), from 'mortificare' (to put to death).

3. NOUN

/ ˌmɔrtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɔːtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Christianity) The act of suppressing bodily desires or passions, especially by self-denial, privation, or inflicting discomfort or pain on oneself as a religious discipline.
self-denial or self-inflicted discomfort for religious reasons

Examples

The monk practiced mortification through fasting and sleep deprivation as part of his spiritual discipline.
Some saints are known for their rigorous mortification of the flesh, seeking holiness through self-denial.
In medieval times, physical mortification was seen as a means to overcome worldly temptations.

Etymology

From Latin mortificatio, from mortificare (to put to death), referring originally to putting sinful desires to death.
