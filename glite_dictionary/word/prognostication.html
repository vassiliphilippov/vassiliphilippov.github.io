prognostication

1. NOUN

/ prɑːɡˌnɑːstəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / prɒɡˌnɒstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of predicting or foretelling future events, especially through signs or supernatural means
prediction or foretelling of future events

Examples

The oracle's prognostication foretold a great disaster for the city.
His prognostication was dismissed as mere superstition until events began to unfold as he had predicted.
Ancient cultures often relied on prognostication to make important decisions about harvests and wars.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prediction - an act of predicting what will happen in the future

Etymology

From Latin prognosticatio (a foretelling), from prognosticare (to foretell) and Greek prognosis (knowledge beforehand).

2. NOUN

/ prɑːɡˌnɑstɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / prɒɡˌnɒstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
a sign or indication of a future event; an omen or prediction
sign of a future event; omen

Examples

The sudden drop in stock prices was seen as a prognostication of economic trouble.
Many considered the eclipse to be a dark prognostication for the kingdom.
Her prognostication of rain came true when the storm arrived that afternoon.

Synset Examples

he looked for an omen before going into battle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: omen - a sign thought to predict the future

Etymology

From Latin prognosticatio (foretelling), from Greek prognōstikos (foreknowing).

3. NOUN

/ prɑgˌnɑstəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / prɒgˌnɒstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
A statement or prediction about what will happen in the future, often based on analysis or foresight.
prediction about the future

Examples

Her prognostication about the economy proved to be remarkably accurate.
Despite the meteorologist's prognostication of rain, the day remained sunny.
He dismissed the gloomy prognostication as mere speculation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prediction - A forecast or statement about what will happen in the future

Etymology

From Latin prognosticatio(n-), from prognosticare 'to foretell'.
