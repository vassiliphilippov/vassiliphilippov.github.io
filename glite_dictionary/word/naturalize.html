to naturalize

1. VERB

/ ˈnætʃrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnatʃrəlʌɪz / (BrE)
to adapt a wild plant or unclaimed land to the local environment, making it able to grow or be productive there
adapt plant or land to environment

Examples

Many settlers tried to naturalize foreign crops to increase food supply in the new land.
Scientists are working to naturalize the area by reintroducing native plants.
Gardeners often naturalize daffodils by allowing them to spread naturally in the wild.

Synset Examples

domesticate oats
tame the soil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to domesticate - to tame (an animal or plant) for human use
  • Synonym: to cultivate - to prepare and use land for crops or gardening

Etymology

From French 'naturaliser', from Latin 'naturalis' (natural).

2. VERB

/ ˈnætʃərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnatʃ(ə)rəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make something appear more natural or lifelike
make more natural or lifelike

Examples

The artist skillfully naturalized the scenery in her painting, making it look vibrant and lifelike.
To naturalize the habitat, the designers added native plants and flowing water features.
The director wanted to naturalize the actors' movements to create a more realistic atmosphere.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: artificialize - to make something less natural; to give an unnatural appearance

Etymology

From French 'naturaliser', from Medieval Latin 'naturalizare', meaning 'to make natural'.

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈnætʃərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnatʃərəlaɪz / (BrE)
to adapt or accustom something to a new environment, culture, or context, so that it seems normal or typical there
to adapt to a new environment or culture

Examples

The ancient myths were naturalized into the culture of the new country.
Foreign words are often naturalized into a language when they are commonly used.
The French recipe has been naturalized for American tastes over the years.

Synset Examples

The stories had become naturalized into an American setting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assimilate - to absorb and integrate into a wider society or culture

Etymology

From Latin 'naturalizare' (to make natural), from 'natura' (nature).

4. VERB

/ ˈnætʃrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnatʃrəlʌɪz / (BrE)
to make (someone) a citizen of a country they were not born in, especially through a legal process
to grant citizenship

Examples

After living in Canada for several years, she decided to naturalize and became a Canadian citizen.
The law allows permanent residents to naturalize if they meet certain requirements.
Thousands of immigrants wish to naturalize and participate fully in civic life.

Synset Examples

The French family was naturalized last year

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to denaturalize - to deprive (someone) of citizenship

Etymology

From Middle French naturaliser, from Medieval Latin naturalizare, from Latin natura (birth, nature).

5. VERB

/ ˈnætʃərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnatʃrəlʌɪz / (BrE)
to explain or interpret something by reference to natural causes or the laws of nature, rather than supernatural or arbitrary explanations
explain by reference to nature

Examples

Many philosophers seek to naturalize ethics by grounding it in human biology and psychology.
The historian attempted to naturalize religious phenomena by attributing them to social and environmental factors.
Scientists often naturalize mysterious events by uncovering underlying physical laws.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to rationalize - to explain or justify with logical reasons
  • Antonym: supernaturalize - to explain as being caused by supernatural forces

Etymology

From French 'naturaliser', based on Latin 'natura' (nature).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00941788-v
Synset POS: v
