giddy

1. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪdi / (AmE), / ˈɡɪdi / (BrE)
lacking seriousness or behaving in a light-hearted or frivolous way; silly or playful.
frivolous or silly; lacking seriousness

Examples

Her giddy laughter made everyone in the office smile.
The students grew giddy with excitement on the last day of school.
I felt a bit giddy after hearing the ridiculous rumor.

Synset Examples

a dizzy blonde
light-headed teenagers
silly giggles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: silly - lacking in seriousness or common sense; foolish
  • Antonym: serious - thoughtful, with gravity or earnestness

Etymology

From Old English *gidig* (insane, mad), related to *giddan* (to sing, speak in ecstasy).

2. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪdi / (AmE), / ˈɡɪdi / (BrE)
feeling as if you might fall because you are lightheaded or experiencing a whirling sensation; likely to make someone feel unsteady or dizzy
feeling dizzy or unsteady

Examples

Standing on the edge of the tall building made me feel giddy.
She felt giddy after spinning around in circles for so long.
The giddy height of the mountain left the climbers breathless and unsteady.

Synset Examples

had a dizzy spell
a dizzy pinnacle
had a headache and felt giddy
a giddy precipice
feeling woozy from the blow on his head
a vertiginous climb up the face of the cliff

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dizzy - feeling as if you might fall or lose balance
  • Antonym: steady - not likely to fall; firmly fixed and balanced

Etymology

From Old English 'gidig' meaning insane or mad, related to 'giddiness'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02554818-s
Synset POS: s
