vowel

1. NOUN

/ ˈvaʊəl / (AmE), / ˈvaʊəl / (BrE)
A speech sound produced with the vocal tract open, allowing the air to flow freely through the mouth.
a speech sound made with open vocal tract

Examples

The letter "a" represents a vowel sound in many words.
Children learn the difference between vowel and consonant sounds early in school.
Each syllable in English must contain at least one vowel.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consonant - a speech sound produced with some constriction or closure in the vocal tract

Etymology

From Latin 'vocalis' (relating to voice), from 'vox' (voice).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvaʊəl / (AmE), / ˈvaʊəl / (BrE)
A letter of the alphabet that represents a vowel sound, such as 'a', 'e', 'i', 'o', or 'u'.
letter representing a vowel sound

Examples

The word 'apple' begins with a vowel in both spelling and pronunciation.
English has five main vowel letters: a, e, i, o, and u.
When writing a plural, add 'es' if the noun ends in a vowel followed by 'y'.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consonant - a letter representing a non-vowel sound

Etymology

From Old French 'vowel', from Latin 'vocalis' (vocal).
