impulse

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
a sudden force or influence that causes something to move or change direction, especially in physics, an impelling force acting over a short time
a sudden force causing movement

Examples

The rocket received an additional impulse from its booster engines during launch.
An unexpected impulse sent the ball flying across the field.
In physics, impulse is calculated as the product of force and the time over which it acts.

Synset Examples

the car's momentum carried it off the road

Etymology

From Latin impulsus (a push against), from impellere (to push against).

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)

sudden desire or urge

Examples

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
(electronics) A sharp, brief surge or wave of electrical energy, or a series of such transients, marking a sudden change in a circuit's electrical state.
sudden electrical surge or wave

Examples

The circuit board is designed to respond quickly to an impulse.
A sharp impulse was detected in the signal during the test.
The radio receiver filtered out every unwanted impulse to improve sound clarity.

Synset Examples

the pulsations seemed to be coming from a star

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - a single vibration or short burst of sound, electric current, light, or other wave

Etymology

From Latin 'impulsus' (a push against), from 'impellere' (to drive or push against).

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)

medical

Examples

Doctors study the speed of impulses along nerves to diagnose certain diseases.
The transmission of a single impulse can activate several muscles at once.
