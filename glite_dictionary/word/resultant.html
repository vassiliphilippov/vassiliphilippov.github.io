resultant

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈzʌltənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzʌltənt / (BrE)
occurring as a result or consequence of something; following as an effect.
due to a previous cause or event

Examples

The resultant damage from the storm was much worse than expected.
Costs were reduced, and the resultant savings helped the company survive.
Pollution was an unfortunate resultant effect of rapid industrial expansion.

Synset Examples

an excessive growth of bureaucracy, with attendant problems
snags incidental to the changeover in management
attendant circumstances
the period of tension and consequent need for military preparedness
the ensuant response to his appeal
the resultant savings were considerable
collateral target damage from a bombing run

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consequent - following as a result or effect
  • Antonym: causal - expressing or indicating a cause

Etymology

From Latin 'resultare' (to spring back).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈzʌltənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzʌltənt / (BrE)
the final outcome or point reached as a result of a process
final outcome, end point of a process

Examples

The resultant of years of research was a groundbreaking discovery.
After months of negotiation, the resultant agreement satisfied all parties.
Careful planning and execution led to a positive resultant for the team.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: result - an outcome or consequence of an action, event, or process

Etymology

From Latin 'resultare' (to spring back, result), via French 'resultant'.

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈzʌltənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzʌltənt / (BrE)
a thing that is produced as a result of something else, especially in mathematics and science
something produced as a result

Examples

The combined forces produce a resultant that points to the right.
When these chemicals react, the resultant is a harmless gas.
Mathematicians often calculate the resultant of two polynomials to determine their common roots.

Synset Examples

he listened for the results on the radio

Related Terms

  • Synonym: result - an outcome or consequence of an action or process

Etymology

From Latin resultans, present participle of resultare (to rebound, result).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07307418-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ rɪˈzʌltənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzʌltənt / (BrE)
a vector representing the sum of two or more other vectors
vector formed by vector addition

Examples

The resultant is obtained by adding the two force vectors together.
When calculating the resultant of several vectors, direction must also be considered.
The engineer drew the resultant to represent the combined effect of the applied forces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vector - a quantity with both magnitude and direction

Etymology

From Latin resultare (to spring back), via the noun use of the English 'resultant' (that which results).
