to liquefy

1. VERB

/ ˈlɪkwɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈlɪkwɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to make or become liquid, especially by heating or melting a solid
make or become liquid

Examples

The heat will soon liquefy the candle wax.
Scientists can liquefy nitrogen for use in experiments.
It is important to liquefy the chocolate before adding it to the recipe.

Synset Examples

liquefy the silver

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melt - to change from a solid to a liquid by heating
  • Antonym: to solidify - to make or become solid

Etymology

From Latin liquefacere, from liquere (to be liquid) + facere (to make).

2. VERB

/ ˈlɪkwəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈlɪkwɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to become liquid, or to make something become liquid; to turn from a solid or gas into a liquid state
to become or make liquid

Examples

Heat will liquefy the wax, making it easy to mold into any shape.
As the ice cream sat in the sun, it began to liquefy and drip down the cone.
Certain gases liquefy when compressed at low temperatures.

Synset Examples

The garden air overnight liquefied into a morning dew

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to solidify - to make or become solid
  • Synonym: melt - to change from a solid to a liquid under heat
  • Antonym: freeze - to become solid due to cold

Etymology

From Medieval Latin liquefacere, from Latin liquere (to be liquid) + facere (to make).

3. VERB

/ ˈlɪkwəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈlɪkwɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to become or make something become liquid, especially as a result of heating
to become liquid (often by heat)

Examples

The intense heat caused the metal to liquefy almost instantly.
If the ice cream begins to liquefy, put it back in the freezer quickly.
Certain gases can liquefy under high pressure and low temperature.

Synset Examples

the frozen fat liquefied

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melt - to change from solid to liquid state by heating
  • Antonym: to solidify - to make or become solid

Etymology

From Latin 'liquefacere', from 'liquere' (to be liquid) + 'facere' (to make).
