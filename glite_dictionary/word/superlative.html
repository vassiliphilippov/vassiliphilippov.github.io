superlative

1. NOUN

/ suːˈpɜːrlətɪv / (AmE), / suːˈpɜːlətɪv / (BrE)
the form of an adjective or adverb that expresses the highest degree of a quality, used to compare three or more things (usually formed in English by adding '-est' or by using 'most' or 'least')
highest degree form of adjective or adverb

Examples

The superlative of 'happy' is 'happiest'.
When writing, you can use a superlative to show that something has the most of a quality.
She received the superlative form of praise from her teachers.

Synset Examples

‘fastest’ is the superlative of the adjective ‘fast’
‘least famous’ is the superlative degree of the adjective ‘famous’
‘most surely’ is the superlative of the adverb ‘surely’

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: comparative - the form of an adjective or adverb expressing a higher degree, used to compare two things

Etymology

From Latin superlativus (exaggerated, highest degree), from superlatus, past participle of superferre (carry over).

2. NOUN

/ suˈpɝː.lə.tɪv / (AmE), / suːˈpɜː.lə.tɪv / (BrE)
an exaggerated or extremely enthusiastic expression, especially one of praise
an exaggerated expression of praise

Examples

Every review was filled with superlatives about his performance.
She spoke of the accomplishment using a superlative that bordered on flattery.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hyperbole - exaggerated statements or claims not meant to be taken literally

Etymology

From Latin superlativus (carried beyond), from superlatus, past participle of superferre (to carry over or beyond).

3. NOUN

/ suːˈpɝːlətɪv / (AmE), / suːˈpɜːlətɪv / (BrE)
the highest degree or quality attainable; something or someone at the utmost level of excellence or achievement
the highest degree, the utmost level

Examples

The judge described the cake as a superlative among all the entries in the competition.
Winning the Nobel Prize is considered a superlative in the field of literature.
