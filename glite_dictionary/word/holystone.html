holystone

1. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊliˌstoʊn / (AmE), / ˈhəʊliˌstəʊn / (BrE)
a block of soft sandstone used for scrubbing and cleaning the wooden decks of ships
sandstone block for scrubbing ship decks

Examples

Sailors once used a holystone to clean the wooden decks every morning.
The captain inspected the crew as they scrubbed the ship with holystones.
A worn holystone lay abandoned by the rail after the cleaning was done.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sandstone - a sedimentary rock composed mainly of sand-sized minerals or rock grains

Etymology

From the practice of sailors kneeling in a posture reminiscent of prayer ('holy') when scrubbing decks.

2. VERB

/ ˈhoʊliˌstaɪn / (AmE), / ˈhəʊliˌstaɪn / (BrE)
to scrub the deck of a ship with a block of soft sandstone called a holystone, traditionally as a method of cleaning wooden decks
scrub a ship's deck with a holystone

Examples

The sailors would holystone the deck every morning to keep it spotless.
After a long voyage, the crew carefully holystoned the wooden planks to remove all traces of salt and dirt.
It was tradition for young seamen to spend hours holystoning the deck as part of their daily duties.

Synset Examples

holystone the ship's deck

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scrub - clean something by rubbing hard, especially with a brush
  • Synonym: polish - to make something smooth and shiny by rubbing

Etymology

From the noun 'holystone,' said to derive from sailors kneeling in prayer-like positions while scrubbing, or from the stones sometimes being taken from church ruins.
