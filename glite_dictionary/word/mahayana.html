Mahayana

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɑː.həˈjɑː.nə / (AmE), / ˌmɑː.hɑːˈjɑː.nə / (BrE)
a major school of Buddhism that emphasizes universal salvation and social concern, widely practiced in China, Japan, Tibet, Nepal, Korea, and Mongolia
a major school of Buddhism

Examples

Scholars have long debated the origins of Mahayana Buddhism and its distinctive doctrines.
Zen Buddhism developed as a branch within Mahayana traditions in China and Japan.
Many temples across East Asia are dedicated to the teachings of Mahayana Buddhism.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Hinayana - the other main school of Buddhism, meaning 'lesser vehicle'

Etymology

From Sanskrit mahāyāna, meaning 'great vehicle'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɑː.həˈjɑː.nə / (AmE), / ˌmɑː.həˈjɑː.nə / (BrE)
one of the two main branches of Buddhism, emphasizing the quest for universal salvation and accessible to a broader group of followers; the dominant form in China, Tibet, and Japan
major branch of Buddhism focusing on universal salvation

Examples

Mahayana Buddhism stresses compassion and the path toward enlightenment for all beings.
Many temples in Japan practice the Mahayana tradition of Buddhism.
The principles of Mahayana deeply influenced art and culture throughout East Asia.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Theravada - the other principal branch of Buddhism, focusing on personal enlightenment; prevalent in Southeast Asia

Etymology

From Sanskrit महायान (mahāyāna, literally ‘great vehicle’).
