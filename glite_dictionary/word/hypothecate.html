to hypothecate

1. VERB

/ haɪˈpɑːθəˌkeɪt / (AmE), / hɪˈpɒθɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to pledge property as security for a loan without giving up possession or title to it
pledge property without transferring possession
legal

Examples

Under the agreement, the borrower may hypothecate the car as collateral while continuing to use it.
Banks sometimes hypothecate securities on behalf of their clients to secure additional funding.
You should never hypothecate your house unless you fully understand the terms of the loan.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pledge - to formally promise or give something as security for a debt

Etymology

From Latin hypothecare, from Greek hupothēkē (pledge).

2. VERB

/ haɪˈpɑːθəˌkeɪt / (AmE), / hɪˈpɒθɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to suppose or believe something based on uncertain or tentative grounds; to conjecture
suppose based on uncertain grounds

Examples

Some historians hypothecate that the civilization collapsed due to prolonged drought.
The scientist hypothecated that a new planet might exist beyond the known solar system.
Without strong evidence, we can only hypothecate about the origins of this ancient artifact.

Synset Examples

Scientists supposed that large dinosaurs lived in swamps

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppose - to believe something to be true without certain proof
  • Synonym: conjecture - to form an opinion or supposition about something on the basis of incomplete information

Etymology

From Late Latin hypothecat-, past participle of hypothecare (to pledge), from Greek hupotithenai (to suppose, to put under).
