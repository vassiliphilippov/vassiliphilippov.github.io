Amaranthus hypochondriacus

1. NOUN

/ ˌæməˈrænθəs ˌhaɪpoʊˌkɑnˌdriˈækəs / (AmE), / ˌæməˈrænθəs ˌhɪpəʊˌkɒndrɪˈakəs / (BrE)
A species of amaranth whose leaves are sometimes eaten as greens and seeds used as a cereal, native to the southern United States, Central America, India, and China.
species of amaranth plant used for food

Examples

The seeds of Amaranthus hypochondriacus can be ground into flour and used in baking.
Traditional dishes in Mexico sometimes include the young leaves of Amaranthus hypochondriacus as a vegetable.
Farmers in India cultivate Amaranthus hypochondriacus both for its leafy greens and its nutritious seeds.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining 'Amaranthus' (the genus name from Greek amaranthos, unfading flower) and 'hypochondriacus', of obscure origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˌæməˈrænθəs haɪpəˌkɑndriˈækəs / (AmE), / ˌæməˈranθəs hʌɪpəˌkɒndriˈakəs / (BrE)
a tall, showy annual plant native to tropical America, with hairy stems and long spikes of usually red flowers; its seeds are sometimes used as a cereal
tropical American annual plant with red flower spikes

Examples

Amaranthus hypochondriacus is often grown in gardens for its striking red flower spikes.
Farmers in some regions cultivate Amaranthus hypochondriacus for its protein-rich seeds.
The leaves of Amaranthus hypochondriacus are sometimes used as a leafy vegetable in traditional cuisine.

Etymology

Amaranthus from Greek 'amarantos' (unfading); hypochondriacus from Latin, originally referring to the region below the cartilage of the breastbone.
