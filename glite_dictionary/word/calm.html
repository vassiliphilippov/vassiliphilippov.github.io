calm

1. NOUN

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
A state of mental composure and steadiness, especially in difficult or stressful situations.
mental composure or steadiness

Examples

Even in the face of criticism, she maintained her calm and addressed the issue professionally.
The coach's calm under pressure helped inspire confidence in the team.
During the emergency, his calm was reassuring to everyone around him.

Synset Examples

he accepted their problems with composure and she with equanimity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: composure - calmness and self-control, especially under stress
  • Antonym: agitation - a state of nervousness or anxiety

Etymology

From Latin 'calma' (sea without wind, quiet weather).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
A period or condition in which the wind speed is less than 1 knot, rated as 0 on the Beaufort scale.
complete absence of wind (meteorology)

Examples

After the storm passed, a profound calm settled over the sea.
The sailors welcomed the calm, taking the opportunity to repair their rigging.
In the early morning calm, not a ripple disturbed the surface of the lake.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stillness - the absence of movement or wind
  • Degree Higher: breeze - a light and gentle wind
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11451731-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
(of weather or water) free from storm, disturbance, or wind; peaceful and still.
weather or water without wind or disturbance

Examples

The calm sea stretched out to the horizon, undisturbed by even the slightest breeze.
They sailed safely thanks to the calm weather predicted for the afternoon.
After the storm passed, the lake was calm and glassy, perfect for rowing.

Synset Examples

calm seas

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stormy - (of weather) characterized by storms, wind, or turbulence

Etymology

From Latin 'calmus' via French 'calme', meaning peaceful or quiet.
