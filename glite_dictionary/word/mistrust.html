mistrust

1. NOUN

/ mɪsˈtrʌst / (AmE), / mɪsˈtrʌst / (BrE)
lack of trust or confidence in someone or something, especially regarding honesty or reliability.
lack of trust or confidence

Examples

Years of secrecy and lies bred mistrust between the two nations.
Her voice was calm but tinged with mistrust.
There is growing mistrust of the government among the public.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: trust - belief in the reliability or honesty of someone or something
  • Synonym: distrust - lack of trust or confidence

Etymology

From Middle English, from mis- (wrongly) + trust.

2. VERB

/ mɪsˈtrʌst / (AmE), / mɪsˈtrʌst / (BrE)
to have no trust or confidence in someone or something; to regard with suspicion.
to regard with suspicion or lack of trust

Examples

I tend to mistrust people who make promises too easily.
She mistrusted the information because it came from an unreliable source.
Many voters mistrust politicians who change their positions frequently.

Synset Examples

I suspect her true motives
she distrusts her stepmother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distrust - to have no trust or confidence in; suspect
  • Antonym: trust - to believe in the reliability or honesty of someone or something

Etymology

From mis- (expressing negation) + trust; modeled after Old French 'mescreire' (to disbelieve).

3. NOUN

/ mɪsˈtrʌst / (AmE), / mɪsˈtrʌst / (BrE)
Lack of trust or confidence in someone or something; the tendency to be suspicious or not to believe others.
lack of trust or confidence

Examples

Her mistrust of strangers made it difficult for her to form new friendships.
Years of betrayal had left him with a deep mistrust of authority figures.
There is a growing sense of mistrust between the two communities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distrust - lack of trust or confidence
  • Antonym: trust - firm belief in reliability, truth, or ability

Etymology

From Old French *mestruste*, from mes- (mis-) + truste (trust).
