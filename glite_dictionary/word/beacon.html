beacon

1. NOUN

/ ˈbiːkən / (AmE), / ˈbiːkən / (BrE)
a tower or structure with a light or fire, set up as a warning signal for ships at sea or to guide them safely
tower or light signaling danger to ships

Examples

The ship navigated safely to shore by following the beacon shining from the rocky cliffs.
During foggy nights, the beacon was vital for sailors to avoid the dangerous shoals.
A powerful beacon flashed every minute to warn vessels of the coastline.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lighthouse - a tower with a powerful light to guide ships

Etymology

From Old English *bēacn* (sign, signal), related to Old High German *bouhhan* (sign).

The sailors relied on the distant beacon to guide them safely through the fog.
At midnight, villagers gathered to watch the beacon blaze against the dark sky.

3. VERB

/ ˈbiːkən / (AmE), / ˈbiːkən / (BrE)
to shine or glow like a beacon, often serving as a guide or signal
shine or glow as a guide

Examples

A lighthouse beacons along the rocky coast, guiding ships safely to shore.
Her smile beaconed across the crowded room, drawing everyone's attention.
In the darkness, the campfire beaconed warmly, offering comfort to the lost hikers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gleam - shine with a faint or brief light
  • Synonym: glow - give out a steady light
  • Antonym: fade - become dim or less bright

Etymology

From Old English beacen (sign, portent, lighthouse).

4. NOUN

/ ˈbiːkən / (AmE), / ˈbiːkən / (BrE)
a radio station that transmits a signal to guide ships, aircraft, or vehicles, especially for navigational purposes
radio signal for navigation

Examples

The pilot relied on the beacon to safely navigate through the fog.
Ships use a coastal beacon to determine their position at sea.
A malfunctioning radio beacon can cause serious confusion for approaching aircraft.

Related Terms

Etymology

From Old English *béacn* (sign, signal), related to signalling devices.
