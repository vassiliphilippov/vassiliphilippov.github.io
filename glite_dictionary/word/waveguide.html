waveguide

1. NOUN

/ ˈweɪvˌɡaɪd / (AmE), / ˈweɪvˌɡaɪd / (BrE)
A hollow metal tube or structure designed to direct and confine the path of electromagnetic waves, especially microwaves.
hollow conductor guiding microwaves

Examples

Microwave ovens often use a waveguide to channel energy from the magnetron to the cooking chamber.
The radar system's efficiency depends on the quality of its waveguide.
Engineers inspected the waveguide for any signs of corrosion or damage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fiber optic cable - a cable that transmits light signals for communication

Etymology

From 'wave' (referring to electromagnetic waves) and 'guide', coined in the 20th century with the advent of microwave technology.

2. NOUN

/ ˈweɪvˌɡaɪd / (AmE), / ˈweɪvɡʌɪd / (BrE)
A hollow metal or dielectric tube that conducts and guides microwaves or radio waves, especially in radar and communications equipment.
hollow tube for guiding microwaves or radio waves

Examples

The microwave signal is directed through the waveguide to the antenna.
Engineers inspected the waveguide for any signs of damage or corrosion.
Radar systems often rely on a waveguide to transmit high-frequency signals efficiently.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transmission line - conductor or structure transmitting electromagnetic signals

Etymology

Formed from 'wave' + 'guide', indicating its function of guiding electromagnetic waves.
