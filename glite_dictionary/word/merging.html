merging

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɝːdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈmɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
the act or process of joining or combining things together, especially so that they become one
the process of joining together

Examples

The merging of the two companies created a powerful new corporation.
At the river delta, there is a noticeable merging of fresh and salt water.
Technological advances are leading to a gradual merging of media platforms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fusion - the process of joining two or more things together to form a single entity
  • Antonym: separation - the act of moving things apart or keeping them apart

Etymology

From the verb ‘merge’ (from Latin ‘mergere’, meaning ‘to dip, plunge’), with -ing suffix forming a noun.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɜrdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈmɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
the act or process of joining two or more things into one
the act of joining together

Examples

The merging of the two companies created a new industry giant.
Rapid merging of traffic lanes caused a slowdown during rush hour.
Her research focuses on the merging of traditional and modern art forms.

Synset Examples

the merging of the two groups occurred quickly
there was no meeting of minds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fusion - the process or result of joining two or more things together to form a single entity
  • Antonym: separation - the action or state of moving or being moved apart

Etymology

From the verb 'merge', from Latin mergere (to dip, immerse).

3. ADJ

/ ˈmɝːdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈmɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
coming together or joining as one, especially of separate parts, ideas, or entities; flowing together.
joining or combining into one

Examples

The merging of the two rivers creates a powerful current.
Her ideas are the result of a merging of Eastern and Western philosophies.
The technology industry is constantly merging different platforms to offer better solutions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: combining - joining or mixing two or more things together
  • Synonym: uniting - bringing together to form a single entity
  • Antonym: separating - moving or being moved apart

Etymology

From the verb 'merge', which comes from Latin 'mergere' (to dip, plunge).
