bludgeon

1. NOUN

/ ˈblʌdʒən / (AmE), / ˈblʌdʒən / (BrE)
a thick, heavy stick or club used as a weapon
heavy club used as a weapon

Examples

The suspect was carrying a bludgeon when he was arrested by the police.
Medieval guards often carried a bludgeon to protect the city gates.
He fashioned a makeshift bludgeon from a broken chair leg.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: club - a heavy stick used as a weapon
  • Synonym: baton - a stick used as a weapon or staff

Etymology

Perhaps of unknown origin, possibly related to earlier English 'bludge' (to beat) or of dialectal origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈblʌdʒən / (AmE), / ˈblʌdʒən / (BrE)
to strike someone repeatedly with a heavy object or club
strike with a heavy club

Examples

The criminal tried to bludgeon the guard during the escape.
He bludgeoned his way through the crowd with a large stick.
The suspect was accused of bludgeoning the victim with a heavy object.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beat - strike repeatedly
  • Synonym: club - to hit with a heavy stick
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01426583-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈblʌdʒən / (AmE), / ˈblʌdʒən / (BrE)
to force or coerce someone to do something by using aggressive pressure or repeated arguments, as if striking with a heavy club
coerce or force (figuratively)

Examples

He tried to bludgeon his colleagues into supporting his plan at the meeting.
The company was accused of bludgeoning customers into buying unnecessary upgrades.
She felt bludgeoned by constant reminders about the deadline.

Synset Examples

The teacher bludgeoned the students into learning the math formulas

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to coerce - to persuade someone forcefully to do something they are unwilling to do
  • Synonym: bully - to use superior strength or influence to intimidate someone
  • Degree Lower: to persuade - to cause someone to do something through reasoning or argument

Etymology

Probably from 'bludgeon' (noun), origin unknown, first attested 18th century.

Source: WordNet
