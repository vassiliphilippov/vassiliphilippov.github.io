gavotte

1. NOUN

/ ɡəˈvɑt / (AmE), / ɡəˈvɒt / (BrE)
a lively dance in quadruple time that was popular in the 17th and 18th centuries, or the music composed for it
a lively 17th–18th century dance or its music

Examples

The orchestra played a beautiful gavotte as the dancers entered the ballroom.
She learned to perform the gavotte as part of her baroque music studies.
The composer included a gavotte in the suite to evoke a sense of elegance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: minuet - a slow, graceful dance in triple time popular in the 17th and 18th centuries

Etymology

From French 'gavotte', from Provençal 'gavoto', after 'Gavot', a native of the Alps.

2. NOUN

/ ɡəˈvɑt / (AmE), / ɡəˈvɒt / (BrE)
a lively French dance in quadruple time, popular in the 17th and 18th centuries, or a piece of music written for this dance
old French dance in quadruple time

Examples

The musicians performed a stately gavotte during the evening's concert.
She learned the steps of the gavotte for the period dance recital.
The composer's suite included a lively gavotte between the minuet and the finale.

Etymology

From French 'gavotte', from Occitan 'gavoto', named after the Gavot people of the Alps.
