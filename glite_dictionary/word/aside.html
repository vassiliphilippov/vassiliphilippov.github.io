aside

1. ADV

/ əˈsaɪd / (AmE), / əˈsaɪd / (BrE)
to one side; in a direction away from something or someone
to or toward one side

Examples

She stepped aside to let the children pass.
He turned his head aside and pretended not to notice.
The chair was pushed aside to make space for more guests.

Synset Examples

turn aside
turn away one's face
glanced away

Related Terms

  • Synonym: away - to or at a different place or direction

Etymology

From Middle English 'asiden', from 'a-' (on, to) + 'side'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00236681-r
Synset POS: r

2. NOUN

/ əˈsaɪd / (AmE), / əˈsaɪd / (BrE)
a remark or passage in a play that is intended to be heard by the audience but not by the other characters on stage
theatrical remark to audience

Examples

The actor delivered a witty aside that made the audience laugh.
In Shakespeare's plays, characters often speak an aside to reveal their true feelings.
She addressed the audience with an aside, sharing her secret intentions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soliloquy - an act of speaking one's thoughts aloud when by oneself or regardless of any hearers, especially by a character in a play
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07024191-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ əˈsaɪd / (AmE), / əˈsaɪd / (BrE)
a remark or comment that is not directly related to the main subject of discussion; in drama, words spoken by a character that are intended to be heard by the audience but not by other characters
remark not related to the main subject

Examples

During the meeting, she made an aside about the weather that made everyone laugh.
The play features several asides that reveal the main character's true feelings.
He couldn't resist a humorous aside during his otherwise serious speech.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: digression - a temporary departure from the main subject in speech or writing

Etymology

From Middle English 'asid', meaning 'to one side'.
