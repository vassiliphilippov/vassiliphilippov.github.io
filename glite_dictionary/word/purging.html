purging

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɜrdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
Serving to purge or rid of sin, especially through cleansing rituals or penance.
serving to cleanse of sin or guilt

Examples

The priest conducted purging ceremonies to help the congregation atone for their mistakes.
Medieval society often relied on purging rituals to cleanse evil spirits from individuals.
Some believe that purging practices are necessary steps toward spiritual renewal.

Synset Examples

purgatorial rites

Related Terms

  • Synonym: purgative - having the power to cleanse or purify

Etymology

Present participle of the verb 'purge', from Old French 'purgier', from Latin 'purgare' (to cleanse, purify).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɝːdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of removing unwanted substances or impurities, often by cleansing or elimination
act of removing impurities or cleansing

Examples

After the chemical spill, a thorough purging of the water system was conducted.
The factory’s purging process ensures that no contaminants remain in the pipes.
Historical reforms often began with the purging of corrupt officials from government ranks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cleansing - the act of making something clean
  • Antonym: contaminating - the act of making something impure or polluted

Etymology

From the verb 'purge', from Latin 'purgare' (to cleanse).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00253297-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɝːdʒɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpɜːdʒɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of clearing oneself or someone else from blame, stigma, or a charge
removal of blame or stigma

Examples

His public apology was a step toward purging his reputation of scandal.
The ceremony was seen as a ritual of purging the community from past wrongs.
She sought purging through confession and acts of contrition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absolution - formal release from blame or guilt
  • Antonym: condemnation - the act of expressing strong disapproval or pronouncing guilt

Etymology

From Latin purgare (to purify, to cleanse), via Old French purger.
