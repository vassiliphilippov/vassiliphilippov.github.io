to tolerate

1. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləreɪt / (BrE)
to allow the existence, occurrence, or practice of something, even if one does not agree with it, without interference
allow or accept without interference

Examples

The company does not tolerate harassment in any form.
We cannot tolerate such unfair treatment of our employees.
She has learned to tolerate the noise from the busy street outside her apartment.

Synset Examples

We don't allow dogs here
Children are not permitted beyond this point
We cannot tolerate smoking in the hospital

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permit - to allow something to happen; grant permission
  • Antonym: to forbid - to refuse to allow something

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to bear, endure).

2. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləˌreɪt / (BrE)
to allow or accept the existence, views, or beliefs of others, even if they differ from one's own
accept differing beliefs or practices

Examples

In a diverse society, it is important to tolerate different cultures and traditions.
She does not tolerate any form of discrimination in her classroom.
They learned to tolerate each other's opinions even when they disagreed.

Synset Examples

We must tolerate the religions of others

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accept - to consent to receive or approve
  • Synonym: respect - to regard with honor or esteem
  • Antonym: to condemn - to express strong disapproval of

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to endure, put up with).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02463017-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləˌreɪt / (BrE)
to allow or endure something unpleasant, difficult, or annoying without protest; to put up with
endure something unpleasant

Examples

It's hard to tolerate such loud noise every night.
She refused to tolerate any kind of discrimination in her classroom.
Some plants can tolerate drought better than others.

Synset Examples

I cannot bear his constant criticism
The new secretary had to endure a lot of unprofessional remarks
he learned to tolerate the heat
She stuck out two years in a miserable marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endure - to suffer patiently, especially something painful or difficult
  • Synonym: bear - to accept or deal with a difficult situation
  • Degree Lower: to accept - to consent to receive or undertake something unpleasant

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to bear, endure), via Old French tolerer.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00670017-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləˌreɪt / (BrE)
to be able to endure or withstand a drug, poison, pathogen, or environmental condition without adverse effects
endure a substance or condition without harm

Examples

Some people cannot tolerate high doses of antibiotics without experiencing side effects.
Desert plants tolerate intense heat and prolonged drought better than most other species.
After months of treatment, she finally began to tolerate the medication without feeling ill.

Synset Examples

The patient does not tolerate the anti-inflammatory drugs we gave him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to withstand - to resist successfully; remain undamaged or unaffected
  • Synonym: to resist - to withstand the action or effect of something

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to bear, endure).
