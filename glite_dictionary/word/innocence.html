innocence

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪnəsəns / (AmE), / ˈɪnəsəns / (BrE)
the state of being free from sin or moral wrongdoing; lack of experience with evil or corruption
state of being unsullied or morally pure

Examples

Her innocence was evident in the way she trusted everyone she met.
The story explores the loss of innocence as children grow up and face harsh realities.
He maintained his innocence despite the temptations around him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: purity - state of being morally or physically clean
  • Antonym: guilt - state of having committed a moral wrong

Etymology

From Latin innocentia (harmlessness, blamelessness), from innocens (not harming).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnəsəns / (AmE), / ˈɪnəsəns / (BrE)
lack of experience or knowledge of the more complex or unpleasant aspects of life; the quality of being simple and naïve.
quality of being naïve or inexperienced

Examples

Her innocence was clear from the way she believed everyone had good intentions.
There was a charming innocence about his questions that made everyone smile.
The film captures the innocence of childhood before reality sets in.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: naivety - lack of experience, wisdom, or judgment
  • Antonym: worldliness - having experience and knowledge of the world

Etymology

From Old French *innocence*, from Latin *innocentia* (harmlessness, blamelessness).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪnəsəns / (AmE), / ˈɪnəsəns / (BrE)
the state of being not guilty of a particular crime or offense
absence of guilt or wrongdoing

Examples

The jury's verdict confirmed his innocence beyond reasonable doubt.
Despite strong accusations, new evidence proved her innocence.
He maintained his innocence throughout the lengthy investigation.

Synset Examples

the trial established his innocence

Related Terms

  • Antonym: guilt - the state of having committed an offense
  • Synonym: blamelessness - the state of being free from blame

Etymology

From Latin innocentia (harmlessness), from innocens (not harming).
