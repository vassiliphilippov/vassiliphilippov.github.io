dubious

1. ADJ

/ ˈduːbiəs / (AmE), / ˈdjuːbiəs / (BrE)
not to be relied upon; open to doubt or suspicion; not certain or trustworthy.
open to doubt or suspicion

Examples

Many investors considered the company's future dubious after the fraud allegations surfaced.
Her explanation for being late sounded rather dubious to the teacher.
He gave me a dubious look when I told him the story.

Synset Examples

the candidate's doubtful past
he has a dubious record indeed
what one found uncertain the other found dubious or downright false
it was more than dubitable whether the friend was as influential as she thought

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubtful - feeling or showing uncertainty; not certain
  • Antonym: certain - known for sure; established beyond doubt

Etymology

From Latin 'dubius' (doubtful), from 'dubium' (doubt).

2. ADJ

/ ˈduːbiəs / (AmE), / ˈdjuːbiəs / (BrE)
not certain; feeling doubt or hesitation about something; unsure or skeptical.
uncertain or doubtful

Examples

She sounded dubious when asked if she could finish the project on time.
Many experts remain dubious about the effectiveness of the new policy.
He gave me a dubious glance before agreeing to my proposal.

Synset Examples

they were doubtful that the cord would hold
it was doubtful whether she would be admitted
dubious about agreeing to go

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubtful - feeling or showing doubt; uncertain
  • Antonym: certain - confident and without doubt; sure

Etymology

From Latin 'dubiosus' (doubtful), from 'dubium' (doubt).

3. ADJ

/ ˈduːbiəs / (AmE), / ˈdjuːbiəs / (BrE)
Not sure whether something is good, true, or can be trusted; feeling doubt or not convinced.
feeling doubt or not convinced

Examples

She looked dubious when I told her my explanation for being late.
The jury remained dubious about the defendant's alibi despite his testimony.
He gave his consent, though his tone was dubious at best.

Synset Examples

they admitted the force of my argument but remained dubious

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubtful - feeling uncertain or having doubts
  • Antonym: convinced - completely certain about something

Etymology

From Latin 'dubium' (a doubt), from 'dubius' (doubtful, uncertain).

Synset POS: s
