lyric

1. ADJ

/ ˈlɪrɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɪrɪk / (BrE)
(of a singing voice) light in volume and modest in range, especially in classical music.
light, modest-range singing voice

Examples

She was cast as the lead due to her beautiful lyric soprano voice.
The role requires a lyric tenor who can sing expressively without overpowering the orchestra.
Critics praised the lyric quality of his performance in the opera.

Synset Examples

a lyric soprano

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dramatic - (of a singing voice) powerful and rich, often with a wide range

Etymology

From Greek 'lyrikos' (singing to the lyre), through Latin 'lyricus'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01471909-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈlɪrɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɪrɪk / (BrE)
(of poetry) expressing the poet's emotions, often in a songlike style; relating to lyric poetry.
relating to poetry expressing emotion

Examples

She has a particular fondness for lyric poetry, which captures deep emotions in concise verse.
The lyric style of the poem made it feel intensely personal and melodic.
In literature class, we analyzed several lyric poems from the Romantic period.

Synset Examples

lyric poetry

Related Terms

  • Antonym: epic - relating to epic poetry, narrating heroic deeds
  • Antonym: dramatic - relating to dramatic poetry

Etymology

From Greek *lyrikos* (singing to the lyre).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02867171-a
Synset POS: a

/ ˈlɪrɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɪrɪk / (BrE)
Relating to or characteristic of opera or musical drama, especially referring to singing or performance on a musical stage.
relating to musical drama or opera

Examples

The city is renowned for its lyric theater and grand opera performances.

Many famous singers began their careers in lyric opera houses across Europe.

Synset Examples

the lyric stage

From Greek *lurikos*, from *lyra* (lyre), originally referring to an instrument used to accompany song.
