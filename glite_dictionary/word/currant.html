currant

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɝːənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌrənt / (BrE)
a small, tart, red or black berry used especially for making jams and jellies
small tart berry used in jams

Examples

The currant bushes in her garden were heavy with ripe fruit.
He made homemade jam from black currant and sugar.
Dried currants are sometimes added to scones for extra flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gooseberry - a similar small, tart berry from the same botanical family

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French 'raisins de Corinthe' (raisins of Corinth), initially referring to dried grapes imported from Corinth.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɝː.ənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌr.ənt / (BrE)
a small dried seedless raisin, especially of a variety grown in the Mediterranean region and California, used in cooking and baking
small dried seedless raisin used in cooking

Examples

She sprinkled a handful of currants over her oatmeal for extra sweetness.
Traditional scones often include dried currants for added flavor.
The recipe calls for soaked currants to provide a burst of tartness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: raisin - a dried grape used as food or in cooking

Etymology

From Anglo-French *raisins de Corinthe*, meaning 'raisins of Corinth,' after the Greek city of Corinth.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɝːənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌr.ənt / (BrE)
any of various small deciduous shrubs of the genus Ribes, bearing clusters of edible red, black, or white berries.
shrub bearing edible berries (genus Ribes)

Examples

The currant bush in our garden produces sweet fruit every summer.
Many birds are attracted to the bright berries of the currant plant.
Gardeners often prune currant shrubs in early spring to encourage healthy growth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gooseberry - a closely related shrub in the genus Ribes bearing greenish berries

Etymology

From Old French 'raisins de Corinthe' (Corinthian grapes), later applied also to Ribes berries in English.
