rearward

1. ADJ

/ ˈrɪr.wərd / (AmE), / ˈrɪə.wəd / (BrE)
directed or moving toward the back or rear of something
toward the back; at the rear

Examples

She cast a rearward glance as she left the room.
The soldiers made a rearward movement to avoid the advancing enemy.
A sudden noise from the rearward direction startled everyone.

Synset Examples

a rearward glance
a rearward movement

Related Terms

  • Antonym: forward - directed or moving toward the front

Etymology

From Middle English, combining 'rear' (back part) and '-ward' (directional suffix).

WordNet ID: oewn-00203842-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ˈrɪrˌwɚd / (AmE), / ˈrɪəwəd / (BrE)
located at or toward the back; situated at the rear
toward the back or rear

Examples

From the rearward window, you could see the entire garden stretching out behind the house.
The rearward wheels of the truck were damaged in the accident.
She moved to a more rearward seat to avoid the crowd at the front.

Synset Examples

the chair's rear legs
the rear door of the plane
on the rearward side

Related Terms

  • Antonym: forward - located at or toward the front

Etymology

From Middle English 'rerewarde', from Old French 'rere' (behind) + 'ward' (direction).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00199483-s
Synset POS: s

4. NOUN

/ ˈrɪr.wɚd / (AmE), / ˈrɪə.wəd / (BrE)
the direction or position toward the back or rear, especially in relation to a group or formation
backwards direction or position

Examples

The supply trucks followed in the rearward of the advancing troops.
A small force was assigned to guard the rearward during the retreat.
The general rode at the rearward of the procession, observing the troops.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: van - the front or leading position, especially in a formation

Etymology

From Middle English 'rerward', from Old French 'rerwarde', from 'rere' (back) + 'ward' (direction).
