quickstep

1. NOUN

/ ˈkwɪk.stɛp / (AmE), / ˈkwɪk.stɛp / (BrE)
a lively ballroom dance with both fast and slow steps, performed in 4/4 time
a type of lively ballroom dance

Examples

They practiced the quickstep for hours before the competition.
The couple impressed the judges with a flawless quickstep routine.
Learning the quickstep requires both agility and precise timing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foxtrot - a smooth ballroom dance, closely related to quickstep

Etymology

From 'quick' + 'step', referring to the brisk pacing of the dance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkwɪkˌstɛp / (AmE), / ˈkwɪkstɛp / (BrE)
a military march or march tempo performed at a faster pace than the ordinary pace of marching
fast-paced military march

Examples

The band performed a lively quickstep as the soldiers marched past the reviewing stand.
The troops responded to the officer’s command with a sharp quickstep across the parade ground.
A traditional quickstep was played to boost the morale of the marching regiment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: march - a piece of music written for marching

Etymology

Formed from 'quick' (fast) and 'step'; also the name of a fast ballroom dance originating in the early 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07072708-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈkwɪkˌstɛp / (AmE), / ˈkwɪkstɛp / (BrE)
to perform the quickstep, a lively ballroom dance characterized by fast and light movements
to perform the quickstep dance

Examples

During the competition, the couple quickstepped across the floor with impressive grace.
She has been practicing for months and can now quickstep effortlessly in sync with her partner.
The dancers quickstepping in perfect unison captivated the entire audience.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: waltz - to perform the waltz dance
  • Synonym: foxtrot - to perform the foxtrot dance
  • Antonym: slow dance - to dance slowly, usually with a partner

Etymology

From the noun 'quickstep', referring to the dance style; originally a 19th-century marching pace.
