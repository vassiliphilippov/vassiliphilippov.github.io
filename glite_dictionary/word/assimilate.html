to assimilate

1. VERB

/ əˈsɪməˌleɪt / (AmE), / əˈsɪməleɪt / (BrE)
to make someone or something become similar to others, especially by integrating into a larger group or culture
make similar or absorb into a group

Examples

Many schools strive to help new students assimilate into the local culture.
The government attempted to assimilate minority communities by promoting a unified national identity.
It can take years for immigrants to fully assimilate into a new society.

Synset Examples

This country assimilates immigrants very quickly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to integrate - to combine into a whole; to become part of a group
  • Antonym: to differentiate - to make or become different in the process of development

Etymology

From Latin 'assimilare' (to make like, compare), from 'ad-' (to) + 'similis' (like, similar).

2. VERB

/ əˈsɪməˌleɪt / (AmE), / əˈsɪmɪleɪt / (BrE)
to absorb and understand new ideas, information, or experiences fully
absorb and understand new ideas

Examples

It can take years to fully assimilate the customs and values of a different culture.
Children often assimilate new vocabulary quickly when immersed in a language-rich environment.
She needed time to assimilate all the information presented during the training session.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to absorb - to take in or soak up knowledge or information

3. VERB

/ əˈsɪməˌleɪt / (AmE), / əˈsɪmɪleɪt / (BrE)
(of a gas, light, or heat) to take into a solution or a substance, so as to become part of it.
to absorb into a solution (science)

Examples

In this process, plants assimilate carbon dioxide from the air into organic compounds.
Certain materials assimilate heat rapidly, making them effective for thermal management.
The solution was able to assimilate more gas as the temperature increased.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to absorb - to take in or soak up (energy, liquid, or other substance)

Etymology

From Latin assimilare (to make similar).

4. VERB

/ əˈsɪməˌleɪt / (AmE), / əˈsɪmɪleɪt / (BrE)
(of a sound) to change so as to become similar to a neighboring sound, especially in pronunciation.
(linguistics) become similar in sound

Examples

In rapid speech, the final nasal sound often assimilates to the place of articulation of the next consonant.
The 'n' in 'input' assimilates to an 'm' sound because of the following 'p'.
Phonemes may assimilate to their neighbors during connected speech.

Synset Examples

The nasal assimilates to the following consonant

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dissimilation - (linguistics) process by which similar sounds become less similar

Etymology

From Latin assimilare (to make like, compare), from ad- (to) + similis (like, similar).
