to carve

1. VERB

/ kɑrv / (AmE), / kɑːv / (BrE)
to form (something) by cutting material into a particular shape, often using tools
form by cutting into shape

Examples

The artist will carve a delicate bird from a single block of wood.
Each student tried to carve a pumpkin with a different face for the festival.
He spent hours trying to carve a statue that resembled an ancient warrior.

Synset Examples

Carve a flower from the ice

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sculpt - to shape or form something by carving or molding material

Etymology

From Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut, carve); related to Old High German 'kerban'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01258721-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kɑrv / (AmE), / kɑːv / (BrE)
to engrave or cut a shape, pattern, or letters by chipping away at a surface, especially in wood, stone, or other materials
engrave or cut by chipping away at a surface

Examples

He decided to carve their initials into the wet cement.
The artist skillfully carved a lion's head from the block of marble.
Some people like to carve their names on tree trunks to leave a mark.

Synset Examples

carve one's name into the bark

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to engrave - to cut or etch a design onto a hard surface

Etymology

From Old English ceorfan (to cut, carve), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01259164-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kɑːrv / (AmE), / kɑːv / (BrE)
to cut (meat, a piece of meat, etc.) into slices or pieces, especially before serving
cut food, especially meat, into pieces

Examples

Every year, Dad carves the turkey at the holiday dinner.
She carefully carved the roast beef into thin, even slices.
Would you like me to carve the chicken for you?

Synset Examples

Father carved the ham

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slice - to cut something into thin, broad pieces

Etymology

From Old English ceorfan (to cut, carve), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01258531-v
Synset POS: v
