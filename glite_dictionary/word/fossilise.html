to fossilise

1. VERB

/ ˈfɑːsəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɒsɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to convert or be converted into a fossil; to become preserved in the form of a fossil
convert into a fossil
british

Examples

Ancient plants fossilised over millions of years beneath layers of sediment.
If bones are quickly buried, they may fossilise instead of decaying.
Some trilobites fossilised so well that their delicate features are still visible today.

Synset Examples

The little animals fossilized and are now embedded in the limestone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to petrify - to change organic matter into a stony substance; to turn to stone

Etymology

From fossil + -ise, ultimately from Latin 'fossilis' (dug up).

WordNet ID: oewn-00508745-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈfɑːsəlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɒsɪlaɪz / (BrE)
(figurative) To become mentally rigid or set in one's ways, unwilling to adapt or change.
become mentally inflexible

Examples

If you never try new things, your mind can fossilise over time.
Some institutions tend to fossilise and resist innovative ideas.
His attitudes have fossilised since his youth, making him indifferent to change.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ossify - to become rigid or fixed, especially in habits or opinions

Etymology

From fossil + -ise, based on the idea of becoming like a fossil (hardened, unchanging).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00250442-v
Synset POS: v
