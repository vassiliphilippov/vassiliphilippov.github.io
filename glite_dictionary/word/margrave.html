margrave

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɑːrˌɡreɪv / (AmE), / ˈmɑːɡreɪv / (BrE)
a military governor of a border province in the Holy Roman Empire, especially in medieval Germany.
medieval German frontier governor

Examples

The emperor appointed a margrave to defend the eastern frontier of the realm.
Each margrave ruled his territory with considerable independence from the central authority.
In the twelfth century, the power of the margrave was both military and administrative.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marquis - a nobleman ranking above a count or earl and below a duke

Etymology

From Middle High German *markgrāve*, from Old High German *marka* (border) + *grāvo* (count).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɑrˌɡreɪv / (AmE), / ˈmɑːɡreɪv / (BrE)
A German nobleman of high rank, ranking above a count and roughly equivalent to a British marquess.
German nobleman, above count

Examples

The powerful margrave ruled over the territory along the empire's eastern border.
After years of service, he was granted the title of margrave by the emperor.
The castle once belonged to a wealthy margrave who protected the frontier.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marquess - A British nobleman ranking above an earl and below a duke
  • Degree Lower: count - A European nobleman ranking below a margrave or marquess

Etymology

From Middle High German *markgrāve* (count of a border area), from *Mark* (border region) + *grāve* (count).
