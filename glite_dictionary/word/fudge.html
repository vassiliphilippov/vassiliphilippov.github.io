fudge

1. NOUN

/ fʌdʒ / (AmE), / fʌdʒ / (BrE)
a soft, sweet candy made from sugar, butter, and milk, often with chocolate and sometimes with nuts or other flavors
soft, creamy sweet candy

Examples

I bought a box of fudge from the candy store as a treat.
She made homemade chocolate fudge for the holiday party.
The rich, creamy fudge melted in my mouth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toffee - chewy caramelized sugar candy

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from the dialect term 'fadge' meaning 'to fit together' or as a fanciful coinage in 19th-century America.

2. VERB

/ fʌdʒ / (AmE), / fʌdʒ / (BrE)
to change or present information dishonestly or inaccurately, often to deceive people
alter information dishonestly

Examples

The accountant tried to fudge the numbers to hide expenses.
If you fudge the results, the report will be inaccurate.
He was caught fudging the details in his official statement.

Synset Examples

Fudge the figures
cook the books
falsify the data

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to falsify - to alter information or evidence deliberately to mislead
  • Synonym: to fabricate - to invent or make up something, often with intent to deceive
  • Synonym: to misrepresent - to give a false or misleading account of something
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02583006-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ fʌdʒ / (AmE), / fʌdʒ / (BrE)
to avoid or try to avoid dealing with a duty, question, or issue directly; to be deliberately vague or evasive
evade or avoid directly addressing

Examples

Instead of answering the question directly, the politician chose to fudge and change the subject.
He tends to fudge his responsibilities when things get complicated at work.
She tried to fudge her way through the interview without giving a clear answer.

Synset Examples

He dodged the issue
she skirted the problem
They tend to evade their responsibilities
he evaded the questions skillfully

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dodge - to avoid (a question, issue, or duty) by being evasive
  • Synonym: to evade - to escape or avoid (something), especially by cleverness or trickery

Etymology

Originally British slang (late 18th century) meaning 'to fabricate or contrive.'
