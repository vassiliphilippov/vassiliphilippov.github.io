enfilade

1. VERB

/ ˌɛn.fəˈleɪd / (AmE), / ˌɛn.fɪˈlɑːd / (BrE)
to direct gunfire along the length of a target, especially a line of troops or a fortification
fire along the length of a target (military)

Examples

The machine guns were positioned to enfilade any advancing enemy troops.
As the soldiers crossed the bridge, they were enfiladed by fire from both sides.
The commander ordered his artillery to enfilade the defensive trench lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rake - to fire at something from end to end

Etymology

From French 'enfiler' meaning 'to thread' or 'to string',

2. NOUN

/ ˌɛn.fəˈleɪd / (AmE), / ˈɛn.fɪ.leɪd / (BrE)
gunfire directed along the length of a target, such as a line of troops or a trench, rather than across its width
gunfire along the length of a target

Examples

The soldiers suffered heavy casualties as they were caught in an enfilade from the enemy machine gun nests.
Trenches were designed to minimize the risk of enfilade fire during World War I.
The commander warned his troops about the danger of advancing into a position exposed to enfilade.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: defilade - protection from enemy fire, especially by using natural or artificial obstacles

Etymology

From French 'enfilade', from 'enfiler' (to thread, string together), referring to threading along a line.
