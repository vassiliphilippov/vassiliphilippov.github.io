preciousness

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɛʃəsnəs / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəsnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being extremely valuable or cherished
the quality of being valuable or cherished

Examples

The preciousness of her grandmother's necklace was obvious to everyone in the family.
He didn't realize the preciousness of time until it was almost too late.
The museum exhibit highlighted the preciousness of ancient artifacts to their cultures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: value - the quality that renders something desirable or valuable
  • Antonym: worthlessness - the lack of value or merit

Etymology

From 'precious' + '-ness', with 'precious' from Old French 'precioso', from Latin 'pretiosus' (of great price, valuable).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɛʃəsnəs / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəsnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being overly fastidious or excessively refined, especially in taste or style
excessive refinement or fastidiousness

Examples

The preciousness of her manner made her seem insincere to some of her colleagues.
His writing sometimes lapses into preciousness, with elaborate metaphors and ornate language.
Critics accused the film of preciousness in its overly stylized dialogue and sets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affectedness - the quality of being artificial or insincere

Etymology

From Latin 'pretiosus' (costly, valuable) via Old French 'precios', with figurative extension to mannerism.

3. NOUN

/ ˈprɛʃəsnəs / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəsnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being very valuable or cherished; great worth or importance
state of being very valuable or cherished

Examples

The preciousness of her grandmother's ring was obvious to everyone who saw it.
Many people fail to realize the preciousness of every moment spent with loved ones.
He spoke about the preciousness of clean water in developing countries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: value - the importance, worth, or usefulness of something
  • Antonym: worthlessness - the lack of value or worth

Etymology

From Middle English 'precious' and suffix '-ness', denoting a quality or state.
