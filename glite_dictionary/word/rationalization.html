rationalization

1. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of attempting to explain or justify behavior or attitudes with logical reasons, even if these are not true or appropriate
attempt to justify or explain with reasons

Examples

Her rationalization for missing the meeting was that she needed more time to prepare.
He used elaborate rationalization to defend his questionable decisions at work.
Sometimes, people resort to rationalization when they feel guilty about their actions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - the action of showing something to be right or reasonable

Etymology

From Latin "ratio" (reason) + English suffix "-alization."

2. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
(mathematics) The process of simplifying an expression or equation by removing radicals, without altering its value or the roots of the equation.
mathematical simplification by removing radicals
medical

Examples

The rationalization of the denominator simplifies calculations in many algebraic problems.
Students are often asked to perform rationalization when dealing with fractions that contain square roots.
After the rationalization of the expression, the result became much easier to interpret.

Etymology

From rational +‎ -ization; 'rational' from Latin rationalis (of reason or ratio).

3. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlʌɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of organizing something more logically or efficiently, often by applying a systematic method or rationale
systematic reorganization for efficiency

Examples

The company's rationalization of its supply chain led to greater efficiency and lower costs.
After the rationalization of the process, there was less confusion among employees.
Governments often implement rationalization to improve the functioning of public services.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: systematization - the act of arranging according to a system or plan

Etymology

From Latin 'ratio' (reason) and English suffix '-ization'.

4. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the organization of a business or process according to systematic, scientific principles to improve efficiency
making a business more efficient by systematic management

Examples

The company underwent a complete rationalization to reduce costs and boost productivity.
Many factories in the 20th century adopted rationalization to compete in global markets.
The rationalization of the supply chain led to faster delivery times and higher profits.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: streamlining - the process of making an organization or system more efficient
  • Antonym: bureaucratization - the process of introducing bureaucracy or rigid administrative procedures

Etymology

From rational + -ization; influenced by German 'Rationalisierung', widely used in early 20th-century management.

5. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
(psychology) a defense mechanism in which a person conceals their true motives by explaining their actions or feelings in a more acceptable or less threatening way.
psychological defense mechanism; justifying actions or feelings
medical

Examples

His constant rationalization of bad decisions prevents him from realizing his true motivations.
She offered a rationalization for missing the meeting, but her colleagues sensed there was another reason.
In therapy, he learned to recognize when he was using rationalization to avoid uncomfortable truths.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - an explanation that makes something seem reasonable or necessary

Etymology

From rational + -ization; based on the verb rationalize (from Latin rationalis 'reasonable').
