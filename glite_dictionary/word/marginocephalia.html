Marginocephalia

1. NOUN

/ mɑːrdʒɪˌnoʊˌsɛˈfeɪliə / (AmE), / mɑːdʒɪnəʊˌsɛˈfɛːliə / (BrE)
a group of herbivorous dinosaurs characterized by a bony shelf or frill at the back of the skull, including the pachycephalosaurs and ceratopsians
dinosaur group with bony-skulled and horned members

Examples

Pachycephalosaurs and ceratopsians are both members of the Marginocephalia.
The defining feature of Marginocephalia is a shelf or frill along the back of the skull.
Scientists believe Marginocephalia first appeared during the Jurassic period.

Etymology

From modern Latin, combining 'margin-' (edge) and Greek 'kephalē' (head).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɑrdʒɪnoʊsɪˈfeɪliə / (AmE), / ˌmɑːdʒɪnəʊsɪˈfiːlɪə / (BrE)
A suborder of herbivorous dinosaurs characterized by a shelf or margin at the back of the skull, including the pachycephalosaurs (bone-headed dinosaurs) and ceratopsians (horned dinosaurs).
suborder of margin-headed dinosaurs

Examples

The suborder Marginocephalia includes both pachycephalosaurs and ceratopsians, known for their distinctive skull ornaments.
Paleontologists study Marginocephalia fossils to learn about the evolution of dinosaur cranial structures.
Triceratops is a famous member of the Marginocephalia, recognized by its impressive horns and frill.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining Latin 'margo' (margin) and Greek 'kephalē' (head).
