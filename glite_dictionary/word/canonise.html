to canonise

1. VERB

/ ˈkænəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkanənʌɪz / (BrE)
to treat someone as if they are sacred or saintly, often giving them special status or reverence
treat as sacred or saintly

Examples

Some critics argue that society tends to canonise certain celebrities regardless of their real achievements.
In his writings, he canonised her as the ideal woman.
The film appears to canonise its protagonist, portraying her as flawless and beyond reproach.

Synset Examples

He canonizes women

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to idolize - to admire or revere excessively
  • Antonym: to vilify - to speak or write about with strong criticism or hatred

Etymology

From Late Latin canonizare, from canon (rule, standard) + -ize.

2. VERB

/ ˈkænəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkanənaɪz / (BrE)
officially declare (a dead person) to be a saint, especially by the Roman Catholic Church.
declare someone a saint officially
british

Examples

Many early Christian martyrs were canonised centuries after their deaths.
The Vatican announced plans to canonise the beloved nun.
He was officially canonised for his lifelong dedication to helping the poor.

Synset Examples

After he was shown to have performed a miracle, the priest was canonized

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: to beatify - declare (a dead person) to be blessed, a step before canonisation
  • Degree Higher: sainthood - the state or condition of being recognized as a saint

Etymology

From Medieval Latin canonizare (to canonize), from Late Latin canon (canon, rule).
