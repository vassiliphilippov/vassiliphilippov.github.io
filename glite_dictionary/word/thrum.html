thrum

1. NOUN

/ θrʌm / (AmE), / θrʌm / (BrE)
a continuous, rhythmic humming or vibrating sound
continuous humming or vibrating sound

Examples

The thrum of the distant machinery filled the quiet warehouse.
She listened to the steady thrum of the rain against the roof.
At night, I find the gentle thrum of the city oddly comforting.

Synset Examples

he could hear the thrum of a banjo

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hum - a low, steady continuous sound
  • Synonym: buzz - a continuous humming or murmuring sound

Etymology

Probably imitative in origin, matching the sound it describes.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07411830-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ θrʌm / (AmE), / θrʌm / (BrE)
to sound the strings of a stringed instrument, especially in a repetitive or gentle manner
play strings (musical instrument) in a repetitive way

Examples

He began to thrum a soft tune on his guitar while waiting for the band.
She would often thrum absentmindedly on the strings as she composed her songs.
The musician thrummed the harp, filling the room with gentle music.

Synset Examples

strum a guitar

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strum - to play (a stringed instrument) by sweeping the fingers across the strings
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02184806-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ θrʌm / (AmE), / θrʌm / (BrE)
to make or produce a continuous, low, monotonous humming sound
to make a humming sound

Examples

The old air conditioning unit would thrum throughout the night, making it hard to sleep.
As the train started, its engine began to thrum steadily beneath our feet.
She sat quietly, letting her fingers thrum on the tabletop in a rhythmic pattern.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hum - to make a low, steady continuous sound
  • Synonym: drone - to make a continuous low dull humming sound

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, representing the sound itself.
