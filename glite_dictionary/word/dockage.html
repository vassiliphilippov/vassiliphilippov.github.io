dockage

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɑkɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdɒkɪdʒ / (BrE)
the act or process of securing an arriving vessel at a dock with ropes or other moorings
securing a vessel at a dock

Examples

The crew completed the dockage of the ship in record time.
Proper dockage is crucial during rough weather to prevent damage to the vessel.
After arrival, the port workers handled the dockage with precision and care.

Etymology

From 'dock' + '-age', forming a noun indicating an action or result.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɑːkɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdɒkɪdʒ / (BrE)
a fee charged for allowing a vessel to use a dock or berth
fee for using a dock

Examples

The marina increased its dockage to cover maintenance costs for the new docks.
Yacht owners are billed monthly for dockage and other harbor services.
Vessels longer than 40 feet pay a higher dockage than smaller boats.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: berthing fee - a fee for mooring a ship in a berth
  • Synonym: moorage - fee for securing a vessel in a particular spot, especially at a dock or harbor

Etymology

From 'dock' + suffix '-age', denoting a charge or fee.

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɑkɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdɒkɪdʒ / (BrE)
a place in a harbor where a ship is docked for loading, unloading, or repairs, sometimes equipped with gates to control water levels
place for docking ships in a harbor

Examples

The cargo ship was assigned dockage so repairs could be made overnight.
Due to limited dockage, smaller vessels had to wait outside the harbor.
Improved dockage facilities have helped the port accommodate more container ships.

Synset Examples

the ship arrived at the dock more than a day late

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dock - a structure extending alongshore or out from the shore into a body of water, to which boats may be moored

Etymology

From 'dock' (a place for berthing ships) + '-age' (denoting a related action or place), formed in English.
