to evict

1. VERB

/ ɪˈvɪkt / (AmE), / ɪˈvɪkt / (BrE)
to officially force someone to leave a property, especially by legal process
force someone to leave property by law

Examples

After months of missed payments, the landlord decided to evict the tenants.
The family was heartbroken when they were evicted from their home.
If you do not pay your rent, the owner may evict you by court order.

Synset Examples

The landlord evicted the tenants after they had not paid the rent for four months

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to expel - to force someone to leave a place
  • Synonym: to eject - to force or throw out, often from a property
  • Antonym: to admit - to allow someone to enter a place

Etymology

From Latin evincere (to overcome, to expel), from e- (out) + vincere (to conquer).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈvɪkt / (AmE), / ɪˈvɪkt / (BrE)
To expel or force someone out of a property or place, especially without using legal procedures.
forcefully remove (without legal process)

Examples

The landlord tried to evict the noisy tenants by changing the locks while they were away.
Some squatters were evicted from the building without any court order or formal warning.
He threatened to evict his guests if they didn't stop causing trouble immediately.

Synset Examples

The landlord wanted to evict the tenants so he banged on the pipes every morning at 3 a.m.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eject - to force someone to leave a place or position
  • Synonym: to expel - to officially force someone to leave a place or organization

Etymology

From Latin *evictus*, past participle of *evincere* (to overcome, to expel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01470956-v
Synset POS: v
