fecundation

1. NOUN

/ ˌfiːkənˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfiːkʌnˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which male and female gametes physically unite, resulting in fertilization in animals or plants
fertilization by union of gametes
medical

Examples

After fecundation, the embryo begins to develop within the seed.
Successful fecundation in mammals requires the sperm to reach the egg first.
In flowering plants, fecundation occurs after pollen reaches the ovule.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fertilization - the act or process of fertilizing, especially by fusion of gametes

Etymology

From Latin 'fecundatio' (making fruitful), from 'fecundus' (fruitful, fertile).

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɛkənˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfiːkʌnˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of making something fertile, especially by applying fertilizer or manure
making something fertile (agriculture)

Examples

The fecundation of the soil was necessary to improve this year's crop yield.
Many traditional farming methods rely on natural fecundation through composting and manure.
Improper fecundation can lead to reduced agricultural productivity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fertilization - the process of making soil fertile by adding substances

Etymology

From Latin 'fecundatio' (fertilization), from 'fecundus' (fruitful, fertile).
