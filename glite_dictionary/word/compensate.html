to compensate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.pənˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.pənˌseɪt / (BrE)
to adjust or counterbalance something so as to correct an effect or offset a disadvantage
adjust to offset or correct

Examples

The thermostat automatically compensates for temperature fluctuations in the room.
Special lighting is used to compensate for the lack of natural sunlight.
He tried to compensate for his lateness by working extra hours.

Synset Examples

engineers will work to correct the effects of air resistance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: offset - to counteract or compensate for something
  • Antonym: to exacerbate - to make a situation worse, the opposite of correct or offset

Etymology

From Latin 'compensare' (to weigh one thing against another), from 'com-' (together) + 'pensare' (to weigh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02678329-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.pɛnˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.pən.seɪt / (BrE)
to pay someone money in exchange for work done, loss, injury, or inconvenience endured
pay money or make amends

Examples

The company agreed to compensate him for the overtime hours he worked.
She was compensated for the damage caused to her car.
Employers are legally required to compensate workers who are injured on the job.

Synset Examples

My efforts were not remunerated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to remunerate - to pay someone for work or services
  • Synonym: to reimburse - to pay back money spent or lost

Etymology

From Latin compensare (to weigh one thing against another, counterbalance).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02254549-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.pənˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.pənˌseɪt / (BrE)
to attempt to offset personal shortcomings or feelings of inferiority by emphasizing or enhancing other qualities or achievements
make up for perceived shortcomings

Examples

Sometimes people compensate for a lack of confidence by acting overly assertive.
He tried to compensate for his poor grades by excelling in sports.
She may compensate for feeling left out by becoming the class clown.

Synset Examples

he is compensating for being a bad father

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: to overcompensate - to excessively make up for a weakness or deficiency by exaggerating other qualities

Etymology

From Latin compensare (to weigh one thing against another, to counterbalance), from com- (together) + pensare (to weigh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02678648-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.pən.seɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.pən.seɪt / (BrE)
to give someone something, often money, in recognition of loss, suffering, or work done; to make amends or provide a counterbalance
to pay or make amends in return

Examples

The company will compensate you for any travel expenses incurred.
She tried to compensate him for his hard work with a generous bonus.
Volunteers are not usually compensated for their time.

Synset Examples

Does she pay you for the work you are doing?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reimburse - to pay back money to someone
  • Synonym: to repay - to pay back or reward someone for something
  • Antonym: to penalize - to punish or disadvantage someone

Etymology

From Latin compensare (to weigh one thing against another, balance out), from com- (together) + pensare (to weigh).

5. VERB

/ ˈkɑmpənˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmpənseɪt / (BrE)
to make amends for something wrong or harmful by giving something, often money or another form of reparation
make amends or reparations

Examples

The company was ordered to compensate the victims for the environmental damage it caused.
The government passed a law to compensate those who lost property during the conflict.
After the error was discovered, the airline agreed to compensate passengers with travel vouchers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: redress - to remedy or set right, especially a wrong or grievance
  • Antonym: to punish - to inflict a penalty for an offense

Etymology

From Latin compensare (to balance, make good), from com- (together) + pensare (to weigh).
