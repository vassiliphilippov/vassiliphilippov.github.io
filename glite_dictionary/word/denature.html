to denature

1. VERB

/ dɪˈneɪtʃər / (AmE), / dɪˈneɪtʃə / (BrE)
to add nonfissionable material to fissionable material, making it unsuitable for use in an atomic bomb
make nuclear material unsuitable for bombs

Examples

Nuclear engineers often denature plutonium to prevent its use in weapons.
The process will denature uranium, making it unsuitable for bomb production.
To enhance security, facilities routinely denature fissionable materials with nonfissionable isotopes.

Etymology

From de- (removal) + nature, by analogy with its use in chemistry/biology.

2. VERB

/ diˈneɪtʃər / (AmE), / diˈneɪtʃə / (BrE)
to alter the natural properties of (a substance), especially a protein, by heat, acid, or other means so that its original characteristics are changed or diminished
to change the natural qualities of a substance

Examples

Heating can denature enzymes, rendering them inactive.
Strong acids may denature proteins, causing them to lose their original function.
Repeated freezing and thawing can denature biological samples.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to degrade - lower in quality or break down (especially in chemistry)
  • Antonym: preserve - maintain original properties or prevent change

Etymology

From Latin 'de-' (removal) + 'natura' (nature).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00554951-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈneɪtʃər / (AmE), / dɪˈneɪtʃə / (BrE)
to make (alcohol) unfit for drinking by adding a substance, without affecting its usefulness for other purposes
make alcohol undrinkable by adding substance

Examples

Industrial alcohol is often denatured to prevent its use as a beverage.
The company must denature its ethanol before distributing it to factories.
After being denatured, the alcohol was safe for use in cleaning products but not for drinking.

Etymology

From Latin 'de-' (removal) + 'natura' (nature); originally meant to change the inherent nature of something.
