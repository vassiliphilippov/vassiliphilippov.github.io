ribbonwood

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɪb.ən.wʊd / (AmE), / ˈrɪb.ən.wʊd / (BrE)
A deciduous tree native to New Zealand, whose inner bark produces a strong fiber resembling flax, also known as New Zealand cotton.
deciduous New Zealand tree with strong fiber bark

Examples

The Maori people traditionally used the bark of the ribbonwood for weaving and making cords.
A single ribbonwood can grow quite tall and is often found in fertile river valleys.
Researchers study ribbonwood fiber for its potential as a sustainable textile material.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɪb.ən.wʊd / (AmE), / ˈrɪb.ən.wʊd / (BrE)
A small tree or shrub native to New Zealand, with clusters of fragrant white flowers and bark used for cordage.
New Zealand tree/shrub with fragrant flowers

Examples

The bark of the ribbonwood was traditionally used by Māori for making strong cords.
In spring, the ribbonwood bursts into masses of honey-scented white flowers.
We spotted a ribbonwood growing at the edge of the forest during our walk.

Etymology

Named for the ribbon-like strips of its bark.
