fond

1. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
having or displaying affection or tenderness; feeling love or liking for someone or something
having affection or tenderness

Examples

She has always been fond of animals and volunteers at the shelter every weekend.
He gave his grandmother a fond hug before leaving for college.
They exchanged fond memories of their childhood during the reunion.

Synset Examples

affectionate children
a fond embrace
fond of his nephew
a tender glance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affectionate - showing fondness or tenderness

Etymology

From Old French *fon* (foolish), later extended in English to affectionate meaning.

WordNet ID: oewn-01467633-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
having a strong liking or affection for someone or something, often used with 'of' or 'to'
having a strong liking or preference

Examples

She has always been fond of animals and volunteers at the shelter every weekend.
I'm particularly fond of jazz music, especially classic albums from the 1950s.
He grew quite fond of his new colleagues after working with them for several months.

Synset Examples

fond of chocolate
partial to horror movies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: partial - having a liking for something, especially more than is reasonable

Etymology

From Middle English founde (foolish, fond), from Old English fund (foolish), later developing the meaning 'affectionate'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01295806-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
Excessively or foolishly loving and indulgent, often resulting in spoiling someone.
excessively loving or indulgent

Examples

Her fond mother could never say no, and as a result, she was terribly spoiled.
The old man was fond of his grandchildren, showering them with gifts at every visit.
Some teachers are so fond of their favorite students that they overlook their faults.

Synset Examples

adoring grandparents
deceiving her preoccupied and doting husband with a young captain
hopelessly spoiled by a fond mother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adoring - showing deep love and admiration
  • Synonym: doting - excessively fond, often to a foolish degree
  • Antonym: aloof - not friendly or forthcoming; cool and distant

Etymology

From Middle English 'fond' (foolish), later coming to mean 'affectionate', related to 'fondle'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01467366-s
Synset POS: s

4. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
(of a hope, belief, etc.) foolish or fanciful because it is unlikely to be fulfilled.
foolish or unlikely (of hopes, beliefs)

Examples

She held the fond belief that he would call her, despite all evidence to the contrary.
His fond hopes of winning the lottery never came true.
We all had some fond illusions about how quickly the problem could be solved.

Synset Examples

fond hopes of becoming President
fond fancies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fanciful - (of ideas or hopes) existing only in the imagination; unrealistic
  • Antonym: realistic - having or showing a sensible and practical idea of what can be achieved or expected

Etymology

From Middle English fond, related to fon (to fool), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02582341-s
Synset POS: s
