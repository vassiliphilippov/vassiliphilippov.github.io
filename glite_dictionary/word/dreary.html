dreary

1. ADJ

/ ˈdrɪri / (AmE), / ˈdrɪəri / (BrE)
dull, bleak, and depressing; making you feel sad or without hope
dull and depressing

Examples

The constant rain made the whole week feel dreary and endless.
He stared out the window at the dreary landscape, longing for a hint of sunshine.
After a dreary Monday at work, Jen decided to treat herself to dinner.

Synset Examples

a blue day
the dark days of the war
a week of rainy depressing weather
a disconsolate winter landscape
the first dismal dispiriting days of November
a dark gloomy day
grim rainy weather

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gloomy - dark or poorly lit, especially in a way that makes one feel depressed
  • Antonym: cheerful - noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

From Middle English drery, from Old English drēorig (sorrowful, bloody).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdrɪri / (AmE), / ˈdrɪəri / (BrE)
dull, bleak, or lacking in liveliness, charm, or excitement.
dull; lacking liveliness or charm

Examples

The weather was so dreary that no one wanted to leave the house.
She found his lectures unbearably dreary and struggled to stay awake.
After months of dreary routine, he craved some excitement.

Synset Examples

her drab personality
life was drab compared with the more exciting life style overseas
a series of dreary dinner parties

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dull - lacking interest or excitement
  • Antonym: exciting - causing great enthusiasm and eagerness

Etymology

From Old English *drēorig* (gory, bloody; later ‘sorrowful’), related to *drēor* (gore, blood).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00810969-s
Synset POS: s
