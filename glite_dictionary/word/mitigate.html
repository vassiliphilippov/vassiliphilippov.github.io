to mitigate

1. VERB

/ ˈmɪt̬.ɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈmɪt.ɪ.ɡeɪt / (BrE)
to make something less severe, harmful, or painful; to lessen the intensity or seriousness of something
make less severe or serious

Examples

Laws were introduced to mitigate the effects of industrial pollution.
Taking swift action can help mitigate the risks involved in this process.
New policies are needed to mitigate the impact of climate change on vulnerable communities.

Synset Examples

mitigating circumstances

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to alleviate - make something less severe or burdensome
  • Antonym: to exacerbate - make a situation worse or more severe

Etymology

From Latin mitigare (to soften, alleviate), from mitis (mild) + agere (to do, make).

2. VERB

/ ˈmɪt̬.ɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈmɪt.ɪ.ɡeɪt / (BrE)
to make something less severe, serious, or painful; to reduce the harmful effects of something
make less severe or serious

Examples

The new policies were designed to mitigate the effects of climate change.
Effective communication can help mitigate conflicts in the workplace.
They took several measures to mitigate the risk of infection after the outbreak.

Synset Examples

The circumstances extenuate the crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to alleviate - make suffering, deficiency, or a problem less severe
  • Antonym: to exacerbate - make a problem, bad situation, or negative feeling worse

Etymology

From Latin mitigare (to soften, alleviate).

Synset POS: v
