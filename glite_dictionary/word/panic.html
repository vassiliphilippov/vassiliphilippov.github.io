panic

1. NOUN

/ ˈpænɪk / (AmE), / ˈpænɪk / (BrE)
A sudden, intense feeling of uncontrollable fear or anxiety, often causing irrational behavior.
sudden intense fear or anxiety

Examples

When the fire alarm sounded, a wave of panic swept through the building.
She tried to control her panic before stepping onto the stage.
In the chaos of the accident, panic made it hard to think clearly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fear - an unpleasant emotion caused by the belief that someone or something is dangerous
  • Antonym: calm - the absence of strong emotions or agitation

Etymology

From Greek 'panikos', relating to the god Pan, whose presence was believed to cause sudden fear.

2. VERB

/ ˈpænɪk / (AmE), / ˈpænɪk / (BrE)
to feel or cause someone to feel sudden, uncontrollable fear or anxiety
to become or cause to become suddenly afraid

Examples

The loud crash outside panicked everyone in the classroom.
Don't panic if you forget your lines during the play.
The sudden smoke alarm panicked the shoppers, causing them to rush to the exits.

Synset Examples

The mere thought of an isolation cell panicked the prisoners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alarm - to cause to feel sudden fear or anxiety
  • Antonym: calm - to make someone free from fear or agitation

Etymology

From Greek 'panikos', relating to the god Pan, who was said to cause sudden fear.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpænɪk / (AmE), / ˈpænɪk / (BrE)
a sudden, overpowering feeling of fear or anxiety affecting a group of people, often in reaction to an anticipated event or danger
sudden mass fear or anxiety

Examples

There was a sudden panic in the crowd when the fire alarm went off.
After the earthquake, panic spread quickly through the city.
Rumors of a gas leak caused panic at the shopping mall.

Synset Examples

panic in the stock market
a war scare
a bomb scare led them to evacuate the building

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scare - a sudden feeling of fear or alarm
  • Antonym: calm - absence of fear or agitation

Etymology

From Greek panikos (pertaining to Pan), after the Greek god Pan whose presence was believed to cause sudden fear.

4. VERB

/ ˈpænɪk / (AmE), / ˈpænɪk / (BrE)
to suddenly feel so afraid or anxious that you lose the ability to think or behave calmly or sensibly
suddenly become extremely afraid

Examples

When the fire alarm sounded, several people began to panic and rushed to the exits.
Try not to panic if you can't find your passport before the flight.
He panicked during the interview and forgot everything he wanted to say.

Synset Examples

The students panicked when told that final exams were less than a week away

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: worry - feel anxious or uneasy about something
  • Synonym: freak out - lose control due to fear or anxiety

Etymology

From Greek panikos, relating to Pan, the god believed to cause sudden fear.
