Caddo

1. NOUN

/ ˈkædoʊ / (AmE), / ˈkædəʊ / (BrE)
a member of a group of Native American peoples historically inhabiting areas of the southern United States, especially in what are now Louisiana, Arkansas, Texas, and Oklahoma
Native American people of the southern US

Examples

Members of the Caddo tribe played a significant role in regional trade networks.
The Caddo developed complex mound-building societies along major rivers.
Artifacts from the Caddo culture are displayed in the state museum.

Etymology

From Caddo *kadohadacho*, referring to a tribal group name.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkædoʊ / (AmE), / ˈkædəʊ / (BrE)
a family of North American indigenous languages traditionally spoken by the Caddo people, especially in the Midwest United States
a North American indigenous language family

Examples

Few people still speak the Caddo language today, making preservation efforts crucial.
Linguists have studied Caddo to understand more about Native American history and culture.
He is one of the last fluent speakers of Caddo in his tribe.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Siouan - a different family of North American indigenous languages

Etymology

From Spanish 'Caddo', derived from the Caddo people's own name for themselves, 'kadohadacho'.
