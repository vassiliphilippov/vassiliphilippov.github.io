illiterate

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈlɪtərət / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪtərət / (BrE)
a person who is unable to read or write
a person unable to read or write

Examples

In rural areas, the number of illiterates remains high due to lack of access to education.
He taught basic reading skills to adult illiterates in the community center.
The campaign aims to reduce the population of illiterates by improving literacy programs.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: literate - a person who is able to read and write

Etymology

From Latin illiteratus (unlearned, ignorant), from in- (not) + litteratus (lettered).

3. ADJ

/ ɪˈlɪt̬.ɚ.ət / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪt.ər.ət / (BrE)
Lacking knowledge of literature or culture, especially in relation to language and the arts, sometimes beyond actual inability to read and write.
lacking cultural and literary knowledge

Examples

Many critics dismissed the painting as the work of an illiterate amateur with no appreciation for tradition.
His remarks revealed an illiterate attitude toward the importance of literature in society.
The film was criticized for being visually impressive but culturally and intellectually illiterate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uncultured - lacking education or refinement

Etymology

From Latin 'illiteratus' meaning 'unlettered, ignorant'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01424364-a
Synset POS: a

4. ADJ

/ ɪˈlɪt̬.ɚ.ət / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪt.ər.ət / (BrE)
Lacking knowledge or education in a particular subject or field.
lacking knowledge in a subject

Examples

Although he is a brilliant writer, he is illiterate when it comes to using a computer.
Many adults feel financially illiterate and struggle to manage their money effectively.
She realized she was musically illiterate after failing to read even the simplest sheet music.

Synset Examples

she is ignorant of quantum mechanics
he is musically illiterate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ignorant - lacking knowledge or awareness in general or about a particular thing
  • Antonym: knowledgeable - possessing knowledge or understanding; well-informed

Etymology

From Latin illiteratus, from in- (not) + litteratus (lettered, educated).
