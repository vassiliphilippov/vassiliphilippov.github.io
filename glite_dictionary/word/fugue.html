fugue

1. NOUN

/ fjuːɡ / (AmE), / fjuːɡ / (BrE)
A dissociative disorder in which a person loses awareness of their identity, leaves home, and assumes a new identity, with no memory of their former life during the state or of the fugue period afterwards.
dissociative disorder involving loss of identity and travel
medical

Examples

After weeks missing, the man was found living under a different name, suffering a fugue state.
Cases of fugue are rare but well-documented in psychiatric literature.
He remembered nothing about the time spent away during his fugue.

Etymology

From French 'fugue', from Latin 'fuga' meaning 'flight'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfjuːɡ / (AmE), / ˈfjuːɡ / (BrE)
A musical composition in which a short melody or phrase (the subject) is introduced by one part and successively taken up by others and developed by interweaving the parts.
a contrapuntal musical form

Examples

Bach composed some of his greatest works in the form of a fugue.
The orchestra performed a complex fugue during the concert, captivating the audience.
She studied the structure of a fugue for her music theory class.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canon - a musical form in which a melody is imitated by one or more voices at a fixed interval

Etymology

From Italian 'fuga' (flight), from Latin 'fuga' (flight, fleeing).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfjuːɡ / (AmE), / ˈfjuːɡ / (BrE)
A dreamlike state of altered consciousness, often with loss of identity and memory, that may last for hours or days.
state of altered consciousness
medical

Examples

The patient entered a fugue after the traumatic event, unable to recall his own name for days.
During her fugue, she traveled several hundred miles without any memory of doing so.
Episodes of fugue are rare but can profoundly affect a person's sense of self.

Etymology

From French 'fugue', from Italian 'fuga', meaning flight; related to the notion of 'flight' from one's own identity.
