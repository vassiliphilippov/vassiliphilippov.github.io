to criticise

1. VERB

/ ˈkrɪtɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkrɪtɪsʌɪz / (BrE)
to express disapproval of someone or something by pointing out faults or shortcomings
express disapproval or find fault
british

Examples

It is unfair to criticise someone without knowing the full story.
The coach was quick to criticise the team's lack of effort after the game.
She doesn't like to criticise her colleagues in public.

Synset Examples

The paper criticized the new movie
Don't knock the food — it's free

Related Terms

  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something
  • Synonym: to criticize - alternative spelling (American English) for 'criticise', meaning to find faults or express disapproval

Etymology

From Greek kritikos (able to judge), via French critiquer and English critic.

2. VERB

/ ˈkrɪtɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkrɪtɪsaɪz / (BrE)
to express disapproval of someone or something by pointing out faults or shortcomings; to act as a critic
express disapproval; point out faults

Examples

She tends to criticise her colleagues even when they're doing their best.
It's important to offer solutions when you criticise someone's work.
He rarely criticises others without giving constructive feedback.

Synset Examples

Those who criticize others often are not perfect, either

Related Terms

  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something
  • Antonym: compliment - to say something nice about someone or something

Etymology

From Greek 'kritikos' (able to judge), via Latin and French.
