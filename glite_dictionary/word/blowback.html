blowback

1. NOUN

/ ˈbloʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈbləʊˌbæk / (BrE)
the backward escape of gases and unburned gunpowder from a firearm after it is discharged.
backward escape of gases from a gun

Examples

The old rifle suffered from excessive blowback whenever it was fired.
Engineers redesigned the chamber to reduce blowback and improve safety.
After pulling the trigger, the shooter noticed some blowback from the side vent.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbloʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈbləʊˌbak / (BrE)
A system of operation in self-loading firearms where energy from the cartridge case being pushed backward by expanding gases is used to cycle the action.
auto-loading firearm operating system

Examples

The pistol uses a simple blowback mechanism, making it easy to maintain and reliable.
Many submachine guns rely on blowback operation due to its cost-effectiveness and simplicity.
The designer chose blowback operation to reduce the number of moving parts in the weapon.

Synset Examples

In most actions that use blowback operation, the breech is not locked mechanically at the time of firing: the inertia of the bolt and recoil spring(s), relative to the weight of the bullet, delays opening of the breech until the bullet has left the barrel

3. NOUN

/ ˈbloʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈbləʊˌbæk / (BrE)
misinformation that returns to the country where it originated after being spread abroad by its own intelligence services
recirculated misinformation from own intelligence

Examples

The government's secret operation led to unexpected blowback when the false story resurfaced at home.
Analysts expressed concern about blowback from disinformation campaigns targeting foreign media.
Recent reports highlighted how blowback can undermine domestic trust in official sources.

Etymology

Originally U.S. intelligence slang (1950s), from 'blow' + 'back', referring to unintended negative consequences.
