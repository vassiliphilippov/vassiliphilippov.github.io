Restoration

1. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
The return of the British monarchy in 1660, marking the re-establishment of the monarchy under Charles II after the English Civil War and Commonwealth period.
re-establishment of the British monarchy in 1660
british

Examples

Historians often debate the long-term impact of the Restoration on British politics and culture.
Theatres that had been closed during the Commonwealth were reopened after the Restoration.
The Restoration brought significant changes to art, literature, and society in 17th-century England.

Etymology

From Latin restauratio (a renewal, restoration); the historical use refers specifically to the restoration of the English monarchy.

2. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the period in England beginning in 1660 with the return of Charles II as king, following the collapse of the Commonwealth
period of Charles II's reign in England (1660–1685)

Examples

The Restoration marked a dramatic shift in English politics and culture.
Many theatres reopened during the Restoration after being closed under Puritan rule.
Literature from the Restoration period often reflects themes of wit and social satire.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Commonwealth - the period of republican government in England (1649–1660) preceding the Restoration

Etymology

From Latin *restauratio* (a renewing, restoration). The name refers to the restoration of the monarchy under Charles II.
