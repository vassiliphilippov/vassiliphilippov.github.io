submarine

1. ADJ

/ ˌsʌb.məˈrin / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.məˈriːn / (BrE)
existing, occurring, or used under the surface of the sea.
beneath the surface of the sea

Examples

The coral reef is a complex submarine ecosystem teeming with life.
Scientists have discovered several submarine volcanoes along the ocean floor.
A network of submarine cables transmits data between continents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: underwater - existing, situated, or taking place beneath the surface of water, especially the sea

Etymology

From Latin 'submarinus,' from 'sub-' (under) + 'marinus' (of the sea).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌb.məˌrin / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.məˈriːn / (BrE)
A warship designed to operate underwater, usually armed with torpedoes.
underwater warship

Examples

The submarine surfaced quietly in the harbor under cover of darkness.
During the war, a submarine sank several enemy ships in the Atlantic.
Modern submarines can stay submerged for weeks without resurfacing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: submersible - a craft designed to operate underwater

Etymology

From Latin 'submarinus', from 'sub-' (under) + 'marinus' (of the sea).

3. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.məˌrin / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.məˌriːn / (BrE)
to move forward or underneath in a sliding motion, typically by slipping under something
slide forward or underneath

Examples

During the scramble for the ball, the player managed to submarine under two defenders.
He submarined beneath the fence to retrieve his hat.
The child quickly submarined under the table to hide from his sister.

Synset Examples

The child was injured when he submarined under the safety belt of the car

Etymology

From the noun 'submarine', by analogy with the movement of a submarine vessel passing beneath something.

4. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.məˌrin / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.məˌriːn / (BrE)
To throw or pitch (a ball) with an underhand motion, especially so that the hand is below the waist level at the point of release.
throw underhanded, as in baseball

Examples

The pitcher decided to submarine the ball for the final strike.
Her unique ability to submarine kept the batters guessing.
He often submarines his throws to add a surprise element.

Etymology

Derived from the noun 'submarine', likening the low, underhand throwing style to traveling below the surface.

large sandwich on a split long roll
american

Examples

Related Terms
