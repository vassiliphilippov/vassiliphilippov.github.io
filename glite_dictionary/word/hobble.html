hobble

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɑbəl / (AmE), / ˈhɒbəl / (BrE)
a manner of walking in which one moves with difficulty, typically due to injury or stiffness in the leg or foot
an uneven gait caused by injury

Examples

After injuring her ankle during the game, she walked with a painful hobble back to the bench.
His hobble was noticeable after the accident, but he refused to use a cane.
The old dog's hobble grew worse in the cold weather.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: limp - a gait in which one walks with difficulty, typically because of a damaged or stiff leg or foot

Etymology

Probably imitative of a hobbling action, or from Middle English 'hoblen', frequentative of 'hoppen' (to hop).

2. VERB

/ ˈhɑbəl / (AmE), / ˈhɒbl / (BrE)
to strap together the foreleg and hind leg on each side of a horse so that the legs on the same side move in unison, usually to restrict movement
to strap a horse's legs together

Examples

The trainer needed to hobble the horses before letting them graze in the open field.
If you hobble a horse properly, it will be unable to wander far from camp.
Farmers sometimes hobble their animals to prevent them from running away.

Synset Examples

hobble race horses

Etymology

Probably of Low German origin, from Low German hobbeln (to hop or limp).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01292236-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈhɑbəl / (AmE), / ˈhɒbəl / (BrE)
A device fastened to the ankles or feet to prevent or restrict movement, often used for animals such as horses.
ankle or foot shackle

Examples

The horse wore a hobble to prevent it from wandering too far.
During transport, the prisoners were put in hobbles for security.
She removed the hobble from the donkey's legs at the end of the day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shackle - a device used for restraining a person's or animal's limbs

Etymology

From Middle English hobelen (to walk with a limp), later used for devices causing restricted movement.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03335892-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈhɑbəl / (AmE), / ˈhɒbl / (BrE)
to walk with difficulty, typically because of pain, injury, or physical limitation
walk with difficulty due to injury or pain

Examples

After spraining his ankle, he could only hobble slowly across the room.
The injured dog managed to hobble back to its owner.
She watched her grandfather hobble down the garden path, leaning on his cane.

Synset Examples

The old woman hobbles down to the store every day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: limp - to walk with difficulty, typically due to injury or stiffness
  • Antonym: stride - to walk with long, decisive steps

Etymology

From Middle English hoblen (to limp), probably of imitative origin.

5. VERB

/ ˈhɑb.əl / (AmE), / ˈhɒb.əl / (BrE)
to limit or restrict the action, progress, or effectiveness of someone or something
impede; restrict progress

Examples

Efforts to reform the system were hobbled by bureaucratic resistance.
Severe budget cuts can hobble a company's growth for years.
She refused to let her injury hobble her career ambitions.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to impede - to slow or prevent the progress of
  • Synonym: hamper - to make movement or progress difficult
  • Synonym: to restrain - to prevent from doing something; to hold back

Etymology

Originally meant 'to tie the legs together' from late Middle English, probably from hob (as in hobbledehoy).
