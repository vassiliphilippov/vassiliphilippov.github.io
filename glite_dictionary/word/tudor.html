Tudor

1. NOUN

/ ˈtuːdər / (AmE), / ˈtjuːdə / (BrE)
a member of the English royal dynasty that ruled from 1485 to 1603
member of English ruling dynasty (1485–1603)

Examples

Henry VIII was a famous Tudor known for his six marriages.
The Tudors played a vital role in establishing the English Reformation.
As a Tudor, Elizabeth I presided over a golden age of English culture.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Stuart - member of the royal dynasty that succeeded the Tudors in England

Etymology

From the Welsh name Tudur, borne by the founder of the dynasty, Henry VII.

2. ADJ

/ ˈtuː.dɚ / (AmE), / ˈtjuː.də / (BrE)
Relating to or characteristic of the style of architecture and design in England during the period of the Tudor dynasty (late 15th to early 17th centuries), often featuring half-timbering and steeply pitched roofs.
English architectural style from Tudor period

Examples

The old manor features a classic Tudor façade with exposed wooden beams.
She prefers Tudor furniture for its intricate carvings and historical charm.
Several Tudor cottages still stand in the village, displaying the distinctive architectural style.

Synset Examples

half-timbered Tudor houses
Tudor furniture

Etymology

From the Tudor dynasty, named after the English royal house that ruled from 1485 to 1603.

Synset POS: a

3. PROPN

/ ˈtuːdər / (AmE), / ˈtjuːdə / (BrE)
a royal dynasty that ruled England from Henry VII to Elizabeth I, from 1485 to 1603
English royal dynasty (1485–1603)

Examples

The reign of the Tudor dynasty marked a significant change in English history.
Many palaces were built during the Tudor period.
Henry VIII was one of the most famous Tudor monarchs.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Stuart - the royal dynasty that succeeded the Tudors in England

Etymology

From the Welsh personal name Tudur, adopted by Henry VII.
