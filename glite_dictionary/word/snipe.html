snipe

1. NOUN

/ snaɪp / (AmE), / snaɪp / (BrE)
a wading bird with a long, straight bill, found in wetlands, belonging to the sandpiper family; similar to the woodcock
wading bird with a long, straight bill

Examples

We spotted a snipe in the marsh early this morning.
The snipe uses its long bill to search for food in muddy ground.
Several species of snipe migrate across continents each year.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: woodcock - a woodland bird similar in appearance to the snipe

Etymology

From Old French *esnipe*, of Germanic origin; related to Swedish *snäpp* (snipe).

2. VERB

/ snaɪp / (AmE), / snaɪp / (BrE)
to criticize someone in a sly or indirect way, especially in speech or writing
criticize slyly or indirectly

Examples

She couldn't help but snipe at her colleague during the meeting.
Many politicians snipe at each other's policies in the media.
Instead of offering solutions, he just kept sniping from the sidelines.

Synset Examples

The editors of the left-leaning paper attacked the new House Speaker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - to take aggressive or hostile action against someone verbally
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

Originally referred to shooting snipes (the bird) from a concealed position; figurative use for verbal attacks developed later.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00864347-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ snaɪp / (AmE), / snaɪp / (BrE)
to shoot at someone or something from a concealed position, often with great precision
to shoot with precision from concealment

Examples

The soldier tried to snipe the enemy from his hidden vantage point.
She managed to snipe the last target in the shooting competition with ease.
Gamers often use the term when they snipe their rivals from afar in online matches.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shoot - to fire a weapon at someone or something

Etymology

Originally from Middle English *snype* (the bird), by analogy with the difficulty of shooting the bird.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01137927-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ snaɪp / (AmE), / snaɪp / (BrE)
a gunshot fired from a concealed position, typically by a sniper
gunshot from a hidden position

Examples

The soldier heard the sharp snipe echo across the field and immediately took cover.
During the standoff, an unexpected snipe shattered the silence.
Police officers are trained to react quickly to a snipe from unknown sources.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sniper - a person who shoots from a concealed position

Etymology

From Dutch 'snipen' (to shoot at snipe), referring to the bird's elusiveness and the skill required to shoot it.

5. VERB

/ snaɪp / (AmE), / snaɪp / (BrE)
to hunt or shoot snipe (a type of wading bird), especially as a sport
hunt or shoot snipe (bird)

Examples

During the autumn migration, skilled hunters often snipe along the marshy banks.
In the past, villagers would snipe for food to supplement their diets.
They spent the morning sniping quietly in the wetlands, hoping to catch sight of a rare species.

Etymology

From the noun 'snipe', which refers to the bird; originally from Old Norse 'snípa'.
