doom

1. VERB

/ duːm / (AmE), / duːm / (BrE)
to make certain that someone or something will experience failure, destruction, or an adverse fate
make certain of failure or destruction

Examples

The judges' remarks may doom the team's chances of winning.
Her constant negativity could doom the project from the start.
The bad weather ultimately doomed their expedition to failure.

Synset Examples

This decision will doom me to lose my position

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to condemn - to sentence someone to a particular punishment, especially death

Etymology

From Old English 'dōm' (judgment, decree).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00892376-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ duːm / (AmE), / duːm / (BrE)
A fate or destiny that is unpleasant and usually unavoidable, often involving destruction or great harm.
unpleasant or disastrous fate

Examples

Everyone felt a sense of doom as the storm clouds gathered over the city.
The prophecy foretold doom for the kingdom if the king broke his vow.
Despite the team’s efforts, defeat and doom seemed unavoidable.

Synset Examples

everyone was aware of the approaching doom but was helpless to avoid it
that's unfortunate but it isn't the end of the world

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fate - the development of events beyond a person's control, regarded as determined by a supernatural power
  • Synonym: destiny - the events that will necessarily happen to a particular person or thing in the future
  • Degree Lower: fortune - chance or luck as an external, arbitrary force affecting human affairs

Etymology

From Old English dōm (judgment, decree, fate), related to Old Norse dómr and German Dom.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07348806-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ duːm / (AmE), / duːm / (BrE)
(archaic) To pronounce judgment or sentence, especially in a court of law.
pronounce judgment or sentence
archaic

Examples

The judge is required by law to doom the accused if found guilty.
In older times, kings would doom criminals to harsh penalties during public trials.
He feared the magistrate would doom him for his crimes, sealing his fate.

Synset Examples

He was condemned to ten years in prison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to condemn - to declare to be guilty and sentence to punishment

Etymology

Old English *dōmian* (to judge, condemn), from *dōm* (judgment).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00908688-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ duːm / (AmE), / duːm / (BrE)
to decide or decree the fate of someone or something, often with the sense of inevitably determining a negative outcome
to decree or determine fate, usually negatively

Examples

The contract dispute may doom the project before it even begins.
The hero was doomed to fail despite his best efforts.
Fate seemed to doom their love from the very start.

Synset Examples

She was destined to become a great pianist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to destine - to intend or choose for a particular purpose or fate
  • Synonym: to condemn - to officially declare to be wrong or to doom to a negative fate

Etymology

Old English *dōm* (judgment, decree).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00748465-v
Synset POS: v
