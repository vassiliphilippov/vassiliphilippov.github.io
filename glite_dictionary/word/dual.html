dual

1. ADJ

/ ˈduːəl, ˈdjuːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
consisting of or involving two parts, elements, or aspects, typically working together or as a pair.
having two parts or aspects

Examples

The new plane has dual controls so both pilots can operate it.
He holds dual citizenship in Canada and France.
The stereo system offers dual speakers for enhanced sound quality.

Synset Examples

an egg with a double yolk
a double (binary) star
double doors
dual controls for pilot and copilot
duple (or double) time consists of two (or a multiple of two) beats to a measure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: double - twice as much in size, number, or amount; having two similar parts

Etymology

From Latin 'dualis', from 'duo' (two).

2. ADJ

/ ˈduːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
having two different and often contrasting aspects or qualities
having two different aspects

Examples

The politician maintained a dual identity, balancing her family life and her career in public service.
He struggled with the dual responsibilities of being both a student and a part-time worker.
Modern smartphones often support dual SIM cards for work and personal use.

Synset Examples

a double (or dual) role for an actor
the office of a clergyman is twofold; public preaching and private influence
every episode has its double and treble meaning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: double - having two parts or twice as much
  • Antonym: single - one only; not double or multiple

Etymology

From Latin 'dualis' (of two), from 'duo' (two).

3. ADJ

/ ˈduːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
(Linguistics) Relating to a grammatical number that refers to exactly two persons or things, as in some languages.
grammatical number referring to two

Examples

In some languages, nouns and verbs take a dual form to indicate two items.
Ancient Greek used the dual to distinguish pairs from single or multiple items.
The dual is no longer present in most modern languages.

Synset Examples

ancient Greek had the dual form but it has merged with the plural form in modern Greek

Related Terms

  • Antonym: singular - grammatical number referring to one item
  • Antonym: plural - grammatical number referring to more than two items

Etymology

From Latin 'dualis', from 'duo' meaning 'two'.
