infiltration

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fɪlˈtreɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fɪlˈtreɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the gradual movement of a liquid into or through a substance, especially by passing through small spaces or a porous material
gradual liquid movement through a material

Examples

The infiltration of rainwater helps replenish underground aquifers over time.
Scientists measured how the infiltration rate of water varied across different soil types.
Reduced infiltration can lead to increased surface runoff and flooding.

Synset Examples

the percolation of rainwater through the soil
the infiltration of seawater through the lava

Related Terms

  • Synonym: percolation - the process of a liquid slowly passing through a filter or porous substance

Etymology

From Latin infiltrare (to filter in), from in- (in) + filtrum (felt, filter).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fɪlˈtreɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fɪlˈtreɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of entering or penetrating a place or group, especially secretly and gradually, such as the surreptitious entry of individuals or small groups into enemy positions without detection
secret entry, especially for military purposes

Examples

The spy's infiltration of the enemy's headquarters went undetected for months.
Special forces are trained in covert infiltration behind enemy lines.
The company fears industrial infiltration by competitors seeking trade secrets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: penetration - the action of entering or passing into or through something
  • Antonym: exfiltration - the process of withdrawing subjects from an area, especially military withdrawal by stealth

Etymology

From Latin 'infiltrare' (to filter into), from 'in-' (into) + 'filtrare' (to filter).
