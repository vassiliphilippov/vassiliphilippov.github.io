Conodonta

1. NOUN

/ ˌkoʊ.nəˈdɒn.tə / (AmE), / ˌkɒ.nəˈdɒn.tə / (BrE)
an extinct order or phylum of primitive, eel-like marine vertebrates known mainly from fossilized tooth-like elements, important in paleontology for dating rocks
extinct group of primitive marine vertebrates

Examples

Fossils of Conodonta are often used by geologists to date Paleozoic rock layers.
The precise evolutionary relationships of Conodonta with other vertebrates remain a subject of scientific debate.
Studies of Conodonta microfossils have greatly contributed to our understanding of ancient marine environments.

Etymology

From Greek 'konos' (cone) + 'odous' (tooth), referring to the cone-shaped fossils.

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːnəˈdɒntə / (AmE), / ˌkɒnəˈdɒntə / (BrE)
an extinct order of eel-like, primitive vertebrates known from their tooth-like fossil elements, with uncertain taxonomy; sometimes regarded as a separate phylum
extinct order of primitive vertebrates

Examples

Fossil remains of Conodonta are often used by geologists to date sedimentary rocks.
The classification of Conodonta remains debated due to uncertain features.
Scientists believe that Conodonta thrived in marine environments over 200 million years ago.

Etymology

From Greek 'konos' (cone) and 'odous' (tooth), referring to their cone-shaped teeth.
