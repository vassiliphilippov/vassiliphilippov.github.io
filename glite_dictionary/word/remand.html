remand

1. VERB

/ rɪˈmænd / (AmE), / rɪˈmɑːnd / (BrE)
to send a case or matter back to another court, committee, or authority for further action or decision
refer a case back to another authority
legal

Examples

The appellate court decided to remand the case to the lower court for a new trial.
If new evidence emerges, the board may remand the matter for further investigation.
The judge will remand the issue to the committee for additional consideration.

Etymology

From Latin remandare (to send back), from re- (back) + mandare (to order).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈmænd / (AmE), / rɪˈmɑːnd / (BrE)
the act of sending an accused person back into custody to await trial or further court proceedings
sending an accused back into custody
legal

Examples

The judge ordered the remand of the defendant until the next hearing.
After a brief court appearance, he was placed on remand pending further investigation.
The suspect has spent three months in remand awaiting trial.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: release - to set a person free from custody

Etymology

From Latin remandare (to send back), via Old French and Middle English.

3. VERB

/ rɪˈmænd / (AmE), / rɪˈmɑːnd / (BrE)
to send someone back into custody, especially await a further hearing or trial; to confine or hold in detention by order of a court
send back into custody by court order
legal

Examples

The judge decided to remand the accused in custody until the next hearing.
After being charged, she was remanded to a local detention center.
It is unusual for a court to remand someone without strong evidence.

Synset Examples

The suspects were imprisoned without trial
the murderer was incarcerated for the rest of his life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to detain - to keep someone in official custody, typically for legal reasons
  • Antonym: release - to set someone free from confinement or custody

Etymology

From Old French remander, from Latin remandare (to send back word, refer back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02500005-v
Synset POS: v
