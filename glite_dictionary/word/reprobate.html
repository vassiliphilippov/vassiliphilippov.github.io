reprobate

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (BrE)
a person who behaves in a morally unprincipled or depraved way
morally unprincipled person

Examples

The judge called him a reprobate who showed no remorse for his crimes.
Although considered a reprobate by his neighbors, he remained charming and witty in conversation.
The preacher warned the congregation not to associate with reprobates who led them astray.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scoundrel - a person who behaves dishonorably or unscrupulously
  • Synonym: villain - a wicked or evil person
  • Antonym: saint - a person of great virtue or holiness

Etymology

From Late Latin reprobatus 'disapproved, condemned', past participle of reprobare 'disapprove, reject'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (BrE)
morally unprincipled; showing no regard for what is right or proper
morally unprincipled or corrupt

Examples

The judge was appalled by the reprobate behavior displayed in the courtroom.
His reprobate habits eventually led to his downfall and exile from polite society.
Despite his charming appearance, the prince led a secret, reprobate life.

Synset Examples

depraved criminals
a perverted sense of loyalty
the reprobate conduct of a gambling aristocrat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: depraved - morally corrupt or wicked
  • Antonym: virtuous - having or showing high moral standards

Etymology

From Late Latin *reprobat-* (disapproved), past participle of *reprobare* (to disapprove).

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛprəʊˌbeɪt / (BrE)
(formal) to express strong disapproval of; to condemn or censure.
formally express strong disapproval

Examples

The board publicly reprobated the author's inflammatory comments.
Many citizens reprobate such dishonesty in political leaders.
She was reprobated for her decision by her peers.

Synset Examples

We condemn the racism in South Africa
These ideas were reprobated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to condemn - to express complete disapproval of, usually in public
  • Antonym: to approve - to have a positive opinion of; to consent to

Etymology

From Latin *reprobat-* (disapproved), past participle of *reprobare* (to disapprove, reject).

4. VERB

/ ˈrɛprəˌbeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛprəʊˌbeɪt / (BrE)
(formal) to condemn someone to eternal damnation; to reject as unworthy of salvation.
condemn to eternal damnation
archaic

Examples

Many preachers warned that sinful behavior could cause God to reprobate an entire community.
Some theological doctrines taught that the wicked would be reprobated forever.
In the sermon, the priest warned his congregation against actions that might cause the Lord to reprobate them.

Synset Examples

God reprobated the unrepenting sinner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to condemn - to pronounce as guilty; to sentence, especially in a legal or religious sense

Etymology

From Latin *reprobat-* (disapproved), from *reprobare* (disapprove, reject).
