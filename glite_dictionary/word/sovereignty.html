sovereignty

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɑːv(ə)rnti, ˈsɑːvrənti / (AmE), / ˈsɒv(ə)rɪnti, ˈsɒvrənti / (BrE)
supreme power or authority, especially that held by a monarch or a governing body
supreme royal or governing authority

Examples

The king's sovereignty was unquestioned throughout the land for decades.
After the revolution, the people's sovereignty replaced that of the monarchy.
The treaty recognized the full sovereignty of the nation over its territories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: autonomy - the right or condition of self-government
  • Antonym: subjugation - the act of bringing under control; conquest

Etymology

From Old French 'sovereinete', from 'sovereign' (ruler, supreme lord).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɑvrənti / (AmE), / ˈsɒvərənti / (BrE)
The authority of a state to govern another state or territory; supreme control exercised by one political entity over another.
a state's authority to govern another

Examples

The colonial power exercised sovereignty over its overseas territories for centuries.
The treaty transferred sovereignty of the region from one country to another.
International law often debates issues of sovereignty when one state interferes in another's affairs.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: autonomy - the right or condition of self-government

Etymology

From Old French 'souveraineté', from 'souverain' (sovereign), ultimately from Latin 'superānus' (chief, above).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05205589-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɑːvrənti / (AmE), / ˈsɒvrənti / (BrE)
the authority of a state to govern itself or control its own affairs, free from outside interference
supreme authority of a state

Examples

Many countries fought hard to gain their sovereignty from colonial powers.
The treaty affirmed the sovereignty of each nation over its territory.
Debates over national sovereignty often arise in the context of international organizations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: autonomy - the right or condition of self-government
  • Antonym: dependency - a territory that is subject to the authority of another state

Etymology

From Old French souveraineté, from souverain (sovereign), from Latin superanus (chief, principal).
