tupelo

1. NOUN

/ ˈtuːpəˌloʊ / (AmE), / ˈtjuːpɪləʊ / (BrE)
A tree of swampy regions in North America, belonging to the genus Nyssa, known for its hard wood and sometimes for its edible fruit.
a North American swamp tree (genus Nyssa)

Examples

The swamp was lined with old tupelo trees, their roots reaching into the water.
Beekeepers prize tupelo trees for the light, sweet honey they produce.
In autumn, the leaves of the tupelo turn a brilliant red, brightening the marshy landscape.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gum tree - a tree that exudes gum, including species like tupelo and eucalyptus

Etymology

From Creek (Muskogean) *ito* (tree) + *opilwa* (swamp).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtuː.pəˌloʊ / (AmE), / ˈtjuː.pɪˌləʊ / (BrE)
A pale, soft wood from a tupelo tree, especially the water gum.
pale soft wood of a tupelo tree

Examples

The craftsman used tupelo to carve delicate fishing lures because of its softness.
Swamps in the southeastern United States are rich with tupelo wood.
Because tupelo is pale and easy to work with, it's favored by woodcarvers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nyssa - genus of deciduous trees including the tupelo

Etymology

From Creek (Muskogee) *ito ‘tree’ + *opilwa ‘swamp’.
