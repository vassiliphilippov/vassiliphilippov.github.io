to aerate

1. VERB

/ ˈɛrˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˈeəreɪt / (BrE)
to supply, combine, or impregnate with air or oxygen, especially to improve or treat a substance
to supply with air or oxygen

Examples

Plants aerate the soil with their roots as they grow.
Doctors sometimes aerate blood outside the body during certain surgical procedures.
You should aerate your lawn every spring to encourage healthy grass growth.

Synset Examples

oxygenate blood

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oxygenate - to supply, treat, or enrich with oxygen

Etymology

From Latin 'aer' (air) + '-ate' (verb-forming suffix).

WordNet ID: oewn-00185610-v

2. VERB

/ ˈɛreɪt / (AmE), / ˈeəreɪt / (BrE)
to supply air to (sewage or liquid) to encourage the growth of organisms that break down organic matter
supply air to sewage to aid decomposition

Examples

The treatment plant routinely aerates the sewage to accelerate the breakdown of waste.
Engineers aerated the wastewater to promote the activity of beneficial bacteria.
Continuous aerating of sewage is essential for effective decomposition in many modern facilities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oxygenate - to supply or enrich with oxygen

Etymology

From Latin aer (air) + -ate (verb-forming suffix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00191331-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɛr.eɪt / (AmE), / ˈeəreɪt / (BrE)
To expose or introduce to air, especially to fresh air; to allow air to circulate through something.
expose to air, let air circulate

Examples

It is important to aerate your room regularly to keep the air fresh.
Gardeners often aerate the soil to help plants grow better.
You should aerate your old sneakers to get rid of unpleasant odors.

Synset Examples

aerate your old sneakers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ventilate - to allow fresh air to enter and circulate

Etymology

From Latin 'aer' (air) + '-ate', meaning 'to make air'.

Synset POS: v
