soggy

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɑɡi / (AmE), / ˈsɒɡi / (BrE)
(of food) having a heavy, dough-like texture, often due to insufficient leavening or being undercooked.
heavy or doughy in texture (esp. food)

Examples

The bread turned out soggy because I didn't bake it long enough.
Nobody wanted a soggy muffin with a doughy center.
Her pancakes were so soggy they stuck to the fork.

Synset Examples

the cake fell: it's a doughy mess

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doughy - having a soft, thick, and sticky texture like dough

Etymology

Probably from 'sog', a dialect word meaning 'to soak', plus '-y'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɑːɡi / (AmE), / ˈsɒɡi / (BrE)
(of soil or other material) very wet and soft, often unpleasantly so, because of water saturation.
wet and soft from water

Examples

After the heavy rain, the football field became soggy and difficult to play on.
Her shoes were soaked from walking across the soggy grass in the morning.
The bread turned soggy after sitting in the soup for too long.

Synset Examples

the ground was boggy under foot
a marshy coastline
miry roads
wet mucky lowland
muddy barnyard
quaggy terrain
the sloughy edge of the pond
swampy bayous

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boggy - soft, wet, and spongy ground; marshy
  • Antonym: dry - not containing or covered with water or moisture

Etymology

Probably from 'sog', of obscure origin, influenced by 'boggy'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02558836-s
Synset POS: s
