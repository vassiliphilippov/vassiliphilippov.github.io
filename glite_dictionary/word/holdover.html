holdover

1. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊldˌoʊvər / (AmE), / ˈhəʊldˌəʊvə / (BrE)
something that remains or survives from an earlier time or previous system
something surviving from the past

Examples

The policy is a holdover from an earlier administration.
Many traditions at the school are holdovers from the 19th century.
The building's ornate design is a holdover from a bygone era.

Synset Examples

a holdover from the sixties
hangovers from the 19th century

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relic - an object or custom that has survived from the past
  • Synonym: leftover - something that remains after the rest has been used or dealt with

Etymology

From the verb phrase 'hold over', meaning to keep or continue beyond a usual time.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊldˌoʊvɚ / (AmE), / ˈhəʊldˌəʊvə / (BrE)
an official who continues to serve in office after their official term has ended, often temporarily until a successor is appointed
official remaining in office past term

Examples

After the election, the mayor became a holdover until the new official was sworn in.
Several holdover officials were asked to stay during the transition period.
The council viewed him as a holdover from the previous administration.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incumbent - a person currently holding office

Etymology

From the phrasal verb 'hold over', meaning to remain or extend beyond a set time.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10200422-n
Synset POS: n
