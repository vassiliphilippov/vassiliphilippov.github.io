Comanche

1. NOUN

/ koʊˈmæn.tʃi / (AmE), / kəˈmæn.tʃi / (BrE)
A member of a Native American people originally living between Wyoming and the Mexican border, now chiefly in Oklahoma.
Native American people of the central U.S.

Examples

The Comanche were renowned for their skill as horsemen on the plains.
Many Comanche now live in Oklahoma, preserving their cultural traditions.
He is proud of his Comanche heritage and often shares stories of his ancestors.

Etymology

From Spanish 'comanche', likely from Ute 'kimantsi' meaning 'enemy, foreigner'.

2. NOUN

/ kəˈmæn.tʃi / (AmE), / kəˈman.tʃi / (BrE)
the Native American language of the Comanche people, belonging to the Shoshonean branch of the Uto-Aztecan language family
Native American language of the Comanche

Examples

The linguist is conducting a study on the endangered Comanche language.
Fewer young people today are fluent speakers of Comanche.
She is working on a dictionary to help preserve Comanche.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Uto-Aztecan - a large language family of the Americas

Etymology

From Spanish Comanche, likely from Ute *kimantsi* (enemy, stranger), adopted by English to refer to the people and their language.
