faintness

1. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪntnəs / (AmE), / ˈfeɪntnɪs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being barely perceptible, especially in sound or light
quality of being barely perceptible

Examples

There was a faintness in her voice that made it hard to hear her words.
The faintness of the distant music made it sound almost like a dream.
He was startled by the faintness of the knock at the door.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: loudness - the quality of being loud

Etymology

From Middle English 'feintness', from Old French 'feint', from Latin 'fingere' (to shape, form).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪnt.nəs / (AmE), / ˈfeɪnt.nəs / (BrE)
The quality of being dim, weak, or lacking distinctness or contrast.
quality of being dim or lacking contrast

Examples

There was a certain faintness to the light in the old attic.
The faintness of the sound made it hard to tell where it was coming from.
Artists sometimes use faintness to create a sense of mystery or distance in their work.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: brightness - the quality of being bright or giving out or reflecting much light
  • Synonym: dullness - the quality of being lacking in liveliness, vividness, or sharpness

Etymology

From Middle English 'feint' (lacking strength) + '-ness' (forming nouns of quality or state).

lack of courage or boldness

Examples

In moments requiring leadership, faintness is often more damaging than outright failure.

5. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪnt.nəs / (AmE), / ˈfeɪnt.nəs / (BrE)
A feeling of weakness or dizziness, as if about to lose consciousness; the state of being faint.
a feeling of weakness or near-fainting

Examples

She felt a sudden faintness as she stood up quickly from her chair.
During the long ceremony, several people complained of faintness and needed to sit down.
The patient's faintness was caused by dehydration and lack of food.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dizziness - a sensation of spinning and loss of balance
  • Synonym: nausea - feeling of sickness with an inclination to vomit
  • Antonym: vigor - physical strength and good health

Etymology

From Middle English 'feint', from Old French, from Latin 'fingere' (to shape, contrive) with '-ness' added to form the noun.
