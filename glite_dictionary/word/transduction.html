transduction

1. NOUN

/ trænzˈdʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / trænzˈdʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of converting energy from one form to another, especially by a device such as a transducer
the conversion of energy from one form to another

Examples

A microphone uses transduction to convert sound waves into electrical signals.
The process of transduction is fundamental in many electronic devices and sensors.
In sonar systems, transduction turns underwater sound into signals that can be analyzed.

Synset Examples

the transduction of acoustic waves into voltages by a microphone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conversion - the act of changing from one form to another

Etymology

From Latin transducere (to lead across), from trans- (across) + ducere (to lead).

2. NOUN

/ trænzˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / trænzˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the process by which genetic material is transferred from one cell to another by means of a plasmid or bacteriophage
genetic transfer by virus or plasmid
medical

Examples

Bacteriophages play a critical role in transduction by carrying DNA between bacterial cells.
Scientists use transduction to study gene function in laboratory experiments.
During generalized transduction, virtually any bacterial gene can be transferred.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transformation - genetic alteration of a cell by uptake of DNA from its surroundings
  • Synonym: conjugation - transfer of genetic material between bacterial cells by direct contact

Etymology

From Latin 'transducere' (to lead across) via 'transductio' (a leading across).
