minuscule

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪnəˌskjul / (AmE), / ˈmɪnəskjuːl / (BrE)
A small, cursive style of writing developed from uncial between the 7th and 9th centuries and used in medieval manuscripts.
medieval small cursive script

Examples

The ancient monk copied the manuscript in elegant minuscule script.
Scholars can distinguish Carolingian minuscule from later medieval handwriting styles.
Original documents written in minuscule are often hard for modern readers to decipher.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: uncial - a rounded script used in Latin and Greek manuscripts from the 3rd to 8th centuries

Etymology

From Latin 'minusculus' (rather small), adopted as a term for the writing style due to its small letter forms.

4. NOUN

/ ˈmɪnəˌskjul / (AmE), / ˈmɪnəskjuːl / (BrE)
A lowercase letter, especially in historical contexts where typographers distinguished such characters from uppercase (majuscule) letters.
lowercase letter; typographical character

Examples

The medieval manuscript was written entirely in minuscule, making it more compact.
Modern editors often distinguish between minuscule and majuscule forms when transcribing ancient texts.
The introduction of minuscule letters greatly improved the legibility and speed of writing.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: majuscule - an uppercase or capital letter in typography

Etymology

From Latin 'minusculus' (rather small), used in paleography for small, cursive writing contrasted with majuscule (large) letters.
