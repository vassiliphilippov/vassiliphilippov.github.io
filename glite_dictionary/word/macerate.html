to macerate

1. VERB

/ ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmæsəreɪt / (BrE)
To soften or break up something into its parts by soaking it in a liquid.
soften or separate by soaking

Examples

After soaking overnight, the dried fruit will macerate and become plump and flavorful.
To make a perfect salsa, you need to macerate the tomatoes with salt and lime juice.
The chef instructed us to macerate the onions in vinegar before adding them to the salad.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soak - to immerse in liquid until softened

Etymology

From Latin 'macerare' (to soften, make soft).

2. VERB

/ ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (BrE)
to soften or break down a substance by soaking it in liquid, often causing it to disintegrate or become mushy
soften or break down by soaking

Examples

The chef decided to macerate the strawberries in sugar to enhance their flavor.
Long exposure to rain will macerate the cardboard, making it fall apart.
Some recipes require you to macerate dried fruits in rum overnight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soak - leave something immersed in liquid
  • Synonym: to disintegrate - to break into small parts or pieces

From Latin macerare (to soften, steep).

3. VERB

/ ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmæsəreɪt / (BrE)
(of something) to become soft or break down as a result of being soaked in liquid for a long time.
become soft/break down from soaking

Examples

If paper is left in water for too long, it will macerate and fall apart.
The fruit began to macerate after soaking in syrup overnight.
The old manuscript macerated in the floodwater, making the text almost impossible to read.

4. VERB

/ ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmæsəˌreɪt / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to grow thin or weak, typically through starvation or wasting away
make thin or weak (often by wasting)

Examples

The disease began to macerate his body until he could barely stand.
Years of hardship and hunger would macerate even the strongest individuals.
The prisoner was macerated by months of neglect and poor nutrition.
