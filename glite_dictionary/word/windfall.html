windfall

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndˌfɔl / (AmE), / ˈwɪndˌfɔːl / (BrE)
an unexpected event that brings good fortune, especially a sudden gain of money or wealth.
unexpected gain or good fortune

Examples

Winning the lottery was an incredible windfall for her, allowing her to retire early.
The company received a financial windfall after selling part of its business.
A surprise inheritance proved to be a welcome windfall for the struggling family.

Synset Examples

the demand for testing has created a boom for those unregulated laboratories where boxes of specimen jars are processed like an assembly line

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boon - a thing that is helpful or beneficial
  • Synonym: bonanza - a situation that creates a sudden increase in wealth, good fortune, or profits

Etymology

Originally referred to fruit blown down by the wind and thus free for the taking.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndˌfɔl / (AmE), / ˈwɪndˌfɔːl / (BrE)
A piece of fruit that has fallen from a tree, especially due to the wind rather than being picked; sometimes gathered and used as food.
fruit fallen from a tree

Examples

We collected several windfalls from beneath the old apple tree.
The ground was covered in windfall plums after last night's storm.
She used windfall pears to make jam.

Etymology

From 'wind' + 'fall', originally referring to fruit blown down by the wind.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndˌfɔl / (AmE), / ˈwɪnd.fɔːl / (BrE)
a sum of money or advantage received unexpectedly or without effort
unexpected gain or advantage

Examples

The company received a windfall after selling its old headquarters.
After paying off her debts, she used the windfall to travel the world.
Taxpayers can expect a small windfall from the government surplus this year.

Synset Examples

The inheritance came as a huge windfall.
Winning the lottery was an unexpected windfall for her.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boon - a thing that is helpful or beneficial
  • Synonym: bonanza - a situation that creates a sudden increase in wealth or good fortune
  • Antonym: loss - the fact or process of losing something

Etymology

Originally referred to fruit blown down by the wind, which could be claimed by anyone.
