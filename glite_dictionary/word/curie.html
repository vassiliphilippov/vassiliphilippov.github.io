Curie

1. PROPN

/ ˈkjʊri / (AmE), / ˈkjʊəri / (BrE)
Pierre Curie (1859–1906), a French physicist known for his work on radioactivity and as the husband of Marie Curie.
French physicist Pierre Curie (1859–1906)

Examples

Pierre Curie collaborated with his wife, Marie Curie, in pioneering research on radioactivity.
The Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Pierre Curie and his wife in 1903.
Many textbooks mention Curie as a key figure in the discovery of radium.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Marie Curie - Polish-born physicist and chemist, wife of Pierre Curie, noted for research on radioactivity

2. PROPN

/ ˈkjʊri / (AmE), / ˈkjʊəri / (BrE)
Marie Curie (1867–1934), a French chemist and physicist born in Poland, renowned for her research on radioactivity and the discovery of radium and polonium; recipient of two Nobel Prizes.
famous scientist; Nobel laureate in chemistry and physics

Examples

Curie was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and remains an inspiration in science.
The discoveries made by Curie and her husband revolutionized the understanding of radioactivity.
Students often learn about Curie when studying the history of chemistry and physics.

Etymology

From the French surname Curie. Marie Curie was born Maria Sklodowska in Poland.
