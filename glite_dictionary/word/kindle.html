to kindle

1. VERB

/ ˈkɪn.dl̩ / (AmE), / ˈkɪn.dl̩ / (BrE)
(of something) to begin to burn or catch fire.
to catch fire; begin burning

Examples

The vast wheat field began to kindle under the scorching sun.
When exposed to a spark, the old newspapers quickly kindled.
A single match is all it takes to kindle a campfire in dry weather.

Synset Examples

The dried grass of the prairie kindled, spreading the flames for miles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ignite - to catch fire or cause to catch fire
  • Antonym: to extinguish - to put out (a fire, light, etc.)

Etymology

From Old Norse *kynda* (to light, make a fire).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɪndl̩ / (AmE), / ˈkɪndl̩ / (BrE)
to arouse or inspire (an emotion, feeling, or response) in someone
arouse or inspire feelings

Examples

Her speech managed to kindle hope in the hearts of the audience.
The film kindled strong emotions among viewers, leaving many in tears.
A simple act of kindness can kindle feelings of gratitude in others.

Synset Examples

arouse pity
raise a smile
evoke sympathy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to arouse - to evoke or awaken (a feeling, emotion, or response)
  • Antonym: to quell - to put an end to (a feeling or emotion); suppress

Etymology

From Old Norse 'kynda' (to set fire to), related to Old English 'cyndel' (torch, candle).

3. VERB

/ ˈkɪndl̩ / (AmE), / ˈkɪndl̩ / (BrE)
to cause something to start burning; to ignite
cause to start burning

Examples

She used a match to kindle the fireplace logs on a chilly evening.
The poem describes how hope can kindle in even the darkest times.
A sudden idea kindled his imagination, inspiring a new painting.

Synset Examples

The setting sun kindled the sky with oranges and reds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ignite - to set on fire, start burning
  • Antonym: to extinguish - to cause a fire or light to stop burning

Etymology

From Old Norse *kynda* (to kindle), related to Old English *cund* (known, manifest).
