scud

1. NOUN

/ skʌd / (AmE), / skʌd / (BrE)
the act of moving swiftly, especially as carried by the wind or before a storm
swift movement, especially before wind

Examples

The clouds began to scud across the sky as the wind picked up.
We watched the leaves in a wild scud along the deserted road.
His boat was caught in the scud of the gale, moving faster than he'd intended.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - a sudden quick movement toward something
  • Antonym: to linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English *scutten* (to move quickly).

2. VERB

/ skʌd / (AmE), / skʌd / (BrE)
to move or run swiftly, especially as driven by the wind
move swiftly, often driven by wind

Examples

Clouds began to scud across the evening sky as the wind picked up.
The boat scudded over the waves, propelled by the strong breeze.
He watched the leaves scudding down the street in the autumn wind.

Synset Examples

She dashed into the yard

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dart - to move suddenly and quickly
  • Synonym: dash - to move quickly and suddenly
  • Antonym: crawl - to move slowly

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse skudda (to push, thrust).

3. VERB

/ skʌd / (AmE), / skʌd / (BrE)
(of a ship or cloud) to move swiftly and smoothly, especially driven by the wind or before a gale.
move quickly as driven by wind

Examples

The fishing boat began to scud across the waves as the storm intensified.
Clouds scudded rapidly across the sky ahead of the approaching front.
As the wind picked up, the yacht scudded before the gale toward the harbor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - to move quickly and suddenly
  • Synonym: drift - to be carried along by currents of air or water
  • Antonym: crawl - to move slowly on hands and knees

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Norwegian 'skudda' (to scud) and Danish 'skud' (a push or shove).
