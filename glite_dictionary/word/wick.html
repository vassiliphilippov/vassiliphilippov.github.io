wick

1. NOUN

/ wɪk / (AmE), / wɪk / (BrE)
a strand of loosely woven material in a candle or oil lamp that draws up liquid fuel to be burned at the tip.
cord in a candle or lamp that draws fuel

Examples

She trimmed the wick of the candle to make it burn more evenly.
The oil lamp stopped working when its wick was too short to draw any fuel.
Always ensure the wick is upright before lighting the lantern.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fuse - a cord in an explosive device that conveys fire to the charge

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'wiecke' (candle wick).

2. NOUN

/ wɪk / (AmE), / wɪk / (BrE)
a slender piece of cord or material used to absorb and draw up liquid by capillary action, as in lamps or for draining wounds
piece of cord that conveys liquid by capillary action

Examples

The nurse inserted a wick into the wound to help it drain.
Oil slowly traveled up the lamp’s wick and fueled the flame.
A cotton wick is often used in chemistry labs to move liquid between containers.

Synset Examples

the physician put a wick in the wound to drain it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: siphon - a tube used to convey liquids by atmospheric pressure

Etymology

Probably from Middle Low German *wiek* (piece of cord in a candle).
