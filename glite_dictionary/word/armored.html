armored

1. ADJ

/ ˈɑːrmərd / (AmE), / ˈɑːməd / (BrE)
(of vehicles, persons, or objects) protected by armor, especially for military purposes.
protected by armor

Examples

The armored vehicle was able to withstand heavy gunfire during the battle.
Many soldiers wore armored vests for protection in combat zones.
Modern banks often have armored doors to secure their vaults.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unarmored - not protected by armor

Etymology

From the word 'armor' (Middle English, from Old French 'armeur', from Latin 'armatura'), plus the suffix '-ed'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈɑrmərd / (AmE), / ˈɑːməd / (BrE)
equipped with armor for protection, especially as worn by soldiers or vehicles in battle.
equipped with armor for protection

Examples

The armored knight rode into battle, glinting in the sunlight.
Medieval soldiers often wore armored suits for protection against swords and arrows.
The museum displayed a fully armored mannequin from the 15th century.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unarmored - not equipped with armor for protection

Etymology

From Old French *armer* (to equip with armor), from Latin *armare* (to arm).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01098340-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈɑːrmɚd / (AmE), / ˈɑːməd / (BrE)
(of animals) having a natural protective covering such as scales, plates, or shells, serving as armor.
having protective natural covering (for animals)

Examples

The armored armadillo rolls into a ball when threatened.
Many dinosaurs, such as the Ankylosaurus, were heavily armored to defend against predators.
The pangolin is an armored mammal covered in hard, overlapping scales.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: armoured - British spelling of 'armored'

Etymology

From Old French 'armure', from Latin 'armatura' (equipment, armor).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00148151-a
Synset POS: a
