pint

1. NOUN

/ paɪnt / (AmE), / paɪnt / (BrE)
A unit for measuring liquids in the US, equal to 16 fluid ounces or one eighth of a gallon.
US liquid measure, 16 fluid ounces

Examples

This recipe calls for one pint of milk.
I bought a pint of strawberries at the store.
A pint is equal to 16 fluid ounces in the US.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: quart - A US liquid unit equal to two pints.
  • Degree Lower: cup - A US liquid unit equal to half a pint.

Etymology

From Old French 'pinte', of uncertain origin, possibly from Vulgar Latin *pincta, meaning a painted mark for measurement.

2. NOUN

/ paɪnt / (AmE), / paɪnt / (BrE)
A unit of dry measurement in the United States equal to 0.5 quart or approximately 33.6 cubic inches.
US dry measure (0.5 quart; 33.6 in³)

Examples

A pint of fresh berries is needed for this recipe.
The farmer sold flour in one pint bags at the market.
Each container holds exactly one US dry pint of rice.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: quart - a unit of liquid or dry capacity equal to two pints
  • Degree Lower: cup - a US unit of dry or liquid measure equal to 0.5 pint

Etymology

From Old French *pinte*, of uncertain origin, possibly from Vulgar Latin *pincta* (painted, marked).

3. NOUN

/ paɪnt / (AmE), / paɪnt / (BrE)
a unit for measuring liquid or dry capacity in the British imperial system, equal to about 568 milliliters
unit of capacity (British, 568 ml)

Examples

I ordered a pint of ale at the pub.
The recipe asks for one pint of milk.
A British pint is larger than the American pint.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: quart - a unit of capacity equal to two pints
  • Degree Lower: gill - a unit of liquid measure equal to a quarter of a pint
  • Synonym: liter - metric unit of capacity, about 1.76 British pints

Etymology

From Old French *pinte*, probably from Vulgar Latin *pincta* (painted mark, measure).
