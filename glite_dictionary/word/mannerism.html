Mannerism

1. NOUN

/ ˈmænərˌɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmænərɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
a style in European art of the late 16th century, characterized by exaggerated or elongated forms and spatial incongruity, especially in painting and sculpture
16th-century European art style with elongated forms

Examples

Many paintings from the late 16th century display the exaggerated forms typical of Mannerism.
The artist was influenced by Mannerism, evident in the elongated figures in his work.
Mannerism emerged as a response to the harmonious ideals of the High Renaissance.

Synset Examples

Mannerism favors compositional tension and instability rather than the balance and clarity of earlier Renaissance painting.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Renaissance - the period of European history at the close of the Middle Ages, marked by a revival of art, literature, and learning
  • Degree Higher: Baroque - a style of European architecture, music, and art of the 17th and early 18th centuries characterized by ornate detail

Etymology

From Italian *manierismo*, from *maniera* (manner, style).
