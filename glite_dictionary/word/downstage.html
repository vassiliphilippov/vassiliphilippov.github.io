downstage

1. ADV

/ ˈdaʊnˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnˌsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
at or toward the front part of the stage, nearest to the audience
at or toward the front of a stage

Examples

The actor moved downstage to deliver his final monologue.
Please stand downstage so the audience can see you better.
During the climax, the lights focused downstage where the main characters stood.

Synset Examples

the actors moved further and further downstage

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upstage - at or toward the back of the stage

Etymology

Formed from 'down' + 'stage', reflecting the historical downward slope of theater stages toward the audience.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00265610-r
Synset POS: r

2. NOUN

/ ˈdaʊnˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnˌsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
the part of a theatre stage nearest to the audience
front part of a theatre stage

Examples

The actors moved downstage to deliver their final lines directly to the audience.
She positioned herself downstage so her face would be fully visible under the spotlight.
Please try not to block the view when you stand downstage.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upstage - the part of the stage furthest from the audience

Etymology

Formed from 'down' + 'stage', reflecting older stage designs where the front was literally lower; used in theatrical contexts since the 19th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03234229-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈdaʊnˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
(in theatre) relating to or situated at the front part of the stage, nearer to the audience.
at or near the front of a stage

Examples

The downstage lights illuminated the actors standing near the edge of the stage.
She moved downstage to deliver her monologue directly to the audience.
Only the downstage props were visible from every seat in the theater.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upstage - at or toward the back of a stage

Etymology

From 'down' + 'stage'; based on traditional raked stage where the front was lower (down) than the back.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02503545-a
Synset POS: a
