muscadine

1. NOUN

/ ˈmʌs.kəˌdaɪn / (AmE), / ˈmʌs.kəˌdaɪn / (BrE)
a variety of dark purple grape native to the southern United States, often used for making wine, jelly, and juice
a grape variety from the southern U.S.

Examples

The muscadine is prized in the South for its sweet, flavorful grapes.
They make delicious jelly from their homegrown muscadines.
This vineyard specializes in wines made from muscadine grapes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scuppernong - a greenish-bronze variety of muscadine grape

Etymology

From French *muscadin* (dandy), possibly from muscat (a type of grape).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌskəˌdaɪn / (AmE), / ˈmʌskəˌdaɪn / (BrE)
a type of grape native to the southeastern United States, known for its thick skin and use in cultivars, wine, and preserves
grape native to southeastern US

Examples

The vineyard specializes in growing muscadine grapes for both eating and winemaking.
Muscadine jelly is a favorite homemade treat in the southern United States.
She tasted the sweetness of muscadine juice at the local farmers' market.

Etymology

Probably an alteration of 'muscadel' (a kind of grape or wine), from Old French 'muscadet'.
