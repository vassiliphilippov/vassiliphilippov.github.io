pawn

1. NOUN

/ pɔn / (AmE), / pɔːn / (BrE)
an object left with a pawnbroker as security for a loan, which may be forfeited if the loan is not repaid
object deposited as collateral

Examples

He left his gold watch as a pawn at the shop when he needed money.
If you fail to repay the loan, the pawn will be sold.
She reclaimed her pawn after paying back the full amount.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pledge - something given as security for payment or performance

Etymology

From Old French 'pan' (pledge), from Medieval Latin 'pandum' (pledge).

2. NOUN

/ pɔn / (AmE), / pɔːn / (BrE)
a person who is used by others to achieve their own goals, often without regard for that person's interests or wellbeing
person used by others for their own ends

Examples

The politician was merely a pawn in a much larger game.
She refused to be anyone's pawn and made her own decisions.
In the end, he realized he had been just a pawn in their scheme.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tool - a person used by others to achieve their aims
  • Synonym: dupe - a person who is deceived or used by others
  • Antonym: agent - a person who acts on behalf of another

Etymology

Originally from Old French 'paon' and Medieval Latin 'pedon-' (foot soldier), referring to the chess piece, later used figuratively for an unimportant person used by others.

3. NOUN

/ pɔn / (AmE), / pɔːn / (BrE)
in chess, the least powerful piece, which moves only forward and captures diagonally; it can be promoted if it reaches the opponent's end of the board
chess piece with limited power, moves forward

Examples

Many beginners underestimate the strategic importance of a pawn in the opening moves.
If a pawn reaches the eighth rank, it can be promoted to a queen or another piece.
She sacrificed a pawn early in the game to gain a strong attacking position.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: queen - the most powerful chess piece
  • Degree Higher: rook - a chess piece stronger than a pawn
  • Degree Higher: bishop - a chess piece stronger than a pawn
  • Degree Higher: knight - a chess piece stronger than a pawn

Etymology

From Old French *paon* (foot soldier), from Medieval Latin *pago*, variant of *pago* (peasant, foot soldier).

4. VERB

/ pɔn / (AmE), / pɔːn / (BrE)
to leave an object with a pawnbroker as security for a loan, with the understanding that it can be redeemed upon repayment
leave as security for a loan

Examples

He had to pawn his watch to pay the rent.
She decided not to pawn her necklace, despite her financial troubles.
If you pawn a valuable item, you can often reclaim it later by repaying the loan.

Related Terms

6. VERB

/ pɔn / (AmE), / pɔːn / (BrE)
to leave a valuable item with a pawnbroker in exchange for a loan, with the option to reclaim it by repaying the loan within a specified period
temporarily sell an item for a loan

Examples

Desperate for cash, he decided to pawn his watch until payday.
She refused to pawn her grandmother's necklace, no matter how much money she needed.
After losing his job, Tom pawned several electronics to cover his bills.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to redeem - to buy back or recover an item from a pawnbroker by repaying the loan

Etymology

From Middle English *paune*, from Old French *pan* (pledge, security), from Medieval Latin *pannus* (cloth, garment used as a pledge).
