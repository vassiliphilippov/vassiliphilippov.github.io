to overlie

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈlaɪ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈlaɪ / (BrE)
(of a person or animal) to kill (another) by lying on top of them, often accidentally.
kill by lying on top of
archaic

Examples

On rare occasions, a large sow may overlie her piglets during the night.
Historical records mention tragic cases where a parent accidentally overlaid an infant.
The farmer warned about placing the lambs where the ewe might overlie them.

Synset Examples

The sow overlay her piglets

Etymology

From over- + lie, meaning to lie over; in this sense, attested since the 17th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01329572-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈlaɪ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈlaɪ / (BrE)
to lie upon or on top of something, especially in physical or geological contexts
lie on top of

Examples

Thick layers of sediment overlie much older rocks in this region.
The newer deposits overlie the fossil-rich limestone beneath.
Sheets of snow overlie the mountain peaks throughout the winter.

Synset Examples

the granite overlies the older rocks

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underlie - to lie under or beneath something

Etymology

From over- + lie, meaning to lie above.
