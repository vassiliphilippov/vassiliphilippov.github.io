bishop

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɪʃ.əp / (AmE), / ˈbɪʃ.əp / (BrE)
A senior member of the Christian clergy who is responsible for overseeing priests or ministers within a diocese, and is often regarded as a successor to the Apostles in some churches.
high-ranking Christian clergy member

Examples

The bishop delivered an inspiring sermon at the cathedral last Sunday.
She was ordained as a bishop after many years of service in the church.
The bishop oversees several parishes within his diocese.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: archbishop - a bishop of higher rank in charge of an archdiocese

Etymology

From Old English 'bisceop', from Latin 'episcopus', from Greek 'episkopos' (overseer).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɪʃ.əp / (AmE), / ˈbɪʃ.əp / (BrE)
A type of mulled port wine flavored with oranges and cloves, traditionally served hot.
mulled port wine with oranges and cloves
archaic

Examples

In Dickensian times, a steaming cup of bishop was often enjoyed during the holidays.
The old recipe for bishop calls for slow-mulling port wine with oranges, cloves, and sugar.
Warm yourself by the fire with a glass of bishop spiced to perfection.

Etymology

From the drink's deep red color, reminiscent of a bishop's robes.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɪʃəp / (AmE), / ˈbɪʃəp / (BrE)
a chess piece that moves any number of unoccupied squares diagonally
chess piece moving diagonally

Examples

She decided to develop her bishop early in the game to control the diagonal.
The white bishop captured the pawn on c4.
Losing a bishop can seriously weaken your position in chess.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rook - a chess piece that moves any number of unoccupied squares vertically or horizontally
  • Synonym: knight - a chess piece that moves in an L-shape

Etymology

From Old English *bisceop*, from Latin *episcopus* (overseer), originally referring to a church official; sense transferred to chess via the piece's original representation as a mitred figure.
