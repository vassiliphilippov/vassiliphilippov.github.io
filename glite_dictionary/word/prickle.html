prickle

1. VERB

/ ˈprɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈprɪkəl / (BrE)
to cause a stinging or tingling sensation, as if being touched by something sharp
cause a stinging or tingling sensation

Examples

A cold breeze made her skin prickle with goosebumps.
His words prickled her with embarrassment, and she blushed deeply.
The rough fabric prickled at my neck all day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tingle - to experience or cause a slight stinging or thrilling sensation

Etymology

From Middle English prickel, related to 'prick' (pointed).

3. VERB

/ ˈprɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈprɪkəl / (BrE)
to make a small hole in (something), as with a needle or thorn
make a small hole with a sharp point

Examples

The needle prickled my skin when I touched it.
She prickled the bread dough to keep it from rising unevenly.
If you prickle the sausage with a fork, fat will escape while cooking.

Synset Examples

The nurse pricked my finger to get a small blood sample

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prick - to make a small hole in something with a sharp point

Etymology

From Middle English 'priklen', from 'prikle' (a small point).

4. NOUN

/ ˈprɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈprɪkəl / (BrE)
a small, sharp-pointed projection resembling a spike, especially on the stem or leaf of a plant
small, sharp-pointed projection on plants

Examples

The rose bush was covered in prickles that scratched my hands as I trimmed it.
You can tell the difference between a true thorn and a prickle by its position on the plant.
The leaf surface had a single sharp prickle near the base.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thorn - a stiff, sharp-pointed, woody projection on a plant
  • Synonym: spine - a stiff, sharp-pointed structure on a plant or animal
  • Synonym: burr - a rough or prickly seed case or fruit

Etymology

From Middle English 'prikel', related to Old English 'pricel' (a small point or spike).

Source: WordNet
