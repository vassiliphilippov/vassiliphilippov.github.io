purgation

1. NOUN

/ pɚˈɡeɪʃən / (AmE), / pɜːˈɡeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of clearing oneself or another from blame, suspicion, or stigma
the act of clearing from blame or stigma

Examples

After years of accusations, the court's verdict brought complete purgation of his reputation.
The ritual was designed as a symbolic purgation of past wrongdoings.
Public confession served as a form of purgation for the leaders of the community.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exoneration - the act of officially absolving someone from blame or fault

Etymology

From Latin purgatio (a cleansing, purifying, clearing away), from purgare (to cleanse).

2. NOUN

/ pɚˈɡeɪʃən / (AmE), / pɜːˈɡeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of cleansing or purifying, especially by a ceremonial or ritual act to remove defilement or uncleanness
ceremonial cleansing or purification

Examples

The priest led a solemn purgation to cleanse the temple after the festival.
According to ancient custom, the purgation of the community involved several sacred rites.
Each year, a ritual purgation is performed to ward off evil spirits and ensure harmony.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: purification - the act of making something pure, often by removing dirt or contaminants

Etymology

From Latin 'purgatio' (cleansing, purification), from 'purgare' (to cleanse).

3. NOUN

/ pɝːˈɡeɪʃən / (AmE), / pɜːˈɡeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of cleansing the body, especially by using a cathartic to stimulate evacuation of the bowels
cleansing the body, especially bowels
medical

Examples

After the operation, the doctor prescribed a mild laxative to induce purgation.
In earlier times, purgation was often used as a remedy for various illnesses.
The patient experienced relief following the purgation of his bowels.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: catharsis - the process of releasing strong emotions or purging the body
  • Antonym: constipation - condition in which evacuation of the bowels is infrequent or difficult

Etymology

From Latin 'purgatio' (a cleansing), from 'purgare' (to cleanse).
