salvage

1. NOUN

/ ˈsæl.vɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈsæl.vɪdʒ / (BrE)
the act of saving a ship, its crew, or its cargo from danger such as shipwreck or fire
rescue of ship, crew, or cargo from danger

Examples

The salvage of the sunken ship’s treasure took several weeks to complete.
They received a reward for their salvage efforts after rescuing the crew from the burning vessel.
During the storm, the coast guard organized a salvage operation to recover the cargo.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rescue - the act of saving someone or something from danger

Etymology

From Old French *salvage* (act of saving), from Latin *salvāre* (to save).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsælvɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈsal.vɪdʒ / (BrE)
the act of saving goods or property from damage or destruction, especially from the sea, fire, or other disasters
the act of rescuing goods from danger

Examples

The crew managed the salvage of valuable cargo from the sinking ship.
Firefighters coordinated the salvage of documents from the burning office building.
The insurance company discussed the terms of salvage after the flood.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rescue - the act of saving someone or something from danger

Etymology

From Old French *salvage*, from Late Latin *salvaticum*, from Latin *salvus* (safe).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsæl.vɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈsæl.vɪdʒ / (BrE)
property or goods that have been saved from damage, especially from a shipwreck, fire, or disaster
property saved from disaster or damage

Examples

After the fire, only a few items of salvage could be recovered from the ruins.
The ship's salvage was carefully inventoried by the insurance company.
Much of the salvage from the wreck was auctioned off to collectors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wreckage - debris or remains of something destroyed
  • Antonym: loss - property or goods that have been destroyed or cannot be recovered

Etymology

From Old French 'salvage' (act of saving), from Late Latin 'salvaticum', from Latin 'salvus' (safe).
