deictic

1. NOUN

/ daɪkˈtɪk / (AmE), / daɪkˈtɪk / (BrE)
A word that specifies identity, place, or time relative to the position or perspective of the speaker or hearer in the context of communication.
word specifying identity or location in context

Examples

Words like 'here', 'that', and 'now' are classic examples of deictics in English.
In the sentence 'I am leaving now,' the word 'now' functions as a deictic.
Linguists study how deictics shape the meaning of utterances depending on context.

Synset Examples

words that introduce particulars of the speaker's and hearer's shared cognitive field into the message

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pronoun - a word that can replace a noun and whose meaning varies depending on context
  • Synonym: demonstrative - a word like 'this' or 'that' used to indicate specific things

Etymology

From Greek deiktikos (able to show), from deiknunai (to show, to point out).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdaɪktɪk / (AmE), / ˈdaɪktɪk / (BrE)
(of a word or expression) referring to or dependent on the context in which it is used, such as time, place, or speaker-related terms (e.g., 'here', 'now', 'I').
context-dependent in meaning (linguistics)

Examples

The word 'this' is a classic deictic term, as its meaning shifts with context.
She explained the difference between deictic and non-deictic expressions in her linguistics class.
'Now' and 'there' are both deictic words whose reference depends on who is speaking and when.

Synset Examples

deictic pronouns

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indexical - (of a word or expression) indicating or pointing to something by virtue of context; similar technical term in linguistics

Etymology

From Greek deiktikos (able to show), from deiknynai (to show).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03147071-a
Synset POS: a
