lysogeny

1. NOUN

/ laɪˈsɑdʒəni / (AmE), / laɪˈsɒdʒəni / (BrE)
the condition in which the genetic material of a bacteriophage is incorporated into the genome of a host bacterium and remains dormant until triggered to enter a lytic cycle
incorporation of a phage into bacterial DNA
medical

Examples

Lysogeny allows certain viruses to hide within bacterial chromosomes for many cell generations.
The study of lysogeny has provided great insight into virus-host interactions.
Environmental stress can sometimes trigger a switch from lysogeny to the lytic cycle.

Synset Examples

when a phage infects a bacterium it can either destroy its host or be incorporated in the host genome in a state of lysogeny

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lysis - the disintegration of a cell by rupture of the cell wall or membrane
  • Antonym: lytic cycle - the process by which a virus destroys the host cell

Etymology

From Greek 'lysis' (loosening, dissolution) + '-geny' (mode of production or origin).
