to invest

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛst / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛst / (BrE)
(formal) To endow a person or thing with a particular quality, ability, or attribute.
endow with a quality or ability (formal)

Examples

The constitution invests the president with the power to veto legislation.
She was invested with great authority upon her appointment to the role.
Tradition invests the ceremony with deep symbolic meaning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endow - to provide with a quality, ability, or asset

Etymology

From Latin investire (to clothe, dress), later meaning to endow with power or quality.

formally grant authority or power

The board voted to invest the committee with the necessary authority to make decisions.

Related Terms

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛst / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛst / (BrE)
to formally install someone in an office or position, often with a ceremony or ritual
formally install in office or position

Examples

The board will invest her as the new CEO during a formal ceremony next week.
Traditionally, the monarch invests new knights in an elaborate ritual at the palace.
He was invested with the powers of the presidency after taking the oath of office.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to install - place someone formally in an office or position

Etymology

From Latin investire, meaning 'to clothe, dress', later 'to endow with authority'.

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛst / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛst / (BrE)
to put money, time, or effort into something, especially financial assets, with the expectation of achieving a profit or benefit
put money or effort into for profit or gain

Examples

Many people invest in the stock market to build wealth over time.
She decided to invest her savings in a new business venture.
Banks often invest heavily in government bonds for stability.

Synset Examples

Put money into bonds

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: to speculate - to engage in risky financial transactions expecting significant returns
  • Degree Lower: save - to keep money aside instead of spending it

Etymology

From Latin investīre ('to clothe'), later meaning to endow with power or property, developing to its modern financial sense.

5. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛst / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛst / (BrE)
to formally give someone power, authority, or office, especially in a ceremony; to endow with rights or rank
formally give power or authority

Examples

The constitution will invest the new president with sweeping executive powers.
The monarch was invested with full authority in a grand coronation ceremony.
Ancient rituals were performed to invest the emperor with divine right.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to divest - to deprive someone of power, rights, or possessions

Etymology

From Latin investire (to clothe in, invest with authority), from in- (in) + vestire (to clothe).
