displacement

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
the act of removing someone from a position, office, or employment
removal from office or employment

Examples

His sudden displacement from the company shocked the entire staff.
The displacement of the director was met with both relief and anger among employees.
Union leaders protested the unfair displacement of workers during the corporate restructuring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dismissal - the act of removing someone from a job or position
  • Antonym: appointment - the act of assigning someone to a job or position

Etymology

From Old French *desplacement*, from *desplacer* (to displace).

3. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
In psychiatry, a defense mechanism by which an emotional response or impulse is redirected from its original object to a safer or more acceptable substitute.
psychiatric defense mechanism
medical

Examples

She used displacement to cope with her anger at her boss by becoming frustrated with her family instead.
In psychotherapy, displacement may be recognized as a way patients avoid confronting difficult emotions directly.
The child's sudden aggression toward his toys was viewed as displacement of anxiety about starting school.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: projection - A defense mechanism involving attributing one's own feelings to another person

Etymology

From French *déplacement*, from déplacer (to move), based on Latin dis- (apart) + placēre (to place).

5. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
(Chemistry) A reaction in which an element replaces another element in a compound, setting free the replaced element.
a reaction where one element replaces another in a compound (chemistry)
medical

Examples

A classic example of displacement is when zinc reacts with copper sulfate, displacing copper from the solution.
The displacement of hydrogen from acids by metals is a common topic in chemistry classes.
In a displacement reaction, a more reactive element pushes out a less reactive one from its compound.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: redox - a type of chemical reaction involving reduction and oxidation

Etymology

From displace + -ment; 'displace' from Old French 'desplacer'.

6. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
(Physics) The movement of something from its place or position, especially the distance moved in a particular direction, measured as a straight line from the starting point to the end point.
distance moved from original position (physics)

Examples

The displacement of the car was measured as 100 meters east from its original position.
In physics, displacement is different from distance because it has both magnitude and direction.
Her calculations showed the displacement of the particle after five seconds of motion.

7. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
the act of moving something from its natural or original environment, often resulting in its removal or relocation
removal from natural environment

Examples

The construction of the dam led to the displacement of several animal species from their habitats.
Surging river levels can cause the displacement of soil and nutrients downstream.
Industrial pollution often results in the displacement of plants from their natural ecosystems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relocation - the act of moving something or someone to a new place

Etymology

From Old French *desplacer* (replace, remove), from Latin *dis-* (apart) + *placere* (to place, please) + English suffix *-ment*.
