mocking

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɑkɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈmɒkɪŋ / (BrE)
Making fun of someone or something in a cruel or contemptuous way; showing ridicule or scorn.
showing ridicule or contempt

Examples

She gave him a mocking smile as she walked by.
His mocking tone made it clear he didn't take the idea seriously.
The students burst into mocking laughter during the awkward presentation.

Synset Examples

derisive laughter
a jeering crowd
her mocking smile
taunting shouts of ‘coward’ and ‘sissy’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: derisive - expressing contempt or ridicule
  • Synonym: taunting - intended to provoke or mock someone
  • Antonym: respectful - showing respect or deference

Etymology

Present participle of the verb 'mock', from Old French 'moquer' (to mock or make fun of).

2. ADJ

/ ˈmɑkɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈmɒkɪŋ / (BrE)
Making fun of someone or something in a way that ridicules or scorns, often playfully or sarcastically.
ridiculing; making fun of

Examples

Her mocking tone made it clear she did not take him seriously.
He gave a mocking smile and turned away without answering.
The students burst into mocking laughter when the teacher made a mistake.

Synset Examples

his face wore a somewhat quizzical almost impertinent air

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridiculing - making fun of someone or something
  • Antonym: respectful - showing respect or deference

Etymology

From Middle English mokken, related to Old French mocquer (to mock).
