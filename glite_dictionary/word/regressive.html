regressive

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈɡrɛsɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛsɪv / (BrE)
Opposing progress or tending to return to a former, less advanced state or condition.
opposing progress; returning to a former state

Examples

The new policy was criticized as regressive and detrimental to social advancement.
Some tax systems are considered regressive because they disproportionately affect those with lower incomes.
The movement's regressive ideals sparked debate among advocates of modernization.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: progressive - favoring or implementing progress, reform, or innovation

Etymology

From Latin 'regressus' (a return), from 'regredi' (to go back).

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈɡrɛsɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛsɪv / (BrE)
(of taxes or taxation) decreasing in rate as the amount subject to taxation increases, so that those with lower incomes pay a higher proportion of their income than those with higher incomes.
(of tax) decreasing rate as income rises

Examples

Many economists criticize regressive tax systems for placing a heavier burden on low-income individuals.
Sales taxes are often considered regressive because they take a larger percentage of income from the poor than from the rich.
The government debated whether a regressive tax was fair to all citizens.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: progressive - (of taxes) increasing in rate as taxable amount increases

Etymology

From Latin regredi (to go back), via regressivus (going back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01884018-a
Synset POS: a
