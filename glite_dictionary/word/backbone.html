backbone

1. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌboʊn / (AmE), / ˈbæk.bəʊn / (BrE)
the column of bones (vertebrae) that runs down the center of the back in vertebrates, supporting the body and protecting the spinal cord
the spinal column in vertebrate animals

Examples

The human backbone is made up of 33 vertebrae.
A strong backbone helps support good posture throughout life.
Injury to the backbone can result in serious mobility issues.

Synset Examples

the fall broke his back

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spine - the backbone; the series of vertebrae in the body
  • Synonym: vertebral column - the series of vertebrae forming the backbone

2. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌboʊn / (AmE), / ˈbæk.bəʊn / (BrE)
the narrow part of a book's cover that encloses the inner side of the pages and is visible when the book is shelved, often bearing the title and author
part of a book's cover facing outward on shelf

Examples

The title of the novel was boldly printed on the backbone of the book.
Some older books have a leather backbone that shows signs of wear.
Librarians often label the backbone to help identify the book easily on the shelf.

Synset Examples

the title and author were printed on the spine of the book

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spine - the part of a book's binding that covers the inner edge of the pages

3. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌboʊn / (AmE), / ˈbækbəʊn / (BrE)
the main part of a network, such as the internet, that connects different, smaller networks and carries the most important traffic.
main connecting part of a network

Examples

All offices are connected via the company's backbone, which links regional networks together.
Upgrading the internet backbone improves bandwidth and reduces latency for all users.
The new fiber-optic backbone can handle much more data than the old copper wires.

Synset Examples

the backbone is the part of a communication network that carries the heaviest traffic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: core network - the central network infrastructure of a telecommunications system
  • Antonym: edge network - the peripheral parts of a network, connected by the backbone

Etymology

Originally from anatomy, denoting the spine; metaphorically used for central components since the 19th century.

4. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌboʊn / (AmE), / ˈbækbəʊn / (BrE)
strength of character; courage and resolve to face difficulties
strength of character; determination

Examples

It takes real backbone to start your own business and never give up.
She finally showed some backbone and stood up to the bully at work.
Without the backbone to make tough decisions, the team quickly fell apart.

Synset Examples

he didn't have the guts to try it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guts - courage or determination
  • Synonym: spine - backbone as courage or fortitude
  • Antonym: cowardice - lack of courage or determination

Etymology

From Old English 'bæc' (back) + 'bān' (bone), originally referring to the spinal column; figurative meaning from the idea of the backbone providing support.

main support or strength

Examples

Her managerial skills were the backbone of the project's success.
Small businesses form the backbone of the local economy.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mainstay - chief support or most important element
  • Synonym: linchpin - an essential element that holds things together
  • Synonym: keystone - the central, most important part of something

Etymology

From back + bone, originally referring to the spine, later used figuratively for support or strength.
