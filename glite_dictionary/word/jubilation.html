jubilation

1. NOUN

/ ˌdʒuːbəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdʒuːbɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
A feeling or expression of great joy and triumph.
great joy or celebration

Examples

The crowd erupted in jubilation when the final whistle blew.
After hearing the good news, she was overcome with jubilation.
There was a sense of jubilation throughout the city as the parade marched by.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elation - a feeling of great joy or pride
  • Antonym: grief - deep sorrow or sadness

Etymology

From Latin jubilationem (shouting for joy), from jubilare (to rejoice).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdʒuːbəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdʒuːbɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the expression of great happiness or joy, often shown by cheerful shouting or singing
expression of great joy

Examples

The stadium erupted in jubilation when the home team scored the winning goal.
Songs of jubilation could be heard throughout the neighborhood during the festival.
Her announcement was met with cries of jubilation from her friends and family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rejoicing - the action of expressing great happiness
  • Synonym: elation - a feeling of great joy or pride
  • Antonym: sorrow - a feeling of deep distress caused by loss or disappointment

Etymology

From Latin 'iūbilātiō' (shouting for joy), from 'iūbilāre' (to shout for joy).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdʒuːbəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdʒuːbɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
A feeling or expression of great joy and celebration, especially on a special occasion.
great joy; festive celebration

Examples

The streets were filled with jubilation after the team's unexpected victory.
There was a sense of jubilation in the air during the festival.
Her announcement was greeted with jubilation by friends and family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: celebration - the action of marking one's pleasure at an important event or occasion
  • Antonym: sorrow - a feeling of deep distress caused by loss, disappointment, or other misfortune

Etymology

From Latin 'iūbilātiō', from 'iūbilātus' (to shout for joy).
