tottering

1. ADJ

/ ˈtɑːt̬.ɚ.ɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈtɒt.ər.ɪŋ / (BrE)
moving in an unsteady way, especially because of weakness, infirmity, or old age.
moving unsteadily due to weakness

Examples

The old bridge looked dangerously tottering after the storm.
She gave her tottering grandmother a steady arm to lean on.
The tottering pile of books finally collapsed onto the floor.

Synset Examples

a tottering skeleton of a horse
a tottery old man

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unsteady - not steady; shaky or insecure
  • Antonym: stable - not likely to change or fail; firmly established

Etymology

From the verb 'totter', of uncertain origin, possibly imitative of unsteady movement.

2. ADJ

/ ˈtɑːtərɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈtɒtərɪŋ / (BrE)
(of structures or institutions) unstable and likely to collapse; failing or near collapse.
unstable, likely to collapse

Examples

The old manor house stood tottering on the hill, its foundation weakened by years of neglect.
Many believed the government was tottering and close to being overthrown by popular revolt.
The company’s financial problems left its future tottering in an uncertain economy.

Synset Examples

a tottering empire

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unstable - not stable; easily shaken or likely to collapse
  • Antonym: secure - firm or stable; not likely to fail or collapse

Etymology

Present participle of 'totter', from Middle English 'toteren'.
