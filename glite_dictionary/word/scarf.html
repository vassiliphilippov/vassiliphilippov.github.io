scarf

1. VERB

/ skɑrf / (AmE), / skɑːf / (BrE)
to join two pieces of wood or metal together with a scarf joint, especially so as to make them appear as one continuous piece
join pieces with a scarf joint

Examples

The carpenter skillfully scarfed the two beams together to form a longer support.
To repair the damaged section, they scarfed new timber onto the existing framework.
Shipbuilders often scarf planks to ensure a smoother hull surface.

Etymology

From Old Norse *skarfr* (end piece of a plank).

4. NOUN

/ skɑːrf / (AmE), / skɑːf / (BrE)
A long piece of fabric worn around the head, neck, or shoulders for warmth, protection, or decoration.
piece of fabric worn around neck/head

Examples

She wrapped a red scarf around her neck to keep warm.
At the party, his silk scarf added a dash of elegance to his outfit.
During winter, children often lose their scarves while playing outside.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shawl - a piece of fabric worn over the shoulders or head, often larger and not wrapped as tightly

Etymology

From Old Northern French *escarpe*, meaning 'sash' or 'scarf'.

5. NOUN

/ skɑrf / (AmE), / skɑːf / (BrE)
A joint formed by notching the ends of two pieces of wood or metal so that they lock together end-to-end.
joint where wood or metal pieces lock end-to-end

Examples

The beams were joined with a scarf to make them long enough for the roof.
A well-made scarf ensures the timber sections are securely joined.
Engineers used a metal scarf to connect the structural girders.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: splice - joining two pieces together, especially by overlapping

Etymology

From Old Norse 'skarf' (notch, joint).
