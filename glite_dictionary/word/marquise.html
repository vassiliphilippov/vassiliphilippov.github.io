marquise

1. NOUN

/ mɑrˈkiz / (AmE), / mɑːˈkiːz / (BrE)
A noblewoman of high rank, ranking below a duchess and above a countess.
noblewoman below a duchess

Examples

The marquise greeted her guests with great elegance during the grand ball.
Several portraits of the marquise hang in the family château.
As a marquise, she played an influential role in the local court.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: duchess - a woman holding the rank of duke or the wife of a duke
  • Degree Lower: countess - a woman holding the rank of count or the wife of a count

Etymology

From French 'marquise', feminine of 'marquis', from Old French 'marchis' (march-warder, ruler of a border area).

2. NOUN

/ mɑrˈkiz / (AmE), / mɑːˈkiːz / (BrE)
a permanent canopy or structure projecting over the entrance of a building, especially a hotel or theater, providing shelter and often bearing signage
permanent canopy over a building entrance

Examples

The hotel's grand entrance was sheltered by a glass marquise that sparkled at night.
Guests gathered under the marquise to escape the sudden rain before entering the theater.
The vintage cinema still has the original marquise displaying movie titles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marquee - a large tent or a projecting sign over a theater entrance

Etymology

From French 'marquise' (marquess), possibly referring to the grandeur or elegance associated with such canopies.
