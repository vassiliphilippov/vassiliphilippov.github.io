eristic

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈrɪstɪk / (AmE), / ɪˈrɪstɪk / (BrE)
a person who argues or disputes, often for the sake of argument rather than reaching the truth
a person who enjoys argument or controversy

Examples

The philosopher was an eristic who preferred endless debate to clear answers.
Some television hosts act as eristics, provoking controversies for higher ratings.
It is difficult to reach consensus when an eristic dominates the discussion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: debater - someone who participates in a formal argument or debate
  • Antonym: peacemaker - a person who tries to bring about peace or reconciliation

Etymology

From Greek eristikos (fond of wrangling), from eris (strife).

2. ADJ

/ ɛˈrɪstɪk / (AmE), / ɪˈrɪstɪk / (BrE)
Given to arguing or disputing for its own sake, often using specious or deceptive reasoning.
disputatious; inclined to argue for argument's sake

Examples

The philosopher's eristic style made debates lively but sometimes unproductive.
Her eristic arguments annoyed her peers, as she seemed to argue only to win, not to find the truth.
The forum was dominated by an eristic approach, with participants focused on scoring points rather than resolving issues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: argumentative - tending to argue or dispute

Etymology

From Greek eristikos (fond of wrangling), from erizein (to wrangle, quarrel).

3. NOUN

/ ɛˈrɪstɪk / (AmE), / ɪˈrɪstɪk / (BrE)
the art or practice of logical argumentation or disputation, especially when aimed at winning rather than seeking the truth
argumentative disputation, often specious

Examples

The debate descended into mere eristic, with both sides focusing on winning rather than on truth.
His style of eristic was criticized as being more about verbal victory than understanding.
Some scholars value eristic as a means of sharpening their argumentation skills.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disputation - debate or argument, especially formal or academic

Etymology

From Greek eristikos (fond of wrangling), from eris (strife).
