to verbalise

1. VERB

/ ˈvɜrbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvɜːbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to convert a word, especially a noun, into a verb
convert a word into a verb

Examples

Linguists often verbalise nouns to create new action words in English.
The process to verbalise certain adjectives can be complex in some languages.
Teachers explained how writers frequently verbalise objects to make their writing more dynamic.

Synset Examples

many English nouns have become verbalized

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominalise - convert into a noun

Etymology

From 'verb' + '-alise', based on Latin 'verbum' (word).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00116011-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈvɜrbəlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvɜːbəlaɪz / (BrE)
To express oneself in an overly wordy or verbose manner.
speak or write at excessive length
british

Examples

The politician tends to verbalise at every meeting, often losing the audience's attention.
Please don't verbalise the report; just stick to the key points.
She sometimes verbalises when nervous, adding unnecessary detail to her explanations.

Synset Examples

This lawyer verbalizes and is rather tedious

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ramble - to speak or write at length in a confused or inconsequential way
  • Antonym: be concise - to express briefly and clearly, without unnecessary words

Etymology

From verb 'verbal' + suffix '-ise', parallel to 'verbalize' in American English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00945313-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈvɜrbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvɜːbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to express (an idea, feeling, or thought) in words; to put into words
express in words

Examples

She found it difficult to verbalise her feelings after the accident.
Children often verbalise their frustrations in unexpected ways.
In therapy, patients are encouraged to verbalise their thoughts and emotions.

Synset Examples

She expressed her anger
He uttered a curse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: articulate - to express (something) clearly and effectively in speech or writing
  • Synonym: express - to convey (a thought or feeling) in words or by gestures and conduct
  • Antonym: to suppress - to prevent something from being expressed or known

Etymology

From 'verbal' + '-ise'; ultimately from Latin 'verbum' (word).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00942415-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈvɜrbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvɜːbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to express thoughts, feelings, or ideas in words, especially by speaking
express in words, especially by speaking
british

Examples

It can be difficult for children to verbalise their emotions.
She was unable to verbalise what she was feeling at that moment.
Therapists encourage patients to verbalise their thoughts openly during sessions.

Synset Examples

She talks a lot of nonsense
This depressed patient does not verbalize

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to speak - to utter words or articulate sounds with the ordinary voice
  • Synonym: articulate - express (an idea or feeling) fluently and coherently
  • Synonym: to verbalize - American English spelling of 'verbalise'

Etymology

From verb + -alise; compare the American English 'verbalize'.
