Gaul

1. NOUN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
a member of the Celtic people who lived in the region of ancient Gaul (roughly present-day France and parts of neighboring countries) before and during Roman times
member of an ancient Celtic people of Gaul

Examples

The Gaul fought bravely against the invading Roman army.
As a Gaul, he spoke a Celtic language unknown to the Romans.
Historians study the culture of the ancient Gaul to understand early European societies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Celt - member of an ancient European people, related ethnically and linguistically to the Gauls

Etymology

From Latin Gallus (a Gaul), referring to a people of ancient Europe.

2. PROPN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
an ancient region in western Europe encompassing present-day France, Belgium, northern Italy, and parts of Germany and the Netherlands
ancient region in western Europe

Examples

Julius Caesar famously conquered Gaul during the Gallic Wars.
The tribes of Gaul were known for their fierce resistance to Roman rule.
Many present-day French customs can trace their origins back to Gaul.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Gallia - Latin name for Gaul, the ancient region

Etymology

From Latin Gallia, the Roman name for the region.

3. NOUN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
a person from ancient Gaul or, more generally, of French descent
a person from ancient Gaul or France

Examples

Julius Caesar famously led his army against the Gauls during his campaigns in ancient Europe.
She discovered that her ancestors were Gauls, tracing her family back to pre-Roman France.
Historians debate the exact boundaries of the land inhabited by the Gauls in classical times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Frenchman - a person from France or of French descent

Etymology

From Latin Gallus (inhabitant of Gaul), from Gallia (Gaul).
