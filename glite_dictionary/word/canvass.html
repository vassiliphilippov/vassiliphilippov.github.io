canvass

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.vəs / (AmE), / ˈkæn.vəs / (BrE)
an instance of trying to discover public opinion on a particular subject, especially by interviewing a sample of people
survey of public opinion

Examples

The government ordered a canvass to gauge the public's reaction to the new policy.
A canvass of local residents revealed widespread support for building the new community center.
The results of the canvass helped the campaign refine their strategy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poll - an inquiry into public opinion by questioning a random sample

Etymology

From old French 'convoisier' (to go about, solicit), later influenced by the verb 'canvass'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkæn.vəs / (AmE), / ˈkæn.vəs / (BrE)
to try to persuade people to vote for a particular candidate or party in an election, typically by going door to door or making direct contact
solicit votes in an election

Examples

Volunteers will canvass the neighborhood this weekend to encourage people to vote.
Party members canvassed door to door to gain support for their candidate.
They spent hours canvassing potential voters in the district before election day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to solicit - to ask for something, such as votes, help, or information
  • Synonym: campaign - to work in an organized and active way toward a political goal, often during an election

Etymology

From obsolete canvas (to examine closely), probably from canvas (the fabric), metaphorically referencing examination by tossing items on canvas for inspection.

3. VERB

/ ˈkæn.vəs / (AmE), / ˈkæn.vəs / (BrE)
to ask people for their views, votes, or support, especially by making systematic inquiries or visiting people.
to ask people's opinions or support

Examples

Volunteers were sent out to canvass local residents about the new park.
They hired a team to canvass voters ahead of the election.
After canvassing the employees, management decided to change the company's vacation policy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to solicit - to ask for something, such as information or support

Etymology

From Old French 'canvasser', originally meaning to toss in a canvas sheet, hence figuratively to sift or discuss thoroughly.

4. VERB

/ ˈkæn.vəs / (AmE), / ˈkæn.vəs / (BrE)
to examine or discuss something in detail, especially in order to discover its essential features or meaning
examine or discuss in detail

Examples

Literary critics often canvass different interpretations of a challenging poem.
We should canvass the pros and cons of each proposal thoroughly before proceeding.

5. VERB

/ ˈkæn.vəs / (AmE), / ˈkæn.vəs / (BrE)
to try to persuade people to support a political party, candidate, or cause, especially by visiting them to ask for their vote or opinion
seek support or votes, especially politically

Examples

During the election, volunteers canvassed neighborhoods to encourage people to vote.
He spent the afternoon canvassing for support among local residents.
Several politicians canvassed the crowd, hoping to win more votes.

Synset Examples

To win the vote, she decided to canvass every house.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to solicit - to ask for something, such as votes or support
  • Synonym: campaign - to work in an organized way towards a particular goal, especially in politics

Etymology

From early modern English 'canvass', originally meaning 'to toss in a canvas sheet' (a form of punishment or sport), later developing the sense of 'examine carefully' and then 'solicit votes'.
