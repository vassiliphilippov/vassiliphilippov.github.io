trickster

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪk.stɚ / (AmE), / ˈtrɪk.stə / (BrE)
a person who deceives people or plays practical jokes on others, often for amusement or advantage
someone who plays tricks or pranks

Examples

The carnival was full of tricksters pulling pranks on unsuspecting visitors.
In many folktales, the trickster outwits his enemies using clever schemes.
He always saw himself as a harmless trickster, making his friends laugh with his antics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prankster - a person who plays practical jokes
  • Synonym: deceiver - a person who causes someone to believe something untrue
  • Antonym: honest person - a person who is truthful and does not deceive others

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪkstɚ / (AmE), / ˈtrɪkstə / (BrE)
a person who deceives or cheats people, often by making them believe something that is not true
a deceptive or cheating person

Examples

The trickster convinced the villagers that he could turn stones into gold.
In many fairy tales, a clever trickster outwits the more powerful characters.
She warned her son not to trust any unknown trickster who made big promises.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cheat - a person who acts dishonestly to gain an advantage
  • Synonym: swindler - a person who uses deception to deprive someone of money or possessions
  • Antonym: honest person - a person who tells the truth and does not deceive others

Etymology

From 'trick' + '-ster' (agent suffix).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪkstɚ / (AmE), / ˈtrɪkstə / (BrE)
a mischievous supernatural figure, often appearing in folklore, who uses cunning or deception to achieve aims, sometimes marked by exaggerated features or appetites
mischievous supernatural figure in folklore

Examples

The trickster in many myths uses cleverness to outwit gods or humans.
In Native American tradition, the coyote is often portrayed as a trickster.
The story features a trickster who causes mischief among the villagers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deceiver - someone who deceives or tricks others
  • Antonym: hero - the principal character in a story, usually admired for courage or noble qualities

Etymology

From 'trick' + '-ster', patterned after agentive nouns in English.
