crenel

1. NOUN

/ ˈkrɛnəl / (AmE), / ˈkrɛnəl / (BrE)
a notch or open space between two raised sections (merlons) in a battlement, used historically for defense and observation
notch in a battlement

Examples

The archers took aim through the crenel of the castle wall.
Each crenel provided a narrow opening for defenders to launch arrows at attackers.
During the tour, the guide explained how soldiers used every crenel for both attack and defense.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: merlon - the solid upright section between two crenels in a battlement

Etymology

From Old French 'crenel', diminutive of 'cran' (notch, gap).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkrɛnəl / (AmE), / ˈkrɛnəl / (BrE)
a rounded or square indentation or notch along the edge of something, especially one of a series such as those found on a leaf, shell margin, or the surface of certain cells
notch or indentation along an edge

Examples

Each leaf had a delicate crenel at the edge, giving it a scalloped appearance.
The biologist examined the red blood cell's crenel structure under the microscope.
A lace border often features a repeating crenel pattern for decorative effect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crenation - the rounded projection between notches, especially along a leaf or cell margin

Etymology

From Old French *crenel* (notch, loophole), diminutive of *cran* (notch, gap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13897197-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈkrɛn.əl / (AmE), / ˈkrɛn.əl / (BrE)
to provide a wall or building with battlements or crenellations
to supply with battlements

Examples

The castle walls were creneled during the renovations to enhance their medieval appearance.
Architects decided to crenel the top of the tower for decorative effect.
They spent months carefully creneling the fortifications to recreate the historical style.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to crenellate - to provide a building with battlements

Etymology

From Old French *crenel* (notch in a wall, battlement), diminutive of *cren* (notch).
