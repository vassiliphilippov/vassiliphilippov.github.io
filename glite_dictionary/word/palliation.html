palliation

1. NOUN

/ ˌpæliˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpalɪˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of making a wrongdoing or offense appear less serious or severe than it is
reducing the apparent severity of an offense

Examples

His speech was a palliation of the manager's mistakes, making them seem like minor oversights.
The defense attorney argued in palliation of her client's actions, emphasizing his difficult upbringing.
Attempts at palliation only made the public trust in the company decline further.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mitigation - the act of making something less severe, serious, or painful

Etymology

From Medieval Latin palliatio(n-), from 'palliare' (to cloak, cover).

2. NOUN

/ ˌpæliˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpalɪˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of reducing the severity or intensity of pain or a disease without eliminating its cause
relief of symptoms without cure
medical

Examples

The doctor focused on palliation to make the last months as comfortable as possible.
In many chronic illnesses, palliation is as important as searching for a cure.
Effective palliation improved the patient’s quality of life despite the progression of the disease.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relief - alleviation of pain or distress
  • Synonym: alleviation - making suffering less severe
  • Antonym: cure - a complete solution to a disease or problem

Etymology

From Latin 'palliatus' (cloaked, covered), from 'palliare' (to cloak).
