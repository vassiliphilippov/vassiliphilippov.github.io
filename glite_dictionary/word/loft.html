loft

1. VERB

/ lɔft / (AmE), / lɒft / (BrE)
to create a full-scale drawing of the hull lines of a vessel, typically used in shipbuilding.
draw hull lines full-scale (shipbuilding)

Examples

The shipwright spent weeks carefully lofting the lines to ensure the hull was symmetrical.
Before construction began, the team lofted the design onto the workshop floor.
It's essential to loft the hull accurately for a vessel to perform well at sea.

3. NOUN

/ lɑft / (AmE), / lɒft / (BrE)
(in golf) the backward slant of the face of a golf club that determines how high the ball will rise when struck.
backward slant of a golf club's face

Examples

The driver has less loft than the wedge, making the ball travel farther but lower.
He prefers a club with more loft for shorter, higher shots onto the green.
Adjusting the loft of your club can help you control the ball's trajectory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lie - the angle between the shaft and the ground when the club is in a normal playing position (golf)
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05076616-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ lɔft / (AmE), / lɒft / (BrE)
an upper floor in a building, especially one above a factory or warehouse, usually open and without interior partitions
large open upper floor in a building

Examples

The artist’s loft in the old warehouse was full of natural light and open space.
They converted the top floor into a spacious loft that served as both a studio and an apartment.
Many modern lofts are desired for their high ceilings and industrial character.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attic - a room or space directly under the roof of a house

Etymology

From Old Norse 'lopt' (air, upper room, loft).
