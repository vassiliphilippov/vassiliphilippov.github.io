amiss

1. ADV

/ əˈmɪs / (AmE), / əˈmɪs / (BrE)
in a mistaken or incorrect way; not as it should be
in an incorrect or wrong way

Examples

When we noticed that something felt amiss, we decided to review the project plan.
If anything goes amiss during the ceremony, let me know immediately.
She sensed that all was amiss even before the problems became obvious.

Synset Examples

something has gone awry in our plans
something went badly amiss in the preparations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: awry - away from the usual or expected course; wrong
  • Antonym: rightly - in a correct or proper way

Etymology

From Middle English 'amis', from Old Norse 'amisso' (wrongly), originally from 'miss' (failure, fault).

2. ADV

/ əˈmɪs / (AmE), / əˈmɪs / (BrE)
In a mistaken, improper, or unfortunate way; wrongly or incorrectly.
wrongly, improperly, or unfavorably

Examples

If you interpret her silence as anger, you judge amiss.
No one took it amiss when he arrived late to the meeting.
I hope my honest feedback is not taken amiss.

Synset Examples

if you think him guilty, you judge amiss
he spoke amiss
no one took it amiss when she spoke frankly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wrongly - in an incorrect or mistaken manner
  • Antonym: correctly - in an accurate or correct manner

Etymology

From Middle English 'amis', from Old French 'en mes' (at fault, in error), from Latin 'missus' (sent).

/ əˈmɪs / (AmE), / əˈmɪs / (BrE)
imperfectly or wrongly; not quite right or as it should be
wrongly; imperfectly; not as it should be

Examples

She felt that something was amiss in the conversation, though she couldn't say what.
The machine was working amiss after the lightning strike.
If you spellcheck your paper, nothing should be amiss with the text.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wrongly - in an incorrect or mistaken way
  • Antonym: correctly - in a manner that is right or proper

Etymology

From Old Norse 'amis', from 'a-' (on) + 'miss' (failure, fault).
