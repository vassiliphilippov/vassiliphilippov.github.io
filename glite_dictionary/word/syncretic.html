syncretic

1. ADJ

/ sɪnˈkrɛtɪk / (AmE), / sɪŋˈkrɛtɪk / (BrE)
(of a language) characterized by the reduction or merging of inflections, often resulting in a simpler grammatical structure.
characterized by reduction of inflections

Examples

The evolution of English from Old to Modern forms is often described as syncretic.
Latin developed into several syncretic Romance languages over the centuries.
A syncretic approach to grammar can be observed in some creole languages.

Synset Examples

modern English is a syncretic language

Related Terms

  • Antonym: analytic - (of language) characterized by little or no use of inflections; opposite of syncretic

Etymology

From Greek synkretismos (union of communities), from syn- (together) + kretismos (Cretan federation).

2. ADJ

/ sɪnˈkrɛtɪk / (AmE), / sɪŋˈkrɛtɪk / (BrE)
combining different beliefs, philosophies, or practices, especially in religion, into a new or unified system
combining different beliefs or systems

Examples

The country's culture is syncretic, blending elements from various indigenous and colonial traditions.
Her research focused on syncretic religions that emerged in the Caribbean after colonization.
The music festival showcased a syncretic mix of jazz, hip hop, and traditional folk rhythms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eclectic - deriving ideas, style, or taste from a broad and diverse range of sources

Etymology

From Greek 'synkretismos' (union of communities), via Latin 'syncretismus'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a
