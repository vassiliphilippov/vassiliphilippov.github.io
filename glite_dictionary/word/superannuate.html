to superannuate

1. VERB

/ ˌsuːpɚˈæn.ju.eɪt / (AmE), / ˌsuː.pərˈæn.ju.eɪt / (BrE)
(of a person) to retire or be retired from employment because of old age or infirmity.
retire due to age or infirmity

Examples

After forty years of dedicated service, the company chose to superannuate the old engineer.
She was superannuated last year when new retirement regulations were introduced.
Organizations often superannuate employees who are no longer able to perform their duties due to age.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to retire - to leave one's job and cease to work, especially because of age or ill health

Etymology

From Latin superannuatus, from super- (over) + annus (year).

2. VERB

/ ˌsuːpərˈæn.ju.eɪt / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəˈran.ju.eɪt / (BrE)
(of something) to become obsolete or out of date, especially because of being old or no longer suitable.
become obsolete or outdated

Examples

Technological advances can quickly superannuate once-revolutionary products.
Many traditional teaching methods have been superannuated by digital learning tools.
If a law fails to keep up with society, it may superannuate before it is repealed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsolete - no longer produced or used; out of date
  • Synonym: outmoded - no longer fashionable or useful
  • Antonym: to modernize - to make something more modern

Etymology

From Latin 'superannuare' (to exceed the year), from 'super-' (over) + 'annus' (year).

4. VERB

/ suːpərˈæn.ju.eɪt / (AmE), / suːpərˈanjʊeɪt / (BrE)
to retire or remove someone from employment, usually with a pension, because of old age or physical incapacity
retire due to age or incapacity

Examples

The company decided to superannuate several long-serving employees this year.
He was superannuated from the police force after reaching the age limit for service.
Many workers fear being superannuated before they are ready to retire.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to retire - to leave one's job and cease to work, typically on reaching old age

Etymology

From Latin superannuatus, past participle of superannuare (to make too old), from super- (over) + annus (year).
