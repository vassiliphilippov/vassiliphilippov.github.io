despatch

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈspætʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈspætʃ / (BrE)
the act of sending someone or something to a destination quickly or officially
the act of sending off

Examples

The despatch of troops was completed within hours of receiving the order.
She arranged the despatch of the documents by courier.
A quick despatch of emergency supplies saved many lives.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dispatch - the act of sending someone or something quickly or officially

Etymology

Variant spelling of 'dispatch', from Italian 'dispacciare', from dis- (apart) + paccare (to pack).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00062110-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈspætʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈspætʃ / (BrE)
an official report or message, especially one sent quickly from a distant place by a government or military officer
official report or message sent quickly

Examples

The general sent a despatch to headquarters reporting the enemy's movement.
War correspondents filed their despatches late into the night.
Her promotion was announced in an official despatch from the ministry.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dispatch - an official report or message sent quickly

Etymology

Variant spelling of 'dispatch', from Italian *dispacciare* via Old French *despechier* (to hasten).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06695539-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈspætʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈspætʃ / (BrE)
Promptness and efficiency in performance or action.
prompt and efficient performance

Examples

The secretary handled all requests with remarkable despatch.
The officers moved with despatch to coordinate the emergency response.
We expect all orders to be processed with despatch during the busy season.

5. VERB

/ dɪˈspætʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈspætʃ / (BrE)
to send someone or something to a particular place for a specific purpose, often promptly or efficiently

british

Examples

An ambulance was quickly despatched to the scene of the accident.
She despatched her assistant to collect the documents from the main office.
