oblique

1. NOUN

/ əˈbliːk / (AmE), / əˈbliːk / (BrE)
a muscle situated on either side of the abdomen, running diagonally from the lower ribs to the pelvis; involved in movements such as twisting and bending the torso
a diagonally arranged abdominal muscle
medical

Examples

Strengthening your obliques can help improve your core stability.
The trainer showed me an exercise that targets the oblique muscles on each side of my torso.
She felt a sharp pain in her oblique after an intense workout session.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rectus abdominis - the straight abdominal muscle running down the front of the abdomen
  • Degree Lower: transversus abdominis - the horizontal abdominal muscle beneath the obliques

Etymology

From Latin 'obliquus' meaning 'slanting' or 'sideways'.

2. ADJ

/ əˈbliːk, oʊˈbliːk / (AmE), / əˈbliːk, əʊˈbliːk / (BrE)
slanting or inclined in direction, course, or position; neither parallel nor perpendicular nor right-angled
slanting; not parallel or perpendicular

Examples

The artist painted the shadow using oblique lines to show the angle of the sun.
They built the ramp at an oblique angle to the main entrance.
In geometry class, we learned that an oblique triangle has no right angle.

Synset Examples

the oblique rays of the winter sun
acute and obtuse angles are oblique angles
the axis of an oblique cone is not perpendicular to its base

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slanted - having a slanting direction; at an angle
  • Antonym: perpendicular - at an angle of 90 degrees to a given line or surface
  • Antonym: parallel - side by side and having the same distance continuously between them

Etymology

From Latin 'obliquus' meaning 'slanting, sidelong'.

4. NOUN

/ əˈbliːk / (AmE), / əˈbliːk / (BrE)
(in grammar) any case of a noun, pronoun, or adjective other than the nominative, often used to indicate a role other than the subject of a sentence.
any grammatical case other than nominative

Examples

In Latin, the accusative is considered an oblique case.
Many modern English pronouns only preserve remnants of the oblique case.
The student found it challenging to distinguish between the nominative and oblique cases.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominative - the grammatical case used for a subject of a verb

Etymology

From Latin 'obliquus' meaning 'slanting, indirect'.
