obscurity

1. NOUN

/ əbˈskjʊrəti / (AmE), / əbˈskjʊərɪti / (BrE)
the quality of being unclear, difficult to understand, or not easily seen or recognized
state of being unclear or difficult to understand

Examples

The obscurity of his writing made it difficult for readers to grasp his main argument.
Her explanation was shrouded in obscurity, leaving the audience feeling confused.
Legal documents are often criticized for their unnecessary obscurity.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand
  • Synonym: ambiguity - the quality of being open to more than one interpretation

Etymology

From Latin obscuritas, from obscurus (dark, obscure).

2. NOUN

/ əbˈskjʊrəti / (AmE), / əbˈskjʊərɪti / (BrE)
the state of being unknown, inconspicuous, or unimportant
state of being unknown or unnoticed

Examples

After years of obscurity, the artist finally gained recognition for her work.
Many historical figures have faded into obscurity despite their significant achievements.
He preferred a life of obscurity over the pressures of fame.

Synset Examples

he worked in obscurity for many years

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anonymity - the condition of being anonymous or unknown
  • Antonym: notoriety - the state of being famous or well known for some bad quality or deed

Etymology

From Latin obscuritas (darkness, indistinctness), from obscurus (dark, obscure).

3. NOUN

/ əbˈskjɚ.ə.ti / (AmE), / əbˈskjʊə.rɪ.ti / (BrE)
the state of being indistinct or unclear because of insufficient light or illumination
state of being unclear due to lack of light

Examples

The landscape was lost in obscurity as the fog thickened at dusk.
The painting emerged slowly from the obscurity of the dimly lit gallery.
In the cave's deep obscurity, even familiar shapes became unrecognizable.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: darkness - the partial or total absence of light
  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear; easily seen or understood

Etymology

From Latin obscūritās, from obscūrus (dark, obscure).
