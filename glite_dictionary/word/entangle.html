to entangle

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtæŋɡəl / (BrE)
to involve someone or something in a difficult or complicated situation from which it is hard to escape
involve in a difficult situation

Examples

The company became entangled in legal disputes that lasted for years.
He found himself entangled in a web of lies and deceit.
Many nations were entangled in alliances that eventually led to war.

Synset Examples

Our people should not be mired in the past

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ensnare - to catch or trap as if in a snare
  • Antonym: free - to release from entanglement or confinement

Etymology

From Old French 'entangler', from 'en-' (in) + 'tangle' (to entangle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02684097-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaŋɡl / (BrE)
to twist together or entwine so that it becomes difficult to separate or straighten out; to make into a confusing mass
twist together into a confusing mass

Examples

The kitten managed to entangle itself in the ball of yarn.
Be careful not to entangle the fishing lines.
Her hair became entangled with seaweed while she was swimming.

Synset Examples

The child entangled the cord

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tangle - to become or make twisted together in a messy mass
  • Antonym: to untangle - to free from a tangle or confusing mass

Etymology

From Old French 'entangler', from en- (in) + tangler (to tangle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01465557-v
Synset POS: v
