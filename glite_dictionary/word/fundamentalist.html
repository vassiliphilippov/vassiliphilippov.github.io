fundamentalist

1. ADJ

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (BrE)
Relating to or having strict adherence to a set of basic beliefs, especially in a religious, political, or ideological context.
strictly adhering to core beliefs

Examples

The party adopted a fundamentalist stance on economic reform, rejecting all compromise.
Some communities are shaped by fundamentalist religious beliefs that influence daily life.
Critics argue that fundamentalist approaches can hinder progress and social change.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fundamentalism - belief in strict adherence to basic principles
  • Antonym: liberal - open to new behavior or opinions; not strictly traditional

Etymology

From 'fundamental' + '-ist', first used in early 20th-century American Protestantism.

2. ADJ

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of Protestant fundamentalism or its adherents.
relating to Protestant fundamentalism

Examples

Many fundamentalist churches interpret the Bible literally and reject modern scientific theories.
The city experienced a rise in fundamentalist influence during the late twentieth century.
Some politicians advocated fundamentalist views on education policy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evangelical - having strong beliefs in the authority of the Christian gospel, often emphasizing conversion and scripture

Etymology

From fundamental + -ist, originally referring to those adhering to fundamental Christian doctrines (early 20th century).

3. NOUN

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (BrE)
a person who strictly adheres to the basic principles of a religion or ideology, especially one who interprets sacred texts literally
person holding strict fundamentalist beliefs

Examples

A religious fundamentalist may insist on following scripture exactly as written.
Some political fundamentalists oppose any compromise with rival groups.
The debate featured a fundamentalist and a secularist discussing education policy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: literalist - person who interprets texts literally
  • Antonym: modernist - person who adapts religious beliefs to modern ideas

Etymology

From 'fundamental', influenced by the Fundamentalist movement of early 20th-century Protestantism, plus '-ist'.

4. NOUN

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪst / (BrE)
a person who supports or believes in strict, literal interpretation of a set of basic principles, often used to describe someone who adheres rigidly to religious or ideological doctrines
supporter of strict fundamental principles

Examples

The fundamentalist group refused to accept any changes to their religious texts.
As a fundamentalist, she believed that every word in the doctrine must be followed exactly.
The rise in fundamentalist movements has led to debates about modern interpretations of religion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extremist - a person who holds extreme views

Etymology

From 'fundamental' + suffix '-ist' (denoting an adherent or advocate). 'Fundamental' ultimately from Latin 'fundamentum' (foundation).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
