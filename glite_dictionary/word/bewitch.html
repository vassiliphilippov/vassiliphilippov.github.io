to bewitch

1. VERB

/ bɪˈwɪtʃ / (AmE), / bɪˈwɪtʃ / (BrE)
to attract or fascinate someone intensely, as if by magic
to attract or fascinate intensely

Examples

The dancer seemed to bewitch everyone in the room with her graceful movements.
His mysterious smile would often bewitch those he met at parties.
The magician bewitched the children with his astonishing illusions.

Synset Examples

She magnetized the audience with her tricks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to captivate - to attract and hold the interest of
  • Synonym: entrance - to fill with delight or wonder
  • Antonym: to repel - to drive back or discourage

Etymology

From Old English *bewiccian*, from be- (about, around) + *wiccan* (to enchant).

2. VERB

/ bɪˈwɪtʃ / (AmE), / bɪˈwɪtʃ / (BrE)
to attract and hold someone's attention or affection as if by magic; to enchant or captivate
to enchant or captivate

Examples

She managed to bewitch everyone in the room with her graceful performance.
His smile could easily bewitch even the most cautious hearts.
The magician's mysterious eyes bewitched the entire audience.

Synset Examples

She captured all the men's hearts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: charm - to attract or delight greatly
  • Synonym: to enchant - to delight or captivate completely
  • Antonym: to repel - to drive or force back; to cause dislike

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + 'witch', originally meaning 'to affect by witchcraft'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01810472-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bɪˈwɪtʃ / (AmE), / bɪˈwɪtʃ / (BrE)
to cast a spell over someone or something; to affect someone as if by magic; to enchant
to cast a spell or enchant

Examples

The mysterious woman seemed to bewitch everyone she met at the party.
The magician tried to bewitch the audience with his spectacular illusions.
Legends say that the witch could bewitch a person just by looking into their eyes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enchant - to attract and delight someone powerfully
  • Antonym: curse - to invoke or pronounce a supernatural power to inflict harm

Etymology

From Old English 'bi-' (intensive prefix) + 'wicche' (witch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00779033-v
Synset POS: v
