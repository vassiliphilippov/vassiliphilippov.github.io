lame

1. ADJ

/ leɪm / (AmE), / leɪm / (BrE)
(of an excuse, argument, etc.) pathetically lacking in force, effectiveness, or conviction; feeble or unconvincing.
weak or unconvincing (excuse, argument, etc.)

Examples

That was a lame excuse for being late and no one believed him.
Her lame argument failed to persuade the committee.
They gave a lame explanation that didn't make any sense.

Synset Examples

a feeble excuse
a lame argument

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feeble - lacking in strength or effectiveness
  • Synonym: weak - lacking strength, power, or intensity
  • Antonym: strong - having great force or effectiveness

/ lɑːˈmeɪ / (AmE), / lɑːˈmeɪ / (BrE)

fabric woven with metallic threads

3. ADJ

/ leɪm / (AmE), / leɪm / (BrE)
unable to walk properly because of an injury or physical disability in the feet or legs; having a limp.
unable to walk properly; having a limp
archaic

Examples

After the accident, the horse was lame in its left hind leg.
The lame soldier struggled to keep up with his companions during the long march.
In older stories, a lame beggar might appear at the city gates.

Synset Examples

a crippled soldier
a game leg

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crippled - unable to walk or move properly due to a physical disability
  • Antonym: able-bodied - not disabled; having no physical disability affecting movement

Etymology

From Old English lama (crippled, lame), from Proto-Germanic *lamaz.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01022380-s
Synset POS: s

4. VERB

/ leɪm / (AmE), / leɪm / (BrE)
to cause someone or an animal to lose the use of a limb, especially a leg, making them unable to walk properly
to deprive of use of a limb

Examples

The disease threatened to lame thousands of cattle in the region.
The horse was badly injured and the fall could lame it for life.
He warned that a careless blow could easily lame a working animal.

Synset Examples

The accident has crippled her for life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cripple - to cause severe and permanent injury to someone, making them unable to use a limb or walk

Etymology

From Old English 'lama' (crippled, paralytic).
