occlusive

1. ADJ

/ əˈkluːsɪv / (AmE), / əˈkluːsɪv / (BrE)
tending to block or close off a passage or opening, especially in a medical context.
tending to block or close off
medical

Examples

The doctor used an occlusive dressing to help the wound heal.
An occlusive clot can prevent blood from reaching vital organs.
Some skin creams have occlusive properties to lock in moisture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: closing - shutting or sealing something so it is not open
  • Antonym: open - allowing passage, not blocked or closed

Etymology

From Latin 'occludere' meaning 'to close up'.

2. NOUN

/ əˈkluːsɪv / (AmE), / əˈkluːsɪv / (BrE)
A consonant sound produced by completely blocking the airflow in the vocal tract and then suddenly releasing it; also known as a stop.
consonant formed by blocking then releasing airflow

Examples

The English sounds /p/, /t/, and /k/ are all examples of occlusives.
In French, the word begins with a voiced occlusive rather than a fricative.
Phoneticians study how the place of articulation affects the quality of an occlusive.

Synset Examples

his stop consonants are too aspirated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stop consonant - a consonant in which the airflow is completely blocked and released

Etymology

From Latin 'occlusus' (shut up, closed), past participle of 'occludere' (to close up).
