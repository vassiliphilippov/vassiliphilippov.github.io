repatriate

1. NOUN

/ riˈpeɪtriˌeɪt / (AmE), / riˈpatriˌeɪt / (BrE)
a person who has returned to their country of origin or whose citizenship has been restored
person returned to home country

Examples

The government offered assistance to each repatriate who returned after the conflict.
After decades abroad, she registered as a repatriate upon arriving at the airport in her homeland.
Many repatriates face challenges adapting to life back in their country of origin.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: immigrant - a person who comes to live permanently in a foreign country

Etymology

From Latin 're-' (back) + 'patria' (native land).

2. VERB

/ riˈpeɪ.triˌeɪt / (AmE), / riˈpæ.tri.eɪt / (BrE)
to send or bring a person back to their own country, especially someone who has been living or held in another country
send or bring back to one's own country

Examples

After the war, the government worked to repatriate refugees back to their homeland.
The company helped repatriate its employees who had been stranded overseas.
Authorities agreed to repatriate the prisoners to their country of origin.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deport - to expel someone from a country

Etymology

From Late Latin repatriatus, past participle of repatriare (to return to one's country), from re- (back) + patria (native country).

3. VERB

/ ˌriˈpeɪ.tri.eɪt / (AmE), / ˌriːˈpæ.tri.eɪt / (BrE)
to send or return someone, especially a refugee or prisoner, back to their own country, often against their will
return someone to their own country

Examples

The government decided to repatriate the refugees despite protests from human rights groups.
After the war, thousands of prisoners were repatriated to their countries of origin.
They plan to repatriate all undocumented immigrants by the end of the year.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deport - to expel someone from a country

Etymology

From Latin re- (back) + patria (fatherland, native land).
