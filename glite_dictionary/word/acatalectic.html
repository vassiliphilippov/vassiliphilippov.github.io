acatalectic

1. ADJ

/ ˌeɪ.kə.təˈlɛk.tɪk / (AmE), / ˌeɪ.kə.təˈlɛk.tɪk / (BrE)
(Of a line of verse) having the full number of syllables, especially as prescribed by the meter.
verse line with full syllables

Examples

The poet skillfully ended each stanza with an acatalectic line.
Unlike many English ballads, this poem maintains an acatalectic structure throughout.
Only an acatalectic verse can preserve the rhythm mandated by classical forms.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: catalectic - a line of verse with an incomplete number of syllables

Etymology

From Greek akatalēktos, from a- (not) + katalēgein (to leave off).

2. ADJ

/ ˌækəˌtəˈlɛktɪk / (AmE), / ˌakəˈtalɛktɪk / (BrE)
(Of a line of verse) metrically complete, having the full number of syllables in the final metrical foot.
metrically complete in poetry

Examples

In classical poetry, an acatalectic line is one that is perfectly complete metrically.
The final line of the stanza is acatalectic, containing all the expected syllables.
Unlike a catalectic verse, an acatalectic line includes the full number of feet.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: catalectic - (Of a line of verse) lacking one or more syllables in the final metrical foot

Etymology

From Greek a- (not) + katalēktikos (ending).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00318784-a
Synset POS: a
