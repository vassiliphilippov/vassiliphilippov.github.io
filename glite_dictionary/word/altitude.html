altitude

1. NOUN

/ ˈæl.tɪ.tud / (AmE), / ˈæl.tɪ.tjuːd / (BrE)
The perpendicular distance from the base of a geometric figure to the opposite vertex or side.
perpendicular height in geometry

Examples

In a triangle, the altitude is drawn from one vertex perpendicular to the opposite side.
To find the area of a parallelogram, multiply the base by its altitude.
The longest altitude of a right triangle meets the hypotenuse at a right angle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: height - measurement from base to top or (in geometry) perpendicular from base to vertex or side

Etymology

From Latin altitudo (height, depth), from altus (high, deep).

2. NOUN

/ ˈæltɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈaltɪˌtjuːd / (BrE)
the angular distance of a celestial object above the horizon, measured in degrees
angular distance above the horizon

Examples

The star reached its highest altitude at midnight.
To measure a planet’s altitude, point your telescope above the horizon.
The altitude of the sun determines the length of the shadow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elevation - angular height above the horizon in astronomy
  • Antonym: azimuth - angular measurement in a horizontal plane, perpendicular concept in navigation

Etymology

From Latin altitūdō (height), from altus (high).

3. NOUN

/ ˈæl.təˌtuːd / (AmE), / ˈæl.tɪ.tjuːd / (BrE)
the height of an object or point in relation to sea level or ground level
height above sea or ground level

Examples

The mountain climbers struggled with the thin air at such a high altitude.
Commercial airplanes typically fly at an altitude of around 35,000 feet.
At this altitude, the temperature drops significantly compared to sea level.

Synset Examples

the altitude gave her a headache

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elevation - height above a given level, especially sea level
  • Antonym: depth - distance below surface level

Etymology

From Latin 'altitudo', from 'altus' meaning 'high'.
