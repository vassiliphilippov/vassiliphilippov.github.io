lucky

1. ADJ

/ ˈlʌki / (AmE), / ˈlʌki / (BrE)
having or bringing good fortune or success; resulting from or characterized by chance that brings a favorable outcome
having or bringing good fortune

Examples

She considered herself very lucky to have found such a wonderful job.
It was pure chance that we had a lucky escape from the accident.
He wore his lucky shirt to every big game.

Synset Examples

my lucky day
a lucky man

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fortunate - receiving good things by chance rather than through effort
  • Antonym: unlucky - having or bringing bad fortune

Etymology

Perhaps from luck + -y, first recorded in the 15th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01471012-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈlʌki / (AmE), / ˈlʌki / (BrE)
happening by chance, especially in a way that brings a good result
happening by chance, often with a good outcome

Examples

He made a lucky guess on the test and got the answer right.
It was a lucky coincidence that they ran into each other at the airport.
She had a lucky escape when her car narrowly missed the fallen tree.

Synset Examples

a lucky escape
a lucky guess

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fortunate - receiving good results by chance rather than effort
  • Antonym: unlucky - having bad luck; not fortunate

Etymology

Perhaps from the Low German *luck* (fortune, happiness) or Middle Dutch *lucke*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01052909-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈlʌki / (AmE), / ˈlʌki / (BrE)
likely to bring good luck or a favorable outcome; giving rise to success by chance.
likely to bring good luck

Examples

He bought the ticket at what he considered a lucky store.
Some people carry lucky charms to important events.
Friday the 13th is not usually thought of as a lucky day.

Synset Examples

a favorable time to ask for a raise
lucky stars
a prosperous moment to make a decision

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unlucky - not likely to bring good luck; marked by bad fortune
  • Synonym: fortunate - receiving good fortune, especially by chance

Etymology

From luck + -y, first attested in the late 15th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00178829-s
Synset POS: s
