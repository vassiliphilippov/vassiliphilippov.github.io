to underlie

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈlaɪ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈlaɪ / (BrE)
to be located or situated beneath something; to form a physical or figurative foundation or basis for something
to lie beneath or form a foundation

Examples

Thick layers of rock underlie the surface soil in this region.
Unseen emotions often underlie people's visible actions.
Ancient ruins underlie much of the modern city.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to bear the weight of or hold up

Etymology

From Old English 'underlicgan', from 'under' + 'lie'.

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈlaɪ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈlaɪ / (BrE)
to be the cause, basis, or foundation of something; to form the supporting or underlying part of.
form the base or foundation of

Examples

Many health problems underlie stress and anxiety in modern life.
Economic factors often underlie social changes within a country.
The principles that underlie our laws are rooted in history.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to bear the weight of; to hold up
  • Antonym: to overlie - to lie over or on top of

Etymology

From under- + lie, influenced by Old English 'underlicgan'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02670653-v
Synset POS: v
