to preach

1. VERB

/ priːtʃ / (AmE), / priːtʃ / (BrE)
to deliver a religious sermon or speak publicly about religious subjects, usually in a formal setting such as a church service
deliver a religious sermon

Examples

The minister will preach at the morning service tomorrow.
Every Sunday, she preaches to a large congregation in the old cathedral.
He was invited to preach during the Easter celebrations.

Synset Examples

The minister is not preaching this Sunday

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sermonize - to deliver a sermon or moral speech
  • Synonym: lecture - to give a formal talk or reprimand someone (in a moralizing way)

Etymology

From Old French *precher*, from Latin *praedicare* (to proclaim, preach).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00829664-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ priːtʃ / (AmE), / priːtʃ / (BrE)
to speak, plead, or argue in favor of a particular cause or idea, often with conviction or moral fervor
speak in favor of something

Examples

He is always quick to preach the benefits of a healthy lifestyle to his friends.
The activist preached against environmental destruction at the community meeting.
Instead of taking action, she just continued to preach about the importance of recycling.

Synset Examples

The doctor advocated a smoking ban in the entire house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advocate - to publicly recommend or support a particular cause or policy
  • Antonym: to oppose - to act against or resist a cause or idea

Etymology

From Old French *preachier*, from Late Latin *praedicare* (to proclaim, preach).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00829391-v
Synset POS: v
