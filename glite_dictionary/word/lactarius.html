Lactarius

1. NOUN

/ lækˈtɛəriəs / (AmE), / lækˈtɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
A large genus of mushrooms, known as milk-caps, that exude a white or milky juice when cut; includes edible and poisonous species.
genus of milk-cap mushrooms

Examples

The Lactarius genus includes mushrooms that are popular with foragers due to their distinctive milky sap.
Some species of Lactarius are highly prized for their flavor, while others can be toxic if consumed.
When identifying wild mushrooms, experts often look for the white latex that oozes from Lactarius species.

Etymology

From Latin lactarius (milky), from lac (milk), referring to the milky fluid produced by these fungi.

2. NOUN

/ lækˈtɛəriəs / (AmE), / lækˈtɛəriəs / (BrE)
a genus of fungi (milk-caps) whose species exude a white or milky juice when cut or broken, some of which are edible and others poisonous
genus of fungi known as milk-caps

Examples

Species in the Lactarius genus often produce a milky liquid when their tissues are damaged.
The forager carefully identified the Lactarius mushrooms before deciding which were safe to eat.
Some Lactarius species are highly prized for cooking, while others can be poisonous.

Etymology

From Latin 'lac, lactis' (milk), referring to the milky liquid exuded by these mushrooms.
