default

1. VERB

/ dɪˈfɔlt / (AmE), / dɪˈfɔːlt / (BrE)
to fail to pay money that you owe or to meet a financial obligation, especially on a loan
fail to pay a debt or obligation

Examples

If you default on your loan, the bank can repossess your car.
Many homeowners defaulted during the financial crisis and lost their homes.
Companies sometimes default despite having made large profits previously.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to repay - to pay back money that you owe

Etymology

From Old French 'defaute', from 'defaillir' (fail, be lacking), from Latin 'defallere' (to fail).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02258574-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈfɔlt / (AmE), / dɪˈfɔːlt / (BrE)
a failure to take part in or appear for an event, resulting in an automatic loss or forfeiture, especially in a competition or legal case
automatic loss due to failing to appear

Examples

The team won the match by default because their opponents never arrived.
If you do not show up for court, you may lose your case by default.
He was declared the loser by default after missing the scheduled debate.

Synset Examples

he lost the game by default

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forfeit - losing or giving up something as a penalty

Etymology

From Old French 'default' (fault, deficiency), from 'defaillir' (to fail).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈfɔlt / (AmE), / dɪˈfɔːlt / (BrE)
failure to fulfill a financial obligation, especially to repay a loan or meet a debt payment.
failure to meet financial obligation

Examples

The company went into default after missing several loan payments.
If you fall into default on your mortgage, the bank may foreclose on your home.
A history of default can damage your credit rating significantly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bankruptcy - legal status of being unable to repay debts
  • Antonym: repayment - the action of paying back money owed

Etymology

From Old French defaute, from Vulgar Latin *defallita, from Latin deficere (to fail).

Examples
