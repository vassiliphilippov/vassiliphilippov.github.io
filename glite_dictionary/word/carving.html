carving

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvɪŋ / (BrE)
the act or art of shaping or decorating something, especially wood or stone, by cutting; or an object made in this way
the act or result of shaping by cutting

Examples

The museum displayed an intricate carving of a dragon made from jade.
He admired the delicate wood carvings on the antique furniture.
During the ceremony, an artist performed live carving of an ice sculpture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sculpture - three-dimensional art created by shaping materials

Etymology

From the verb 'carve', from Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut, carve).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvɪŋ / (BrE)
A sculpture or object made by cutting away material from wood, stone, or another solid substance to form a particular shape or design.
sculpture made by cutting material

Examples

The museum displayed an ancient carving made from ivory.
He admired the delicate carving on the wooden door.
Many cultures have a tradition of stone carving for religious monuments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sculpture - the art of making two- or three-dimensional representative or abstract forms
  • Antonym: engraving - a print or design produced from an engraved plate, block, or other surface

Etymology

From the verb 'carve', from Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut, hew).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvɪŋ / (BrE)
the act or art of shaping something by cutting away parts of it, especially from wood or stone; a figure or design made by cutting into a solid material
the art of shaping by cutting away material

Examples

The museum displayed an ancient carving of a lion made from marble.
He spent hours perfecting the details on his wood carving.
Intricate carving adorned the doors of the old cathedral.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sculpture - the art of making two- or three-dimensional representative or abstract forms, especially by carving stone or wood
  • Synonym: engraving - the process of cutting or carving a design on a hard surface

Etymology

From carve + -ing; ultimately from Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut).
