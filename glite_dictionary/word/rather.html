rather

1. ADV

/ ˈræðər / (AmE), / ˈrɑːðə / (BrE)
used to indicate preference; more willingly or readily; to a greater extent or degree
more willingly; showing preference

Examples

I would rather stay home tonight than go out in the rain.
She said she would rather walk than take the bus.
He rather have coffee than tea in the morning.

Synset Examples

clean it well, preferably with warm water
I'd rather be in Philadelphia
I'd sooner die than give up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preferably - more desirably; expressing preference
  • Synonym: sooner - more willingly; used similarly to express preference

Etymology

From Old English hrathor (more quickly), comparative of hræthe (quickly, soon).

2. ADV

/ ˈræðɚ / (AmE), / ˈrɑːðə / (BrE)
used to introduce a statement that contrasts with what has just been said or suggests an alternative; instead or on the contrary.
instead; on the contrary

Examples

He didn’t call; rather, he wrote her a letter to explain.
The problem was not ignored; rather, it was addressed immediately.
She didn’t eat the cake; rather, she gave it to her friend.

Synset Examples

rather than disappoint the children, he did two quick tricks before he left
he didn't call; rather (or instead), he wrote her a letter
used English terms instead of Latin ones

Related Terms

  • Synonym: instead - used to say that one thing is done or chosen in place of another

Etymology

From Old English 'hrathor', comparative of 'hraðe' (quickly, soon).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: r

3. ADV

/ ˈræðər / (AmE), / ˈrɑːðə / (BrE)
to some (great or small) extent; more than a little but not extremely
to some extent; somewhat

Examples

It was rather cold this morning, so I wore a jacket.
The food was rather bland, but the service was excellent.
She found the explanation rather confusing at first, but it made sense later.

Synset Examples

it was rather cold
the party was rather nice
the knife is rather dull
I rather regret that I cannot attend
He's rather good at playing the cello
he is kind of shy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: somewhat - to a moderate extent or degree
  • Degree Higher: very - to a high degree; extremely

Etymology

From Middle English 'rather', from Old English 'hrathor' (more quickly, earlier).
