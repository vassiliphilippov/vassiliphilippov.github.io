literally

1. ADV

/ ˈlɪt̬.ər.əli / (AmE), / ˈlɪt.ər.əl.i / (BrE)
Used to emphasize that a statement, although sometimes figurative or exaggerated, is true or nearly true; used as an intensifier before figurative expressions.
used to emphasize figurative statements
informal

Examples

She was so surprised she literally jumped out of her seat.
The stadium was literally shaking with the roar of the crowd.
I literally died laughing at his joke.

Synset Examples

our eyes were literally pinned to TV during the Gulf War

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figuratively - in a metaphorical rather than literal way

Etymology

From Latin 'littera' (letter), via Medieval Latin 'literalis' (of a letter), originally meaning 'in a literal sense.'

2. ADV

/ ˈlɪt̬.ɚ.əli / (AmE), / ˈlɪt.ər.əli / (BrE)
In a literal sense; using or interpreting the strict, primary meaning of words or statements, without exaggeration or metaphor.
in a literal, strict sense

Examples

The instructions should be taken literally, not interpreted loosely.
He didn't mean she was literally glowing; it was just a figure of speech.
When she said she would 'die of embarrassment,' she didn't mean it literally.

Synset Examples

literally translated
he said so literally

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figuratively - in a metaphorical or non-literal sense

Etymology

From Latin 'litteralis', from 'littera' (letter).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00341857-r
Synset POS: r
