to ligate

1. VERB

/ ˈlaɪɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to join letters together into a ligature when writing or printing
join letters into a ligature

Examples

The calligrapher skillfully ligated the letters 'f' and 'i' to create a beautiful flourish.
In some fonts, designers automatically ligate certain letter combinations for aesthetic reasons.
She enjoys practicing old scripts where you often have to ligate several characters in a single stroke.

Etymology

From Latin ligatus, past participle of ligare (to bind).

WordNet ID: oewn-01618258-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈlaɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to tie or bind with a bandage or ligature, especially in a medical context to close off an artery or vessel
tie or bind with a ligature (medical)
medical

Examples

The surgeon will ligate the blood vessel to stop the bleeding.
During the procedure, the nurse helped ligate the artery quickly and efficiently.
It is crucial to ligate the vein securely to prevent further blood loss.

Synset Examples

ligate the artery

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tie off - to close off by tying; especially of a blood vessel
  • Antonym: to unbind - to release from a binding

Etymology

From Latin ligatus (bound), past participle of ligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00083087-v

3. VERB

/ ˈlaɪɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to join two molecules or molecular fragments together by forming a chemical bond, especially using an enzyme
to join molecules chemically

Examples

Scientists used an enzyme to ligate the DNA fragments during the experiment.
After amplification, the technician proceeded to ligate the sticky ends of the vectors and inserts.
The enzyme efficiently ligated the broken strands, completing the repair process.

Synset Examples

The enzyme ligated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bind - to fasten or secure tightly; unite or hold together

Etymology

From Latin 'ligatus', past participle of 'ligare' (to bind).
