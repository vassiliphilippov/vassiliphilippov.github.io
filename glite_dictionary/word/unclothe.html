to unclothe

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈkloʊð / (AmE), / ʌnˈkləʊð / (BrE)
to remove clothes from oneself or another; to strip clothing off
remove clothes; strip

Examples

The nurse gently unclothed the patient to examine the injury.
He unclothed quickly before stepping into the shower.
In the painting, the artist unclothes his subject to reveal the human form.

Synset Examples

please don't undress in front of everybody!
She strips in front of strangers every night for a living

Related Terms

  • Synonym: undress - to remove clothing from oneself or another
  • Antonym: to clothe - to put clothes on
  • Synonym: strip - to remove all or most of one's clothes

Etymology

From un- (expressing reversal) + clothe (from Old English 'clāþian', to provide with clothes).

2. VERB

/ ʌnˈkloʊð / (AmE), / ʌnˈkləʊð / (BrE)
to remove clothing from someone or something; to strip something of a covering, often used figuratively
remove clothing or a covering

Examples

She gently unclothed the child before his bath.
The artist's intention was to unclothe the truth behind the myth.
He wished he could unclothe his heart of resentment and anger.

Synset Examples

unclothe your heart of envy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: undress - to remove clothing from someone or oneself
  • Antonym: dress - to put clothes on someone or oneself
  • Synonym: strip - to remove all clothes or coverings from

Etymology

From prefix 'un-' (opposite of) + 'clothe' (to provide with clothes), dating to Middle English.

Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ ʌnˈkloʊð / (AmE), / ʌnˈkləʊð / (BrE)
to remove clothing or covering from someone or something; to strip or expose
remove clothing or cover

Examples

The nurse gently unclothed the child to check for injuries.
The artist asked the model to unclothe for the life drawing session.
Rays of sunlight unclothed the landscape as the clouds drifted away.

Synset Examples

She unclothed her innermost feelings

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to clothe - to put clothes on; to dress
  • Synonym: undress - to remove one's or another's clothing

Etymology

From un- (not, opposite of) + clothe (to dress), dating back to Middle English.

Source: WordNet
