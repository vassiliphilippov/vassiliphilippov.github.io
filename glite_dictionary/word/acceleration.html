acceleration

1. NOUN

/ ækˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / əkˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
an increase in the speed or rate at which something happens or changes
increase in speed or rate of change

Examples

The acceleration of technological innovation has transformed many industries.
There has been a noticeable acceleration in the growth of urban populations.
Improved training techniques led to an acceleration of athletes' progress.

Synset Examples

modern science caused an acceleration of cultural change

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deceleration - a decrease in the speed or rate at which something happens or changes

Etymology

From Latin acceleratio, from accelerare (to hasten), from ad- (to) + celer (swift).

2. NOUN

/ əkˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / əkˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of becoming faster or causing something to become faster
increase in speed; becoming faster

Examples

The car's acceleration made it ideal for highway driving.
Improved acceleration is a key feature of the new electric vehicle.
She felt the sudden acceleration as the roller coaster descended the hill.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deceleration - the act of slowing down; reduction in speed
  • Degree Higher: speed - the rate at which something moves or happens
  • Degree Lower: slowing - the act of becoming slower

Etymology

From Latin acceleratio (a hastening), from accelerare (to hasten).

3. NOUN

/ əkˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / əkˌsɛləˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
In physics, the rate at which the velocity of an object changes with time.
increase in velocity per unit time

Examples

The car's acceleration allows it to go from 0 to 60 mph in just six seconds.
Scientists measured the acceleration of the falling object to calculate the force of gravity.
Proper acceleration is essential for rockets to escape Earth's atmosphere.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deceleration - reduction in the rate of velocity; opposite of acceleration

Etymology

From Latin acceleratio, from accelerare (to hasten).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
