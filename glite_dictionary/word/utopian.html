utopian

1. ADJ

/ juːˈtoʊpiən / (AmE), / juːˈtəʊpiən / (BrE)
characterized by or aspiring to a state of ideal but impractical perfection
idealistically perfect but impractical

Examples

Her plans for education reform were criticized as utopian and unlikely to succeed.
The novel depicts a utopian society free from poverty and crime.
Many early social movements were inspired by utopian visions of a perfect world.

Synset Examples

the dim utopian future
utopian idealists
recognized the utopian nature of his hopes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: idealistic - believing in the pursuit of noble ideals
  • Antonym: realistic - having or showing a sensible and practical idea of what can be achieved

Etymology

From 'Utopia', the imaginary island in Sir Thomas More's book (1516), from Greek ou 'not' + topos 'place'.

2. ADJ

/ juːˈtoʊpiən / (AmE), / juːˈtəʊpiən / (BrE)
relating to or resembling a utopia, especially having idealistic or visionary qualities that are impractical
idealistic; relating to a utopia

Examples

The novel describes a utopian society where poverty and crime have been eliminated.
Their plans for renewable energy relied on utopian assumptions about human cooperation.
Many critics found his vision too utopian to be realistic in today's world.

Synset Examples

a Utopian novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: idealistic - believing in or based on ideals, especially unrealistic ones
  • Antonym: practical - concerned with actual use or practice, not theory or ideals

Etymology

From Utopia, the title of a 1516 book by Sir Thomas More, from Greek ou (not) + topos (place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03031707-a
Synset POS: a
