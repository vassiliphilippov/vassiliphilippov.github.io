to respire

1. VERB

/ rɪˈspaɪɚ / (AmE), / rɪˈspaɪə / (BrE)
to breathe easily again, especially after physical effort or anxiety
breathe easily after exertion or anxiety

Examples

After climbing the long staircase, she paused to respire before entering the office.
He could finally respire once the stressful meeting was over.
The patient began to respire more comfortably as the medicine took effect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to breathe - to take air into the lungs and expel it; to respire

Etymology

From Latin respirare, meaning 'to breathe out' or 'to breathe again'.

2. VERB

/ rɪˈspaɪər / (AmE), / rɪˈspaɪə / (BrE)
to breathe or undergo the process of respiration, taking in oxygen and releasing carbon dioxide
to breathe; undergo respiration

Examples

At high altitudes, it becomes harder to respire due to the thin air.
Most living organisms respire to convert oxygen into energy.
The doctor observed how the patient continued to respire normally after the treatment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to breathe - to take air into the lungs and then expel it

Etymology

From Latin 'respirare' (to breathe out), from 're-' (again) + 'spirare' (to breathe).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00002325-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈspaɪr / (AmE), / rɪˈspaɪə / (BrE)
to breathe in and out; to inhale and exhale air, especially as a biological process
to breathe in and out

Examples

Healthy humans automatically respire even while they are sleeping.
Many aquatic animals have gills that allow them to respire underwater.
The climber struggled to respire at high altitudes due to the thin air.

Synset Examples

I can breathe better when the air is clean
The patient is respiring

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to breathe - to take air into and expel it from the lungs
  • Antonym: to suffocate - to die or cause to die from lack of air or inability to breathe

Etymology

From Latin respīrāre (to breathe out, breathe again), from re- (again) + spīrāre (to breathe).
