to unhinge

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈhɪndʒ / (AmE), / ʌnˈhɪndʒ / (BrE)
to remove (a door or other object) from its hinges
remove from the hinges

Examples

It took four men to unhinge the heavy oak door.
They had to unhinge the gate to bring in the new furniture.
The workers unhinged the windows before starting the renovations.

Synset Examples

unhinge the door

Etymology

From un- (not, opposite of) + hinge (the movable joint of a door etc.), first recorded in the 17th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ʌnˈhɪndʒ / (AmE), / ʌnˈhɪndʒ / (BrE)
to disturb the normal operation of someone's mind; to make someone mentally unbalanced, anxious, or upset
make mentally disturbed or upset

Examples

The shocking revelation seemed to unhinge him completely.
Constant stress at work can unhinge even the most stable person.
Losing her closest friend nearly unhinged her with grief.

Synset Examples

She was rather perturbed by the news that her father was seriously ill

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disturb - to interrupt the normal function or peace of mind
  • Synonym: to derange - to cause mental disturbance or insanity
  • Antonym: calm - to make someone peaceful or less anxious

Etymology

From un- (not) + hinge (from Old English heng), originally meaning to remove from hinges, later used metaphorically for mental disturbance.

Source: WordNet
