diffuse

1. VERB

/ dɪˈfjus / (AmE), / dɪˈfjuːz / (BrE)
(of a substance or quality) to spread through or be transmitted across a space or among people; to become widely distributed.
to spread widely through something

Examples

The smell of freshly baked bread began to diffuse through the entire house.
Rumors about the merger quickly diffused throughout the company.
Sunlight diffuses across the room in the early morning.

Synset Examples

An atmosphere of distrust has permeated this administration
music penetrated the entire building
His campaign was riddled with accusations and personal attacks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to permeate - to spread throughout something; to pass through
  • Synonym: to penetrate - to spread into or throughout; to pass into or through

Etymology

From Latin 'diffundere' (to pour out, to spread abroad), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'fundere' (to pour).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01231635-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ dɪˈfjuz / (AmE), / dɪˈfjuːs / (BrE)
spread out over a large area and not concentrated in one place
spread out; not concentrated

Examples

The light from the window was diffuse, creating a soft glow in the room.
A diffuse network of volunteers coordinated aid across the country.
Her explanation was too diffuse to follow easily.

Synset Examples

a large diffuse organization

Related Terms

  • Antonym: concentrated - focused and gathered in a small area
  • Synonym: scattered - spread or dispersed over a wide area

Etymology

From Latin 'diffusus', past participle of 'diffundere' (to pour out, spread out).

5. ADJ

/ dɪˈfjuz / (AmE), / dɪˈfjuːs / (BrE)
(of light) spread out and softened, as by being transmitted through a broad source or scattered by reflection, so as not to produce sharp shadows.
(of light) spread and not concentrated

Examples

The studio was flooded with diffuse light from the large skylights.
Clouds act as a natural filter, creating diffuse sunlight on overcast days.
Photographers often prefer diffuse lighting to avoid harsh shadows in portraits.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scattered - (of light) spread in many directions, not direct
  • Antonym: direct - (of light) coming from a single, focused source, not scattered

Etymology

From Latin 'diffusus', past participle of 'diffundere' (to pour out, spread out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01161394-a
Synset POS: a

6. ADJ

/ dɪˈfjuz / (AmE), / dɪˈfjuːs / (BrE)
(of speech or writing) lacking conciseness; wordy or overly spread out in style.
lacking conciseness; wordy

Examples

The author's diffuse writing style made the report difficult to follow.
Her speech was interesting but rather diffuse, lacking a clear point.
Some readers found the novel's narrative too diffuse to be engaging.

Synset Examples

a diffuse historical novel

Related Terms

  • Antonym: concise - giving a lot of information clearly and in a few words
  • Synonym: verbose - using or expressed in more words than needed

Etymology

From Latin diffusus, past participle of diffundere 'to spread out'.
