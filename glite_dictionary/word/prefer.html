to prefer

1. VERB

/ prəˈfɜr / (AmE), / prɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to like or choose one thing rather than another when given a choice
choose one thing over another

Examples

Most people prefer coffee to tea in the morning.
Would you prefer to sit by the window or the aisle?
He prefers walking to work instead of driving.

Synset Examples

I always choose the fish over the meat courses in this restaurant
She opted for the job on the East coast

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to choose - to select from a number of possibilities
  • Antonym: reject - to refuse to accept, believe in, or agree with something

Etymology

From Latin praeferre, from prae- (before) + ferre (to carry).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00681308-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ prəˈfɜr / (AmE), / prɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to like or choose one person or thing over another; to promote or show favor towards one over others
choose or show favor for one over another

Examples

The manager tends to prefer employees who show initiative over those who simply follow orders.
She clearly preferred her older child when making family decisions.
When asked to choose, the teacher preferred the project that demonstrated originality.

Synset Examples

he favors his second daughter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: favor - to prefer or give support to one person or thing over others
  • Antonym: disfavor - to show dislike or lack of support for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin 'praeferre', meaning 'to carry before, to prefer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02405179-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ prəˈfɜr / (AmE), / prɪˈfəː / (BrE)
to like one thing or person better than another, or to choose one thing rather than something else
like or choose one thing over another

Examples

I prefer tea to coffee in the morning.
Do you prefer working alone or in a team?
Many children prefer playing outside when the weather is nice.

Synset Examples

Some people prefer camping to staying in hotels
We prefer sleeping outside

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: like - to find something agreeable, enjoyable or satisfactory
  • Degree Higher: love - to feel intense affection or deep fondness for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin praefērre, from prae- (before) + ferre (to carry).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01830440-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ prəˈfɝː / (AmE), / prɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
(of a debtor) to give one creditor priority of payment over another, often by settling their claim before others are paid.
give priority to one creditor over another
legal

Examples

The company was accused of attempting to prefer certain creditors ahead of others during the bankruptcy proceedings.
It is illegal to prefer one creditor over another when distributing assets under insolvency law.
The court examined whether the debtor's actions had served to prefer the bank at the expense of smaller creditors.
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02405393-v
Synset POS: v
