cordon

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrd(ə)n / (AmE), / ˈkɔːd(ə)n / (BrE)
A cord or ribbon, often worn as a mark of honor or rank, especially as part of ceremonial dress.
ornamental cord or ribbon of honor

Examples

The general wore a blue cordon across his chest, signifying his distinguished service.
A red silk cordon adorned the ambassador's uniform during the ceremony.
Receiving the official cordon is considered a great honor in many orders of chivalry.

Etymology

From French 'cordon' (small cord).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrdən / (AmE), / ˈkɔːdən / (BrE)
an ornamental ribbon or cord worn as a decoration or mark of distinction, often used with medals or ceremonial dress
ornamental ribbon or cord

Examples

The military officer was presented with a golden cordon as part of his ceremonial uniform.
A red silk cordon adorned the base of the ornate lamp.
She wore a blue cordon across her shoulder to signify her academic achievement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sash - a long strip or loop of cloth worn over one shoulder or around the waist as a decoration or insignia

Etymology

From French 'cordon' (riband, small cord), diminutive of 'corde' (cord).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrdən / (AmE), / ˈkɔːd(ə)n / (BrE)
a line or circle of police officers, soldiers, or guards placed around an area to prevent people from entering or leaving
a line of guards around an area

Examples

Police set up a cordon around the crime scene to keep onlookers away.
A military cordon was established to prevent anyone from entering the restricted area.
The rescue workers broke through the cordon to reach people trapped inside.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barrier - a physical structure that blocks or limits movement
  • Synonym: perimeter - the outer edge or boundary, especially guarded or protected

Etymology

From French 'cordon' (small cord), originally referring to a ribbon or cord, later to a line of people.
