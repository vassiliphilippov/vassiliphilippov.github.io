argyll

1. NOUN

/ ɑrˈɡaɪl / (AmE), / ɑːˈɡaɪl / (BrE)
A sock knitted or woven with a diamond-shaped argyle design, typically in contrasting colors.
sock with argyle pattern design

Examples

He pulled up his argyll to reveal the colorful diamond pattern above his shoe.
She bought a new pair of argylls for the golf tournament.
Classic argyll socks are often worn with kilts at formal events in Scotland.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: argyle - a design of diamond shapes in a knit pattern, often in contrasting colors

Etymology

From Argyle, a region in western Scotland, associated with the origin of the argyle pattern.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɑr ɡaɪl / (AmE), / ˈɑː ɡaɪl / (BrE)
A covered metal container with a detachable central vessel for hot water, used to keep gravy warm at the table.
covered gravy holder with hot water vessel

Examples

The silver argyll was brought to the dining table to keep the gravy piping hot.
She inherited a beautiful antique argyll from her grandmother's collection.
At formal dinners, an argyll was often used to serve sauce in style.

Etymology

Named after the Duke of Argyll, who is said to have introduced it in the 18th century.

3. NOUN

/ ˈɑr·gaɪl / (AmE), / ˈɑː·ɡaɪl / (BrE)
a pattern of multicolored diamonds, often on a solid background, used especially in knitted items such as socks and sweaters
diamond-patterned textile design

Examples

He wore a sweater with a classic argyll pattern in shades of blue and green.
The argyll socks added a touch of color to his otherwise plain outfit.
She prefers argyll designs for her winter accessories because of their traditional look.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tartan - a pattern of criss-crossed horizontal and vertical bands in multiple colors, associated with Scottish clans
  • Synonym: plaid - checked or tartan twilled cloth, typically of wool

Etymology

Named after Argyll, a region of western Scotland, associated with the tartan of the local Campbell clan.
