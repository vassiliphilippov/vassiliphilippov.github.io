abrasive

1. ADJ

/ əˈbreɪsɪv / (AmE), / əˈbreɪsɪv / (BrE)
(of a substance or material) capable of causing abrasion by rubbing, grinding, or wearing away surfaces.
causing abrasion; capable of wearing away surfaces

Examples

Steel wool is an abrasive material used for cleaning and polishing surfaces.
The cleaner contains abrasive particles that help remove tough stains from the floor.
Use a non-abrasive sponge to avoid scratching the glass.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rough - having an uneven or irregular surface

Etymology

From Latin 'abras-, abrasus', past participle of 'abradere' (to scrape off), via French 'abrasif'.

2. NOUN

/ əˈbreɪsɪv / (AmE), / əˈbreɪsɪv / (BrE)
a substance used for grinding, polishing, or cleaning by rubbing away material from surfaces
substance for grinding and polishing

Examples

Mix a small amount of abrasive with water to scrub the stubborn stains on the sink.
Industrial abrasives are essential in the manufacturing of metal components to achieve a smooth finish.
Always use a gentle abrasive when cleaning delicate surfaces to avoid scratches.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: polish - to make smooth or shiny by rubbing

Etymology

From Latin abradere (to scrape off), via French abrasif.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14823414-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ əˈbreɪsɪv / (AmE), / əˈbreɪsɪv / (BrE)
(of a person or manner) showing little concern for the feelings of others; harsh or overly direct.
harsh or insensitive (of manner)

Examples

Her abrasive comments alienated her colleagues during the meeting.
He has an abrasive manner that some people find difficult to tolerate.
Despite his abrasive approach, he gets results.

Synset Examples

the harsh facts of court delays
an abrasive character

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harsh - unpleasantly rough or severe (of people or actions)
  • Antonym: gentle - having or showing a mild, kind, or tender temperament or character

Etymology

From Latin 'abradere' (to scrape off), via French 'abrasif'.
