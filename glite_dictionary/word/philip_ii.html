Philip II

1. PROPN

/ ˈfɪlɪp ðə ˈsɛkənd / (AmE), / ˈfɪlɪp ðə ˈsɛkənd / (BrE)
(1165–1223) King of France, son of Louis VII, whose reign was marked by wars with England, resulting in the recovery of Normandy, Anjou, and most of Poitou.
King of France (1165–1223)

Examples

Philip II expanded the French royal domain more than any of his predecessors.
The victory at the Battle of Bouvines in 1214 secured Philip II's power in France.
Many historians credit Philip II with laying the foundations of the modern French state.

2. PROPN

/ ˈfɪlɪp səˈkənd / (AmE), / ˈfɪlɪp ˌsɪˈkənd / (BrE)
(1527–1598) King of Spain and Portugal, husband of Mary I of England, known for supporting the Counter-Reformation and sending the Spanish Armada to invade England.
King of Spain and Portugal (1527–1598)

Examples

Philip II ruled over Spain during a period of great imperial expansion.
Many historians regard Philip II as one of the most powerful monarchs of the 16th century.
The defeat of the Spanish Armada marked a turning point in Philip II's ambitions.

3. PROPN

/ ˈfɪlɪp ðə ˌsɛkənd / (AmE), / ˈfɪlɪp ði ˈsɛkənd / (BrE)
King of ancient Macedonia (382–336 BC) and father of Alexander the Great, known for unifying Macedonia and laying the foundations for his son's empire.
king of ancient Macedonia, father of Alexander the Great

Examples

Philip II transformed Macedonia into a powerful kingdom through military and diplomatic reforms.
The assassination of Philip II paved the way for Alexander's succession to the throne.
Historians credit Philip II with unifying the Greek states under Macedonian rule.
