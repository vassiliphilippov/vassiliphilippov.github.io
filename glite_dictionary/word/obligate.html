obligate

1. ADJ

/ ˈɑːblɪɡət / (AmE), / ˈɒblɪɡət / (BrE)
(of an organism) restricted to a particular condition of life and unable to survive or function elsewhere.
restricted to a particular life condition (biology)

Examples

An obligate parasite cannot complete its life cycle without exploiting a suitable host.
The bacterium is an obligate aerobe, requiring oxygen to survive.
Some fungi are obligate symbionts, forming essential relationships with plants.

Synset Examples

an obligate anaerobe can survive only in the absence of oxygen

Related Terms

  • Antonym: facultative - able to live under varying conditions (biology)

Etymology

From Latin 'obligare' (to bind, oblige).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01621827-a
Synset POS: a

2. VERB

/ ˈɑːblɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒblɪˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to bind someone legally or morally to do something; to make someone feel indebted or compelled to act
bind by obligation

Examples

The contract will obligate the company to complete the work within six months.
Parents are legally obligated to provide for their children.
By signing the agreement, you obligate yourself to follow its terms.

Synset Examples

He's held by a contract
I'll hold you by your promise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oblige - to make someone legally or morally bound to an action
  • Antonym: release - to free from an obligation

Etymology

From Latin obligatus, past participle of obligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00886738-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɑblɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒblɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to make someone legally or morally bound to do something; to compel or force someone to act
compel or bind someone to do something

Examples

The contract obligates the company to pay compensation in case of delays.
Parents are legally obligated to care for their children.
You should never obligate people to help if they are unwilling.

Synset Examples

We compel all students to fill out this form

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compel - to force someone to do something
  • Synonym: force - to make someone do something against their will
  • Antonym: free - not constrained or obligated

Etymology

From Latin obligare (to bind), from ob- (toward) + ligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02512195-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈɑblɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒblɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to commit or earmark (resources, especially money) for a particular purpose, usually to meet a legal or formal requirement
commit money/resources to fulfill an obligation

Examples

The government must obligate funds before the end of the fiscal year.
We have already obligated most of the grant money for this project.
The contract requires the company to obligate resources for employee training.

Synset Examples

obligate money

Related Terms

  • Synonym: earmark - to designate something for a specific purpose

Etymology

From Latin obligatus, past participle of obligare, meaning 'to bind' or 'to oblige'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02353178-v
Synset POS: v
