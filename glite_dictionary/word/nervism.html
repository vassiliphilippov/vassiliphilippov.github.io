nervism

1. NOUN

/ ˈnɜrˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈnɜːvɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The theory that all behavioral and physiological processes are controlled by the nervous system, as proposed by Pavlov.
theory that the nervous system regulates all processes
medical

Examples

Pavlov's experiments with conditioned reflexes helped establish nervism as a central principle in physiology.
Modern neuroscience owes much to the early ideas of nervism championed by Russian scientists.
The doctrine of nervism emphasizes the regulatory role of the nervous system in all bodily functions.

Synset Examples

Based on the research of I. M. Sechenov, the concept of nervism was introduced into physiology by I. P. Pavlov in 1883.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: neurophysiology - the study of the functions of the nervous system
  • Degree Lower: behaviorism - the theory that behavior can be explained in terms of conditioning, without appeal to thoughts or feelings

Etymology

From Latin 'nervus' (nerve) + '-ism' (forming terms denoting theories or doctrines).
