aggravated

1. ADJ

/ ˈæɡrəˌveɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈaɡ.rə.veɪ.tɪd / (BrE)
(of an offense or circumstance) made more serious by specific factors such as intent, violence, or other circumstances, especially in law.
made more serious; legal context
legal

Examples

He was charged with aggravated assault after the incident involving a weapon.
The court found her actions to be aggravated by prior convictions.
An aggravated offense usually results in a longer sentence than a simple one.

Synset Examples

aggravated assault

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mitigated - (of an offense) made less severe or serious

Etymology

From Latin aggravatus, past participle of aggravare (to make heavier), from ad- (to) + gravis (heavy).

2. ADJ

/ ˈæɡrəˌveɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈaɡrəveɪtɪd / (BrE)
made more angry or irritated, especially as a result of deliberate provocation.
made angry or provoked

Examples

He seemed aggravated by the constant noise outside his window.
The manager became aggravated when his instructions were deliberately ignored.
After hours of delays, passengers grew visibly aggravated at the airport.

Synset Examples

aggravated by passive resistance
the provoked animal attacked the child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: provoked - incited to anger or a strong reaction
  • Antonym: calm - not angry or agitated

Etymology

From Latin 'aggravatus', past participle of 'aggravare' (to make heavier, to aggravate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00115077-s
Synset POS: s
