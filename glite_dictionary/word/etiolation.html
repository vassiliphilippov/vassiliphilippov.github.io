etiolation

1. NOUN

/ ˌiːti.əˈleɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌiːti.əˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of weakening or stunting the growth or development of something
process of weakening or stunting growth

Examples

The etiolation of traditional values has been a concern for many cultural critics.
Long periods of neglect led to the etiolation of the organization.
Critics noted the etiolation of public interest in the arts over recent years.

Synset Examples

the etiolation of the critical tradition

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weakening - the process of becoming weaker

Etymology

From French *étiolation*, from *étioler* (to blanch, make pale), from Old French.

2. NOUN

/ ˌiːtiəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌiːtɪəˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
a pale and sickly appearance, especially as a result of lack of light or vitality
pale, sickly appearance

Examples

The prisoner’s etiolation was apparent after years without sunlight in the cell.
Her face showed the etiolation that comes from weeks of illness.
Extended confinement indoors can result in etiolation of both body and spirit.

Synset Examples

his etiolation signaled years in prison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pallor - an unhealthy pale appearance
  • Antonym: vitality - state of being strong and active

Etymology

From French *étiolation*, from *étioler* (to make pale or weak), originally referring to blanching plants.

3. NOUN

/ ˌiːti.əˈleɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌiːti.əˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(Botany) The process or result of growing a plant in insufficient light, causing it to become pale, weak, and lacking chlorophyll.
paleness and weakening of plants from lack of light
medical

Examples

The lack of sunlight in the cellar led to the etiolation of the potato shoots.
Gardeners may use etiolation to create tender, pale stalks of certain vegetables.
Excessive etiolation can weaken houseplants, making them more susceptible to disease.

Synset Examples

the etiolation of celery

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blanching - the process of making something pale or white, especially in cooking or plants

Etymology

From French *étiolation*, from *étioler* (to make pale), ultimately from Old French *éteuler* (to become pale).
