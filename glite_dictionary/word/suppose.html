to suppose

1. VERB

/ səˈpoʊz / (AmE), / səˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to assume something for the sake of argument or to express a hypothetical situation
assume or express a hypothesis

Examples

Let us suppose you win the lottery—how would you spend the money?
Suppose we arrive early; what should we do while we wait?
If you suppose the rumors are true, what does that mean for the company?

Synset Examples

Let us say that he did not tell the truth
Let's say you had a lot of money — what would you do?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to accept something as true without proof for the sake of argument

Etymology

From Old French 'supposer', from Latin 'supponere' meaning 'put under, assume'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00919271-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ səˈpoʊz / (AmE), / səˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to assume something to be true without proof, often as a basis for reasoning or action
assume to be true without proof

Examples

I suppose she must have left early, since she's not here.
Let's suppose for a moment that your theory is correct.
Many people suppose that all cats hate water, but that's not always true.

Synset Examples

I presuppose that you have done your work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to presume - to suppose something is true based on probability; to assume
  • Synonym: to assume - to take something for granted or accept without proof

Etymology

From Old French 'supposer', from Latin 'supponere' meaning 'to put under, assume'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00718436-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ səˈpoʊz / (AmE), / səˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to think or believe something to be true, especially on uncertain or tentative grounds
to believe on uncertain grounds

Examples

The manager supposed that the package had already been shipped.
Many historians suppose that the artifact is over a thousand years old.
When you don’t have all the facts, you can only suppose what really happened.

Synset Examples

Scientists supposed that large dinosaurs lived in swamps

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to suppose something to be the case without proof
  • Synonym: to presume - to suppose something to be true based on probability
  • Antonym: doubt - to feel uncertain about; to disbelieve

Etymology

From Old French 'supposer', from Latin 'supponere' (to put under, to suppose).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00635105-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ səˈpoʊz / (AmE), / səˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to require as a necessary antecedent or precondition; to presuppose
require as a precondition

Examples

Following this procedure supposes prior knowledge of the theory.
The argument supposes that all members agree on the basic facts.
Success in this field supposes a solid understanding of mathematics.

Synset Examples

This step presupposes two prior ones

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to presuppose - to require as a necessary precondition

Etymology

From Old French supposer, from Latin supponere (to put under, assume).

5. VERB

/ səˈpoʊz / (AmE), / səˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to believe or assume something to be true, especially without certain proof; to expect or think likely
assume or believe something is true

Examples

I suppose he will arrive sometime this evening, but I'm not certain.
She supposed the restaurant would be open late, so they went at eleven.
Do you suppose it will rain today, or should we leave the house without umbrellas?

Synset Examples

I imagine she earned a lot of money with her new novel
I thought to find her in a bad state
he didn't think to find her in the kitchen
I guess she is angry at me for standing her up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to accept as true without proof
  • Synonym: guess - to form an opinion or estimate without sufficient information
  • Antonym: to know - to have clear and certain knowledge

Etymology

From Old French 'supposer', from Latin 'supponere' meaning 'to put under, assume'.
