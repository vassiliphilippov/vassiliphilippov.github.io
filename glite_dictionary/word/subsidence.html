subsidence

1. NOUN

/ səbˈsaɪdəns / (AmE), / səbˈsɪdəns / (BrE)
a reduction or lessening in intensity, degree, or severity; especially, the diminishing of symptoms in a medical condition
a lessening in intensity or severity

Examples

The doctor noted a subsidence in the swelling after the treatment.
After the storm, there was a gradual subsidence of floodwaters in the town.
Her symptoms showed a clear subsidence after starting the new medication.

Synset Examples

his cancer is in remission

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remission - a period during which the symptoms of a disease are reduced or disappear

Etymology

From Latin 'subsidentia', from 'subsidere' (to sink, settle down).

2. NOUN

/ səbˈsaɪdəns / (AmE), / səbˈsɪdəns / (BrE)
the process or an instance of sinking or collapsing of a surface into an empty space below, especially when sudden
sinking or collapse of a surface

Examples

The subsidence of the road created a dangerous sinkhole overnight.
Engineers warned that continued mining could lead to sudden subsidence in the area.
After the earthquake, significant subsidence was observed around the old building.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: collapse - a sudden falling down or caving in
  • Antonym: uplift - the act of raising something or a rise in land

Etymology

From Latin subsidentia, from subsīdere (to settle, sink down).

3. NOUN

/ səbˈsaɪdəns / (AmE), / səbˈsɪdəns / (BrE)
the gradual sinking or downward settling of an area of land or a structure
gradual sinking of land or structure

Examples

The historic church was damaged by subsidence caused by flooding.
Engineers are monitoring the area for signs of further subsidence.
Over time, subsidence may affect the stability of buildings in the region.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: collapse - the sudden falling down or giving way of a structure
  • Synonym: settlement - the gradual sinking of a structure's foundation

Etymology

From Latin 'subsidentia', from 'subsidere' (to settle, sink down).
