fennel

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɛnəl / (AmE), / ˈfɛn.əl / (BrE)
a tall aromatic herb of the parsley family, with feathery leaves and edible seeds, used in cooking and medicine
aromatic herb with edible seeds and leaves

Examples

She added chopped fennel to the salad for a fresh, anise-like flavor.
The garden was filled with the sweet aroma of blooming fennel.
In some recipes, fennel seeds are toasted to enhance their flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anise - aromatic herb with licorice-flavored seeds, similar to fennel

Etymology

From Old English *fenol*, from Latin *feniculum* (diminutive of *fenum*, 'hay').

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɛnəl / (AmE), / ˈfɛnəl / (BrE)
A plant with a thick, bulbous stem base that is aromatic and eaten as a vegetable, either cooked or raw, especially in salads.
edible aromatic bulbous vegetable

Examples

She sliced the fennel thinly and added it to the salad for a crisp texture.
Roasted fennel brings a subtle sweetness to vegetable dishes.
The market had fresh fennel bulbs stacked next to the herbs and lettuces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anise - aromatic plant with a similar flavor profile

Etymology

From Latin 'feniculum', diminutive of 'fēnum' (hay).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɛnəl / (AmE), / ˈfɛnəl / (BrE)
A tall aromatic plant of the parsley family, with feathery leaves and yellow flowers, whose seeds are used as a spice or flavoring.
aromatic plant; seeds used as spice

Examples

She sprinkled crushed fennel seeds over the roasted vegetables for extra flavor.
You can find fennel in many Mediterranean dishes, both as a vegetable and as a spice.
The recipe calls for ground fennel to enhance the aroma of the bread.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anise - an aromatic plant also used for its seeds as a spice

Etymology

From Old English *finol*, from Latin *feniculum* (diminutive of *fenum* 'hay').
