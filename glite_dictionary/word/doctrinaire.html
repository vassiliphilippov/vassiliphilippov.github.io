doctrinaire

1. ADJ

/ ˌdɑktrəˈnɛr / (AmE), / ˌdɒktrɪˈnɛə / (BrE)
believing in or applying a set of ideas or theories very rigidly, without considering whether they are practical or suitable in a given situation
rigidly applying theory without regard for practicality

Examples

The committee rejected his doctrinaire approach in favor of more flexible methods.
Her doctrinaire beliefs made it difficult for her to compromise with colleagues.
Some politicians are too doctrinaire to adapt their policies to changing circumstances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dogmatic - expressing opinions very strongly as if they are facts

Etymology

From French 'doctrinaire' (one who teaches doctrine), from 'doctrine' (teaching, principle).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɑːktrəˈner / (AmE), / ˌdɒktrəˈneə / (BrE)
A person who stubbornly adheres to a fixed theory or set of beliefs, often without regard to practical considerations.
an unyieldingly dogmatic person

Examples

The committee was composed of both pragmatists and a single doctrinaire who refused to compromise.
She was known as a doctrinaire, unwilling to accept any views but her own.
Despite mounting evidence, the doctrinaire refused to alter his position.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dogmatist - a person who lays down principles as incontrovertibly true

Etymology

From French 'doctrinaire', from 'doctrine'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
