woeful

1. ADJ

/ ˈwoʊ.fəl / (AmE), / ˈwəʊ.fəl / (BrE)
Feeling or showing great sorrow, distress, or misery.
full of sorrow or misery

Examples

Her woeful expression told everyone she had received bad news.
The woeful cries of the child echoed through the empty house.
After the disappointing results, the team looked woeful as they left the field.

Synset Examples

his sorrow … made him look … haggard and … woebegone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: miserable - feeling or showing unhappiness or discomfort
  • Synonym: hapless - unlucky or unfortunate, often pitiful
  • Antonym: joyful - feeling, expressing, or causing great happiness

Etymology

From Middle English *woful*, from *wo* (woe) + *-ful* (full of).

2. ADJ

/ ˈwoʊfəl / (AmE), / ˈwəʊfʊl / (BrE)
Very poor in quality or condition; extremely bad or inadequate.
very poor in quality or condition

Examples

The team delivered a woeful performance in the final match, losing by a wide margin.
Residents complained about the woeful state of the public transport system in their area.
Her knowledge of the subject was woeful, leading to many mistakes in the report.

Synset Examples

deplorable housing conditions in the inner city
woeful treatment of the accused
woeful errors of judgment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deplorable - of shockingly bad quality
  • Antonym: excellent - extremely good or outstanding in quality

Etymology

From Middle English *woful*, from Old English *wā* (woe) + *-ful* (full of).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02355625-s
Synset POS: s
