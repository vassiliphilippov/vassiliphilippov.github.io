multitude

1. NOUN

/ ˈmʌltɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈmʌltɪˌtjuːd / (BrE)
a large number of things or people; a great quantity
a large number or quantity

Examples

A multitude of stars lit up the night sky.
There is a multitude of reasons why the project succeeded.
She addressed a multitude of questions from the audience.

Synset Examples

a battalion of ants
a multitude of TV antennas
a plurality of religions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: host - a large number of people or things
  • Synonym: crowd - a large group of people gathered together
  • Antonym: handful - a small number of people or things

Etymology

From Latin multitudo, from multus (much, many).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌltɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈmʌltɪtjuːd / (BrE)
a large number of people gathered together; a crowd
large crowd of people

Examples

A multitude of fans cheered loudly as the team entered the stadium.
He disappeared into the multitude lining the streets for the parade.
The announcement attracted a multitude of curious onlookers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - a large number of people gathered together in one place
  • Synonym: throng - a large, densely packed crowd of people or animals

Etymology

From Latin multitūdō (great number, multitude), from multus (much, many).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmʌl.təˌtud / (AmE), / ˈmʌl.tɪ.tjuːd / (BrE)
the common people considered as a large group; the masses.
the common people; the masses

Examples

The politician promised to improve the lives of the multitude he served.
A loud cheer rose from the multitude gathered in the square.
Throughout history, the multitude has often played a crucial role in shaping society.

Synset Examples

separate the warriors from the mass
power to the people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mass - the common people considered as a large group
  • Antonym: elite - a group or class of people seen as having the greatest power or influence

Etymology

From Latin multitudo (crowd, multitude), from multus (much, many).
