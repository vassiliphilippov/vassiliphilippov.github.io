tenure

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɛnjɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjə / (BrE)
the period of time during which someone holds a job, position, or office
length of time holding a position

Examples

Her tenure as CEO was marked by significant growth for the company.
During his long tenure at the university, he mentored hundreds of students.
The governor's tenure ended after just one term in office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: term - a fixed or limited period for which something lasts
  • Synonym: appointment - the act of assigning a job or position, or the conditions of holding it
  • Antonym: resignation - the act of leaving or giving up a job or position

Etymology

From Latin 'tenere' (to hold) via Old French 'tenure'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɛn.jɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjə / (BrE)
the legal right to hold or occupy property or land, especially as part of a historical system of landholding
the right to hold property or land

Examples

Under medieval law, tenure determined the obligations of the landholder to their lord.
The tenure of these estates often came with feudal duties and restrictions.
After centuries, the system of land tenure in Europe has fundamentally changed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ownership - the state or fact of having control of property
  • Degree Lower: leasehold - the holding of property by lease
  • Degree Higher: freehold - permanent and absolute tenure of land or property with freedom to dispose of it at will

Etymology

From Old French *tenure* (a holding), from tenir (to hold), from Latin *tenēre* (to hold).

3. VERB

/ ˈtɛn.jɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjə / (BrE)
to grant someone a permanent position, especially as a teacher or professor at a university or college
grant permanent employment (esp. in academia)

Examples

After years of dedicated research and teaching, Professor Kim was finally tenured by the university.
Many young academics hope to be tenured before they turn forty.
The committee will decide next month whether to tenure the assistant professor.

Synset Examples

She was tenured after she published her book

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to dismiss - remove someone from their job or position

Etymology

From Latin 'tenor' (holder, tenure), via Old French 'tenure' (a holding).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
