investigator

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛstəˌɡeɪtər / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛstɪɡeɪtə / (BrE)
a police officer whose job is to examine crimes and uncover evidence or information
police officer who investigates crimes

Examples

The lead investigator presented the evidence to the court.
After years of experience, she became a top investigator in the homicide unit.
The investigator questioned several witnesses about the incident.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: detective - a person, especially a police officer, whose job is to investigate crimes

Etymology

From Latin 'investigare' meaning 'to track or trace.'

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛstəˌɡeɪtər / (AmE), / ɪnˈvestɪɡeɪtə / (BrE)
a person whose job is to examine facts or evidence in order to discover the truth about something, especially a crime or scientific matter
a person who investigates

Examples

The private investigator uncovered new evidence in the case.
As a scientific investigator, she was committed to finding the facts.
The police assigned an experienced investigator to the robbery.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: detective - a person, especially a police officer, whose job is to investigate crimes
  • Synonym: researcher - a person who carries out academic or scientific investigation

Etymology

From Latin 'investigātor' (one who searches into), from 'investigāre' (to track, search after).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10235293-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛstəˌɡeɪtər / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛstəɡeɪtə / (BrE)
a scientist or researcher who conducts systematic investigation or study in a particular field
scientific researcher

Examples

The clinical trial was overseen by the principal investigator from the university's medical school.
As a leading investigator, she published several groundbreaking studies on cellular biology.
Each research team must designate an investigator to ensure compliance with ethical guidelines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: researcher - a person who carries out academic or scientific investigation
  • Antonym: detective - a person whose job is to investigate crimes and obtain evidence

Etymology

From Latin investigare (to trace out, search after), via Old French investigateur.
