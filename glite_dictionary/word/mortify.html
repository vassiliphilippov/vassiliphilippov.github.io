to mortify

1. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrtɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel great embarrassment or shame; to hurt someone's pride deeply
cause deep embarrassment or shame

Examples

She was mortified when everyone heard about her mistake.
It would mortify him to admit that he was wrong.
I didn't mean to mortify you in front of your friends.

Synset Examples

He humiliated his colleague by criticising him in front of the boss

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to humiliate - to make someone feel ashamed or foolish
  • Synonym: to embarrass - to make someone feel awkward or self-conscious
  • Antonym: honor - to show great respect or admiration for someone

Etymology

From Latin 'mortificare' (to put to death, subdue), from 'mort-' (death) + 'facere' (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01803805-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈmɔːr.t̬əˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɔː.tɪ.faɪ / (BrE)
(of tissue) to undergo necrosis; to become dead or gangrenous as a result of injury or disease.
(of tissue) undergo necrosis or gangrene
medical

Examples

If the blood supply is not restored, the tissue may mortify and require amputation.
Doctors examined the wound and saw that some cells had already begun to mortify.
Without prompt treatment, infected tissue can rapidly mortify and spread infection.

Synset Examples

the tissue around the wound necrosed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to necrose - (of tissue) to undergo necrosis
  • Degree Higher: gangrene - localized tissue death due to lack of blood supply

Etymology

From Latin *mortificare* (to cause death), from *mort-* (death) + *facere* (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00097148-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrtɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɔː.tɪ.faɪ / (BrE)
to control or restrain (one's desires, appetites, or the body), often for moral or religious reasons
restrain one's desires or body

Examples

Some monks mortify their bodies by fasting for extended periods.
He tried to mortify his desire for sweets during Lent.
The tradition required her to mortify her senses as an act of discipline.

Synset Examples

subdue one's appetites
mortify the flesh

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to subdue - to bring under control, especially by force
  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to by force; to keep under control

Etymology

From Latin mortificare (to put to death), from mort- (death) + facere (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01805829-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrtɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to subdue or discipline one's body or desires, especially by self-denial or self-inflicted hardship, often for religious reasons
subdue bodily desires by self-denial

Examples

Certain monks mortify the flesh to reach spiritual enlightenment.
She would mortify her appetite by fasting for days at a time.
His strict routine was designed to mortify physical desires and focus the mind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discipline - to train or control by enforcing rules or self-restraint

Etymology

From Latin mortificare, from mort-, 'death', and facere, 'to make'.

Source: WordNet
