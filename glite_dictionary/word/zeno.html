Zeno

1. PROPN

/ ˈziːnoʊ / (AmE), / ˈziːnəʊ / (BrE)
(c. 335–263 BC) An ancient Greek philosopher who founded the Stoic school of philosophy.
ancient Greek Stoic philosopher

Examples

Zeno founded the Stoic school of philosophy in Athens.
Many of Zeno's ideas greatly influenced later philosophical thought.
The teachings of Zeno emphasized the importance of virtue and reason.

Etymology

From Greek Ζήνων (Zénōn), a personal name.

2. PROPN

/ ˈziːnoʊ / (AmE), / ˈziːnəʊ / (BrE)
(c. 495–430 BC) An ancient Greek philosopher known for his paradoxes that challenge the concepts of motion and change, contributing to philosophical debates on the nature of reality.
ancient Greek philosopher, formulated paradoxes

Examples

Many students first encounter Zeno in philosophy courses discussing the paradoxes of motion.
The famous paradoxes of Zeno have fascinated mathematicians and philosophers for centuries.
Plato and Aristotle both referred to Zeno in their writings on logic and metaphysics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Zeno's paradox - a set of philosophical paradoxes concerning motion, attributed to Zeno

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Ζήνων (Zēnōn), a proper name.
