deception

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈsɛpʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈsɛpʃən / (BrE)
the act of making someone believe something that is not true in order to gain an advantage; the practice of deceiving someone
the act of deceiving

Examples

He was accused of deception for lying on his application.
The magician’s trick was based on clever deception and misdirection.
She saw through his deception and confronted him with the truth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception intended for personal gain
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and free from deceit

Etymology

From Latin 'deceptio' (a deceiving), from 'decipere' (to deceive).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈsɛpʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈsɛpʃən / (BrE)
An act or feat that creates a false impression, often appearing magical or extraordinary, especially to someone easily impressed.
an illusory or magical feat

Examples

The magician's deception left the audience completely mystified and in awe.
Some stage artists create their own unique deception to separate themselves from traditional magicians.
For those unfamiliar with the trick, his deception seemed nothing short of real magic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: illusion - a deceptive appearance or impression, especially in magic or trickery
  • Synonym: trick - an act intended to deceive or outwit

Etymology

From Latin 'deceptio' (a deceiving), from 'decipere' (to deceive).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈsɛpʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈsɛpʃən / (BrE)
the act of deliberately making someone believe something that is not true
misleading someone with a falsehood

Examples

His elaborate deception fooled everyone at the party.
The company's deception led to a loss of public trust.
She regretted her deception after seeing the harm it caused.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception intended to result in financial or personal gain
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and free from deceit

Etymology

From Latin 'deceptio' (a deceiving), from 'decipere' (to deceive).
