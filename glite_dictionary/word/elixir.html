elixir

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈlɪksɚ / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪksə / (BrE)
A magical or medicinal substance believed to cure all diseases or grant immortality.
substance believed to cure all ills

Examples

The ancient manuscript described an elixir that could supposedly grant eternal youth.
Many quack doctors once sold an elixir that promised to cure every known ailment.
Despite years of research, scientists have yet to discover an elixir for aging.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: panacea - a remedy for all difficulties or diseases

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'elixir', from Arabic 'al-iksīr' (the philosopher's stone), from Greek 'xērion' (powder for drying wounds).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈlɪksər / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪksə / (BrE)
A sweet-flavored liquid containing a small amount of alcohol, used as a vehicle for medicines taken by mouth to mask unpleasant taste.
sweet medicinal liquid for oral use
medical

Examples

The pharmacist prepared an elixir for the child's cough, making it easier to swallow the medicine.
This elixir contains both the active drug and a sweet syrup to mask its bitter flavor.
Doctors sometimes prescribe an elixir when a tablet form would be too difficult for patients to ingest.

Etymology

From Arabic 'al-ʾiksīr', via Medieval Latin 'elixir' (a medicinal or alchemical preparation).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈlɪksər / (AmE), / ɪˈlɪksə / (BrE)
A hypothetical substance that alchemists believed could transform base metals into gold.
alchemists' substance to change metals into gold

Examples

During the Middle Ages, many alchemists searched for the elixir that would turn lead into gold.
The legendary elixir fascinated scientists and dreamers for centuries.
Stories often feature an elixir with the mystical power to transform ordinary metals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: philosopher's stone - mythical substance believed to enable transmutation of metals

Etymology

From Arabic *al-iksīr*, from Greek *xērion* (powder for drying wounds).
