fish

1. VERB

/ fɪʃ / (AmE), / fɪʃ / (BrE)
to catch or try to catch fish or shellfish, typically as a sport or occupation
catch or try to catch fish or shellfish

Examples

Every summer, we fish for trout in the nearby river.
He fished for hours but didn't catch anything.
They were fishing off the coast all afternoon.

Synset Examples

I like to go fishing on weekends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: angle - to fish with a rod and line

Etymology

Old English *fiscian* (to fish), from *fisc* (fish).

2. NOUN

/ fɪʃ / (AmE), / fɪʃ / (BrE)
the flesh of fish, used as food
flesh of fish used for food

Examples

I ordered grilled fish with a side of vegetables for dinner.
Fresh fish is a staple in coastal cuisines around the world.
Many people prefer to eat baked fish because it is healthier than fried options.

Synset Examples

in Japan most fish is eaten raw
after the scare about foot-and-mouth disease a lot of people started eating fish instead of meat
they have a chef who specializes in fish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meat - the flesh of animals (including fish) used as food
  • Synonym: seafood - edible marine fish and shellfish

Etymology

Old English fisc, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch vis and German Fisch.

3. NOUN

/ fɪʃ / (AmE), / fɪʃ / (BrE)
A cold-blooded aquatic animal that has a backbone, usually breathes through gills, and typically has fins and scales.
cold-blooded aquatic vertebrate

Examples

We saw a school of brightly colored fish swimming near the coral reef.
She keeps a tank of tropical fish in her living room.
The river is popular with anglers hoping to catch big fish.

Synset Examples

the shark is a large fish
in the living room there was a tank of colorful fish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shark - a large predatory fish with a cartilaginous skeleton
  • Antonym: mammal - a warm-blooded vertebrate animal distinguished by the possession of hair or fur, mammary glands

Etymology

Old English fisc, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch vis and German Fisch.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
