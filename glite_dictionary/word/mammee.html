mammee

1. NOUN

/ mæˈmi / (AmE), / mæˈmi / (BrE)
a tropical American tree (Mammea americana) with large, edible fruit that has a thick, leathery rind
tropical tree with edible fruit

Examples

The mammee tree produces large fruit that are a staple in many Caribbean diets.
She harvested ripe mammee from the garden, eager to taste its sweet flesh.
A single mammee can weigh over a kilogram and is prized for its flavor.

Etymology

From Spanish 'mamey', of Taíno origin.

2. NOUN

/ mæˈmi / (AmE), / mæˈmi / (BrE)
A tropical American tree (Mammea americana) with wood similar to mahogany and sweet, edible, egg-shaped fruit.
tropical American tree and its edible fruit

Examples

A ripe mammee is sweet and fragrant, making it a popular treat in the Caribbean.
The wood of the mammee is sometimes used in furniture-making due to its strength.
Botanists studied the mammee and catalogued its unique fruit characteristics.

Etymology

From French 'mammée', from Taíno or related Caribbean languages.

3. NOUN

/ mæˈmi / (AmE), / mæˈmiː / (BrE)
A globular or ovoid tropical fruit with a thick, russet, leathery rind and juicy yellow or reddish flesh, native to the Caribbean and Central America.
tropical fruit with thick rind and yellow or reddish flesh

Examples

The sweet, fragrant flesh of the mammee is often eaten fresh or made into preserves.
Farmers in the Caribbean cultivate mammee for local markets and export.
She was delighted to find ripe mammee at the fruit stall during her trip to Jamaica.

Etymology

From French 'mamey', possibly from Taino origin.

4. NOUN

/ mæˈmiː / (AmE), / mamˈiː / (BrE)
A tropical American tree (Mammea americana) producing a large, brown, oval fruit with sweet, orange-yellow flesh, often used to make sherbet or preserves.
tropical fruit with sweet orange-yellow flesh

Examples

We tasted the delicious sherbet made from ripe mammee fruit.
The mammee tree flourishes in the tropical climate of the Caribbean islands.
Vendors at the market sell fresh mammee alongside mangoes and papayas.

Etymology

From French 'mamme' and Spanish 'mammee', from Taíno origin.
