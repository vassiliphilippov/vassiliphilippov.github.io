elemental

1. ADJ

/ ˌɛləˈmɛntəl / (AmE), / ˌɛlɪˈmɛntəl / (BrE)
relating to the forces of nature, especially severe or destructive weather conditions.
relating to natural forces or severe weather

Examples

The hikers battled elemental forces as the storm raged around them.
The village was devastated by an elemental disaster that struck without warning.
She admired the elemental power of the ocean during the hurricane.

Synset Examples

a race against hail or cold rains or some other elemental catastrophe

Related Terms

  • Synonym: natural - relating to nature or the natural world
  • Antonym: manmade - made or caused by human beings

Etymology

From Latin elementum (element, rudiment) + -al.

2. ADJ

/ ˌɛl.əˈmɛn.tl̩ / (AmE), / ˌɛl.ɪˈmɛn.tl̩ / (BrE)
Forming or relating to the essential or most basic part of something.
basic or essential

Examples

The urge for safety and shelter is an elemental part of human nature.
Fire and water are often described as the most elemental of natural forces.
His elemental need for companionship was clear from his actions.

Synset Examples

an elementary need for love and nurturing
a basal reader

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elementary - relating to the most basic or fundamental level of something

Etymology

From Latin 'elementalis', from 'elementum' (element).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01862151-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˌɛləˈmɛntl̩ / (AmE), / ˌɛlɪˈmɛntl̩ / (BrE)
Relating to or being a chemical element in its pure form, rather than combined with others.
relating to a pure chemical element

Examples

The mineral contains elemental iron rather than an iron compound.
Pure elemental mercury is liquid at room temperature.
Chemists isolated elemental oxygen from air in the eighteenth century.

Synset Examples

elemental sulphur

Related Terms

  • Antonym: compound - a substance formed from two or more elements chemically united

Etymology

From Latin 'elementum' (element).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02730131-a
Synset POS: a
