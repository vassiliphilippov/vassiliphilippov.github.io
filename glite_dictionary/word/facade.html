facade

1. NOUN

/ fəˈsɑd / (AmE), / fəˈsɑːd / (BrE)
the front or face of a building, especially one that is decorative or imposing
front face of a building

Examples

The museum's new facade blends modern glass with historic brickwork.
Several tourists admired the ornate facade of the cathedral.
They cleaned the facade to make the building look more inviting.

Etymology

From French 'façade', from Italian 'facciata', from 'faccia' (face), from Latin 'facies' (face, form).

2. NOUN

/ fəˈsɑd / (AmE), / fəˈsɑːd / (BrE)
an outward appearance that is maintained to conceal a less pleasant or creditable reality; a deceptive front
deceptive outward appearance

Examples

She put on a cheerful facade to hide her anxiety from her friends.
Beneath the company's successful facade lurked serious financial problems.
His arrogance was just a facade for his deep insecurity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pretense - a way of behaving that is intended to deceive people
  • Synonym: mask - a covering or behavior used to disguise or hide something real
  • Antonym: truth - the real facts about a situation, rather than what appears or is pretended

Etymology

From French façade, from Italian facciata (front of a building), from faccia (face).

3. NOUN

/ fəˈsɑd / (AmE), / fəˈsɑːd / (BrE)
the front of a building, especially a prominent or decorative one
front of a building

Examples

The restored facade of the theater attracts many visitors each year.
Architects paid special attention to the facade to ensure the building made a strong impression.
Behind its modern glass facade, the building holds centuries of history.

Synset Examples

She admired the fac of the ancient temple.
The fac of the building was quite impressive.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: front - the part or side of something that faces forward
  • Antonym: back - the rear part of a building or object

Etymology

From French *façade* (front of a building), from Italian *facciata*, from *faccia* (face).
