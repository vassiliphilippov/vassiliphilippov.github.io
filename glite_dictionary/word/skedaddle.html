skedaddle

1. NOUN

/ skəˈdæd.əl / (AmE), / skɪˈdæd.əl / (BrE)
a hurried or disorderly departure or escape, usually in order to avoid trouble or danger
a hasty or disorderly escape
informal american

Examples

At the first sign of trouble, the crowd made a skedaddle for the exit.
The thief beat a quick skedaddle when the police arrived.
After the alarm sounded, there was a mad skedaddle from the building.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retreat - an act of withdrawing or escaping suddenly
  • Synonym: flight - the act of fleeing or running away
  • Antonym: advance - moving forward towards a place, opposite of retreat or skedaddle

Etymology

Origin unknown; possibly an Americanism from the mid-19th century, perhaps imitative or related to dialectal words meaning 'to scatter.'

2. VERB

/ skɪˈdæd.əl / (AmE), / skɪˈdæd.əl / (BrE)
to run away quickly, often because of fear or panic
run away hurriedly
informal american

Examples

The children saw the angry dog and decided to skedaddle as fast as they could.
As soon as the alarm sounded, everyone in the shop skedaddled out onto the street.
If you don't hurry up and skedaddle, you'll miss your train.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to flee - to run away from a place or situation of danger
  • Synonym: bolt - to move or run away suddenly, often out of fear
  • Antonym: to linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly an American English slang word from the mid-19th century, perhaps imitative or related to dialectal words denoting running or departure.
