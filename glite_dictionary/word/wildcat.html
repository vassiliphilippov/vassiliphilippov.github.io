wildcat

1. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪlˌkæt / (AmE), / ˈwaɪlˌkat / (BrE)
an exploratory oil well drilled in an area not known to be an oil field
exploratory oil well

Examples

The company invested millions in a wildcat to search for oil in remote territory.
Drilling a wildcat can be risky, as there's no guarantee of finding oil.
Several wildcats struck out, but one hit a major oil reserve.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exploratory well - an oil well drilled to discover new oil fields

Etymology

Originally American English, possibly from the risky and unpredictable nature of wildcats, used figuratively from the mid-19th century.

2. ADJ

/ ˈwaɪlˌkæt / (AmE), / ˈwaɪlˌkat / (BrE)
(of a strike or similar action) carried out without official approval or authorization from the relevant authority, especially by workers.
unauthorized (esp. strike or action)

Examples

The workers initiated a wildcat strike in protest of the new policy.
Management responded quickly to the wildcat work stoppage at the factory.
A wildcat protest can disrupt operations and catch employers by surprise.

Synset Examples

an unauthorized strike
wildcat work stoppage

Related Terms

  • Antonym: authorized - officially recognized or approved
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01638242-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪlˌkæt / (AmE), / ˈwaɪl.kæt / (BrE)
a small or medium-sized wild feline resembling the domestic cat, typically living in forests or grasslands
a small or medium-sized wild feline

Examples

The Scottish wildcat is now considered critically endangered.
Farmers are sometimes troubled by wildcats preying on poultry at night.
Unlike the domestic cat, a true wildcat avoids human settlements.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: domestic cat - a cat that has been domesticated and lives with humans
  • Degree Higher: lynx - a larger wild cat with tufted ears
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02127275-n
Synset POS: n

4. ADJ

/ ˈwaɪlˌkæt / (AmE), / ˈwaɪl.kæt / (BrE)
(of a mine, oil well, or drilling operation) undertaken speculatively in an area not known to be productive.
speculative mining or drilling operation

Examples

The company invested in a wildcat well, hoping to discover oil in the remote region.
Many fortunes have been lost in wildcat drilling operations that yielded nothing.
Geologists warned that the area was risky, but some firms pursued wildcat mines anyway.

Synset Examples

drilling there would be strictly a wildcat operation
a wildcat mine
wildcat drilling
wildcat wells

Related Terms

  • Synonym: speculative - involving a high risk of loss
  • Antonym: proven - known to be productive or reliable (opposite of wildcat in this context)

Etymology

Originally American English, from 'wildcat', referring to risky ventures, likened to the unpredictability of a wildcat.

Source: WordNet

5. ADJ

/ ˈwaɪlˌkæt / (AmE), / ˈwaɪl.kæt / (BrE)
(of business or financial activities) carried out without official approval, legitimacy, or ethical standards, especially in a speculative or risky way.
unauthorized or speculative (esp. business/finance)

Examples

The government cracked down on several wildcat investment firms operating without licenses.
Experts warned of the risks associated with wildcat financial schemes targeting inexperienced investors.
She uncovered a wildcat loan network preying on small business owners.

Synset Examples

wildcat currency issued by irresponsible banks
wildcat stock speculation
a wildcat airline
wildcat life insurance schemes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: illegitimate - not authorized by the law; not in accordance with accepted standards
  • Antonym: authorized - officially recognized or permitted

Etymology

Originally from the reputation of 'wildcat banks' in 19th-century America, which were unstable and often dishonest.
