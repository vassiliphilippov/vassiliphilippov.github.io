anaphora

1. NOUN

/ əˈnæfərə / (AmE), / əˈnafərə / (BrE)
the repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences, used for emphasis or effect
repetition at clause beginnings

Examples

The famous speech used anaphora with the phrase "I have a dream" repeated at the start of each sentence.
Writers often employ anaphora to create rhythm and emphasis in their works.
In poetry, anaphora can enhance the emotional impact of repeated ideas.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: epiphora - repetition of a word or phrase at the end of successive clauses

Etymology

From Late Latin anaphora, from Greek anaphora (repetition, reference), from ana- (back) + pherein (to carry).

2. NOUN

/ əˈnæfərə / (AmE), / əˈnafərə / (BrE)
the use of a word, often a pronoun, to refer back to a word or phrase used earlier in a sentence or text, so as to avoid repetition
use of a referring word (esp. pronoun) to avoid repetition

Examples

In the sentence 'Sarah lost her keys and she can't find them,' anaphora is used as 'she' and 'them' refer back to 'Sarah' and 'keys'.
Writers rely on anaphora for cohesion, helping readers link ideas without unnecessary repetition.
Understanding anaphora is important for analyzing how pronouns function in sentences.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cataphora - use of a word to refer forward to a later expression

Etymology

From Greek anaphora (repetition, carrying back), from ana- (back) + pherein (to carry).
