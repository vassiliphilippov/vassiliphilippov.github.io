Dardanelles

1. PROPN

/ ˌdɑːrdəˈnɛlz / (AmE), / ˌdɑːdəˈnɛlz / (BrE)
A narrow strait between the Aegean Sea and the Sea of Marmara, separating European Turkey from Asian Turkey.
strait between Aegean and Sea of Marmara

Examples

The strategic importance of the Dardanelles has made it the focus of many military campaigns.
Ships passed through the Dardanelles as they made their way to the Black Sea.
Control of the Dardanelles was crucial during World War I.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Bosporus - strait connecting the Black Sea and the Sea of Marmara

Etymology

From French Dardanelles, from Turkish 'Çanakkale Boğazı', from the ancient city of Dardania.

2. PROPN

/ ˌdɑrdəˈnɛlz / (AmE), / ˌdɑːdəˈnɛlz / (BrE)
a major World War I campaign (1915) in which British and French forces attempted, unsuccessfully, to secure a sea route to Russia by capturing the Dardanelles Strait, defeated by the Ottoman Turks.
WWI campaign to capture the Dardanelles Strait

Examples

The failure of the Dardanelles campaign had lasting effects on Allied strategy in World War I.
Many Australian and New Zealand soldiers fought bravely at the Dardanelles during the Gallipoli campaign.
Churchill's reputation suffered greatly after the disastrous Dardanelles operation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Gallipoli campaign - the associated land campaign during the attempt to capture the Dardanelles

Etymology

From the Turkish name 'Çanakkale Boğazı'; Dardanelles derives from the ancient city of Dardanos.
