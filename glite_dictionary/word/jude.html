Jude

1. PROPN

/ dʒuːd / (AmE), / dʒuːd / (BrE)
a book of the New Testament, addressed to Christians and attributed to Saint Jude.
New Testament book attributed to Saint Jude

Examples

She quoted from the Book of Jude during her sermon.
The letter of Jude is one of the shortest books in the New Testament.
Biblical scholars often debate the authorship of Jude.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Epistle - a letter, especially one of the New Testament letters

Etymology

From the name Jude, short form of Judas (Greek Ioudas, from Hebrew Yehudah).

2. PROPN

/ dʒud / (AmE), / dʒuːd / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) one of the Apostles, traditionally regarded as the brother of St. James and invoked by many as the patron saint of desperate cases.
New Testament Apostle, brother of St. James

Examples

Many Christians pray to Jude when facing seemingly impossible difficulties.
The feast day of Jude is celebrated on October 28th in the Western church.
He is known as Jude, the patron saint of lost causes.

Etymology

From Latin 'Iudas', from Greek 'Ioudas', from Hebrew 'Yehudah' (Judah).
