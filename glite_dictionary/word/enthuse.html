to enthuse

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈθuz / (AmE), / ɪnˈθjuːz / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel enthusiastic or excited about something
cause to feel enthusiasm

Examples

The speaker tried to enthuse the audience with stories of success and perseverance.
It was difficult to enthuse the team after so many setbacks.
The coach's enthusiasm can really enthuse the players before a big game.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to excite - to arouse strong feelings of enthusiasm or interest
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel uninterested or dull

Etymology

Formed from 'enthusiasm', modeled after the noun.

2. VERB

/ ɛnˈθuz / (AmE), / ɪnˈθjuːz / (BrE)
to speak or express oneself with enthusiasm; to say something excitedly
express with enthusiasm

Examples

He began to enthuse about his recent trip to Japan.
Teachers often enthuse their students by sharing personal stories.
She enthused over the new technology during the presentation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exclaim - to cry out suddenly, especially in surprise, anger, or pain

Etymology

From 'enthusiasm', derived from Greek 'enthousiasmos' (divine inspiration).

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈθuz / (AmE), / ɪnˈθjuːz / (BrE)
to make someone feel excited and eager, especially when addressing an audience
to excite or engage (someone, esp. an audience)

Examples

The speaker managed to enthuse the audience with her passionate delivery.
It is important for a teacher to enthuse students about learning new subjects.
He hopes his presentation will enthuse the audience and inspire action.

Synset Examples

He loves to enthuse his audience with stories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to excite - to cause strong feelings of enthusiasm or eagerness
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel uninterested or dull

Etymology

From Greek enthusiasmos (inspiration), from enthousiazein (to be inspired by a god).
