to scrounge

1. VERB

/ skraʊndʒ / (AmE), / skraʊndʒ / (BrE)
to search for or collect something, especially food or provisions, by foraging or making do with what is available, often in a resourceful or opportunistic way.
search for or collect food or provisions

Examples

During the harsh winter, the fox had to scrounge for food wherever it could find it.
After losing his job, he would scrounge meals from restaurant leftovers downtown.
The stray cats often scrounge around garbage bins looking for scraps.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forage - to search widely for food or provisions

Etymology

Origin unknown; possibly from dialectal English, related to 'scrunge' or 'scringe' (to squeeze or press).

2. VERB

/ skraʊndʒ / (AmE), / skraʊndʒ / (BrE)
to obtain or try to obtain something, usually by asking or persuading without offering anything in return; to cadge or wheedle
to obtain by cadging or wheedling

Examples

She managed to scrounge enough money for a train ticket home.
He always tries to scrounge free meals from his friends.
It's amazing how much food you can scrounge from the office kitchen.

Synset Examples

he is always shnorring cigarettes from his friends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cadge - to ask for or obtain something to which one is not strictly entitled
  • Degree Lower: to borrow - to take and use something belonging to someone else with the intention of returning it

Etymology

Originated in British military slang (early 20th century), possibly from dialectal 'scrunge' or related to 'scrounge' (to search thoroughly).
