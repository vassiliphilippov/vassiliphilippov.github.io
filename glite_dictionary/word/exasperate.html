to exasperate

1. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzæspəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɑːspəreɪt / (BrE)
(rare) To make a problem, situation, or feeling worse; to aggravate.
to make worse; to aggravate

Examples

The excessive noise only exasperated his headache.
Adding salt to the wound would exasperate the situation even further.
His careless remarks exasperated the ongoing conflict between the two teams.

Synset Examples

This drug aggravates the pain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to aggravate - to make a situation or condition worse

Etymology

From Latin exasperare (to make rough, irritate), from ex- (out, thoroughly) + asper (rough).

2. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzæspəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɑːspəreɪt / (BrE)
to make someone very annoyed, especially by repeatedly doing something that upsets them
to make someone extremely annoyed

Examples

The constant noise from the construction site began to exasperate the residents.
Her brother's refusal to listen only exasperated her further.
These delays are really exasperating everyone in the office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infuriate - to make someone extremely angry
  • Degree Lower: to irritate - to annoy someone slightly
  • Degree Higher: to enrage - to make someone extremely angry or furious

Etymology

From Latin exasperatus (made rough, irritated), past participle of exasperare.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01790725-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzæspəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɑːspəˌreɪt / (BrE)
to make someone very annoyed, especially by repeatedly doing something that upsets them
make extremely annoyed

Examples

His constant interruptions began to exasperate everyone in the meeting.
It can exasperate a teacher when students ignore instructions repeatedly.
She was exasperated by the endless delays and lack of progress.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to irritate - to make someone slightly angry or impatient
  • Antonym: to soothe - to make someone calmer or less angry

Etymology

From Latin exasperare (to make rough, irritate).
