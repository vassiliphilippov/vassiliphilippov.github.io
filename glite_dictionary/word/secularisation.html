secularisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛkjələraɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛkjʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the transfer of property or institutions from religious (especially church) to civil ownership or control
transfer from church to civil ownership

Examples

The secularisation of the monastery's lands was ordered by the new government.
During the Reformation, many regions saw the secularisation of church holdings.
Debate continues over the secularisation of education in the 19th century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secularization - the process of making secular, especially transfer from religious to civil ownership

Etymology

From Latin 'saecularis' (worldly) via French 'sécularisation'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛkjəlɚɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛkjʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or activity of removing religious influence or control from something, such as art, education, or society
removal of religious influence

Examples

The secularisation of education in the nineteenth century changed the way schools were run.
Many scholars argue that secularisation is a defining feature of modern Western societies.
The secularisation of public life meant that religious rituals were no longer part of government ceremonies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secularization - alternative spelling (US) for the same process
  • Synonym: secularism - belief in or advocacy of a secular society, separate from religion

Etymology

From Latin 'saecularis' (worldly) via Late Latin and Old French, combined with English '-ation'.
