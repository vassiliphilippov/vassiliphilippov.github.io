canal

1. NOUN

/ kəˈnæl / (AmE), / kəˈnæl / (BrE)
(astronomy) an indistinct linear marking on the surface of Mars, once believed to be channels but now considered an optical illusion.
faint linear Mars feature, now disproved

Examples

Early astronomers believed they could see canals crisscrossing the surface of Mars through their telescopes.
Many textbooks once depicted the Martian canals as evidence of intelligent life on the planet.
Modern imaging techniques have shown that the supposed Martian canals are just optical illusions.

2. NOUN

/ kəˈnæl / (AmE), / kəˈnæl / (BrE)
An artificial waterway constructed to allow the passage of boats or to convey water for irrigation.
artificial waterway for boats or irrigation

Examples

The city is famous for its historic canal system and boat tours.
Farmers use the irrigation canal to bring water to their fields.
The construction of the canal boosted trade in the region.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: channel - natural or artificial watercourse for navigation or drainage

Etymology

From Old French 'canal', from Latin 'canalis' (pipe, groove, channel).

3. NOUN

/ kəˈnæl / (AmE), / kəˈnæl / (BrE)
a tubular passage in the body, lined with epithelial cells, through which fluid or other substances are conveyed
tubular bodily passage
medical

Examples

The canal of the spinal cord is known as the central canal.
Damage to the ear canal can affect hearing significantly.
The dentist explained that the infection had spread into the root canal.

Synset Examples

the tear duct was obstructed
the alimentary canal
poison is released through a channel in the snake's fangs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: duct - a tube or channel in the body for conveying fluids
  • Synonym: channel - a passage in the body for fluids or other substances

Etymology

From Latin canalis (pipe, groove, channel).

4. VERB

/ kəˈnæl / (AmE), / kəˈnæl / (BrE)
To provide or supply (an area, especially a city) with a canal or canals.
to build a canal in (a place)

Examples

In the 19th century, several European cities were canalled to improve transportation and trade.
The government plans to canal the river through the heart of the city to boost tourism.
Engineers spent years canalling the region to prevent seasonal flooding.

Etymology

From French 'canal', from Latin 'canalis' (pipe, channel).
