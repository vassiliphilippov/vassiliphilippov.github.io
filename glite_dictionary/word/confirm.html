to confirm

1. VERB

/ kənˈfɜrm / (AmE), / kənˈfɜːm / (BrE)
to establish the truth or validity of something by providing additional evidence or facts
establish with new evidence

Examples

The witness's testimony confirmed the suspect's alibi.
New research confirms the effectiveness of the treatment.
I need one more source to confirm this information.

Synset Examples

his story confirmed my doubts
The evidence supports the defendant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to corroborate - to support or strengthen with additional evidence
  • Antonym: to refute - to prove that something is wrong or false

Etymology

From Latin confirmare, from con- (together) + firmare (to strengthen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00667804-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kənˈfɝːm / (AmE), / kənˈfɜːm / (BrE)
to administer the Christian rite of confirmation to (a person), admitting them as a full member of the Church
administer religious confirmation

Examples

The bishop will confirm all the candidates during Sunday's service.
She was confirmed into the Church at the age of thirteen.
Every spring, the church prepares to confirm a new group of young members.

Synset Examples

the children were confirmed in their mother's faith

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confirmation - Christian sacrament admitting someone to full church membership

Etymology

From Latin 'confirmare' (to make firm, strengthen), via Old French 'confirmer'; in this sense, relating to the Christian sacrament of confirmation.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02480026-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kənˈfɝːm / (AmE), / kənˈfɜːm / (BrE)
to make something firmer or stronger, especially a feeling, belief, or quality
make stronger or more certain

Examples

The experience helped to confirm her resolve to succeed.
The coach's words confirmed the team's determination to win.
His calm response confirmed their faith in his leadership.

Synset Examples

Confirm thy soul in self-control!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to strengthen - to make something stronger or more forceful

Etymology

From Latin confirmare (to strengthen, establish), from con- (together) + firmare (to make firm).
