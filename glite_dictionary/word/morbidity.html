morbidity

1. NOUN

/ mɔrˈbɪdəti / (AmE), / mɔːˈbɪdəti / (BrE)
The quality or condition of being unhealthy or harmful to health.
the state of being unhealthy or harmful

Examples

High rates of morbidity were observed in polluted urban areas.
Smoking is a major cause of increased morbidity worldwide.
The report highlights the morbidity associated with poor diet and lack of exercise.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: health - the state of being free from illness or injury
  • Synonym: mortality - the state of being subject to death

Etymology

From Latin morbiditas, from morbidus (sickly, diseased).

2. NOUN

/ mɔrˈbɪdəti / (AmE), / mɔːˈbɪdəti / (BrE)
the rate or proportion of disease occurrence in a particular population, often expressed as a percentage or ratio
rate of disease in a population
medical

Examples

The annual morbidity for influenza in the region was reported as 12 percent.
Doctors track the morbidity of certain diseases to monitor public health trends.
Improved sanitation has helped reduce the morbidity associated with waterborne illnesses.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mortality - the incidence of death in a population
  • Synonym: prevalence - the total number of cases of a disease in a population

Etymology

From Latin morbiditas (sickness), from morbus (disease).

3. NOUN

/ mɔːrˈbɪdəti / (AmE), / mɔːˈbɪdɪti / (BrE)
A state of mind characterized by excessive gloominess or preoccupation with unwholesome or disturbing subjects.
gloomy or unhealthy state of mind

Examples

Her poems are often marked by a subtle morbidity that unsettles the reader.
He spoke with a morbidity that made his friends worry about his mental health.
There is a certain morbidity to his fascination with tragic events.

Synset Examples

his fear of being alone verges on morbidity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melancholy - a deep, persistent sadness or gloom
  • Antonym: cheerfulness - a state of being noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

From Latin morbiditas (unwholesomeness), from morbus (disease).
