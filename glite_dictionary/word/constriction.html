constriction

1. NOUN

/ kənˈstrɪkʃən / (AmE), / kənˈstrɪkʃən / (BrE)
a narrowing of a passage or channel that reduces or obstructs flow, especially of liquid or air
narrowing that reduces flow in a channel

Examples

The blood vessel showed a severe constriction, leading to reduced circulation in the limb.
Engineers detected a constriction in the pipe, which limited the flow of water to the building.
Difficulty breathing can result from a constriction in the airways caused by swelling or mucus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: narrowing - a reduction in width or diameter
  • Degree Higher: blockage - an obstruction that blocks passage

Etymology

From Latin constrictio(n-), from constringere 'to draw together'.

2. NOUN

/ kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
a feeling of tightness or pressure in a part of the body, such as the chest or throat
tightness or pressure in the body

Examples

She noticed a strange constriction in her throat when she became anxious.
After running up the stairs, he complained of a constriction in his chest.
Panic attacks often produce a sensation of constriction in the body.

Synset Examples

emotion caused a constriction of his throat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tightness - the state of being tight or under tension, especially in the body

Etymology

From Latin 'constrictio' (a contracting or binding together).

3. NOUN

/ kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
the action or process of becoming narrower or tighter; a tight or narrow compression
tightening; narrowing

Examples

The snake killed its prey by constriction around its body.
A sudden constriction in the blood vessel can cause chest pain.
The feeling of constriction in my throat made it hard to breathe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compression - the act of pressing or squeezing something together
  • Antonym: dilation - the act of becoming wider or more open

Etymology

From Latin constrictio (a compressing), from constringere (to bind together).

4. NOUN

/ kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˈstrɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
the action or process of becoming narrower or tighter by being compressed or squeezed
the act of making something narrower or tighter

Examples

The snake kills its prey by constriction, tightening its coils with every breath the animal takes.
Because of the constriction in the pipe, water flow was reduced significantly.
He felt a sudden constriction in his chest and found it difficult to breathe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compression - the act of pressing or squeezing something together
  • Antonym: expansion - the act of becoming larger or more extensive

Etymology

From Latin constrictio (a binding together), from constringere (to bind together, constrict).
