Falconiformes

1. NOUN

/ fælˌkoʊnɪˈfɔrmiːz / (AmE), / falˌkəʊnɪˈfɔːmiːz / (BrE)
an order of chiefly diurnal birds of prey, including falcons, hawks, eagles, ospreys, caracaras, and some vultures, characterized by hooked beaks and strong talons
order of diurnal birds of prey

Examples

The Falconiformes include some of the most skilled hunters in the avian world.
Members of the order Falconiformes are known for their keen eyesight and sharp talons.
Conservation efforts have focused on several species within the Falconiformes due to declining populations.

Etymology

From New Latin, from Latin 'falco' (falcon) + '-formes' (having the form of).

2. NOUN

/ ˌfælkənəˈfɔːrmiːz / (AmE), / ˌfælkənɪˈfɔːmiːz / (BrE)
an order of chiefly diurnal birds of prey, including falcons, hawks, eagles, ospreys, caracaras, and vultures, characterized by hooked beaks and strong talons
order of predatory birds (falcons, eagles, etc.)

Examples

The Falconiformes are known for their exceptional vision and hunting skills.
Scientists debated the evolutionary origins of the Falconiformes for many years.
Some species within the Falconiformes are threatened due to habitat loss and pollution.

Etymology

From Latin 'falco' (falcon) + '-formes' (having the form of), used in zoological classification.
