to generalise

1. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛn(ə)rəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛnərəlaɪz / (BrE)
to adapt something to make it more accessible or widely understood by the general public; to bring into general or common use
make popular or widely accessible

Examples

The new textbook generalises complex scientific concepts for young readers.
Her goal was to generalise advanced computer skills for everyday users.
Several authors have tried to generalise the findings so they can benefit a broader audience.

Synset Examples

They popularized coffee in Washington State
Relativity Theory was vulgarized by these authors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to popularize - make something widely liked or accepted by the general public
  • Synonym: to vulgarize - make technical or complex ideas simpler and accessible to ordinary people

Etymology

From French généraliser, from général (general), with the '-ise' suffix.

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛn(ə)rəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make broad statements or conclusions that apply to a group or situation, often ignoring details or exceptions
make broad statements, overlook details
british

Examples

She tends to generalise when talking about people from different cultures.
It's risky to generalise based on a single experience.
We shouldn't generalise; every case is unique.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to generalize - US spelling of 'generalise'

Etymology

From French 'généraliser', from 'général' (general).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01024915-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛnərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛnərəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make a broad statement or form a general principle based on specific cases or limited evidence
make broad statement from specifics
british

Examples

It's dangerous to generalise about people based on their appearance.
After observing a few cases, the scientist began to generalise her findings.
We shouldn't generalise from one bad experience to all future situations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to generalize - alternate spelling used in American English

Etymology

From French 'généraliser', from 'général' (general).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01024429-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛn(ə)rəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛn(ə)r(ə)laɪz / (BrE)
(of a disease or infection) to become widespread throughout the body, rather than being confined to one area.
(of disease) spread throughout the body
medical british

Examples

If left untreated, the infection may generalise and become life-threatening.
Some rashes can generalise from a single spot to cover most of the skin.
Doctors are concerned when a localized illness starts to generalise in the patient.

Synset Examples

this kind of infection generalizes throughout the immune system

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to localise - (of a disease) to remain confined to a particular area of the body
