tax

1. VERB

/ tæks / (AmE), / tæks / (BrE)
to make heavy demands on someone's resources, patience, or abilities; to use to the limit
to make heavy demands on; to use to the limit

Examples

The complex project taxed all of her organizational skills.
His endless questions taxed my patience to the breaking point.
After the long hike, I felt completely taxed by the effort.

Synset Examples

you are taxing my patience

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strain - to make intense or excessive demands on
  • Synonym: overburden - to load with too great a burden
  • Antonym: to relieve - to reduce the burden of; to ease

Etymology

From Latin taxare (to assess, handle, censure), related to 'tax' as a levy, extended metaphorically to 'demand' or 'strain'.

2. VERB

/ tæks / (AmE), / tæks / (BrE)
to impose a tax on goods, income, or activities; to require payment of a government levy
impose a government levy

Examples

The government decided to tax luxury cars at a higher rate.
Groceries are not taxed in some states to ease the burden on families.
Many countries tax tobacco products to discourage their use.

Synset Examples

The State taxes alcohol heavily
Clothing is not taxed in our state

Related Terms

  • Antonym: untax - to remove a tax from

Etymology

From Latin 'taxare' (to evaluate or assess), from 'taxo' (I touch, handle, estimate).

5. NOUN

/ tæks / (AmE), / tæks / (BrE)
a compulsory charge imposed by a government on a person's income, property, or activities to raise revenue for public purposes
a compulsory government charge on income or property

Examples

Every citizen is required to pay a tax on their annual income.
The government increased the property tax to fund local schools.
Importing goods from abroad usually involves paying a customs tax.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: duty - a government charge, especially on imported goods
  • Synonym: fee - a payment made for a service or privilege
  • Antonym: subsidy - money paid by government to support an activity, the opposite of tax in some contexts

Etymology

From Latin taxare (to assess, to value), via Old French taxer.
