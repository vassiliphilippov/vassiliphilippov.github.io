inwardness

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪnwɚdnɪs / (AmE), / ˈɪnwədnɪs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being inward, internal, or existing within; the inner nature or essence of something
the quality of being inward or internal

Examples

The inwardness of the body's organs protects them from external harm.
Her poetry explores the inwardness of human experience and emotion.
Scientists study the inwardness of cells to better understand how the body functions.

Synset Examples

the inwardness of the body's organs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: internality - the condition of being internal
  • Antonym: outwardness - the quality of being outward or external

Etymology

From inward + -ness; modeled after Old English structures for forming abstract nouns.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnwɚdnəs / (AmE), / ˈɪnwədnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being concerned with one's own inner nature, especially in relation to ethical or ideological values; introspectiveness or self-reflection.
concern with inner self or values

Examples

The philosopher's inwardness led him to question the very nature of existence.
Her poetry expresses a deep sense of inwardness and spiritual searching.
Meditation can promote inwardness, helping people to reflect on their true values.

Synset Examples

Socrates' inwardness, integrity, and inquisitiveness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: introspection - the examination of one's own thoughts and feelings
  • Antonym: outwardness - concern with external matters rather than inner nature

Etymology

Formed from 'inward' + suffix '-ness', meaning state or quality.

focus on inner thoughts or values

Examples

The philosopher was known for her intense inwardness and constant self-reflection.
Many poets explore inwardness as a way to understand their deepest motivations.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

4. NOUN

/ ˈɪnwərdnɪs / (AmE), / ˈɪnwədnɪs / (BrE)
the most essential or central quality or meaning of something; the heart or core of an idea or experience
the essential core or heart of something

Examples

The philosopher's essay sought to reveal the inwardness of human virtue.
She felt she finally understood the inwardness of his message after much reflection.
Debates about policy often miss the inwardness of the underlying issues.

Synset Examples

the gist of the prosecutor's argument
the heart and soul of the Republican Party
the nub of the story

Related Terms

  • Synonym: essence - the intrinsic nature or indispensable quality of something
  • Synonym: core - the central or most important part of something
  • Antonym: surface - the outside part or uppermost layer of something, often contrasted with core

Etymology

From 'inward' plus the noun suffix '-ness', dating from the late Middle English period.
