swan

1. NOUN

/ swɑn / (AmE), / swɒn / (BrE)
A large, stately waterbird with a long neck and usually white feathers, known for its graceful appearance.
large, graceful waterbird

Examples

The elegant swan glided silently across the still lake.
A pair of swans built their nest near the riverbank each spring.
Tourists stopped to take photographs of the white swan stretching its wings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: goose - a large waterbird related to the swan, with a shorter neck
  • Synonym: duck - a smaller waterbird, also related to the swan

Etymology

Old English swan, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch zwaan and German Schwan.

2. VERB

/ swɑn / (AmE), / swɒn / (BrE)
to move or travel about in a relaxed, aimless, or leisurely manner, often with an air of grandeur or nonchalance
move or travel in a relaxed, grand, or aimless way

Examples

She loves to swan around the city, stopping at every café she fancies.
Tourists would swan through the gardens, taking in the beauty without haste.
He spent his gap year swanning across Europe with no fixed plans.

Synset Examples

Airplanes were swanning over the mountains

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wander - to move about without a fixed course or purpose
  • Degree Higher: strut - to walk with a proud, stiff, or affected gait

Etymology

From the noun 'swan,' alluding to the graceful motion of a swan on water.
