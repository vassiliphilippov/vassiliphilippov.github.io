stubborn

1. ADJ

/ ˈstʌbərn / (AmE), / ˈstʌbən / (BrE)
(of a thing) difficult to remove, deal with, or cure; not easily yielding to effort or treatment.
difficult to remove or treat

Examples

The stubborn stain on my shirt would not come out after several washes.
Doctors struggled to treat the stubborn infection that resisted antibiotics.
No matter what cleaner I used, the stubborn rust marks remained on the sink.

Synset Examples

a stubborn infection
a refractory case of acne
stubborn rust stains

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persistent - continuing to exist or endure over a prolonged period, especially despite attempts to resolve it
  • Synonym: refractory - resistant to a process or treatment, especially in medical or chemical contexts

Etymology

From Old French *estoborn*, of Germanic origin.

2. ADJ

/ ˈstʌbərn / (AmE), / ˈstʌb.ən / (BrE)
Unwilling to change one's attitude or position on something, often despite good reasons to do so; difficult to move, remove, or deal with.
unwilling to change or yield

Examples

She remained stubborn even after hearing everyone's advice.
A stubborn stain refused to come out of the shirt.
Despite the challenges, his stubborn determination kept him going.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obstinate - refusing to change one's mind; very stubborn
  • Antonym: flexible - willing to change or compromise
  • Synonym: rigid - not willing to change or adapt

Etymology

From Middle English 'stoburn', perhaps from Old French 'estoborn' (violent, strong-willed).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02334687-a
Synset POS: a
