John

1. PROPN

/ dʒɑn / (AmE), / dʒɒn / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) one of the twelve apostles of Jesus, traditionally regarded as the author of the fourth Gospel, three epistles, and the Book of Revelation.
apostle and author in the New Testament

Examples

Many scholars debate whether John was actually the author of the Book of Revelation.
The Gospel according to John offers a distinct perspective on the life of Jesus.
In Christian tradition, John is often described as the beloved disciple.

Etymology

From Hebrew יוֹחָנָן (Yochanan), meaning 'Yahweh is gracious,' via Latin Johannes and Greek Ioannes.

2. PROPN

/ dʒɑn / (AmE), / dʒɒn / (BrE)
the youngest son of Henry II, King of England from 1199 to 1216, notable for being forced to sign the Magna Carta in 1215
King of England (1199–1216), signatory of Magna Carta
american british

Examples

John ruled England from 1199 until his death in 1216.
Many historians debate the effectiveness of King John's leadership.
John is often remembered for signing the Magna Carta under pressure from the barons.

Etymology

From the Latin name Johannes, from Greek Iōannēs, from Hebrew Yochanan (meaning 'God is gracious').

3. PROPN

/ dʒɑn / (AmE), / dʒɒn / (BrE)
the fourth Gospel in the New Testament, traditionally attributed to St. John the Apostle
the fourth Gospel in the New Testament

Examples

Many theological discussions focus on the unique perspective found in John.
The Gospel according to John differs significantly from the Synoptic Gospels.
He read a passage from John during the church service.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Luke - the third Gospel in the New Testament

From the Latin 'Ioannes', from Greek 'Iōannēs', from Hebrew 'Yôḥānān' (God is gracious).
