presto

1. ADJ

/ ˈprɛstoʊ / (AmE), / ˈprɛstəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) at a very fast tempo.
very fast (musical tempo)

Examples

The final movement of the symphony is marked presto, challenging even the most skilled musicians.
During rehearsal, the conductor demanded an even faster presto from the string section.
Mozart often composed scherzos with presto tempo indications for dramatic effect.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: allegro - fast, lively tempo in music
  • Degree Lower: vivace - lively and fast tempo in music

Etymology

From Italian 'presto' meaning 'quick' or 'fast'.

2. ADV

/ ˈprɛstoʊ / (AmE), / ˈprɛstəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) at a very fast tempo; faster than allegro.
very fast musical tempo

Examples

The final movement is marked presto, giving it a lively and energetic feel.
She played the passage presto, astonishing the audience with her speed.
The orchestra transitioned from allegro to presto in the last section.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: allegro - at a fast, lively tempo (but slower than presto)
  • Degree Lower: vivace - in a lively and brisk manner (slower than presto)

Etymology

From Italian 'presto' meaning 'quickly, promptly'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00062437-r
Synset POS: r

3. ADV

/ ˈprɛs.toʊ / (AmE), / ˈprɛs.təʊ / (BrE)
used to indicate that something happens suddenly or instantly, especially in a magical or dramatic way.
suddenly; by magic

Examples

The magician waved his wand and, presto, the rabbit appeared out of the hat.
She pressed the button and, presto, the lights came back on.
You add the water, stir for ten seconds, and presto—your meal is ready!

Synset Examples

Presto! begone! 'tis here again

Related Terms

  • Synonym: suddenly - quickly and unexpectedly
  • Antonym: gradually - slowly or in small steps over time

Etymology

From Italian presto (quick, quickly), from Latin praestus (ready).

Source: WordNet
