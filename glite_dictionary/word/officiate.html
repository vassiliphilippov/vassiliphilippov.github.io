to officiate

1. VERB

/ əˈfɪʃiˌeɪt / (AmE), / əˈfɪʃieɪt / (BrE)
to perform the official duties or responsibilities of a particular position, office, or function
perform official duties or responsibilities

Examples

The principal will officiate at the graduation ceremony this year.
She was asked to officiate as the interim manager during his absence.
Despite her inexperience, she officiated with confidence at the board meeting.

Synset Examples

His wife officiated as his private secretary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preside - to be in a position of authority at a meeting or event

Etymology

From Medieval Latin officiāre (to perform the duty of an office), from Latin officium (duty, service).

2. VERB

/ əˈfɪʃiˌeɪt / (AmE), / əˈfɪʃieɪt / (BrE)
to perform an official duty at a ceremony, especially as a religious or formal authority
perform a formal or religious ceremony

Examples

The local pastor will officiate at their wedding ceremony next month.
She was asked to officiate at her niece’s baptism.
The judge will officiate over the signing of the marriage certificate.

Synset Examples

Who officiated at your wedding?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preside - to be in charge of a formal event or meeting

Etymology

From Medieval Latin officiatus, past participle of officiari (to perform a duty), from Latin officium (duty).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02387229-v
Synset POS: v
