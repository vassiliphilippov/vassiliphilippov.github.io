watercourse

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɔːtɚˌkɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːtəkɔːs / (BrE)
a natural channel along which water flows, especially a river, stream, or brook
natural channel of flowing water

Examples

The construction project must avoid disrupting the watercourse that runs through the valley.
Many species rely on the watercourse for drinking water and habitat.
Heavy rain caused the watercourse to overflow its banks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stream - small narrow river or brook
  • Degree Higher: river - large natural flowing watercourse

Etymology

From Middle English water cours, influenced by Old French cours (course).

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɔːtɚˌkɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːtəkɔːs / (BrE)
a channel through which water flows, such as a stream, river, or artificial conduit
channel or conduit for flowing water

Examples

The new park was designed around an existing watercourse that winds through the area.
Heavy rains caused the watercourse to overflow its banks and flood nearby fields.
Fish have returned to the clean watercourse after years of pollution control.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stream - a small, narrow river
  • Synonym: river - a large natural stream of water
  • Synonym: canal - an artificial watercourse for navigation or irrigation

Etymology

From Old English 'wætercors', a compound of 'water' and 'course'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɔt̬ɚˌkɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːtəkɔːs / (BrE)
A natural or artificial channel through which water flows, such as a river, stream, or canal.
natural or artificial water channel

Examples

The engineers built a dam to control the flow of the watercourse.
During the flood, the watercourse overflowed its banks and inundated the fields.
Birds gathered along the watercourse to drink and bathe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: channel - a passage for water or other fluids to flow
  • Synonym: river - a large natural watercourse

Etymology

From Middle English 'water cours', from Old French 'cours' (course) and 'water'.
