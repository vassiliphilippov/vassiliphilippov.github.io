canker

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæŋkər / (AmE), / ˈkaŋkə / (BrE)
A small, painful ulcer or sore in the mouth or on the lips.
mouth or lip ulcer
medical

Examples

My dentist explained that this painful canker on my tongue should heal within a week.
She often gets a canker inside her lower lip when she is stressed.
The canker made it difficult for him to enjoy spicy foods.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ulcer - an open sore on the skin or mucous membrane

Etymology

From Old English 'cancer', from Latin 'cancer' (crab, ulcerous sore), influenced by Old North French 'cancre'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæŋkər / (AmE), / ˈkæŋkə / (BrE)
A fungal disease of woody plants that causes localized damage to the bark, often resulting in dead areas and potential weakening of branches or stems.
plant fungal disease causing bark damage

Examples

Fruit trees are often susceptible to canker if the weather is damp and warm.
The gardener pruned away the branches infected with canker.
If left untreated, canker can kill entire sections of a tree.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blight - a plant disease caused by fungi or bacteria resulting in wilting, blight, or death

Etymology

From Old English *cancer* (spreading sore), related to Latin *cancer* (crab, ulcer, cancer).

3. VERB

/ ˈkæŋkɚ / (AmE), / ˈkaŋkə / (BrE)
To infect or corrupt with a canker; to cause to become diseased or decayed, especially morally or physically.
to infect or corrupt with a canker

Examples

The old tree had been cankered by disease over many seasons.
Unchecked malice can canker even the noblest of hearts.
If wounds are not properly cleaned, they can quickly canker and spread infection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrupt - to make morally corrupt or rotten

Etymology

From Old English 'cancer' (ulcer, malignant sore), from Latin 'cancer' (crab; in later Latin, a type of sore).
