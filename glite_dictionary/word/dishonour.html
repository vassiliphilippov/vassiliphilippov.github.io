dishonour

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈɑːnər / (AmE), / dɪsˈɒnə / (BrE)
A state of shame or disgrace; loss of respect or reputation.
state of shame or disgrace
british

Examples

He lived the rest of his life in dishonour after the scandal was revealed.
The knight refused to accept dishonour, even in defeat.
Her actions brought dishonour to her entire family.

Synset Examples

he was resigned to a life of dishonor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - loss of reputation or respect
  • Antonym: honour - a state of high respect or esteem

Etymology

From Old French deshonor, from Latin dishonor (disgrace).

2. NOUN

/ dɪsˈɑnər / (AmE), / dɪsˈɒnə / (BrE)
Loss of honor or respect; a state of lacking integrity or good reputation.
loss of honor or integrity
british

Examples

He brought dishonour to his family by being involved in the scandal.
The knight feared dishonour more than death itself.
Her actions resulted in dishonour and the loss of her good reputation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shame - a painful feeling of humiliation or distress caused by consciousness of wrong or foolish behavior
  • Antonym: honour - high respect; great esteem

Etymology

From Old French 'deshonor', from Latin 'dishonor', meaning loss of honor.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04881134-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈɑnər / (AmE), / dɪsˈɒnə / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to lose respect or bring shame upon; to disgrace
cause loss of respect; disgrace

Examples

His actions dishonoured the entire team in front of the spectators.
The politician feared he would dishonour his oath of office by breaking his promises.
They believe cheating would dishonour their school's reputation.

Synset Examples

he dishonored his family by committing a serious crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - to bring shame or discredit on
  • Antonym: honour - to show respect or bring credit to

Etymology

From Old French *deshonorer*, from Latin *dishonorare* (to disgrace).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02552922-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪsˈɑnər / (AmE), / dɪsˈɒnə / (BrE)
(of a financial instrument) to refuse to accept or pay when due, especially in reference to a check or draft.
refuse payment or acceptance (esp. of checks)

Examples

The bank decided to dishonour the check due to insufficient funds.
If your account lacks funds, your payment may be dishonoured by the bank.
Several drafts were dishonoured after the company declared bankruptcy.

Synset Examples

dishonor checks and drafts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bounce - to be refused by a bank because of lack of funds (of a check)

Etymology

From Old French deshonorer, from Latin dishonorare (to disgrace).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
