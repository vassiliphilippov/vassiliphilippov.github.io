throb

1. NOUN

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
a deep, regular, pulsing sensation, especially referring to pain
deep, pulsing pain

Examples

The dull throb in her head refused to go away.
He felt a sharp throb of pain each time he moved his arm.
After running the marathon, a persistent throb ached in his legs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ache - a continuous or dull pain
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14356739-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
a strong, regular beat or pulsation, especially as felt in the body or heard as a sound; a rhythmic vibration or sensation
a strong, regular pulsation or vibration

Examples

She felt a sharp throb in her wrist after the accident.
The only sound in the dark room was the slow throb of the engine.
A dull throb pulsed in his temples as the headache worsened.

Synset Examples

he felt a throbbing in his head

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulsation - a throbbing or rhythmic beating
  • Antonym: ache - a continuous or dull pain (contrasts with throbbing)
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07416326-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
to tremble or shake convulsively, especially as a result of strong emotion such as fear or excitement
tremble with strong emotion

Examples

Her hands began to throb with fear as she stood before the crowd.
He could feel his heart throb in his chest from the excitement.
The child's body throbbed uncontrollably after the sudden fright.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tremble - to shake involuntarily, typically as a result of anxiety, excitement, or frailty
  • Synonym: quiver - to shake with slight, rapid motion

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, reflecting the sound or movement of a throbbing pulse.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01892939-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
to beat or pulsate with a strong, regular rhythm, especially as felt in the body or an object
beat or pulse rhythmically, often strongly

Examples

After running up the stairs, he could feel his heart throb in his chest.
The temple began to throb painfully after I bumped my head.
The engine made the floor beneath our feet throb with its power.

Synset Examples

The baby's heart was pulsating again after the surgeon massaged it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pulsate - to expand and contract rhythmically; to throb
  • Degree Lower: flutter - to move with a light, quick, irregular motion (weaker than throb)

Etymology

Old English 'thrafian', meaning 'to rage', related to Old Norse 'þrefa' (to quarrel) and Middle Dutch 'draven' (to shuffle).

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
(of a part of the body or a machine) to pulsate or beat strongly or with a regular rhythm, often with pain or strong emotion.
pulsate or beat with force or rhythm

Examples

My temples throb whenever I get a headache.
He could feel his heart throb with excitement as he opened the letter.
After running up the hill, her legs began to throb painfully.

Synset Examples

my head is throbbing
Her heart was throbbing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pulsate - to expand and contract rhythmically; to beat or throb
  • Synonym: pound - to beat repeatedly or with force, often with a regular rhythm

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, first recorded in the 15th century.
