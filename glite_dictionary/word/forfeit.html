forfeit

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔr.fɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɔː.fɪt / (BrE)
something lost or given up as a penalty for a fault, mistake, or breach of rules
penalty involving loss or giving up something

Examples

If you break the rule, you must pay a forfeit to the group.
The company faces a heavy forfeit if they miss the shipping deadline.
His recklessness resulted in the forfeit of his security deposit.

Synset Examples

the contract specified forfeits if the work was not completed on time

Related Terms

  • Synonym: penalty - a punishment imposed for breaking a law, rule, or contract

Etymology

From Old French *forfait*, past participle of *forfaire* 'to transgress'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɔr.fɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɔː.fɪt / (BrE)
something that is lost, surrendered, or given up as a penalty for wrongdoing or failure to fulfill an obligation
something lost as a penalty

Examples

The team paid a heavy forfeit for breaking the tournament rules.
Failure to submit the report on time may result in the forfeit of your deposit.
His license became a forfeit after repeated violations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: penalty - a punishment imposed for breaking a law, rule, or contract
  • Antonym: reward - something given in recognition of effort or achievement; opposite of a penalty

Etymology

From Old French *forfait* (crime, transgression), past participle of *forfaire* (to transgress).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɔːrfɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɔːfɪt / (BrE)
the loss of a right, property, or privilege as a penalty for wrongdoing or failure to fulfill an obligation
loss as a penalty for wrongdoing

Examples

If you are late to the tournament, your forfeit will result in automatic disqualification.
The company had to pay a heavy forfeit for breaching the contract.
He accepted the forfeit and handed over his driver’s license as required.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: penalty - a punishment imposed for breaking a law, rule, or contract

Etymology

From Old French *forfait* (transgression), past participle of *forfaire* (to commit a crime), from Medieval Latin *forisfacere* (to transgress).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00206740-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈfɔr.fɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɔː.fɪt / (BrE)
to lose or be deprived of something, especially as a penalty for wrongdoing or as a consequence of breaking a rule or law
lose as a penalty or consequence

Examples

If a player is caught cheating, they may forfeit the game.
He had to forfeit his driver's license after repeated offenses.
By arriving late, you forfeit your chance to take the test.

Synset Examples

you've forfeited your right to name your successor
forfeited property

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to lose - to be deprived of or cease to have or retain something

Etymology

From Old French *forfait* (forfeited), from *forfaire* (to transgress), from Latin *foris* (outside) and *facere* (to do).

Source: WordNet

5. ADJ

/ ˈfɔːrfɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɔːfɪt / (BrE)
(of property or rights) surrendered or taken away as a penalty for wrongdoing or breach of rules.
lost or given up as a penalty

Examples

The athlete was stripped of his forfeit medal after the doping scandal.
Any forfeit funds will be transferred to the state's general budget.
The forfeit property was auctioned to the public by the authorities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confiscated - taken away by authority as a penalty
  • Synonym: penalized - subject to a penalty or punishment

Etymology

From Old French 'forfait' (crime, transgression), past participle of 'forfaire' (to commit a crime), from Latin 'foris' (outside) + 'facere' (to do).
