to unleash

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈliʃ / (AmE), / ʌnˈliːʃ / (BrE)
To suddenly release a strong force, feeling, or action that was previously held back or controlled.
to release suddenly (emotion, force)

Examples

He could no longer control his temper and finally unleashed his anger.
The new policy unleashed a wave of protests across the city.
When the gate opened, the dog was unleashed and ran freely in the park.

Synset Examples

unleash one's anger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: release - to set free, let go
  • Antonym: to restrain - to hold back, control

Etymology

From un- (prefix meaning 'not') + leash, originally referring to letting a dog off the leash.

2. VERB

/ ʌnˈliʃ / (AmE), / ʌnˈliːʃ / (BrE)
to release from a leash or restraint; to set free, especially suddenly or forcefully
to release from restraint

Examples

The owner decided to unleash his dog when they reached the open field.
As soon as the ceremony began, they unleashed dozens of doves into the sky.
She waited for the right moment to unleash her creative ideas on the project.

Synset Examples

unleash the dogs in the park

Related Terms

  • Synonym: release - to set free from confinement or restraint

Etymology

Formed from prefix un- (reverse action) + leash (Middle English *leesche*, from Old French *laiche*, a strap for leading animals).

3. VERB

/ ʌnˈliʃ / (AmE), / ʌnˈliːʃ / (BrE)
to release or set free from restraint or control, often causing something powerful to happen
release from restraint or control

Examples

The new policy may unleash a wave of innovation across the industry.
She was careful not to unleash her anger during the meeting.
Heavy rainfall can unleash dangerous floods in the region.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: release - to set free or allow to escape
  • Antonym: to restrain - to hold back or keep under control

From un- (not, opposite of) + leash (Middle English *lesche*, from Old French *laissier*, to let loose).
