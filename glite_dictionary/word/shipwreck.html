shipwreck

1. VERB

/ ˈʃɪpˌrɛk / (AmE), / ˈʃɪpˌrɛk / (BrE)
(of an enterprise or plan) to suffer complete failure or ruin.
to suffer complete failure

Examples

Their business shipwrecked after the sudden loss of funding.
The project shipwrecked due to poor planning and lack of communication.
Many promising careers have shipwrecked on the rocks of unrealistic ambition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fail - to be unsuccessful in achieving a goal

Etymology

From Old English 'scipwræc' (wreck of a ship). Extended metaphorically to enterprises and plans.

2. VERB

/ ˈʃɪpˌrɛk / (AmE), / ˈʃɪprɛk / (BrE)
(of a ship or its passengers) to be destroyed, lost, or stranded as a result of a shipwreck.
to suffer or cause a shipwreck

Examples

Many sailors have been shipwrecked on this treacherous coastline over the centuries.
They feared they would shipwreck before reaching the island in the storm.
After the boat capsized, the family was shipwrecked and stranded for several days.

Synset Examples

They were shipwrecked in one of the mysteries at sea

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wreck - to destroy or severely damage something

Etymology

From 'ship' + 'wreck', first attested as a verb in the 16th century.

4. NOUN

/ ˈʃɪpˌrɛk / (AmE), / ˈʃɪp.rɛk / (BrE)
a ship that has been destroyed or badly damaged, often by sinking or running aground, or any part of such a ship
a wrecked or destroyed ship

Examples

Divers explored the remains of the shipwreck lying on the ocean floor.
Many legends have been inspired by the mysterious treasures found in a shipwreck.
During the storm, the coast guard spotted a shipwreck near the rocky shore.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wreck - something that has been badly damaged or destroyed

Etymology

From ship + wreck; 'wreck' from Old English wrecan (to drive, exile) and Old Norse reka (to drive, push).
