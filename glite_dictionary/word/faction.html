faction

1. NOUN

/ ˈfækʃən / (AmE), / ˈfækʃən / (BrE)
A small organized group within a larger one, especially one that opposes the main group or has dissenting views.
dissenting subgroup within a larger group

Examples

The political party was divided by a rival faction that opposed the leader's policies.
During the meeting, a faction of students demanded changes to school rules.
Power struggles between factions often lead to instability in organizations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clique - a small, exclusive group of people
  • Synonym: party - an organized political group
  • Antonym: unity - the state of being united or joined as a whole

Etymology

From Latin 'factio' (a making, doing, party, faction), from 'facere' (to do, make).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfæk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˈfæk.ʃən / (BrE)
a small, organized group within a larger one, especially one that seeks power or influence and may act secretly or with intrigue
a group seeking power within a larger organization

Examples

The political party was divided into rival factions that often undermined each other.
Rumors spread quickly about a faction plotting to take control of the company.
Within the student council, a faction was secretly organizing resistance against the president's plans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clique - a small, exclusive group of people
  • Synonym: cabal - a secret political clique or faction
  • Antonym: unity - the state of being united or joined as a whole

Etymology

From Latin 'factio' (a group of people acting together), from 'facere' (to do, to make).

Synset POS: n
