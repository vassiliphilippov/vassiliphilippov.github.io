leverage

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvərɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈliːvərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the use of borrowed capital in order to increase the potential return of an investment, while also increasing risk
use of borrowed money to invest

Examples

Many companies increased their leverage to boost profits during the bull market.
High leverage can lead to significant losses if investments perform poorly.
The fund manager warned that excessive leverage makes the portfolio more volatile.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gearing - use of borrowed funds in business or investments

Etymology

From Old French *levier*, from lever (to raise); in finance, by analogy with mechanical leverage.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01101851-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈlɛv.ə.rɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈliː.vər.ɪdʒ / (BrE)
to use something that one already has, such as funds or resources, to maximum advantage, especially to achieve a desired result or to increase return; often used in business or finance.
use resources to maximum advantage

Examples

Many companies leverage their assets to finance new projects.
She plans to leverage her professional connections to grow the business.
The startup leveraged existing technology to create an innovative product.

Synset Examples

leverage the money that is already available

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exploit - to make full use of something for benefit
  • Synonym: to utilize - to make practical and effective use of something

Etymology

From lever + -age, originally referring to mechanical advantage gained by a lever; later extended to business and finance.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02364838-v
Synset POS: v

use something to advantage

We must leverage every available resource to achieve our goals.

Related Terms

Etymology

4. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvərɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈliːvərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the mechanical advantage gained by using a lever
mechanical advantage with a lever

Examples

Using a longer handle increases the leverage you have to lift a heavy object.
The carpenter used the leverage of the crowbar to pry open the crate.
By positioning the fulcrum closer to the load, you gain more leverage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mechanical advantage - the factor by which a mechanism multiplies the force put into it

Etymology

From lever + -age, dating from the mid-19th century.

5. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvərɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈliːvərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the power or ability to influence people, events, or decisions; strategic advantage
power or strategic advantage

Examples

The company used its financial leverage to negotiate better contract terms.
Having experience in both fields gave her significant leverage in the job market.
The organization gained political leverage after its successful campaign.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: influence - the capacity to have an effect on the character, development, or behavior of someone or something
  • Synonym: advantage - a condition or circumstance that puts one in a favorable or superior position
  • Antonym: disadvantage - an unfavorable circumstance or condition that reduces the chances of success or effectiveness

Etymology

From lever + -age, originally referring to mechanical leverage; sense of strategic advantage dates from the 19th century.
