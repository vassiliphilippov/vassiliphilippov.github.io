to resist

1. VERB

/ rɪˈzɪst / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪst / (BrE)
to remain unaffected by, or to withstand the effect or force of something
withstand the force or effect of something

Examples

These materials are designed to resist extreme temperatures and harsh conditions.
The walls were built strong enough to resist even the fiercest storms.
Modern fabrics can resist stains much more effectively than older materials.

Synset Examples

The trees resisted her
stand the test of time
The mountain climbers had to fend against the ice and snow

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to succumb - to give way to force or pressure
  • Synonym: to endure - to suffer something difficult or painful patiently

Etymology

From Latin 'resistere' (to withstand, stand back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01118334-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈzɪst / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪst / (BrE)
to try to prevent by action or argument; to refuse to accept or be changed by something
oppose or withstand

Examples

The community decided to resist any attempt to close their local library.
She could not resist the urge to check her phone during the meeting.
Despite heavy pressure, the council resisted changing its environmental policy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oppose - to actively resist or work against something
  • Antonym: yield - to give way to arguments, demands, or pressure

Etymology

From Latin resistere (to stand back, oppose).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈzɪst / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪst / (BrE)
(medicine) For a person's immune system to oppose and potentially destroy introduced foreign tissue or an organ transplant.
oppose foreign tissue or transplant immunologically
medical

Examples

After the transplant, her immune system began to resist the new heart tissue.
Doctors monitor patients closely to see if their bodies will resist the transplanted organ.
Immunosuppressive drugs are used to prevent the body from resisting foreign tissues.

Synset Examples

His body rejected the liver of the donor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reject - (medicine) For the body to attack and fail to accept a transplanted organ or tissue

Etymology

From Latin resistere (to withstand, oppose).

4. VERB

/ rɪˈzɪst / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪst / (BrE)
(of something) to elude or remain impervious to explanation, understanding, or description, especially in a way that is difficult to grasp or baffling.
elude or baffle explanation or description

Examples

The complex pattern resists easy explanation by scientists.
His motives resist all attempts at rational analysis.
The beauty of the landscape resists adequate description in words.

Synset Examples

This behavior defies explanation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defy - to resist or withstand openly or successfully, often used of things which are difficult to explain or describe

Etymology

From Latin resistere (to stand back, withstand).

5. VERB

/ rɪˈzɪst / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪst / (BrE)
to refuse to accept or comply with something; to oppose or withstand authority, rules, or change
refuse to comply; oppose

Examples

She decided to resist the new company policy despite pressure from management.
Protesters gathered to resist the government's controversial decision.
He tried to resist arrest, but the police quickly restrained him.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to submit - to yield or comply with a request or authority
  • Synonym: to defy - to openly resist or refuse to obey

Etymology

From Latin resistere (to withstand, oppose), from re- (again) + sistere (to cause to stand).
