picket

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɪkət / (AmE), / ˈpɪkɪt / (BrE)
a form of military punishment formerly used by the British, in which a soldier was forced to stand on one foot on a pointed stake
historical British military punishment
archaic british

Examples

The soldier was sentenced to the picket for his insubordination.
Historical records describe the picket as a cruel and painful punishment.
Few punishments were as dreaded as the picket in the British army of the 17th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɪkɪt / (BrE)
A group of soldiers stationed ahead of a main force to guard against a surprise attack.
military guard detachment

Examples

The commander sent out a picket to watch for enemy movement at dawn.
A picket was stationed at the edge of the camp to guard against surprises.
During the night, a small picket patrolled the perimeter to ensure safety.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sentry - a soldier stationed to keep guard

Etymology

From French *piquet*, diminutive of *pic* (pike), originally referring to soldiers armed with pikes.

4. NOUN

/ ˈpɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɪkɪt / (BrE)
a person who is stationed outside a place of work as part of a protest, especially by a labor union, to dissuade or inform others entering the workplace
a protester outside a workplace

Examples

A group of pickets stood outside the factory entrance, holding signs and chanting slogans.
During the strike, each picket took turns monitoring the gate to talk to arriving workers.
The union instructed its members not to cross the picket line until negotiations resumed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strike - a period of time when workers stop working as a form of protest
  • Antonym: scab - a worker who refuses to join a strike or continues to work during one

Etymology

Originally from French *piquet* (pointed stake); sense of 'protester' developed from idea of stationed guard.
