takeout

1. NOUN

/ ˈteɪkˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈteɪk.aʊt / (BrE)
(in bridge) a bid made with the intention of prompting one's partner to bid another suit.
in bridge, a bid prompting partner to change suit

Examples

His takeout bid signaled that he wanted his partner to switch suits.
When you have a strong hand but no support for your partner's suit, a takeout is a good strategy.
She explained that a takeout shows support for the unbid suits in bridge.

2. ADJ

/ ˈteɪkˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈteɪkaʊt / (BrE)
(of food or drink) intended to be taken away from the place where it is bought and eaten elsewhere.
food to be eaten off the premises
american

Examples

We ordered takeout pizza for dinner after a long day at work.
The restaurant has a special takeout menu available for busy customers.
She picked up some takeout coffee on her way to the office.

Synset Examples

takeout pizza
the takeout counter
‘take-away’ is chiefly British

Related Terms

  • Synonym: takeaway - food to be taken and eaten elsewhere; chiefly British term

Etymology

Formed from the phrasal verb 'take out'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈteɪkˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈteɪkˌaʊt / (BrE)
food that is prepared and sold by a restaurant or store to be eaten elsewhere
prepared food to eat elsewhere
american

Examples

We decided to get takeout instead of cooking dinner at home.
She brought some Chinese takeout to the office for lunch.
After a long day, ordering takeout is the easiest option.

Synset Examples

in England they call takeout food ‘takeaway’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: takeaway - food bought at a restaurant or shop to be eaten elsewhere (British English)

Etymology

From the verb 'take out', American English sense dating from the mid-20th century.
