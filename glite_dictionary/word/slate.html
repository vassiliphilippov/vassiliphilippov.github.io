slate

1. NOUN

/ sleɪt / (AmE), / sleɪt / (BrE)
a fine-grained, layered metamorphic rock that splits easily into thin, smooth sheets, often used for roofing or writing surfaces
fine-grained metamorphic rock

Examples

The roof was covered in dark slate that contrasted with the white walls.
She wrote the calculation on a small slate before wiping it clean.
The region is known for its rich deposits of slate, used for both construction and artistic purposes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: schist - a coarse-grained metamorphic rock with a foliated structure

Etymology

From Old French *esclate* (splinter, sliver), related to Old English *scealu* (shell, husk).

2. NOUN

/ sleɪt / (AmE), / sleɪt / (BrE)
(historical) A small, thin tablet of slate formerly used for writing on, especially by schoolchildren.
writing tablet made of slate
archaic

Examples

In Victorian classrooms, children practiced arithmetic on a slate instead of on paper.
She wiped the chalk from her slate before starting the next lesson.
The museum displays an old school slate alongside quill pens and ink bottles.

Etymology

From Old French esclate (splinter, sliver), related to Middle Dutch or Old High German scalzan (to split).

3. NOUN

/ sleɪt / (AmE), / sleɪt / (BrE)
A fine-grained, flat metamorphic rock that splits easily into thin, smooth layers and is commonly used for roofing or writing surfaces.
fine-grained rock used for roofing

Examples

The cottage was roofed with dark slate to match the surrounding landscape.
They chose slate for the house because it is both durable and attractive.
Many old buildings have slate tiles that have lasted for over a century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tile - a thin slab used to cover roofs, floors, or walls
  • Synonym: shingle - thin, flat piece of material used for roofing

Etymology

From Old French *esclate* (a splinter, sliver), related to Old English *slittan* (to split).
