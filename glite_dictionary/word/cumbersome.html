cumbersome

1. ADJ

/ ˈkʌmbərsəm / (AmE), / ˈkʌmbəsəm / (BrE)
large or heavy and therefore difficult to carry, handle, or use; unwieldy.
hard to handle due to size or weight

Examples

The old printer was too cumbersome to fit on his small desk.
She found the suitcases cumbersome to carry through the crowded airport.
The procedure was so cumbersome that they decided to update their entire system.

Synset Examples

a cumbersome piece of machinery
cumbrous protective clothing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unwieldy - difficult to move or use because of size, shape, or weight

Etymology

From Middle English cumber (to hinder) + -some (forming adjectives).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkʌm.bər.səm / (AmE), / ˈkʌm.bə.səm / (BrE)
(of a style or expression) lacking grace or elegance; awkward or unwieldy.
awkward or unwieldy in style or expression

Examples

The report was written in a cumbersome style that made it difficult to read.
His explanation became so cumbersome that the audience quickly lost interest.
Academic writers should avoid cumbersome sentences that obscure their main points.

Synset Examples

an awkward prose style
a clumsy apology
his cumbersome writing style
if the rumor is true, can anything be more inept than to repeat it now?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: awkward - lacking grace or smoothness
  • Synonym: clumsy - lacking skill or finesse; awkward
  • Antonym: graceful - showing elegance or smoothness

Etymology

From Middle English cumbrus + -some, influenced by Old French encombrer (to obstruct, hinder).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01004599-s
Synset POS: s
