revolt

1. VERB

/ rɪˈvoʊlt / (AmE), / rɪˈvəʊlt / (BrE)
(of a thing or action) to cause someone to feel intense disgust or distaste.
to cause disgust or distaste

Examples

The thought of eating snails revolts her.
Many people are revolted by graphic images of violence.
It revolts me to see animals being mistreated.

Synset Examples

This spoilt food disgusts me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgust - to cause a strong feeling of dislike or disapproval
  • Synonym: repulse - to cause someone to feel intense aversion or disgust

Etymology

From Latin 'revolvere' (to roll back) via Old French 'revolter' (to rebel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02199359-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈvoʊlt / (AmE), / rɪˈvəʊlt / (BrE)
to rise up in rebellion against authority, especially a government or ruler
rebel against authority or government

Examples

When the king raised taxes, the peasants decided to revolt against his rule.
The soldiers refused to obey orders and revolted overnight, taking control of the base.
Entire regions threatened to revolt if their needs were ignored any longer.

Synset Examples

The people revolted when bread prices tripled again

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebel - to resist or fight against authority or control
  • Antonym: to submit - to accept or yield to an authority or superior force

Etymology

From Latin 'revolvere' (to roll back), via Old French 'revolter' (to rebel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02589865-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈvoʊlt / (AmE), / rɪˈvəʊlt / (BrE)
an organized attempt to oppose and remove the authority of a government or ruler, often using force
organized rebellion against authority

Examples

The government struggled to suppress the revolt that spread through the region.
The revolt finally ended after months of fierce fighting and negotiation.
Many were imprisoned for participating in the revolt against the monarchy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebellion - an act of violent or open resistance to an established government or ruler
  • Synonym: uprising - an act of resistance or revolt, usually by a group against authority
  • Antonym: submission - the action of accepting or yielding to a superior force or authority

Etymology

From French 'révolte', from Italian 'rivolta', from Latin 'revolvere' (to turn back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00964240-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ rɪˈvoʊlt / (AmE), / rɪˈvəʊlt / (BrE)
to make someone feel intense disgust or moral aversion; to offend or nauseate
cause disgust or moral aversion

Examples

The graphic scenes in the film revolted the audience.
The idea of cruelty to animals revolts him.
She was revolted by the blatant corruption in the organization.

Synset Examples

The pornographic pictures sickened us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgust - to make someone feel strong dislike or revulsion
  • Antonym: appeal - to attract or please someone

Etymology

From Latin 'revolvere' (to roll back).
