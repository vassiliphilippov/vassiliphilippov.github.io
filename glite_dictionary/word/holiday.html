holiday

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɑːləˌdeɪ / (AmE), / ˈhɒlɪdeɪ / (BrE)
A period of time when you do not have to go to work or school and can relax or travel for pleasure.
period of leisure away from work

Examples

In August, many families go on holiday to the seaside.
After months of hard work, she finally took a much-needed holiday.
Do you have any plans for your next holiday?

Synset Examples

we get two weeks of vacation every summer
we took a short holiday in Puerto Rico

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vacation - a period of time when you are not at work or school, used for rest or travel (esp. US)
  • Antonym: workday - a day on which you work as opposed to a holiday

Etymology

From Old English hāligdæg, meaning 'holy day'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɑːlɪˌdeɪ / (AmE), / ˈhɒlɪdeɪ / (BrE)
A day when most people do not have to work, usually because of a law or custom; a public holiday.
day when work is suspended

Examples

Most businesses will close for the national holiday next week.
Our family always travels during the winter holidays.
Because it was a holiday, the streets were quieter than usual.

Synset Examples

no mail is delivered on federal holidays
it's a good thing that New Year's was a holiday because everyone had a hangover

Related Terms

  • Synonym: public holiday - an official day when most people do not have to work
  • Antonym: workday - a day on which work is usually done

Etymology

From Old English hāligdæg (holy day).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-15208263-n

3. VERB

/ ˈhɑːlɪˌdeɪ / (AmE), / ˈhɒlɪˌdeɪ / (BrE)
to spend or take a vacation, especially during a designated period of rest or celebration
to take a vacation or break
british

Examples

We usually holiday in Italy during the summer months.
Last year, they holidayed on the coast for two weeks.
She is holidaying in Greece this August.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vacation - to spend a period away from home or work for rest or recreation

Etymology

From Old English 'hāligdæg' (holy day), originally referring to religious festivals.
