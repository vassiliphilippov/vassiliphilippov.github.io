gait

1. NOUN

/ ɡeɪt / (AmE), / ɡeɪt / (BrE)
a person's manner of walking or moving on foot
manner of walking

Examples

The doctor observed his gait to check for any signs of injury.
She had a confident gait as she walked across the stage.
An unusual gait can be a sign of neurological problems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: walk - to move at a regular pace by lifting and setting down each foot in turn

Etymology

From Middle English *gait* (a way, path) from Old Norse *gata* (path, way).

2. NOUN

/ ɡeɪt / (AmE), / ɡeɪt / (BrE)
The manner in which a horse moves, especially as characterized by a particular pattern of the legs' action.
horse's manner of moving

Examples

The judge admired the horse's smooth gait as it circled the arena.
Each breed has its own distinctive gait that can be recognized by experts.
She trained her horse to change from a walk to a faster gait on command.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: trot - a moderately fast horse gait in which the legs move in diagonal pairs
  • Degree Lower: walk - a slow, regular horse gait where each foot hits the ground separately
  • Degree Higher: canter - a controlled, three-beat horse gait faster than a trot
  • Degree Higher: gallop - the fastest horse gait, a four-beat movement

Etymology

From Old Norse 'gata' (road, path).

3. NOUN

/ ɡeɪt / (AmE), / ɡeɪt / (BrE)
the speed or manner of walking or running, especially as classified in animals and humans
rate or style of walking or running

Examples

The horse slowed its gait as it approached the fence.
After his injury, his walking gait changed noticeably.
Athletes often adjust their running gait to improve speed and performance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pace - the speed at which someone or something moves or does something
  • Synonym: stride - a step or stage in walking or running; also, the rhythm or manner of moving
  • Degree Lower: canter - a smooth and easy pace of a horse, slower than a gallop
  • Degree Higher: gallop - the fastest pace of a horse, with all feet off the ground together

Etymology

From Middle English *gait* (way, manner of walking), from Old Norse *gata* (road, path).
