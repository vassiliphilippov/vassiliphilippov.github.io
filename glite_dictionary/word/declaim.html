to declaim

1. VERB

/ dɪˈkleɪm / (AmE), / dɪˈkleɪm / (BrE)
to speak forcefully and with passion, especially against something; to denounce publicly and with strong feeling
speak forcefully against something

Examples

The activist declaimed against the new policy in front of a large crowd.
During the debate, she declaimed passionately against corruption and injustice.
He often declaims about environmental issues at public gatherings.

Synset Examples

he declaimed against the wasteful ways of modern society

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to denounce - to publicly declare something to be wrong or evil
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of

Etymology

From Latin declamare (to cry out, make a speech), from de- (down, away) + clamare (to shout).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈkleɪm / (AmE), / dɪˈkleɪm / (BrE)
to speak or recite something, especially in a formal or theatrical manner, often with strong emotion or rhetorical effect
recite or speak theatrically

Examples

The actor began to declaim Shakespeare’s lines with dramatic gestures and a booming voice.
She would often declaim poetry at local gatherings, mesmerizing her audience.
Rather than simply read the speech, he chose to declaim it passionately from memory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to recite - to repeat aloud from memory before an audience
  • Synonym: to orate - to speak formally in public, often at length
  • Antonym: mumble - to speak in a low indistinct manner

Etymology

From Latin 'declamare' (to shout or cry out), from 'de-' (down, away) + 'clamare' (to cry out).

Synset POS: v
