pretender

1. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛndər / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛndə / (BrE)
a person who claims the right to a throne or high office, especially without a legitimate title
one who claims a throne or position

Examples

After the king's death, a pretender emerged to stake his claim to the throne.
History books often discuss the exploits of the Jacobite pretender to the British crown.
Many saw him as nothing more than a pretender with no real right to rule.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: claimant - a person making a claim, especially to a throne or title
  • Synonym: usurper - a person who seizes a position without legal right
  • Antonym: ruler - a person who rules or governs

Etymology

From Old French 'pretendre', from Latin 'praetendere' (to put forward, claim).

2. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛndər / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛndə / (BrE)
a person who falsely claims or feigns to be something, especially to deceive others
person who feigns or makes false claims

Examples

He was nothing but a pretender who tricked everyone into believing he was a doctor.
Many saw the self-proclaimed leader as a pretender with no real authority.
The company exposed the pretender and warned customers about his scams.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impostor - a person who pretends to be someone else, often for deceitful purposes
  • Synonym: fraud - a person who deceives others by pretending to be something they are not

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'praetenditor', from Latin 'praetendere' (to stretch forth, allege).

3. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛndər / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛndə / (BrE)
a person who claims to have beliefs, feelings, or motives that they do not genuinely possess, often to conceal their true intentions
someone who feigns beliefs or feelings

Examples

Everyone called him a pretender because he never revealed his true intentions.
Though she acted supportive, her colleagues saw through her facade as a pretender.
Politicians are sometimes accused of being pretenders who say one thing and do another.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hypocrite - a person who pretends to have moral standards or opinions that they do not actually possess

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *pretendre* (to lay claim), from Latin *praetendere* (to stretch forth, allege).
