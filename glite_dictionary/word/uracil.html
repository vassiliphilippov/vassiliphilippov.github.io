uracil

1. NOUN

/ ˈjʊrəsɪl / (AmE), / ˈjʊərəsɪl / (BrE)
a nitrogenous base present in RNA, replacing thymine and pairing with adenine
a base found in RNA
medical

Examples

Uracil is one of the four bases found in RNA, replacing thymine.
The scientist noted that uracil pairs with adenine in RNA.
A mutation can occur if uracil is mistakenly incorporated into DNA.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adenine - a nitrogenous base in DNA and RNA that pairs with uracil in RNA
  • Antonym: thymine - a base found in DNA, replaced by uracil in RNA

Etymology

From German Uracil, from 'ur(ea)' (urea) + '-acil' (from acetic acid).

2. NOUN

/ ˈjʊrəsɪl / (AmE), / ˈjʊərəsɪl / (BrE)
a nitrogen-containing base found in RNA, where it pairs with adenine, but not present in DNA
a base in RNA, pairs with adenine
medical

Examples

Uracil is one of the four bases that make up RNA, along with adenine, cytosine, and guanine.
Unlike DNA, RNA contains uracil instead of thymine as one of its bases.
Scientists discovered that uracil can be synthesized in laboratories from simpler organic compounds.

Etymology

From German 'Uracil', from 'ur' (ura-) and 'acyl', coined in 19th-century chemistry.
