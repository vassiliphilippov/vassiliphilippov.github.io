Dicotyledonae

1. NOUN

/ daɪˌkɑːtɪlɪˈdoʊniː / (AmE), / daɪˌkɒtɪlɪˈdəʊniː / (BrE)
A major group of flowering plants whose seeds typically have two embryonic leaves (cotyledons), often with net-veined leaves; now largely replaced by the term 'dicots' or Magnoliopsida in modern classifications.
group of flowering plants with two cotyledons
medical

Examples

The sunflowers in the garden are members of the Dicotyledonae, having two seed leaves.
Most deciduous trees belong to the Dicotyledonae, as opposed to monocotyledons such as grasses.
Botanists study the Dicotyledonae to understand the evolution of flowering plants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monocotyledon - a group of flowering plants with a single cotyledon

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'di-' (two) + 'kotyledon' (cotyledon, seed leaf).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdaɪˌkɑtəˈlidəni / (AmE), / ˌdɪkɒtɪˈliːdəniː / (BrE)
a large class of flowering plants whose seeds typically contain two embryonic leaves or cotyledons, with net-veined leaves and flower parts usually in fours or fives
major group of flowering plants with two seed leaves
medical

Examples

Most broad-leaved forest trees belong to the Dicotyledonae.
The Dicotyledonae are distinguished from monocots by their seed and leaf structure.
Botanists have reclassified many plants in the Dicotyledonae group based on genetic studies.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Monocotyledonae - group of flowering plants with one embryonic seed leaf

Etymology

From Greek 'di-' meaning 'two' and 'kotyledon' meaning 'cup-shaped cavity, seed-lobe'.
