introjection

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪntroʊˈdʒɛkʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪntrəʊˈdʒɛkʃən / (BrE)
(psychology) the unconscious process of incorporating the qualities, attitudes, or attributes of others or the outside world into one's own psyche so that these qualities become part of the self.
psychological internalization process
medical

Examples

The child's introjection of her parent's values shaped her personality throughout adulthood.
Therapists often discuss introjection as a mechanism by which people internalize society's expectations.
Freud described introjection as an important defense mechanism in early psychological development.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: projection - the process of attributing one's own thoughts or feelings to others in psychology

Etymology

From Latin 'intro-' (inward) + 'iacio, iectum' (to throw), modeled after 'projection'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪntroʊˈdʒɛkʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪntrəʊˈdʒɛkʃən / (BrE)
(in psychoanalysis) the unconscious incorporation of the values, attitudes, or qualities of other people, especially parental figures, into one's own personality.
unconscious adoption of others' values (psychoanalysis)
medical

Examples

The child's introjection of parental rules helps him develop a sense of right and wrong.
In therapy, they discussed how introjection can influence self-esteem and decision-making.
Freud believed that introjection is essential to the development of the superego.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: identification - process of associating oneself closely with another person or group
  • Antonym: projection - (psychoanalysis) attributing one's own feelings to others

Etymology

From Latin 'intro-' (inward) + 'iacere' (to throw), via German 'Introjektion'.
