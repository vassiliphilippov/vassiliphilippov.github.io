introject

1. VERB

/ ˈɪn.tɹoʊˌdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.trəʊˌdʒɛkt / (BrE)
(in psychology) to unconsciously incorporate the attitudes or ideas of others into one's own personality or mental life.
unconsciously absorb ideas or attitudes

Examples

Children often introject parental values without realizing it.
In therapy, he discovered he had introjected his father's critical voice.
Patients may unconsciously introject negative beliefs from significant others.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to internalize - to make attitudes or behavior part of one's own personality or beliefs

Etymology

From Latin *intro-* (into) + *iacere* (to throw), via early 20th-century psychological terminology.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.troʊˌdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.trəʊˌdʒɛkt / (BrE)
(in psychoanalysis) an internalized representation of another person, especially a parent, whose attitudes and values are unconsciously adopted.
internalized figure in psychoanalysis
medical

Examples

In therapy, she explored the impact of her father's introject on her self-esteem.
The critical introject often manifests as an inner voice of judgment or disapproval.
Understanding the role of the parental introject is essential in many psychoanalytic approaches.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: internalization - the process of making attitudes, values, or standards part of one's own thinking

Etymology

From Latin 'intro-' (inward) + 'jacere' (to throw), i.e., something thrown or taken inward.

Synset POS: n
