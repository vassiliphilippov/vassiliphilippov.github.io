annex

1. NOUN

/ ˈænɛks / (AmE), / ˈænɛks / (BrE)
a building added on to a larger one or situated close to it and serving as an extension or addition
an extension or addition to a building

Examples

The council built a new annex to accommodate the growing number of students.
The museum's annex houses a collection of contemporary art.
We met in the annex adjacent to the main conference room.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extension - a part added to enlarge or prolong something; an addition

Etymology

From Latin 'annexus', past participle of 'annectere' meaning 'to tie or bind to'.

2. VERB

/ əˈnɛks / (AmE), / əˈnɛks / (BrE)
to take control of a territory, especially by force or without permission, and declare it as part of one's own country
seize territory and make it part of a country

Examples

The country decided to annex the disputed regions after years of conflict.
Germany attempted to annex Austria in 1938.
Critics argue that it is illegal to annex territory by military force.

Synset Examples

Hitler annexed Lithuania

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take something quickly and firmly, often by force

Etymology

From Latin 'annexare', from 'ad-' (to) + 'nectere' (to tie, bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02220137-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ əˈnɛks / (AmE), / əˈnɛks / (BrE)
to add or attach something, especially territory or an extra part, to something larger or more important
add or attach (especially territory)

Examples

The country decided to annex the disputed region after months of conflict.
The city plans to annex several nearby communities next year.
The museum hopes to annex the building next door to expand its exhibit space.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to append - to add something as an attachment or supplement
  • Antonym: to detach - to remove or separate something that was attached

Etymology

From Latin 'annexare' (to tie to, connect), from 'ad-' (to) + 'nectere' (to tie, bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01331823-v
Synset POS: v
