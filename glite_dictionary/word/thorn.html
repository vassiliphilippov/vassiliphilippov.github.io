thorn

1. NOUN

/ θɔrn / (AmE), / θɔːn / (BrE)
a small sharp-pointed projection on the stem or leaf of a plant, often capable of causing injury if touched
a sharp-pointed projection on a plant

Examples

She pricked her finger on a thorn while picking roses in the garden.
Many desert plants have long, sharp thorns to protect themselves from animals.
The bush was covered in thorns that made it difficult to approach.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spine - a stiff, pointed structure on certain plants or animals
  • Synonym: prickle - a small, sharp, needle-like structure on plants

Etymology

Old English thorn, from Proto-Germanic *thurnuz.

2. NOUN

/ θɔrn / (AmE), / θɔːn / (BrE)
a person or thing that continually irritates, annoys, or troubles someone
source of persistent irritation or annoyance

Examples

His constant criticism became a real thorn in her side at work.
The new parking regulations are a thorn for local business owners.
That loud alarm has been a thorn to everyone living nearby.

Synset Examples

he's a thorn in my flesh

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nuisance - a person or thing causing inconvenience or annoyance
  • Antonym: blessing - a welcome event or thing that brings happiness

Etymology

From Old English 'þorn', related to Old Norse 'þorn' and German 'Dorn', meaning thorn or prickle.

3. NOUN

/ θɔrn / (AmE), / θɔːn / (BrE)
A letter (þ or Þ) used in Old English and some other early Germanic alphabets to represent the 'th' sound, derived from the runic script.
letter (þ) in Old English alphabets

Examples

The Old English word 'þæt' begins with the character thorn.
The thorn was gradually replaced by the 'th' digraph in Middle English.
Linguists often discuss the role of thorn in Anglo-Saxon manuscripts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eth - a letter (ð or Ð) used in Old English to represent the voiced 'th' sound

Etymology

From Old English 'þorn', derived from the runic name for the letter, itself from Proto-Germanic *þurnuz.
