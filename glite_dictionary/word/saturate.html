to saturate

1. VERB

/ ˈsætʃəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈsætjʊreɪt / (BrE)
to cause (a chemical compound, vapor, solution, or magnetic material) to unite with or contain the greatest possible amount of another substance
cause to absorb the maximum of another substance

Examples

The chemist tried to saturate the solution with salt until no more would dissolve.
If you saturate iron with magnetism, it cannot become magnetized further.
The vapor in the chamber began to saturate the air, reaching its maximum possible concentration.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: supersaturate - to increase the concentration of a solution beyond its normal saturation point

Etymology

From Latin saturatus, past participle of saturare (to fill, sate), from satur (full, sated).

2. VERB

/ ˈsætʃ.əˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈsatʃ.əˌreɪt / (BrE)
to soak, fill, or infuse something so thoroughly that no more can be absorbed or added
to soak or fill completely

Examples

The heavy rain began to saturate the soil, making the ground muddy.
Please saturate the sponge with water before cleaning the table.
The artist likes to saturate her canvases with vivid colors.

Synset Examples

Impregnate the cloth with alcohol

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soak - to make thoroughly wet by immersing
  • Antonym: dry - make or become free from moisture

Etymology

From Latin saturatus, past participle of saturare (to fill, saturate), from satur (full).
