rice

1. NOUN

/ raɪs / (AmE), / raɪs / (BrE)
short, white or brown seeds of a cereal plant, used as a staple food, either in unpolished or polished form.
a cereal grain used as food

Examples

She cooked a pot of rice to serve with the curry.
Brown rice is considered healthier than white rice because it contains more fiber.
He had rice for lunch nearly every day while living in Asia.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wheat - a cereal grain used as food, especially for making flour
  • Synonym: barley - a cereal grain used as food and for brewing

Etymology

From Old French 'ris', from Italian 'riso', from Latin 'oryza', from Greek 'oryza', probably of Oriental origin.

2. VERB

/ raɪs / (AmE), / raɪs / (BrE)
to push (food, especially cooked vegetables) through a ricer so that it becomes the consistency of rice
push food through a ricer

Examples

After boiling the potatoes, she likes to rice them for a fluffier texture.
Many chefs recommend that you rice cauliflower to use as a low-carb alternative.
To make the gnocchi, first rice the cooked potatoes and let them cool.

Synset Examples

rice the potatoes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mash - to crush food into a soft, pulpy mass, often with a masher

3. NOUN

/ raɪs / (AmE), / raɪs / (BrE)
a cereal plant grown in warm climates, whose seeds are eaten as food; also, the seeds themselves used as a staple food
a cereal plant; its edible grains

Examples

She served steamed rice alongside the spicy curry for dinner.
Large fields of rice stretched across the valley, shimmering green in the sunlight.
Brown rice is often considered healthier than white rice because it retains more nutrients.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grain - the small hard seeds of food plants such as wheat, rice, or corn

Etymology

From Old French riz, from Italian riso, from Latin oryza, from Greek oryza, from Sanskrit vrihi.
