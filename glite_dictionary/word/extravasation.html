extravasation

1. NOUN

/ ɛkˌstrævəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɛkˌstrævəˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of pouring out fumes or lava from a volcano, or the material deposited in this way
discharge of lava or fumes from a volcano

Examples

The extravasation of lava covered the entire valley after the volcanic eruption.
Scientists measured the extravasation of fumes to assess the activity of the volcano.
This region is characterized by frequent extravasation events that have shaped its unique landscape.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eruption - the act or process of erupting (especially of a volcano)

Etymology

From Latin 'extravasare' (to overflow), from 'extra' (outside) + 'vas' (vessel).

2. NOUN

/ ɛkˌstrævəˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / ɛkˌstrævəˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
an abnormal escape of bodily fluid such as blood, lymph, or urine, from a vessel into surrounding tissue, or the material that has leaked out
accidental leakage of body fluid into tissue
medical

Examples

The nurse monitored the IV site for any signs of extravasation.
Extravasation of blood can cause bruising and tissue damage around the injury.
The doctor explained that the extravasation observed was likely due to a ruptured blood vessel.

Etymology

From Latin 'extravasare' (to force out of the vessel), from 'extra' (outside) + 'vas' (vessel).

3. NOUN

/ ɛkˌstrævəˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪkˌstrævəˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
the escape of fluid, such as blood, lymph, or urine, from a vessel into surrounding tissue
leakage of fluid from a vessel into tissue
medical

Examples

The intravenous drug caused extravasation at the injection site, resulting in localized swelling.
Extravasation of urine was observed following the injury to the bladder.
Prompt recognition of extravasation can prevent severe tissue damage in chemotherapy patients.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leakage - the accidental escape of fluid or gas from a container

Etymology

From Latin extravasare (to force out of the vessels), from extra- (outside) + vas (vessel).
