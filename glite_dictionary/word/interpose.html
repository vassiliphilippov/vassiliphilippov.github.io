to interpose

1. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to place or come between two things or people; to intervene or interrupt by inserting oneself or something else
place or come between

Examples

The mediator tried to interpose herself between the two arguing parties.
A dense fog seemed to interpose itself between us and the distant shore.
He quickly interposed a question before the meeting moved on.

Synset Examples

An interposing thicket blocked their way

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else
  • Synonym: to intervene - come between to alter or prevent a result or course of events

Etymology

From Latin interponere, from inter- (between) + ponere (to place).

2. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to place or insert something between two or more things or people, often to interrupt or intervene
insert or place between

Examples

He chose to interpose a question during the heated debate.
A security guard interposed himself between the fans and the singer.
She tried interposing her opinion, but the discussion moved on quickly.

Synset Examples

She interjected clever remarks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put or place something inside or between other things
  • Synonym: to interject - to say something as an interruption

Etymology

From Latin 'interponere', from 'inter-' (between) + 'ponere' (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00916722-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to become involved in a situation in order to change or prevent what is happening, often by using one's authority or by force
get involved to change or block an action

Examples

The United Nations decided to interpose peacekeeping forces between the two warring countries.
She tried to interpose herself between the arguing colleagues to calm things down.
The police had to interpose quickly to prevent the fight from escalating.

Synset Examples

Why did the U.S. not intervene earlier in WW II?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intervene - to become involved in order to help or alter a situation
  • Synonym: to intrude - to put oneself deliberately into a situation without invitation

Etymology

From Latin interponere (to put between), from inter- (between) + ponere (to put).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02544431-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to introduce or place something between other things, often as an intervention or to interrupt
to intervene or insert

Examples

She tried to interpose herself between the arguing friends to calm them down.
He interposed a comment during the heated debate, hoping to change the subject.
The government may interpose regulations to prevent unfair practices.

Synset Examples

God interposed death

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intervene - to come between so as to prevent or alter a result
  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove or take away something that was interposed

Etymology

From Latin interponere (to put between), from inter- (between) + ponere (to put).
