assibilation

1. NOUN

/ əˌsɪbəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪbɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of pronouncing a sound, especially a consonant, as a sibilant (producing a hissing or whistling sound)
pronunciation as a sibilant sound
medical

Examples

The assibilation of certain consonants is evident in the evolution of Latin to modern Romance languages.
You can hear assibilation in the way some speakers pronounce 't' as a 'ts' sound.
Linguists study assibilation to understand historical changes in pronunciation patterns.

Etymology

From Latin 'assibilare' (to hiss).

2. NOUN

/ əˌsɪbəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪbɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
The process by which a consonant sound changes into a sibilant sound, such as 's' or 'sh', during the development of a language.
phonological process where a consonant becomes a sibilant

Examples

The assibilation of Latin 't' before 'i' is evident in the evolution to the Spanish 'c'.
Linguists study assibilation to trace the historical changes in different language families.
In some dialects, assibilation causes the 'd' sound to be pronounced as a 'z'.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: palatalization - the process by which a consonant becomes closer to a palatal sound

Etymology

From Latin 'sibilare' (to hiss), after the term 'sibilant'.
