to syncretize

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋkrəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋ.krɪ.taɪz / (BrE)
to unite or attempt to unite different beliefs or conflicting principles, especially in philosophy or religion
unite different beliefs or principles

Examples

Some philosophers have tried to syncretize Eastern and Western thought.
The new religion aimed to syncretize local traditions with imported doctrines.
Cultural celebrations often syncretize elements from different faiths and customs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to integrate - combine parts into a whole
  • Synonym: to harmonize - bring into agreement or harmony
  • Antonym: divide - separate or keep apart

Etymology

From Greek 'synkretismos' (union of communities); via French 'syncrétisme'.

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋkrəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋkrɪtʌɪz / (BrE)
To cause different beliefs, systems, or practices to fuse into one; to become fused or unified, especially in reference to ideas or religions.
fuse different beliefs or systems

Examples

The two religious traditions began to syncretize over centuries of peaceful coexistence.
As cultures interact, their languages often syncretize elements from each other.
Various philosophical systems can gradually syncretize into a more comprehensive worldview.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to merge - combine or cause to combine to form a single entity
  • Synonym: to unify - make or become united, uniform, or whole

Etymology

From syncretism, from Greek synkretismos (union of communities), with suffix -ize.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00554693-v
Synset POS: v
