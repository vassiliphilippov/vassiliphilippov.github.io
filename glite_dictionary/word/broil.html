broil

1. VERB

/ brɔɪl / (AmE), / brɔɪl / (BrE)
to cook food by exposing it to direct radiant heat, typically under a broiler
to cook under direct heat
american

Examples

She decided to broil the chicken to give it a crispy skin.
It's better to broil the fish for just a few minutes on each side.
Many recipes tell you to broil vegetables to bring out their natural sweetness.

Synset Examples

broil fish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grill - to cook food over direct heat, usually above the heat source

Etymology

From Old French *bruller* (to burn), from Latin *brusulare* (to singe).

2. VERB

/ brɔɪl / (AmE), / brɔɪl / (BrE)
to be or become very hot, especially due to hot weather or exposure to the sun
become very hot from sun or heat

Examples

After an hour on the beach, we were broiling under the hot midday sun.
The apartment broils in the afternoon when the sun shines directly through the windows.
Hikers broiled as they crossed the exposed desert trails during the summer heat.

3. NOUN

/ brɔɪl / (AmE), / brɔɪl / (BrE)
A method of cooking food by exposing it directly to radiant heat, such as over a fire or under a grill.
cooking with direct radiant heat

Examples

The chef used a broil to sear the steak perfectly on both sides.
Many seafood dishes are prepared using the broil method for extra flavor.
She prefers the taste of vegetables cooked with a quick broil rather than boiling.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grill - cooking method involving direct heat, often from below
  • Synonym: roast - cooking food by dry heat in an oven or over a fire
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

/ brɔɪl / (AmE), / brɔɪl / (BrE)
to become extremely hot or to make something extremely hot, especially as a result of exposure to intense natural heat such as the sun
become or make extremely hot; heat intensely

Examples

In the afternoons, the desert broils under the relentless sun.
Tourists broiled on the crowded beach during the heatwave.
Crops can wither if fields broil for too many days without rain.
