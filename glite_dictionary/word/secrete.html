to secrete

1. VERB

/ sɪˈkrit / (AmE), / sɪˈkriːt / (BrE)
(of cells or glands) to produce and release a substance, such as a hormone or enzyme, into the body or a bodily fluid.
produce and release a bodily substance
medical

Examples

Certain glands in the body secrete hormones that regulate growth.
The pancreas secretes insulin to help control blood sugar levels.
Some frogs secrete toxins through their skin as a defense mechanism.

Synset Examples

secrete digestive juices
release a hormone into the blood stream

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to excrete - to eliminate waste matter from the body

Etymology

From Latin 'secretus', past participle of 'secernere' (to separate, set apart).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00069066-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ sɪˈkrit / (AmE), / sɪˈkriːt / (BrE)
to hide something carefully; to place out of sight, often for safekeeping or concealment
to hide or conceal something

Examples

He secreted the letter behind a loose brick in the wall.
The documents were secreted in a locked drawer to prevent them from being discovered.
She secretes her spare keys in a plant pot on the porch.

Synset Examples

The money was secreted from his children

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to conceal - keep something from being seen or found; hide
  • Antonym: to reveal - make something known or visible

Etymology

From Latin secretus (set apart, hidden), past participle of secernere (to separate, distinguish).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02149701-v
Synset POS: v
