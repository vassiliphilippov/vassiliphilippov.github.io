to lambast

1. VERB

/ læmˈbæst / (AmE), / læmˈbɑːst / (BrE)
to criticize or reprimand severely and angrily
to criticize harshly

Examples

The coach lambasted the team for their poor performance in the final match.
After hearing the details, the journalist lambasted the company in her latest editorial.
He was lambasted by critics for his controversial comments during the interview.

Synset Examples

The mother scolded the child for entering a stranger's car
The deputy ragged the Prime Minister
The customer dressed down the waiter for bringing cold soup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scold - to angrily criticize, especially of a child
  • Synonym: to berate - to rebuke or scold harshly
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

Probably an alteration of 'bombast' (to attack verbally) or from 'lambaste', combining 'lam' (to beat) and 'baste' (to thrash).

2. VERB

/ læmˈbæst, læmˈbeɪst / (AmE), / ləmˈbɑːst, læmˈbeɪst / (BrE)
to beat or strike repeatedly, especially with a cane or similar object
to beat with a cane or strike repeatedly

Examples

The headmaster threatened to lambast any student caught cheating.
In the old days, some teachers would lambast misbehaving children with a cane.
He was lambasted for his insolence, both with words and with a stick.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrash - to beat repeatedly with a whip or stick
  • Degree Lower: to criticize - to express disapproval or find fault

Etymology

Originally a blend of 'lam' (to beat) and 'baste' (to thrash), both from older English slang.
