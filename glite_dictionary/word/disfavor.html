disfavor

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈfeɪvɚ / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
the state of being out of favor with someone, especially someone in a position of power or influence
the state of being out of favor

Examples

After the scandal, the politician fell into disfavor with the public.
His bold ideas brought him the disfavor of his conservative colleagues.
The artist's work was in disfavor for many years before being rediscovered.

Synset Examples

he is in disfavor with the king

Related Terms

  • Antonym: favor - approval, support, or liking for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin 'dis-' (expressing negation) + 'favour'.

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈfeɪvər / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
to put someone or something at a disadvantage or hinder their progress; to harm
to put at a disadvantage; hinder

Examples

Some government policies tend to disfavor low-income families.
The new rules disfavor foreign competitors in the local market.
Such decisions may unintentionally disfavor minority groups.

Synset Examples

This rule clearly disadvantages me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hinder - to make it difficult for someone to do something or for something to happen
  • Synonym: harm - to physically or emotionally injure or damage
  • Antonym: favor - to support or give preference to

Etymology

From Latin dis- (expressing reversal) + favor (approval).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02519126-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ dɪsˈfeɪvər / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
the state or feeling of being regarded with dislike or lack of approval
lack of approval; disapproval

Examples

Her actions placed her in the disfavor of her colleagues.
The politician fell into disfavor after the scandal was exposed.
Wearing casual clothes to the formal event drew the disfavor of the organizers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disapproval - the feeling or expression of dislike or lack of approval
  • Antonym: favor - approval, support, or liking for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin dis- (apart, away) + favor (favor, goodwill).

Synset POS: n
