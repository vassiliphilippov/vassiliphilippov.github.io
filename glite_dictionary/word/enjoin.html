to enjoin

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʒɔɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɔɪn / (BrE)
(of a court or authority) to order someone to do or refrain from doing something by issuing an injunction.
to legally order (especially via injunction)
legal

Examples

The court decided to enjoin the company from dumping waste into the river.
A federal judge can enjoin the enforcement of the disputed law while the case proceeds.
The city was enjoined from proceeding with the demolition until further notice.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to prohibit - to formally forbid something by law or authority
  • Antonym: permit - to allow or authorize

Etymology

From Old French 'enjoindre', from Latin 'injungere' (to join, impose, prescribe).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʒɔɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɔɪn / (BrE)
to instruct or order someone to do something with authority; to prescribe or command firmly
order or instruct with authority

Examples

The court enjoined the company to stop using the trademark.
Her doctor enjoined her to get more rest and exercise regularly.
The manager enjoined his staff to complete the report by Friday.

Synset Examples

I said to him to go home
She ordered him to do the shopping
The mother told the child to get dressed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: order - to instruct or command someone to do something
  • Synonym: command - to give an authoritative order
  • Antonym: to forbid - to order not to do something

Etymology

From Old French enjoindre, from Latin injungere (to join, impose).
