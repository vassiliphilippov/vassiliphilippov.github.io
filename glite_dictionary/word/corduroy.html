corduroy

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɔːrdəˌrɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈkɔːdjʊˌrɔɪ / (BrE)
A road made of logs laid crosswise, typically used in wet or marshy areas to improve passage.
road made of logs laid crosswise

Examples

The old trail became impassable until a corduroy was built across the swamp.
During the pioneer era, travelers often used makeshift corduroy to traverse muddy ground.
The construction crew laid logs side by side to create a temporary corduroy.

Etymology

From the resemblance to corduroy fabric, which has a ridged or ribbed surface.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɔr.dəˌrɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈkɔː.djʊ.rɔɪ / (BrE)
A thick, durable fabric with a raised, ribbed texture running parallel to the selvage, typically made from cotton.
fabric with ribbed texture, usually cotton

Examples

He wore a brown corduroy jacket with elbow patches to the lecture.
The sofa was upholstered in soft green corduroy that felt warm to the touch.
She prefers pants made from corduroy in the winter for extra warmth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: velvet - A soft, luxurious fabric with a dense pile

Etymology

Perhaps from French 'corde du roi', meaning 'cord of the king'.

3. VERB

/ ˈkɔrdəˌrɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈkɔːdəˌrɔɪ / (BrE)
to build (a road) by laying logs side by side to create a stable surface, especially over swampy ground.
construct a road from logs placed side by side

Examples

Early settlers had to corduroy the road through the marshy forest.
The construction crew corduroyed the trail to make it passable for wagons.
Teams spent weeks corduroying new routes for supply convoys during the rainy season.
