Manx

1. NOUN

/ mæŋks / (AmE), / mæŋks / (BrE)
the Gaelic language historically spoken on the Isle of Man, now rarely used except for ceremonial or revival purposes
Gaelic language of the Isle of Man

Examples

Few people today are fluent speakers of Manx.
Traditional songs sung in Manx are an important part of the island's heritage.
Some schools on the Isle of Man offer lessons in Manx to preserve the language.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Irish - the Goidelic language spoken in Ireland
  • Synonym: Scottish Gaelic - the Goidelic language spoken in Scotland

Etymology

From Manx Gaelic 'Gaelg Vanninagh' (Manx language), relating to the Isle of Man.

2. ADJ

/ mæŋks / (AmE), / mæŋks / (BrE)
of or relating to the Isle of Man, its people, or their language
related to the Isle of Man or its people/language

Examples

The Manx language has seen a revival in recent years.
Traditional Manx customs are still celebrated on the island.
Many Manx people are proud of their unique heritage.

Synset Examples

the Manx fishing industry
there are few Manx speakers alive today

Etymology

From 'Man', the name of the island, with -x by analogy to English ethnonyms (e.g. 'Lynx').

3. NOUN

/ mæŋks / (AmE), / mæŋks / (BrE)
a breed of domestic cat with a short coat and typically no tail or only a very short tail, believed to have originated on the Isle of Man
short-tailed breed of cat

Examples

The Manx is easily recognized by its distinctive lack of a tail.
She adopted a friendly Manx from the animal shelter last year.
Many people believe the Manx breed originated due to a genetic mutation on the Isle of Man.

Etymology

Named after the Isle of Man, where the breed originated.

Synset POS: n
