descriptivism

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈskrɪptɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪptɪvɪzəm / (BrE)
The doctrine or practice in linguistics that aims to objectively describe how language is actually used, rather than prescribing how it should be used.
doctrine describing actual language use

Examples

Modern linguistics largely embraces descriptivism rather than trying to enforce strict language rules.
The debate between descriptivism and prescriptivism is common among grammar enthusiasts.
His teaching was grounded in descriptivism, reflecting actual language usage rather than idealized forms.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prescriptivism - the doctrine of prescribing how language should be used

Etymology

Formed from 'descriptive' + '-ism', meaning the system or practice of describing.

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈskrɪptɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪptɪvɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
(In ethics) The doctrine that moral statements express propositions and therefore can be true or false.
the view that moral statements have truth value

Examples

Some philosophers argue that descriptivism fails to capture the complexity of moral language.
According to descriptivism, ethical statements can be assessed as true or false.
Recent debates in ethics have questioned the validity of descriptivism as a comprehensive theory.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: non-descriptivism - The view that moral statements do not have truth value
  • Antonym: emotivism - Doctrine that moral statements express emotions, not facts

Etymology

From 'descriptive' + '-ism', indicating a doctrine or system.
