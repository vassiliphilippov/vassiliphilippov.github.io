Teuton

1. NOUN

/ ˈtuːtɑn / (AmE), / ˈtjuːtɒn / (BrE)
a member of an ancient Germanic people who migrated from Jutland to southern Gaul and were destroyed by the Romans in the 2nd century BC
ancient Germanic people, defeated by Romans

Examples

The Roman army decisively defeated the Teutons at the Battle of Aquae Sextiae.
Historians still debate the origins of the Teutons and their migration from Jutland.
Some legends credit the Teutons with influencing early Germanic culture.

Etymology

From Latin Teutones, name of the tribe, related to Proto-Germanic *þeudanōz (people).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtuːtɑn / (AmE), / ˈtjuːtən / (BrE)
a member of an ancient Germanic people; sometimes used to refer to a German or a speaker of a Germanic language
member of an ancient Germanic people

Examples

Historians believe the Teuton tribes migrated across much of Europe in ancient times.
The legend of the valiant Teuton warriors appears in many early medieval texts.
She described her ancestry as tracing back to the Teuton peoples.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: German - a native or inhabitant of Germany
  • Synonym: Saxon - a member of a Germanic people; especially one of the early settlers of England

Etymology

From Latin 'Teutones', referring to a Germanic tribe; possibly from Proto-Germanic 'þeudanōz' (people).
