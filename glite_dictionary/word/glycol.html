glycol

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡlaɪˌkɑl / (AmE), / ˈɡlaɪˌkɒl / (BrE)
any of a class of alcohols containing two hydroxyl groups in each molecule, commonly used as solvents or antifreeze agents
a type of alcohol with two hydroxyl groups

Examples

Ethylene glycol is commonly used as antifreeze in car engines.
Propylene glycol is often found in food and cosmetic products.
Scientists studied the properties of the new glycol in their experiments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: diol - an organic compound with two hydroxyl groups

Etymology

From Greek 'glykys' (sweet) combined with '-ol' (chemical suffix for alcohols).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡlaɪˌkɔl / (AmE), / ˈɡlʌɪkɒl / (BrE)
a sweet, colorless, and viscous liquid used as an antifreeze and solvent, and also in the manufacture of resins and plastics; generally refers to ethylene glycol or similar dihydric alcohols.
a sweet, poisonous liquid used as antifreeze

Examples

The mechanic added glycol to the car's radiator to prevent it from freezing in the winter.
Many industrial processes use glycol as a solvent because of its unique properties.
Accidental ingestion of glycol can be extremely dangerous due to its toxicity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ethylene glycol - a common type of glycol used in antifreeze

Etymology

From French 'glycol', based on Greek 'glykys' (sweet) + '-ol' indicating an alcohol.
