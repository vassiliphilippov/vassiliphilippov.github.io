dowser

1. NOUN

/ ˈdaʊzər / (AmE), / ˈdaʊzə / (BrE)
a person who uses a divining rod to search for underground water or minerals
person who uses a divining rod

Examples

The dowser walked slowly across the field, holding the rod steady in his hands.
Many farmers hired a dowser to locate underground springs during the drought.
Skeptics question whether a dowser can truly detect hidden sources of water.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: diviner - someone who practices divination

Etymology

From the verb 'douse' (to plunge into water), possibly influenced by archaic use of 'dowse' (to strike or tap).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdaʊzər / (AmE), / ˈdaʊzə / (BrE)
a forked stick or similar instrument said to detect the presence of underground water, minerals, or oil by dipping or twitching when held over such sources
divining rod for underground water or oil

Examples

The farmer used a dowser to find a new well on his property.
Many people remain skeptical about the effectiveness of a dowser in locating underground water.
She carried a hazel dowser as she walked across the field, looking for a place to drill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: divining rod - a stick or rod used in dowsing to locate underground water or minerals

Etymology

From 'dowsing', a practice dating from the 17th century; origins likely from Cornish or English dialect.
