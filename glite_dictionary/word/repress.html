to repress

1. VERB

/ rɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to deliberately exclude impulses, memories, or feelings from one's conscious awareness, often as a psychological defense mechanism
exclude from conscious awareness

Examples

Some people repress traumatic memories as a way of coping with difficult experiences.
She tried to repress her feelings of anger, but they eventually surfaced.
Therapists often help clients recall emotions they have long repressed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to the expression or activity of something; in psychology, to consciously inhibit thoughts or feelings

Etymology

From Latin 'reprimere', meaning 'to press back'.

2. VERB

/ rɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to prevent something, especially a process or action, from happening or developing; to block the activity of something
block or prevent activity

Examples

Certain proteins can repress the transcription of specific genes.
The drug is designed to repress abnormal cell growth in the body.
Environmental factors may repress the flowering process in plants.

Synset Examples

the process of repressing gene transcription

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to something by force or authority

Etymology

From Latin 'reprimere', from 're-' (again) + 'premere' (to press).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00587675-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to prevent the natural development or self-expression of someone or something, typically by restraining or controlling feelings, thoughts, or impulses
prevent natural development or self-expression

Examples

Society should not repress the creativity of young people.
Years of strict upbringing can repress a child's emotional development.
She tried to repress her natural enthusiasm in the formal meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to forcibly put an end to or hold back (especially feelings or actions)
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support, confidence, or hope to someone so they can express themselves

Etymology

From Latin repressus, past participle of reprimere, meaning 'to press back, check'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02457192-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ rɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to subdue or put down (an uprising, dissent, etc.) by force or intimidation
subdue by force or intimidation

Examples

The military tried to repress the protest by deploying armed troops in the city.
Totalitarian regimes often repress opposition by arresting activists and silencing the media.
He criticized the government for its efforts to repress any voices of dissent.

  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support or confidence to an activity or person

Etymology

5. VERB

/ rɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to prevent yourself from expressing a feeling, emotion, or reaction, often in order to hide it
suppress or hide a feeling or reaction

Examples

She tried to repress her laughter during the serious meeting.
He repressed a sigh when he heard the disappointing news.
It’s not healthy to constantly repress your emotions.

Synset Examples

smother a yawn
muffle one's anger
strangle a laugh
repress a cry of fear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to stop yourself from showing a feeling or emotion
  • Antonym: express - to show or make known a feeling or emotion

Etymology

From Latin 'reprimere', from re- 'back' + premere 'to press'.
