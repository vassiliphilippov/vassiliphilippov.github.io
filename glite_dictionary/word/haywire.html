haywire

1. NOUN

/ ˈheɪˌwaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈheɪˌwaɪə / (BrE)
Thin wire used for binding bales of hay.
wire for tying bales of hay

Examples

The farmer used haywire to secure the loose bales in the truck.
Old fences on the property were held together with pieces of haywire.
A length of haywire was all he needed to quickly fix the broken gate.
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03506212-n
Synset POS: n

2. ADJ

/ ˈheɪˌwaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈheɪˌwaɪə / (BrE)
(informal) Mentally confused or erratic; behaving in a wild or uncontrolled way.
(informal) mentally confused or erratic
informal slang

Examples

Sarah went haywire when she heard the unexpected news and started laughing uncontrollably.
After a week without sleep, his thoughts seemed to go completely haywire.
The children went haywire during the birthday party, running and shouting everywhere.

Synset Examples

it used to drive my husband balmy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crazy - mentally deranged; extremely foolish or wild
  • Antonym: sane - mentally healthy and rational

Etymology

Originally U.S. slang, from 'haywire' (thin wire for baling hay), associated with makeshift repairs and disorder.

3. ADJ

/ ˈheɪˌwaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈheɪˌwaɪə / (BrE)
(of a system, machine, or situation) not working or behaving as it should; out of control or malfunctioning.
not functioning properly; out of control
informal

Examples

Ever since the power outage, the computer has gone completely haywire.
When the signal started acting haywire, the technician tried to reset the equipment.
Her plans went haywire after the unexpected storm ruined the festival.

Synset Examples

something is amiss
has gone completely haywire
something is wrong with the engine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amiss - not quite right; out of order
  • Synonym: malfunctioning - not working correctly
  • Antonym: working - functioning as intended

Etymology

Originally from the use of actual haywire to make temporary repairs, especially in rural US English.
