to transpire

1. VERB

/ trænˈspaɪər / (AmE), / trænˈspaɪə / (BrE)
(of a plant or leaf) to give off water vapor through the surface of leaves or other parts.
release water vapor (plants)

Examples

During hot weather, leaves transpire more to keep the plant cool.
Wheat plants transpire large amounts of water throughout the day.
If soil is dry, some plants may transpire less to conserve moisture.

Synset Examples

plants transpire

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evaporate - to turn from liquid into vapor

Etymology

From French *transpirer*, from Latin *transpirare* (to breathe through).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ trænˈspaɪɚ / (AmE), / trænˈspaɪə / (BrE)
(of a gas or liquid) to pass through the tissue or pores of a substance.
to pass through tissue or pores (gas/liquid)

Examples

Water vapor can transpire from the leaves of a plant into the surrounding air.
Scientists observed how chemicals transpire through the cell membrane during the experiment.
Certain fabrics allow sweat to transpire, making them ideal for athletic wear.

Related Terms

Etymology

From French 'transpirer', from Latin 'transpirare' (to breathe through).

3. VERB

/ trænˈspaɪɚ / (AmE), / trænˈspaɪə / (BrE)
(of secret or unknown information) to become known or be revealed
become known; come to light

Examples

It soon transpired that the rumors were true.
No details had transpired about the agreement until yesterday.
After the meeting, it transpired that several members had already resigned.

4. VERB

/ trænˈspaɪər / (AmE), / trænˈspaɪə / (BrE)
(of a plant or animal) to give off water vapor, especially through the skin or surface tissues.
give off water vapor through tissue

Examples

Plants transpire water through tiny openings in their leaves.
After exercise, the skin may transpire more to help cool the body.
Many leaves transpire significant amounts of moisture during hot summer days.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evaporate - to change from liquid to vapor
  • Antonym: to absorb - to take in or soak up (opposite process, context-dependent)

Etymology

From Latin 'transpirare' (to breathe through), from 'trans-' (across) + 'spirare' (to breathe).

5. VERB

/ trænˈspaɪɚ / (AmE), / trænˈspaɪə / (BrE)
to happen or occur, especially in a way that is noteworthy or becomes known
happen or occur

Examples

No one expected such a surprising turn of events to transpire during the meeting.
It soon transpired that the rumors were, in fact, true.
We don't yet know what may transpire over the next few months.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to occur - to happen or take place
  • Synonym: to happen - take place or occur

Etymology

From Latin 'transpirare', meaning 'to breathe through'; later extended to 'become known' or 'happen'.
