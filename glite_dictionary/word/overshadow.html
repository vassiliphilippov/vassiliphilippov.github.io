to overshadow

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈʃædoʊ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈʃædəʊ / (BrE)
(of a thing) to cast a shadow over something else, making it less bright or less noticeable.
cast a shadow over; obscure

Examples

The old cathedral overshadows the surrounding market square during the afternoon.
A new shopping center overshadowed the smaller shops on the street, both literally and figuratively.
Towering skyscrapers now overshadow much of the city’s historic district.

Synset Examples

The tall tree overshadowed the house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obscure - make something less visible or clear

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'over-' and 'shadow', meaning to cast one's shadow over.

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈʃædoʊ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈʃædəʊ / (BrE)
to be more important or significant than something else, often making it less noticeable or less important
to be more important than something else

Examples

The scandal managed to overshadow all the company's recent achievements.
Her talent tends to overshadow that of her peers.
The argument overshadowed what was supposed to be a joyful reunion.

Synset Examples

the tragedy overshadowed the couple's happiness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eclipse - to make something less important or noticeable by comparison
  • Synonym: dwarf - to cause to seem small or unimportant by comparison
  • Antonym: highlight - to make something more prominent or important

Etymology

Prefix 'over-' meaning 'above, across' and 'shadow' from Old English 'sceadu' meaning 'shade, shadow'.

3. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈʃædoʊ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈʃædəʊ / (BrE)
to make someone or something seem less important, successful, or impressive by comparison
make seem less important by comparison

Examples

Despite her achievements, her brother's fame tended to overshadow her.
The excitement of the festival was overshadowed by reports of bad weather.
His generosity can sometimes overshadow his flaws.

Synset Examples

This year's debt dwarfs that of last year

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dwarf - make appear very small by comparison
  • Synonym: eclipse - surpass in importance or prominence

Etymology

From over- (above) + shadow, meaning to cast a shadow over; first recorded in the late Middle English period.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02702148-v
Synset POS: v
