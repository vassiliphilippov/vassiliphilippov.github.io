fossilisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɑːsələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɒsɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which the remains of a plant or animal become preserved as a fossil, typically turning into stone over a long period
process of becoming a fossil

Examples

The fossilisation of dinosaur bones can take millions of years.
Scientists study the fossilisation process to understand ancient ecosystems.
Rapid burial increases the chances of fossilisation in sedimentary environments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fossilization - American spelling of fossilisation

Etymology

From fossil + -isation, after Latin *fossilis* (dug up).

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɑːsəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɒsɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of becoming rigid, fixed, or outdated, especially in ideas, systems, or practices
process of becoming rigid or outdated

Examples

The fossilisation of old traditions can make it hard for organizations to adapt to change.
Language fossilisation can result in learners retaining persistent errors despite continued practice.
Some critics argue that fossilisation in government bureaucracy prevents effective reform.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stagnation - lack of movement or progress; inactivity
  • Antonym: innovation - the introduction of new ideas or methods

Etymology

From fossil + -isation, by analogy with the physical process of becoming a fossil.
