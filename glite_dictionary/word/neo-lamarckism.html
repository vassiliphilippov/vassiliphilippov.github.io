Neo-Lamarckism

1. NOUN

/ ˌniː.oʊ.ləˈmɑːrk.ɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌniː.əʊ.ləˈmɑː.kɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
A theory in evolutionary biology that emphasizes environmental influence on genetic changes and retains the idea that acquired characteristics can be inherited.
theory of inheritance of acquired traits

Examples

Recent debates in evolutionary biology have brought renewed attention to Neo-Lamarckism and its historical impact.
Unlike Darwinism, Neo-Lamarckism argues that acquired traits can be transmitted to offspring.
She wrote an essay examining the decline of Neo-Lamarckism in modern scientific thought.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Lamarckism - the theory that organisms can pass on characteristics acquired during their lifetime
  • Antonym: Darwinism - the theory of biological evolution by natural selection developed by Charles Darwin

Etymology

Formed from 'neo-' (new) and 'Lamarckism', after Jean-Baptiste Lamarck (1744–1829), French naturalist.
