to patronise

1. VERB

/ ˈpeɪtrəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpatrənʌɪz / (BrE)
to regularly use the services of a business, shop, restaurant, etc. as a customer
be a regular customer of a business
british

Examples

We always patronise the local bakery when we're in town.
She prefers to patronise small, family-owned restaurants rather than big chains.
Art lovers regularly patronise the gallery to support new exhibitions.

Synset Examples

We patronize this store
Our sponsor kept our art studio going for as long as he could

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to patronize - to regularly use the services of a business (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin 'patronus' (protector, master) via Old French and Medieval Latin.

2. VERB

/ ˈpeɪtrəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpætrənaɪz / (BrE)
to support or sponsor an organisation, activity, or person, especially by providing financial aid or encouragement
to sponsor or support (financially or otherwise)

Examples

The theatre was able to continue its work because wealthy citizens chose to patronise it.
Several charities are patronised by the royal family each year.
Local artists rely on people willing to patronise their exhibitions and workshops.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sponsor - to support an event, activity, or person as a sponsor

Etymology

From Latin patronus (protector, sponsor) via French and Medieval Latin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02224722-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈpætɹəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpætrənaɪz / (BrE)
to treat someone in a way that shows you think you are more intelligent or important than they are; to behave condescendingly toward someone
to treat someone condescendingly

Examples

Please don't patronise me—I'm perfectly capable of making my own decisions.
He tends to patronise his coworkers by offering unnecessary advice.
She felt insulted when her teacher started to patronise her during the discussion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to condescend - to show that you believe you are more intelligent or better than other people
  • Antonym: respect - to feel or show admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From French *patroniser* and medieval Latin *patronizare* (to act as a patron), originally meaning 'to act as a patron', later gaining the sense of condescension.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00910930-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈpeɪtrəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpætrənaɪz / (BrE)
to regularly shop at, use the services of, or be a customer of a business or establishment
to be a customer of
british

Examples

Many locals choose to patronise small family-run shops instead of big supermarkets.
She has patronised that bakery for more than ten years.
If more customers patronise this café, it will be able to stay open longer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frequent - to visit or go to a place often
  • Synonym: support - to give business or custom to

Etymology

From French *patroniser*, from *patron* (patron).
