to prise

1. VERB

/ praɪz / (AmE), / praɪz / (BrE)
to inquire into something in a way that is intrusive or improper; to try to obtain information that someone does not wish to reveal
intrusively inquire; seek private information
british

Examples

The reporter tried to prise details from the unwilling witness.
It's not easy to prise secrets out of him, as he's very private.
She hates it when people prise into her personal life.

Synset Examples

They pried the information out of him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pry - to try to find out private facts about someone

Etymology

British variant of 'pry' meaning to inquire, from Old Norse 'prýja' (to look inquisitively).

2. VERB

/ praɪz / (AmE), / praɪz / (BrE)
to value highly; to regard as very important or precious
to value highly or esteem greatly

Examples

She always prises honesty above everything else in her employees.
Many collectors prise rare stamps more than gold or silver.
He prised his friendship with her and was careful never to let her down.

Synset Examples

I respect his judgement
We prize his creativity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: value - to consider something important or beneficial; regard highly
  • Synonym: esteem - to respect and admire highly
  • Antonym: scorn - to treat or regard with contempt or disdain

Etymology

From Old French *prisier* (to value), from Latin *pretium* (price).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00695834-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ praɪz / (AmE), / praɪz / (BrE)
to use force or leverage to move, open, or separate something, especially when it is difficult to do so
to force open or separate with leverage
british

Examples

We tried to prise the old lid off the paint can, but it wouldn't budge.
He managed to prise open the window with a screwdriver.
She carefully prised the gem from its setting without damaging it.

Synset Examples

The burglar jimmied the lock
Raccoons managed to pry the lid off the garbage pail

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pry - to use force in order to move or open something, often with a lever

Etymology

From Old French *preisier* (to press, squeeze), influenced by 'prise' (a taking), related to Latin *prehendere* (to seize).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01596175-v
Synset POS: v
