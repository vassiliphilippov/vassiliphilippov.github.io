to boggle

1. VERB

/ ˈbɑɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbɒɡl / (BrE)
to hesitate or be unable to think clearly when faced with confusion, doubt, or fear
hesitate or be confused

Examples

She began to boggle at the complexity of the instructions given to her.
His mind would boggle whenever he thought about the vastness of the universe.
I saw him boggle in fear before stepping onto the stage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: waver - show indecision or hesitation
  • Synonym: to hesitate - pause before doing something, often due to uncertainty

Etymology

Perhaps from the Middle English 'bugge' (specter) or related to 'bogle' (a goblin or scarecrow).

2. VERB

/ ˈbɑːɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbɒɡəl / (BrE)
(of the mind or a person) to be astonished or overwhelmed to the point of being unable to think clearly; to be amazed.
be amazed or overwhelmed

Examples

The sheer size of the universe will boggle your mind.
It still boggles me how quickly technology has advanced in the past decade.
Their generosity boggled everyone at the charity gala.

Synset Examples

This boggles the mind!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to astonish - to surprise or amaze someone a lot
  • Synonym: to amaze - to fill with wonder; astonish greatly

Etymology

Of uncertain origin, perhaps related to Scots 'bogle' (ghost or goblin), suggesting something that startles or confounds.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00728115-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbɑːɡəl / (AmE), / ˈbɒɡl / (BrE)
to be filled with astonishment or fear; to hesitate or be overwhelmed due to amazement or shock
be startled or overwhelmed by amazement or fear

Examples

The complexity of the puzzle made my mind boggle.
His story was so incredible that it would boggle anyone's mind.
It boggles me how anyone could solve this in an hour.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stagger - to astonish or deeply shock someone

Etymology

Probably frequentative of 'bogle' (to hesitate), of uncertain origin, possibly related to Scottish dialect.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01914772-v
Synset POS: v
