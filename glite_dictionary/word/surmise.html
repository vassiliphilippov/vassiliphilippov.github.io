surmise

1. NOUN

/ sərˈmaɪz / (AmE), / səˈmaɪz / (BrE)
an idea or opinion based on little evidence; a conjecture or guess
an opinion based on limited evidence

Examples

Her surmise about the result of the experiment turned out to be correct.
He offered only a surmise, not a proven fact, about why she left.
Without clear evidence, any accusation is nothing more than a surmise.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guess - an estimate or opinion formed without sufficient information
  • Synonym: conjecture - an opinion or conclusion formed on the basis of incomplete information

Etymology

From Old French *surmise* (accusation), from *surmettre* (to accuse), from Latin *supermittere* (to throw upon).

2. VERB

/ sərˈmaɪz / (AmE), / səˈmaɪz / (BrE)
to suppose that something is true without having evidence to confirm it
suppose without evidence

Examples

From the look on her face, I surmised she had received bad news.
Without any concrete evidence, the detective could only surmise what had happened.
He surmised that they had left early to avoid the traffic.

Synset Examples

I suspect he is a fugitive
I surmised that the butler did it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guess - to form an opinion or answer without definite evidence
  • Degree Higher: to deduce - to reach a conclusion by reasoning

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *surmis*, past participle of *surmettre* (to allege), from Latin *supermittere* (to send over, submit).

3. VERB

/ sərˈmaɪz / (AmE), / səˈmaɪz / (BrE)
to guess or infer something without sufficient evidence; to form an opinion or idea based on incomplete information
to guess or infer without full evidence

Examples

From the clues in the letter, she surmised that he had left the country.
I can only surmise what happened based on these fragments of information.
He surmised that the company was going bankrupt after hearing rumors from staff.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guess - to estimate or suppose without sufficient information
  • Synonym: conjecture - to form an opinion based on incomplete information
  • Antonym: to know - to be aware of through observation, inquiry, or information

Etymology

From Old French *surmise* (accusation), from Medieval Latin *supermittere* (to accuse).
