proud

1. ADJ

/ praʊd / (AmE), / praʊd / (BrE)
Having or displaying great dignity or nobility, often used to describe things or places that inspire respect or admiration.
having great dignity or nobility

Examples

The proud towers of the castle stood out against the skyline.
The general spoke of his proud army marching into the city.
We gazed at the proud peaks of the mountains, rising above the clouds.

Synset Examples

a gallant pageant
lofty ships
majestic cities
proud alpine peaks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: majestic - having great dignity or grandeur

Etymology

From Old English *prūd*, adopted from Old French *prud*, from Late Latin *prōdus* (useful, virtuous).

2. ADJ

/ praʊd / (AmE), / praʊd / (BrE)
feeling deep pleasure, satisfaction, or self-respect as a result of one's own achievements, qualities, or possessions, or those of someone with whom one is closely associated.
feeling pleasure or satisfaction in achievement

Examples

Her parents were extremely proud when she graduated with honors.
Winning the championship was a proud moment for the whole team.
He felt proud to represent his community at the national conference.

Synset Examples

proud parents
a proud moment
proud to serve his country
a proud name
proud princes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ashamed - feeling embarrassed or guilty due to one's actions or characteristics
  • Degree Higher: arrogant - having an exaggerated sense of one's own importance or abilities
  • Degree Lower: modest - having a moderate or humble view of one's abilities or achievements

Etymology

From Old French 'prud' (brave, valiant), from Latin 'prode' (useful, advantageous).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01895355-a
Synset POS: a
