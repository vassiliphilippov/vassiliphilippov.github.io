transubstantiation

1. NOUN

/ ˌtrænsəbˌstænʃiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænsʌbstænʃɪˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
(in Roman Catholic doctrine) the conversion of the whole substance of the bread and wine into the body and blood of Christ during the consecration in the Eucharist, although the appearances of bread and wine remain.
change of bread and wine into Christ's body and blood (Catholic doctrine)

Examples

The doctrine of transubstantiation is central to Roman Catholic theology.
Many Christians do not interpret transubstantiation literally, preferring a symbolic understanding of the Eucharist.
At Mass, believers witness the transubstantiation of bread and wine into the body and blood of Christ.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin transubstantiatio, from trans- (across) + substantia (substance).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtræn.səbˌstæn.ʃiˈeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌtræns.ʌbˌstæn.ʃiˈeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the change of the substance or essence of something into a different substance, especially the Christian doctrine that the bread and wine during the Eucharist become the body and blood of Christ while retaining their outward appearance
change of substance; Christian doctrine about Eucharist

Examples

The doctrine of transubstantiation is central to Catholic theology.
Some poets use the term transubstantiation metaphorically to describe spiritual or personal transformation.
During the Mass, believers affirm their faith in transubstantiation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metamorphosis - a complete change of form, structure, or character
  • Synonym: transformation - a thorough or dramatic change in form or appearance

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'transubstantiatio', from Latin 'trans-' (across) + 'substantia' (substance)
