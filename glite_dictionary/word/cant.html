cant

1. NOUN

/ kænt / (AmE), / kɑːnt / (BrE)
an angle or surface formed when two surfaces meet at an angle other than 90 degrees, especially in carpentry or engineering
angle formed by surfaces meeting not at 90°

Examples

The roof's cant allowed rainwater to run off efficiently.
The engineer measured the cant of the railway track for proper alignment.
She adjusted the cant of the board to fit the sloped ceiling.

Etymology

From Old Norse kanta (to edge, trim).

2. NOUN

/ kænt / (AmE), / kænt / (BrE)
hypocritical or insincere talk, especially about religion or morals
hypocritical talk about morals or religion

Examples

The politician's speech was full of cant about honesty and service.
People grew tired of the cant preached from the pulpit every Sunday.
She saw through his cant and doubted his true intentions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hypocrisy - claiming to have moral standards without actually possessing them

Etymology

Probably from Latin 'cantare' (to sing), referring to chanting or singsong, later used derogatorily for insincere speech.

3. VERB

/ kænt / (AmE), / kænt / (BrE)
to tilt or cause to lean at an angle; to set at a slant or inclination
tilt or set at an angle

Examples

As the ship took on water, it began to cant dangerously to one side.
Workers canted the heavy slab to slide it into place more easily.
The painting was canted slightly so it hung at an odd angle.

Synset Examples

The tower is tilting
The ceiling is slanting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tilt - to cause to slope or slant
  • Synonym: lean - to incline or bend from a vertical position

Etymology

Probably from Middle French cant (edge, corner), or related to Old Norse kanta (to edge).

4. NOUN

/ kænt / (AmE), / kænt / (BrE)
a slope or tilt, especially in a road or track, where the outer edge is higher than the inner edge to counteract centrifugal force
sloping edge of a road or track

Examples

The cant of the racing track allows cars to take the bend at higher speeds.
Engineers calculated the proper cant for the railway curve to ensure passenger comfort.
Rainwater runs off quickly due to the cant built into the road surface.

Etymology

From Latin *canthus* (iron tire of a wheel), influenced by Old French *chant* (edge, corner).

6. NOUN

/ kænt / (AmE), / kɑːnt / (BrE)
stock phrases or expressions that have become meaningless or trite through overuse; formulaic or conventional language lacking sincerity or originality.
overused, trite expressions

Examples

Political speeches are often full of empty cant that fails to inspire real change.
His apology sounded like nothing more than the usual cant you hear from public figures.
Journalists tried to get past the official cant to uncover the real story.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cliché - an overused phrase or expression
  • Synonym: platitude - a remark or statement, especially one with a moral content, that has been used too often to be interesting or thoughtful

Etymology

From Latin 'cantare' (to sing), later meaning 'chant', and then shifting to 'jargon, insincere language' in English.
