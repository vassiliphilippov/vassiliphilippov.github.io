revel

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛvəl / (AmE), / ˈrɛvəl / (BrE)
to celebrate or take great pleasure in a lively and noisy way, especially by drinking and dancing
celebrate noisily; take great pleasure

Examples

The students revelled in their victory by dancing and singing through the night.
He loves to revel in the excitement of the festival every year.
Crowds revelled in the city streets after the national team's win.

Synset Examples

The members of the wedding party made merry all night
Let's whoop it up — the boss is gone!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to celebrate - to observe with festivities or ceremonies
  • Synonym: carouse - to drink and party noisily
  • Antonym: to mourn - to feel or express sadness, especially for someone's death

Etymology

From Old French 'reveler' (to rebel, make merry), from Latin 'rebellare' (to rebel).

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛvəl / (AmE), / ˈrɛvəl / (BrE)
a lively and noisy celebration, especially with drinking and dancing
lively, noisy celebration

Examples

The town square was filled with music and laughter during the annual revel.
After the victory, the team joined a joyous revel that lasted all night.
Centuries ago, people would gather in the castle for a grand revel each midsummer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: celebration - a special event or party
  • Synonym: party - a social gathering for enjoyment
  • Degree Higher: orgy - an event involving excessive or unrestrained activity, especially revelry

Etymology

From Old French *revel* (rebellion, outcry, revelry), from *reveler* (to rebel), from Latin *rebellare* (to rebel).

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛvəl / (AmE), / ˈrɛvəl / (BrE)
to take great pleasure or delight in something
take great pleasure; delight

Examples

She revels in the thrill of competition and refuses to back down.
He reveled in the praise he received for his hard work.
They revel in the beauty of the countryside during their weekend hikes.

Synset Examples

he delights in his granddaughter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delight - to take great pleasure or joy in
  • Antonym: to abhor - to regard with disgust and hatred

Etymology

From Old French *reveler* (to rebel, make merry), from Latin *rebellare* (to rebel).
