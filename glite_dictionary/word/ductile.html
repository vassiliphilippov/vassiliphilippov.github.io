ductile

1. ADJ

/ ˈdʌk.təl / (AmE), / ˈdʌk.taɪl / (BrE)
able to be drawn out into a thin wire or bent into shape without breaking; easily shaped or molded, especially describing metals.
able to be shaped or drawn out (metals)

Examples

Copper is a highly ductile metal, making it ideal for electrical wiring.
The blacksmith praised the ductile nature of the heated steel.
Gold is both ductile and malleable, allowing it to be shaped into fine jewelry.

Synset Examples

ductile copper
malleable metals such as gold
they soaked the leather to made it pliable
pliant molten glass
made of highly tensile steel alloy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: malleable - able to be easily shaped or hammered without breaking
  • Antonym: brittle - hard but likely to break or shatter easily

Etymology

From Latin 'ductilis' (that may be led or drawn), from 'ducere' (to lead).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdʌktaɪl / (AmE), / ˈdʌktaɪl / (BrE)
(of a person or character) easily influenced or led; yielding readily to influence or persuasion.
easily influenced or persuaded

Examples

She was a ductile student, always willing to accept guidance from her mentors.
The politician found the young advisor to be especially ductile in adopting his viewpoints.
Children can be quite ductile when exposed to strong role models.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: malleable - (of a person) easily influenced; pliable
  • Synonym: pliant - easily influenced or directed; yielding
  • Antonym: stubborn - refusing to change one's mind or to be influenced

Etymology

From Latin ductilis ‘that may be led or drawn’, from ducere ‘to lead’.
