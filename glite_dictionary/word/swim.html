swim

1. NOUN

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
an act or period of moving through water by using the arms and legs
an act of swimming

Examples

After a long hike, they enjoyed a refreshing swim in the lake.
She goes for a swim every morning to stay fit.
The children raced to see who could finish their swim the fastest.

Synset Examples

it was the swimming they enjoyed most
they took a short swim in the pool

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swimming - the activity or sport of moving through water using the body
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00443055-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
(of an object) to be covered with or submerged in a liquid, especially abundantly.
be covered/submerged in liquid

Examples

The vegetables were swimming in a pool of oil.
His pancakes arrived swimming in syrup, just the way he liked them.
The pasta was swimming in creamy sauce, almost too much to eat.

Synset Examples

the meat was swimming in a fatty gravy
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02761786-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
to move through water by making movements with the body, arms, or legs
move through water using body

Examples

She learned to swim at a young age.
He watched the ducks swim across the pond every morning.
The children swam in the lake during their summer vacation.

Synset Examples

We had to swim for 20 minutes to reach the shore
a big fish was swimming in the tank

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: float - to remain on the surface of a liquid without sinking
  • Degree Higher: dive - to jump into water headfirst or go deep below the surface

Etymology

Old English 'swimman' (to move in water), related to Old High German 'swimmen', Old Norse 'svimma'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01964770-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
(of a person's head or brain) to feel dizzy or as if it is spinning, especially after drinking alcohol or when overwhelmed.
feel dizzy or as if spinning

Examples

After spinning around quickly, my head began to swim and I had to sit down.
His mind started to swim with confusion after hearing the shocking news.
The wine made her head swim, so she leaned against the wall for support.

Synset Examples

my brain is swimming after the bottle of champagne

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spin - feel as if turning around rapidly; dizzy sensation
  • Synonym: reel - feel unsteady or dizzy; stagger

5. VERB

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
to move smoothly or as if gliding through a fluid or yielding substance, especially in a way similar to moving through water.
move or glide smoothly as through water

Examples

The clouds seemed to swim across the sky in the gentle wind.
Tiny fish swim effortlessly through the coral reefs.
His thoughts began to swim as the medication took effect.

Synset Examples

this snake swims through the soil where it lives

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glide - to move smoothly and continuously along, as if without effort

Etymology

Old English *swimman* (to swim), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01908642-v
Synset POS: v

6. VERB

/ swɪm / (AmE), / swɪm / (BrE)
to be or remain afloat either on or just below the surface of a liquid and not sink
remain afloat in water or other liquid

Examples

The wood will swim on the surface and not sink to the bottom.
After falling overboard, she managed to swim until help arrived.
Some insects can swim just below the water's surface without sinking.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: float - to rest or move on or near the surface of a liquid without sinking
  • Antonym: sink - descend below the surface of a liquid; cease to float

Etymology

Old English *swimman* (to move in water), of Germanic origin.
