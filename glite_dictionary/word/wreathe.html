to wreathe

1. VERB

/ riːð / (AmE), / riːð / (BrE)
to decorate or cover something with a wreath or wreaths
decorate with a wreath

Examples

They wreathed the door with fresh holly for the holidays.
Each grave was wreathed with flowers by the mourners.
Volunteers spent hours wreathing the hall with garlands for the celebration.

Synset Examples

wreathe the grave site

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to decorate - to make something look more attractive by adding items to it
  • Synonym: to adorn - to make more beautiful or attractive

Etymology

From Old English wrīthan (to twist), related to the noun 'wreath'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01519687-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rið / (AmE), / riːð / (BrE)
to move or twist with slow, sinuous, or twisting motions
move in a twisting, sinuous way

Examples

Smoke began to wreathe around the ruined chimney.
Vines wreathe along the old garden fence, moving slowly with the breeze.
Her body wreathed gracefully through the water as she swam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coil - to move in a spiral or circular course
  • Synonym: twist - to move in a winding or turning motion

Etymology

From Old English *wrīthan* (to twist) via Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01872892-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rið / (AmE), / riːð / (BrE)
to form or arrange into a wreath, or to cover or surround with something in the shape of a wreath
form into a wreath or ring

Examples

The florist began to wreathe the flowers into a beautiful garland for the wedding.
Smoke seemed to wreathe the old cottage after the fire started in the kitchen.
She liked to wreathe ribbons and leaves together to decorate the door.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twist - to form into a spiral or coil
  • Synonym: wrap - to cover or enclose something

Etymology

From Old English 'wrīthan' (to twist, wind), related to 'writhe'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01519597-v
Synset POS: v
