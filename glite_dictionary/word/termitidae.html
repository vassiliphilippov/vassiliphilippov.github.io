Termitidae

1. NOUN

/ tərˈmɪtɪˌdi / (AmE), / tɜːˈmɪtɪdiː / (BrE)
A large family of termites whose members are social insects known for living in colonies and feeding on wood and plant material.
family of termites (insects)

Examples

The Termitidae are among the most diverse and abundant termite families worldwide.
Researchers studied the nesting habits of Termitidae in tropical forests.
Many species in the Termitidae play an important role in decomposition and soil formation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: termite - a small, pale soft-bodied social insect that lives in colonies

Etymology

From modern Latin 'Termitidae', from 'Termes' (the type genus) plus '-idae' (indicating family).

2. NOUN

/ tərˈmɪtɪˌdiː / (AmE), / tɜːˈmɪtɪˌdiː / (BrE)
the largest family of termites, comprising most of the commonly known termites.
a large family of termites

Examples

Termitidae are responsible for building the massive mounds seen in some African landscapes.
Scientists discovered several new species within the Termitidae family during their expedition.
Most termite infestations in homes are caused by members of the Termitidae family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: termites - social insects of the order Blattodea, known for living in colonies

Etymology

From New Latin, based on 'Termes' (a genus of termites) + '-idae' (family suffix in zoological taxonomy).
