ironic

1. ADJ

/ aɪˈrɑnɪk / (AmE), / aɪˈrɒnɪk / (BrE)
happening in a way that is different from what is expected, often producing an amusing or poignant result; relating to or characteristic of irony.
showing or involving irony

Examples

It is ironic that the fire station burned down last night.
Her ironic smile made it clear that she doubted his story.
The fact that the doctor fell ill on his own birthday was deeply ironic.

Synset Examples

madness, an ironic fate for such a clear thinker
it was ironical that the well-planned scheme failed so completely

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ironic - using words to express something different from or opposite to the literal meaning
  • Antonym: sincere - showing genuine, honest feelings without irony

Etymology

From French ironique, from Latin ironicus, from Greek eirōnikos (dissembling, feigning ignorance).

2. ADJ

/ aɪˈrɑːnɪk / (AmE), / aɪˈrɒnɪk / (BrE)
Expressing an idea, remark, or situation in a way that is deliberately contrary to what is expected, often to be humorously sarcastic or mocking.
humorously sarcastic or mocking

Examples

Her ironic comments during the debate made the audience laugh.
He gave her an ironic smile after she missed the obvious answer.
The ironic tone of his letter made it clear he was not truly angry.

Synset Examples

dry humor
an ironic remark often conveys an intended meaning obliquely
an ironic novel
an ironical smile
with a wry Scottish wit

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sarcastic - using irony to mock or convey contempt
  • Antonym: sincere - genuine; not ironic or mocking

Etymology

From French 'ironique', from Latin 'ironicus', from Greek 'eirōnikos' (dissembling, feigning ignorance).
