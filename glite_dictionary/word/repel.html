to repel

1. VERB

/ rəˈpɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈpɛl / (BrE)
to force someone or something back or away, especially by fighting or resistance
drive back or ward off

Examples

The soldiers successfully repelled the enemy attack at dawn.
A good mosquito spray is designed to repel insects.
He managed to repel their advances by standing firm.

Synset Examples

repel the attacker
fight off the onslaught
rebuff the attack

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repulse - to drive back, especially an attack; to reject with force
  • Antonym: to attract - to cause to come towards; opposite of repel

Etymology

From Latin repellere (to drive back), from re- (back) + pellere (to drive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01133623-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈpɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈpɛl / (BrE)
to cause a strong feeling of dislike or aversion in someone
cause aversion or strong dislike

Examples

The smell of the rotten food would repel almost anyone.
His arrogance tends to repel people rather than impress them.
Some people are repelled by horror movies, while others love them.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to attract - draw to oneself; cause interest or liking

Etymology

From Latin repellere, from re- 'back' + pellere 'to drive'.

3. VERB

/ rɪˈpɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈpɛl / (BrE)
to reject or refuse (someone or something) in a blunt or abrupt manner
to reject outright or bluntly

Examples

She repelled his request to join the club without hesitation.
The company repelled repeated offers to be acquired by a competitor.
He tried to make amends, but she repelled all his attempts at reconciliation.
