transitive

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrænzətɪv / (AmE), / ˈtrænsətɪv / (BrE)
A verb that requires a direct object to complete its meaning.
verb requiring a direct object

Examples

The verb 'eat' is a transitive in the sentence 'She ate an apple.'
In grammar class, we learned to distinguish between a transitive and an intransitive verb.
Some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive, depending on how they are used.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intransitive - a verb that does not require a direct object

Etymology

From Latin 'transitivus', from 'transire' (to go across).

2. ADJ

/ ˈtræn.sə.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈtræn.zɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
(of a verb) taking a direct object; requiring a direct object to complete its meaning.
verb requiring a direct object

Examples

In the sentence 'She reads books,' the verb 'reads' is transitive because it takes an object.
Most English verbs are either transitive, intransitive, or can be both depending on context.
Can you give an example of a transitive verb in a sentence?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intransitive - (of a verb) not taking a direct object

Etymology

From Latin transitivus (passing over), from transitus, past participle of transire (to go across).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02498383-a
Synset POS: a
