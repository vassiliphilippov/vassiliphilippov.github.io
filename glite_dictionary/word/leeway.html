leeway

1. NOUN

/ ˈliˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈliːˌweɪ / (BrE)
freedom to move or act within set limits; a margin of safety or error allowed
freedom or margin within limits

Examples

The manager gave her team ample leeway to complete the project as they saw fit.
You have a little leeway in your schedule to accommodate unexpected delays.
There isn't much leeway for mistakes during a live performance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: latitude - freedom to act or think as one wishes

Etymology

Originally a nautical term, from 'lee' (the sheltered side) + 'way' (movement).

2. NOUN

/ ˈliˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈliːweɪ / (BrE)
The sideways drift of a ship or aircraft away from its intended course, especially due to wind or currents.
sideways drift off course (ship/plane)

Examples

Strong winds caused the ship to develop significant leeway during the storm.
The pilot compensated for leeway to stay on the correct flight path.
Careful navigation is required to account for leeway in rough seas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drift - gradual movement away from course or position

Etymology

Nautical term from 'lee' (sheltered side) + 'way'.

Synset POS: n
