artiodactyl

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑːrti.oʊˈdæk.təl / (AmE), / ˌɑː.ti.əˈdæk.tɪl / (BrE)
a hoofed mammal with an even number of functional toes on each foot, belonging to the order Artiodactyla
even-toed hoofed mammal

Examples

The giraffe is an artiodactyl distinguished by its long neck and legs.
Many farm animals, such as cows and sheep, are classified as artiodactyls.
Fossil evidence suggests that whales evolved from ancient artiodactyl ancestors.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: perissodactyl - a mammal with an odd number of toes on each foot (order Perissodactyla)

Etymology

From Greek 'artios' (even-numbered) + 'daktulos' (finger, toe).

2. ADJ

/ ˌɑːr.ti.oʊˈdæk.tɪl / (AmE), / ˌɑː.ti.əˈdæk.tɪl / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of mammals of the order Artiodactyla, which have an even number of toes on each foot, such as deer, pigs, and camels
relating to even-toed hoofed mammals

Examples

The artiodactyl lineage includes animals like cows, sheep, and hippos.
Some prehistoric artiodactyl species are known only from fossil records.
A key distinguishing feature of artiodactyl mammals is their even-toed hoof structure.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: perissodactyl - relating to mammals of the order Perissodactyla (odd-toed ungulates)

Etymology

From New Latin Artiodactyla, from Greek artios (even) + daktulos (finger, toe).
