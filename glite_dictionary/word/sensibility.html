sensibility

1. NOUN

/ sɛn.səˈbɪl.ə.ti / (AmE), / sɛn.sɪˈbɪl.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the ability to respond emotionally or intellectually to something; mental responsiveness and awareness.
mental responsiveness and awareness

Examples

The novel demonstrates the author's remarkable sensibility to cultural differences.
Her refined artistic sensibility set her work apart from others in her field.
Traveling broadened his sensibility and made him more open-minded.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensitivity - the quality of being sensitive to sensations or impressions; responsiveness
  • Antonym: insensitivity - lack of responsiveness to emotional or intellectual impressions

Etymology

From French 'sensibilité', from Late Latin 'sensibilitas' (capability of being perceived by the senses).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛnsəˈbɪlɪti / (AmE), / ˌsɛnsɪˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
the ability to perceive or respond to external stimuli; the faculty of sensation
ability to feel or sense stimuli

Examples

Damage to the nerve reduced the sensibility of her fingertips.
Certain medications can dull your sensibility to pain.
High sensibility in the skin helps animals detect environmental changes.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensitivity - the quality of being sensitive to stimuli

Etymology

From Latin sensibilitas, from sensibilis ‘perceptible by the senses’.

3. NOUN

/ ˌsɛn.səˈbɪl.ə.ti / (AmE), / ˌsɛnsɪˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
the ability to perceive and respond to delicate or subtle influences, especially with refined emotional sensitivity; acute awareness of or responsiveness to emotional or aesthetic experiences
refined sensitivity or emotional awareness

Examples

The film's violence was disturbing to her sensibility.
Artists often have a heightened sensibility to color and form.
The cruelty of the remark offended his sensibility.

Synset Examples

cruelty offended his sensibility

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensitivity - the quality of being sensitive; responsiveness to external influences
  • Antonym: insensibility - lack of awareness or responsiveness to emotional or physical stimuli

Etymology

From Latin 'sensibilitas' (capability of sensation), from 'sensibilis' (able to feel).
