to deputize

1. VERB

/ ˈdɛpjəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɛpjʊtʌɪz / (BrE)
to appoint someone as a substitute or give someone the power to act on behalf of another, especially officially or temporarily
appoint as substitute or representative

Examples

The sheriff decided to deputize several townspeople during the emergency.
She was deputized to represent her manager at the conference.
In his absence, he will deputize his assistant to run the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to appoint - to choose someone for a job or position

Etymology

From French *députer* (appoint, delegate), from Latin *deputare* (to assign, allot).

2. VERB

/ ˈdɛpjəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɛpjʊtaɪz / (BrE)
to act as a substitute for another person, especially in an official capacity
act as a substitute

Examples

The assistant manager was asked to deputize for the boss during her vacation.
If the team leader is absent, someone else must deputize at the meeting.
She will deputize for the principal at tomorrow's assembly.

Synset Examples

She stood in for the soprano who suffered from a cold

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stand in - to act as a substitute for someone
  • Synonym: to replace - to take the place of someone or something

Etymology

From Medieval Latin deputizare, from deputiare (to assign, appoint).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02399804-v
Synset POS: v
