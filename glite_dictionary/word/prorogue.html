to prorogue

1. VERB

/ proʊˈroʊɡ / (AmE), / prəˈrəʊɡ / (BrE)
(of a legislative assembly) to discontinue a session without dissolving it, especially by royal or executive authority.
suspend a legislature's session without dissolution
legal

Examples

The monarch retains the power to prorogue Parliament at the government's request.
The Prime Minister announced his intention to prorogue the legislative session until autumn.
It is controversial to prorogue Parliament during a period of political uncertainty.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adjourn - suspend proceedings to another time or place
  • Antonym: dissolve - to officially bring a legislative body to an end

Etymology

From Latin prorogare (to prolong, defer), from pro- (forward) + rogare (to ask).

2. VERB

/ proʊˈroʊɡ / (AmE), / prəˈrəʊɡ / (BrE)
(formal) To discontinue a session of a parliament or other legislative assembly without dissolving it, or to postpone or defer to a later time.
formally end or postpone (a legislative session or event)

Examples

The president has the power to prorogue parliament during a political crisis.
The hearing was prorogued until further evidence could be gathered.
After much debate, the committee decided to prorogue the vote until next week.

Synset Examples

let's postpone the exam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to postpone - defer to a later time
  • Synonym: to adjourn - suspend a meeting or session to a future time

Etymology

From Latin *prorogare* (to prolong, defer), via Old French *proroguer*.
