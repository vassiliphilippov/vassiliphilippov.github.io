nosedive

1. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊz.daɪv / (AmE), / ˈnəʊz.daɪv / (BrE)
A steep, sudden downward movement with the front end of an aircraft pointing down.
steep dive by an aircraft

Examples

The plane went into a nosedive after losing engine power.
A sudden nosedive caused panic among the passengers.
The pilot quickly corrected the nosedive and regained control.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dive - a steep downward drop or movement

Etymology

From 'nose' (front part of an aircraft) + 'dive'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊzˌdaɪv / (AmE), / ˈnəʊzˌdaɪv / (BrE)
A sudden and steep fall or rapid decline in value, level, or amount.
sudden sharp drop or decline

Examples

After the scandal, the company's reputation took a nosedive.
There was a dramatic nosedive in demand during the winter months.
The sudden nosedive of the stock market shocked investors.

Synset Examples

the stock took a nosedive

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunge - a sudden and significant drop or fall
  • Antonym: increase - an upward change in value or amount, opposite of decrease

Etymology

Formed from "nose" + "dive", originally referring to the steep downward dive of an aircraft.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13544291-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈnoʊzˌdaɪv / (AmE), / ˈnəʊzˌdaɪv / (BrE)
(of an aircraft) to plunge suddenly downwards with the nose or front first; to drop sharply in a similar manner.
plunge nose first (of aircraft)

Examples

The plane began to nosedive after engine failure.
Stocks may nosedive if the company reports poor earnings next week.
She felt her confidence nosedive when she received the criticism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunge - to fall or jump suddenly and quickly
  • Antonym: to ascend - to go up or rise

Etymology

From 'nose' + 'dive', originally used in aviation during the early 20th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
