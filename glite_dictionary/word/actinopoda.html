Actinopoda

1. NOUN

/ ˌæktɪˈnɑːpədə / (AmE), / ˌæktɪˈnɒpədə / (BrE)
A group of single-celled aquatic organisms, including heliozoans and radiolarians, that move and feed using slender, ray-like extensions.
group of protozoan organisms
medical

Examples

Many marine biologists study Actinopoda to understand microscopic aquatic ecosystems.
Radiolarians, a type of Actinopoda, have intricate mineral skeletons visible under a microscope.
Actinopoda play an important role in the ocean's food web by feeding on smaller plankton.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protozoan - single-celled microscopic organism
  • Synonym: heliozoan - type of spherical, aquatic protozoan with radiating pseudopods

Etymology

From Greek 'aktis' (ray) + 'pous, podos' (foot), referring to their ray-like pseudopodia.

2. NOUN

/ ˌæktɪˈnɑpədə / (AmE), / ˌæktɪˈnɒpədə / (BrE)
A subclass of protozoans that includes the heliozoans and radiolarians, characterized by their radiating threadlike pseudopodia.
subclass of protozoans (heliozoans and radiolarians)

Examples

Heliozoans belong to the Actinopoda, distinguished by their radial symmetry and stiff pseudopodia.
Radiolarians, part of the Actinopoda, are known for their intricate mineral skeletons.
Scientists study the Actinopoda to better understand protozoan evolution and diversity.

Etymology

From Greek 'aktis' (ray) + 'pous' (foot), referring to their ray-like pseudopodia.
