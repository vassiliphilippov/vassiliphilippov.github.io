ratiocination

1. NOUN

/ ˌrætiˌɑːsɪˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrætiːˌɒsɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of reasoning in a logical and methodical manner
logical, methodical reasoning

Examples

His detailed ratiocination led the committee to accept his proposal.
Detective stories often feature characters known for their exceptional ratiocination.
She preferred ratiocination over intuition when making important decisions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reasoning - the act of thinking about something in a logical, sensible way
  • Antonym: intuition - the ability to understand something instinctively, without the need for conscious reasoning

Etymology

From Latin ratiocinatio (reasoning), from ratiocinari (to reason), from ratio (reason).

2. NOUN

/ ˌrætiˌɑsəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrætiˌɒsɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the conclusion or proposition arrived at by logical reasoning, especially as the result of syllogistic argument
conclusion from logical reasoning

Examples

Her brilliant ratiocination convinced even her most skeptical colleagues.
The trial hinged on the ratiocination presented by the defense attorney.
Logical argument requires clear premises and careful ratiocination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inference - a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning

Etymology

From Latin 'ratiocinatio' (a reckoning, calculation, reasoning), from 'ratiocinari' (to calculate, deliberate).
