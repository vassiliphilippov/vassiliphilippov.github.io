largo

1. ADV

/ ˈlɑrɡoʊ / (AmE), / ˈlɑːɡəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) slowly and broadly.
in music, slowly and broadly

Examples

The piece should be played largo to convey its solemn mood.
The conductor instructed the orchestra to start largo and gradually increase the tempo.
This sonata opens with a largo movement, setting a reflective tone.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: adagio - at a slow tempo (in music)
  • Antonym: presto - at a very fast tempo (in music)

Etymology

From Italian largo (broad, wide), used in music to indicate tempo.

2. ADJ

/ ˈlɑrɡoʊ / (AmE), / ˈlɑːɡəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) very slow in tempo and broad in manner.
very slow and broad (music)

Examples

The first movement of the symphony is marked largo, creating an atmosphere of solemnity.
The conductor instructed the orchestra to play the piece largo for a more dramatic effect.
Her new composition opens with a haunting largo passage that sets a somber mood.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: adagio - slow in tempo (music)
  • Degree Higher: andante - moderately slow tempo (music)
  • Antonym: presto - very fast tempo (music)

Etymology

From Italian 'largo' meaning 'broad, wide', used in musical contexts to denote a broad, slow tempo.

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɑrˌgoʊ / (AmE), / ˈlɑːgəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) a passage or composition to be performed very slowly and with dignified style.
music: very slow, dignified passage

Examples

The symphony’s third movement is marked largo, requiring the orchestra to play with great restraint.
Her favorite part of the concerto is the opening largo, which beautifully sets a solemn mood.
The conductor asked the musicians to pay special attention to the dynamics during the largo section.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: adagio - a musical passage or tempo that is slow and leisurely
  • Degree Higher: andante - a musical passage or tempo that is at a walking pace

Etymology

From Italian largo (broad, wide), used as a musical term to indicate broad tempo.
