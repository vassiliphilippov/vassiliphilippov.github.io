equinoctial

1. ADJ

/ ˌiːkwəˈnɑːkʃəl / (AmE), / ˌiːkwɪˈnɒkʃəl / (BrE)
relating to or near the equator, especially referring to geographic or climatic features.
relating to the equator

Examples

The equinoctial regions experience little variation in daylight throughout the year.
Ancient sailors were wary of the violent equinoctial storms near the equator.
Many unique plant species thrive in the equinoctial climates of South America.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equatorial - relating to or situated at the equator

Etymology

From Late Latin aequinoctialis, from aequinoctium (equinox), relating originally to the time when day and night are equal.

2. NOUN

/ ˌiːkwəˈnɑːkʃəl / (AmE), / ˌiːkwɪˈnɒkʃəl / (BrE)
The great circle on the celestial sphere that is midway between the celestial poles; also known as the celestial equator.
the celestial equator

Examples

Ancient astronomers mapped the equinoctial to chart the movement of the stars.
The sun crosses the equinoctial twice a year at the equinoxes.
Many star charts prominently display the equinoctial as a reference point.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: celestial equator - the imaginary circle equidistant from the celestial poles

Etymology

From Latin aequinoctialis, from aequinoctium (equinox).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08537373-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˌiːkwɪˈnɑːkʃəl / (AmE), / ˌiːkwɪˈnɒkʃəl / (BrE)
Relating to or happening at the time of an equinox, when day and night are of equal length.
relating to the equinox

Examples

The equinoctial storm arrived just as the days and nights reached equal length.
During the equinoctial period, the sunrise and sunset times are nearly twelve hours apart.
Sailors once dreaded the equinoctial gales that swept across the Atlantic in the spring and autumn.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equinox - the time or date twice each year at which day and night are of equal length

Etymology

From Latin aequinoctialis, from aequinoctium (equinox).
