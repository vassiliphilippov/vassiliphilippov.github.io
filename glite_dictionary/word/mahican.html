Mahican

1. NOUN

/ məˈhiːkən / (AmE), / məˈhiːkən / (BrE)
the Algonquian language traditionally spoken by the Mohican people of the northeastern United States
Algonquian language of the Mohican people

Examples

Few people today are fluent in Mahican, as the language has nearly disappeared.
Linguists are working to preserve Mahican through recordings and educational programs.
The stories were originally told in Mahican, but now exist mostly in English translation.

Etymology

From the name of the Mohican people, potentially via Dutch 'Mohikaan'.

2. NOUN

/ məˈhiːkən / (AmE), / məˈhiːkən / (BrE)
a member of an Algonquian-speaking Native American people formerly living in the Hudson Valley and eastward to the Housatonic River
a Native American people from the Hudson Valley

Examples

The Mahican were historically located along the Hudson River in what is now New York State.
A Mahican delegation met with colonial leaders to discuss land treaties.
Descendants of the Mahican people still preserve aspects of their language and culture today.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Mohican - a different Native American people often confused with the Mahican

Etymology

From Mahican self-designation, possibly via Dutch from the tribe's own name.
