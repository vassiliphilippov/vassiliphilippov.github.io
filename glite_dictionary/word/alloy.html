alloy

1. VERB

/ əˈlɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈælɔɪ / (BrE)
to combine (a metal) with one or more other elements, usually metals, to form an alloy.
combine metals to make an alloy

Examples

Many manufacturers alloy copper with tin to produce bronze.
Gold is often alloyed with other metals to create jewelry of various strengths.
The company has developed a new method for alloying lightweight metals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mix - to combine or blend substances together

Etymology

From Old French 'aloiier', from Late Latin 'alligare' (to bind together).

2. VERB

/ əˈlɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈælɔɪ / (BrE)
to reduce the value or purity of (a precious metal) by mixing it with a less valuable metal; to debase.
reduce metal purity by mixing with base metal

Examples

Dishonest merchants would sometimes alloy gold coins with copper to increase their profits.
The quality of the silver was alloyed by adding cheaper metals during production.
To reduce costs, the jeweler decided to alloy the platinum with a small amount of nickel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to debase - to reduce in quality or value

Etymology

From Old French *aloi* (alloy), from Latin *alligare* (to bind to).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00494453-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈælˌɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈælɔɪ / (BrE)
A substance made by mixing two or more metals, or a metal and another element, usually by melting them together.
mixture of metals

Examples

Brass is an alloy of copper and zinc, commonly used for making musical instruments.
Many coins are made from an alloy rather than pure metal to increase durability.
Stainless steel is an alloy containing iron, chromium, and often nickel.

Synset Examples

brass is an alloy of zinc and copper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metal - a solid material, typically hard, shiny, malleable, fusible, and ductile, with good electrical and thermal conductivity

Etymology

From Old French 'aloi' or 'aloier' (combine), from Latin 'alligare' (to bind to).
