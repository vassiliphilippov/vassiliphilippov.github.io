to debouch

1. VERB

/ dɪˈbaʊtʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈbaʊtʃ / (BrE)
(of a body of troops or people) to emerge or march out from a narrow or confined space into open ground.
emerge from a narrow place into open ground

Examples

The infantry will debouch from the forest just after sunrise.
As they reached the end of the pass, the troops debouched onto a wide plain.
The hikers debouched from the narrow alley into the bustling square.

Synset Examples

The regiments debouched from the valley

Etymology

From French 'déboucher', from dé- (dis-) + bouche (mouth, opening).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈbaʊtʃ / (AmE), / dɪˈbaʊtʃ / (BrE)
(of a river, road, or passage) to emerge or flow out from a confined space into a wider area.
emerge or flow out (esp. of rivers)

Examples

The narrow stream debouches into a wide lagoon just beyond the town.
At the end of the canyon, the trail debouches suddenly onto an open plain.
Several mountain passes debouch into the fertile valley below.

Synset Examples

The tributary debouched into the big river

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to emerge - to move out of or away from something and become visible

Etymology

From French 'déboucher', from 'des-' (from) + 'bouche' (mouth).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00531371-v
Synset POS: v
