pantheon

1. NOUN

/ ˈpænθiˌɑn / (AmE), / ˈpanθɪən / (BrE)
A monument commemorating a nation's most celebrated dead heroes, often serving as their burial place or a site of honor.
monument for a nation's dead heroes

Examples

The country built a grand pantheon to honor its fallen soldiers.
Many heroes of the French Revolution are buried in the Pantheon in Paris.
Visiting the national pantheon inspired a sense of pride and remembrance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mausoleum - a building for housing tombs
  • Synonym: memorial - a structure commemorating a person or event

Etymology

From Greek 'pantheion', meaning 'temple of all the gods'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpænθiˌɑn / (AmE), / ˈpanθɪən / (BrE)
all the gods of a particular religion or tradition regarded collectively.
all the gods of a religion

Examples

In ancient Roman culture, the pantheon included Jupiter, Mars, Venus, and many other deities.
Greek mythology features a complex pantheon that has influenced literature and art for centuries.
Every religion has its own pantheon of figures, both benevolent and malevolent.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deity - a god or goddess
  • Synonym: polytheism - belief in or worship of more than one god

Etymology

From Greek 'pantheion' (temple of all the gods), from 'pan-' (all) + 'theos' (god).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpænθiˌɑn / (AmE), / ˈpænθiən / (BrE)
(in antiquity) A temple dedicated to all the gods, especially in ancient Rome or Greece.
temple to all the gods (antiquity)

Examples

The Pantheon in Rome is one of the best-preserved buildings from ancient times.
Tourists were amazed by the grandeur of the ancient pantheon they visited during their trip.
In antiquity, rulers would often commission a pantheon to honor the gods of their city.

Etymology

From Greek pantheion, from pan- (all) + theoi (gods).
