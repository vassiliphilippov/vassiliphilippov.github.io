to insufflate

1. VERB

/ ˈɪn.səˌfleɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.sə.fleɪt / (BrE)
to blow or breathe air, gas, or vapor into a body cavity, organ, or tube, especially for medical or scientific purposes
blow air or gas into (esp. medically)
medical

Examples

The doctor must insufflate air into the patient's lungs during the procedure.
Surgeons often insufflate carbon dioxide into the abdomen to improve visibility during laparoscopic surgery.
Technicians carefully insufflate the gas to control internal pressure in the chamber.

Etymology

From Latin insufflare (to blow into), from in- (into) + sufflare (to blow).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈsʌfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈsʌfleɪt / (BrE)
to breathe or blow onto something as a ritual or sacramental act, especially to symbolize the action of the Holy Spirit
to blow on as a ritual act

Examples

During the ancient ceremony, the priest would insufflate the baptismal water to invoke the Holy Spirit.
It was customary for the celebrant to insufflate over the bread before the blessing.
The bishop insufflated upon the candidate’s forehead as part of the rite.

Etymology

From Latin insufflare (to blow into, to inspire), from in- (in, into) + sufflare (to blow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02314781-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈsʌfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈsʌfleɪt / (BrE)
to treat by blowing a powder or vapor into a bodily cavity, especially for medical purposes
introduce powder or vapor into a cavity (medical)
medical

Examples

The doctor decided to insufflate the medication directly into the patient's nasal cavity.
During surgery, carbon dioxide was insufflated to expand the abdominal cavity.
Some treatments require nurses to insufflate powdered drugs through a special device.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to inhale - to breathe in; take into the lungs
  • Synonym: to instill - introduce (a liquid, especially as a medical treatment) drop by drop

Etymology

From Latin insufflare, meaning 'to blow into', from in- (into) + sufflare (to blow).
