log

1. NOUN

/ lɑɡ / (AmE), / lɒɡ / (BrE)
In mathematics, the exponent to which a base must be raised to produce a given number; shorthand for 'logarithm'.
mathematical exponent (logarithm)

Examples

The log of 100 to base 10 is 2.
To solve the equation, take the log of both sides.
In calculus, we often use the log function when dealing with exponential growth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: logarithm - the exponent indicating the power to which a base number is raised to produce a given number

Etymology

Shortened from 'logarithm', itself from Greek 'logos' (proportion, word) and 'arithmos' (number).

2. NOUN

/ lɑɡ / (AmE), / lɒɡ / (BrE)
an official written record of events, especially the details of a voyage by ship or plane
official written record of a journey

Examples

The captain updated the log every hour during the voyage.
According to the flight log, the plane left on time and arrived early.
The scientist kept a detailed log of weather conditions throughout the expedition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: journal - a diary or record of daily events

Etymology

From Middle English logge (chunk of wood), with the sense of 'record' originating from the nautical practice of using a log to measure a ship's speed.

4. NOUN

/ lɑːɡ / (AmE), / lɒɡ / (BrE)
a device, consisting of a float attached to a knotted line towed from a ship, used to measure the ship's speed through the water
device for measuring a ship's speed

Examples

The captain ordered the crew to cast the log to determine the ship's speed.
Before modern instruments, sailors relied on the log to estimate how fast they were traveling.
The old sailor explained how the knots in the log line were used to calculate speed.

Etymology

Originally referring to a piece of wood used for this purpose; later generalized to various speed-measuring devices.
