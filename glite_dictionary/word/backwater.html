backwater

1. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌwɑtɚ / (AmE), / ˈbækwɔːtə / (BrE)
a part of a river or other body of water that is out of the main current, often created by flood, tide, or by being held or forced back, such as by a dam
part of a river or water held back from the main flow

Examples

The flood left a large backwater on the edge of the field.
The dam created a calm backwater behind its walls.
Frogs and insects thrive in the backwater formed by the rising tide.

Synset Examples

the bayous and backwaters are breeding grounds for mosquitos

Etymology

From 'back' + 'water', indicating water forced back or away from the main flow.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbækˌwɔtər / (AmE), / ˈbækwɔːtə / (BrE)
a place, situation, or area in which no significant progress or development is taking place
unprogressive or stagnant place

Examples

The small town was seen as a backwater, untouched by modern industry.
After years of neglect, the once-thriving port had become a cultural backwater.
The reforms aimed to revitalize what was long considered an educational backwater.

Synset Examples

the country is an economic backwater

Related Terms

  • Synonym: backwoods - an area far from cities, often considered undeveloped
  • Synonym: outpost - a remote part of a country or empire
  • Antonym: center - a place where important development or activity happens

Etymology

From 'back' + 'water', originally describing a part of a river not affected by current, later used metaphorically.
