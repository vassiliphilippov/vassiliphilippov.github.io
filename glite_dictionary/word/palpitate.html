to palpitate

1. VERB

/ ˈpæl.pɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈpal.pɪ.teɪt / (BrE)
(of the heart) to beat rapidly, strongly, or irregularly, especially because of strong emotion or illness.
beat rapidly or irregularly (of the heart)

Examples

Her heart began to palpitate with anxiety before the important meeting.
When he saw the frightening scene, his chest seemed to palpitate uncontrollably.
After running up the stairs, she could feel her heart palpitating in her chest.

Synset Examples

His heart palpitated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - to beat with a strong, regular rhythm

Etymology

From Latin palpitare (to throb, tremble).

2. VERB

/ ˈpæl.pəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈpal.pɪ.teɪt / (BrE)
to tremble, throb, or beat rapidly and irregularly, especially from strong emotion or as a physical reaction
to beat or tremble rapidly and irregularly

Examples

Her heart began to palpitate with excitement when she heard the news.
The bird's wings seemed to palpitate as it prepared to take flight.
He watched his reflection as his nostrils palpitated with each anxious breath.

Synset Examples

His nostrils palpitated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - to beat or sound with a strong, regular rhythm; to pulsate strongly

Etymology

From Latin palpitat-, past participle of palpitare (to throb, flutter).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01893122-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈpæl.pəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈpal.pɪ.teɪt / (BrE)
to cause (something, especially the heart) to throb or beat rapidly and strongly
make (the heart) beat rapidly

Examples

The shocking news palpitated his heart with fear.
Excitement and anxiety palpitated her chest all at once.
The horror movie palpitated the audience's hearts from start to finish.

Synset Examples

Her violent feelings palpitated the young woman's heart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - to beat or sound with a strong, regular rhythm
  • Synonym: flutter - to move quickly in a way that is not smooth or regular, especially for the heart

Etymology

From Latin palpitare (to throb, flutter).
