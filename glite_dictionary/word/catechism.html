catechism

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæt̬.ɪˌkɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈkat.ɪ.kɪz.əm / (BrE)
a summary of the basic principles of a Christian faith, typically in the form of questions and answers, used for religious instruction
book of Christian religious questions and answers

Examples

She studied the catechism in preparation for her First Communion.
The priest taught from the catechism every Sunday morning.
Many children memorize the catechism as part of their religious education.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creed - a formal statement of Christian beliefs

Etymology

From Late Latin catechismus (instruction by word of mouth), from Ancient Greek katēkhismos (oral instruction).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæt̬.əˌkɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈkat.ɪ.kɪz.əm / (BrE)
a formal series of questions, particularly one used to elicit someone's views or opinions, especially in political or public contexts
series of formal questions to reveal views

Examples

The senator faced a catechism of challenging questions from the press after the debate.
During the interview, she was subjected to a catechism designed to clarify her political stance.
The documentary concluded with a catechism that revealed the candidate's true opinions on controversial issues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interrogation - the act of formally and systematically questioning someone

Etymology

From Greek 'katēkhizein' (to instruct orally), via Late Latin 'catechismus'.
