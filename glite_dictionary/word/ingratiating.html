ingratiating

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈɡreɪʃiˌeɪtɪŋ / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪʃɪeɪtɪŋ / (BrE)
Intended to gain someone's favor or approval, often in a way that is perceived as insincere or overly eager.
trying to please to gain favor

Examples

The politician greeted everyone with an ingratiating smile at the campaign rally.
His ingratiating manner won over the skeptical audience.
She spoke in such an ingratiating way that it felt almost unnatural.

Synset Examples

with open arms and an ingratiating smile

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsequious - excessively eager to please or obey
  • Antonym: unsubtle - not delicate or refined in approach (contrasts with ingratiating manner)

Etymology

From Latin 'ingratiatus', past participle of 'ingratiare' (to bring into favor), from 'in-' + 'gratia' (favor).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈɡreɪʃieɪtɪŋ / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪʃieɪtɪŋ / (BrE)
Behaving in a way intended to gain approval or favor; trying to please others, often in an insincere or excessive manner.
seeking to please to gain favor

Examples

Her ingratiating smile made everyone feel welcome, even if it seemed a bit forced.
He adopted an ingratiating tone when speaking to his boss, hoping for a promotion.
The salesperson's ingratiating manner was designed to win over hesitant customers.

Synset Examples

a smooth ingratiating manner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsequious - excessively eager to please or obey
  • Antonym: unpleasant - not pleasant or agreeable

Etymology

From Latin ingratiari (to bring into favor), from in- (in) + gratia (favor).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00911365-s
Synset POS: s
