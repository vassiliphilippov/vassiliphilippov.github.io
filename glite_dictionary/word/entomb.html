to entomb

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtuːm / (AmE), / ɪnˈtuːm / (BrE)
to place (a dead body) in a tomb or grave, often in a formal or ceremonial way
to place in a tomb or grave

Examples

The ancient kings were entombed with treasures to accompany them in the afterlife.
The archaeologists believe the chamber was specially built to entomb high-ranking officials.
After the ceremony, his body was entombed in the family mausoleum.

Synset Examples

Stalin was buried behind the Kremlin wall on Red Square
The pharaohs were entombed in the pyramids
My grandfather was laid to rest last Sunday

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bury - to place a dead body in the ground
  • Antonym: to cremate - to dispose of a body by burning

Etymology

From Old French entomber, from en- (in) + tombe (tomb).

2. VERB

/ ɛnˈtuːm / (AmE), / ɪnˈtuːm / (BrE)
to place someone or something in a tomb, grave, or burial chamber; to enclose or trap as if in a tomb
to place in a tomb or trap inside

Examples

The ancient ruins were entombed beneath layers of volcanic ash for centuries.
His secrets would forever be entombed within the walls of the mansion.
Rescue teams rushed to help workers entombed by a collapsed tunnel.

Synset Examples

The old castle seemed to entomb secrets of the past.
The archaeologists were excited to entomb the ancient artifact.
They planned to entomb the remains with great ceremony.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bury - to place a dead body in the ground
  • Antonym: to disinter - to dig up something that was buried, especially a body

Etymology

From Old French 'entomber', based on Latin 'in' (in) + 'tumba' (tomb).
