to integrate

1. VERB

/ ˈɪn.təˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.tɪ.ɡreɪt / (BrE)
to combine one thing with another to form a whole, or to make something become part of a larger group or system
combine parts to form a whole

Examples

The company plans to integrate several smaller units into one large division.
It's important to integrate new employees into the team quickly and smoothly.
He worked hard to integrate his ideas with those of his colleagues.

Synset Examples

She incorporated his suggestions into her proposal

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incorporate - combine or include as part of a whole

Etymology

From Latin integratus, past participle of integrare (to make whole), from integer (whole).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00467564-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈɪntəˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪntɪɡreɪt / (BrE)
(Mathematics) To calculate the integral of a function or equation.
to calculate the integral (maths)

Examples

To solve this equation, you need to integrate both sides with respect to x.
The students learned how to integrate complex functions during calculus class.
She integrated the velocity function to find the total distance traveled.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to differentiate - to calculate the derivative of a function

Etymology

From Latin integratus, past participle of integrare (to make whole), from integer (whole).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɪntəˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪntɪɡreɪt / (BrE)
to become part of a whole or to make people or groups become part of a larger group or society, especially so that differences are minimized
to become part of a whole

Examples

At the new school, children from different backgrounds quickly integrate into the community.
It can take time for immigrants to fully integrate into a new society.
The company aims to help its remote teams integrate with the office culture.

Synset Examples

The students at this school integrate immediately, despite their different backgrounds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assimilate - to become part of a group and be accepted by them

Etymology

From Latin integratus, past participle of integrare (to make whole), from integer (whole).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00468364-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈɪntəˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪntɪɡreɪt / (BrE)
to open (a place, institution, etc.) to people of all races or ethnic groups; to remove racial or ethnic segregation.
open to all races; end segregation

Examples

After years of legal battles, the city finally decided to integrate its public schools.
Several restaurants in the area chose to integrate during the civil rights movement.
The community center was among the first to be integrated in the 1960s.

Synset Examples

This school is completely desegregated

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to segregate - to separate people based on race, religion, etc.
  • Synonym: to desegregate - to end segregation by removing restrictions

Etymology

From Latin integratus, past participle of integrare (to make whole), from integer (whole).
