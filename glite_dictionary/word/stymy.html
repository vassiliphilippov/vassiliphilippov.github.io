stymy

1. NOUN

/ ˈstaɪmi / (AmE), / ˈstaɪmi / (BrE)
(Golf, historical) A situation in match play where an opponent’s ball blocks the direct line between your ball and the hole.
situation in golf where a ball blocks the hole
archaic

Examples

In the days before the rule change, players often found themselves in a stymy on the green.
Because of the stymy, he had to chip the ball over his opponent’s.
The introduction of new rules made the stymy an obsolete part of golf.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to obstruct or get in the way of

Etymology

Of uncertain origin; popularized in golf in the late 19th century.

2. VERB

/ ˈstaɪmi / (AmE), / ˈstaɪmi / (BrE)
to prevent someone from achieving a goal; to hinder or obstruct progress
hinder or obstruct progress

Examples

New regulations may stymy the development of innovative technology in the region.
His constant interruptions stymied their efforts to finish the project on time.
We can't allow fear of failure to stymy our ambition.

3. NOUN

/ ˈstaɪmi / (AmE), / ˈstaɪmi / (BrE)
A situation in which progress is blocked or thwarted, often causing frustration or distress.
thwarting or distressing situation
american

Examples

The project hit a stymy when funding was unexpectedly withdrawn.
Negotiations reached a stymy that neither side could overcome.
She found herself in a stymy, unable to move forward with her plans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impasse - a situation in which no progress is possible
  • Synonym: obstacle - a thing that blocks one's way or prevents progress
  • Antonym: opportunity - a set of circumstances that makes it possible to do something

Etymology

Originally from golf; perhaps from Scottish 'stymie', meaning a condition in which an opponent's ball blocks one's own route to the hole.
