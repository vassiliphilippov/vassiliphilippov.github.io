flounder

1. NOUN

/ ˈflaʊndɚ / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
a small, flat sea fish with both eyes on one side of its body, found in European and non-European waters and valued as food
a type of flat sea fish

Examples

Fishermen caught several flounder off the coast this morning.
The chef prepared grilled flounder with lemon and herbs for dinner.
Young flounder often hide themselves in the sand to avoid predators.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sole - a different type of flatfish often confused with flounder
  • Degree Higher: halibut - a larger relative in the flatfish family

Etymology

From Middle English floundre, from Old Norse flóðra.

2. NOUN

/ ˈflaʊndɚ / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
the edible flesh of any of various American and European flatfish, often served as food
flesh of a flatfish (food)

Examples

The chef recommended the grilled flounder with lemon butter sauce.
She bought fresh flounder at the fish market for tonight's dinner.
Baked flounder is a popular dish along the coast.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sole - the flesh of a flatfish (Solea solea), often used as food

Etymology

From Old French *flondre*, from Old Norse *flyðra* (a kind of flatfish).

3. VERB

/ ˈflaʊndɚ / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
to move or walk with great difficulty, especially by stumbling or struggling awkwardly
move with difficulty; stumble or struggle

Examples

The injured hiker tried to flounder through the thick mud to reach the road.
Children floundered in the deep snow, struggling to stay upright.
She floundered across the rocky field, nearly losing her balance several times.

Synset Examples

He staggered along in the heavy snow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stagger - to walk or move unsteadily, as if about to fall
  • Synonym: struggle - to make forceful or violent efforts to get free

Etymology

Probably from earlier sense 'to flop about,' of Scandinavian origin; compare Norwegian dialect flondra (to flop, struggle).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈflaʊndər / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
to struggle mentally or physically; to have difficulty or act clumsily, especially when trying to achieve something
struggle or have difficulty

Examples

Many new businesses flounder during their first year due to inexperience.
After moving to the city, he began to flounder socially and academically.
She could see him floundering for the right words during the interview.

Synset Examples

She is floundering in college

Related Terms

  • Synonym: struggle - to try very hard to do something difficult
  • Antonym: to succeed - to achieve something desired or intended

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Norwegian 'flundra' (to roll about).
