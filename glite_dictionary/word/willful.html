willful

1. ADJ

/ ˈwɪlfəl / (AmE), / ˈwɪlfʊl / (BrE)
(of a bad or undesirable action) done deliberately or intentionally, especially with the intention of causing harm or defiance.
deliberate or intentional (esp. of wrongdoing)

Examples

The manager fired him for willful disobedience of company policies.
The jury found the defendant guilty of willful neglect of duty.
Her actions were not accidental, but clearly willful and calculated.

Synset Examples

willful disobedience

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intentional - done on purpose, not by accident
  • Antonym: accidental - happening by chance, not intended

Etymology

From Middle English wilful, from will (desire, wish) + -ful (full of).

2. ADJ

/ ˈwɪl.fəl / (AmE), / ˈwɪl.fəl / (BrE)
(of a person or their actions) habitually inclined to disobey or resist authority, often stubbornly or deliberately.
habitually disobedient or stubborn

Examples

The willful child refused to follow any instructions, no matter how much his parents pleaded.
Her willful disregard for the rules often got her into trouble at school.
Despite repeated warnings, he remained willful in his opposition to authority.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obstinate - stubbornly refusing to change one's opinion or chosen course of action
  • Synonym: headstrong - energetic and determined to do as one pleases, even if advised otherwise
  • Antonym: compliant - willing to comply or obey

Etymology

From Middle English wilful, from Old English willan (to wish, desire) + -ful (full of).
