generalisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (AmE), / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rələˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(psychology) The process by which a response learned to one stimulus is transferred to a similar stimulus.
transferring a learned response to similar stimuli (psychology)

Examples

In psychological studies, generalisation can lead subjects to react to stimuli that only resemble the original trigger.
Her dog’s fear of thunder extended by generalisation to loud noises in general.
The experiment measured how generalisation affected the animal’s behavior in different environments.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: discrimination - the ability to distinguish between different stimuli in psychological conditioning

Etymology

From French généralisation, from généraliser (to generalize); see 'general.'

2. NOUN

/ ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rələˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
The process of forming general ideas or principles by identifying common features among specific cases or instances.
process of forming general concepts

Examples

Her research led to the generalisation that children learn languages in similar ways.
The risk of generalisation is that specific cases may be overlooked.
Through observation and analysis, scientists often formulate a generalisation about a phenomenon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: generalization - alternative spelling, chiefly American English

Etymology

From general + -isation, ultimately from Latin generālis (general) and -izatio (making, becoming).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (AmE), / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
A statement or idea that applies broadly, rather than to particular cases.
broad idea or conclusion
british

Examples

It is dangerous to make sweeping generalisations about an entire group based on a few individuals.
His comments were full of vague generalisations and provided little useful information.
Academic writing should avoid generalisations and present specific evidence.

Synset Examples

he spoke in broad generalities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: generalization - US spelling of the same word

Etymology

From French généralisation, from généraliser (to generalize).

4. NOUN

/ ˌdʒɛnərəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdʒɛnərəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of deriving general principles from specific examples or facts
reasoning from specific cases to general principles

Examples

The scientist's generalisation was based on years of careful observation.
Making a generalisation from just a few examples can lead to errors.
His ability to move from details to generalisation made his arguments compelling.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: induction - the process of reasoning from specific cases to general rules
  • Antonym: specification - the process of stating something in detail, opposite of generalization

Etymology

From French 'généralisation', based on 'généraliser' (to generalize), from 'général' (general).
