mercantilism

1. NOUN

/ mərˈkæntɪˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / mɜːˈkæntɪlɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
An economic theory and system, popular in Europe in the 16th to 18th centuries, that promoted government regulation of a nation's trade and commerce to increase national wealth and power.
economic policy favoring regulated national wealth

Examples

Many European countries adopted mercantilism to compete for global influence and wealth.
The decline of mercantilism led to the rise of free-market economic theories.
Historians debate the long-term impacts of mercantilism on colonial expansion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protectionism - the policy of protecting domestic industries by restricting imports

Etymology

From French 'mercantilisme', from Italian 'mercante' (merchant).

2. NOUN

/ mərˈkæn.tɪˌlɪ.zəm / (AmE), / mɜːˈkæn.tɪ.lɪ.zəm / (BrE)
An economic theory and practice, prominent in Europe in the 16th to 18th centuries, that advocated government regulation of international trade to increase state power and wealth, often by maximizing exports and accumulating precious metals.
economic policy focused on trade and wealth

Examples

The rise of mercantilism in Europe influenced the colonization of the Americas.
Many historians debate the long-term effects of mercantilism on global trade.
British government policies during the 18th century were shaped largely by principles of mercantilism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protectionism - the theory or practice of shielding a country's domestic industries from foreign competition
  • Antonym: free trade - international trade left to its natural course without tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions

Etymology

From French *mercantilisme*, from Italian *mercante* (merchant), from Latin *mercari* (to trade).
