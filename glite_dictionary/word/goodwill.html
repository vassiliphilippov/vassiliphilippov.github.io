goodwill

1. NOUN

/ ɡʊdˈwɪl / (AmE), / ɡʊdˈwɪl / (BrE)
(Accounting) An intangible asset representing the value of a business's reputation, customer relationships, or other advantages that are not physical assets but add to its overall worth.
intangible business asset from reputation or advantage

Examples

The company's goodwill was valued at several million dollars in the acquisition deal.
After years of excellent service, the store built up significant goodwill in the local community.
Loss of key clients may result in a decrease in goodwill on the balance sheet.
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ɡʊdˈwɪl / (AmE), / ɡʊdˈwɪl / (BrE)
friendly or kindly feelings or hope that someone or something will succeed
friendly hope for success

Examples

They sent a letter of goodwill to wish us luck with the new project.
After the debate, the candidates shook hands as a gesture of goodwill.
The company donated to charity as an act of goodwill toward the community.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: benevolence - the quality of being well-meaning; kindness
  • Antonym: ill will - hostile or unfriendly feeling; the opposite of goodwill

Etymology

From Middle English 'good wille', meaning good intention or benevolence.

3. NOUN

/ ˌɡʊdˈwɪl / (AmE), / ˌɡʊdˈwɪl / (BrE)
(Accounting) An intangible asset representing the value of a business's reputation, customer relationships, and other non-physical advantages, beyond its tangible assets.
intangible business asset (accounting)

Examples

The company's goodwill increased significantly after the successful launch of its new product line.
During the acquisition, the buyer paid a premium for the goodwill associated with the brand.
Impairment of goodwill must be tested annually in accordance with accounting standards.

Etymology

From the phrase 'good will', originally meaning favor or benevolence.
