differentiable

1. ADJ

/ ˌdɪfəˈrɛnʃəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˌdɪfəˈrenʃɪəbl̩ / (BrE)
(of a function) having a derivative at every point in its domain or at a particular point.
having a derivative (mathematics)

Examples

Every continuous and differentiable function on a closed interval is integrable.
The graph of a differentiable function is always smooth, with no sharp corners.
If a function is not differentiable at a point, it may still be continuous there.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: non-differentiable - not having a derivative at a point or on a domain
  • Degree Lower: continuous - (mathematics) a function with no breaks or jumps in its domain

Etymology

From differentiate (from Latin differentia 'difference') + -able.

2. ADJ

/ ˌdɪfəˈrɛnʃəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˌdɪfəˈrɛnʃɪəbl̩ / (BrE)
able to be distinguished or recognized as different from something else; capable of being differentiated.
able to be distinguished from something else

Examples

The two samples are easily differentiable under a microscope.
Their results showed that the two populations were not differentiable based on appearance alone.
Species with similar markings may not be differentiable without genetic testing.

Synset Examples

differentiable species

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distinct - recognizably different in an obvious way
  • Antonym: indistinguishable - not able to be distinguished or perceived as different

Etymology

Formed from 'differentiate' + '-able', patterned after similar adjectives.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00584987-s
Synset POS: s
