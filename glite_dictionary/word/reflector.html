reflector

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈflɛktɚ / (AmE), / rɪˈflɛktə / (BrE)
a device that reflects light, heat, or other radiation, often used to direct or redirect it
device that reflects radiation

Examples

Bicycles are often equipped with a red reflector in the rear for safety at night.
The satellite uses a parabolic reflector to focus radio waves onto the receiver.
She adjusted the reflector to direct sunlight onto her plants during the winter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mirror - an object that reflects light to produce an image

Etymology

From Latin reflectere (to bend back), via English 'reflect' + '-or'.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈflɛktər / (AmE), / rɪˈflɛktə / (BrE)
an optical telescope that uses a large concave mirror to form an image, which is then magnified by an eyepiece
optical telescope with a concave mirror

Examples

The astronomer inspected the night sky through his reflector, searching for new stars.
Many observatories prefer a reflector because of its superior image quality and lower cost for large apertures.
He assembled his first homemade reflector in his garage at the age of fifteen.

Synset Examples

Isaac Newton invented the reflecting telescope in 1668

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reflecting telescope - telescope using a mirrored system to gather and focus light
  • Antonym: refractor - optical telescope that uses lenses to form images

Etymology

From reflect, meaning to throw back light, with the suffix -or (one that reflects).
