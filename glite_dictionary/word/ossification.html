ossification

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑːsəfəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which bone is formed, renewed, or repaired in the body
formation of bone tissue
medical

Examples

Infant bones are composed mostly of cartilage before ossification gradually converts them into hard bone.
Disturbances in ossification can lead to developmental skeletal disorders.
The study focuses on the timeline of ossification during human fetal growth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: calcification - the process by which tissues become hardened by deposits of calcium salts

Etymology

From Latin 'ossificare' (to make into bone), from 'os' (bone) + 'facere' (to make).

Related Terms

3. NOUN

/ ˌɑːsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which soft tissue becomes hardened and turns into bone-like material, often due to calcification
calcification or hardening into bone
medical

Examples

The ossification of cartilage is essential in the growth of long bones during childhood.
After the injury, abnormal ossification was detected in the patient's muscle tissue.
Pathological ossification can lead to restricted movement in the affected joint.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: calcification - the accumulation of calcium salts in body tissue
  • Antonym: decalcification - the process of removing calcium or calcium compounds

Etymology

From Latin 'ossificare' (to make into bone), from 'os' (bone) + 'facere' (to make).

4. NOUN

/ ˌɑːsəfəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of becoming rigidly fixed and unable to change, especially in attitudes, systems, or customs; excessive conventionality or inflexibility
rigid inflexibility, esp. in ideas or systems

Examples

The ossification of management structures makes it difficult for the company to adapt to new challenges.
Social progress often stalls due to the ossification of traditional beliefs.
The historian warned against the ossification of cultural practices that once encouraged innovation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rigidity - the quality of being rigid and inflexible
  • Antonym: flexibility - the quality of being able to change or adapt easily

Etymology

From Latin ossificare (to make into bone), from os (bone) + facere (to make).
