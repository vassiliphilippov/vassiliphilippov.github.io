to carnalize

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑrnəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnəlaɪz / (BrE)
to attribute something to sensual or bodily origin
ascribe to sensual or bodily origin

Examples

It is unfair to carnalize her motives simply because she showed compassion.
Some philosophies tend to carnalize all forms of human love, ignoring their spiritual aspects.
Critics often carnalize artistic inspiration, reducing it to mere physical urges.

Etymology

From Latin 'carnalis' (fleshly), from 'caro' (flesh).

WordNet ID: oewn-00728974-v

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑrnəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnəlaɪz / (BrE)
To represent in a physical, material, or bodily form; to make material, especially in art or sculpture.
represent in physical or bodily form

Examples

The artist sought to carnalize abstract concepts by depicting them as tangible human forms.
Some critics argue that modern sculpture too often carnalizes spiritual ideas, reducing their mystery.
By carnalizing the myth, the painter gave it a vivid sense of reality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to materialize - make something material or physical; render tangible

Etymology

From Latin 'carnalis' (fleshly) + English suffix '-ize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01684368-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑːrnəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnəlaɪz / (BrE)
to debase or corrupt by indulging in sensual or sexual pleasures
to debase through sensual gratification

Examples

The author warned that unchecked desire could carnalize even the purest intentions.
Some critics argue that modern culture has carnalized our appreciation of art and beauty.
He feared that material excess would carnalize the spiritual search for meaning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to debase - to lower in quality, value, or dignity

Etymology

From Latin 'carnalis' (fleshly) + English suffix '-ize'.
