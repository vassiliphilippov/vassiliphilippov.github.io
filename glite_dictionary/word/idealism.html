idealism

1. NOUN

/ aɪˈdiəlɪzəm / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪə.lɪ.zəm / (BrE)
(Philosophy) The theory that reality is fundamentally based on the mind or ideas, and that physical objects do not exist independently of perception.
philosophical theory; reality as ideas

Examples

In his lectures, the professor explained idealism as the belief that reality is constructed by the mind.
German philosophers like Kant were significant contributors to the development of idealism.
Some critics argue that idealism overlooks the importance of the physical world.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: materialism - the doctrine that nothing exists except matter and its movements and modifications
  • Antonym: realism - the view that physical objects exist independently of perception

Etymology

From French idéalisme, from idéal (ideal) + -isme (-ism).

2. NOUN

/ aɪˈdiəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪə.lɪ.zəm / (BrE)
The attitude of seeing and pursuing things as perfect or as they ought to be, rather than as they are; impracticality by focusing on ideals instead of reality.
belief in or pursuit of ideals over reality

Examples

His idealism led him to believe that every problem could be solved with goodwill.
She admired his idealism, but sometimes wished he were more practical.
Despite his idealism, the harsh realities of politics soon challenged his views.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: realism - the attitude of accepting things as they are and dealing with them practically

Etymology

From French 'idéalisme', based on 'idéal' (ideal), itself from Latin 'idea'.

3. NOUN

/ aɪˈdiəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
The belief in or pursuit of noble ideals, often with the expectation that these ideals can be realized or should guide conduct.
belief in or pursuit of high ideals

Examples

Her idealism inspired many people to work toward positive change in their community.
Despite his idealism, he was sometimes frustrated by the slow pace of progress.
The novel is marked by the youthful idealism of its main characters.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: realism - practical view of things as they are; focus on reality over ideals

Etymology

From French idéalisme, from idéal (ideal), ultimately from Greek idea (form, pattern).
