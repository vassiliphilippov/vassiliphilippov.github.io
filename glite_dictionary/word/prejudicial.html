prejudicial

1. ADJ

/ ˌprɛdʒəˈdɪʃəl / (AmE), / ˌprɛdʒʊˈdɪʃəl / (BrE)
causing harm or disadvantage, especially to someone's rights or interests; detrimental
causing harm or disadvantage

Examples

The media coverage was highly prejudicial to the defendant's chance of a fair trial.
Such comments can be prejudicial to a person's reputation and future opportunities.
It would be prejudicial to disclose confidential information without consent.

Synset Examples

damaging to career and reputation
the reporter's coverage resulted in prejudicial publicity for the defendant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: damaging - causing harm or injury; detrimental
  • Antonym: beneficial - producing good or helpful results; advantageous

Etymology

From Late Latin praeiudicialis, from Latin praeiudicium (a previous judgment).

2. ADJ

/ ˌprɛdʒəˈdɪʃəl / (AmE), / ˌprɛdʒʊˈdɪʃəl / (BrE)
causing or tending to cause harm or disadvantage, especially by supporting preconceived ideas or bias
causing harm or based on bias

Examples

The judge ruled that the evidence was prejudicial and could not be presented in court.
Such prejudicial comments can undermine the fairness of a debate.
Media coverage may sometimes be prejudicial to the accused in a criminal trial.

Synset Examples

the presence of discriminatory or prejudicial attitudes in the white population

Related Terms

  • Synonym: biased - showing an unreasonable preference or prejudice
  • Antonym: impartial - treating all rivals or disputants equally; fair and just

Etymology

From Medieval Latin praedictialis, from Latin praejudicium (prejudice).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02772853-a
Synset POS: a
