caucus

1. VERB

/ ˈkɔkəs / (AmE), / ˈkɔːkəs / (BrE)
to meet as a group, especially within a political party, to select candidates or decide on policy
meet to select candidates or set policy

Examples

The committee will caucus tomorrow to discuss their endorsement.
Several lawmakers caucused privately ahead of the public vote.
After caucusing for hours, the group finally agreed on a candidate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to convene - to come together for a meeting

Etymology

Originated in North American English, possibly from an Algonquian word for 'counsel' or 'group'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɔːkəs / (AmE), / ˈkɔːkəs / (BrE)
A meeting of members of a political party or group, typically held to select candidates, decide policy, or coordinate strategy, usually closed to outsiders.
closed political party meeting

Examples

The party members held a caucus to discuss their strategy for the upcoming election.
After a heated caucus, they finally agreed on a candidate to support.
She attended the caucus every year to have her voice heard in party decisions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: convention - a large formal assembly of people, especially political party members or delegates

Etymology

Probably from Algonquian (possibly from the Algonquian word for 'counsel' or 'group of people'), popularized in American English in the 18th century.
