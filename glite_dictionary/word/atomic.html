atomic

1. ADJ

/ əˈtɑːmɪk / (AmE), / əˈtɒmɪk / (BrE)
relating to or consisting of atoms; concerned with atoms or their properties
relating to atoms

Examples

Scientists are studying the atomic structure of various elements.
The atomic model helps explain chemical reactions at a fundamental level.
Atomic hydrogen is used in certain types of spectroscopy.

Synset Examples

atomic structure
atomic hydrogen

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: molecular - relating to molecules

Etymology

From Greek atomos (indivisible), from a- (not) + temnein (to cut).

2. ADJ

/ əˈtɑmɪk / (AmE), / əˈtɒmɪk / (BrE)
Extremely small; relating to or characteristic of an atom or atoms.
extremely small; related to atoms

Examples

The scientist examined particles of atomic size under the microscope.
Her patience for nonsense is atomic, almost nonexistent.
They designed a machine capable of manipulating matter on the atomic scale.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: microscopic - Extremely small; so small as to be visible only with a microscope
  • Antonym: gigantic - Extremely large; enormous

Etymology

From atom + -ic, ultimately from Greek atomos (indivisible).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01395023-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ əˈtɑmɪk / (AmE), / əˈtɒmɪk / (BrE)
relating to or using the energy released from atoms, especially in weapons or bombs
relating to energy from atoms (weapons)

Examples

The threat of an atomic bomb terrified the world during the Cold War.
Many nations signed treaties to limit their atomic weapons arsenals.
The devastation caused by an atomic explosion is immense and long-lasting.

Synset Examples

nuclear war
nuclear weapons
atomic bombs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nuclear - relating to nuclei or the energy released from nuclear reactions, especially in weapons or power

Etymology

From Greek atomos (indivisible), via Latin atomicus.
