Salamandridae

1. NOUN

/ səˌlæm.ənˈdraɪ.di / (AmE), / ˌsæləˈmændrɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of amphibians comprising the true salamanders and newts, characterized by elongated bodies, soft moist skin, and a lizard-like appearance.
family of salamanders and newts

Examples

Members of the Salamandridae family are often found in moist woodland habitats.
The Salamandridae include both newts and true salamanders.
Scientists study the skin toxins of Salamandridae for their potential medical applications.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: salamander - any tailed amphibian of the order Caudata

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Latin 'salamandra' (salamander) + '-idae', a standard suffix for animal families.

2. NOUN

/ sæləˈmændrəˌdi / (AmE), / sæləˈmændrɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of amphibians comprising the true salamanders and newts, typically characterized by slender bodies, smooth skin, and long tails.
family of salamanders and newts

Examples

The Salamandridae are widely distributed in temperate regions of Europe, Asia, and North America.
Most members of the Salamandridae family undergo a larval stage before maturing into adults.
Unlike frogs, Salamandridae species retain their tails throughout their lives.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: salamander - a tailed amphibian of the order Caudata, usually with a slender body

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'salamandra' (salamander) + standard taxonomic suffix '-idae' for animal families.
