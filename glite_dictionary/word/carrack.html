carrack

1. NOUN

/ ˈkærək / (AmE), / ˈkærək / (BrE)
A large, three- or four-masted sailing ship, used mainly as a merchant vessel in the Mediterranean and European waters during the 15th to 17th centuries.
historical large merchant sailing ship

Examples

The Portuguese explorers sailed a sturdy carrack across the Atlantic Ocean.
A carrack could carry vast amounts of spices and goods between Europe and the East.
Historians often describe the carrack as a crucial development in maritime trade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: galleon - a large square-rigged sailing ship with three or more masts; used from the 15th to 18th centuries

Etymology

From Middle French 'caraque', from Old Spanish 'carraca', probably from Arabic 'qarāqir' (large merchant ships).
