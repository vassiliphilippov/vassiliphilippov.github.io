moor

1. VERB

/ mʊr / (AmE), / mɔː / (BrE)
to secure a boat or ship with cables, ropes, or anchors
secure with cables or ropes (usually a boat)

Examples

They moored the yacht in the harbor before sunset.
Please moor the boat securely to prevent it from drifting away.
After a long day of sailing, we finally moored near the docks.

Synset Examples

moor the boat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anchor - to secure a vessel to the sea bottom with an anchor
  • Antonym: to cast off - to release a boat from being secured

Etymology

From Old English *mōrian* (to moor a ship), probably from Middle Low German *mōren* (to moor).

2. NOUN

/ mʊr / (AmE), / mɔː / (BrE)
an area of open, uncultivated land, often with peaty soil and covered with heather, bracken, and moss, commonly found in highland areas
area of open, uncultivated land

Examples

The vast moor stretched out beneath a grey sky, dotted with patches of purple heather.
They got lost while hiking across the windswept moor in northern England.
Many rare birds nest on the moor, taking advantage of its remote and open habitat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heath - an area of open uncultivated land, especially in Europe, with coarse grasses, heather, and gorse

Etymology

From Old English *mōr* (marsh, moor), related to Old High German *muor* (moor, fen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09381648-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ mʊr / (AmE), / mɔː / (BrE)
to fasten a boat or ship to a fixed object or anchor so that it stays in place
fasten a boat or ship in place

Examples

We decided to moor our yacht near the small fishing village.
The captain instructed the crew to moor the ship at the dock for the night.
You must always moor the boat securely to prevent it from drifting away.

Synset Examples

tie up the boat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anchor - to secure a vessel by dropping an anchor

Etymology

From Middle English "moren", from Old English "mōrian" (to moor a ship), possibly from Old Norse "mǫra".

Synset POS: v
