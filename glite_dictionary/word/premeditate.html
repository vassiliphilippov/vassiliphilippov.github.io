to premeditate

1. VERB

/ priˈmɛdəˌteɪt / (AmE), / priˈmɛdɪteɪt / (BrE)
to think about or plan something before carrying it out, especially a crime or harmful act
plan or consider ahead of time

Examples

The crime was premeditated and carefully executed.
Jurors had to decide whether the act was premeditated or done in the heat of the moment.
She would never premeditate such a harmful action against her friends.

Synset Examples

premeditated murder

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plan - to decide on and arrange in advance
  • Antonym: to improvise - to do something without preparation

Etymology

From Latin praemeditari, from prae- (before) + meditari (to think, to contemplate).

2. VERB

/ ˌpriˈmɛdɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˌpriːˈmɛdɪteɪt / (BrE)
to think about or plan something before doing it; to consider in advance
to plan or consider in advance

Examples

She tried to premeditate her actions to avoid making impulsive decisions.
The crime was found to be premeditated rather than spontaneous.
It's important to premeditate before making such a significant commitment.

Synset Examples

I rarely premeditate, which is a mistake

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plan - to arrange or decide on something in advance
  • Antonym: to improvise - to do something without prior preparation

Etymology

From Latin praemeditari (to think over beforehand), from prae- (before) + meditari (to meditate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00632906-v
Synset POS: v
