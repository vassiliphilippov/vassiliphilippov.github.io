to subside

1. VERB

/ səbˈsaɪd / (AmE), / səbˈsaɪd / (BrE)
to gradually sink or settle down into a soft surface or a lower position, often from exhaustion or relief
to sink or settle into something soft

Examples

After a long day, she subsided into the armchair and closed her eyes.
He subsided into bed, too tired to even pull up the covers.
As soon as the meeting ended, he subsided into his seat, exhausted.

Synset Examples

He sank into bed
She subsided into the chair

Etymology

From Latin subsīdēre, from sub- (down) + sīdere (to settle).

2. VERB

/ səbˈsaɪd / (AmE), / səbˈsaɪd / (BrE)
to sink to a lower level, or to form a hollow or depression in the surface
sink or form a depression

Examples

After the earthquake, part of the mountain began to subside and created a deep valley.
The ground may subside when water is drained from beneath the surface.
Old mine tunnels can cause land above them to subside unexpectedly.

Synset Examples

the valleys subside

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sink - to move downward, especially below the surface of a liquid or soil

Etymology

From Latin subsīdere, from sub- (down) + sīdere (to settle).

3. VERB

/ səbˈsaɪd / (AmE), / səbˈsʌɪd / (BrE)
(of a material, liquid, or structure) to sink to a lower level or settle down, often after disturbance or agitation.
to sink or settle down

Examples

After the flood, the water in the fields began to subside slowly over several days.
It took hours for the stirred-up sediment in the pond to subside and allow the water to clear.
The house started to crack as the ground beneath it began to subside unexpectedly.

Synset Examples

the mud subsides when the waters become calm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to sink or subside to a lower level
  • Antonym: rise - to move upward or go up to a higher level

Etymology

From Latin subsīdere, from sub- (down) + sīdere (to sit, settle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01990199-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ səbˈsaɪd / (AmE), / səbˈsaɪd / (BrE)
(of a feeling, pain, noise, or activity) to become less intense, violent, or severe; to wear off or die down.
become less intense; die down

Examples

After the thunderstorm, the winds finally subsided and the streets became quiet.
The swelling in his ankle gradually subsided after he applied an ice pack.
As the excitement began to subside, people returned to their normal routines.

Synset Examples

The pain subsided

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to diminish - to become or make something less
  • Antonym: to intensify - to become or make something stronger or more extreme

Etymology

From Latin 'subsīdere' (to settle, sink down).
