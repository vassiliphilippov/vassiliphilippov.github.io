unlearned

1. ADJ

/ ʌnˈlɜrnɪd / (AmE), / ʌnˈlɜːnɪd / (BrE)
(of a response or behavior) not acquired through experience, instruction, or conditioning; innate or instinctive.
not acquired by learning; innate

Examples

The baby's cry is an unlearned response to discomfort.
Many animal instincts are considered unlearned behaviors vital to their survival.
Yawning is often cited as an unlearned reflex in humans.

Synset Examples

an unconditioned reflex

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innate - existing naturally rather than acquired by learning
  • Antonym: acquired - developed or learned; not innate

Etymology

Formed from prefix 'un-' (not) + 'learned'.

Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ʌnˈlɝːnɪd, ˌʌnˈlɜrnɪd / (AmE), / ʌnˈlɜːnɪd, ˌʌnˈlɜːnɪd / (BrE)
lacking education, knowledge, or sophistication; not having been taught or learned much.
not educated or knowledgeable

Examples

The panel included both highly educated experts and a few unlearned members of the public.
Her unlearned opinions were often dismissed by those with more experience.
He admitted he was unlearned in matters of modern technology.

Synset Examples

an ignorant man
nescient of contemporary literature
an unlearned group incapable of understanding complex issues
exhibiting contempt for his unlettered companions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ignorant - lacking knowledge or awareness
  • Antonym: educated - having received a good education or instruction

Etymology

Formed from un- (not) + learned (educated) in Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00834764-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ʌnˈlɜrnɪd, ˌʌnˈlɜrnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈlɜːnɪd, ˌʌnˈlɜːnd / (BrE)
lacking knowledge or education; not well educated or informed.
not well educated or informed

Examples

He was an unlearned man, but he had a great deal of common sense.
The lecture was too complex for the unlearned student to follow.
In ancient times, most people were unlearned and could not read or write.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ignorant - lacking knowledge or awareness
  • Antonym: educated - having knowledge or education

Etymology

From un- (expressing negation) + learned, dating from Middle English.
