sheath

1. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
a woman's close-fitting dress, typically straight and narrow in cut, designed for formal occasions
close-fitting straight dress

Examples

She wore a stunning red sheath to the cocktail party.
The black sheath accentuated her elegant silhouette.
Designers are showcasing the classic sheath this season in various fabrics.

Etymology

From the notion of the dress fitting the body like a sheath (protective covering).

2. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
a close-fitting cover, typically made of leather or metal, for the blade of a knife or sword
protective cover for blades

Examples

He drew the sword from its sheath and prepared to defend himself.
The ornate sheath was decorated with silver engravings.
Always return the knife to its sheath after use for safety.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scabbard - a sheath for the blade of a sword or dagger

Etymology

Old English sceaþ, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch schede and German Scheide.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04194104-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
an enclosing structure or covering that surrounds an organ or part in animals and plants
an enclosing covering for an organ or part

Examples

The nerve is protected by a thin sheath of connective tissue.
Each leaf is enclosed by a tubular sheath at its base.
The muscle fibers are wrapped in a sheath that helps support their function.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: covering - something that covers or envelops
  • Antonym: sheathless - lacking a sheath or covering

Etymology

From Old English sceath (covering, sheath), related to Proto-Germanic *skaidō.
