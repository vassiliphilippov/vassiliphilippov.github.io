night

1. NOUN

/ naɪt / (AmE), / naɪt / (BrE)
The period of darkness each day between sunset and sunrise, commonly considered as a unit of time.
period of darkness in a day

Examples

We stayed up all night talking by the campfire.
Three nights in a row, the child had trouble sleeping.
They decided to travel by train to avoid driving at night.

Synset Examples

three nights later he collapsed

Related Terms

  • Antonym: day - the period of light between sunrise and sunset

Etymology

From Old English 'niht', related to Dutch 'nacht' and German 'Nacht'.

3. NOUN

/ naɪt / (AmE), / naɪt / (BrE)
The darkness which follows sunset and precedes sunrise; the period of darkness in each twenty-four hours.
the period of darkness

Examples

The forest was completely silent as night fell.
Suddenly, the city disappeared into the night as the power failed.
Animals often become active when night covers the landscape.

Synset Examples

it vanished into the night

Related Terms

  • Antonym: day - the period of light between sunrise and sunset
  • Synonym: darkness - the partial or total absence of light

Etymology

From Old English *niht*, from Proto-Germanic *nahts*, and ultimately from Proto-Indo-European *nókʷts* (night).

7. NOUN

/ naɪt / (AmE), / naɪt / (BrE)
The period of time between sunset and sunrise when it is dark outside.
period of darkness between sunset and sunrise

Examples

Owls are most active at night when they hunt for food.
She always leaves a light on in the hallway during the night.
The city takes on a different atmosphere at night compared to the day.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: day - the period of time when it is light outside, between sunrise and sunset
  • Degree Lower: evening - the period of time at the end of the day, usually from 6 p.m. to bedtime
  • Degree Higher: midnight - 12 o'clock at night; the middle of the night

Etymology

From Old English 'niht', related to German 'Nacht' and Latin 'nox' (night).
