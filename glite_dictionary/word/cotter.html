cotter

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈkɒtə / (BrE)
a peasant farmer, especially in the Scottish Highlands, who typically rented a small cottage and piece of land
Scottish Highland peasant farmer

Examples

As a cotter, he worked long hours on the laird's land for a modest living.
Many cotters lost their homes during the Highland Clearances.
The cotter lived with his family in a small, thatched cottage at the edge of the village.

Etymology

From Scottish Gaelic 'còtair', meaning cottager.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈkɒtə / (BrE)
a wedge or pin inserted through a hole or slot to secure or fasten two or more parts together
wedge or pin fastener

Examples

He secured the wheel with a cotter to prevent it from slipping off.
The machinery requires a strong cotter to keep the axle in place.
Before starting the repair, she removed the rusty cotter from the joint.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pin - a thin piece of metal used for fastening parts together

Etymology

From Middle English *coter* (bolt, pin), ultimately from Old French *coter* (to fasten).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈkɒtə / (BrE)
In medieval England, a laborer occupying a cottage, typically with little or no land, and bound in service to a lord; a villein of low status.
medieval English peasant or villein
archaic

Examples

In medieval England, a cotter lived on a small plot provided by his lord.
The manor census recorded every cotter alongside freemen and villeins.
As a cotter, she owed both rent and labor to the estate's overlord.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: villein - a feudal tenant in medieval Europe, especially one bound to the land and owned by a lord
  • Synonym: serf - an agricultural laborer bound under the feudal system to work on his lord's estate

Etymology

From Middle English 'cotter', from Old English 'cot' (cottage) + agent suffix '-er'.
