bowdlerisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌbaʊdlɚaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌbaʊdləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of removing or altering parts of a text considered offensive or improper, often resulting in a modified version of the original
removal of objectionable content from texts
british

Examples

The novel's bowdlerisation sparked a debate about artistic freedom and censorship.
Many critics argue that bowdlerisation diminishes the authenticity of classic literature.
Some schools prefer the bowdlerisation of plays to avoid controversial themes in the classroom.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: censorship - the suppression or prohibition of speech or writing considered unacceptable
  • Synonym: expurgation - the process of cleansing a text by removing offensive material

Etymology

From the name of Thomas Bowdler (1754–1825), who published expurgated editions of Shakespeare's works, plus the suffix -isation.

2. NOUN

/ ˌbaʊdlɚəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌbaʊdləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The act of removing or altering parts of a text that are considered offensive or indecent, often weakening its original meaning or impact.
removal of offensive content from texts

Examples

Some critics argue that bowdlerisation of classic literature diminishes its cultural value.
The school's policy resulted in the bowdlerisation of several important texts.
Many authors oppose the bowdlerisation of their works as it alters their original message.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: censorship - the suppression or prohibition of parts of books, films, or news

Etymology

From the name of Thomas Bowdler, who published an expurgated edition of Shakespeare's works in 1818.
