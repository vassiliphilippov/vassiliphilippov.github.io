to moil

1. VERB

/ mɔɪl / (AmE), / mɔɪl / (BrE)
to make wet or dirty; to moisten or soil
make wet or dirty
archaic

Examples

Her tears moiled the letter, leaving damp marks on the page.
The muddy water moiled his shoes as he walked through the field.
Years of hard work have moiled the old farmer's hands.

Synset Examples

Her tears moiled the letter

Etymology

Probably from Middle English moillen (to moisten, soil), of uncertain origin, possibly from Old French moillier (to wet, moisten).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00218640-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ mɔɪl / (AmE), / mɔɪl / (BrE)
to work hard, especially at something physically demanding or monotonous
work hard, toil

Examples

The miners would moil underground for hours in harsh conditions.
She continued to moil over her research late into the night.
Generations before us had to moil to make a living.

Synset Examples

She was digging away at her math homework
Lexicographers drudge all day long

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toil - to work extremely hard or incessantly
  • Synonym: drudge - to do hard, menial, or monotonous work

Etymology

From Old French *moillier* (to moisten, stir up), probably influenced by Latin *mollis* (soft).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02424873-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ mɔɪl / (AmE), / mɔɪl / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to be agitated or churned, especially by turbulence or movement.
(of liquid) to be agitated or churned

Examples

Waves moiled around the rocks during the fierce storm.
The river moiled with silt after the heavy rains.
Beneath the surface, the dark waters moiled and frothed in constant turmoil.

Synset Examples

the sea was churning in the storm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: churn - move or stir (liquid) vigorously
  • Antonym: calm - (of liquid) still and undisturbed

Etymology

From Middle English 'moilen' (to moisten, toil), from Old French 'moillier' (to wet), from Latin 'mollis' (soft).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
