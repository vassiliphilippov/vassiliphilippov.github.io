Fahrenheit

1. PROPN

/ ˈfærənˌhaɪt / (AmE), / ˈfærənˌhaɪt / (BrE)
Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit (1686–1736), the German physicist who invented the mercury thermometer and developed the Fahrenheit temperature scale.
German physicist, inventor of Fahrenheit scale

Examples

Fahrenheit was responsible for significant advancements in temperature measurement.
Many historians credit Fahrenheit with inventing the modern mercury thermometer.
The temperature scale named after Fahrenheit is still widely used in the United States.

Etymology

From the name of Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit, the inventor of the temperature scale.

2. ADJ

/ ˈfærənˌhaɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɑːrənhaɪt / (BrE)
Of or relating to the temperature scale on which water freezes at 32 degrees and boils at 212 degrees, commonly used in the United States.
relating to the Fahrenheit temperature scale

Examples

The oven should be set to 350 degrees Fahrenheit for this recipe.
Yesterday, the temperature reached 100 degrees Fahrenheit in the city.
She preferred to record the results in Fahrenheit rather than Celsius.

Synset Examples

water freezes at 32 degrees Fahrenheit under normal conditions

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Celsius - relating to the temperature scale on which water freezes at 0 degrees and boils at 100 degrees

Etymology

Named after Gabriel Fahrenheit (1686–1736), German physicist and engineer.
