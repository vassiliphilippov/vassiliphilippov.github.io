tenuous

1. ADJ

/ ˈtɛnjuəs / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjuːəs / (BrE)
(of a substance or fluid) having a thin or weak consistency; not dense or thick.
having a thin or weak consistency

Examples

The atmosphere on Mars is so tenuous that humans cannot breathe it without help.
A tenuous mist hovered above the cool morning grass.
The liquid was so tenuous that it barely left a mark on the surface.

Synset Examples

a tenuous fluid

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dense - having much matter in a given space; thick or compact

Etymology

From Latin 'tenuis' (thin, slender).

2. ADJ

/ ˈtɛnjuəs / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjʊəs / (BrE)
Very weak, insubstantial, or lacking significance; slight or flimsy.
very weak or insubstantial

Examples

The evidence supporting their theory was extremely tenuous and failed to convince the panel.
Her claim to the inheritance was considered tenuous by the other family members.
There was only a tenuous connection between the suspect and the crime.

Synset Examples

slight evidence
a tenuous argument
a thin plot
a fragile claim to fame

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weak - lacking strength or solidity
  • Antonym: strong - having strength or solidity
  • Synonym: insubstantial - not solid or firm; weak

Etymology

From Latin 'tenuis' (thin, slight).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02172469-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈtɛnjuəs / (AmE), / ˈtɛnjʊəs / (BrE)
very thin, slender, or insubstantial in form, especially in gauge or diameter
very thin or slender

Examples

A single tenuous thread held the delicate spider's web together.
The tenuous wires easily broke under the slightest pressure.
Sunlight filtered through the tenuous curtains, softly illuminating the room.

Synset Examples

a tenuous thread

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thin - having little thickness or depth
  • Antonym: thick - having a large distance between opposite sides

Etymology

From Latin tenus (thin, drawn out).
