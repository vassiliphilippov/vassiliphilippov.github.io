barbarous

1. ADJ

/ ˈbɑːr.bɚ.əs / (AmE), / ˈbɑː.bə.rəs / (BrE)
(of people or customs) lacking civilization, refinement, or culture; primitive or uncivilized.
primitive or uncivilized

Examples

The explorers described the remote tribe as barbarous in their customs and daily life.
In the nineteenth century, Europeans often labeled unfamiliar traditions as barbarous.
Some historians have challenged the view that ancient societies were wholly barbarous in their practices.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: civilized - characterized by advanced social and cultural development

Etymology

From Latin 'barbarus' (foreign, uncivilized), from Greek 'barbaros' (foreign).

2. ADJ

/ ˈbɑrbərəs / (AmE), / ˈbɑːbərəs / (BrE)
(of a person or action) extremely cruel or brutal; able or willing to inflict pain or suffering.
cruel or brutal; inflicting pain

Examples

The dictator was known for his barbarous treatment of political prisoners.
She described the attack as a barbarous act that shocked the entire nation.
Historical records detail the barbarous punishments once inflicted on criminals.

Synset Examples

a barbarous crime
brutal beatings
cruel tortures
Stalin's roughshod treatment of the kulaks
a savage slap
vicious kicks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cruel - willing to cause pain or suffering to others
  • Synonym: brutal - savagely violent; cruel
  • Antonym: kind - having or showing a friendly, generous, and considerate nature

Etymology

From Latin barbarus (foreign, uncivilized), itself from Greek barbaros (foreign, non-Greek).

Source: WordNet
