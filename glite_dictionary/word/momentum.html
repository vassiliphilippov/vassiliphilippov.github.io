momentum

1. NOUN

/ moʊˈmɛntəm / (AmE), / məˈmɛntəm / (BrE)
in physics, the quantity of motion of a moving body, measured as the product of its mass and its velocity.
product of mass and velocity (physics)

Examples

The momentum of the truck made it difficult to stop quickly.
In physics class, we calculated the momentum of a moving ball.
When two objects collide, the total momentum is conserved.

Synset Examples

the momentum of the particles was deduced from meteoritic velocities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impulse - the change in momentum produced by a force

Etymology

From Latin momentum (movement, motion).

2. NOUN

/ moʊˈmɛntəm / (AmE), / məˈmɛntəm / (BrE)
the force or strength that keeps something moving or making progress after it has started
driving force or motion

Examples

The football team gained momentum after scoring their first touchdown.
The company's new marketing strategy gave its sales momentum throughout the year.
Once the train started rolling downhill, its momentum was impossible to stop quickly.

Synset Examples

the car's momentum carried it off the road

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impetus - the force that makes something happen or move

Etymology

From Latin 'momentum' meaning movement or motion, from 'movere' (to move).
