purine

1. NOUN

/ ˈpjʊrˌin / (AmE), / ˈpjʊəriːn / (BrE)
a colorless crystalline compound containing nitrogen, forming the basic structure of several important biological molecules including adenine and guanine
nitrogenous base in biological molecules

Examples

Purine bases like adenine and guanine are essential components of DNA and RNA.
Excess purine intake can contribute to the development of gout in susceptible individuals.
Scientists first isolated purine from uric acid in the 19th century.

Etymology

From German 'Purine', coined in the late 19th century from Latin 'purus' (pure) + 'uricum' (uric acid).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpjʊˌrin / (AmE), / ˈpjʊəriːn / (BrE)
a colorless crystalline organic compound with a double-ring structure, which serves as the parent compound of a group of bases found in DNA and RNA, such as adenine and guanine
a double-ring organic base found in nucleic acids
medical

Examples

Adenine and guanine are both classified as purines in the structure of DNA.
Some medications are prescribed to lower purine levels in patients with gout.
The study focused on how different enzymes break down purines in the body.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: pyrimidine - a single-ring heterocyclic base found in nucleic acids, such as cytosine, thymine, and uracil

Etymology

From German Purin, from Latin purus (pure) + -in (a common chemical suffix).
