talkative

1. ADJ

/ ˈtɔkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈtɔːkətɪv / (BrE)
tending to talk a lot, especially about trivial matters; fond of talking.
fond of talking a lot

Examples

My new coworker is talkative, so there is rarely a dull moment in the office.
She grew more talkative after getting to know everyone at the party.
The teacher asked the talkative students to pay more attention during class.

Synset Examples

kept from her housework by gabby neighbors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chatty - informal; fond of chatting or casual talk
  • Antonym: quiet - making little or no noise; reserved in speech

Etymology

From Middle English, from talk + -ative (patterned after narrative, etc.).

2. ADJ

/ ˈtɔk.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈtɔː.kə.tɪv / (BrE)
willing and eager to talk; fond of talking, often in a friendly and open way.
willing to talk; fond of talking

Examples

Her talkative nature made her popular at every party she attended.
The children became talkative after eating all that birthday cake.
He is less talkative than his sister, who loves to share stories.

Synset Examples

wine made the guest expansive

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chatty - informal synonym meaning fond of talking in a friendly way
  • Antonym: reticent - opposite meaning: not revealing one's thoughts or feelings readily

Etymology

From 'talk' + '-ative', patterned after similar adjectives.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00498983-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈtɑːkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈtɔːkətɪv / (BrE)
tending to talk a lot, often more than is wise or desirable; fond of speaking.
inclined to talk a lot

Examples

My sister is very talkative and can keep a conversation going for hours.
During the meeting, his talkative nature sometimes distracted others from the main topic.
She became less talkative after moving to a new city.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chatty - informal, friendly, and inclined to talk a lot
  • Antonym: reserved - slow to reveal emotion or opinions; not talkative
  • Antonym: silent - not speaking; making no sound

Etymology

From Middle English, from talk + -ative, influenced by equivalent French forms.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00776858-s
Synset POS: s
