substantive

1. NOUN

/ ˈsʌb.stən.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.stən.tɪv / (BrE)
(Linguistics) A word or group of words that functions as a noun in a sentence.
word or phrase functioning as a noun

Examples

In linguistics, a substantive is any word or expression used as a noun.
The phrase 'the poor' serves as a substantive in this sentence.
Grammarians often distinguish between adjectives and substantives in Latin.

Etymology

From Latin substāntivum (noun), from substantia (substance).

2. ADJ

/ ˈsʌbstənˌtɪv / (AmE), / ˈsʌbstəntɪv / (BrE)
having a firm basis in reality, meaningful and important; considerable in amount or substance, especially in prompting thought or discussion
important and meaningful; thought-provoking

Examples

The professor led a substantive debate that challenged everyone to think deeply about the issue.
Her report provided substantive evidence to support the claims made in the article.
Let’s focus on substantive questions rather than getting lost in trivial details.

Synset Examples

a meaty discussion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meaty - rich in content; thought-provoking
  • Antonym: insubstantial - lacking substance or importance

Etymology

From Latin 'substantivus' (serving to substantiate), from 'substantia' (substance).

3. ADJ

/ ˈsʌbstənˌtɪv / (AmE), / ˈsʌbstəntɪv / (BrE)
relating to the essential or real elements of something; having a firm basis in reality and therefore important, meaningful, or considerable.
relating to essentials or real importance

Examples

The committee is seeking substantive changes to the proposed legislation.
Before making a decision, let's focus on the substantive issues rather than minor details.
Her argument lacked substantive evidence and was therefore unconvincing.

Synset Examples

a substantial argument

Related Terms

  • Synonym: essential - absolutely necessary or extremely important
  • Synonym: substantial - of considerable importance, size, or worth
  • Antonym: incidental - happening as a minor accompaniment or by chance

Etymology

From Latin 'substantivus', from 'substantia' (substance).

4. ADJ

/ ˈsʌbstənˌtɪv / (AmE), / ˈsʌbstəntɪv / (BrE)
(law) relating to rights and duties themselves, as opposed to the procedures for enforcing them.
(law) relating to rights and duties, not procedures
legal

Examples

The court ruled that the issue must be resolved under substantive law, not procedural rules.
Understanding the difference between substantive and procedural rights is crucial for law students.
This statute contains the substantive rules governing contracts, outlining what parties can and cannot agree to.

Synset Examples

substantive law

Related Terms

  • Antonym: procedural - relating to the rules and methods by which legal rights and duties are enforced

Etymology

From Medieval Latin substantivus, from Latin substantia (‘substance’).
