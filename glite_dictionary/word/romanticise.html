to romanticise

1. VERB

/ roʊˈmæntəˌsaɪz / (AmE), / rəˈmantɪsaɪz / (BrE)
to make something romantic in style, character, or feeling
make romantic in style
british

Examples

The designer romanticised the little black dress with lace and soft fabrics.
Modern films often romanticise historical events, focusing on emotion over fact.
The author romanticised city life, describing it as endlessly vibrant and mysterious.

Synset Examples

The designer romanticized the little black dress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to romanticize - to make something romantic in style (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'romantic' + '-ise', modelled after French 'romantiser'.

2. VERB

/ roʊˈmæntɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / rəʊˈmæntɪsaɪz / (BrE)
to interpret or present something in an idealized or unrealistic manner, often making it seem more appealing or attractive than it actually is
interpret or present in an idealized way

Examples

It's easy to romanticise the past, forgetting the hardships people faced.
Many movies tend to romanticise life in the countryside.
You shouldn't romanticise a job that is actually very demanding and stressful.

Synset Examples

Don't romanticize this uninteresting and hard work!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to idealize - to regard or represent as perfect or better than reality
  • Antonym: to realise - to recognize or accept as real or true

Etymology

From 'romantic', ultimately from Old French 'romanz', via Latin 'romanice' (in the Roman way).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00694938-v
Synset POS: v
