symbolism

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪmbəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsɪmbəlɪzəm / (BrE)
a late 19th-century artistic and literary movement that sought to express abstract or mystical ideas by using symbolic images and indirect suggestion rather than direct statement
late 19th-century artistic and literary movement

Examples

Her thesis explores the influence of symbolism on early modernist poetry.
Many paintings from the Symbolism movement contain dreamlike, enigmatic figures.
Although less popular today, symbolism deeply impacted twentieth-century literature.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: impressionism - an artistic movement focused on representing the impression of a moment, especially regarding light and color

Etymology

From French 'symbolisme', based on Greek 'symbolon' (token, mark, sign).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪmbəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsɪmbəlɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
a system or practice of using symbols or symbolic representations to express ideas, particularly in art or literature
use of symbols to represent ideas

Examples

The novel relies heavily on symbolism to convey its deeper themes.
In many religions, color symbolism is crucial to rituals and iconography.
Modern art often employs symbolism to represent emotions and ideas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allegory - a story, poem, or picture with a hidden meaning, typically moral or political
  • Antonym: literalism - the interpretation of words in their most basic, literal sense without symbolic meaning

Etymology

From French ‘symbolisme’, from Greek ‘symbolon’ (token, sign).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɪmbəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsɪmbəlɪzəm / (BrE)
the practice of representing ideas or qualities by using symbols; the attribution of symbolic meanings to objects, events, or actions
the use of symbols to represent ideas

Examples

The poet's use of symbolism adds depth and meaning to his verses.
Modern art often relies heavily on symbolism to express complex themes.
She explained the symbolism behind the colors chosen for the national flag.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metaphor - a figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable
  • Antonym: literalism - adherence to the exact letter or literal sense, as in translation or interpretation

Etymology

From French 'symbolisme', from 'symbole' (symbol) + '-isme' (-ism)
