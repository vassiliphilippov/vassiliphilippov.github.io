Inka

1. PROPN

/ ˈiŋkə / (AmE), / ˈɪŋkə / (BrE)
a member of the Quechua-speaking people of the Cuzco Valley in Peru who established a vast empire in South America before the Spanish conquest in the early 16th century
member of the indigenous Inka (Inca) people and empire

Examples

The Inka built impressive cities and a vast road network across the Andes.
Historians have uncovered artifacts that shed light on Inka religious practices.
The Inka empire fell to Spanish conquerors in the 16th century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Inca - a member of the same people; common variant spelling

Etymology

From Quechua *Inka* (lord, ruler), hispanicized as 'Inca'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈiŋ.kə / (AmE), / ˈɪŋ.kə / (BrE)
A member of the Quechuan people who lived in the Cuzco valley in Peru, known for establishing the Inca Empire before the Spanish conquest.
member of the indigenous people of Peru's Cuzco valley

Examples

The Inka civilization developed advanced agricultural techniques in the Andean highlands.
Archaeologists discovered artifacts that belonged to the ancient Inka people near Cuzco.
Many descendants of the Inka still live in Peru and preserve their traditions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Inca - member of the South American indigenous culture and empire

Etymology

From Quechua *Inka* (ruler, lord, or the royal people).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09746533-n
Synset POS: n
