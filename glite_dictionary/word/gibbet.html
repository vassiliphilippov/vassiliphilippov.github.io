gibbet

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɪbɪt / (AmE), / ˈdʒɪbɪt / (BrE)
a structure, typically with a crossbeam, used for hanging criminals; a gallows
structure for hanging criminals

Examples

The condemned man was led to the gibbet at dawn.
In medieval times, the bodies of criminals were often left hanging on the gibbet as a warning.
The old gibbet still stands at the edge of the village as a grim reminder of the past.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gallows - structure for execution by hanging

Etymology

From Old French *gibet* (gallows), of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒɪbət / (AmE), / ˈdʒɪbɪt / (BrE)
to hang or expose (a body) on a gibbet as a form of execution or public display
to hang or display a body on a gibbet
archaic

Examples

The authorities decided to gibbet the traitor as a warning to others.
After the execution, the body was gibbeted at the crossroads for several days.
In medieval times, criminals were sometimes gibbeting in public to deter crime.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hang - to suspend or execute by hanging

Etymology

From Old French *gibet* (gallows), of uncertain origin.

3. VERB

/ ˈdʒɪbət / (AmE), / ˈdʒɪbɪt / (BrE)
to expose someone or something to ridicule or public scorn, often in a way that humiliates or shames them
expose to public ridicule or scorn

Examples

The politician was gibbeted in the press for his controversial remarks.
Writers throughout history have gibbeted hypocrisy and corruption in their works.
She feared he would be gibbeted by colleagues for his unconventional ideas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to humiliate - to make someone feel ashamed or foolish
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something

Etymology

From the noun 'gibbet', originally meaning a gallows for execution, from Old French 'gibet' (gallows).
