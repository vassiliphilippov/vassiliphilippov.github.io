bogey

1. VERB

/ ˈboʊɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊɡi / (BrE)
(in golf) to score one stroke over par on a hole.
score one over par (golf)

Examples

She managed to bogey the 18th hole after a tricky putt.
If you bogey every hole, you'll finish well above par.
He bogeyed the last three holes and missed qualifying for the tournament.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: par - the number of strokes a skilled golfer is expected to take for a hole or course
  • Degree Higher: birdie - to score one stroke under par in golf

Etymology

From golf slang; possibly derived from 'bogey man'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈboʊ.ɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊ.ɡi / (BrE)
an unidentified, and possibly enemy, aircraft (especially as detected by radar)
unidentified aircraft, possibly enemy

Examples

The radar operator reported a bogey approaching from the north sector.
Pilots were instructed to intercept the bogey without firing unless fired upon.
When a bogey appears on the radar screen, protocols demand immediate identification.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: bandit - an enemy aircraft identified as hostile
  • Degree Lower: contact - any detected aircraft, not yet identified

Etymology

Originally British military slang, first used in World War II.

3. NOUN

/ ˈboʊ.ɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊ.ɡi / (BrE)
(in golf) a score of one stroke over par on a hole.
golf score of one over par

Examples

He made a bogey on the final hole, missing par by just one stroke.
After two birdies and a bogey, she finished the front nine just over par.
If you play safe on this hole, you’ll probably end up with a bogey.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: par - the standard number of strokes for a hole or round in golf
  • Degree Higher: birdie - a golf score of one under par on a hole

Etymology

From earlier slang meaning 'devil' or 'bugbear', possibly from Scots or Middle English, applied in golf from mid 19th century.

4. NOUN

/ ˈboʊɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊɡi / (BrE)
(archaic) A bogle, goblin, or evil spirit, sometimes used as a name for the Devil.
archaic: goblin or evil spirit
archaic

Examples

The old tale warned children of the bogey lurking in the woods at night.
In some stories, the bogey is blamed for unexplained disappearances.
They spoke in hushed tones, fearful of summoning the bogey by name.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: goblin - a mischievous, ugly fairy or sprite
  • Synonym: sprite - a small, elusive supernatural being
  • Synonym: Devil - the supreme spirit of evil in Christianity

Etymology

Probably from Scots 'bogle' (ghost, goblin).
