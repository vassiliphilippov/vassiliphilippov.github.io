commensal

1. ADJ

/ kəˈmɛnsəl / (AmE), / kəˈmɛnsəl / (BrE)
(of organisms) living together in a relationship in which one benefits while the other is neither helped nor harmed.
living in a commensal relationship
medical

Examples

Many bacteria in the human gut are considered commensal rather than harmful.
The remora is a commensal fish that attaches itself to larger marine animals.
Most fungi in soil have a commensal relationship with plant roots.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: parasitic - living at the expense of another, causing harm
  • Antonym: mutualistic - living together for mutual benefit

Etymology

From Medieval Latin commensalis, from Latin com- (together) + mensa (table), originally meaning 'sharing a table.'

2. NOUN

/ kəˈmɛn.səl / (AmE), / kəˈmɛn.səl / (BrE)
an animal or plant that lives in close association with another of a different species, without being dependent on it or harming it
organism living in association with another without dependency

Examples

The barnacle is a classic commensal that attaches to whales without affecting them.
In the study, scientists identified several commensals living alongside coral reefs.
A commensal may benefit from the host's presence without providing any return benefit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: symbiont - an organism living in a close, often long-term, biological interaction with another
  • Antonym: parasite - an organism that lives in or on another organism and benefits at the host's expense
  • Antonym: mutualist - an organism that is part of a relationship where both partners benefit

Etymology

From Latin 'commensalis' (sharing a table), from 'com-' (together) + 'mensa' (table)
