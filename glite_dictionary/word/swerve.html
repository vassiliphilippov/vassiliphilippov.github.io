swerve

1. NOUN

/ swɝv / (AmE), / swɜːv / (BrE)
an act of turning or changing direction suddenly, especially to avoid something
sudden change in direction

Examples

The car made a sudden swerve to avoid hitting the dog.
His unexpected swerve startled everyone on the road.
There was a dangerous swerve near the bend in the highway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to veer - a sudden change in direction
  • Antonym: to straighten - to become or make straight; to return to original course

Etymology

From Middle English swerven, from Old English sweorfan (to rub, file away).

2. NOUN

/ swɝːv / (AmE), / swɜːv / (BrE)
a sudden, sharp movement to the side away from a straight course
sudden sideways movement

Examples

The car made a swerve to avoid hitting the deer.
His sudden swerve surprised the other drivers on the road.
With a quick swerve, the cyclist dodged the pothole.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction suddenly
  • Antonym: to straighten - to return to or move in a straight line

Etymology

From Old English sweorfan (to rub, scour, or file away), evolving to mean turning aside.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ swɝv / (AmE), / swɜːv / (BrE)
to change direction suddenly, especially to avoid hitting something
change direction suddenly

Examples

The cyclist had to swerve to avoid hitting the dog that ran into the road.
If you swerve suddenly on a wet road, you might lose control of your car.
The football player managed to swerve past three defenders before scoring a goal.

Synset Examples

The car cut to the left at the intersection
The motorbike veered to the right

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction suddenly
  • Synonym: turn - to move in a different direction

Etymology

From Middle English 'swerwen', from Old English 'sweorfan' (to rub, scour, sweep), influenced by Old Norse 'sverfa'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02037140-v
Synset POS: v
