to persuade

1. VERB

/ pərˈsweɪd / (AmE), / pəˈsweɪd / (BrE)
to cause someone to do or believe something by giving them good reasons; to convince someone to take a particular action or adopt an opinion
cause someone to do or believe something by reasoning

Examples

She managed to persuade her friend to join the gym with her.
No matter what he said, he could not persuade his parents to change their minds.
The advertisement is designed to persuade people to try the new product.

Synset Examples

You can't persuade me to buy this ugly vase!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to convince - to cause someone to believe firmly in the truth of something
  • Antonym: to dissuade - to persuade someone not to take a particular course of action

Etymology

From Latin persuasus, past participle of persuadere ‘advise, urge’.

2. VERB

/ pərˈsweɪd / (AmE), / pəˈsweɪd / (BrE)
to win approval or support for something by reasoning or appealing to others
win support by reasoning or appeal

Examples

She tried to persuade the committee to support the proposal, but they remained unconvinced.
The politician's speech failed to persuade voters to change their minds.
His charm and determination helped him persuade colleagues to join his cause.

Synset Examples

Carry all before one
His speech did not sway the voters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to convince - to cause someone to believe firmly in the truth of something
  • Antonym: to dissuade - to persuade someone not to take a particular course of action

Etymology

From Latin persuadere (to advise, urge, convince), from per- (through) + suadere (to advise, recommend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02592213-v
Synset POS: v
