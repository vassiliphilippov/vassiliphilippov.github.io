rudder

1. NOUN

/ ˈrʌdɚ / (AmE), / ˈrʌdə / (BrE)
A hinged vertical flat surface at the tail of an aircraft, used to control its horizontal direction by deflecting airflow.
vertical control surface on aircraft tail

Examples

The pilot used the rudder to turn the plane smoothly to the right.
Strong winds tested the effectiveness of the rudder during the landing approach.
A damaged rudder can make it difficult to maintain a stable course in flight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aileron - a hinged surface on the wing of an aircraft for controlling roll

Etymology

From Old English 'rother' (paddle, oar), originally referring to a boat's steering device.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrʌdər / (AmE), / ˈrʌdə / (BrE)
a flat, usually vertical piece hinged at the stern of a vessel, used for steering by changing the direction of water flow
steering blade at a vessel's stern

Examples

The ship’s rudder was damaged during the storm, making navigation difficult.
The captain turned the rudder to steer the boat safely away from the rocks.
Older ships often used a simple wooden rudder attached to the back.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: helm - the steering apparatus of a ship, including handle or wheel
  • Degree Lower: keel - the central structural base of a vessel's hull

Etymology

From Old English 'rother,' meaning paddle or oar, influenced by Middle Dutch 'roer.'
