Lamiaceae

1. NOUN

/ læmiˈeɪsiˌi / (AmE), / læmiˈeɪsiːiː / (BrE)
A large family of aromatic herbs and shrubs, including mint, thyme, sage, and rosemary, characterized by flowers with a split-lip appearance and four-lobed ovaries producing nutlets.
botanical family of mint and similar herbs

Examples

Many culinary herbs, such as mint and basil, belong to the Lamiaceae family.
The Lamiaceae are known for their aromatic oils, which are used in perfumes and cooking.
Scientists studied the medicinal properties of plants in the Lamiaceae.

Etymology

From Latin 'Lamium' (dead-nettle) + '-aceae', a standard suffix for plant family names.
