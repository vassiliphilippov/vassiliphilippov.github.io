theanthropism

1. NOUN

/ θiˈænθɹəˌpɪzəm / (AmE), / θiˈænθɹəˌpɪzəm / (BrE)
(Theology) The doctrine that Jesus Christ was both fully human and fully divine, representing a union of the human and the divine.
doctrine that Jesus was both human and divine

Examples

The doctrine of theanthropism is central to many branches of Christian theology.
Some early church debates focused on interpretations of theanthropism and the nature of Christ.
The Council addressed heresies that challenged theanthropism in explaining the relationship between Jesus's divinity and humanity.

Etymology

From Greek 'theos' (god) + 'anthrōpos' (man) + '-ism'.

2. NOUN

/ θiˈænθrəˌpɪzəm / (AmE), / θiˈænθrəˌpɪzəm / (BrE)
the attribution of human characteristics or form to a deity or non-human entity
attribution of human traits to a god or object

Examples

Many mythologies display theanthropism by depicting gods with human emotions and bodies.
The artist's painting of the sun with a face is a classic example of theanthropism.
Debates about theanthropism are common in comparative religion studies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anthropomorphism - the attribution of human attributes to non-human entities

Etymology

From Greek theos (god) + anthrōpos (man).
