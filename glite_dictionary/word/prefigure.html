to prefigure

1. VERB

/ priˈfɪɡjər / (AmE), / priˈfɪɡjə / (BrE)
to imagine or consider something in advance; to foresee or anticipate.
to imagine or anticipate in advance

Examples

She tried to prefigure the outcome of the negotiations before making a decision.
I had prefigured that the test would be difficult, but it was actually quite manageable.
He often prefigures potential problems before they arise, which helps him prepare.

Synset Examples

It wasn't as bad as I had prefigured

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to anticipate - to regard as probable; expect or predict
  • Synonym: to foresee - to be aware of beforehand; to predict

Etymology

From Latin praefigurare, from prae- (before) + figurare (to form, shape).

2. VERB

/ priˈfɪɡjɚ / (AmE), / priˈfɪɡjə / (BrE)
to represent, indicate, or suggest something in advance, especially as a sign or omen
to foreshadow; to suggest in advance

Examples

The artist’s early work prefigures the style that would make him famous later in his career.
Dark clouds on the horizon prefigure an approaching storm.
The collapse of the bridges prefigured the disaster that followed.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foreshadow - to be a warning or indication of a future event
  • Synonym: to predict - to say or estimate that something will happen in the future
  • Antonym: to reveal - to make known something previously hidden or unknown

Etymology

From Latin prae- (before) + figurare (to form, shape).

WordNet ID: oewn-00873606-v
Synset POS: v
