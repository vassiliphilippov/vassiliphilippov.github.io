truckle

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrʌkəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌkəl / (BrE)
a low bed on wheels that can be stored under a higher bed
a low bed stored under another bed

Examples

The guest room had a truckle hidden under the main bed for extra sleeping space.
When her cousins visited, she slept on the truckle to save room.
He pulled the truckle out at night and pushed it away in the morning.

Etymology

From Latin 'trochlea' (pulley, sheaf), via Middle French and Middle English; referring to rolling or wheels.

2. VERB

/ ˈtrʌkəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌkəl / (BrE)
to act in a subservient or obsequious manner in order to gain favor; to submit or yield obsequiously
to act in a subservient or cringing way

Examples

He would truckle to anyone in authority, hoping for a promotion.
Some politicians truckle to powerful interests instead of standing by their principles.
She refused to truckle to pressure from her colleagues and stayed true to her ideals.

Synset Examples

He is always kowtowing to his boss

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kowtow - to act in an excessively subservient manner
  • Synonym: fawn - to flatter or behave obsequiously in order to gain favor
  • Antonym: to defy - to openly resist or refuse to obey

Etymology

From Middle English 'truckle bed', from Latin 'trochlea' (pulley, sheaf), referring to something that rolls or is diminished in position, later extended to mean submitting.

3. VERB

/ ˈtrʌkəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌkəl / (BrE)
to submit or yield obsequiously or out of weakness, often to someone in authority
to yield or submit obsequiously

Examples

He refused to truckle to his boss's unreasonable demands.
Some politicians truckle to special interests instead of standing up for what is right.
She despised those who truckled to authority out of fear.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yield - to give way to pressure or submit to another's authority
  • Synonym: to submit - to accept or yield to a superior force or to the authority of another
  • Antonym: to defy - to openly resist or refuse to obey

Etymology

From Latin *trochlea* (pulley), via Middle English and Old French, originally referring to a small wheel or pulley (see noun sense); figurative sense of 'yielding' from the idea of rolling along.
