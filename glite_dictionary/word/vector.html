vector

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɛktɚ / (AmE), / ˈvɛktə / (BrE)
(genetics) a virus or other agent that is used to deliver DNA or genetic material into a cell.
agent delivering DNA to cells (genetics)

Examples

The scientist used a modified virus as a vector to introduce the gene into the plant cells.
Plasmids are commonly employed as vectors in genetic engineering experiments.
Choosing the right vector is crucial for successful gene therapy.

Etymology

From Latin vector (carrier, transporter), from vehere (to carry).

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɛktər / (AmE), / ˈvɛktə / (BrE)
an organism, such as an insect, that carries and transmits a disease-causing agent from one host to another
organism transmitting disease
medical

Examples

The Zika virus is primarily spread by mosquitoes acting as vectors.
Researchers study the role of ticks as vectors for Lyme disease.
Controlling insect vectors is crucial in the prevention of many tropical diseases.

Synset Examples

mosquitos are vectors of malaria and yellow fever
fleas are vectors of the plague
aphids are transmitters of plant diseases
when medical scientists talk about vectors they are usually talking about insects

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carrier - an organism that harbors a pathogen and transmits it to others

Etymology

From Latin vector (carrier), from vehere (to carry).

3. NOUN

/ ˈvɛk.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈvɛk.tə / (BrE)
A quantity in mathematics and physics represented by a straight line segment with both length (magnitude) and direction.
mathematical entity with magnitude and direction

Examples

In physics, a vector is used to represent quantities like velocity and force.
You must add the vectors head-to-tail to find the resultant displacement.
The vector points north with a magnitude of five units.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: scalar - a quantity having only magnitude and no direction

Etymology

From Latin 'vector' (carrier), from 'vehere' (to carry).

Source: WordNet

4. NOUN

/ ˈvɛktɚ / (AmE), / ˈvɛktə / (BrE)
a quantity that has both magnitude and direction and can be represented by an arrow or resolved into components, especially in mathematics and physics
quantity with magnitude and direction (math, physics)

Examples

In physics, a vector can represent velocity, which has both speed and direction.
The force acting on the object was described by a mathematical vector.
You can break a vector into horizontal and vertical components for easier calculation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: scalar - a quantity that has magnitude but no direction

Etymology

From Latin 'vector' (carrier, conveyer), from 'vehere' (to carry).
