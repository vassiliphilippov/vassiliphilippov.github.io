adagio

1. ADJ

/ əˈdɑdʒiˌoʊ / (AmE), / əˈdɑːdʒiˌəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) to be played or performed at a slow and leisurely tempo.
slow tempo (music)

Examples

The piece should be performed adagio to highlight its gentle character.
The conductor instructed the orchestra to slow down to adagio for the final movement.
In the score, the transition from allegro to adagio is clearly marked.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: allegro - (in music) fast and lively tempo
  • Degree Lower: largo - (in music) very slow tempo
  • Degree Higher: andante - (in music) at a walking pace, moderately slow

Etymology

From Italian adagio, meaning 'at ease' or 'leisurely'.

2. NOUN

/ əˈdɑdʒioʊ / (AmE), / əˈdɑːdʒɪəʊ / (BrE)
(music) a passage or composition played at a slow and graceful tempo.
slow, graceful musical composition

Examples

The orchestra performed the adagio with remarkable sensitivity and control.
Mozart’s Clarinet Concerto features a beautiful adagio in the second movement.
She found the adagio section of the ballet especially moving.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: largo - (music) a passage or composition played at a very slow tempo
  • Degree Higher: andante - (music) a passage or composition played at a moderately slow tempo

Etymology

From Italian adagio (at ease), from Latin ad (to) + agio (ease).

a slow section of a pas de deux in ballet that requires great skill and strength from the dancers
slow, skillful ballet section

Examples

The dancers performed the adagio with remarkable grace and perfect synchronization.
During the adagio, the male dancer demonstrated incredible strength as he lifted his partner effortlessly.
Her favorite part of the ballet was the adagio, where the music slowed and the emotion became more intense.

Related Terms

From Italian adagio (slowly), originally from Latin ad agium (at ease).
