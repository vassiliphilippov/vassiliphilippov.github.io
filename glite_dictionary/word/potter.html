potter

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈpɒtə / (BrE)
a person who makes pottery by shaping clay and firing it in a kiln
a person who makes pottery

Examples

The potter shaped the wet clay into a beautiful vase on his wheel.
She has been a skilled potter for over twenty years, selling her work at local markets.
A master potter always knows the right temperature to fire the clay in the kiln.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ceramist - an artist who makes objects from ceramic materials
  • Synonym: sculptor - an artist who makes sculptures

Etymology

From Old French potier, from pot (pot).

2. VERB

/ ˈpɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈpɒtə / (BrE)
to move or work casually or without hurry, often doing small or unimportant tasks
move or work in a leisurely way
british

Examples

On Sunday mornings, I like to potter around the kitchen, trying new recipes.
She spent the afternoon pottering in the garden, pulling weeds and watering plants.
He pottered about his workshop, tinkering with various projects but finishing none.

Synset Examples

The old lady is pottering around in the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: putter - to occupy oneself in a leisurely, aimless, or ineffective manner

Etymology

Probably from earlier sense 'to poke about, search', of uncertain origin.

4. VERB

/ ˈpɑːtər / (AmE), / ˈpɒtə / (BrE)
to move or occupy oneself in a leisurely, aimless, or ineffective way
move or act aimlessly and leisurely

Examples

She likes to potter around the garden on weekends, tending to her plants.
After breakfast, I pottered about the house with nothing in particular to do.
He spent the afternoon pottering in his workshop, fixing odds and ends.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: putter - to occupy oneself in a leisurely or aimless way
  • Synonym: to dawdle - to waste time by moving slowly or idly
  • Antonym: rush - to move with urgent haste

Etymology

Possibly related to 'potter' (noun), but the verb sense may be of imitative origin, referring to puttering or fussing about.
