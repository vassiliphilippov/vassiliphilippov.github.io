affirmation

1. NOUN

/ ˌæfərˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌafəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
The act of stating or asserting something as true; a positive statement or declaration.
the act of stating or asserting something

Examples

The lawyer's affirmation was crucial to the case.
He nodded in affirmation when asked if he agreed with the proposal.
Her daily affirmation boosted her confidence and motivation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assertion - a confident and forceful statement of fact or belief
  • Antonym: denial - the assertion that something is not true

Etymology

From Latin affirmatio (assertion, confirmation), from affirmare (to affirm).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæfɚˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæfəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
(in law and religion) a solemn declaration, formally made as an alternative to an oath, by a person who objects to swearing an oath on religious or ethical grounds.
a solemn declaration made instead of an oath
legal

Examples

The witness made an affirmation instead of swearing an oath in court.
Some people prefer to take an affirmation because of their personal beliefs.
By signing the legal affirmation, she formally accepted the truth of her statement.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: oath - a solemn promise, often invoking a divine witness

Etymology

From Latin affirmatio, from affirmare (to affirm).

3. NOUN

/ ˌæfɚˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæfəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Law) A judgment by a higher court that confirms the decision of a lower court as correct and allows it to stand.
a higher court upholding a lower court's judgment
legal

Examples

The appellate court's affirmation of the lower court's ruling brought relief to the defendant.
After a thorough review, the Supreme Court issued an affirmation of the original verdict.
The judge explained the significance of the affirmation in upholding judicial consistency.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reversal - a court's judgment overturning a lower court's decision

Etymology

From Latin affirmatio (assertion), related to affirmare (to affirm).
