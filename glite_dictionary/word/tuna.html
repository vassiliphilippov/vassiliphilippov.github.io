tuna

1. NOUN

/ ˈtuːnə / (AmE), / ˈtjuːnə / (BrE)
A tropical American prickly pear plant native to Jamaica; also, the edible fruit of this cactus.
Jamaican prickly pear cactus or fruit

Examples

The tuna grows wild in many parts of Jamaica and produces a sweet, fleshy fruit.
Local markets in Jamaica often sell tuna as both a fruit and a traditional medicinal plant.
She tasted the ripe tuna and was surprised by its bright, refreshing flavor.

Etymology

Spanish 'tuna', from Taíno 'tuna' (name for the prickly pear fruit).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtuːnə / (AmE), / ˈtjuːnə / (BrE)
A large, fast-swimming, warm-water fish of the genus Thunnus, valued for its firm and oily flesh, often served as steaks or canned.
large warm-water edible fish

Examples

Fresh tuna steaks are a popular option at many seafood restaurants.
She opened a can of tuna to make a quick sandwich for lunch.
The fisherman caught a massive tuna off the coast of Hawaii.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: albacore - a type of tuna with light flesh, also a member of the genus Thunnus
  • Degree Lower: mackerel - a similar fish in the same family as tuna

Etymology

From Spanish 'atún', from Arabic 'tūn', from Latin 'thunnus', from Greek 'thynnos' (tuna).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtuːnə / (AmE), / ˈtjuːnə / (BrE)
a large marine fish of the genus Thunnus, related to the mackerel, found in warm ocean waters and valued as food and game fish
large marine food fish (Thunnus)

Examples

Large fishing boats trawl the sea in search of tuna each year.
A fresh tuna steak is delicious when grilled with lemon and herbs.
Sushi lovers often enjoy slices of raw tuna called maguro.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: albacore - a type of tuna with long pectoral fins
  • Synonym: bonito - a related fish to tuna, smaller and also a game fish

Etymology

From Spanish 'atún', from Arabic 'al-tūn', from Latin 'thunnus', from Ancient Greek 'θύννος' (thúnnos).

4. NOUN

/ ˈtuːnə / (AmE), / ˈtjuːnə / (BrE)
an eel found in New Zealand rivers and lakes, especially species important in Māori culture
a New Zealand freshwater eel
british

Examples

The Māori traditionally catch tuna during the annual migration season.
We saw several tuna swimming in the shallow river near the village.
This conservation program aims to protect the habitat of native tuna in New Zealand.

Etymology

From Māori 'tuna' (eel), used in New Zealand English.
