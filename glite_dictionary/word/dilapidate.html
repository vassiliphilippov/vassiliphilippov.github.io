to dilapidate

1. VERB

/ dɪˈlæpɪˌdeɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈlapɪdeɪt / (BrE)
to fall into a state of disrepair, decay, or ruin, often as a result of neglect
fall into disrepair or ruin

Examples

The abandoned mansion began to dilapidate after years of neglect.
If left uncared for, even the finest buildings will eventually dilapidate.
Without regular maintenance, the bridge started to dilapidate over the decades.

Synset Examples

The unoccupied house started to decay

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decay - to rot or decompose through natural processes
  • Synonym: ruin - to fall into a state of complete disrepair
  • Antonym: to restore - to return something to a former condition

Etymology

From Latin dilapidare (to demolish, scatter like stones), from dis- (apart) + lapis (stone).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈlæpɪˌdeɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈlæpɪdeɪt / (BrE)
to bring something into a state of decay or ruin as a result of neglect or misuse
bring into decay through neglect

Examples

The owner allowed the old mansion to dilapidate over the years, ignoring repeated calls for repairs.
If we do not maintain our public buildings, they will quickly dilapidate and become unsafe.
Prolonged neglect had dilapidated the once-grand estate, leaving it nearly uninhabitable.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ruin - to destroy or severely damage
  • Antonym: to restore - to bring back into a good condition

Etymology

From Latin dilapidare (to demolish, scatter), from di- (apart) + lapis (stone).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01666329-v
Synset POS: v
