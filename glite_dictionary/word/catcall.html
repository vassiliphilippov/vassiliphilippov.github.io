catcall

1. VERB

/ ˈkætˌkɔl / (AmE), / ˈkatˌkɔːl / (BrE)
to shout, whistle, or make sexually suggestive or mocking comments at someone, especially in public
make loud, mocking or suggestive calls

Examples

A group of men began to catcall the actress as she walked down the street.
She felt uncomfortable when construction workers catcalled her on her way to work.
They were reprimanded for catcalling at the passing students outside the college gates.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heckle - to interrupt a performer or speaker with derisive or aggressive comments

Etymology

Probably from imitation of a cat's call, influenced by use in theatres since the 17th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkætˌkɑl / (AmE), / ˈkatkɔːl / (BrE)
a loud, harsh, or shrill call or whistle expressing disapproval, annoyance, or aggression, often made at public performances or in the street
loud disapproving cry or whistle

Examples

The comedian was interrupted by a loud catcall from the back of the audience.
During the play, several catcalls erupted when the actor forgot his lines.
Ignoring the catcall, the politician continued his speech without hesitation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boo - a sound made to show disapproval

Etymology

Probably from the combined sense of 'cat' (imitative of a cat's howl) and 'call'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07136726-n
Synset POS: n
