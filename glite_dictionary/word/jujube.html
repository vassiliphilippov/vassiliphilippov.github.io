jujube

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒuːˌdʒuːb / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːˌdʒuːb / (BrE)
A chewy, fruit-flavored jellied candy, sometimes medicated to soothe a sore throat.
chewy fruit-flavored jellied candy

Examples

She bought a bag of jujubes at the corner store before the movie.
My grandmother always kept jujube candies in her purse for us.
Some cough drops taste just like jujube gummies.

Etymology

From French 'jujube', from Latin 'iūjúba', referring to the jujube fruit, as the candy was originally flavored with extracts from the jujube plant.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒuːˌdʒub / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdʒuːb / (BrE)
The small, dark red, plum-like fruit of the Old World buckthorn trees, especially Ziziphus jujuba, often eaten fresh or dried.
a small, dark red fruit from the jujube tree

Examples

The market was selling fresh jujube alongside more common fruits like apples and oranges.
I tasted a dried jujube for the first time during my travels in China.
Researchers are studying the health benefits of jujube as a traditional remedy.

Etymology

From French 'jujube', from Medieval Latin 'jujuba', alteration of Latin 'zizyphum', from Greek 'zizyphon'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈdʒuˌdʒub / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdʒuːb / (BrE)
A spiny tree, native to Asia, that bears small, dark red, edible fruits; also, the fruit of this tree.
spiny tree with edible red fruits

Examples

The jujube tree thrives in warm climates and is often grown for its sweet fruit.
Dried jujubes are commonly used in traditional Chinese medicine and desserts.
He planted a jujube in his backyard to enjoy its fruit each autumn.

Etymology

From French *jujube*, from Latin *zizyphum*, from Greek *zizyphon* (jujube), ultimately from an Eastern Mediterranean language.
