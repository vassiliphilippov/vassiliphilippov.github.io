alum

1. NOUN

/ ˈæləm / (AmE), / ˈaləm / (BrE)
a white crystalline double sulfate of aluminum and another element (often potassium), commonly used in dyeing and as an astringent.
a white crystalline double sulfate of aluminum

Examples

The ancient Egyptians used alum for its preservative qualities.
After shaving, he applied a block of alum to stop the bleeding.
Industries often use alum in the purification of water.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: potash alum - the most common form of alum, specifically potassium aluminum sulfate

Etymology

From Latin alumen (bitter salt, alum).

2. NOUN

/ ˈæləm / (AmE), / ˈaləm / (BrE)
a colorless or white crystalline double sulfate of aluminum and potassium, used as an astringent and in various industrial processes
double sulfate compound, used as an astringent

Examples

The pharmacist recommended alum to help stop the bleeding from the small cut.
Many water treatment plants add alum to help clarify the water.
A pinch of alum can be used in pickling to keep vegetables crisp.

Etymology

From Latin 'alumen, aluminis' meaning 'bitter salt'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈæləm / (AmE), / ˈaləm / (BrE)
a white crystalline compound that is a double sulfate of aluminum and another element, such as potassium or ammonium, used in dyeing, tanning, and as astringents.
a white crystalline aluminum compound

Examples

The chemist added alum to the solution to initiate the reaction.
Some pickling recipes call for a small amount of alum to keep vegetables crisp.
Historically, alum was used in textile dyeing to help fix colors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: potash alum - potassium aluminum sulfate, a common form of alum

Etymology

From Latin *alumen*, meaning 'bitter salt, alum'.
