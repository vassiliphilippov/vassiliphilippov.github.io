mimesis

1. NOUN

/ maɪˈmiːsɪs / (AmE), / mɪˈmiːsɪs / (BrE)
the imitation or representation of aspects of the real world, especially human behavior, in art and literature
imitation of reality in art or literature

Examples

The artist relies on mimesis to capture the subtle emotions of his subjects.
Greek philosophers debated the role of mimesis in both poetry and drama.
Many modern works reject mimesis in favor of abstraction and symbolism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imitation - the act of copying someone or something

Etymology

From Greek mīmēsis (imitation), from mimeisthai (to imitate).

/ maɪˈmiːsɪs / (AmE), / mɪˈmiːsɪs / (BrE)
the imitation or direct representation of another person's words in speech or writing
imitation in speech or writing

Examples

The novelist frequently employs mimesis to create realistic dialogue between her characters.
Ancient Greek philosophers debated the value of mimesis in both art and literature.

3. NOUN

/ maɪˈmiːsɪs / (AmE), / mɪˈmiːsɪs / (BrE)
a disease that exhibits symptoms characteristic of another disease, making diagnosis more difficult
disease showing symptoms of another
medical

Examples

The patient's condition was initially misdiagnosed due to mimesis of another illness.
Doctors must be aware of mimesis to avoid confusing one disease for another.
In some rare cases, mimesis leads to significant delays in proper treatment.

Etymology

From Greek mimeisthai (to imitate) via Latin mimesis (imitation).
