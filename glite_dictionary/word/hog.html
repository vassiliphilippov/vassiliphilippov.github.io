hog

1. NOUN

/ hɑɡ / (AmE), / hɒɡ / (BrE)
a large domesticated pig, especially one kept for meat
large domesticated pig

Examples

The farmer raised several hogs for their meat.
At the county fair, the prize hog weighed over 300 pounds.
They visited a farm where hundreds of hogs were kept in pens.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pig - a domesticated hoofed mammal kept for meat or as a pet

Etymology

From Old English *hogg* (pig), of uncertain origin.

2. NOUN

/ hɑɡ / (AmE), / hɒɡ / (BrE)
A young sheep up to the age of one year, especially one that has not yet been sheared.
young sheep not yet sheared

Examples

The farmer separated the hog from the older sheep for special feeding.
Before its first shearing, a young sheep is called a hog in some regions.
In spring, the hogs are among the last to be brought into the shearing shed.
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02415433-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ hɑɡ / (AmE), / hɒɡ / (BrE)
A person who is extremely greedy, especially one who takes more than their fair share.
greedy or selfish person

Examples

Don't be such a hog—leave some cake for the rest of us.
He acted like a total hog at the buffet, piling his plate high.
She called him a hog for refusing to share the remote.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glutton - a person who overindulges in food or drink
  • Antonym: miser - a person who hoards wealth and spends as little money as possible

Etymology

From Old English *hogg* (swine); figurative sense applied since the 19th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10199280-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ hɑɡ / (AmE), / hɒɡ / (BrE)
to take or use more than one's share of something, often in a greedy way
take more than one's share; act greedily

Examples

Don't hog all the blankets—let someone else have some.
She tends to hog the conversation whenever we're together.
As soon as the pizza arrived, he started hogging the biggest slices for himself.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to monopolize - to obtain exclusive possession or control of something
  • Antonym: share - to use or enjoy something jointly with others

Etymology

Originally from Old English *hogg* (a castrated male pig); the verb sense developed metaphorically from the animal's greedy feeding habits.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
