hardness

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɑrd.nəs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːd.nəs / (BrE)
The quality of water containing dissolved mineral salts, especially calcium and magnesium compounds, which prevent soap from lathering easily.
quality of water with dissolved minerals

Examples

The hardness of tap water varies greatly depending on the source and local geography.
Regularly testing water hardness can help maintain household appliances in good condition.
A water softener is often used to reduce the hardness of well water.

Synset Examples

the costs of reducing hardness depend on the relative amounts of calcium and magnesium compounds that are present

Related Terms

  • Antonym: softness - the quality of water that easily forms lather with soap due to low concentrations of dissolved minerals

5. NOUN

/ ˈhɑrd.nəs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːd.nɪs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being firm or rigid and resistant to pressure, especially the degree to which a material is difficult to scratch or dent, as measured by the Mohs scale.
rigidity; resistance to pressure or scratching

Examples

The hardness of diamond makes it ideal for cutting other materials.
Geologists used the Mohs scale to determine the hardness of the mineral sample.
Water with high hardness can cause scale buildup in pipes and appliances.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: softness - the quality of being easily penetrated, bent, or scratched; opposite of hardness

Etymology

From Old English *heardnes* (firmness, harshness), from *heard* (hard) + *-ness*.
