to weigh

1. VERB

/ weɪ / (AmE), / weɪ / (BrE)
to determine the weight of something using a scale or similar device
determine weight of something

Examples

The grocer quickly weighed the apples before packing them.
Please weigh your luggage at the counter before checking in.
After weighing the package, she calculated the shipping cost.

Synset Examples

The butcher weighed the chicken

Related Terms

  • Synonym: measure - to determine the size, amount, or degree of something

Etymology

From Old English *wegan* (to carry, bear, weigh).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ weɪ / (AmE), / weɪ / (BrE)
to consider carefully, especially by comparing different factors or possibilities before making a decision
consider carefully; take into account

Examples

She carefully weighed all her options before accepting the job offer.
The committee must weigh the potential risks against the benefits before approving the plan.
Judges are expected to weigh all the evidence presented in court.

Synset Examples

You must consider her age
The judge considered the offender's youth and was lenient

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to consider - to think about carefully when making a decision

Etymology

Old English *wegan* (to carry, to balance in scales), from Proto-Germanic *wegan* (to move, carry, weigh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00952463-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ weɪ / (AmE), / weɪ / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel worried, anxious, or burdened; to be oppressive or heavy on the mind or spirit
be oppressive or burdensome

Examples

The guilt seemed to weigh heavily on his conscience every day.
Worries about the future often weigh on people's minds at night.
The responsibilities of leadership can sometimes weigh too much on a person.

Synset Examples

weigh heavily on the mind
Something pressed on his mind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oppress - to keep (someone) in hardship, especially by unjust exercise of authority
  • Synonym: burden - to weigh down with a heavy load or responsibility
  • Antonym: to relieve - to reduce or remove a burden or distress

Etymology

From Old English 'wegan' (to carry, to bear); related to weight and weighing as bearing a burden.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02652148-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ weɪ / (AmE), / weɪ / (BrE)
to have importance or influence; to carry weight in a particular context
have importance or influence

Examples

Her opinion does not weigh as much as his in these matters.
In scholarship applications, extracurricular activities often weigh less than academic achievements.
His words weigh heavily in every meeting because of his experience.

Synset Examples

It does not matter much

Related Terms

  • Synonym: matter - to be important or to have significance
  • Synonym: count - to be significant or have importance

Etymology

From Old English *wegan* (to carry, to bear), related to weight and importance.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02651923-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ weɪ / (AmE), / weɪ / (BrE)
to have a specified weight
have a particular weight

Examples

This suitcase weighs 15 kilograms, so it's too heavy for carry-on luggage.
The baby weighs just over 3 kilograms at birth.
Each apple weighs about 200 grams on average.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: measure - to ascertain the size, amount, or degree of something

Etymology

From Old English *wegan* (to carry, to weigh), related to Dutch *wegen* and German *wiegen*.
