iodine

1. NOUN

/ ˈaɪəˌdin / (AmE), / ˈaɪ.ə.diːn / (BrE)
a nonmetallic chemical element (symbol I), belonging to the halogens, used especially in medicine, photography, and dyes, and found naturally only in small quantities, typically in combination
a nonmetallic chemical element used in medicine and photography

Examples

Doctors often use iodine to disinfect wounds before surgery.
Seaweed is a natural source of iodine, important for thyroid health.
The dark purple vapor of heated iodine is distinctive and easily recognizable.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: bromine - another halogen element, similar in chemical properties to iodine
  • Degree Lower: chlorine - a lighter halogen element, similar to iodine
  • Degree Lower: fluorine - the lightest halogen element

Etymology

From French iode, from Greek ioeidēs (violet-coloured), because of the violet vapour given off when heated.

2. NOUN

/ ˈaɪ.oʊˌdiːn / (AmE), / ˈaɪ.əˌdiːn / (BrE)
A tincture consisting of a solution of iodine in ethyl alcohol, used as a topical antiseptic for wounds.
antiseptic iodine solution for wounds

Examples

Apply iodine to the cut to prevent infection.
The nurse dabbed iodine onto my scraped knee before covering it with a bandage.
She winced as the iodine stung her wound.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antiseptic - a substance that prevents the growth of microorganisms

Etymology

From French 'iode', from Greek 'ioeidēs' (violet-coloured), referring to the element's color.

3. NOUN

/ ˈaɪədiːn / (AmE), / ˈaɪədiːn / (BrE)
a chemical element (symbol I), a dark purple-black nonmetal belonging to the halogen group, used especially in medicine and photography
a nonmetallic chemical element; symbol I

Examples

Doctors sometimes apply iodine to cuts to prevent infection.
Seaweed is a natural source of iodine in the diet.
The dark stains on the cloth were from a drop of iodine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bromine - a chemical element similar to iodine, also a halogen
  • Synonym: fluorine - another halogen element (symbol F)

Etymology

From French iode, from Greek ioeidēs (violet-coloured), referring to the element's violet vapour.
