Creole

1. ADJ

/ ˈkriː.oʊl / (AmE), / ˈkriː.əʊl / (BrE)
relating to a language that has developed from the mixing of two or more languages, typically one that has become stable and is spoken by a community
relating to a mixed-origin language

Examples

Several Caribbean islands have their own Creole languages that blend African and European elements.
She specializes in the study of Creole grammars and their development.
The region is known for its Creole dialects, which evolved from colonial and indigenous languages.

Synset Examples

Creole grammars

Etymology

From French créole, from Spanish criollo, originally meaning 'native to a locality'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈkriːoʊl / (AmE), / ˈkriːəʊl / (BrE)
relating to native-born people of French descent in Louisiana, or to their culture or language
of native-born Louisiana French descent

Examples

The restaurant specializes in Creole cuisine influenced by French and African traditions.
She learned several Creole phrases while visiting New Orleans.
The colorful houses reflect the unique Creole heritage of the city.

Etymology

From French créole, from Spanish criollo (native to a locality), originally from Portuguese crioulo.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkriː.oʊl / (AmE), / ˈkriːəʊl / (BrE)
(in the southern United States, especially Louisiana) a person descended from French ancestors, often with a distinct cultural and linguistic heritage.
descendant of French ancestors in Louisiana

Examples

Several generations of Creole families have lived in New Orleans since the eighteenth century.
The festival celebrates Creole traditions through music, food, and dance.
As a Creole, she is proud of her French and Spanish ancestry.

Etymology

From French créole, from Spanish criollo (native to a locality), originally from Portuguese crioulo.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈkriː.oʊl / (AmE), / ˈkriː.əʊl / (BrE)
a person of European descent, especially Spanish, French, or Portuguese, born in the West Indies or Latin America, rather than in Europe
person of European descent born in the Americas

Examples

The wealthy Creole families were influential in the development of the colony.
She identified herself as a proud Creole with deep roots in Latin America.
Many Creoles sought independence from European colonial powers in the early 19th century.

Etymology

From French créole, Spanish criollo; originally from Portuguese crioulo, meaning 'person native to a locality.'

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
