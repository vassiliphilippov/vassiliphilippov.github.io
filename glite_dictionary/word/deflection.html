deflection

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈflɛkʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkʃən / (BrE)
the movement of the pointer or pen of a measuring instrument away from its zero position, indicating a measurement.
indicator movement on measuring instrument

Examples

The voltmeter showed a slight deflection when the circuit was completed.
A large deflection on the galvanometer indicated a strong current.
Even a tiny current can produce a measurable deflection in sensitive equipment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: displacement - the amount of movement from a reference position

Etymology

From Latin 'deflexio' (a bending aside), from 'deflectere' (to bend away).

/ dɪˈflɛk.ʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛk.ʃən / (BrE)
The action or process of being bent or turned aside from a straight course or position.
the act or state of being bent or turned aside

Examples

Engineers measured the deflection of the bridge under heavy loads.
A sudden deflection of the ball changed the outcome of the game.
The material's deflection shows how much it can bend before breaking.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deviation - the act of departing from an established course or norm
  • Antonym: straightness - the quality or state of being straight, without bending

4. NOUN

/ dɪˈflɛkʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkʃən / (BrE)
the amount by which a propagating wave is bent from its original path as it travels through a medium or is affected by an object
amount a wave is bent

Examples

The deflection of light as it passes through a prism creates a spectrum of colors.
Radio waves can experience significant deflection when they encounter metal structures.
Scientists measured the deflection of the X-rays caused by the earth's atmosphere.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reflection - the return of a wave from a surface
  • Synonym: refraction - the change in direction of a wave passing from one medium to another

Etymology

From Latin deflexio (a bending aside), from de- ‘away from’ + flectere ‘to bend’.
