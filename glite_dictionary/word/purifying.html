purifying

1. ADJ

/ ˈpjʊrəˌfaɪɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpjʊərɪfaɪɪŋ / (BrE)
Having the effect of cleansing someone of sin, guilt, or moral impurity, especially through rituals or acts.
cleansing of sin or guilt

Examples

The monks performed a purifying ceremony to cleanse the temple of evil spirits.
Some traditions believe that fasting can have a purifying effect on the soul.
The purifying fire is said to rid the spirits of their past misdeeds.

Synset Examples

purgatorial rites

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cleansing - making something clean or pure
  • Antonym: polluting - making impure or dirty

Etymology

Present participle of 'purify', from Old French purifer, from Latin purificare ('to make pure').

2. ADJ

/ ˈpjʊrəˌfaɪɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpjʊərɪˌfaɪɪŋ / (BrE)
removing harmful or unwanted substances to make clean or pure
removing impurities; making pure

Examples

Drinking water passes through a purifying filter before reaching the tap.
The purifying process removes toxins from the air.
She found the mountain air to be fresh and purifying.

Synset Examples

filtration is a purifying agent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cleansing - removing dirt or impurities
  • Antonym: contaminating - making something impure or dirty

Etymology

From the verb 'purify', originally from Old French 'purifier', from Latin 'purificare' (to make pure).

3. ADJ

/ ˈpjʊr.əˌfaɪ.ɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpjʊər.ɪ.faɪ.ɪŋ / (BrE)
Having the quality of cleansing, particularly by killing germs or removing impurities, similar to an antiseptic.
cleansing; acting like an antiseptic

Examples

The doctor used a purifying solution to clean the wound.
This soap contains purifying agents that help protect against bacteria.
Many traditional medicines include purifying herbs to prevent infections.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antiseptic - preventing the growth of disease-causing microorganisms

Etymology

Present participle of "purify," from Old French *purifier*, from Latin *purificare* (to make pure).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02123584-s
Synset POS: s
