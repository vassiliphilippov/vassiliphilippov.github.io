descant

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛsˌkænt / (AmE), / ˈdɛs.kænt / (BrE)
a decorative musical accompaniment, often improvised, added above a basic melody.
musical accompaniment above main melody

Examples

The choir sang a beautiful descant over the main hymn tune.
She improvised a descant to complement the melody on her flute.
The descant added brightness and complexity to the final chorus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterpoint - the relationship between two or more musical lines which are harmonically interdependent

Etymology

From Old French 'descant', from Medieval Latin 'discantus' (counterpoint).

2. VERB

/ ˈdɛskænt / (AmE), / ˈdɛskənt / (BrE)
to sing or perform a descant, typically an ornamental melody sung above the main tune
sing or perform a descant

Examples

The choir asked Ella to descant during the festival's final hymn.
Several sopranos descanted beautifully while the rest of the choir sang the melody.
She enjoys descanting on traditional choral pieces during services.

Etymology

From Old French 'descant', from Medieval Latin 'discantus' (a part-song).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01714695-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdɛs.kænt / (AmE), / ˈdɛs.kænt / (BrE)
to talk or write at length about a particular subject, especially in a detailed or elaborate way
talk at length about a subject

Examples

He descanted on the history of the architecture for over an hour.
She often descants on her favorite novels during book club meetings.
The professor descanted upon the importance of ethical conduct in research.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to expatiate - to speak or write in detail about a subject
  • Synonym: ramble - to talk or write at length in a confused or inconsequential way

Etymology

From Latin 'discantus', past participle of 'discantare' (to sing apart) via Old French and Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01036519-v
Synset POS: v
