martingale

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɑrtɪŋˌɡeɪl / (AmE), / ˈmɑːtɪŋɡeɪl / (BrE)
A spar fixed under the bowsprit of a sailboat to counteract upward pull and maintain rigging tension.
spar beneath the bowsprit of a sailboat

Examples

The crew replaced the damaged martingale before heading out to sea.
A strong martingale is essential for maintaining the integrity of the bowsprit in rough waters.
Inspecting the martingale regularly can prevent rigging failures on a sailboat.

Etymology

From French *martingale*, originally referring to a strap or device for controlling movement.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɑrtɪnˌɡeɪl / (AmE), / ˈmɑːtɪnɡeɪl / (BrE)
a strap on a horse's harness that runs from the noseband to the girth, used to keep the horse from raising its head too high
strap on a horse's harness to control head position

Examples

The trainer attached a martingale to the horse's bridle before the competition.
Without a martingale, the horse kept tossing its head uncomfortably.
She checked that the martingale was properly fitted before riding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: breastplate - a strap in a horse's gear that helps prevent the saddle from slipping backward

Etymology

From Old French *martingale*, of uncertain origin.
