Luke

1. PROPN

/ luːk / (AmE), / luːk / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) the apostle closely associated with St. Paul, traditionally regarded as the author of the third Gospel.
New Testament apostle; author of the third Gospel

Examples

Many scholars believe that Luke was a physician before he became an evangelist.
The Gospel according to Luke is the third book of the New Testament.
Tradition holds that Luke accompanied St. Paul on several missionary journeys.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: St. Paul - apostle who spread Christianity and wrote many New Testament letters

Etymology

From Latin Lucas, from Greek Loukas, a shortened form of Loukanos, meaning 'from Lucania' (a region in southern Italy).

2. PROPN

/ luːk / (AmE), / luːk / (BrE)
the third of the four Gospels in the New Testament, describing the birth, life, and teachings of Jesus Christ, traditionally attributed to St Luke
New Testament Gospel recounting Jesus's life

Examples

Many parables unique to Luke appear in no other Gospel.
Scholars often compare the account of the nativity in Luke with that in Matthew.
The Gospel of Luke emphasizes compassion for the poor and marginalized.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Matthew - the first Gospel in the New Testament
  • Synonym: Mark - the second Gospel in the New Testament
  • Synonym: John - the fourth Gospel in the New Testament

Etymology

From Latin 'Lucas', from Ancient Greek 'Loukas', a proper name.
