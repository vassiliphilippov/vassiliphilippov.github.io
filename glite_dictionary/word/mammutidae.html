Mammutidae

1. NOUN

/ mæˈmjuː.təˌdiː / (AmE), / mæˈmjuː.tɪˌdiː / (BrE)
An extinct family of large, proboscidean mammals commonly known as mastodons, which lived from the Late Miocene to the end of the Pleistocene.
extinct family of mastodons

Examples

The Mammutidae roamed North America and Eurasia before becoming extinct at the end of the Ice Age.
Fossils of Mammutidae have helped scientists understand more about prehistoric proboscideans.
Unlike mammoths, the teeth of Mammutidae were adapted for browsing rather than grazing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mastodon - an extinct large, tusked mammal of the Mammutidae family

Etymology

From scientific Latin, based on genus Mammut + suffix -idae indicating a family (zoology).

2. NOUN

/ məˌmjutɪˈdiː / (AmE), / məˌmjuːtɪˈdiː / (BrE)
A family of extinct large mammals commonly known as mastodons, which lived from the Miocene through the Pleistocene epochs.
family of extinct mastodons

Examples

The Mammutidae roamed North America millions of years ago before becoming extinct.
Fossils from the Mammutidae family help scientists study prehistoric environments.
Unlike mammoths, Mammutidae had distinctive teeth adapted for browsing shrubs and trees.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mastodon - an extinct, elephant-like mammal of the family Mammutidae
  • Degree Higher: Elephantidae - the family that includes modern elephants and mammoths

Etymology

From genus name Mammut (Latinized from German), with suffix -idae denoting a zoological family.
