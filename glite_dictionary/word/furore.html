furore

1. NOUN

/ fjʊˈrɔri / (AmE), / fjʊəˈrɔːri / (BrE)
a sudden and widespread outbreak of public excitement, anger, or protest
sudden outbreak of public protest or excitement

Examples

The politician's remarks caused a furore among the audience, leading to a heated debate.
There was a furore in the press after the controversial decision was announced.
The celebrity's sudden departure created a furore on social media.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uproar - a loud and impassioned noise or disturbance, usually caused by a crowd
  • Synonym: commotion - a state of confused and noisy disturbance
  • Antonym: calm - a state of peace and quiet, free from disturbance

Etymology

From Italian 'furore' (rage), from Latin 'furor' (madness, rage).

2. NOUN

/ fjʊˈrɔːr / (AmE), / fjʊəˈrɔːri / (BrE)
A sudden and intense interest, excitement, or enthusiasm for something, often followed with exaggerated zeal by many people.
intense popular excitement or enthusiasm

Examples

The new smartphone model caused a furore among tech enthusiasts when it was released.
Her bold artwork created a furore in the contemporary art world.
There was a furore over the chef’s unusual new recipe, with foodies rushing to try it.

Synset Examples

he always follows the latest fads
it was all the rage that season

Related Terms

  • Synonym: craze - something that becomes very popular for a short time
  • Synonym: fad - an intense and short-lived fashion or enthusiasm
  • Antonym: apathy - lack of interest, enthusiasm, or concern

Etymology

From Italian 'furore' (fury, rage), from Latin 'furor' (rage, frenzy).
