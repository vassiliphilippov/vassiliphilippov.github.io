loan

1. NOUN

/ loʊn / (AmE), / ləʊn / (BrE)
an amount of money that is borrowed, often from a bank, and is expected to be paid back with interest
money borrowed, to be repaid with interest

Examples

She took out a loan to help pay for her college tuition.
The bank approved his loan application after reviewing his credit history.
Without a loan, the company would have struggled to finance its new project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: credit - an arrangement to receive goods or services now and pay later, often with interest
  • Synonym: debt - money that is owed to someone else

Etymology

From Old Norse *lán* (loan, gift) via Middle English.

Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ loʊn / (AmE), / ləʊn / (BrE)
to give something, especially money, to someone for a limited period of time, expecting it to be returned
give temporarily with expectation of return

Examples

She agreed to loan him her textbook for the week.
Could you loan me twenty dollars until tomorrow?
The museum loaned several paintings to a gallery for a special exhibition.

Synset Examples

I will lend you my car
loan me some money

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to lend - to give something temporarily to someone, expecting it to be returned

Etymology

From Old Norse lán (loan, gift), related to Old English lǣn.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02329178-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ loʊn / (AmE), / ləʊn / (BrE)
a word adopted from another language and used as part of the vocabulary of the borrowing language
a word borrowed from another language

Examples

The English word piano is actually a loan from Italian.
Many culinary terms in English, such as 'soufflé', are French loans.
Linguists study how loans change their form and pronunciation in new languages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: borrowing - the process of taking words from other languages

Etymology

From Old Norse *lán* (loan), related to Old English *lǣn*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06304241-n
Synset POS: n
