rebuke

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈbjuk / (AmE), / rɪˈbjuːk / (BrE)
an expression of sharp criticism or disapproval
expression of criticism or disapproval

Examples

She received a stern rebuke from her manager for being late to the meeting.
His careless comments earned him a sharp rebuke from the judge.
Instead of getting angry, he accepted the rebuke gracefully and promised to improve.

Synset Examples

he had to take the rebuke with a smile on his face

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reprimand - a formal expression of disapproval
  • Antonym: praise - expression of approval or admiration

Etymology

From Old French *rebuke*, from *rebouquier* (to beat back), possibly from Latin *re-* (back) + *bucare* (to strike).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈbjuk / (AmE), / rɪˈbjuːk / (BrE)
to express sharp or severe disapproval of someone, especially officially or because of their behavior
express sharp disapproval

Examples

The manager did not hesitate to rebuke the employee for arriving late.
She rebuked her friend for making insensitive comments during the meeting.
The teacher rebuked the class for their lack of attention.

Synset Examples

The mother scolded the child for entering a stranger's car
The deputy ragged the Prime Minister
The customer dressed down the waiter for bringing cold soup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scold - to angrily criticize or reprimand someone, especially a child
  • Synonym: reprimand - to express official disapproval or criticism
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From Old French *rebuchier* (to cut back, beat back), from re- (back) + buschier (to strike, drive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00826456-v
Synset POS: v
