sluice

1. VERB

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
to let water or another substance flow through or out by means of a sluice
let water flow through a sluice

Examples

They sluiced the excess water from the canal after the heavy rain.
The mill was sluiced each morning to ensure proper flow.
Engineers decided to sluice the sediment out of the reservoir to increase capacity.

Synset Examples

sluice water

Etymology

From Old French *escluse* (sluice), from Latin *exclusa* (barrier, canal lock).

2. NOUN

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
A channel or passage for directing a flow of water, typically controlled by a gate or shutter.
water channel with a gate

Examples

The mill was powered by water flowing through the sluice behind the building.
Engineers opened the sluice to release excess water after the heavy rains.
Algae collected at the mouth of the sluice and had to be cleared every week.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canal - a man-made waterway for navigation or irrigation
  • Synonym: dam - a barrier constructed to hold back water

Etymology

From Old French *escluse*, from Latin *excludere* (to shut out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04251507-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
(of liquid) to pour or flow out forcefully in a stream, as if from a sluice.
to flow or pour forcefully

Examples

After the heavy rain, water sluiced down the mountain paths, flooding the valley below.
She sluiced the mud from her boots with a powerful jet of water.
During the storm, runoff sluiced over the garden, carrying leaves and soil away.

Synset Examples

An aggressive tide sluiced across the barrier reef

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pour - (of liquid) to flow rapidly in a steady stream
  • Degree Lower: dribble - (of liquid) to fall slowly in drops or a thin stream

Etymology

From Old French 'escluse' (sluice, floodgate), from Latin 'excludere' (to shut out).
