kilobyte

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɪləˌbaɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɪləˌbaɪt / (BrE)
a unit of information equal to 1024 bytes, commonly used to measure digital data
unit of digital information (1024 bytes)

Examples

The document was only one kilobyte in size, making it easy to email.
An image file can range from a single kilobyte to several megabytes depending on its resolution.
Early computer disks could store just a few hundred kilobytes of data.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: megabyte - unit of information equal to 1024 kilobytes or 1,048,576 bytes
  • Degree Lower: byte - unit of digital information consisting of 8 bits

Etymology

From 'kilo-' (thousand) + 'byte', formed by analogy to other metric units.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɪləˌbaɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɪləˌbaɪt / (BrE)
a unit of digital information equal to 1,000 bytes, often used to measure the size of digital files or storage capacity
unit of digital information (1,000 bytes)

Examples

The image is only 150 kilobytes in size, so it loads quickly.
A kilobyte is often used to measure small text files or simple graphics.
Most emails are just a few kilobytes when they contain only text.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: megabyte - a unit of information equal to 1,000,000 bytes
  • Degree Lower: byte - a unit of digital information equal to 8 bits
  • Degree Higher: gigabyte - a unit of information equal to 1,000,000,000 bytes

Etymology

From 'kilo-' (Greek 'khilioi' meaning 'thousand') + 'byte' (computing term for a unit of digital information).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
