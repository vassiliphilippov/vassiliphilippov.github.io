shepherd

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛpərd / (AmE), / ˈʃɛpəd / (BrE)
a clergyman who cares for and guides a congregation or group of people, especially in a spiritual context
clergyman guiding a congregation

Examples

The shepherd delivered a heartfelt sermon that inspired the entire community.
As a shepherd of his flock, Reverend Martin visited each family in times of need.
Many considered her the shepherd of the small rural parish.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pastor - a clergyman in charge of a Christian congregation

Etymology

From Old English sceaphierde, from sceap (sheep) + hierde (herder, guardian).

2. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛpərd / (AmE), / ˈʃɛpəd / (BrE)
a person whose job is to take care of and guide a flock of sheep, especially on open land
person who herds sheep

Examples

The shepherd led the flock across the field at dawn.
As a shepherd, he spent most of his days outdoors in the hills.
The loyal dog helped the shepherd keep the sheep together.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: herdsman - a person who looks after a herd of livestock
  • Synonym: sheepdog - a dog trained to help with gathering and moving sheep

Etymology

From Old English sceaphierde, from sceap (sheep) + hierde (herder).

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈʃɛpərd / (AmE), / ˈʃɛpəd / (BrE)
to guide or watch over someone carefully, as a shepherd does with sheep; to lead or look after a group, especially in a protective or mentoring way
guide or watch over carefully

Examples

The teacher shepherded her students through the complex project with patience and care.
He was chosen to shepherd the new recruits during their training period.
Throughout the trip, the guide shepherded the visitors safely through the crowded city.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guide - to show or lead the way for someone
  • Synonym: to oversee - to supervise or watch over people or work

Etymology

From Old English sceaphyrde, from sceap (sheep) + hyrde (herder, guardian).

guide or manage (in a mentoring way)

Examples
