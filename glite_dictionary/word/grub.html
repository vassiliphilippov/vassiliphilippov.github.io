grub

1. NOUN

/ ɡrʌb / (AmE), / ɡrʌb / (BrE)
informal term for food or a meal.
informal word for food
informal

Examples

Let's get some grub before we head to the movie.
He always brings tasty grub to the picnic.
After a long day, all I want is some good grub to eat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: food - any nutritious substance that people eat or drink

Etymology

Originally British slang, perhaps from a dialectal word meaning 'to dig', suggesting the act of eating or foraging.

2. VERB

/ ɡrʌb / (AmE), / ɡrʌb / (BrE)
to ask for and get something, especially food or money, without paying for it; to act as a parasite
ask for and get for free; be a parasite
informal

Examples

He always tries to grub a meal from his friends instead of buying his own.
Don't let him grub off you—he needs to learn responsibility.
She was known to grub money from relatives whenever she needed it.

3. NOUN

/ ɡrʌb / (AmE), / ɡrʌb / (BrE)
the soft, thick, wormlike larva of certain beetles and some other insects, especially as found living underground or inside decaying wood
larva of beetles or other insects

Examples

We found a large grub under the log in the garden.
The wood was infested with grubs eating away at the inside.
Birds often feed on grub larvae hidden in the soil.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: larva - an immature form of an insect that does not look like the adult form
  • Antonym: adult - the mature form of an insect after metamorphosis

Etymology

Probably from Middle English grubben (to dig), related to the larva's habit of burrowing.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02314658-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ɡrʌb / (AmE), / ɡrʌb / (BrE)
to search or dig about busily, especially in the ground or among other things, often using the hands or tools
search or dig busily

Examples

The children began to grub in the dirt looking for worms.
She grubbed through old boxes in the attic searching for her grandmother's photos.
Farmers grub in the field early each morning to remove weeds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dig - break up and move earth, or search for something by digging

Etymology

From Old English *grybban* (to dig, grub up).
