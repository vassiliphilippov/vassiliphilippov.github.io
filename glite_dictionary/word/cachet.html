cachet

1. NOUN

/ kæˈʃeɪ / (AmE), / kaˈʃeɪ / (BrE)
a warrant formerly issued by a French king, authorizing imprisonment or death, typically in the form of a signed letter under royal seal
royal French warrant for arrest or death

Examples

The infamous cachet condemned many individuals to imprisonment with no trial.
After receiving the cachet, the officer arrested the suspect on the king’s orders.
Writers of the period often referenced the terror caused by a royal cachet.

Etymology

From French *cachet* (seal), referring to the royal seal on such documents.

2. NOUN

/ kæˈʃeɪ / (AmE), / ˈkæʃeɪ / (BrE)
a seal or impression placed on a letter or document as a mark of authentication or distinction
official seal on a letter or document

Examples

The royal cachet on the envelope indicated its importance.
Collectors value old envelopes that still bear the original cachet.
The document was given a special cachet by the official seal.

Etymology

From French cachet (seal), from Old French, diminutive of cacher (to press, to hide).

3. NOUN

/ kæˈʃeɪ / (AmE), / kæˈʃeɪ / (BrE)
a quality that marks someone or something as prestigious or having approved or superior status
prestige; mark of high status

Examples

Owning a home in that neighborhood carries a certain cachet among the city's elite.
The designer's name adds extra cachet to the clothing line.
Winning the award gave him considerable cachet within his profession.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prestige - widespread respect and admiration; high status
  • Synonym: distinction - excellence or unique quality that sets someone apart
  • Antonym: obscurity - lack of fame, importance, or recognition

Etymology

From French 'cachet' (seal, mark), originally meaning a seal used for stamping.
