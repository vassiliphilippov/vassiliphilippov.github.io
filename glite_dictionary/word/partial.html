partial

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɑrʃəl / (AmE), / ˈpɑːʃəl / (BrE)
Existing or happening only in part; not complete or total.
not complete; only a part

Examples

The report only included a partial list of the available resources.
After the storm, the museum suffered partial damage to its roof.
The scientist observed a partial eclipse through specialized equipment.

Synset Examples

a partial description of the suspect
partial collapse
a partial eclipse
a partial monopoly
partial immunity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: complete - having all parts; entire; whole

Etymology

From Medieval Latin partialis (pertaining to a part), from Latin pars (part).

/ ˈpɑrʃəl / (AmE), / ˈpɑːʃəl / (BrE)

Examples

having a strong liking for something or someone; especially used as 'partial to'
having a strong liking for

Examples

Synset Examples

6. NOUN

/ ˈpɑrʃəl / (AmE), / ˈpɑːʃəl / (BrE)
(in acoustics and music) a component of a musical sound whose frequency is a multiple of the fundamental frequency; a harmonic or overtone.
harmonic component of a sound

Examples

In music, each note played on a violin contains several partials that give the instrument its unique sound.
The scientist studied how the different partials combine to create complex tones.
A pure tone has only the fundamental frequency without any other partial present.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harmonic - a wave or tone whose frequency is an integer multiple of the fundamental
  • Synonym: overtone - a tone whose frequency is higher than the fundamental frequency
  • Antonym: fundamental - the lowest frequency of a periodic waveform

Etymology

From Latin 'partialis' (divisible, partial), from 'pars' (part).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05728040-n
Synset POS: n
