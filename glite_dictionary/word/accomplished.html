accomplished

1. ADJ

/ əˈkɑmplɪʃt / (AmE), / əˈkɒmplɪʃt / (BrE)
(of a fact or event) completed, established, and not able to be changed.
completed and unchangeable (of a fact or event)

Examples

The agreement is now an accomplished fact and cannot be revoked.
Once the results were published, electoral victory became an accomplished fact.
Her promotion is now an accomplished reality, despite earlier controversy.

Synset Examples

that smoking causes health problems is an accomplished fact

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: certain - sure, definite, without doubt

Etymology

Past participle of 'accomplish', from Old French accomplir, from Latin ad- + complēre (to fill up, complete).

2. ADJ

/ əˈkɑmplɪʃt / (AmE), / əˈkɒmplɪʃt / (BrE)
highly skilled or proficient in a particular activity or field
highly skilled; proficient

Examples

She is an accomplished violinist who has performed in concerts around the world.
His accomplished storytelling made the evening truly memorable.
The chef is highly accomplished in both traditional and modern cuisines.

Synset Examples

an accomplished pianist
a complete musician

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skilled - having skill; proficient
  • Antonym: incompetent - lacking skill or ability

Etymology

From Old French 'acompli', past participle of 'accomplir', from Latin 'ad-' (to) + 'complere' (to fill up, complete).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ əˈkɑmplɪʃt / (AmE), / əˈkʌmplɪʃt / (BrE)
successfully completed or achieved; brought to a conclusion
successfully completed or achieved

Examples

With the renovations accomplished, the family finally moved into their new home.
The team's accomplished work was praised by the board of directors.
She felt a sense of relief when the difficult task was accomplished.

Synset Examples

his mission accomplished, he took a vacation
the completed project
the joy of a realized ambition overcame him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: completed - finished, brought to an end
  • Antonym: unfinished - not completed or brought to an end

Etymology

Formed from 'accomplish' (Middle English, from Old French 'acomplir', from Latin 'ad-' + 'complēre' meaning to fill up, complete) plus '-ed'.
