choir

1. NOUN

/ kwaɪɚ / (AmE), / kwaɪə / (BrE)
A group or family of similar musical instruments playing together, especially within an orchestra or ensemble.
group of similar instruments played together

Examples

In the symphony's second movement, the woodwind choir creates a warm, mellow sound.
The brass choir played a powerful fanfare at the concert's start.
A typical orchestra consists of strings, woodwinds, brass, and percussion choirs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: section - a group of instruments within an orchestra, such as strings or brass

Etymology

From Old French *quer*, from Latin *chorus* (company of singers or dancers).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkwaɪər / (AmE), / ˈkwaɪə / (BrE)
A group of singers, especially one that performs as part of a religious ceremony or service.
group of singers in a religious ceremony

Examples

The church choir sang a beautiful hymn during the Sunday service.
She joined the school choir to improve her singing and make new friends.
The choir rehearses every Wednesday evening in the chapel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chorus - a group of singers or performers who sing or speak together

Etymology

From Old French 'quer', from Latin 'chorus', from Ancient Greek 'khoros' (dance, band of dancers and singers).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08205568-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ kwaɪr / (AmE), / kwaɪə / (BrE)
the part of a church, typically between the nave and the sanctuary, where the singers sit during services
area in a church for singers

Examples

The church's choir was beautifully decorated with carved wooden stalls.
During the ceremony, the singers gathered in the choir to perform an anthem.
Light from the stained glass windows filled the choir with a soft, colorful glow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chancel - the space around the altar at the east end of a church

Etymology

From Old French *quer*, from Latin *chorus* (chorus, a dance in a circle, the chorus in a play).
