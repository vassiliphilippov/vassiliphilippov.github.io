foster

1. VERB

/ ˈfɑːstər / (AmE), / ˈfɒstə / (BrE)
to bring up or care for a child who is not one's own by birth, especially by formal arrangement
bring up a child not one's own

Examples

After her parents died, her aunt decided to foster her until she finished school.
Many families in the community choose to foster children who need a safe home.
They have fostered three siblings for over a year now.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adopt - to legally take another's child and bring it up as one's own

Etymology

Old English fostrian (to supply with food, nourish), from fostor (food, nourishment).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02546266-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ ˈfɑstər / (AmE), / ˈfɒstə / (BrE)
(of a relationship or care) involving the raising or nurturing of a child by someone who is not a biological or legal parent.
nurturing without blood or legal ties

Examples

After living with a foster family for two years, he was reunited with his mother.
She has taken in several foster children over the past decade.
The organization helps place children in loving foster homes across the region.

Synset Examples

foster parent
foster child
foster home
surrogate father

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adoptive - relating to a parent or child established by legal adoption

Etymology

From Old English *fostrian* (to nourish, support), related to *fostor* (food, nourishment, upbringing).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01409390-s
Synset POS: s
