steerage

1. NOUN

/ ˈstɪrɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈstɪərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the part of a passenger ship providing the cheapest accommodations, originally located near the steering apparatus
cheapest accommodations on a ship

Examples

Many immigrants to America traveled in steerage to save money.
The poor conditions in steerage were a stark contrast to the luxury above deck.
She described her journey across the ocean in steerage as uncomfortable but affordable.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: cabin - private room or compartment on a ship

Etymology

From steer, referring to the steering gear area of a ship, where such accommodations were located.

2. NOUN

/ ˈstɪrɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈstɪərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the act or practice of steering a ship or other vessel
the act of steering a ship

Examples

The crew's skill in steerage was tested during the violent storm.
Successful steerage of the sailing ship depended on quick decisions by the helmsman.
Accurate steerage is essential when maneuvering through narrow channels.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: navigation - the process of planning and controlling the course of a vessel

Etymology

From Middle English *stere* (to steer) + *-age* (denoting action or process).
