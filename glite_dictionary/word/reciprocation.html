reciprocation

1. NOUN

/ rɪˌsɪprəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌsɪprɒˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
alternating back-and-forth movement, typically in a straight line, as occurs in certain mechanical systems
alternating back-and-forth movement

Examples

The engine operates through the reciprocation of its pistons within the cylinders.
The reciprocation of the saw blade makes it suitable for cutting through tough materials.
The reciprocation in the mechanical arm must be perfectly timed to maintain efficiency.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oscillation - movement back and forth in a regular rhythm

Etymology

From Latin reciprocatio (a returning the same way), from reciprocus (moving backward and forward).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˌsɪprəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌsɪprəˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of doing something in return for what someone else has done; a mutual exchange of actions or favors
the act of giving in return

Examples

Her gesture of kindness called for reciprocation from everyone in the group.
Without reciprocation, their relationship felt one-sided and unfair.
The success of the agreement relied on the reciprocation of trust between the parties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reciprocity - mutual exchange; the practice of exchanging things with others for mutual benefit
  • Antonym: retaliation - the act of returning an attack; revenge

Etymology

From Latin reciprocationem, from reciprocus (returning the same way).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˌsɪprəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌsɪprəˈkeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the act of exchanging things with others for mutual benefit, especially actions or information; a mutual exchange or interaction
mutual exchange or interaction

Examples

Effective communication depends on the reciprocation of ideas between all team members.
Generosity often leads to reciprocation in human relationships, creating a cycle of kindness.
In science, the reciprocation of knowledge among researchers accelerates discovery.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exchange - the act of giving one thing and receiving another
  • Synonym: reciprocity - mutual exchange, especially in social or legal interactions

Etymology

From Latin reciprocatio, from reciprocus (moving back and forth).
