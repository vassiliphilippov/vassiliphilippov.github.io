sire

1. NOUN

/ saɪr / (AmE), / saɪə / (BrE)
a formal title of address for a man of rank and authority, especially a king; now archaic
formal address for a man of rank (archaic)
archaic

Examples

“Sire, your loyal subjects await your command,” the advisor said to the king.
In medieval times, people would address their monarch as sire.
“Yes, sire, I shall do as you wish,” the knight replied respectfully.

Etymology

From Old French *sire*, from Latin *senior* (older, elder).

2. NOUN

/ saɪɚ / (AmE), / saɪə / (BrE)
the male parent of an animal, especially a domesticated animal such as a horse
male parent of an animal

Examples

The champion stallion was a famous sire whose offspring won many races.
The pedigree documents list both the sire and the dam of each foal.
Farmers carefully select which sire to use for breeding their livestock.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dam - the female parent of an animal
  • Synonym: stud - a male animal, especially a horse, kept for breeding

Etymology

From Old French sire (lord, father), from Latin senior (older, elder).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01325996-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ saɪɚ / (AmE), / saɪə / (BrE)
(of a male animal or man) to be the father of (an offspring); to father or cause to be born.
to father offspring

Examples

The prize-winning stallion sired over fifty foals during his lifetime.
He hopes his champion bull will sire next year's calves.
The male lion sired several cubs with different females in the pride.

Synset Examples

Abraham begot Isaac
John fathered four daughters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: father - to be the biological male parent of a child or offspring
  • Synonym: to beget - to father or produce (offspring); to cause to be born
  • Antonym: mother - to give birth to or be the female parent of offspring

Etymology

From Old French *sire* (lord or father), from Latin *senior* (elder).
