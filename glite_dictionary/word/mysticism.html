mysticism

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪstɪˌsɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmɪstɪˌsɪzəm / (BrE)
a belief system or religious practice centered on direct, personal communion with an ultimate reality or the divine, often through contemplation or spiritual experiences
belief in direct spiritual communion

Examples

She devoted her life to studying mysticism and the pursuit of spiritual enlightenment.
The poetry of Rumi often explores themes of mysticism and union with the divine.
Some critics argue that mysticism offers answers that logic and science cannot provide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spiritualism - belief in communication with spiritual entities
  • Antonym: rationalism - belief in reason as the chief source of knowledge

Etymology

From Greek 'mystikos' (secret, mystic) via French 'mysticisme'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɪstɪˌsɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmɪstɪsɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
Obscure or irrational thinking, especially beliefs or ideas that are difficult to explain in rational terms.
obscure or irrational thought

Examples

His lecture was criticized for descending into mysticism rather than offering clear arguments.
The philosopher rejected all forms of mysticism in favor of logical analysis.
Some scientific theories are sometimes dismissed as mysticism when lacking empirical evidence.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rationalism - The doctrine that reason rather than experience is the foundation of certainty in knowledge

Etymology

From Greek 'mystikos' (secret, mystical) via Medieval Latin 'mysticus'.
