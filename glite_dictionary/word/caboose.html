caboose

1. NOUN

/ kəˈbus / (AmE), / kəˈbuːs / (BrE)
a small kitchen or area for preparing food on a ship, especially formerly on sailing vessels
ship's kitchen or galley (historical)

Examples

The cook worked tirelessly in the caboose to prepare meals for the crew.
After a long day at sea, the sailors gathered near the caboose for dinner.
During storms, it was difficult to keep a fire going in the tiny caboose.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: galley - the kitchen on a ship or aircraft

Etymology

From Dutch 'kabuis' (ship's galley), influenced by Middle Low German.

2. NOUN

/ kəˈbus / (AmE), / kəˈbuːs / (BrE)
a railroad car attached usually to the end of a freight train, used as living quarters or office for the train crew
railroad car for crew, at end of freight train

Examples

The conductor sat at his desk in the caboose as the train sped through the night.
After a long day, the crew gathered in the caboose to eat dinner and rest.
The old red caboose at the end of the freight train is now a museum display.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boxcar - a railroad freight car with enclosed sides and roof
  • Antonym: locomotive - the engine of a train

Etymology

From Dutch 'kabuis' meaning 'ship's galley'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
