escheat

1. NOUN

/ ɪsˈtʃit / (AmE), / ɪsˈtʃiːt / (BrE)
the reversion of property to the state when the owner dies without legal heirs or a will
property reverting to the state
legal

Examples

If someone dies without heirs, their land may escheat to the state.
Historically, escheat ensured that property was never left ownerless.
Modern laws limit how often escheat occurs by requiring thorough searches for heirs.

Etymology

From Old French 'eschete', from medieval Latin 'excadere' (to fall out), referring to property that 'falls out' of individual ownership.

2. NOUN

/ ɪsˈtʃit / (AmE), / ɪsˈtʃiːt / (BrE)
The reversion of property to the state when the owner dies without legal heirs or a will.
reversion of property to the state
legal

Examples

The abandoned property was subject to escheat after the owner died without heirs.
Many states have specific laws about escheat of unclaimed bank accounts.
Without a valid will, a person's assets may become subject to escheat.

Etymology

From Old French eschete, from Late Latin *excadere* (to fall out), Latin *ex-* (out) + *cadere* (to fall).
