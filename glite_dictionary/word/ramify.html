to ramify

1. VERB

/ ˈræməˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈramɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
(of a plant or structure) to spread out and develop branches or branch-like parts.
form or spread branches

Examples

The roots of the old oak tree ramify far beneath the surrounding fields.
In spring, the bush begins to ramify and quickly covers the fence.
Complex river systems often ramify across vast regions, forming intricate networks.

Synset Examples

these plants ramify early and get to be very large

Related Terms

  • Synonym: branch - to produce branches; to divide into branches

Etymology

From Latin ramificare, from ramus (branch) + facere (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00330054-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈræməˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈræmɪfaɪ / (BrE)
(of an action, event, or situation) to develop or spread out into more complicated or numerous consequences or results.
develop into more complex consequences

Examples

Delaying the decision may ramify the problems faced by the committee.
The consequences of their disagreement began to ramify throughout the organization.
New regulations can ramify, creating a web of unexpected complications.

Synset Examples

These actions will ramify

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to complicate - to make or become more complicated

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *ramificare* (to form branches), from Latin *ramus* (branch) + *-ficare* (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00402279-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈræməˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈramɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to divide or spread out into branches or branchlike parts
divide into branches

Examples

In spring, the trees ramify rapidly, creating a dense canopy.
The company's network of partnerships continued to ramify across industries.
These arteries ramify throughout the body to supply blood to various organs.

Synset Examples

The road forks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: branch - to divide into subdivisions or offshoots

Etymology

From Latin ramificare (to form branches), from ramus (branch) + facere (to make).
