outrage

1. VERB

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel intense anger, disgust, or revulsion
cause intense anger or disgust

Examples

His insensitive remarks outraged everyone at the meeting.
The government's decision to cut funding outraged local communities.
Many people were outraged by the graphic scenes shown in the documentary.

Synset Examples

The scandalous behavior of this married woman shocked her friends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shock - to cause someone to feel surprised or upset

Etymology

From Old French *outrage* (excess), from Vulgar Latin *ultraticum* (going beyond).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01814414-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
A shocking or disgraceful event or action that causes strong anger or indignation.
a shocking or disgraceful event

Examples

The destruction of the historic monument was an outrage that shocked the entire country.
Many considered the government's decision to be an outrage against justice.
The scandal caused public outrage and demands for accountability.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scandal - an action or event causing public outrage or shock
  • Synonym: crime - an illegal act; an offense

Etymology

From Old French 'outrage', from 'ultrage' (excess), from Latin 'ultra' (beyond).

5. VERB

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
To violate or desecrate something that is considered sacred or holy.
violate something sacred

Examples

He was arrested for trying to outrage the graves in the old cemetery.
It is a crime to outrage a place of worship by defacing its altar.
The community was shocked when vandals outraged the sacred grounds.

Synset Examples

desecrate a cemetery
violate the sanctity of the church
profane the name of God

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to desecrate - to treat a sacred place or thing with violent disrespect
  • Synonym: profane - to treat something sacred with irreverence or disrespect

Etymology

From Old French *outrage* (excess, insult), from *outrer* (to go beyond), from Latin *ultra* (beyond).
