carotene

1. NOUN

/ ˈkærətiːn / (AmE), / ˈkærətiːn / (BrE)
a yellow or orange pigment found in plants, which can be converted into vitamin A in the liver
yellow or orange plant pigment
medical

Examples

Carrots are rich in carotene, giving them their characteristic orange color.
The liver converts carotene from vegetables into vitamin A, which is vital for vision.
Researchers studied the effects of carotene supplements on human health.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: beta-carotene - a specific, well-known type of carotene
  • Degree Higher: vitamin A - nutrient produced from carotene in animals

Etymology

From Latin 'carota' (carrot) + '-ene' (chemical suffix), referring to its discovery in carrots.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkær.əˌtin / (AmE), / ˈkær.əˌtiːn / (BrE)
a yellow or orange-red fat-soluble pigment found in plants, especially carrots and other vegetables, important for vitamin A production in the body
plant pigment; vitamin A precursor

Examples

Carrots are rich in carotene, which gives them their orange color.
Some fruits get their vibrant hue from high carotene content.
A deficiency in carotene can affect your body's ability to make vitamin A.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carotenoid - a class of pigments including carotene and its derivatives
  • Antonym: chlorophyll - the green pigment in plants

Etymology

From Latin 'carota' (carrot) and chemical suffix '-ene'.
