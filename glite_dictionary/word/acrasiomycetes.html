Acrasiomycetes

1. NOUN

/ əˌkreɪsiˌoʊmaɪˈsiːtiːz / (AmE), / əˌkrasɪəʊmaɪˈsiːtiːz / (BrE)
A class of cellular slime molds, sometimes classified within the kingdom Protoctista, consisting of amoeboid organisms that aggregate to form multicellular structures.
a class of cellular slime molds

Examples

Scientists often study Acrasiomycetes to understand the evolution of multicellularity.
The Acrasiomycetes aggregate when food is scarce, forming a fruiting body to disperse spores.
Unlike true fungi, Acrasiomycetes spend part of their life cycle as single-celled amoebae.

Etymology

From Greek 'akrasia' (lack of command) and 'mycetes' (fungus), referring to the organism's lifestyle and resemblance to fungi.

2. NOUN

/ ˌæk.rəˌsaɪ.oʊ.maɪˈsiːtiːz / (AmE), / ˌakrəsiəʊmaɪˈsiːtiːz / (BrE)
A class of cellular slime molds sometimes classified in the kingdom Protoctista.
class of cellular slime molds

Examples

The Acrasiomycetes are known for their unique life cycle that includes both single-celled and multicellular stages.
Scientists study Acrasiomycetes to understand the evolution of multicellularity in eukaryotes.
Certain Acrasiomycetes species aggregate to form a slug-like structure under specific conditions.

Etymology

From Greek 'akrasia' (lack of mixing) and 'mycetes' (fungi).
