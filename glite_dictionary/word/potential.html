potential

1. NOUN

/ pəˈtɛnʃəl / (AmE), / pəˈtɛnʃəl / (BrE)
the difference in electric charge between two points in a circuit, measured in volts
difference in electric charge (volts)

Examples

The potential across the resistor was measured to be five volts.
A battery provides a potential that drives current through the circuit.
If there is no potential difference, no current will flow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: voltage - electric potential difference, measured in volts

Etymology

From Latin 'potentia' (power, being able), referring to power or capacity.

2. ADJ

/ pəˈtɛnʃəl / (AmE), / pəˈtɛnʃəl / (BrE)
Having the capacity to develop into something in the future; likely to become or exist.
possible or likely to happen in future

Examples

The company is looking for potential partners to expand its market reach.
Many scientists are studying the potential impact of climate change on agriculture.
We interviewed several potential candidates for the new position.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: actual - existing in fact; real; contrasted with potential

Etymology

From Latin 'potentialis', from 'potentia' (power).

3. NOUN

/ pəˈtɛnʃəl / (AmE), / pəˈtɛnʃəl / (BrE)
the inherent ability or capacity for development or becoming something in the future
capacity for development or future achievement

Examples

She has a lot of potential as a pianist, but she needs to practice more.
The idea has great potential for changing the way we use technology.
We are looking for employees with leadership potential to join our team.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possibility - a thing that may happen or be the case
  • Antonym: actuality - the state of existing in reality; being actual

Etymology

From Latin 'potentialis', from 'potentia' meaning 'power'.
