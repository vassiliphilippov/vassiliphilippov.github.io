leaden

1. ADJ

/ ˈlɛdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈlɛdn̩ / (BrE)
(of the sky or weather) dark, grey, and heavy with clouds; overcast.
overcast and grey (of the sky)

Examples

A leaden sky threatened rain all morning.
The photographer captured the leaden clouds hanging over the city.
By late afternoon, the leaden weather gave the whole landscape a somber tone.

Synset Examples

a dull sky
the sky was leaden and thick

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overcast - of the sky: covered with clouds; gloomy
  • Antonym: bright - full of light; shining strongly (of the sky)

Etymology

From Old English 'lēaden', meaning 'made of lead', from 'lead' + '-en'. The color association informs the modern figurative use.

2. ADJ

/ ˈlɛdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈlɛdn̩ / (BrE)
Feeling or appearing heavy and slow, as if weighed down by fatigue, weariness, or emotion.
heavy or weighted down (figurative)

Examples

After running the marathon, his leaden legs barely carried him across the finish line.
She struggled to keep her leaden eyelids open after a sleepless night.
The long, exhausting day left him with a leaden feeling in his limbs.

Synset Examples

his leaden arms
weighted eyelids

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heavy - weighing a lot; difficult to lift or carry
  • Antonym: light - not heavy; easy to lift

Etymology

From Old English 'leaden', from 'lead' (the metal) + '-en'.

/ ˈlɛdən / (AmE), / ˈlɛdən / (BrE)
(of movement) slow and heavy, lacking energy or liveliness.
slow and heavy in movement

Examples

Her leaden gait made it clear she was too tired to continue walking.
The workers moved at a leaden pace after hours of hard labor.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sluggish - moving slowly or lacking energy
  • Antonym: nimble - quick and light in movement or action

Etymology

From Old English 'lēaden', from 'lēad' (lead, the metal), referring to the heavy quality of lead.
