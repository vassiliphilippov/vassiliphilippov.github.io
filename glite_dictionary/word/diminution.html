diminution

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɪməˈnuːʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪmɪˈnjuːʃən / (BrE)
In music, the statement of a theme in notes of shorter duration, typically half the length of the original.
musical theme played in shorter note values

Examples

In Baroque music, composers often use diminution to vary familiar melodies.
The fugue subject returns in diminution, doubling the speed of the original theme.
Her piece demonstrates mastery of diminution and other contrapuntal techniques.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: augmentation - the statement of a theme in longer note values

Etymology

From Latin *diminutio* (a lessening), from *diminuere* (to diminish).

2. NOUN

/ dɪməˈnuːʃən / (AmE), / dɪmɪˈnjuːʃən / (BrE)
the act of making something smaller or less; a reduction in size, amount, or importance
the act of reducing or decreasing

Examples

The new measures led to a diminution in air pollution levels across the city.
There has been a noticeable diminution of support for the government in recent months.
The company reported a diminution in profits over the last quarter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reduction - the act of making something smaller or less
  • Antonym: increase - the process of becoming or making greater in size or amount

Etymology

From Old French *diminucion*, from Latin *dīminūtiō* (a lessening, diminishing).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdɪməˈnuːʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪmɪˈnjuːʃən / (BrE)
The process of becoming smaller or less; a reduction in size, extent, or importance.
reduction; decrease in size or importance

Examples

There has been a noticeable diminution in the size of the polar ice caps over recent decades.
The company reported a diminution of profits due to rising costs.
A diminution in enthusiasm among team members affected the project's progress.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reduction - the action or fact of making something smaller or less
  • Antonym: increase - the process of becoming larger or greater

Etymology

From Latin 'diminutio' (a lessening), from 'deminuere' (to lessen).
