implicit

1. ADJ

/ ɪmˈplɪsɪt / (AmE), / ɪmˈplɪsɪt / (BrE)
(of trust, faith, or belief) complete and without any doubt or reservation.
complete; without doubt or reservation

Examples

She placed her implicit trust in her friends, never doubting their loyalty.
His authority was accepted with implicit confidence by everyone on the team.
Parents often have implicit faith that teachers will look after their children.

Synset Examples

implicit trust

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unquestioning - (of belief or trust) not doubting or questioning
  • Antonym: explicit - stated clearly and in detail, leaving no room for confusion or doubt

Etymology

From Latin implicitus, past participle of implicare (to entangle).

2. ADJ

/ ɪmˈplɪsɪt / (AmE), / ɪmˈplɪsɪt / (BrE)
suggested or understood without being directly stated; existing as an inherent part of something.
not directly stated, implied

Examples

Her trust in him was implicit, even though he had never promised anything.
An implicit threat could be felt in his words, though he spoke calmly.
The contract contained implicit terms that both parties understood without discussion.

Synset Examples

an implicit agreement not to raise the subject
there was implicit criticism in his voice
anger was implicit in the argument
the oak is implicit in the acorn

Related Terms

  • Antonym: explicit - stated clearly and in detail, leaving no room for confusion
  • Synonym: implied - suggested but not directly stated

Etymology

From Latin implicitus, past participle of implicare (to entwine, entangle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00944754-a
Synset POS: a
