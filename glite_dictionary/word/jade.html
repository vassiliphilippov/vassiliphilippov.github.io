jade

1. NOUN

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
an old or worn-out horse, especially one that is overworked or in poor condition
old or over-worked horse
archaic

Examples

The farmer finally set his faithful old jade out to pasture after many years of hard work.
The knight dismounted his tired jade at the edge of the woods.
The race was lost for no horse could outrun that broken jade.

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Old Norse *jalda* (mare).

2. NOUN

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
a light green color that varies from bluish green to yellowish green, similar to the color of the gemstone jade
light green color

Examples

The walls were painted a soothing jade to create a calming atmosphere.
She wore a beautiful dress in a rich jade shade.
The artist mixed just enough yellow and blue to achieve the perfect jade for his landscape painting.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: emerald - a bright green color, like that of the emerald gemstone
  • Degree Lower: mint - a pale green color, like mint leaves

Etymology

From Old Spanish *jade*, from Latin *ilia* (flanks, reins), referring originally to the stone believed to cure pain in the side.

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
Of a color resembling jade; especially varying from bluish green to yellowish green.
bluish or yellowish green color

Examples

She wore a jade dress that brought out the green in her eyes.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: olive - dark yellowish-green color

From Spanish *jade* (jade stone), itself from Latin *ilia* (flanks, kidneys), through Old French *l'ejade*.

4. NOUN

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
a hard, typically green semiprecious stone that can be highly polished, used for ornaments and jewelry; composed chiefly of jadeite or nephrite
a green semiprecious gemstone

Examples

She wore a beautiful necklace made of jade and gold.
The museum displayed ancient Chinese carvings in jade.
The ring’s stone was a deep green jade that shimmered in the light.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nephrite - a variety of jade, mainly composed of a calcium magnesium silicate
  • Synonym: jadeite - a variety of jade, a sodium aluminum silicate

Etymology

From Spanish 'jade', from French 'jade', from Latin 'ilia' (flanks, loins), from the belief that it cured kidney diseases.

5. VERB

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
(of a person) to lose interest or become bored or weary, especially after overexposure or excess.
become bored or weary

Examples

After years of attending the same conferences, she began to jade of the endless speeches.
Some students jade quickly when faced with repetitive assignments.
He tried not to jade of his favorite hobby, but eventually lost interest.

Synset Examples

I'm so tired of your mother and her complaints about my food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weary - to become physically or mentally tired of something
  • Synonym: bore - to make or become uninterested

Etymology

From earlier 'to jade' (to tire out a horse), from 'jade' (a worn-out horse).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00075867-v
Synset POS: v

6. NOUN

/ dʒeɪd / (AmE), / dʒeɪd / (BrE)
(Archaic) A woman who is morally loose, often referring to a female adulterer or a disreputable woman.
(archaic) disreputable woman; female adulterer
archaic

Examples

In the old tales, the village gossips would call her a jade for consorting with married men.
He dismissed her harshly as nothing but a common jade, unworthy of respect or trust.
The playwright's comedy mocked the town's so-called jade in a scandalous subplot.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harlot - a woman who has many sexual partners, particularly one who is considered immoral
  • Synonym: adulteress - a woman who commits adultery
  • Antonym: lady - a courteous or respectable woman (contrast: archaic disreputable woman)

Etymology

Perhaps from Old Norse *jalda* (mare); used about women figuratively from the notion of a nag or ill-tempered horse.

to tire, weary, or diminish the strength, enthusiasm, or interest of someone through overexposure, repeated experience, or disappointment.
to make weary; lessen enthusiasm

Examples

Her constant complaints started to jade my enthusiasm.
The daily repetition of chores can sometimes jade your interest in them.
Negative feedback over time might jade his enthusiasm for the project.

Synset Examples

Her constant complaints started to jade my enthusiasm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weary - to make or become tired
  • Antonym: to refresh - to restore strength or enthusiasm

Etymology

Probably from Old Norse *jalda* (mare), with figurative use in English meaning to exhaust or wear out (as from an overworked horse).
