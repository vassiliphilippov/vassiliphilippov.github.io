bustle

1. VERB

/ ˈbʌsəl / (AmE), / ˈbʌsl̩ / (BrE)
to move energetically and busily; to be full of activity or movement
move energetically or busily

Examples

The kitchen staff bustled around, preparing dinner for the guests.
She bustled into the office, balancing a stack of papers.
The market square bustled with shoppers and street vendors on Saturday mornings.

Synset Examples

The cheerleaders bustled about excitedly before their performance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scurry - to move quickly and with short steps
  • Antonym: to dawdle - to move slowly and waste time

2. NOUN

/ ˈbʌsəl / (AmE), / ˈbʌsəl / (BrE)
A framework worn at the back below the waist to give fullness to a woman's skirt, especially in late 19th-century fashion.
support used to puff out a woman's skirt

Examples

The museum exhibits included a Victorian dress complete with a bustle.
She wore a replica gown with a padded bustle to achieve the period look.
Fashion in the 1880s was characterized by the prominence of the bustle beneath women's skirts.

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly related to Middle English "bustelen" (to move or bustle) or a variant of "bosom" in the sense of swelling.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbʌs.əl / (AmE), / ˈbʌs.əl / (BrE)
energetic or noisy activity and movement, often in a busy setting
energetic or noisy activity

Examples

There was a bustle of activity in the kitchen as dinner was being prepared.
The bustle of the city can be overwhelming for some people.
In the morning bustle of the train station, she almost missed her train.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: commotion - a state of confused and noisy disturbance
  • Antonym: calm - a peaceful or quiet state

Etymology

From 16th-century English, possibly of imitative origin, reflecting the sound/activity of bustling.

Synset POS: n
