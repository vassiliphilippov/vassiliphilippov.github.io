to subsume

1. VERB

/ səbˈsuːm / (AmE), / səbˈsjuːm / (BrE)
to include or absorb something as part of a larger group, category, or principle
include or absorb in a larger category

Examples

The new training manual will subsume all previous guidelines.
In philosophy, individual cases are often subsumed under general principles.
Many small tasks were subsumed into one large project.

Synset Examples

This new system subsumes the old one

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to include - to contain as a part or member
  • Antonym: to exclude - to leave out or not include

Etymology

From Latin 'subsumere' (to take under), from 'sub-' (under) + 'sumere' (to take).

2. VERB

/ səbˈsuːm / (AmE), / səbˈsjuːm / (BrE)
to include or absorb (something) under a more general category or principle
include under a general category

Examples

The law subsumes individual cases under a general principle.
Many minor offenses are subsumed within this broader category.
In his theory, personal interests are subsumed by the needs of society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to include - to contain as part of a whole
  • Antonym: to exclude - to leave out; not include

Etymology

From Latin 'subsumere' (to take from under), from 'sub-' (under) + 'sumere' (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00687125-v
Synset POS: v
