spectacle

1. NOUN

/ ˈspɛktəkl̩ / (AmE), / ˈspɛktəkəl / (BrE)
an embarrassing or ridiculous display or situation that attracts attention, especially as in the phrase 'make a spectacle of yourself'
embarrassing or ridiculous display

Examples

She realized she had made a spectacle of herself by singing loudly in the quiet restaurant.
Don’t make a spectacle of yourself at the meeting by arguing with everyone.
His dramatic entrance turned into an unintended spectacle that everyone stared at.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scene - a display that attracts public attention; commotion

Etymology

From Latin 'spectaculum' (a show, public display), from 'spectare' (to look at).

2. NOUN

/ ˈspɛk.tə.kəl / (AmE), / ˈspɛk.tə.kəl / (BrE)
an impressive and visually striking display, often on a grand or elaborate scale
elaborate visual display or event

Examples

The fireworks were a stunning spectacle visible across the entire city.
Crowds gathered to witness the spectacle of the parade.
The opening ceremony was a grand spectacle of lights and music.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: show - a public entertainment performance or display
  • Synonym: display - an event or item shown to the public for viewing
  • Antonym: modesty - the opposite of showiness or extravagant display

Etymology

From Latin 'spectaculum' (a show, public event), from 'spectare' (to watch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04279001-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈspɛk.tə.kəl / (AmE), / ˈspɛk.tə.kəl / (BrE)
an unusual, impressive, or striking sight or display, especially one that attracts attention
impressive or striking sight

Examples

The parade was a colorful spectacle that drew visitors from all over the city.
They stopped to watch the amazing spectacle of fireworks lighting up the night sky.
The opening ceremony was a grand spectacle broadcast live on television.

Synset Examples

the tragic spectacle of cripples trying to escape

Related Terms

  • Synonym: show - a public performance or display
  • Synonym: display - a visually striking arrangement or scene

Etymology

From Latin 'spectaculum' (a show, spectacle), from 'spectare' (to watch, look at).
