summation

1. NOUN

/ sʌˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / sʌˈmeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the total amount obtained by adding several amounts or things together; the aggregate result or final total.
final total; total amount

Examples

The summation of his achievements was truly impressive.
After months of hard work, their summation was a project that exceeded everyone's expectations.
Her speech was a summation of all the evidence presented during the trial.

Synset Examples

the sum of all our troubles did not equal the misery they suffered

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sum - the total amount resulting from the addition of two or more numbers or items
  • Synonym: aggregate - a whole formed by combining several elements

Etymology

From Latin 'summatio', from 'summare' (to sum up), from 'summa' (sum, total).

2. NOUN

/ səˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / sʌˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
(physiology) The process by which repeated or simultaneous stimuli combine to produce a response in a muscle, nerve, or other tissue that one stimulus alone would not cause.
combined response to multiple stimuli (physiology)
medical

Examples

The summation of nerve impulses can lead to a stronger muscular contraction.
Temporal summation occurs when several stimuli are delivered in quick succession to a neuron.
Without summation, a single stimulus would not generate an action potential in the muscle fiber.

Etymology

From Latin summatio (a summing up), from summare (to sum).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ səˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / sʌˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of calculating the total of two or more numbers; addition
the act of adding numbers together

Examples

The mathematician carefully performed the summation of a long series of numbers.
In statistics, the symbol Σ represents the concept of summation.
A quick summation of the expenses showed the project was over budget.

Synset Examples

the summation of four and three gives seven

Related Terms

  • Synonym: addition - the process of adding numbers together
  • Antonym: subtraction - the arithmetic operation of taking one number away from another

Etymology

From Latin summatio, from summare (to sum up), from summa (sum, total).

4. NOUN

/ sʌˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / sʌˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
A final summary or review, especially one that concludes an argument or case, such as at the end of a trial.
final summary, especially in law

Examples

The defense attorney began his summation to reinforce the main points of the case.
In her closing summation, the prosecutor reminded the jury of the key evidence.
Each side presented a concise summation before the judge delivered the verdict.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: summary - a brief statement of the main points

Etymology

From Latin 'summatio' (an adding up), from 'summare' (to sum).
