to liberate

1. VERB

/ ˈlɪbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪbəreɪt / (BrE)
to give equal rights or freedom to a group, especially to women or minorities
grant equal rights or freedom

Examples

The new law was designed to liberate women from traditional gender roles in the workplace.
Many activists dedicate their lives to liberating minorities from social discrimination.
Reforms were introduced to liberate marginalized groups and ensure equal rights.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to emancipate - to set free, especially from legal, social, or political restrictions
  • Antonym: to oppress - to keep someone in subservience; to limit freedom or rights

Etymology

From Latin 'liberare' (to set free), from 'liber' (free).

2. VERB

/ ˈlɪbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪbəreɪt / (BrE)
to set someone or something free, especially from imprisonment, slavery, or oppression.
set free from imprisonment or oppression

Examples

The military moved quickly to liberate the occupied city.
Many activists worked tirelessly to liberate those imprisoned for their beliefs.
After years of captivity, the hostages were finally liberated by the rescue team.

Synset Examples

The students liberated their slaves upon graduating from the university

Related Terms

  • Synonym: free - to make someone free; to release from confinement or oppression
  • Antonym: to imprison - to confine someone, especially as punishment for a crime
  • Antonym: to enslave - to make someone a slave or deprive of freedom

Etymology

From Latin liberatus, past participle of liberare (to set free), from liber (free).

3. VERB

/ ˈlɪbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈlɪbəreɪt / (BrE)
(of a chemical reaction or process) to release (a gas, energy, or other substance).
release as a result of a reaction

Examples

Heating limestone liberates carbon dioxide gas as a byproduct of decomposition.
The reaction liberates a considerable amount of energy in the form of heat.
When water splits during electrolysis, it liberates hydrogen and oxygen gases.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: release - allow something to move, escape, or be set free

Etymology

From Latin liberatus, past participle of liberare ‘to set free’.
