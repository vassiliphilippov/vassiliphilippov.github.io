deposition

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of placing, laying down, or accumulating something in a particular location
the act of laying down or placing

Examples

The deposition of sediment by the river created a new sandbar.
Frost forms on a window by the deposition of water vapor onto the cold glass.
After the volcanic eruption, the deposition of ash covered the surrounding villages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deposit - to put or set down (something); to place for safekeeping

Etymology

From Latin depositio (a laying down), from deponere (to lay down).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of removing someone from office or a position of power
removal from office or power

Examples

The sudden deposition of the king led to months of unrest.
His deposition from office shocked political observers across the country.
The general orchestrated the deposition of the ruling president.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dismissal - the act of removing someone from a position or job
  • Antonym: enthronement - the act of installing someone into a position of power, especially as a monarch

Etymology

From Latin 'depositio' (a laying down, removal).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
(law) a formal statement made by a witness under oath, taken outside of court before a trial.
legal pretrial witness statement
legal

Examples

The lawyer scheduled the deposition for next Friday afternoon.
During her deposition, the witness provided crucial information about the case.
The judge reviewed the deposition transcripts before the trial began.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: testimony - a formal written or spoken statement given in a court of law

Etymology

From Latin 'deponere' (to lay down, put aside), via Medieval Latin 'depositio' (testimony).

4. NOUN

/ ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛpəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
the natural process by which material settles or is laid down in a new location, especially by wind, water, or ice
process of material settling naturally

Examples

The deposition of sand at the river delta created new land over time.
Wind causes the deposition of dust particles on windowsills and surfaces.
Glaciers are responsible for the deposition of rocks and soil as they move and melt.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: erosion - the process by which material is worn away, often contrasted with deposition

Etymology

From Latin depositio (a laying aside), from deponere (to lay down).
