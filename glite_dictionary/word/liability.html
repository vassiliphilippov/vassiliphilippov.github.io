liability

1. NOUN

/ laɪˈæbɪlɪti / (AmE), / laɪəˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
a legal obligation to pay money to another person or entity; something for which someone is responsible, especially a debt or financial obligation
legal responsibility; financial obligation
legal

Examples

The company listed its total liability to creditors in its annual report.
If you cause an accident, you may have a liability to pay damages.
Reducing tax liability is a common goal in financial planning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: debt - something, typically money, that is owed or due
  • Antonym: asset - something valuable owned by a person or company

Etymology

From Old French *liabilité* (responsibility), from Late Latin *ligare* (to bind).

2. NOUN

/ ˌlaɪəˈbɪləti / (AmE), / ˌlaɪəˈbɪləti / (BrE)
the state or quality of being a disadvantage or hindrance; something that causes difficulties or puts one at a disadvantage
state of being a disadvantage

Examples

His poor communication skills have become a real liability at work.
In the fast-paced world of finance, hesitation can be a serious liability.
The company's outdated computers were a liability during the software upgrade.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asset - a useful or valuable quality or person

Etymology

From Old French *liabilité* (legal responsibility), from Late Latin *ligare* (to bind).

3. NOUN

/ laɪˈæbɪlɪti / (AmE), / laɪəˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
the state of being legally responsible for something, especially for paying money that is owed or for damages caused
legal responsibility or obligation
legal

Examples

The company accepted full liability for the accident and paid compensation to the victims.
If you borrow someone else's car, you may assume liability for any damage you cause.
Their insurance policy protects them against financial liability arising from workplace injuries.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asset - something valuable owned by a person or company

Etymology

From Old French *lïabilité* or Medieval Latin *liabilitas*, from Latin *ligare* (to bind).
