to empower

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpaʊər / (AmE), / ɪmˈpaʊə / (BrE)
to give someone the authority or power to do something
give authority or power

Examples

The new law will empower local councils to make their own decisions.
Her manager empowered her to lead the project without supervision.
Training programs are designed to empower employees with new skills.

Synset Examples

She authorized her assistant to sign the papers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to authorize - to give official permission for or approval to (an action)
  • Synonym: to enable - to make someone able to do something
  • Antonym: disempower - to take away power or authority from someone

Etymology

From Old French *empouvoir*, based on Latin *potere* (to be able).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpaʊ.ɚ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpaʊ.ə / (BrE)
to give someone the authority, power, or ability to do something; to make someone stronger or more confident, especially in controlling their life or claiming their rights.
give power or authority; make stronger

Examples

Good managers know how to empower their employees to reach their full potential.
Education can empower individuals to break the cycle of poverty.
The new policy aims to empower people with disabilities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enable - to make able; to provide someone with the means or ability to do something
  • Synonym: to authorize - to give official permission for something
  • Antonym: disempower - to deprive of power or authority

Etymology

From Old French 'empouvoir', from en- (in) + pouvoir (to be able), from Latin 'potere' (to be able).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02479662-v
Synset POS: v
