Constantine I

1. PROPN

/ ˌkɑn.stənˈtin ðə ˈfɜrst / (AmE), / ˌkɒn.stənˈtiːn ðə ˈfɜːst / (BrE)
Roman emperor (280–337) who ended the persecution of Christians, established Christianity as the religion of the Roman Empire, and moved the capital to Byzantium (renaming it Constantinople).
Roman emperor, ended Christian persecution

Examples

Constantine I is known for his role in making Christianity the dominant religion of the Roman Empire.
After Constantine I moved the capital, Byzantium became known as Constantinople.
Many historians consider Constantine I one of the most influential Roman emperors.

Etymology

From Latin 'Constantinus', Roman family name, from 'constans' (constant, steadfast).
