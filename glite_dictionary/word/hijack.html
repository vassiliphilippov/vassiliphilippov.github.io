hijack

1. VERB

/ ˈhaɪ.dʒæk / (AmE), / ˈhaɪ.dʒæk / (BrE)
to take control of a vehicle or other object by force, especially to use it for one's own purposes
take control by force (esp. a vehicle)

Examples

Armed men hijacked the bus and demanded a ransom for the passengers.
The terrorists attempted to hijack the plane but were quickly subdued by security.
She accused her rival of trying to hijack the meeting to push a different agenda.

Synset Examples

The Cubans commandeered the plane and flew it to Miami

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commandeer - to take control of something, often for military or official use, by force

Etymology

Probably from 'highway' and 'jacking', originally US slang (1920s), referring to robbing vehicles on the highway.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhaɪˌdʒæk / (AmE), / ˈhaɪ.dʒæk / (BrE)
an act of seizing control of a vehicle in transit, especially to rob it or force it to go to a different destination
seizure of a vehicle in transit

Examples

The police responded quickly to the hijack of the delivery truck on the highway.
The recent hijack brought increased security at all airports.
He was a witness to the hijack on the city bus last year.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carjacking - the act of violently stealing a car while it is occupied

Etymology

Probably from 'high' + 'jack', originally criminal slang for robbery in the 1920s.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00771433-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈhaɪ.dʒæk / (AmE), / ˈhaɪ.dʒæk / (BrE)
To illegally seize control of a vehicle, aircraft, ship, or other process, especially by force; used figuratively to mean seizing control of something not intended to be controlled by you.
seize control unlawfully or by force

Examples

Armed men attempted to hijack the plane in midair.
A small group tried to hijack the meeting to push their own agenda.
Hackers managed to hijack the company's website for several hours.

Synset Examples

they hijacked the judicial process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commandeer - to officially take control of something, especially for military use
  • Synonym: to seize - to take hold of something suddenly and forcibly

Etymology

Probably from high(way) + jack (slang for robbery), originally referring to robbery of cars and then extended to aircraft and other contexts.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02279879-v
Synset POS: v
