bruise

1. NOUN

/ bruz / (AmE), / bruːz / (BrE)
An injury to body tissue in which the skin is not broken but becomes discolored, usually due to a blow or impact.
an injury causing skin discoloration without a cut

Examples

She had a large bruise on her arm after bumping into the door.
The child fell off his bike and got a bruise on his knee.
It took two weeks for the bruise on my leg to heal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contusion - a medical term for a bruise (an area where blood has collected under the skin)
  • Antonym: cut - an injury that breaks the skin

Etymology

From Old French *bruisier* (to break, crush), of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ bruːz / (AmE), / bruːz / (BrE)
to injure the underlying soft tissue or bone of (a part of the body) without breaking the skin, causing a discoloration
injure body tissue without breaking skin

Examples

She bruised her arm while moving the heavy furniture.
If you bruise the fruit, it will spoil faster.
He accidentally bruised his shin on the coffee table.

Synset Examples

I bruised my knee

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to contuse - to injure tissue without breaking the skin
  • Antonym: to heal - to become healthy again after an injury

Etymology

From Old French bruisier (to break), of Germanic origin.

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bruːz / (AmE), / bruːz / (BrE)
to crush or break something into small pieces, especially in food preparation, usually without cutting completely through
crush food into small pieces

Examples

Gently bruise the mint leaves before adding them to the drink to release their flavor.
Some recipes suggest you lightly bruise the garlic cloves instead of chopping them.
To make the sauce, first bruise the berries with a fork.

Synset Examples

bruise the berries with a wooden spoon and strain them

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crush - to press or squeeze something so that it breaks or is damaged
  • Synonym: mash - to crush food until it becomes soft and smooth

Etymology

From Old English 'brȳsan' (to crush), related to Old Norse 'brjóta' (to break).

Source: WordNet
