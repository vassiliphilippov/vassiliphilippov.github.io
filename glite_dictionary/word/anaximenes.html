Anaximenes

1. PROPN

/ ˌænəkˈsɪməˌniːz / (AmE), / ˌanəkˈsɪməniːz / (BrE)
(c. 585–c. 528 BC) A presocratic Greek philosopher who taught that air is the fundamental substance of the universe and that all things are formed from air in varying degrees of density.
presocratic Greek philosopher; proposed air as primary substance

Examples

Anaximenes argued that air is the essential principle underlying all matter.
The philosophy of Anaximenes influenced later thinkers in ancient Greece.
Unlike his contemporary Anaximander, Anaximenes identified air, not the boundless, as the primary element.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Ἀναξιμένης (Anaximénēs), a personal name.
