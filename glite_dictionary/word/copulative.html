copulative

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːpjəˌleɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈkɒpjʊlətɪv / (BrE)
a verb, such as 'be' or 'become', that links the subject of a sentence with its complement; a linking verb
a verb linking subject and complement

Examples

In the sentence 'She is happy', the word is functions as a copulative.
The most common copulative in English is the verb 'to be'.
Teachers often explain that a copulative connects the subject to additional information about the subject.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: linking verb - a verb that connects the subject with a subject complement

Etymology

From Latin 'copulativus', from 'copulare' (to link together).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːpjəˌleɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈkɒpjʊlətɪv / (BrE)
(of a word, especially a conjunction) connecting words, phrases, or clauses in a sentence to indicate connected or additive meaning.
connecting; linking (linguistics)

Examples

The word copulative refers to conjunctions that link elements in a sentence, such as 'and'.
In grammar lessons, teachers often highlight the copulative function of the word 'and'.
Some linguists classify 'as well as' as a copulative connector.

Synset Examples

‘and’ is a copulative conjunction

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coordinating - joining words or groups of equal grammatical rank
  • Antonym: disjunctive - (of a conjunction) expressing an alternative or contrast, e.g., 'or'

Etymology

From Latin copulativus (serving to connect), from copulare (to join together).
