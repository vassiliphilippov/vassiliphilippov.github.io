to countervail

1. VERB

/ ˌkaʊntərˈveɪl / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntəˈveɪl / (BrE)
to act against something so as to neutralize or offset its effect
offset or neutralize the effect of

Examples

The government's new tax policy will countervail the effects of inflation.
The union's efforts were intended to countervail the influence of large corporations.
Strict regulations can sometimes countervail the dangers posed by industrial expansion.

Synset Examples

This will counteract the foolish actions of my colleagues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to counteract - to act against something in order to reduce its force or neutralize it

Etymology

From Old French 'contrevaloir', from Latin 'contravalēre' (to have equal force with).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02549571-v

2. VERB

/ ˌkaʊntərˈveɪl / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntəˈveɪl / (BrE)
to act or exert force against something so as to counterbalance or mitigate its effects
offset the effect of something

Examples

The new taxes will countervail the benefits of the subsidy to some extent.
The effects of inflation were largely countervailed by rising wages.
They introduced policies to countervail the potential economic risks.

Synset Examples

offset deposits and withdrawals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: offset - to counterbalance or compensate for something

Etymology

From Old French 'contrevaloir', from Latin 'contra' (against) + 'valere' (to be strong).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02723890-v
Synset POS: v
