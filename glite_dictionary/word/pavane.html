pavane

1. NOUN

/ pəˈvɑːn / (AmE), / pəˈvɑːn / (BrE)
A slow and stately dance popular in 16th- and 17th-century Europe, or music composed for such a dance.
slow, stately Renaissance dance or its music

Examples

The orchestra played a beautiful pavane that evoked the elegance of a royal court.
Ravel's most famous pavane was dedicated to the memory of a princess.
During the Renaissance festival, the musicians performed a pavane for the crowd.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: galliard - a lively Renaissance dance in triple time, often paired with the pavane

Etymology

From French 'pavane', from Italian 'pavana', possibly from 'Padovana' (of Padua).

2. NOUN

/ pəˈvɑn / (AmE), / pəˈvɑːn / (BrE)
a slow and stately dance that was popular in European courts during the 16th and 17th centuries
stately Renaissance court dance

Examples

The orchestra performed a beautiful pavane to open the concert.
Dancers in elaborate costumes moved in a dignified pavane across the ballroom floor.
He studied the steps of the pavane for the historical performance.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: galliard - a lively dance popular in Renaissance Europe, often performed after a pavane
  • Synonym: minuet - a slow, graceful dance of the 17th and 18th centuries

Etymology

From French 'pavane', from Italian 'pavana' (from 'Pádua', the city of Padua).
