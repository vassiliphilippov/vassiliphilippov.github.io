to resonate

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛzəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛzəneɪt / (BrE)
(of an idea, feeling, etc.) to be received or understood in a meaningful or sympathetic way by someone; to have a lasting or powerful effect.
be understood or have an effect

Examples

Her speech really resonated with the audience, inspiring many to take action.
The themes in the novel resonate with readers from different backgrounds.
His experiences resonated deeply, reminding me of my own childhood.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to strike a chord - to produce a strong feeling or response
  • Antonym: to fall flat - to fail to have intended effect or impact

Etymology

From Latin 'resonare' (to sound again, resound).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00593432-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈrɛzəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɛzəneɪt / (BrE)
(of a sound) to produce or be filled with a deep, full, reverberating sound; to echo or vibrate with resonance.
produce or echo with a deep sound

Examples

The singer's deep voice resonated throughout the concert hall, leaving the audience mesmerized.
Thunder resonated across the mountains, filling the valleys with sound.
Her laughter resonated in the empty room, making the moment feel less lonely.

Synset Examples

The sound resonates well in this theater

Related Terms

  • Synonym: echo - to be repeated or reverberated as a sound

Etymology

From Latin resonare (to resound), from re- (again) + sonare (to sound).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02180402-v
Synset POS: v
