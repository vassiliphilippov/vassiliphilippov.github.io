syphon

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaɪ.fən / (AmE), / ˈsʌɪ.fən / (BrE)
a tubular organ in certain aquatic animals, especially mollusks, used to draw in or expel water
tubular organ in aquatic animals

Examples

The clam extends its syphon to filter water for food particles.
Some cephalopods use a syphon to quickly propel themselves by expelling water.
The syphon in certain snails allows them to breathe while buried in sand.

Etymology

From Greek 'sīphōn' meaning 'pipe' or 'tube', via Latin 'siphon'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsaɪ.fən / (AmE), / ˈsʌɪ.fən / (BrE)
a tube used to move liquid from one container to another at a lower level by the force of gravity, once flow has been started
tube for transferring liquids by gravity

Examples

We used a syphon to transfer the wine from the barrel to the bottle.
A syphon is handy for emptying fish tanks without spilling water everywhere.
The brewer attached a syphon to let the beer flow smoothly into the new container.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: siphon - alternate spelling of syphon (tube for transferring liquids by gravity)

Etymology

From Greek 'siphōn' (pipe, tube) via Latin 'sipho'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04231230-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈsaɪ.fən / (AmE), / ˈsaɪ.fən / (BrE)
to draw off, convey, or transfer a liquid (or something else) from one place to another using or as if using a siphon, often by means of suction or gravity.
draw off liquid using a siphon

Examples

He used a tube to syphon gasoline from the tank.
Funds were illegally syphoned off into a private account.
They tried to syphon water from the flooded basement to the garden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: siphon - draw off or convey a liquid from one container to another using a tube

Etymology

Variant spelling of 'siphon', from Greek 'siphōn' (pipe, tube).

Source: WordNet
