Counter-Reformation

1. NOUN

/ ˌkaʊntərˌrɛfəˈrmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntərəˌrɛfəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
the reform movement within the Roman Catholic Church in the 16th and 17th centuries, initiated in response to the Protestant Reformation and intended to revive and strengthen Catholic doctrine and practices
Catholic revival against Protestant Reformation

Examples

The Counter-Reformation implemented many changes within the Catholic Church to combat Protestant influence.
Art and architecture from the Counter-Reformation period are known for their dramatic and emotional style.
The Jesuit order played a key role during the Counter-Reformation, focusing on education and missionary work.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Reformation - the 16th-century religious movement leading to the establishment of Protestant churches

Etymology

From Latin contra (against) + reformatio (restoration); first used in early 19th century to describe the Catholic revival.
