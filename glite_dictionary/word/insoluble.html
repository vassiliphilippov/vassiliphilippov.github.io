insoluble

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɑljəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɒljʊb(ə)l / (BrE)
(of a problem or question) impossible to solve or explain; having no possible solution.
impossible to solve or explain

Examples

The committee faced an insoluble conflict that no one could mediate.
For centuries, philosophers have debated the insoluble mystery of consciousness.
Despite hours of investigation, the cause of the error remained insoluble.

Synset Examples

an insoluble doubt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unsolvable - impossible to solve
  • Antonym: resolvable - able to be solved or explained

Etymology

From Latin insolubilis (not to be loosened or resolved), from in- (not) + solvere (to loosen, solve).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɑːljəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɒljʊbəl / (BrE)
(of a substance) not able to be dissolved in a liquid, especially water.
not able to be dissolved in liquid

Examples

Salt is soluble in water, but sand is insoluble and settles at the bottom.
Most plastics are insoluble in water, making them difficult to break down in the environment.
The chemist explained that the powder was completely insoluble in alcohol.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soluble - able to be dissolved in liquid

Etymology

From Latin insolubilis, from in- (not) + solubilis (that can be loosened, dissolved).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02273647-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɑːljəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɒljʊbəl / (BrE)
(of a problem or difficulty) impossible to solve or deal with.
impossible to solve

Examples

The committee faced an insoluble problem no one could resolve.
Climate change sometimes seems like an insoluble challenge for humanity.
Despite hours of discussion, the puzzle remained insoluble.

Synset Examples

an insoluble problem

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unsolvable - not able to be solved
  • Antonym: soluble - able to be solved or resolved

Etymology

From Latin insolubilis, from in- (not) + solubilis (solvable).
