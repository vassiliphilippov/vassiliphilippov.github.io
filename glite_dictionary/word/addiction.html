addiction

1. NOUN

/ əˈdɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / əˈdɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
A strong and harmful need to regularly have or do something, especially a substance such as drugs or alcohol.
a strong and harmful craving or dependence

Examples

Her addiction to caffeine made it difficult for her to start the day without coffee.
He struggled for years to overcome his addiction to gambling.
The documentary explored how social media addiction is affecting young people.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dependence - the state of relying on or being controlled by something; especially a substance
  • Synonym: obsession - an idea or thought that continually preoccupies or intrudes on a person's mind
  • Antonym: sobriety - the state of not being affected by alcohol or drugs

Etymology

From Latin 'addictio' (an awarding, a giving over), from 'addicere' (to assign).

2. NOUN

/ əˈdɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / əˈdɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
the condition of being unable to stop using or doing something, especially a substance such as drugs or alcohol, because it is habit-forming or causes dependency
compulsive dependence on a substance or activity
medical

Examples

Her struggle with addiction affected every aspect of her life, from work to relationships.
Many celebrities have spoken openly about their battles with addiction to alcohol or drugs.
Recognizing addiction as a medical condition is an important step toward helping those affected.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dependence - state of relying on or being controlled by something else, especially a drug or substance
  • Degree Lower: habit - a settled or regular tendency or practice, especially one that is hard to give up

Etymology

From Latin addictio (a giving over, surrender), from addicere (to give over, award).

3. NOUN

/ əˈdɪk.ʃən / (AmE), / əˈdɪk.ʃən / (BrE)
(in Roman law) a formal award by a magistrate of a person or thing to another, such as a debtor to a creditor; an act of surrendering someone or something to another's control.
formal award to another in Roman law
legal

Examples

In ancient Rome, addiction was one justification for placing a debtor into servitude.
The magistrate’s addiction of property determined to whom the goods were lawfully transferred.
Addiction could result in a person's surrender to another as repayment for a debt.

Synset Examples

under Roman law, addiction was the justification for slavery

Related Terms

  • Antonym: manumission - the act of freeing a slave in Roman law

Etymology

From Latin 'addictio', meaning 'an awarding' or 'a surrender'.
