Anaximander

1. PROPN

/ ˌænəkˈsɪmˌændər / (AmE), / ˌanəksɪˈmandə / (BrE)
(c. 610–546 BC) A presocratic Greek philosopher, student of Thales, who proposed that the universal substance was the infinite (apeiron) rather than something resembling ordinary objects.
presocratic Greek philosopher

Examples

Anaximander was one of the earliest Greek philosophers to propose that the universe originated from the infinite.
Many scholars study Anaximander's theories about the apeiron and the origins of the cosmos.
According to ancient sources, Anaximander was a student of Thales and influenced later philosophical thought.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Thales - presocratic Greek philosopher and teacher of Anaximander

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Ἀναξίμανδρος (Anaxímandros).
