neurotic

1. ADJ

/ nʊˈrɑːtɪk / (AmE), / njʊəˈrɒtɪk / (BrE)
Having, caused by, or relating to mild mental or emotional disorders; excessively anxious, obsessive, or emotionally unstable.
emotionally unstable or anxious (psychological)

Examples

Her neurotic tendencies made her worry about every little detail.
He became increasingly neurotic after months of constant stress at work.
The character in the novel was portrayed as deeply neurotic and unable to cope with daily life.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: psychotic - relating to or affected by psychosis

Etymology

From Greek neūron (nerve) and -otic, after neurosis.

WordNet ID: oewn-01587448-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ njʊˈrɑtɪk / (AmE), / njʊəˈrɒtɪk / (BrE)
Showing symptoms of neurosis, or having an abnormally sensitive, obsessive, or anxious personality.
relating to or affected by neurosis
medical

Examples

His neurotic behavior made it difficult for him to relax in social situations.
She became increasingly neurotic about the cleanliness of her apartment.
The counselor explained that neurotic symptoms were common under extreme stress.

Synset Examples

neurotic disorder
neurotic symptoms

Related Terms

  • Antonym: psychotic - relating to or affected by psychosis
  • Synonym: anxious - experiencing worry, unease, or nervousness

Etymology

From Greek neûron (nerve) + -otic (relating to), via modern Latin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02988070-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ nʊˈrɑːdɪk / (AmE), / njʊəˈrɒtɪk / (BrE)
a person who suffers from neurosis, exhibiting excessive anxiety or emotional instability
person suffering from neurosis
medical

Examples

The hospital had a separate ward for neurotics and those with more severe conditions.
As a neurotic, he often worried excessively about minor problems.
Some neurotics respond well to therapy and medication.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: psychotic - a person suffering from psychosis

Etymology

From Greek 'neuron' (nerve) + modern suffix '-otic'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10374597-n
Synset POS: n
