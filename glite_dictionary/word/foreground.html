foreground

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌɡraʊnd / (AmE), / ˈfɔːɡraʊnd / (BrE)
the part of a scene or picture that is closest to the viewer
part of a scene nearest to the viewer

Examples

The tree in the foreground gives the photo a sense of depth.
Several children played in the foreground while the mountains loomed in the background.
Bright flowers dominated the foreground of the painting.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: background - the part of a scene or picture that is farthest from the viewer

Etymology

Formed from 'fore-' (ahead) and 'ground,' first used in art and painting contexts in the 17th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌɡraʊnd / (AmE), / ˈfɔːɡraʊnd / (BrE)
(computing) The window or application currently active and in focus on a computer screen.
active window or application (computing)

Examples

Make sure the browser is in the foreground before you start the meeting.
If you click on the icon, that program will move to the foreground.
The chat window popped up in the foreground, covering my document.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: background - (computing) the state or place where an application is running but not currently active or visible
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03387554-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈfɔrˌɡraʊnd / (AmE), / ˈfɔːɡraʊnd / (BrE)
to make something more prominent, noticeable, or important in attention or consideration
make prominent or noticeable

Examples

The exhibition foregrounds the work of female artists from the twentieth century.
The report foregrounded the urgent need for environmental reform.
In her analysis, she foregrounds social and cultural influences on the text.

Synset Examples

The introduction highlighted the speaker's distinguished career in linguistics

Related Terms

  • Synonym: highlight - to emphasize something as particularly important or prominent
  • Antonym: obscure - to make less visible or less prominent

Etymology

From 'foreground' (noun), itself from fore- (before) + ground; used as a verb since late 20th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
