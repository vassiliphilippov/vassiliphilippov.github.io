ruddle

1. NOUN

/ ˈrʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˈrʌd.əl / (BrE)
a red iron ore, used especially for dyeing and marking surfaces such as sheep or stone
red iron ore for dyeing or marking

Examples

The farmers used ruddle to mark their sheep during the sorting process.
Traces of ruddle were found on the ancient cave walls, suggesting it was used for painting.
Miners extracted ruddle to supply local dyers and craftspeople.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: red ochre - a natural earth containing ferric oxide, often used as a pigment

Etymology

From Middle English *rudel*, related to 'rud' meaning red.

2. VERB

/ ˈrʌdəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌdəl / (BrE)
to color or redden something with, or as if with, red ochre
to color with red ochre

Examples

The shepherd would ruddle his sheep to identify them in the flock.
Centuries ago, artists would ruddle cave walls with vivid imagery.
Her cheeks seemed to ruddle with excitement on the brisk winter morning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to redden - to make or become red or redder
  • Antonym: to blanch - to make white or pale

Etymology

From Middle English *ruddel*, from *rud* (redness, red ochre).

3. VERB

/ ˈrʌdəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌdəl / (BrE)
to twist or braid together; to interlace
twist or braid together

Examples

She carefully ruddled the threads to create a colorful bracelet.
The basket was ruddled from thin strips of willow.
You must ruddle the wires together for a stronger connection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intertwine - to twist together or be twisted together
  • Synonym: braid - to weave together three or more strands
  • Antonym: to untangle - to separate things that have been twisted together
