to consubstantiate

1. VERB

/ ˌkɑːnsəbˈstænʃiˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˌkɒnsəbˈstænʃieɪt / (BrE)
to unite into one common substance; to combine or embody two or more substances into one
unite into one common substance

Examples

Some philosophers argue that mind and matter can consubstantiate to form conscious experience.
The doctrine claims that the divine nature will consubstantiate with the elements during the ritual.
In his writings, he proposed that spirit and body consubstantiate in human existence.

Synset Examples

Thought is consubstantiated with the object

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to unite - to come or bring together to form a single entity
  • Antonym: separate - to move or be apart; to divide into different parts

Etymology

From Medieval Latin consubstantiare, from Latin con- (together) + substantia (substance).

2. VERB

/ ˌkɑːnsəbˈstænʃiˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˌkɒnsəbˈstænʃieɪt / (BrE)
to become united in substance; to make or regard as of the same substance or essence, especially in philosophical or theological contexts.
become united in substance

Examples

In some philosophical discussions, the mind and world are said to consubstantiate through perception.
The two theories consubstantiate into a single coherent framework when examined together.
Over centuries, doctrines about the divine nature have claimed that Christ's human and divine natures consubstantiate.

Synset Examples

thought and the object consubstantiate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to unite - to come or bring together for a common purpose or action
  • Antonym: separate - to move or be apart; to stop being united

Etymology

From Late Latin consubstantiare, from Latin con- (together) + substantia (substance).
