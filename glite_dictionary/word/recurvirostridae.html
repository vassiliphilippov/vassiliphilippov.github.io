Recurvirostridae

1. NOUN

/ riˌkɜːrvəˈrɑstrɪˌdeɪ / (AmE), / rɪˌkɜːvɪˈrɒstrɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of long-legged wading birds, including stilts and avocets, known for their slender bills and habitat in marshes and shorelines.
family of wading birds (stilts and avocets)

Examples

The Recurvirostridae family includes both stilts and avocets, found on every continent except Antarctica.
Birdwatchers were excited to spot several members of the Recurvirostridae wading in the coastal marsh.
Characteristics of Recurvirostridae include long legs and thin, upturned bills ideal for shallow water feeding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stilt - a long-legged wading bird of the genus Himantopus
  • Synonym: avocet - a slender, long-legged wading bird with an upcurved bill

Etymology

From Latin *recurvus* (bent backwards) + *rostrum* (beak), referring to the upcurved bills of some species.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˌkɜrvəˈrɒstrɪdiː / (AmE), / rɪˌkɜːvɪˈrɒstrɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of long-legged shorebirds that includes avocets and stilts, characterized by slender upward-curving bills and habit of wading in shallow waters.
family of long-legged shorebirds

Examples

Scientists conducted a study on the feeding habits of Recurvirostridae found in the wetlands.
The Recurvirostridae are easily recognized by their long legs and distinctive upturned bills.
Many conservation efforts focus on protecting habitats crucial for Recurvirostridae populations worldwide.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining Latin 'recurvus' (bent backward) and 'rostrum' (beak), referring to the shape of the birds' bills.
