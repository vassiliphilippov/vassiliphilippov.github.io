to calcify

1. VERB

/ ˈkæl.səˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈkælsɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to become rigid or inflexible in attitude, ideas, or structure; to stop changing or adapting
become rigid or unchanging

Examples

Over time, organizational procedures can calcify and resist necessary reforms.
When debate is discouraged, opinions tend to calcify into dogma.
If leaders become too comfortable, their thinking may calcify and hinder innovation.

Synset Examples

Old folks can calcify

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ossify - become rigid or fixed in attitude or position
  • Synonym: to harden - to become or make less flexible or more inflexible
  • Antonym: to soften - to become less rigid or more adaptable

Etymology

From Latin calcificare, from calx (lime, limestone) + facere (to make).

2. VERB

/ ˈkælsɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈkælsɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to become or cause to become hard by the deposition of calcium salts; to turn into calcium carbonate or a similar compound, typically becoming stony or rigid
harden by calcium deposits

Examples

After years of mineral deposits, the pipes began to calcify and block the water flow.
Arthritic joints can calcify over time, reducing their flexibility.
If bones do not calcify properly, it can indicate a metabolic disorder.

Synset Examples

The rock calcified over the centuries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ossify - to turn into bone or become bony

Etymology

From Latin 'calx' (lime, limestone) + '-ify' (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00499743-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkælsɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈkælsɪfaɪ / (BrE)
(of tissue or other matter) to become hardened or impregnated with calcium salts.
become hardened with calcium salts
medical

Examples

Over time, the cartilage in the joint can calcify and lose its flexibility.
If the arteries calcify, blood flow may become restricted, increasing the risk of heart disease.
Certain marine organisms secrete substances that allow them to calcify their shells.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to harden - to become or make hard or harder

Etymology

From Latin 'calx' (lime, limestone) + '-ify' (to make).

/ ˈkælsəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈkælsɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to turn or be turned into calcium salts or lime, making something hard or stony
make or become hard with calcium or lime

Examples

Mineral deposits can calcify the pipes, reducing water flow over time.
Over many years, the remains began to calcify in the soil.
If the mixture is left exposed, it will calcify into a hard mass.
