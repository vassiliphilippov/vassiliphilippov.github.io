precursor

1. NOUN

/ priˈkɝː.sɚ / (AmE), / prɪˈkɜː.sə / (BrE)
a person or thing that comes before another of the same kind, especially as a forerunner or announcer
person or thing that comes before another

Examples

The telephone was a precursor to modern communication technologies.
In ancient cultures, a priest often acted as a precursor to important events.
The steam engine was a significant precursor of the Industrial Revolution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forerunner - a person or thing that precedes the coming or development of someone or something else
  • Antonym: successor - a person or thing that succeeds another

Etymology

From Latin praecursor (forerunner), from praecurrere 'to run before'.

2. NOUN

/ priˈkɝːsər / (AmE), / prɪˈkɜːsə / (BrE)
a person or thing that comes before another of the same kind; a forerunner or sign that indicates the arrival of something
thing that comes before, forerunner

Examples

The invention of the telegraph was a significant precursor to modern communication systems.
Gray clouds are often a precursor to a heavy rainstorm.
Some scientists consider certain chemical compounds as the precursor of today's advanced medicines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forerunner - a person or thing that precedes the coming or development of someone or something else
  • Antonym: successor - a person or thing that succeeds another

Etymology

From Latin 'praecursor' (forerunner), from 'prae-' (before) + 'currere' (to run).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ priˈkɝːsər / (AmE), / prɪˈkɜːsə / (BrE)
a substance from which another is formed, especially by a metabolic or chemical reaction
substance from which another is formed
medical

Examples

Cholesterol is a precursor of several important hormones in the body.
Glucose acts as a precursor in the synthesis of many cellular compounds.
The scientist identified the precursor that leads to this vital enzyme.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: product - a substance produced from another in a chemical reaction

Etymology

From Latin praecursor, from praecurrere (to run before).
