Chlorophyta

1. NOUN

/ kloʊˈrɑːfəˌtə / (AmE), / klɔːˈrɒfɪtə / (BrE)
a large division of primarily freshwater algae, commonly known as green algae, that contain chlorophyll a and b, store food as starch, and have cellulose cell walls; includes classes such as Chlorophyceae, Ulvophyceae, and Charophyceae, and are considered ancestral to land plants
a division of green algae

Examples

Chlorophyta are known for their distinctive bright green color due to the presence of chlorophyll a and b.
Scientists believe that land plants evolved from ancient forms of Chlorophyta.
Many freshwater ponds and lakes are home to various species belonging to Chlorophyta.

Etymology

From Greek 'khlōros' (green) + 'phyton' (plant).

2. NOUN

/ klɔːˈrɑːfɪtə / (AmE), / klɔːˈrɒfɪtə / (BrE)
A large division of mostly freshwater, eukaryotic green algae that contain chlorophyll a and b, store food as starch, and have cellulose cell walls. They are considered ancestral to land plants.
division of green algae

Examples

Many species in the Chlorophyta play an important role in freshwater ecosystems.
Scientists believe that land plants evolved from ancestral Chlorophyta.
Common forms of Chlorophyta include green algae found in ponds and lakes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: green algae - photosynthetic algae of the division Chlorophyta
  • Antonym: Rhodophyta - division of red algae, related group of algae distinguished from Chlorophyta

Etymology

From Greek 'chloros' (green) + 'phyton' (plant).
