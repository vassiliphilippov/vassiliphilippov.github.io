upstage

1. NOUN

/ ˈʌpˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈʌpsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
the area of a stage that is farthest from the audience
the rear part of a stage

Examples

The director asked the actors to move upstage during the dramatic scene.
A beautiful backdrop was set at the upstage to create a sense of depth.
She quietly exited upstage as the lights dimmed.
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04522925-n
Synset POS: n

2. ADJ

/ ˈʌpˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈʌpsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
(in theatre) situated at or toward the back part of the stage, away from the audience.
at or toward the back of a stage

Examples

The actor quickly moved upstage to avoid drawing attention during the main character’s speech.
Props that are placed upstage are less visible to the audience.
He took an upstage position during the scene to create a sense of distance.

Synset Examples

she crossed to the upstage chair forcing the lead to turn his back to the audience

Related Terms

  • Antonym: downstage - at or toward the front of the stage, nearest the audience

Etymology

From theatrical terminology: 'up' (toward the rear) + 'stage', historically linked to raked (sloped) stages.

4. VERB

/ ˈʌpˌsteɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈʌpsteɪdʒ / (BrE)
(theatre) To move towards the rear of the stage, causing other actors to turn away from the audience.
move to the rear of the stage (theatre)

Examples

During rehearsal, the director asked Mark to upstage so the others would face the audience.
If you upstage your fellow actors, it can disrupt the entire scene's flow.
She subtly upstaged him, causing him to turn his back to the audience.

Etymology

From theatrical locational terms: 'upstage' (the area of a stage farthest from the audience).
