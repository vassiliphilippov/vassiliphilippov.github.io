to enforce

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɔrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈfɔːs / (BrE)
to make sure that people obey a law, rule, or obligation by compelling them to comply
make people obey a law or rule

Examples

The school decided to enforce a strict dress code to maintain discipline.
Police officers are responsible for enforcing the laws in the community.
If rules are not enforced, people may ignore them entirely.

Synset Examples

Social relations impose courtesy
duty constrains one to act often contrary to one's desires or inclinations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compel - force someone to do something
  • Synonym: to oblige - require someone to do something by law or moral pressure
  • Antonym: to allow - permit or let happen, the opposite of enforcing

Etymology

From Old French *enforcier*, from Late Latin *inforcāre* (to strengthen, enforce).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɔrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈfɔːs / (BrE)
to make sure that laws or rules are obeyed by using authority or force
compel observance of laws or rules

Examples

The government has a duty to enforce the new regulations strictly.
Police are often called to enforce the law when disputes arise.
It is impossible to enforce every minor traffic rule all the time.

Synset Examples

Apply the rules to everyone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: implement - to put something (such as a plan or law) into effect
  • Antonym: overlook - to fail to enforce or notice rules or laws

Etymology

From Old French 'enforcier', from Latin 'infortiare', from 'in-' (in) + 'fortis' (strong).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02565990-v
Synset POS: v
