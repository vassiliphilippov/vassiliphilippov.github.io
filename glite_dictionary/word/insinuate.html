to insinuate

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈsɪn.ju.eɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɪn.ju.eɪt / (BrE)
to introduce or insert oneself or something into a situation or group in a subtle, often indirect way
introduce oneself subtly

Examples

He managed to insinuate himself into the exclusive club despite not being invited.
The undercover agent tried to insinuate himself into the organization to learn more about their activities.
By volunteering for small tasks, she began to insinuate herself into the leader’s inner circle.

Synset Examples

He insinuated himself into the conversation of the people at the nearby table

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else
  • Synonym: to intrude - to enter without permission or welcome

Etymology

From Latin insinuare (to wind in, creep in, make one's way into); in- (in) + sinus (curve, fold).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈsɪn.ju.eɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɪn.jʊ.eɪt / (BrE)
to suggest or hint at something in an indirect or unpleasant way
suggest or hint indirectly

Examples

Are you trying to insinuate that I was at fault for the mistake?
She insinuated her doubts about his loyalty without saying it outright.
He kept insinuating that my report was inaccurate, though he never said so directly.

Synset Examples

I insinuated that I did not like his wife

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imply - to suggest something without stating it directly
  • Synonym: to suggest - to mention an idea, possible plan, or action for other people to consider
  • Antonym: state - to express something directly or clearly

Etymology

From Latin 'insinuare' (to introduce into a curve, to creep in), from 'in-' (into) and 'sinuare' (to bend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00929682-v
Synset POS: v
