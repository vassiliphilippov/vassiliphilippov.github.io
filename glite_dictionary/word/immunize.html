to immunize

1. VERB

/ ˈɪmjəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɪmjʊnaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something resistant to a particular disease, especially by giving them a vaccine
make resistant to a disease by vaccination
medical

Examples

Doctors recommend that parents immunize their children against measles and other preventable diseases.
The campaign aims to immunize millions of people in rural areas each year.
Before traveling abroad, she decided to immunize herself against yellow fever.

Synset Examples

We vaccinate against scarlet fever
The nurse vaccinated the children in the school

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vaccinate - to administer a vaccine to protect against disease
  • Antonym: to infect - to affect with a disease-causing microorganism

Etymology

From French *immuniser*, from Latin *immunis* (exempt, free from) + -ize.

2. VERB

/ ˈɪmjəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɪmjʊnaɪz / (BrE)
(in law) to officially grant legal immunity from prosecution to a person, typically in exchange for testimony or cooperation.
grant legal immunity from prosecution
legal

Examples

The witness was immunized by the prosecution in exchange for his testimony.
Authorities may immunize minor offenders to build a case against higher-level suspects.
The attorney negotiated to have his client immunized before giving any statements.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to prosecute - to institute legal proceedings against someone

Etymology

From Latin immunis (exempt from public service or charge), via French immuniser.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01131204-v
Synset POS: v
