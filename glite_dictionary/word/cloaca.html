cloaca

1. NOUN

/ kloʊˈeɪkə / (AmE), / kləʊˈeɪkə / (BrE)
A waste pipe or conduit for carrying away sewage or surface water, especially in historical contexts.
waste pipe for sewage or water

Examples

The ancient Romans constructed a large cloaca to divert wastewater from the city.
Archaeologists found the remains of a cloaca beneath the ruins of the old settlement.
Regular maintenance of the cloaca was essential to prevent flooding and disease in medieval towns.

Etymology

From Latin *cloaca* (sewer, drain).

2. NOUN

/ kloʊˈeɪkə / (AmE), / kləʊˈeɪkə / (BrE)
(in zoology) a cavity at the end of the digestive tract in birds, reptiles, amphibians, most fish, and monotremes, into which the intestinal, genital, and urinary tracts open.
common exit cavity in many animals

Examples

In birds, waste and eggs both leave the body through the cloaca.
The anatomy lab included a demonstration of a frog's cloaca for the students.
Unlike other mammals, monotremes possess a cloaca instead of separate openings.

Etymology

From Latin 'cloaca' meaning 'sewer, drain'.
