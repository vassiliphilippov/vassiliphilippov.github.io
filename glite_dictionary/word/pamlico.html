Pamlico

1. NOUN

/ ˈpæmlɪkoʊ / (AmE), / ˈpæmlɪkəʊ / (BrE)
the Algonquian language spoken by the Pamlico people, now extinct.
extinct Algonquian language

Examples

Linguists have studied Pamlico to better understand the history of Algonquian languages.
Few written records of Pamlico remain after the language became extinct in the 18th century.
The tribe's oral traditions and their use of Pamlico have greatly influenced regional history.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Algonquian - a family of indigenous languages spoken in North America

Etymology

From the name of the Pamlico people of North Carolina.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpæm.lɪˌkoʊ / (AmE), / ˈpæm.lɪ.kəʊ / (BrE)
a member of the Algonquian people who formerly lived in the valley of the Pamlico River in North Carolina, USA
member of an Algonquian people of North Carolina

Examples

Archaeologists have uncovered pottery made by the Pamlico in eastern North Carolina.
The Pamlico were known for their fishing and farming along the river valley.
Throughout the 18th century, the Pamlico faced displacement from their ancestral lands.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Algonquian - relating to a group of Native American peoples and languages in North America

Etymology

From the name of the Pamlico River in North Carolina, itself possibly from an Algonquian term for the people.
