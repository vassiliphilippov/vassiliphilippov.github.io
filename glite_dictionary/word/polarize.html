to polarize

1. VERB

/ ˈpoʊ.ləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊ.ləˌraɪz / (BrE)
To cause or become divided into two sharply opposing factions or viewpoints, especially in a conflict or debate.
become divided into opposing groups

Examples

The debate over climate change has begun to polarize public opinion.
Political issues can quickly polarize communities, making compromise difficult.
Social media tends to polarize people by exposing them mainly to similar viewpoints.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unify - bring together or make into a single group

Etymology

From French 'polariser', based on 'pôle' (pole), referring to opposite ends.

2. VERB

/ ˈpoʊləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊləraɪz / (BrE)
to cause people or opinions to divide into two sharply contrasting groups or sets of beliefs
divide into opposing groups or viewpoints

Examples

Her controversial statements began to polarize the community, forcing people to take sides.
The debate over climate change continues to polarize scientists and politicians alike.
Social media can polarize public opinion by amplifying extreme viewpoints.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unify - to bring together into a single group or whole

Etymology

From French polariser, from pôle (pole), referring to polarity or two extremes.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00488286-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈpoʊləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊləˌraɪz / (BrE)
to cause (light waves or other types of waves) to vibrate in a specific direction or pattern
make light or waves vibrate in a particular pattern

Examples

Special filters can polarize light to reduce glare from surfaces such as water or glass.
Scientists polarize laser beams in their experiments for greater accuracy.
This lens polarizes the incoming light, making it easier for photographers to get clear images.

Synset Examples

polarize light waves

Etymology

From French 'polariser', from 'polaire' (polar) + '-iser' (-ize).
