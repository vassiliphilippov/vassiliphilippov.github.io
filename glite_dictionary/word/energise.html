to energise

1. VERB

/ ˈɛnərˌdʒaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɛnədʒaɪz / (BrE)
to cause something to be raised to a higher energy level, especially in a physical or chemical process
raise to a higher energy level

Examples

Adding heat will energise the molecules, causing them to move more rapidly.
Scientists use lasers to energise electrons and observe their reactions.
Microwave radiation can energise water molecules, making them vibrate faster.

Synset Examples

excite the atoms

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to excite - to increase the energy of atoms or molecules
  • Antonym: to de-energise - to remove energy or reduce to a lower energy state

Etymology

From Greek 'energein' (to be in action), via French and Late Latin.

2. VERB

/ ˈɛnərˌdʒaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɛnəˌdʒaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone feel full of energy or more alert and active; to give energy to something
make energetic or give energy to

Examples

A brisk morning walk always energises me for the day ahead.
The coach's inspiring speech energised the whole team before the match.
Drinking fresh orange juice in the morning energises many people.

Synset Examples

Coffee and tea stimulate me
This herbal infusion doesn't stimulate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stimulate - to encourage something to become more active; to make more alert
  • Synonym: to invigorate - to give strength or energy to; to refresh
  • Antonym: to weaken - to make less strong or less energetic

Etymology

From Greek 'energos' (active) via French 'énergiser' and English 'energy'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00022679-v
Synset POS: v
