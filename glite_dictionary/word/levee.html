levee

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvi / (AmE), / ˈlɛvi / (BrE)
an embankment constructed alongside a river to prevent it from overflowing its banks
embankment to prevent river flooding

Examples

The city built a new levee to protect neighborhoods from seasonal flooding.
During the storm, water threatened to breach the levee along the riverbank.
After heavy rains, engineers inspected the levee for any signs of weakness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dyke - an embankment built to prevent flooding, especially in the Netherlands
  • Synonym: embankment - a wall or bank of earth or stone built to prevent a river flooding an area
  • Synonym: dam - a barrier constructed to hold back water and raise its level

Etymology

From French *levée* (raised), from Old French *lever* (to raise).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvi / (AmE), / ˈlɛvi / (BrE)
a quay or embankment built along a river, serving as a landing place for boats or ships
embankment or quay along a river

Examples

The old city built a stone levee to allow ferries to dock along the riverbank.
Fishermen gathered at the wooden levee to unload their catch at sunrise.
A steamboat was moored at the levee, waiting for passengers to board.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quay - a platform lying alongside or projecting into water for loading and unloading ships
  • Synonym: dock - an enclosed area of water for loading or unloading ships

Etymology

From French 'levée', from Old French 'lever' (to raise), referring to the raised embankment.

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvi / (AmE), / ˈlɛvi / (BrE)
a formal reception or assembly of visitors or guests, especially at a royal court or by a person of high rank
formal reception or assembly (royal court)
archaic

Examples

The ambassador was received at the royal levee with great ceremony.
During Queen Victoria's reign, the annual levee was an important social event.
He dressed in formal attire to attend the spring levee at the governor's residence.

Etymology

From French 'levée', literally 'rising', originally referring to a monarch's morning reception.
