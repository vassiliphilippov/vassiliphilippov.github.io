to colonize

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.ləˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.əˌnaɪz / (BrE)
To establish control over an area and settle it as a colony, often by sending people from a parent country.
establish a colony in a place

Examples

The British attempted to colonize many regions during the era of imperial expansion.
Several countries competed to colonize distant islands for their strategic value.
By the 18th century, European powers had colonized large parts of Africa and Asia.

Synset Examples

Europeans colonized Africa in the 17th century

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to establish residence in a new place
  • Antonym: to decolonize - to withdraw from a colony, leaving it independent

Etymology

From Latin 'colonia' (settlement, farm), via French 'coloniser'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.ləˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ə.naɪz / (BrE)
to settle among and establish political control over the indigenous people of an area; to establish a colony in or on (a place)
to establish a colony in a place

Examples

European powers raced to colonize vast areas of Africa during the 19th century.
Some species of plants quickly colonize vacant lots after a fire.
The Portuguese were among the first to colonize parts of South America.

Synset Examples

The British colonized the East Coast

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to establish a community in a new place
  • Antonym: to decolonize - to withdraw from a colony, leaving it independent

Etymology

From Latin colonizare, from colonia (colony).
