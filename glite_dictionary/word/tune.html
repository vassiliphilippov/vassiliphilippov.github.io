tune

1. VERB

/ tun / (AmE), / tjuːn / (BrE)
to adjust the pitch of a musical instrument so that it produces the correct notes
adjust the pitch of an instrument

Examples

He called a professional to tune his violin before the concert.
You should tune your guitar regularly to keep it sounding good.
She patiently tuned the piano until every note was in harmony.

Synset Examples

My piano needs to be tuned

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retune - to adjust the pitch of an instrument again

Etymology

From Old English *tune* (tone, song) via Old French *ton* and Latin *tonus* (tone).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00296127-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ tuːn / (AmE), / tjuːn / (BrE)
the process of adjusting a radio receiver or other electronic circuit to a particular frequency for optimal operation
adjustment to a required frequency

Examples

The tune of the radio was slightly off, making the signal unclear.
He carefully adjusted the tune on his receiver to listen to his favorite station.
For better reception, you need to check the tune of your device.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tuning - the act of adjusting an instrument or device for proper functioning
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01002785-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ tuːn / (AmE), / tjuːn / (BrE)
A sequence of musical notes that are played or sung in a particular order to form a recognizable melody.
a recognizable melody or musical sequence

Examples

She kept humming a cheerful tune all morning while she worked.
The children quickly learned the catchy tune of the new song.
I can't get that silly tune out of my head.

Synset Examples

she was humming an air from Beethoven

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melody - a sequence of single notes that is musically satisfying
  • Synonym: song - a short piece of music with words
  • Antonym: harmony - the combination of simultaneously sounded musical notes to produce chords

Etymology

From Old French 'ton' (tone), from Latin 'tonus' (a sound, pitch, accent).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07041860-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ tun / (AmE), / tjuːn / (BrE)
to adjust (a machine, system, or device) so that it works more efficiently or performs as desired
adjust for better functioning

Examples

The mechanic will tune your car to improve its fuel efficiency.
Technicians regularly tune the broadcasting equipment to ensure clear transmission.
You may need to tune the air conditioner for optimal performance in hot weather.

Synset Examples

tune the engine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adjust - to change something slightly to make it work better
  • Synonym: to calibrate - to set or correct the measurements of an instrument

Etymology

From Old French 'toner' (to tune, sound) and Latin 'tonus' (tone).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00296747-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ tun / (AmE), / tjuːn / (BrE)
the state or quality of producing the correct musical pitch; the accuracy of musical notes as performed or produced
accuracy of musical pitch

Examples

After tuning her violin, she played beautifully and every note was perfectly in tune.
If a guitar string is loose, it won't produce a sound that is in tune.
The choir struggled to stay in tune during the rehearsal.

Synset Examples

he cannot sing in tune
the clarinet was out of tune

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pitch - the quality of a sound governed by the rate of vibrations producing it; the degree of highness or lowness of a tone

Etymology

From Old French *ton* (tone, musical sound), from Latin *tonus* (tone).
