rumination

1. NOUN

/ ˌruːməˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌruːmɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
A medical condition in which small amounts of food are regurgitated, commonly seen in some infants after feeding.
regurgitation of food (medical)
medical

Examples

The pediatrician diagnosed the baby's frequent regurgitation as rumination syndrome.
After feeding, the infant exhibited signs of rumination, prompting concern from the caregiver.
Rumination is often mistaken for simple spit-up, but it is a more persistent condition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: regurgitation - the act of bringing swallowed food up again into the mouth

Etymology

From Latin 'ruminatio', from 'ruminare' (to chew cud).

2. NOUN

/ ˌruːməˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌruːmɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which ruminant animals chew previously swallowed food (cud) to aid digestion
chewing of cud by ruminants

Examples

Cows spend several hours each day in rumination to properly digest their food.
The veterinarian observed the sheep’s rumination for any signs of illness.
Decreased rumination in cattle can be an early indicator of digestive problems.

Synset Examples

ruminants have remarkable powers of rumination

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chewing - the act of using the teeth to break up food

Etymology

From Latin 'ruminatio', from 'ruminare' (to chew the cud).

3. NOUN

/ ˌruːməˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌruːmɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
Careful, prolonged, and thoughtful consideration of something.
lengthy, thoughtful consideration

Examples

Her rumination on the problem led to a creative solution.
Long periods of rumination can sometimes make it harder to reach a decision.
During a quiet afternoon, he found himself in deep rumination about his future.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contemplation - deep reflective thought
  • Synonym: reflection - serious thought or consideration

Etymology

From Latin 'ruminatio', from 'ruminari' (to chew cud, muse).
