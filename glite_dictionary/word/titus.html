Titus

1. PROPN

/ ˈtaɪtəs / (AmE), / ˈtaɪtəs / (BrE)
(c. 39–81 AD) Roman emperor from 79 to 81, son of Vespasian, known for completing the Colosseum and for his short but popular reign.
Roman emperor (79–81 AD)

Examples

Titus became emperor of Rome after the death of his father, Vespasian.
The Arch of Titus still stands in Rome as a monument to his victories.
During the reign of Titus, the Colosseum was completed and inaugurated.

Etymology

From Latin 'Titus', an ancient Roman given name.

2. PROPN

/ ˈtaɪtəs / (AmE), / ˈtaɪtəs / (BrE)
A book of the New Testament consisting of Saint Paul's letter to Titus, offering advice on pastoral duties and church leadership.
book of the New Testament; epistle to Titus

Examples

Many churches study the teachings in Titus as a guide for leadership and community life.
The pastor referenced Titus in his sermon about responsibility and faith.
Titus is often cited for its instructions on appointing elders in the early church.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Epistle - A letter, especially a formal or didactic one, often used for the books of the New Testament.

Etymology

From Latin 'Titus', the name of the recipient of the epistle.

3. PROPN

/ ˈtaɪ.təs / (AmE), / ˈtaɪ.təs / (BrE)
(39–81 AD) Emperor of Rome, son of Vespasian, who ruled from 79 to 81 AD and oversaw the completion of the Colosseum.
Roman emperor, son of Vespasian

Examples

Titus completed the construction of the Colosseum during his reign as emperor.
Many historians praise Titus for his efforts in disaster relief after the eruption of Mount Vesuvius.
The rule of Titus is remembered as a period of generosity and public works in Ancient Rome.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Vespasian - Roman emperor, father of Titus
  • Synonym: Domitian - Roman emperor, brother and successor of Titus

Etymology

From Latin Titus, a Roman given name.
