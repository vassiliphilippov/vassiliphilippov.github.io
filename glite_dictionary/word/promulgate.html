to promulgate

1. VERB

/ ˈprɑːməlˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɒməlɡeɪt / (BrE)
to put a law or decree into effect by formal proclamation or declaration
formally put a law into effect
legal

Examples

The government will promulgate the new regulations next month.
After the bill passed, Congress moved quickly to promulgate it into law.
The Supreme Court can review rules before agencies promulgate them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enact - to make a bill into law

Etymology

From Latin promulgare (to make publicly known).

WordNet ID: oewn-00863224-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈprɑːməlˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɒməlˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to make an official announcement or formally declare, especially of a law, decree, or doctrine
formally announce or declare

Examples

The government will promulgate the new law next week after it passes parliament.
Throughout history, leaders have promulgated decrees to maintain social order.
The university is promulgating new guidelines to improve campus safety.

Synset Examples

‘I am not a Communist,’ he exclaimed
The King will proclaim an amnesty

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to proclaim - to announce officially or publicly
  • Synonym: to declare - to announce something clearly, firmly, publicly, or officially

Etymology

From Latin promulgare (to publish, proclaim).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00979368-v
Synset POS: v
