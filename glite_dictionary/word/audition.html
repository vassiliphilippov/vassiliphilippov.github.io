audition

1. NOUN

/ əˈdɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ɔːˈdɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
a short performance given to demonstrate a performer's suitability for a role or job, especially in music, theatre, or film
test performance for a role or job

Examples

She was nervous before her audition for the lead role in the play.
Hundreds of dancers attended the audition, hoping to be selected for the show.
His impressive audition earned him a spot in the orchestra.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tryout - an act of testing a person's suitability for something

Etymology

From Latin 'auditio' (a hearing), via Old French and late Middle English.

2. NOUN

/ ɑːˈdɪʃən / (AmE), / ɔːˈdɪʃən / (BrE)
the sense or ability of hearing; the faculty by which sound is perceived
ability or faculty of hearing
medical

Examples

Damage to the auditory nerve can affect a person's audition.
Scientists are studying how audition develops in newborn babies.
Normal audition is essential for language learning in children.

Synset Examples

his hearing was impaired

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hearing - the sense by which sound is perceived
  • Antonym: deafness - lack or absence of the sense of hearing

Etymology

From Latin 'auditio' (a hearing), from 'audire' (to hear).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05665563-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ɔˈdɪʃən / (AmE), / ɔːˈdɪʃən / (BrE)
to perform or try out in order to obtain a role as an actor, singer, musician, etc.
perform to compete for a role

Examples

Hundreds of actors auditioned for the lead role in the new film.
She is auditioning for several theater companies this summer.
He decided to audition even though he had little experience on stage.

Synset Examples

She auditioned for a role on Broadway

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to try out - to sample or test something, especially one's skill for a position

Etymology

From Latin 'auditio' (a hearing), via French 'audition'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
