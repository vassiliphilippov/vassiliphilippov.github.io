snorter

1. NOUN

/ ˈsnɔrtər / (AmE), / ˈsnɔːtə / (BrE)
an informal term for something that is exceptionally difficult or challenging
something exceptionally difficult or challenging (informal)
informal

Examples

That final math question was a real snorter and took everyone by surprise.
The interview threw me a snorter I hadn't prepared for.
Solving the puzzle was a snorter, but she didn't give up.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stumper - a problem or question that is hard to solve
  • Antonym: pushover - something or someone that is easily overcome or defeated

2. NOUN

/ ˈsnɔrtər / (AmE), / ˈsnɔːtə / (BrE)
a person who expresses contempt or indignation by making a snorting sound
person who snorts contemptuously

Examples

As soon as she heard the ridiculous suggestion, the snorter made her feelings clear with a loud, derisive sound.
In the meeting, the snorter could barely contain his disdain whenever someone disagreed with him.
The play's villain was written as a perpetual snorter, reacting to every minor slight with an audible snort.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snort - to make a sudden explosive sound through the nose, often expressing contempt or indignation

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'snort' (to make a sudden explosive sound through the nose), with agent noun suffix '-er'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsnɔrɾɚ / (AmE), / ˈsnɔːtə / (BrE)
something that is particularly remarkable, impressive, or notable, especially in its intensity or effect
something remarkable or impressive
informal american

Examples

The novel's final chapter was a real snorter, keeping me on the edge of my seat.
That was a snorter of a game, with both teams refusing to give up.
A sudden snorter of a thunderstorm sent everyone running for cover.

Synset Examples

a snorter of a sermon
the storm wasn't long but it was a snorter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whopper - something unusually large or remarkable
  • Antonym: dud - something that fails to work or is disappointing

Etymology

From the verb 'snort,' likely by analogy to something that takes one's breath away; colloquial American usage from the 19th century.
