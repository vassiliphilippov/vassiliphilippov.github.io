sail

1. NOUN

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
a journey by boat or ship taken for pleasure or recreation, especially on the sea
pleasure trip by boat or ship

Examples

They went for a sail around the island to celebrate their anniversary.
A leisurely sail on the bay was just what we needed to relax.
The couple enjoyed an afternoon sail with friends on their yacht.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cruise - a voyage for pleasure, usually with stops at various ports
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00313758-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
a large piece of strong fabric attached to a vessel and used to catch the wind to propel it forward
fabric used to propel a vessel using wind

Examples

The crew hoisted the sail before leaving the harbor.
A sudden gust of wind filled the sail and pushed the boat forward.
The old sail was torn and needed to be replaced.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mast - a tall upright post on a boat that sails are attached to
  • Antonym: oar - a tool used for rowing a boat (antonym, method of propulsion not using wind)

Etymology

From Old English *segl*, related to Old Norse *segl* and Dutch *zeil*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04135005-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
to travel on water in a boat or ship, especially one propelled by wind in sails
travel on water by wind power

Examples

The crew learned how to sail before embarking on their journey.
We watched the boats sail into the harbor at sunset.

Synset Examples

  • Antonym: row - to propel a boat forward using oars

From Old English *seġlan*, related to 'sail' (noun), influenced by Germanic roots.

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
any structure or object that resembles a sail in shape or function, especially one used to catch or be moved by the wind or air.

Examples
