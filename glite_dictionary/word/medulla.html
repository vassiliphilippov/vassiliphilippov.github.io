medulla

1. NOUN

/ məˈdʌlə / (AmE), / mɪˈdʌlə / (BrE)
the lower part of the brainstem, continuous with the spinal cord, responsible for regulating vital functions such as breathing and heartbeat
lower part of the brainstem
medical

Examples

Damage to the medulla can result in the loss of basic bodily functions such as breathing.
The medulla controls involuntary activities like heartbeat and blood pressure.
A stroke affecting the medulla may have life-threatening consequences.

Synset Examples

the medulla oblongata is the most vital part of the brain because it contains centers controlling breathing and heart functioning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: medulla oblongata - full term for the medulla; the lower part of the brainstem

Etymology

From Latin 'medulla' meaning 'marrow, pith'.

2. NOUN

/ məˈdʌlə / (AmE), / mɪˈdʌlə / (BrE)
a white fatty substance that forms a protective sheath (medullary sheath) around the central part of some nerve fibers
fatty sheath of nerve fibers
medical

Examples

Damage to the medulla can disrupt nerve signal transmission in the body.
The medulla is essential for the rapid conduction of electrical impulses along nerve cells.
Scientists studied the role of the medulla in neurological disorders such as multiple sclerosis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: myelin - the insulating layer, or sheath, that forms around nerves

Etymology

From Latin 'medulla' meaning 'marrow'.

3. NOUN

/ məˈdʌlə / (AmE), / mɪˈdʌlə / (BrE)
the inner region or core of an organ or structure in a plant or animal, often distinguished from the outer layer
inner part of an organ or structure
medical

Examples

The medulla of the kidney is responsible for concentrating urine before excretion.
Scientists studied the medulla of the plant stem to understand how nutrients are transported.
Damage to the medulla in the brain can have serious effects on vital functions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cortex - the outer layer of an organ or structure in plants or animals

Etymology

From Latin 'medulla' meaning 'marrow' or 'pith'.
