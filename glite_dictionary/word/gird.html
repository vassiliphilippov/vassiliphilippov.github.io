to gird

1. VERB

/ ɡɜrd / (AmE), / ɡɜːd / (BrE)
to put a belt, band, or girdle around something, especially the waist; to encircle or prepare by binding or fastening
put a belt or girdle around

Examples

Before the race, he decided to gird himself with a supportive belt.
The walls gird the old city, protecting it from invaders.
As the storm approached, they girded the tents tightly to withstand the wind.

Synset Examples

gird your loins

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Antonym: to unbind - to remove or release from a binding or restraint

Etymology

From Old English 'gyrdan', related to Old High German 'gurten' (to gird).

2. VERB

/ ɡɜrd / (AmE), / ɡɜːd / (BrE)
to prepare oneself for a confrontation or challenge, especially a military one
prepare oneself for a challenge or conflict

Examples

The general urged the army to gird themselves for the coming battle.
As the storm approached, the residents began to gird for possible evacuation.
Nations must now gird for a period of economic uncertainty.

3. VERB

/ ɡɝd / (AmE), / ɡɜːd / (BrE)
to encircle or bind with a band, belt, or similar object; to surround closely
to encircle or bind with a band

Examples

The knight girded his sword around his waist before battle.
Clouds girded the mountaintop, hiding it from view.
She girded the package with a thick ribbon before sending it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to surround - to encircle on all sides
  • Antonym: to unbind - to release from a binding or encirclement

Etymology

From Old English *gyrdan* (to encircle with a belt).
