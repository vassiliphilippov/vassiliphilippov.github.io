garrison

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡær.ə.sən / (AmE), / ˈɡær.ɪ.sən / (BrE)
a body of troops stationed in a fortress or fortified town to defend it; the military force assigned to guard a particular place
troops stationed to guard a place

Examples

The garrison held out bravely during the long siege.
After the battle, the victorious army left a small garrison in the captured town.
A new garrison was stationed at the border fort to strengthen its defenses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: army - a large organized body of armed personnel trained for war, especially on land
  • Antonym: civilian - a person not in the armed services or police force

Etymology

From Old French 'garnison' (defense, protection), from 'garnir' (to equip or fortify).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡær.ə.sən / (AmE), / ˈɡær.ɪ.sən / (BrE)
A fortified military post where troops are stationed to defend it.
fortified military post with stationed troops

Examples

The soldiers marched into the garrison at dawn to begin their watch.
The old stone garrison still stands as a reminder of the town's military past.
During the siege, food supplies in the garrison quickly ran out.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fort - a fortified building or military post
  • Synonym: outpost - a small military camp or position at some distance from the main force

Etymology

From Old French 'garnison' (defense, protection), from 'garnir' (to equip, to defend).

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈɡær.ə.sən / (AmE), / ˈɡær.ɪ.sən / (BrE)
to station troops in a fort or other military post for its defense
station troops in a fort or post

Examples

The general decided to garrison additional troops at the border post to reinforce security.
During the occupation, the army garrisoned the captured towns to maintain order.
Plans are underway to garrison the military base with a new regiment next month.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: station - to assign troops or personnel to a place for duty

Etymology

From Old French *garnison* (defense, protection), from *garnir* (to furnish, equip).
