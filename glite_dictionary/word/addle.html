to addle

1. VERB

/ ˈædəl / (AmE), / ˈædəl / (BrE)
to make or become confused; to mix up mentally
to confuse or muddle

Examples

The constant noise began to addle his thinking during the meeting.
A lack of sleep can addle your mind and make it hard to concentrate.
Don't addle the situation with unnecessary details.

Synset Examples

He muddled the issues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: muddle - to bring into a disordered or confusing state
  • Antonym: to clarify - to make something clearer or easier to understand

Etymology

From Middle English 'adel', meaning liquid filth or urine, later associated with 'addle-brained'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00622204-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈæd.əl / (AmE), / ˈæd.əl / (BrE)
(of eggs or other organic matter) to become rotten or putrid.
become rotten (esp. eggs)
archaic

Examples

If eggs are kept too long, they may addle and smell unpleasant.
Neglected in the nest, the bird's eggs began to addle.
In the heat, food quickly starts to addle and attracts flies.

Synset Examples

addled eggs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spoil - to become unfit for use; to go bad (esp. food)

Etymology

From Middle English 'adel', probably from Old English 'adela' (mud, filth).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00211073-v
Synset POS: v
