concordance

1. NOUN

/ kənˈkɔr.dəns / (AmE), / kənˈkɔː.dəns / (BrE)
A harmonious arrangement or agreement among elements, resulting in congruity and pleasant effect.
harmonious arrangement or congruity

Examples

There was a pleasing concordance between the colors of the walls and the furniture.
The concordance of instruments created an enchanting atmosphere during the concert.
Designers aim for concordance among all elements in a room to achieve a unified look.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: discordance - lack of harmony or agreement
  • Synonym: harmony - pleasing arrangement of parts
  • Synonym: congruity - quality of being in agreement or harmony

Etymology

From Latin concordantia (agreement), from concordare (to agree), from concors (of the same mind).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈkɔrdəns / (AmE), / kənˈkɔːdəns / (BrE)
an alphabetical list of the principal words used in a book or body of text, with their immediate contexts, often used as a reference tool
index of words with contexts in a text

Examples

The biblical concordance helped her locate every mention of 'forgiveness' in the scriptures.
He compiled a detailed concordance of Shakespeare's plays for literary scholars.
Before digital texts, researchers relied on a concordance to trace the frequency of specific words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: index - an alphabetical list of topics in a book

Etymology

From Latin 'concordantia' (agreement, harmony), from 'concordare' (to agree).

3. NOUN

/ kənˈkɔrdəns / (AmE), / kənˈkɔːdəns / (BrE)
agreement or harmony between people or groups, especially in opinions or actions.
agreement; harmony of opinions

Examples

There was a remarkable concordance among the team members when making the final decision.
The nations sought concordance on environmental policies to address climate change.
Her views were in concordance with those of the committee, leading to a smooth agreement.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: discord - disagreement or lack of harmony between people
  • Synonym: harmony - agreement or concord
  • Synonym: agreement - shared opinion or consent

Etymology

From Latin 'concordantia', from 'concordare' (to agree), from 'concordia' (harmony).
