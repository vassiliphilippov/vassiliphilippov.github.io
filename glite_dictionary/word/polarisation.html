polarisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌpoʊlərɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the condition of having or imparting polarity, especially in physical or chemical systems
the state of having polarity

Examples

The polarisation of the molecule affects its interactions with other substances.
Researchers studied the polarisation of light waves passing through the crystal.
Electrical polarisation occurs when there is a separation of positive and negative charges in a material.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: polarization - US spelling of the same concept; the condition of having or imparting polarity
  • Synonym: polarity - the property of having distinct and opposite charges or characteristics

Etymology

From French 'polarisation', derived from 'pôle' (pole), modeled after 'polarize'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌpoʊlərəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The process or phenomenon in which the vibrations of light or other electromagnetic waves are restricted to a particular direction.
restriction of wave vibrations to a direction
british

Examples

The polarisation of light can be observed using special filters.
Scientists study the polarisation of radio waves to learn about space.
Certain sunglasses use polarisation to reduce glare from surfaces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: polarization - the same phenomenon—restriction of wave vibrations, American spelling

Etymology

From French 'polarisation', based on Latin 'polaris' (of or belonging to a pole).
