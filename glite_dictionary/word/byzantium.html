Byzantium

1. PROPN

/ bɪˈzæn.ti.əm / (AmE), / bɪˈzæn.tɪ.əm / (BrE)
an ancient Greek city on the Bosporus, later known as Constantinople and now Istanbul
ancient city on the Bosporus, later Constantinople

Examples

The ancient city of Byzantium was strategically located on the Bosporus strait.
In 330 AD, Emperor Constantine made Byzantium the capital of the Roman Empire, renaming it Constantinople.
Historians study the art and architecture of Byzantium to learn about early Eastern Roman civilization.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Constantinople - the city formerly known as Byzantium, later Istanbul

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Byzantion, the original Greek name for the city.

2. PROPN

/ bɪˈzæn(t)iəm / (AmE), / bɪˈzæntiəm / (BrE)
the eastern part of the Roman Empire after its division in 395 AD, known as the Byzantine Empire, with its capital at Constantinople.
eastern Roman Empire after 395 AD

Examples

In the sixth century, Byzantium was at the height of its power under Emperor Justinian.
The treasures of Byzantium influenced art across medieval Europe.
After the fall of Rome, Byzantium became the center of Christian civilization in the East.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Byzantine Empire - the empire ruled from Byzantium/Constantinople after the division of the Roman Empire
  • Synonym: Constantinople - the capital city of Byzantium, later called Istanbul
  • Antonym: Rome - the capital of the Western Roman Empire, contrasted with Byzantium

Etymology

From Greek Byzantion, the original name of the city later called Constantinople (modern Istanbul).
