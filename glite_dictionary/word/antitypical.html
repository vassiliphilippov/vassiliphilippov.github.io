antitypical

1. ADJ

/ ˌæn.tiˈtɪp.ɪ.kəl / (AmE), / ˌæn.tɪˈtɪp.ɪ.kəl / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of an antitype, especially as a symbol that fulfills or corresponds to an earlier type or symbol
relating to an antitype (symbolic counterpart)

Examples

The Old Testament rituals are seen as types, while Christ's sacrifice is considered antitypical in Christian theology.
In literature, certain characters may serve an antitypical function, completing symbolic patterns established earlier.
The antitypical fulfillment of the prophecy was believed to occur centuries after the original events.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: typical - relating to or serving as a type; representative

Etymology

From antitype + -ical; antitype from Greek antitypos (responding as an impression to a die).

2. ADJ

/ ˌæn.tiˈtɪp.ɪ.kəl / (AmE), / ˌæn.tɪˈtɪp.ɪ.kəl / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of an antitype, especially one that represents an opposite or contrast
of or relating to an antitype

Examples

The Old Testament stories are often seen as antitypical to events in the New Testament.
His interpretation focuses on antitypical meanings, revealing hidden oppositions in the text.
In literary criticism, the use of antitypical characters highlights contrasts between good and evil.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antithetical - directly opposed or contrasted; mutually incompatible

Etymology

From Greek antítypos (corresponding, opposite) + English -ical.
