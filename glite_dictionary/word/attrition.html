attrition

1. NOUN

/ əˈtrɪʃən / (AmE), / əˈtrɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
The process of gradually wearing down or rubbing away something by friction.
gradual wearing down by friction

Examples

The constant attrition of the waves slowly wore down the rocky shore.
Metal parts in an engine are subject to attrition due to continuous movement.
Sandpaper causes attrition on the surface of the wood, smoothing it over time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abrasion - the process of scraping or wearing away

Etymology

From Latin attritio (a rubbing against), from atterere (to rub against).

3. NOUN

/ əˈtrɪʃən / (AmE), / əˈtrɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
the gradual wearing down or erosion of rock particles by friction caused by water, wind, or ice
wearing down of rock by friction

Examples

Glaciers cause attrition as rocks grind against each other beneath the ice.
Coastal cliffs undergo constant attrition from the impact of waves and wind-blown sand.
Over time, attrition reduces large pebbles to smooth, rounded stones in riverbeds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abrasion - the process of scraping or wearing down by friction, especially in geology

Etymology

From Latin attritio (a rubbing against, wearing away), from atterere (to rub against).

5. NOUN

/ əˈtrɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / əˈtrɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
(in Christian theology) sorrow for sin arising chiefly from fear of divine punishment rather than from a pure motive of love of God.
sorrow for sin out of fear of punishment (theology)

Examples

The priest explained that attrition differs from true contrition because it arises from fear rather than love.
In the confession, she was told that attrition is sufficient for forgiveness when accompanied by the sacrament.
Many theologians have debated whether attrition truly leads to spiritual transformation.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: contrition - sincere remorse for sin out of love for God

Etymology

From Latin attritio (a rubbing against, friction), later used in theological sense for 'imperfect contrition'.
