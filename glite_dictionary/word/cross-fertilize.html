to cross-fertilize

1. VERB

/ ˈkrɔs ˌfɜrtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkrɒs ˌfəːtɪˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to cause (a plant or animal) to undergo cross-fertilization, especially by introducing pollen or reproductive material from another individual or variety
cause to undergo cross-fertilization

Examples

The scientists decided to cross-fertilize the two plant species to observe the characteristics of the hybrids.
Gardeners often cross-fertilize flowers in order to produce new and interesting varieties.
Gregor Mendel famously cross-fertilized pea plants to understand how traits are inherited.

Synset Examples

Mendel cross-fertilized different kinds of beans

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-fertilize - to fertilize with one's own pollen or gametes

Etymology

Formed from 'cross-' (across or between) and 'fertilize', patterned after scientific breeding terminology.

2. VERB

/ krɑs ˈfɜrt̬əˌlaɪz / (AmE), / krɒs ˈfɜːtɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to undergo or cause to undergo cross-fertilization; to become or make fertile by mixing genetic material from different individuals or sources
to fertilize by mixing genetic material

Examples

Some plants must cross-fertilize in order to produce viable seeds.
The two scientific disciplines began to cross-fertilize as researchers collaborated on new projects.
Certain animal species cannot cross-fertilize without the aid of pollinators.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-fertilize - to fertilize with gametes from the same individual or flower

Etymology

From 'cross' (meaning 'across' or 'between') and 'fertilize', modeled after the biological process 'cross-fertilization'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00053606-v
Synset POS: v
