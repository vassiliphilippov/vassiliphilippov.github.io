doctor

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɑktər / (AmE), / ˈdɒktə / (BrE)
A person who is qualified to treat sick or injured people and has been licensed to practice medicine.
licensed medical practitioner

Examples

After several days of fever, I finally made an appointment to see my doctor.
The doctor examined the patient thoroughly before making a diagnosis.
My doctor recommended that I get more exercise to improve my health.

Synset Examples

I felt so bad I went to see my doctor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: physician - a person qualified to practice medicine
  • Antonym: patient - a person under medical care

Etymology

From Latin 'doctor' (teacher), from 'docēre' (to teach).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɑktər / (AmE), / ˈdɒktə / (BrE)
a person who holds a doctorate degree, such as a Ph.D., from a university or equivalent institution
holder of a doctorate degree

Examples

After years of research, she finally became a doctor of philosophy in chemistry.
The university awarded him the title of doctor for his outstanding dissertation.
Many doctors in academia spend their careers teaching and publishing research papers.

Synset Examples

she is a doctor of philosophy in physics

Related Terms

  • Synonym: professor - a senior academic who teaches at a university
  • Antonym: student - a person who is studying at a university or other place of higher education

Etymology

From Latin 'doctor' (teacher), from 'docere' (to teach).

6. VERB

/ ˈdɑktər / (AmE), / ˈdɒktə / (BrE)
to repair or restore something by replacing a part or fixing what is torn or broken
repair or restore by fixing parts

Examples

He managed to doctor the broken fence so it looked almost new.
After the accident, she quickly doctored her torn dress before going back out.
Could you doctor this old chair for me until I can buy a new one?

Synset Examples

She repaired her TV set
Repair my shoes please

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repair - to restore something that is damaged, faulty, or worn to a good condition
  • Synonym: fix - to mend or repair something

Etymology

From Latin 'doctor' (teacher), but this sense derives from the verb meaning 'to treat or adjust'.
