immersion

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈmɜrʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈmɜːʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of being completely submerged in a liquid, especially water.
the act of being submerged in water

Examples

The immersion of the baptismal candidate took place in the river early in the morning.
Complete immersion can help ensure all the vegetables are thoroughly cleaned.
The tradition requires the ritual immersion of idols during the festival.

Etymology

From Latin immersio (a dipping in).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈmɚʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈmɜːʃən / (BrE)
a method of baptism in which a person is partially or completely submerged in water
form of baptism by submerging

Examples

The church practices immersion as the standard method of baptism for new members.
Full immersion symbolizes the washing away of sins in many Christian traditions.
Debate continues among denominations about whether immersion or sprinkling is the most authentic form of baptism.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: sprinkling - method of baptism involving sprinkling water on the person

Etymology

From Latin 'immersio' (a dipping in), from 'immergere' (to plunge into).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈmɜrʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈmɜːʃən / (BrE)
the act of submerging something completely in a liquid, especially so that it becomes wet or saturated
submerging in liquid

Examples

The immersion of the vegetables in boiling water helps to soften them quickly.
Complete immersion of the sample is required for the test to be accurate.
He demonstrated the process by the immersion of a cloth in dye.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: submersion - the act of being under water or a liquid
  • Antonym: emersion - the process of emerging from a liquid

Etymology

From Latin 'immersio', from 'immergere' meaning 'to plunge into'.

4. NOUN

/ ɪˈmɜrʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈmɜːʃən / (BrE)
(Astronomy) The disappearance of a celestial body as it passes into the shadow of another or behind another body, such as during an eclipse.
disappearance of a celestial body during eclipse

Examples

The immersion of the moon occurred just before midnight, marking the start of the eclipse.
Astronomers precisely recorded the immersion of the star as it passed behind Jupiter.
Observers waited patiently for the immersion, knowing it would only last a few minutes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: emersion - the reappearance of a celestial body after being hidden during an eclipse

Etymology

From Latin immersio (a dipping or plunging in), from immergere.
