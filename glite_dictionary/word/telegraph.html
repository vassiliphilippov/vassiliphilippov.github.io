telegraph

1. VERB

/ ˈtɛl.əˌɡræf / (AmE), / ˈtɛl.ɪ.ɡrɑːf / (BrE)
to send a message by telegraph or cable, especially using electrical signals over a distance
send a message by telegraph

Examples

He promised to telegraph the latest news as soon as he arrived.
The company will telegraph the confirmation to your office tomorrow.
In those days, people often telegraphed family members about important events.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wire - to send a message by telegram
  • Synonym: cable - to send a message by cablegram

Etymology

From Greek tele- (far) + graphein (to write); referring to long-distance writing.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01009249-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɛləˌɡræf / (AmE), / ˈtɛlɪɡrɑːf / (BrE)
a system or device for sending messages over long distances by wire, especially using Morse code
device for sending messages over wire

Examples

The telegraph revolutionized long-distance communication in the nineteenth century.
She received news from her family via telegraph when she lived abroad.
Before the telephone, messages were sent using the telegraph and Morse code.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegram - a message sent by telegraph
  • Antonym: telephone - device for transmitting speech over distance by wire or radio

Etymology

From French télégraphe, from Greek tēle (far) + graphein (to write).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04407634-n
Synset POS: n
