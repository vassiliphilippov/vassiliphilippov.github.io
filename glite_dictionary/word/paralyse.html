to paralyse

1. VERB

/ ˈpærəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpærəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something unable to move or function, usually by causing a loss of control over movement or action
make unable to move or function
british

Examples

The venom of the snake can paralyse its prey within seconds.
She was so shocked by the news that it seemed to paralyse her.
A massive power outage can paralyse a city for hours.

Synset Examples

The poison paralyzed him
Fear paralyzed her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to paralyze - to make unable to move or act (American spelling)
  • Synonym: to immobilize - to prevent from moving
  • Antonym: to enable - to make able to do or be something

Etymology

From French *paralyser* or Medieval Latin *paralysare*, from *paralysis* (loss of movement).

2. VERB

/ ˈpærəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpærəlaɪz / (BrE)
To make someone or something powerless or unable to function or act, especially by preventing movement or action.
make powerless or unable to function
british

Examples

The new regulations could paralyse small businesses if not carefully implemented.
News of the sudden strike seemed to paralyse everyone in the office momentarily.
Heavy snow can paralyse an entire city, bringing transport to a standstill.

Synset Examples

The bureaucracy paralyzes the entire operation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cripple - to cause serious impairment or disable completely
  • Synonym: to immobilize - to prevent from moving or operating
  • Antonym: to enable - to make able or allow to function

Etymology

From French 'paralyser', from Latin 'paralysis', from Greek 'paralysis' (disabling, palsy).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00269682-v
Synset POS: v
