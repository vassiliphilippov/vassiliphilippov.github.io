exact

1. ADJ

/ ɪɡˈzækt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzækt / (BrE)
precise and completely correct in every detail; conforming strictly to fact or standard
precise; strictly accurate

Examples

She gave an exact description of what happened that night.
The two signatures are an exact match, down to the smallest detail.
Please tell me the exact time of your arrival.

Synset Examples

an exact mind
an exact copy
hit the exact center of the target

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accurate - correct in all details; exact
  • Antonym: inexact - not strictly precise or accurate

Etymology

From Latin exactus, past participle of exigere (to demand, finish, weigh).

WordNet ID: oewn-00917535-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ɪɡˈzækt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzækt / (BrE)
(of ideas, measurements, representations, etc.) strictly correct or completely accurate; conforming precisely to fact or truth.
completely accurate or correct

Examples

Her calculations needed to be exact to ensure safety.
The scientist recorded the exact temperature during the experiment.
He remembered the exact words she used in her speech.

Synset Examples

a precise image
a precise measurement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accurate - free from errors or mistakes; precise
  • Synonym: precise - marked by exactness or accuracy of expression or detail
  • Antonym: inexact - not exactly accurate or correct

Etymology

From Latin exactus (precise, accurate), past participle of exigere (to demand, enforce, determine).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00634639-s
Synset POS: s

3. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzækt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzækt / (BrE)
to demand and obtain something, especially as a right or due
demand and obtain as a right

Examples

The king exacted tribute from his subjects every year.
The teacher exacts complete attention from her students during lessons.
Governments may exact taxes to fund public services.

Synset Examples

The bank demanded payment of the loan

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demand - to ask for forcefully; to require

Etymology

From Latin exactus, past participle of exigere 'to demand, enforce'.

4. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzækt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɑːkt / (BrE)
to demand or cause to be paid, suffered, or experienced as a consequence, especially in a way that is severe or difficult; to require as payment or retribution
demand or cause (a toll, price, consequence)

Examples

The war exacted a heavy toll on the local population.
Years of neglect exacted a price on the old building.
Strict training exacts significant sacrifices from young athletes.

Synset Examples

the accident claimed three lives

Related Terms

  • Synonym: claim - to take as a consequence or result, typically causing loss or suffering
  • Synonym: demand - to ask for as a right or due

Etymology

From Latin exāctus, past participle of exigere (to demand, drive out).
