cogency

1. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.dʒən.si / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.dʒən.si / (BrE)
the quality of being clear, logical, and convincing
quality of being convincing and logical

Examples

The cogency of her argument impressed everyone in the meeting.
Judges often assess the cogency of the evidence presented in court.
His essay lacks the cogency needed to persuade the reader.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: validity - the quality of being logically or factually sound
  • Antonym: incoherence - the quality of being unclear or illogical

Etymology

From Latin cogentia (force, power), from cogere (to drive together, compel).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.dʒən.si / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.dʒən.si / (BrE)
the quality of being clear, logical, and convincing; the power to persuade by means of clear and compelling reasoning
quality of being convincing and persuasive

Examples

The cogency of her argument was impressive and convinced everyone in the room.
Legal decisions depend greatly on the cogency of the evidence presented.
He doubted the cogency of the proposal, finding the reasoning lacking in substance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persuasiveness - the quality of being able to persuade someone
  • Synonym: conviction - the quality of showing that one firmly believes in what they say
  • Antonym: weakness - the lack of strength or effectiveness in an argument

Etymology

From Latin 'cogentia', from 'cogere' meaning 'to drive together, force'.
