hypertonic

1. ADJ

/ ˌhaɪ.pɚˈtɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / ˌhaɪ.pəˈtɒn.ɪk / (BrE)
(of a solution) having a higher osmotic pressure than another solution, resulting in the movement of water out of cells placed in it.
having higher osmotic pressure than another solution
medical

Examples

When red blood cells are placed in a hypertonic solution, they shrink as water leaves the cells.
Doctors may prescribe a hypertonic saline solution to treat certain medical conditions.
The plant cells lost water and became flaccid in the hypertonic environment.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hypotonic - (of a solution) having lower osmotic pressure than another solution
  • Antonym: isotonic - (of a solution) having the same osmotic pressure as another solution

Etymology

From Greek 'hyper-' (over, above) + 'tonos' (tension).

2. ADJ

/ ˌhaɪpərˈtɑːnɪk / (AmE), / ˌhʌɪpəˈtɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of living tissue) having or involving abnormally high tension or tone in the muscles or tissues.
having abnormally high tension (medical)
medical

Examples

Patients with hypertonic muscle tissue often experience stiffness and limited mobility.
The therapist noted a hypertonic response in the patient's arm during the examination.
Certain neurological disorders can result in hypertonic muscles, making movement difficult.

Synset Examples

hypertonic muscle tissue

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hypotonic - (of living tissue) having abnormally low tension
  • Degree Lower: tonic - having normal muscle tension; relating to muscle tone

Etymology

From Greek hyper- (over, excessive) + tonos (tension, tone) + -ic.
