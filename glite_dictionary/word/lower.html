lower

1. VERB

/ ˈloʊər / (AmE), / ˈləʊə / (BrE)
to move something or somebody to a position that is less high or less elevated
move to a lower position

Examples

She carefully lowered the fragile vase from the top shelf.
The flag was lowered to half-mast as a sign of respect.
Please lower your voice when you enter the library.

Synset Examples

take down the vase from the shelf

Related Terms

  • Antonym: raise - to move something or somebody to a higher position

Etymology

From Old French *lorer*, from Latin *laurare*, frequentative of *laurus*, meaning 'lower'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01977139-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ ˈloʊər / (AmE), / ˈləʊə / (BrE)
(of two things) situated below or at a smaller distance from the top; situated at the bottom.
situated at the bottom of two

Examples

The lower shelf is used for storing heavy items.
She lives in the lower apartment of the building.
Please place the box on the lower bunk bed.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upper - the top one of two

Etymology

From Old English *lǣra*, comparative of *lǣfe* (low).

/ ˈlaʊ.ər / (AmE), / ˈlaʊ.ə / (BrE)
(of a person) to look angry or sullen; to wrinkle one's forehead or draw down one's brows, typically as a sign of disapproval.
look angry or sullen; scowl or frown

Examples

He tends to lower when he's unhappy with the decision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scowl - to frown in an angry or bad-tempered way

Examples
