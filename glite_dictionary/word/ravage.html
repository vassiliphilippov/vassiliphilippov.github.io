ravage

1. NOUN

/ ˈrævɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈravɪdʒ / (BrE)
(usually plural) a destructive effect or action, especially one causing severe damage over time.
destructive effects or actions

Examples

The coastal town slowly recovered from the ravages of the hurricane.
Years of war left their ravages on the landscape and the people.
Museums often struggle to protect their collections from the ravages of time and neglect.

Synset Examples

the ravages of time
the depredations of age and disease

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devastation - extensive destruction or damage
  • Synonym: damage - physical harm that impairs value or usefulness

Etymology

From French ravager, from Latin rapina (plundering).

2. VERB

/ ˈrævɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈravɪdʒ / (BrE)
to cause severe and extensive damage or destruction to something
cause severe destruction

Examples

A severe storm can ravage a coastal town in just a few hours.
The crops were ravaged by an unexpected swarm of locusts.
Years of war have ravaged the city's historic neighborhoods.

Synset Examples

The enemy lay waste to the countryside after the invasion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to devastate - to destroy or ruin completely
  • Synonym: to destroy - to damage something so badly that it no longer exists or cannot be repaired
  • Antonym: to rebuild - to build something again after it has been damaged or destroyed

Etymology

From French *ravager*, from *ravage* (devastation), related to *ravir* (to seize).

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈrævɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈravɪdʒ / (BrE)
to cause severe damage or destruction to a place, especially by violent action or pillaging
cause severe destruction, usually by violence or raid

Examples

The invading army ravaged the countryside, leaving destruction in its wake.
After years of war, famine and disease had ravaged the region.
Wildfires ravaged thousands of homes, forcing many families to evacuate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to devastate - to destroy or ruin a place completely
  • Antonym: repair - to restore something that is damaged to good condition

Etymology

From French *ravager* (to devastate), related to *ravir* (to seize).
