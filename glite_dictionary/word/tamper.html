tamper

1. NOUN

/ ˈtæmpər / (AmE), / ˈtæmpə / (BrE)
a tool used for pressing down material, such as tobacco in a pipe bowl or charges in a drill hole.
tool for pressing down material

Examples

He used a tamper to press the tobacco firmly into the pipe.
The explosives expert reached for his tamper before sealing the drill hole.
Gardeners sometimes employ a hand tamper to compact soil evenly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tamping iron - an iron tool for tamping explosive charges in drill holes
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04394793-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈtæmpɚ / (AmE), / ˈtæmpə / (BrE)
to interfere in a situation, matter, or someone else's affairs, often in a harmful or unwanted way
interfere in others' affairs

Examples

You shouldn't tamper in their personal matters without being asked.
He was warned not to tamper with the company's financial records.
It's wrong to tamper in other people's family affairs.

Synset Examples

Don't meddle in my affairs!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to meddle - to interfere in something that is not one's concern
  • Synonym: to interfere - to become involved in something that is not your concern, often with a negative effect

Etymology

Probably from Middle French *tamper* (to touch, handle), of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02544767-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈtæmpər / (AmE), / ˈtæmpə / (BrE)
to interfere with something, especially in order to damage or alter it, often secretly or dishonestly
interfere with to alter or damage (secretly)

Examples

Someone had clearly tampered with the evidence, making it unreliable in court.
Please don't tamper with the settings on my computer without asking me first.
The company was fined after it was discovered they had tampered with the safety data.

Synset Examples

Someone tampered with the documents on my desk
the reporter fiddled with the facts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fiddle - to touch or manipulate something, often restlessly or dishonestly
  • Synonym: to alter - to change something
  • Antonym: to restore - to return something to a former condition

Etymology

Probably from Middle French 'tamper', from Old French 'temperer' (to restrain or moderate), influenced by 'temper'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
