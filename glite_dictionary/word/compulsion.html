compulsion

1. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
a strong, often irrational urge to perform a repetitive or trivial action, even if you do not want to
irrational urge to perform an action
medical

Examples

Some people experience a compulsion to check the door locks multiple times every night.
She struggled with a compulsion to count objects repeatedly, which disrupted her daily life.
His compulsion to keep things perfectly organized often frustrated his family.

Synset Examples

her compulsion to wash her hands repeatedly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsession - an idea or thought that continually preoccupies the mind

Etymology

From Latin compulsio (a driving or urging), from compellere (to drive together).

2. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
a strong, often irresistible urge to do or say something, especially one that is difficult to control and may be unwise
strong urge to do or say something

Examples

He felt a compulsion to check his phone every few minutes, even during meetings.
Despite her compulsion to speak, she managed to stay silent during the interview.
Many people experience the compulsion to apologize repeatedly, even when it isn't necessary.

Synset Examples

he felt a compulsion to babble on about the accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impulse - a sudden strong urge to act
  • Antonym: inhibition - a feeling that restrains or prevents certain actions

Etymology

From Latin compulsio (a driving together, constraint), from compellere (to force).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09204227-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
the action or state of forcing or being forced to do something; constraint or coercion
the act of forcing someone to do something

Examples

He acted under compulsion and had no real choice in the matter.
Laws exist to prevent the compulsion of witnesses to testify against themselves.
The school joined the program out of compulsion, not because it wanted to.

Synset Examples

though pressed into rugby under compulsion I began to enjoy the game
they didn't have to use coercion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coercion - the practice of persuading someone to do something by force or threats

Etymology

From Latin compulsio (a driving force), from compellere (to force, drive together).
