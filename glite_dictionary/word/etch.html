to etch

1. VERB

/ ɛtʃ / (AmE), / ɛtʃ / (BrE)
to make something stand out, be clearly defined, or sharply visible, often in a way that is strongly impressed or memorable
make stand out or clearly defined

Examples

Her expression was etched with sorrow as she listened to the news.
The silhouette of the mountains was etched against the sunrise.
Those painful memories are etched in my mind forever.

Synset Examples

a face etched with pain
the leafless branches etched against the sky

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to engrave - to carve or cut a design into a surface

Etymology

From Dutch 'etsen', from German 'ätzen', meaning 'to etch, to eat (by acid)'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02203633-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɛtʃ / (AmE), / ɛtʃ / (BrE)
to selectively dissolve or remove material from the surface of a semiconductor or printed circuit using a solvent, laser, or stream of electrons
remove surface material from electronics

Examples

Modern chip fabrication facilities use lasers to etch extremely fine patterns onto silicon wafers.
The technician carefully etched the circuit board to create precise pathways for the electrical signals.
Certain chemicals are used to etch away unwanted layers during the semiconductor manufacturing process.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to engrave - to carve or cut a design into a hard surface

Etymology

From Dutch *etsen* (to etch), from German *ätzen* (to etch, corrode), related to acid.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00449065-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɛtʃ / (AmE), / ɛtʃ / (BrE)
to produce a drawing or design by cutting into a surface, especially metal, with acid or a pointed tool
to engrave or carve a design; to make an etching

Examples

The artist carefully etched a portrait into the copper plate.
She decided to etch her initials onto the glass pane.
He etched the landscape scene for his next exhibition.
