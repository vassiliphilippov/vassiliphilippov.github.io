to reload

1. VERB

/ ˌriˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌriːˈləʊd / (BrE)
to put a new load or supply onto something, especially after removing the previous one
put a new load onto

Examples

After unloading the supplies, the workers began to reload the truck for the next trip.
You must reload the dishwasher once the first cycle is complete.
They stopped to reload the wagon with fresh produce at the farm.

Synset Examples

The movers reloaded the truck

Related Terms

  • Synonym: load - to put a load or cargo onto something

Etymology

Formed from re- (again) + load (from Old English lād, way, course, carrying), meaning 'to load again'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01493347-v

2. VERB

/ riˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌriːˈləʊd / (BrE)
to load something, especially a weapon or device, again after it has been used or emptied
load again, especially a weapon or device

Examples

After firing the last shot, he quickly reloaded the rifle.
Please reload the printer with new paper when it runs out.
She reloaded her phone with more data before traveling.

Synset Examples

She reloaded the gun carefully

Related Terms

  • Antonym: load - to put something into a device so it is ready for use

Etymology

From re- (again) + load, first used in the 17th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01493229-v
Synset POS: v
