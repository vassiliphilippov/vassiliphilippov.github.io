adaptation

1. NOUN

/ ˌæd.əpˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌæd.æpˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of adjusting or becoming suited to new conditions, especially environmental or situational changes
the process of adjusting to new conditions

Examples

The adaptation of species to changing climates can occur over many generations.
Her quick adaptation to the new workplace impressed her managers.
Successful adaptation to stress can improve overall well-being.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adjustment - a small change to make something fit or work better
  • Antonym: maladaptation - failure to adapt to new conditions

Etymology

From Latin 'adaptatio', from 'adaptare' meaning 'to fit to'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌæd.əpˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌæd.æpˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(Physiology) The process by which a sense organ, such as the eye, adjusts to different conditions, such as changes in light.
physiological adjustment of a sense organ
medical

Examples

Adaptation of the eye to darkness takes several minutes.
Rapid adaptation to loud noise can affect hearing sensitivity.
Retinal adaptation helps us see clearly when moving from bright sunlight to a dim room.

Etymology

From Latin 'adaptatio' (a fitting to).

3. NOUN

/ ˌæd.əpˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌæd.æpˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a book, play, film, or other work that has been changed so that it can be presented in a different form or medium
work reinterpreted in a new form

Examples

The film is an adaptation of a bestselling novel.
A new stage adaptation of the classic fairy tale premiered last night.
Many people prefer the original to its adaptation for television.

Synset Examples

the play is an adaptation of a short novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: version - a particular form of something differing in certain respects from an earlier form

Etymology

From French adaptation, from Latin adaptatio (a fitting to).
