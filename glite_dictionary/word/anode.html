anode

1. NOUN

/ ˈænoʊd / (AmE), / ˈænəʊd / (BrE)
The positively charged electrode through which electrons exit an electrical device.
positively charged electrode

Examples

In a battery, the anode is where oxidation occurs, releasing electrons into the circuit.
The chemist replaced the corroded anode to ensure the experiment could continue.
During electrolysis, copper ions are deposited at the anode and dissolved from the cathode.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cathode - negatively charged electrode in an electrical device

Etymology

From Greek anodos meaning 'way up' (ana- 'up' + hodos 'way'), coined in the 19th century for electrical science.

2. NOUN

/ ˈænoʊd / (AmE), / ˈænəʊd / (BrE)
The negatively charged terminal of a voltaic cell or storage battery from which current flows out to an external circuit.
negative terminal in a battery

Examples

The positive ions move toward the anode during the electrochemical reaction.
Each battery has an anode and a cathode connected by an electrolyte.
If you connect the wire to the anode, current will flow into the circuit.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cathode - the positively charged terminal of an electrical cell

Etymology

From Greek 'anodos' meaning 'way up', coined in the 19th century for electrical science.
