Ciliata

1. NOUN

/ sɪliˈeɪtə / (AmE), / sɪlɪˈɑːtə / (BrE)
a class of protozoa characterized by the presence of cilia or hairlike appendages covering all or part of the body during at least one stage of the life cycle
class of ciliated protozoa

Examples

Scientists studied the movement of Ciliata under the microscope to understand their locomotion.
Many species in the Ciliata group play important roles in aquatic ecosystems.
The classification of Ciliata has been revised as genetic analysis has improved.

Etymology

From Latin 'cilium' (eyelash) and modern Latin suffix '-ata', denoting taxa.

2. PROPN

/ sɪliˈeɪtə / (AmE), / sɪlɪˈɑːtə / (BrE)
a class of single-celled protozoa characterized by the presence of cilia or hairlike appendages during some stage of their life cycle
class of protozoa with cilia

Examples

Many species in the class Ciliata live in freshwater and use cilia for movement and feeding.
Researchers have studied the reproductive processes of Ciliata to understand cell division.
The diversity within the Ciliata makes them important subjects in evolutionary biology.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ciliate - a protozoan of the class Ciliata

Etymology

From Latin 'cilium' (eyelash, small hair), referencing the cilia on the organism's surface.
