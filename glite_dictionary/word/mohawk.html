Mohawk

1. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊˌhɑk / (AmE), / ˈməʊˌhɑːk / (BrE)
a member of the Iroquoian people originally from the Mohawk River valley in New York State
member of an Iroquoian Native American people

Examples

The Mohawk were one of the original five nations of the Iroquois Confederacy.
Many Mohawk people maintain strong cultural traditions today.
A Mohawk delegation attended the annual festival of Native American heritage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Iroquois - group of Native American peoples including the Mohawk

Etymology

From the Algonquian term *Mohowawog*, meaning 'they eat living things', applied by their neighbors.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊˌhɑk / (AmE), / ˈməʊˌhɑːk / (BrE)
the Iroquoian language spoken by the Mohawk people, primarily in northeastern North America
language of the Mohawk people

Examples

She is fluent in both English and Mohawk, and often teaches classes in her community.
The Mohawk language is being revitalized among the younger generation with new educational programs.
Researchers studied the grammatical structure of Mohawk to better understand Iroquoian languages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Iroquoian - family of indigenous languages of North America including Mohawk

Etymology

From the name of the Mohawk people, itself from the Narragansett word 'Mohowawog' (they eat living things).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06929089-n
Synset POS: n
