anamorphism

1. NOUN

/ ˌænəˈmɔrˌfɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌanəˈmɔːfɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
A type of metamorphism that occurs deep within the Earth's crust, transforming simple minerals into more complex minerals.
deep crustal metamorphism; formation of complex minerals
medical

Examples

Deep within the Earth's crust, anamorphism transforms existing rocks by creating new, more complex minerals.
Geologists study anamorphism to understand how mineral structures change under extreme pressure and temperature.
The formation of garnet from simpler minerals is an example of anamorphism in metamorphic rocks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metamorphism - the process of change in the structure or composition of rocks due to environmental changes

Etymology

From Greek ana- (up, again) + morphē (form).

2. NOUN

/ ˌænəˈmɔrˌfɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌænəˈmɔːfɪzəm / (BrE)
the gradual evolution of one type of organism into another through a long series of small changes
gradual evolutionary change in organisms

Examples

The fossil record provides evidence for anamorphism throughout the history of life on Earth.
Darwin's theory of evolution includes the concept of anamorphism as species adapt to their environments over time.
Biologists study anamorphism to understand how complex organisms developed from simpler ancestors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evolution - gradual development of organisms over generations

Etymology

From Greek 'ana-' (up, again) and 'morphē' (form, shape), referring to change of form.
