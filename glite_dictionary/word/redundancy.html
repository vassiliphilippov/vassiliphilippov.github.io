redundancy

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌndənsi / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌndənsi / (BrE)
The inclusion of extra data or repetition in a message to reduce the likelihood of errors during transmission.
repetition to prevent transmission errors

Examples

The engineer increased the redundancy in the signal to prevent data loss during transmission.
Modern communication systems rely heavily on redundancy to maintain the integrity of the information.
Without enough redundancy, even a small amount of noise can corrupt the original message.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: error correction - The process of detecting and correcting errors in transmitted messages

Etymology

From Latin redundare (to overflow); applied technically in information theory in the mid-20th century.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌn.dən.si / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌn.dən.si / (BrE)
The unnecessary repetition of words or actions; the state of being redundant.
unnecessary repetition

Examples

The editor removed redundancy from the article to make it more concise.
Avoiding redundancy improves the clarity and flow of your writing.
Her speech was criticized for its redundancy and repetitive phrases.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repetition - The action of repeating something that has already been said or written; the act of doing something again
  • Antonym: conciseness - The quality of being brief and to the point, without unnecessary words

Etymology

From Latin redundantia (overflow, abundance), from redundare (to overflow).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌndənsi / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌndənsi / (BrE)
The inclusion of extra components in a system, so that if one part fails, another can take over and maintain the system's operation.
extra components for system reliability

Examples

The redundancy built into the power supply ensures continuous service during a failure.
Modern airplanes use redundancy in their control systems for increased safety.
To avoid data loss, the servers have redundancy at both the hardware and software levels.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: backup - a duplicate component used as a substitute if the original fails
  • Antonym: failure - lack of proper functioning or breakdown in a system

Etymology

From Latin redundantia (overflow, abundance), from redundāre (to overflow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05738108-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌndənsi / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌndənsi / (BrE)
the state of being unnecessary, excessive, or no longer needed, especially when something is repeated or duplicated
state of being superfluous or unnecessary

Examples

The report was criticized for its redundancy and excessive length.
Installing two backup systems creates redundancy in the network, ensuring reliability if one fails.
Editors removed redundancy from the manuscript to improve its clarity.

Related Terms
