to quantise

1. VERB

/ ˈkwɑnˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkwɒn.tʌɪz / (BrE)
(in electronics or signal processing) to approximate a continuously varying signal by restricting its amplitude to a set of discrete values.
approximate a signal with discrete values

Examples

Modern audio systems quantise the analog signal before storage.
The software can automatically quantise notes entered by the user.
If you quantise the data too coarsely, quality will suffer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quantize - US spelling of 'quantise'

Etymology

From 'quantum' (Latin: how much) + '-ise', modelled after 'quantum' in physics.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00675014-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkwɑːn.taɪz / (AmE), / ˈkwɒn.taɪz / (BrE)
to apply quantum theory to something, making certain quantities or states take on only specific discrete values rather than a continuous range
restrict to discrete quantum values

Examples

The researchers aimed to quantise the electromagnetic field in their experiment.
Physicists have long tried to quantise gravity, but it remains an unsolved problem.
In digital audio, engineers quantise the signal to convert it into discrete values.

Synset Examples

Quantize gravity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quantize - alternate spelling, esp. in American English, with the same meaning

Etymology

Formed from "quantum" (Latin: how much, quantity) + "-ize"; popularized with the advent of quantum theory.

Source: WordNet
