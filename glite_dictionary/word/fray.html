fray

1. NOUN

/ freɪ / (AmE), / freɪ / (BrE)
a noisy or intense fight, struggle, or dispute, typically involving several people
noisy fight or commotion

Examples

When the argument escalated, John found himself drawn into the fray.
At midnight, the police broke up the fray outside the bar.
Despite his desire for peace, he was forced to join the fray to defend his friend.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brawl - a rough or noisy fight usually involving several people
  • Synonym: melee - a confused fight or scuffle
  • Antonym: peace - a state of tranquility or quiet, opposite of turmoil or conflict

Etymology

From Middle English *frai*, from Old French *frayer* (to rub, to quarrel).

2. VERB

/ freɪ / (AmE), / freɪ / (BrE)
(of cloth or rope) to become worn at the edges, causing threads to separate, typically as a result of rubbing.
become worn at the edges (cloth/rope)

Examples

After years of constant use, the edges of his shirt began to fray.
If you wash this scarf too often, the fabric will fray and lose its shape.
She noticed that the old rope had started to fray near the knot.

Synset Examples

The friction frayed the sleeve

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wear - to damage or diminish by long or hard use; to become thin or less effective

Etymology

From Middle English 'fraien', from Old French 'fraier' (to rub), from Latin 'fricare' (to rub).

Synset POS: v
