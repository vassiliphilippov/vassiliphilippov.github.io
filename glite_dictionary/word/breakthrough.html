breakthrough

1. NOUN

/ ˈbreɪkˌθru / (AmE), / ˈbreɪkˌθruː / (BrE)
an act of making a significant advance past a barrier, especially by overcoming opposition or difficulties, such as an enemy's defense
an advance through a barrier or obstacle

Examples

The army's sudden breakthrough enabled them to capture the city within hours.
Scientists announced a breakthrough in cancer research, marking a new era in treatment.
Negotiators finally achieved a breakthrough after weeks of stalled talks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advance - a forward movement, especially by a military force
  • Antonym: setback - a reversal or check in progress

Etymology

From break + through, first used in military contexts in the early 20th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbreɪkˌθru / (AmE), / ˈbreɪkˌθruː / (BrE)
an important and sudden discovery, development, or achievement that removes an obstacle to progress
important discovery or development

Examples

The scientists achieved a major breakthrough in cancer research last year.
The invention represented a technological breakthrough that changed daily life.
After months of negotiation, there was finally a diplomatic breakthrough between the two countries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advance - a forward step or improvement in progress
  • Antonym: setback - a reversal or check in progress

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'break through', recorded since the early 20th century.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbreɪkˌθru / (AmE), / ˈbreɪkˌθruː / (BrE)
an important and sudden advance, discovery, or insight, especially in knowledge or progress
an important sudden discovery or advance

Examples

The scientist's research led to a major breakthrough in cancer treatment.
After months of negotiations, the two countries finally made a breakthrough toward peace.
Her breakthrough came when she discovered a simple solution to the complex problem.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advance - forward movement or progress in development
  • Antonym: setback - an obstacle or reversal in progress

Etymology

From the verb 'break through', first used in English in the early 20th century as a noun for significant advances.
