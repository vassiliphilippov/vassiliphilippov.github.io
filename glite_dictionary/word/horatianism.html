Horatianism

1. NOUN

/ hɔˈreɪʃiəˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / hɒˈreɪʃiəˌnɪzəm / (BrE)
The literary style or approach to satire characterized by gentleness, mild mockery, and urbane wit, as exemplified by the Roman poet Horace.
gentle, witty style of satire after Horace

Examples

The professor explained how Horatianism differs from other forms of satire in both style and intent.
Writers employing Horatianism often use gentle humor to critique social customs rather than direct attack.
Her essays were models of Horatianism, offering witty but kindly observations on human folly.

Synset Examples

The fountain of Horatianism in Spain was the imitation of Epode II, Beatus Ille, by the Marquis de Santillana, one of Castile's two first sonneteers, in the first half of the fifteenth century.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Juvenalian - A harsher, more biting form of satire, after the Roman poet Juvenal
  • Synonym: satire - Use of humor, irony, or ridicule to expose and criticize

Etymology

From the name of the Roman poet Horace (Quintus Horatius Flaccus) + -ianism (suffix forming nouns).
