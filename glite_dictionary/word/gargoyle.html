gargoyle

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑrˌɡɔɪl / (AmE), / ˈɡɑːɡɔɪl / (BrE)
A decorative waterspout carved in the form of a grotesque figure, typically found on buildings to carry rainwater clear of a wall.
grotesque carved waterspout on buildings

Examples

The medieval cathedral is famous for its stone gargoyles perched along the roofline.
Rainwater flows through the mouth of the gargoyle and away from the building's walls.
During the restoration, several gargoyles were carefully repaired to preserve the historic facade.

Etymology

From Old French *gargouille* (throat, gullet, waterspout), from Latin *gurgulio* (throat, gullet).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑrˌɡɔɪl / (AmE), / ˈɡɑːɡɔɪl / (BrE)
a grotesquely carved stone figure, often of a person or animal, typically used as a decorative spout on buildings, especially in Gothic architecture
grotesque carved figure, often on buildings

Examples

The medieval cathedral was adorned with gargoyles perched along its stone ledges.
Rainwater poured from the mouth of a gargoyle on the old church.
Many tourists stopped to take pictures of the eerie stone gargoyles overlooking the square.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chimera - mythical or grotesque carved figure, especially in architecture

Etymology

From Old French 'gargouille' (throat, waterspout), related to Latin 'gurgulio' (throat, gullet).
