incubation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪŋkjəˈbeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪŋkjʊˈbeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Pathology) The period between exposure to an infective agent and the appearance of the first symptoms of infection.
interval before symptoms appear (medical)
medical

Examples

The incubation period for the flu typically ranges from one to four days.
During the incubation stage, a person may not be aware they are infectious.
Doctors tracked the incubation time of the virus to understand its spread.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: latency period - the time between infection and the onset of symptoms

Etymology

From Latin incubatio, from incubare 'to lie upon, brood'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪŋkjuˈbeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪŋkjʊˈbeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a bird, or sometimes another animal, sits on eggs in order to keep them warm until they hatch
sitting on eggs to hatch them

Examples

The mother hen’s incubation of her eggs lasted just over three weeks.
Successful incubation requires maintaining a constant, warm temperature for the eggs.
In some species, both males and females take turns with the incubation process.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brooding - keeping eggs warm for hatching, especially by birds

Etymology

From Latin incubatio, from incubare (to lie upon).

3. NOUN

/ ɪn.kjuˈbeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪŋ.kjʊˈbeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of keeping something at an optimal temperature to promote its development, especially in reference to eggs or microorganisms
maintaining at optimal temperature for development

Examples

The incubation of chicken eggs requires a constant, warm environment for proper development.
Modern laboratories use special chambers for the incubation of bacterial cultures.
The scientist carefully monitored the incubation period to ensure optimal growth conditions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brooding - the act of sitting on eggs to hatch them
  • Degree Higher: hatching - the process of eggs developing and young emerging

Etymology

From Latin incubatio (brooding or hatching), from incubare (to lie upon, to hatch).
