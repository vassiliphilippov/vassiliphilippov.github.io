pardon

1. VERB

/ ˈpɑːrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈpɑːdn / (BrE)
to officially forgive someone for a crime and free them from punishment
forgive officially for a crime

Examples

The governor decided to pardon the prisoner after new evidence came to light.
In a famous ceremony, the President will pardon a turkey before Thanksgiving each year.
She hoped the king would pardon her brother for his crime.

Synset Examples

Ford pardoned Nixon
The Thanksgiving turkey was pardoned by the President

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to forgive - to stop feeling angry or resentful towards someone for an offense or mistake

Etymology

From Old French 'pardoner', from Medieval Latin 'perdonare' (to forgive entirely).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈpɑːdn̩ / (BrE)
An official or formal act of forgiving someone for a mistake, offense, or crime, often releasing them from punishment.
the act of forgiving an offense or mistake

Examples

The governor granted a full pardon to the prisoner after reviewing the evidence.
He asked for a pardon after admitting his mistake in front of the group.
She received a formal pardon for her role in the incident years later.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forgiveness - the act of forgiving or being forgiven
  • Antonym: punishment - the infliction of a penalty as retribution

Etymology

From Old French *pardon* (forgiveness), from Latin *perdonare* (to forgive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01229679-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈpɑːdn̩ / (BrE)
an official document or decision granting exemption from punishment for a crime or offense
official release from punishment

Examples

The governor issued a pardon to the wrongly convicted man, releasing him from prison.
She applied for a pardon in hopes of having her criminal record cleared.
After years of appeals, the president finally granted him a full pardon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amnesty - an official pardon for people who have been convicted of political offenses
  • Antonym: punishment - the infliction or imposition of a penalty as retribution for an offense

Etymology

From Old French *pardoner*, from Medieval Latin *perdonare* (to grant freely, forgive).

4. NOUN

/ ˈpɑrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈpɑːdn̩ / (BrE)
an official decision by a person in authority to forgive someone for a crime and free them from punishment
official act of forgiveness or release from punishment

Examples

After much debate, the court finally issued a full pardon for the accused.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reprieve - a temporary delay in carrying out a punishment, especially death
  • Antonym: conviction - a formal declaration that someone is guilty of a criminal offense

Etymology

From Old French 'pardon', from Latin 'perdonare' (to give wholeheartedly).

5. VERB

/ ˈpɑrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈpɑːdn̩ / (BrE)
to accept an excuse for a fault or offense; to forgive
forgive or excuse a fault

Examples

I hope you can pardon my mistake in the report.
She quickly pardoned him for arriving late to dinner.
Please pardon the mess while we renovate the kitchen.

Synset Examples

Please excuse my dirty hands

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to forgive - to stop feeling angry or resentful towards someone for an offense, flaw, or mistake
  • Synonym: excuse - to overlook or forgive a fault or offense
  • Antonym: to condemn - to express complete disapproval of; to criticize or punish

Etymology

From Old French *pardoner*, from Medieval Latin *perdonare* (to remit, forgive, excuse).
