jump-start

1. VERB

/ dʒʌmp stɑrt / (AmE), / dʒʌmp stɑːt / (BrE)
to start or re-start something vigorously or energetically, especially a process, activity, or project that has stalled or is inactive
to start or re-start energetically

Examples

The government hopes to jump-start the economy by investing in new infrastructure projects.
She brought in a consultant to jump-start the company's marketing efforts.
After the long break, the team needed some motivation to jump-start their productivity.

Synset Examples

The Secretary of State intends to jumpstart the Middle East Peace Process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to kick-start - to provide fresh impetus or initiate something energetically

Etymology

Originally from automotive jargon relating to starting a car with jumper cables; figurative use since the late 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00349877-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌmp stɑːrt / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌmp stɑːt / (BrE)
an act of starting the engine of a vehicle, especially one with a weak or dead battery, by connecting it to another vehicle's battery with jumper cables
starting a vehicle with external power

Examples

My car wouldn't start until I got a jump-start from a friend.
If your battery is dead, you might need a jump-start to get moving again.
She offered to give me a jump-start when she saw me stranded in the parking lot.

Synset Examples

my battery was dead so I had to get a jumpstart from my neighbor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jump - to start or move suddenly

Etymology

From 'jump' (to start suddenly) + 'start', origin in automobile repair slang mid-20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01268414-n
Synset POS: n
