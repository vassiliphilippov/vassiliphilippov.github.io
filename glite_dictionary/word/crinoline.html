crinoline

1. NOUN

/ ˈkrɪn.əˌlɪn / (AmE), / ˈkrɪn.əl.ɪn / (BrE)
A full, stiff petticoat, often made of horsehair and cotton or linen, worn to give volume to a skirt.
stiff petticoat for skirt volume

Examples

The actress wore a crinoline beneath her gown to create an elegant, voluminous silhouette.
In Victorian times, women often struggled to sit comfortably while wearing a crinoline.
The museum displayed a 19th-century dress complete with its original crinoline.

Etymology

From French 'crinoline', from Italian 'crinolino' (crino = horsehair + lino = linen).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkrɪn.ə.lɪn / (AmE), / ˈkrɪn.əl.ɪn / (BrE)
a stiffened or structured skirt, or the fabric used for such a garment, often supported by hoops to hold out the fabric away from the body
stiffened skirt or fabric supported by hoops

Examples

The actress wore a beautiful crinoline under her gown in the period drama.
Victorian fashion often featured dresses supported by a crinoline to create a wide silhouette.
She found a vintage crinoline at the costume shop for her theater production.

Etymology

From French 'crinoline', from Italian 'crinolino', from 'crino' (horsehair) + 'lino' (linen), originally a fabric made from horsehair and linen.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkrɪnəlɪn / (AmE), / ˈkrɪnəlɪn / (BrE)
a stiff, coarse fabric used to stiffen hats or clothing, especially in women's fashion of the 19th century
stiff fabric for stiffening hats or clothing

Examples

The seamstress used crinoline to give the skirt its distinctive bell shape.
Victorian dresses often featured layers of crinoline beneath the fabric for volume.
Some hats are still stiffened today with a strip of crinoline.

Etymology

From French 'crinoline', from Italian 'crinolino' (horsehair fabric), from 'crino' (horsehair) + 'lino' (linen).
