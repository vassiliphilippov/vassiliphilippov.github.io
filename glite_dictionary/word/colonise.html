to colonise

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.ləˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ə.naɪz / (BrE)
to settle among and establish control over the indigenous people of an area, often by establishing a colony
establish a colony or settle in a new area

Examples

The British were among the first Europeans to colonise Australia.
Many nations sought to colonise new lands during the Age of Discovery.
As disease spread, the settlers continued to colonise the region, displacing its original inhabitants.

Synset Examples

The British colonized the East Coast

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to establish a community or place of residence
  • Antonym: to decolonise - to withdraw from or reverse the process of colonization

Etymology

From Latin 'colonia' (settlement, farm), via French 'coloniser'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.lə.naɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ə.naɪz / (BrE)
to establish a colony in a place and settle among and control its local population, often by a foreign power
establish a colony and settle in a place
british

Examples

European powers sought to colonise vast regions of Asia and Africa during the nineteenth century.
Some believe it is unethical for rich nations to colonise economically weaker countries.
The explorers hoped to colonise the island and claim it for their homeland.

Synset Examples

Europeans colonized Africa in the 17th century

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to move to and establish residence in a new place
  • Antonym: to decolonise - to withdraw from a colony, leaving it independent

Etymology

From Latin 'colonia' (settlement, farm) via French 'coloniser'.
