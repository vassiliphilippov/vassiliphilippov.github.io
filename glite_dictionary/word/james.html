James

1. PROPN

/ ˈdʒeɪmz / (AmE), / ˈdʒeɪmz / (BrE)
A book of the New Testament, attributed to Saint James the Apostle, containing teachings and exhortations to early Christians.
New Testament book attributed to Saint James

Examples

Many scholars have debated the authorship of James in the New Testament.
The epistle of James emphasizes the importance of faith and good works.
In our Bible study group, we recently finished reading James.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Epistle - A letter, especially one in the New Testament

Etymology

From Latin Iacobus, from Greek Iakōbos, from Hebrew Ya‘aqōb (Jacob, James).

2. PROPN

/ ˈdʒeɪmz / (AmE), / ˈdʒeɪmz / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) one of Jesus' disciples, the brother of John, and traditionally considered the author of the Epistle of James.
disciple of Jesus; New Testament author

Examples

James and his brother John were among the first disciples called by Jesus.
The Epistle of James offers practical advice for Christian living.
In many paintings, James is depicted holding a pilgrim’s staff.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: John - another disciple and brother of James in the New Testament

Etymology

From Late Latin 'Iacobus', from Greek 'Iakōbos', from Hebrew 'Ya‘aqōb' (Jacob).

4. PROPN

/ ˈdʒeɪmz / (AmE), / ˈdʒeɪmz / (BrE)
(1566–1625) The first Stuart king of England and Ireland (as James I, 1603–1625) and of Scotland (as James VI, 1567–1625); the son of Mary, Queen of Scots, known for commissioning the King James Bible and asserting the divine right of kings.
King of England, Scotland, and Ireland (1566–1625)

Examples

James united the crowns of England and Scotland in 1603, marking the start of the Stuart dynasty.
The King James Bible, commissioned by James I, remains one of the most influential English translations.
Parliament often clashed with James over his views on royal authority and the divine right of kings.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Elizabeth I - the predecessor Queen of England whom James succeeded
  • Degree Higher: Charles I - son and successor of James I as king of England, Scotland, and Ireland

Etymology

From the Late Latin 'Iacomus', variant of 'Iacobus' (Jacob).
