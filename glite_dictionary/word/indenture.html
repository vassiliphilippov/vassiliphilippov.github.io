indenture

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdɛntʃər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɛntʃə / (BrE)
a formal legal agreement, especially one specifying the terms of a bond issued by a corporation or government
formal agreement for bonds or debts
legal

Examples

The corporation's indenture outlines all the repayment conditions for its bonds.
Investors should review the indenture carefully before purchasing any new securities.
The trustee is responsible for ensuring that the terms of the indenture are observed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contract - a legally binding agreement between two or more parties

Etymology

Late Middle English, from medieval Latin indentura (a written agreement with notched edges).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdɛntʃər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɛntʃə / (BrE)
the space left between the margin and the beginning of an indented line in a text
space at start of indented line

Examples

The first line of each paragraph has an indenture to make the text easier to read.
Using the ruler in your word processor, you can adjust the indenture of the paragraph.
Older printed books often have a larger indenture at the beginning of new chapters.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indent - to start a line of text further from the margin

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French 'endenture', from 'endenter' (to notch with teeth).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdɛntʃər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɛntʃə / (BrE)
a formal legal contract by which a person is bound to work for another for a specified period, especially as an apprentice or laborer
formal contract for term of service
legal

Examples

In colonial America, many immigrants signed an indenture to pay for their passage by working for several years.
The blacksmith took on a new apprentice under a seven-year indenture.
When the indenture expired, she was finally free to start her own business.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: apprenticeship - the position of being an apprentice, often under an indenture

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French 'endeture' (a written agreement), from 'endenter' (to notch), alluding to the notched edges of the contract.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06785638-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdɛntʃər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɛntʃə / (BrE)
a concave cut or notch made into a surface or edge, typically a natural feature such as a coastline
concave cut or notch in a surface or edge

Examples

The indenture along the bay formed a natural harbor for small boats.
A distinct indenture in the cliff side marked the entrance to an ancient cave.
Geologists traced the irregular indenture of the coastline on their maps.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: notch - a small cut or indentation on an edge or surface
  • Antonym: protrusion - something that juts out from a surface, opposite of an indentation

Etymology

From Medieval Latin indentura, from indenta (notched), referring to notched, indented edges.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ɪnˈdɛn.tʃɚ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɛn.tʃə / (BrE)
to formally bind someone by a contract, especially as an apprentice or servant
bind by a legal contract (historical)

Examples

The young man was indentured to a blacksmith for seven years to learn the trade.
Many immigrants were indentured as servants upon their arrival in America.
The boy’s family indentured him in exchange for his passage overseas.

Synset Examples

an indentured servant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bind - to tie or secure legally or physically

Etymology

From Middle English 'indenture' (contract with notched edges), from Old French, from Medieval Latin 'indentura'.
