upbeat

1. ADJ

/ ˈʌpˌbit / (AmE), / ˈʌpˌbiːt / (BrE)
cheerful and hopeful, often in a way that may seem unrealistically optimistic
cheerfully optimistic

Examples

Despite the project's challenges, her upbeat attitude inspired the whole team.
He tried to remain upbeat even when things weren't going well.
The news anchor ended the broadcast on an upbeat note, sharing a story about community kindness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: optimistic - expecting the best; hopeful about the future
  • Antonym: pessimistic - expecting the worst; having a negative outlook

Etymology

Originally from the musical term 'upbeat,' referring to the unaccented beat before the downbeat, later figuratively extended to mean 'lively' or 'positive.'

2. NOUN

/ ˈʌpˌbit / (AmE), / ˈʌpˌbiːt / (BrE)
(Music) An unaccented beat, especially the last beat in a measure, that immediately precedes the first beat of the next measure.
an unaccented or last beat in a musical measure

Examples

The conductor raised his baton on the upbeat to cue the orchestra's entrance.
You'll notice the dancers begin their routine precisely on the upbeat.
Musicians must pay attention to both the downbeat and the upbeat when performing a piece.

Etymology

From the upward movement of a conductor's baton on this beat.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07101294-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈʌpˌbit / (AmE), / ˈʌpˌbiːt / (BrE)
a period or state of happiness, optimism, or prosperity; a positive and buoyant condition
a period of optimism or well-being

Examples

After years of struggle, the company found itself on the upbeat once again.
The community is experiencing an upbeat thanks to the new job opportunities.
Economists are reporting an upbeat in consumer confidence this quarter.

Synset Examples

the town was finally on the upbeat after our recent troubles

Related Terms

  • Antonym: downtown - a decline or period of difficulty or low activity
