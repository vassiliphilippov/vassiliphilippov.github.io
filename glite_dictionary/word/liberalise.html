to liberalise

1. VERB

/ ˈlɪbərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈlɪb(ə)rəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make or become more liberal, especially by relaxing laws, rules, or restrictions
make or become more liberal
british

Examples

After the election, the government decided to liberalise its trade policies to attract foreign investment.
The country gradually liberalised its immigration laws over the past decade.
As society changes, some argue that we should liberalise our approach to cultural traditions.

Synset Examples

The laws liberalized after Prohibition

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relax - to make rules or restrictions less strict
  • Antonym: to tighten - to make rules more strict or severe
  • Synonym: to liberalize - alternative spelling, US English

Etymology

From Latin 'liberalis' (of freedom, pertaining to a free person) via French 'libéraliser'.

2. VERB

/ ˈlɪbərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈlɪbərəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make laws, rules, or systems less strict and more open to change or individual freedom
make laws or rules less strict
british

Examples

The government decided to liberalise trade regulations to encourage foreign investment.
Several countries have liberalised their immigration policies in recent years.
They are planning to liberalise the banking system as part of economic reforms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to liberalize - make something more liberal (American spelling)
  • Antonym: to restrict - to make laws, rules, or systems stricter or less free

Etymology

From liberal + -ise, modelled after the French libéraliser and English liberalize.
