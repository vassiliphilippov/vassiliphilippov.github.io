unbound

1. ADJ

/ ʌnˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈbaʊnd / (BrE)
(of a book or manuscript) not secured within a cover; not bound.
not bound (esp. book or manuscript)

Examples

The manuscript was delivered as an unbound stack of pages.
Collectors sometimes prefer unbound editions for custom binding.
She kept an unbound copy of her thesis for easier editing.

Synset Examples

an unbound book

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bound - (of a book) secured within a cover

Etymology

Formed from 'un-' (not) + 'bound' (from bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00258328-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ʌnˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈbaʊnd / (BrE)
(of a substance) not held in chemical or physical combination with something else; free or unattached.
not chemically or physically combined

Examples

The scientist measured the concentration of unbound oxygen in the solution.
In the bloodstream, some drugs exist in an unbound state, making them more active.
The test distinguishes between unbound and bound ions in the sample.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bound - held in chemical or physical combination

Etymology

Formed from prefix 'un-' (not) + 'bound' (joined, held).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01062455-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ʌnˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈbaʊnd / (BrE)
not restrained or tied down; free from physical or figurative bonds.
not restrained or tied down

Examples

After years of captivity, the once-caged bird was finally unbound and soared into the sky.
His imagination seemed unbound by conventional limits, leading to extraordinary creations.
The prisoners were unbound and allowed to walk freely after the peace agreement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: free - not under the control or power of another; able to act or be as one wishes
  • Antonym: bound - restrained or tied down; fastened

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'un-' (not) + 'bound' (past participle of 'bind').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00255018-a
Synset POS: a
