carbonate

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrbəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɑːbənət / (BrE)
a salt or ester of carbonic acid containing the anion CO3, commonly found in minerals and used in various industrial processes
a salt or ester of carbonic acid

Examples

The water in this region is rich in carbonate minerals, which makes it hard.
Sodium carbonate is commonly used in the production of glass and soap.
Some antacid tablets contain calcium carbonate to neutralize stomach acid.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: bicarbonate - a salt containing the HCO3 anion; a related compound with one more hydrogen atom

Etymology

From French 'carbonate', from 'carbonate' (carbonic acid), from Latin 'carbo' (coal).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːr.bəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɑː.bə.neɪt / (BrE)
to turn (a substance) into a carbonate by combining it with carbon dioxide or a carbonate compound
to make into a carbonate

Examples

The factory will carbonate the water to produce sparkling beverages.
Certain minerals can carbonate when exposed to carbon dioxide over time.
Chemists often carbonate samples during laboratory experiments.

Etymology

From French *carbonate*, from *carbone* (carbon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02633302-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑːr.bə.neɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɑː.bə.neɪt / (BrE)
to add carbon dioxide to a liquid, especially to make it fizzy or sparkling
add carbon dioxide to a liquid

Examples

Most soft drink manufacturers carbonate water to create the signature fizz in their beverages.
The bartender offered to carbonate my juice with a soda maker.
If you carbonate the beverage for too long, it can become overly fizzy.

Synset Examples

Carbonated soft drinks

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to decaffeinate - remove caffeine from a substance

Etymology

From French *carbonate*, from Latin *carbonatus* (turned into charcoal), from *carbo* (charcoal).
