Capetian

1. NOUN

/ kəˈpiːʃən / (AmE), / kəˈpiːʃən / (BrE)
a member of the Capetian dynasty, which ruled France from 987 to 1328 and produced several royal families in Europe.
member of the Capetian dynasty

Examples

Many historians consider Philip II to be the most influential Capetian king.
As a Capetian, he was related to several royal families across Europe.
The last direct Capetian king of France died in 1328, ending the dynasty's main line.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Bourbon - member of the Bourbon dynasty, a branch of the Capetians

Etymology

From French Capétien, after Hugh Capet, founder of the dynasty.

2. ADJ

/ kəˈpiːʃən / (AmE), / kəˈpiːʃən / (BrE)
relating to the French royal dynasty founded by Hugh Capet or its members
of the French Capet dynasty

Examples

The Capetian kings reigned in France for over three centuries.
Scholars debate the influence of Capetian policies on medieval Europe.
The Capetian succession established the principle of royal primogeniture in France.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Carolingian - relating to the Frankish dynasty preceding the Capetians

Etymology

From the name of Hugh Capet, King of France (c. 939–996), founder of the Capetian dynasty.
