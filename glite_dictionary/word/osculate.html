to osculate

1. VERB

/ ˈɑskjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒskjʊleɪt / (BrE)
(of a species or taxonomic group) to be intermediate between two groups; to have characteristics of both.
be intermediate between taxonomic groups

Examples

Several subspecies osculate between the two genera, sharing features of both.
In evolutionary biology, some fish lineages osculate, making classification challenging.
The fossil record suggests that the ancient mammal osculated between primates and rodents.

Synset Examples

These species osculate

Etymology

From Latin osculatus, past participle of osculari (to kiss), by metaphorical extension to signify 'touching' or 'connecting'.

2. VERB

/ ˈɑː.skju.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒs.kjʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
to touch with the lips or kiss, especially as a form of greeting, affection, or respect
kiss; touch with the lips

Examples

During their wedding, the couple osculated in front of the guests to seal their vows.
The actor osculated his co-star's cheek in the closing scene of the play.
Before boarding the train, she osculated her child's forehead tenderly.

Synset Examples

She kissed her grandfather on the forehead when she entered the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kiss - to touch someone with your lips as a greeting, sign of affection, or sexual attraction

Etymology

From Latin *osculatus*, past participle of *osculari* (to kiss), from *osculum* (a little mouth, a kiss).

3. VERB

/ ˈɑː.skjə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒs.kjʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
(Mathematics) For two curves or surfaces: to have at least three points, often a point of contact and two additional derivatives, in common at a location.
to share points or contact in mathematics

Examples

In geometry, two circles are said to osculate if they share the same tangent and curvature at a point.
The curves osculate at the origin, making their contact particularly strong there.
Mathematicians often study how surfaces osculate along a curve in higher dimensions.

Synset Examples

one curve osculates the other
these two surfaces osculate

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: touch - to be in contact with something, especially at a single point

Etymology

From Latin osculatus, past participle of osculari (to kiss), originally meaning 'to touch closely'. Adopted into mathematics for close contact between curves or surfaces.
