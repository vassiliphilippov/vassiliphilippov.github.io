parenchyma

1. NOUN

/ pəˈrɛŋkɪmə / (AmE), / pəˈrɛŋkɪmə / (BrE)
the functional tissue in an organ, distinct from the connective and supporting tissue
functional tissue of an organ
medical

Examples

Liver parenchyma cells are responsible for most metabolic processes in the organ.
The disease mainly affects the lung parenchyma, rather than the airways.
Tumors can arise from either the parenchyma or the connective tissue of an organ.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stroma - supporting tissue of an organ, opposite of parenchyma

Etymology

From Greek parenkhuma (substance, viscera), from parenchyein (to pour in beside).

2. NOUN

/ pəˈrɛŋkəmə / (AmE), / pəˈrɛŋkɪmə / (BrE)
The primary tissue in higher plants, made up of thin-walled cells capable of cell division, which forms most of the interior parts of leaves, roots, fruit pulp, and stem pith.
plant tissue of thin-walled cells

Examples

The parenchyma of the apple gives the fruit its soft, juicy texture.
In most leaves, parenchyma cells fill the space between the veins and the outer layers.
Botanists observed rapid healing in the parenchyma after the tree was wounded.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: collenchyma - plant tissue with thicker cell walls that provides support
  • Degree Higher: sclerenchyma - plant tissue with very thick cell walls, offering maximum support

Etymology

From Greek parenkhuma (something poured in beside), from para- (beside) + en- (in) + cheein (to pour).
