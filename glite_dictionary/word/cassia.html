cassia

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæsiə / (AmE), / ˈkæsiə / (BrE)
a tropical or subtropical plant of the genus Cassia, some species of which are often classified in the genera Senna or Chamaecrista, known for their aromatic bark and medicinal or culinary uses
a type of tropical or subtropical plant

Examples

Many spices labeled as cassia are actually made from the bark of related species.
The cassia tree is valued both for its beautiful flowers and its aromatic properties.
Traditional Chinese medicine often includes cassia in its herbal mixtures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cinnamon - aromatic spice from bark of related trees

Etymology

From Latin cassia, from Greek kassia, from Hebrew qĕsīʽāh, a kind of aromatic plant.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæsi.ə / (AmE), / ˈkæsi.ə / (BrE)
any of various trees or shrubs of the genus Cassia, with pinnately compound leaves and usually yellow flowers followed by long seedpods
a tree or shrub of the genus Cassia

Examples

The cassia tree produces beautiful yellow blossoms during the summer.
Some types of cassia are used as a substitute for true cinnamon in cooking.
The herbal medicine contains extracts from the leaves of cassia shrubs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cinnamon - a sweet spice from tree bark, sometimes called 'Chinese cassia'

Etymology

From Latin 'cassia', from Greek 'kassia', originally of Semitic origin.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkæsiə / (AmE), / ˈkæsiə / (BrE)
A Chinese tree with aromatic bark, used as a source of cinnamon that is considered less desirable than Ceylon cinnamon.
Chinese tree used for cinnamon

Examples

The bark of cassia is commonly sold as cinnamon in many grocery stores.
Some people prefer the milder flavor of Ceylon cinnamon over that of cassia.
Traditional Chinese medicine often uses the bark of cassia for its warming properties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cinnamon - A spice made from the inner bark of several tree species, including cassia.

Etymology

From Latin *cassia*, from Greek *kassia*, from Hebrew *qiṣʿāʕ* (cassia tree).
