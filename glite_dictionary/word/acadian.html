Acadian

1. NOUN

/ əˈkeɪdiən / (AmE), / əˈkeɪdiən / (BrE)
a descendant of the early French settlers in the Maritime provinces of Canada, especially Nova Scotia, or relating to their culture and history
early French settler in the Canadian Maritimes

Examples

Many Acadians still live in Nova Scotia, celebrating their distinct heritage.
The museum displays artifacts from the early Acadian settlers who arrived in the 1600s.
After the Great Expulsion, some Acadian families resettled in Louisiana, becoming known as Cajuns.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Cajun - descendant of Acadians relocated to Louisiana

Etymology

From French 'Acadien', referring to Acadia, the name given to parts of eastern Canada colonized by the French in the 17th century.
