animalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈænɪməlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈænɪməlɪzəm / (BrE)
the doctrine or belief that human beings are purely animal in nature and do not possess a spiritual nature
doctrine that humans are purely animal

Examples

The philosopher argued that animalism neglects the complexities of human consciousness.
Some critics believe animalism reduces human beings to their biological instincts.
Debates in philosophy often contrast animalism with views that emphasize the soul or spirit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: materialism - the doctrine that nothing exists except matter and its movements and modifications
  • Antonym: spiritualism - the doctrine that spirits exist and humans have a spiritual nature

Etymology

From animal + -ism, patterned after similar doctrinal terms.

2. NOUN

/ ˈæn.ɪ.məˌlɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈæn.ɪ.mə.lɪz.əm / (BrE)
preoccupation with or emphasis on the satisfaction of physical drives and appetites, especially as opposed to spiritual or intellectual pursuits
focus on bodily appetites over the spiritual or intellectual

Examples

Some critics accuse modern society of animalism and a lack of higher values.
His unrestrained animalism was evident in his pursuit of pleasure without thought for consequences.
She rejected animalism in favor of a more contemplative, spiritual life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hedonism - the pursuit of pleasure as a way of life
  • Antonym: asceticism - severe self-discipline and avoidance of all forms of indulgence

Etymology

From 'animal' + '-ism', modeled after Latin animalis (of animals).
