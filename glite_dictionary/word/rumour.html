rumour

1. NOUN

/ ˈruːmər / (AmE), / ˈruːmə / (BrE)
information or a story that is passed from person to person but has not been proven to be true, often a mix of truth and untruth.
unverified information or gossip
british

Examples

There was a rumour at school that the principal was leaving.
She denied the rumour about her engagement to the actor.
The rumour quickly spread throughout the small town.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gossip - casual or unconstrained conversation about others, often involving unverified information
  • Antonym: fact - information that is known or proved to be true

Etymology

From Old French 'rumor', from Latin 'rumor' (noise, report, hearsay).

2. VERB

/ ˈruːmɚ / (AmE), / ˈruːmə / (BrE)
to circulate or spread information or reports that may not be true
circulate unverified information
british

Examples

The media rumoured that the actor was secretly married.
It was widely rumoured that the company would be sold.
Throughout the office, people rumoured about the manager's sudden resignation.

Synset Examples

It was rumored that the next president would be a woman

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gossip - to spread personal or sensational rumors
  • Antonym: to deny - to declare something is not true; opposite of spreading rumors

Etymology

From Old French 'rumor', from Latin 'rumor' (noise, hearsay).

Synset POS: v
