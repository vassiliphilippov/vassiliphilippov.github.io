to corrode

1. VERB

/ kəˈroʊd / (AmE), / kəˈrəʊd / (BrE)
to be gradually destroyed or damaged by a chemical process, especially by water, air, or an acid
be damaged by chemical action

Examples

If left unprotected, iron will eventually corrode when exposed to moisture in the air.
Saltwater can quickly corrode the metal parts of a ship if they aren't maintained.
Over decades, the underground pipes corroded and needed to be replaced.

Synset Examples

The metal corroded
The pipes rusted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rust - to become covered with a reddish-brown coating due to oxidation, especially iron

Etymology

From Latin 'corrodere', meaning 'to gnaw away', from 'cor-' (completely) + 'rodere' (to gnaw).

2. VERB

/ kəˈroʊd / (AmE), / kəˈrəʊd / (BrE)
(of a substance such as acid, water, or air) to destroy or damage something slowly, especially by chemical action.
to destroy or damage gradually by chemical action

Examples

The salt air will eventually corrode the metal fixtures on the boat.
Harsh chemicals can corrode the pipes if not handled carefully.
If left outside, the fence began corroding after just a few months of rain.

Synset Examples

The acid corroded the metal
The steady dripping of water rusted the metal stopper in the sink

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to erode - to gradually wear away or be worn away, often by natural forces like wind or water
  • Synonym: rust - to undergo corrosion, especially of iron or steel, typically forming a reddish-brown coating

Etymology

From Latin corrodere (to gnaw away), from com- (altogether) + rodere (to gnaw).
