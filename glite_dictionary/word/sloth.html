sloth

1. NOUN

/ sloʊθ / (AmE), / sləʊθ / (BrE)
Laziness or reluctance to work or act, especially as regarded as a moral failing and one of the seven deadly sins.
laziness; deadly sin of inactivity

Examples

The church warns that sloth can be just as destructive as anger or greed.
In medieval times, sloth was condemned as a serious moral failing.
Overcoming sloth requires both discipline and a sense of purpose.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: laziness - lack of willingness to work or use energy
  • Antonym: diligence - careful and persistent work or effort

Etymology

From Old English 'slǣwþ', related to 'slaw' (slow).

2. NOUN

/ sloʊθ / (AmE), / sləʊθ / (BrE)
reluctance to work or make an effort; laziness
habitual laziness; unwillingness to work

Examples

His sloth made it difficult for him to keep a steady job.
Teachers often warn against sloth as a barrier to academic success.
Despite his intelligence, his sloth prevented him from achieving more.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: laziness - the quality of being unwilling to work or use energy
  • Antonym: industriousness - the quality of being diligent and hardworking

Etymology

From Old English *slǣwth* (laziness), related to 'slothful'.

3. NOUN

/ sloʊθ / (AmE), / sləʊθ / (BrE)
a slow-moving, tree-dwelling mammal native to Central and South America, known for hanging upside down from branches and eating leaves and fruits
a slow-moving tree-dwelling mammal

Examples

The sloth spends most of its life hanging upside down from tree branches in the rainforest.
At the zoo, we watched a sloth slowly reach out to grab a leaf.
Scientists study the sloth to learn how it has adapted to a low-energy lifestyle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bradypus - three-toed sloth, a type of sloth
  • Synonym: laziness - the quality of being unwilling to work or use energy (primary sense of sloth)

Etymology

From Middle English 'sloth', related to Old English 'slæwð', originally denoting 'laziness', later applied to the animal for its slow movement.
