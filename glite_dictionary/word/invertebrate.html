invertebrate

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈvɜrtɪbrət / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɜːtɪbrət / (BrE)
(of an animal) lacking a backbone or spinal column.
lacking a backbone or spinal column

Examples

Jellyfish are considered invertebrate species because they have no spinal column.
Many invertebrate animals, such as insects and worms, live in the soil.
The study focused on several invertebrate groups found in marine environments.

Synset Examples

worms are an example of invertebrate animals

Related Terms

  • Antonym: vertebrate - (of an animal) having a backbone or spinal column

Etymology

From Latin in- (not) + vertebratus (having joints, vertebrae).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɜrtəˌbreɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɜːtɪbrət / (BrE)
an animal that does not have a backbone or spinal column, such as an insect, worm, or mollusk; not used as a precise scientific classification
animal lacking a backbone

Examples

Jellyfish are one of the most well-known groups of invertebrates in the ocean.
Earthworms are invertebrates that play a crucial role in soil health.
Unlike mammals, invertebrates do not have a backbone.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: vertebrate - an animal with a backbone or spinal column

Etymology

From modern Latin 'invertebrata', from Latin 'in-' (not) + 'vertebratus' (having joints or vertebrae).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01908302-n
Synset POS: n
