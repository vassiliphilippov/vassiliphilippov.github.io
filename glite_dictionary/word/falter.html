to falter

1. VERB

/ ˈfɔltər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to become weaker or less certain; to lose strength, confidence, or determination
become weak or unsteady

Examples

Her confidence began to falter when she faced unexpected challenges.
The runner started to falter as exhaustion set in during the final lap.
If their resolve falters now, the project may never be completed.

Synset Examples

Their enthusiasm is faltering

Related Terms

  • Synonym: waver - to go back and forth between choices or opinions, to become unsteady
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to become stronger or more firm

Etymology

From Old French 'faltrer', probably from a blend of 'faillir' (to fail) and 'halter' (to totter).

2. VERB

/ ˈfɑltər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to walk unsteadily or with repeated tripping, often due to weakness or uncertainty
to walk unsteadily; trip repeatedly

Examples

Exhausted after the long hike, she began to falter along the rocky path.
He faltered as he tried to make his way through the darkness, nearly losing his balance.
The injured athlete faltered down the corridor, barely able to stay upright.

Synset Examples

The drunk man stumbled about

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stumble - to trip or lose balance while walking; usually used as a synonym
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01905126-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈfɔltɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to move unsteadily or hesitantly, as if about to fall or lose strength
move hesitantly or unsteadily

Examples

He began to falter as he crossed the rocky path, nearly losing his balance.
The runner seemed to falter at the halfway mark, but she quickly regained her pace.
As the ground shook, the tall stack of books started to falter and sway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stumble - to trip or momentarily lose balance while walking or running
  • Antonym: to recover - to regain balance or composure after faltering

Etymology

From Middle English 'falteren', of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01905282-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈfɔltər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to speak hesitantly or unsteadily, often due to uncertainty or emotion
speak hesitantly or unsteadily

Examples

She began to falter when asked about her decision to quit the job.
His voice would falter whenever he spoke of his late parents.
The witness seemed nervous and started to falter during the cross-examination.

Synset Examples

The speaker faltered when he saw his opponent enter the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stammer - to speak with sudden involuntary pauses and a tendency to repeat the initial letters of words
  • Antonym: to speak clearly - to speak in a way that is easy to understand without hesitation

Etymology

From Old French falter, of uncertain origin; perhaps related to Old Norse faltr (stagger).

Source: WordNet
