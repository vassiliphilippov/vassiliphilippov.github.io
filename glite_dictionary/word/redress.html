redress

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈdrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈdrɛs / (BrE)
The act of setting right a wrong or making amends for an error, fault, or injustice.
act of correcting a wrong or fault

Examples

The victims sought redress through the courts for the damages they suffered.
There is little hope of redress if the government ignores the complaint.
He demanded redress for the injustice he had endured.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remedy - a means of correcting a wrong or making things right
  • Synonym: compensation - something, typically money, awarded to make up for a loss or injury

Etymology

From Old French *redresser* (to put upright again), from Latin *redirectus* (set straight again).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈdrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈdrɛs / (BrE)
to correct or set right something that is unfair or wrong; to make amends or compensation for a grievance or loss
make amends or set right (a wrong)

Examples

The government promised to redress the injustices suffered by minority groups.
She sought to redress the damage caused by the error in the report.
Lawsuits were filed to redress those harmed by the defective product.

Synset Examples

right a wrong done to the victims of the Holocaust

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compensate - to give (someone) something, typically money, in recognition of loss or suffering
  • Synonym: remedy - to set right (an undesirable situation)
  • Antonym: to aggravate - to make (a problem, injury, or offense) worse

Etymology

From Old French 'redresser' meaning 'straighten up again,' from re- (again) + dresser (to straighten).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈdrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈdrɛs / (BrE)
Money or other compensation given for a loss, injury, or wrong.
compensation for loss or injury

Examples

The court awarded substantial redress to the victims of the accident.
If you suffer a financial loss, you may seek redress through legal means.
Many people feel they have no redress when bureaucracy makes a mistake.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compensation - something, typically money, awarded to someone as a recompense for loss, injury, or suffering

Etymology

From Old French 'redrecier' (to make straight, restore), from 're-' (again) + 'drecier' (to straighten).

Synset POS: n
