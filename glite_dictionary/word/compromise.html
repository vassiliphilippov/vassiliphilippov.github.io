compromise

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːm.prəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prə.maɪz / (BrE)
an agreement in which each side gives up some demands or makes concessions to reach a settlement
agreement by mutual concession

Examples

After hours of negotiation, they finally reached a compromise acceptable to both sides.
Finding a compromise between the two proposals was challenging but ultimately worthwhile.
She refused to settle for a poor compromise and insisted on a solution that benefited everyone.

Synset Examples

the newly elected congressmen rejected a compromise because they considered it ‘business as usual’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agreement - a negotiated and typically binding arrangement between parties
  • Antonym: disagreement - a lack of consensus or harmony between parties

Etymology

From Latin 'compromissum' (a mutual promise), from 'compromittere' (to make a mutual promise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07194002-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːmprəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmprəˌmaɪz / (BrE)
to settle a dispute by each side making concessions; to reach an agreement in which both sides reduce their demands
to settle by mutual concessions

Examples

The two sides finally agreed to compromise after hours of negotiation.
Rather than argue all night, they decided to compromise and split the bill.
If everyone is willing to compromise, we can find a solution that works for all.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to negotiate - to discuss and settle an agreement
  • Synonym: to concede - to yield or give up something in an argument

Etymology

From Late Latin *compromissum* (a mutual promise), from Latin *compromittere* (to make a mutual promise).

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.prəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prə.maɪz / (BrE)
to expose something or someone to risk, danger, or suspicion; to endanger or undermine.
expose to risk, danger, or suspicion

Examples

He refused to compromise the integrity of the investigation for personal gain.
The company’s security system was compromised by a cyber attack.
Leaking the confidential report would compromise the reputation of the organization.

Synset Examples

The nuclear secrets of the state were compromised by the spy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endanger - to put someone or something at risk or in danger
  • Synonym: to jeopardize - to put in danger or at risk of harm

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *compromissum* (a mutual promise to abide by an arbiter's decision); broadened in modern English.

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmprəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prə.maɪz / (BrE)
an agreement or settlement of a dispute that is reached by each side making concessions; a solution that is a middle way between two extremes
middle way between two extremes

Examples

After hours of debate, the committee reached a compromise that satisfied both sides.
Successful relationships often require some level of compromise from both partners.
She refused to accept the compromise because it did not meet her key demands.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agreement - a negotiated and typically legally binding arrangement between parties
  • Antonym: conflict - a serious disagreement or argument, typically a protracted one
  • Synonym: conciliation - the action of stopping someone from being angry; the action of mediating between two disputing people or groups

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'compromissum' (a mutual promise), from Latin 'compromittere' (to make a mutual promise), from 'com-' (together) + 'promittere' (to promise).

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.prəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prə.maɪz / (BrE)
to settle a dispute by mutual concession; to accept less than one wants in order to reach an agreement
to reach agreement by mutual concession

Examples

Sometimes you have to compromise when working on a team project.
Negotiators failed to compromise, so the talks broke down.
If neither side is willing to compromise, the conflict may continue indefinitely.

Synset Examples

nobody will get everything he wants: we all must compromise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to concede - to admit or accept something, often reluctantly, in an argument
  • Synonym: to agree - to have the same opinion or to consent
  • Antonym: refuse - to indicate or show that one is not willing to do something

Etymology

From Medieval Latin compromissum (a mutual promise to abide by an arbiter's decision), from Latin compromittere (to make a mutual promise).
