idealistic

1. ADJ

/ aɪˌdiəˈlɪstɪk / (AmE), / aɪˌdɪəˈlɪstɪk / (BrE)
Relating to or characteristic of the philosophical doctrine that reality is fundamentally based on ideas or the mind rather than material objects.
relating to the doctrine of idealism in philosophy

Examples

His idealistic beliefs led him to view the world as fundamentally shaped by ideas, not by material forces.
Many philosophers in the 18th century defended idealistic theories that prioritized mind over matter.
The idealistic tradition in philosophy argues that reality depends on the mental rather than the physical.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: materialistic - relating to the doctrine that physical matter is the only reality

Etymology

From 'idealism' + '-ic', with roots in Latin 'idea' via Greek 'idea'.

2. ADJ

/ aɪˌdiəˈlɪstɪk / (AmE), / aɪˌdɪəˈlɪstɪk / (BrE)
Having or showing high moral or intellectual values; aspiring to noble, lofty, or elevated principles.
aspiring to high moral or intellectual ideals

Examples

Her idealistic vision inspired others to strive for a better world.
The speech was filled with idealistic notions of justice and equality.
Young people are often more idealistic before they experience the realities of life.

Synset Examples

an exalted ideal
argue in terms of high-flown ideals
a noble and lofty concept
a grand purpose

Related Terms

  • Antonym: realistic - accepting things as they are; practical and pragmatic

Etymology

From ideal + -istic, ultimately from Greek 'idea'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01593026-s
Synset POS: s
