to famish

1. VERB

/ ˈfæmɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfæmɪʃ / (BrE)
to suffer severely from hunger or to starve
suffer from extreme hunger

Examples

After three days lost in the forest, we began to famish and grew weak.
Many people famished during the long drought.
You will famish if you do not eat something soon.

Synset Examples

Let's eat — I'm starving!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to starve - to suffer or die from lack of food
  • Antonym: feed - to eat or supply with food

Etymology

From Middle English *famisshen*, from Old French *afamiss-,* from Latin *fames* (hunger).

2. VERB

/ ˈfæmɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfæmɪʃ / (BrE)
to suffer severely or die from extreme lack of food; to starve
suffer or die from lack of food

Examples

Many villagers began to famish after the crop failure.
During the siege, thousands were left to famish without supplies.
If left unattended, the animals could famish in their cages.

Synset Examples

The political prisoners starved to death
Many famished in the countryside during the drought

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to starve - to suffer or die from hunger

Etymology

From Middle English *famisshen*, from Old French *afamissir* (to cause to starve), from Latin *fames* (hunger).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01190070-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈfæmɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfæmɪʃ / (BrE)
to deprive someone of food; to cause to suffer from extreme hunger
deprive of food; cause starvation

Examples

The drought threatened to famish the livestock in the region.
Cruel leaders would sometimes famish prisoners as a means of control.
If you famish a plant of light and water, it will wither and die.

Synset Examples

They starved the prisoners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to starve - to suffer or die from lack of food

Etymology

From Middle English *famisshen*, from Old French *afamiss-,* from Latin *fames* (hunger).
