to publicise

1. VERB

/ ˈpʌblɪsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpʌblɪsaɪz / (BrE)
to make something known to the public; to announce or promote openly
to make something publicly known

Examples

The company plans to publicise the new product through a major advertising campaign.
She decided to publicise her findings in the local newspaper.
The charity event was poorly attended because it wasn't well publicised.

Synset Examples

She aired her opinions on welfare

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to announce - to make a formal public statement about something
  • Antonym: to conceal - to prevent something from being known or seen

Etymology

From 'public', influenced by French 'publiciser', patterned after 'advertise'.

2. VERB

/ ˈpʌblɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpʌblɪsaɪz / (BrE)
to make information about something widely known; to draw public attention to something
make widely known; draw public attention
british

Examples

The charity used social media to publicise its latest fundraising campaign.
It's important not to publicise confidential information accidentally.
The event was poorly attended because it wasn't publicised enough.

Synset Examples

Please don't advertise the fact that he has AIDS

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to advertise - to publicize information for promotion or attention
  • Antonym: to conceal - to keep something secret or hidden

Etymology

From public + -ise, based on the root 'public' from Latin 'publicus'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00977934-v
Synset POS: v
