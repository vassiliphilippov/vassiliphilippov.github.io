mistress

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪstrəs / (AmE), / ˈmɪstrəs / (BrE)
a woman who is having a continuing sexual relationship with a married man, typically without being his wife
a man's female lover outside marriage

Examples

The politician's affair with his mistress eventually became public knowledge.
He rented a small apartment for his mistress in the city.
Rumors spread quickly when he was seen dining with his longtime mistress.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lover - a romantic or sexual partner
  • Antonym: wife - the woman a man is married to

Etymology

From Old French 'maistresse', feminine of 'maistre' (master), from Latin 'magister'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɪstrəs / (AmE), / ˈmɪstrɪs / (BrE)
a woman who has authority, control, or power over others, especially one who directs the work of others
female boss or woman in authority

Examples

She was appointed as the new mistress of the school, overseeing all teachers and students.
The stern mistress of the house managed the staff with efficiency and care.
As mistress of the estate, she organized daily tasks and gave instructions to the workers.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: master - a man who has control or authority over others

Etymology

From Old French 'maistresse', feminine of 'maistre' (master), from Latin 'magister'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɪstrəs / (AmE), / ˈmɪstrɪs / (BrE)
(historical, chiefly British) A woman who teaches in a school, especially one known for being strict or authoritative.
female schoolteacher (esp. strict, historical)
archaic british

Examples

The new mistress soon earned a reputation for discipline and high expectations among her pupils.
As a child, he was terrified of his strict mistress at the village school.
In Victorian novels, the head mistress is often depicted as both feared and respected.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: schoolmistress - a woman who teaches at a school, especially in a position of authority
  • Antonym: schoolmaster - a man who teaches at a school, especially in charge or in authority

Etymology

From Old French *maistresse*, feminine of *maistre* (master), from Latin *magistra*.
