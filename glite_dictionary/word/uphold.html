to uphold

1. VERB

/ ʌpˈhoʊld / (AmE), / ʌpˈhəʊld / (BrE)
to maintain or keep in its original or unaltered state; to cause something to continue or last.
maintain or keep unchanged

Examples

It is important to uphold the traditional customs passed down through generations.
A judge must uphold the law without personal bias or favoritism.
Despite challenges, the organization strives to uphold its core values.

Synset Examples

preserve the peace in the family
continue the family tradition
Carry on the old traditions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preserve - to keep something safe from harm or change
  • Synonym: to maintain - to keep in existence or continuance

Etymology

From Old English 'uphǣldan', from 'up' + 'holdan' (to hold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02685709-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ʌpˈhoʊld / (AmE), / ʌpˈhəʊld / (BrE)
to support or maintain a decision, law, or principle, especially in the face of opposition
support or maintain, especially legally

Examples

The judge decided to uphold the ban despite several appeals.
The board voted to uphold the new policy, ignoring public complaints.
Her conviction was upheld by the higher court after a lengthy review.

Synset Examples

The appellate court upheld the verdict

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to overrule - to reject or set aside a decision or ruling
  • Synonym: to affirm - to state or assert positively; to confirm a decision

Etymology

From Middle English 'upholden', combining 'up' and 'hold'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00898147-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ʌpˈhoʊld / (AmE), / ʌpˈhəʊld / (BrE)
to support or defend a law, principle, or idea, often by approving or maintaining it
support or defend a principle or idea

Examples

The judge promised to uphold the Constitution at all times.
We have a duty to uphold the values of honesty and integrity.
Despite criticism, she continued to uphold her beliefs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defend - to protect or support something from attack or criticism
  • Antonym: reject - to refuse to accept, use, or believe something

Etymology

From Old English 'uphéaldan', from 'up' (up) + 'healdan' (to hold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00897613-v
Synset POS: v
