ungregarious

1. ADJ

/ ʌnɡrɪˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ʌnɡrɪˈɡɛəriəs / (BrE)
not inclined to seek the company of others; preferring to be alone
unsociable; preferring solitude

Examples

Despite his colleagues' invitations, he remained ungregarious and preferred to eat lunch alone.
The ungregarious nature of the animal made it difficult to study in groups.
She was an ungregarious child, often found reading by herself rather than playing with others.

Synset Examples

a lonely ungregarious person

Related Terms

  • Antonym: gregarious - fond of company; sociable

Etymology

Formed by adding the prefix un- (not) to 'gregarious', from Latin 'gregarius' (belonging to a flock).

2. ADJ

/ ʌnˌɡrɛˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ʌnɡrɪˈɡɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
(of plants) growing in groups that are not close together; not forming dense clusters.
not growing in close groups (plants)

Examples

Certain wildflowers are ungregarious and appear scattered rather than grouped tightly together.
Unlike its gregarious relatives, this species is typically ungregarious, spreading out over open ground.
Botanists observed that the plant's ungregarious habit allowed it to avoid competition for resources.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: gregarious - (of plants) growing in dense clusters or groups

Etymology

Formed by prefixing un- (not) to 'gregarious', which derives from Latin 'gregarius' (belonging to a flock or herd).

3. ADJ

/ ˌʌnɡrɪˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ˌʌnɡrɪˈɡɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
(of animals) tending not to form groups; not sociable or not living in flocks or communities.
not forming groups (of animals)

Examples

The ungregarious nature of the species makes it difficult to observe in the wild.
Unlike its relatives, this bird is ungregarious and prefers to live alone.
Most insects in this group are ungregarious and rarely form colonies.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: gregarious - (of animals) tending to form groups or live in flocks

Etymology

From un- (not) + gregarious, from Latin 'gregarius' (belonging to a flock or herd).

Synset POS: a
