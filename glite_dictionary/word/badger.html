badger

1. NOUN

/ ˈbædʒər / (AmE), / ˈbædʒə / (BrE)
a sturdy carnivorous burrowing mammal with strong claws, widely distributed in the Northern Hemisphere, known for its broad body and distinct facial markings
burrowing carnivorous mammal

Examples

We saw a badger digging a hole in the forest last night.
The badger has distinctive black and white stripes on its face.
Farmers sometimes have to protect their crops from hungry badgers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: otter - aquatic, fish-eating mammal related to the weasel

Etymology

From early 16th century, perhaps from badge (referring to its facial markings) + -er.

2. VERB

/ ˈbædʒɚ / (AmE), / ˈbadʒə / (BrE)
to repeatedly ask or pressure someone to do something; to pester or nag persistently
to pester or nag persistently

Examples

The reporter continued to badger the politician with tough questions.
His little sister would badger him until he agreed to play the game.
I felt annoyed when my coworkers badgered me about joining their project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pester - to trouble or annoy someone with frequent requests
  • Synonym: to harass - to bother or pressure persistently

Etymology

From the noun 'badger' (the animal), possibly from the animal’s perceived persistence or tenacity.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00769575-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbædʒɚ / (AmE), / ˈbadʒə / (BrE)
to repeatedly annoy or pester someone with persistent requests or questions
annoy or pester repeatedly

Examples

The reporters continued to badger the politician with the same questions all morning.
Stop badgering me about my decision—I've already made up my mind.
She felt badgered by her parents to study harder for the exam.

Synset Examples

The children teased the boy because of his stammer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pester - to bother or annoy someone repeatedly
  • Synonym: to harass - to trouble or worry someone by attacking them repeatedly
  • Antonym: to leave alone - to refrain from bothering or disturbing someone

Etymology

From the noun 'badger', alluding to the persistent nature of the animal when attacked.

Source: WordNet
