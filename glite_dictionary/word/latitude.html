latitude

1. NOUN

/ ˈlætɪˌtud / (AmE), / ˈlætɪˌtjuːd / (BrE)
freedom to act or think within particular limits; scope for individual choice or action
freedom to act or think without restriction

Examples

The manager was given considerable latitude to make decisions for the team.
Teachers have some latitude in designing their lesson plans.
He appreciated the latitude his new job offered in terms of creativity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leeway - extra freedom or flexibility in action or decision
  • Antonym: restraint - limitation or restriction of freedom

Etymology

From Latin latitudo (breadth, width), referring metaphorically to breadth of freedom.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlæ.t̬ɪˌtuːd / (AmE), / ˈlæt.ɪ.tjuːd / (BrE)
an imaginary line around the Earth parallel to the equator, used to measure distance north or south
imaginary line parallel to the equator

Examples

The city lies on the same latitude as Cairo, making their climates similar.
Sailors used to determine their position at sea by measuring latitude with instruments like the sextant.
Tropical regions are located between the latitudes of the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: longitude - imaginary line running from the North Pole to the South Pole

Etymology

From Latin latitudo (breadth, width), from latus (broad).

4. NOUN

/ ˈlæt̬əˌtud / (AmE), / ˈlætɪtjuːd / (BrE)
the angular distance of a place north or south of the equator, measured in degrees along a meridian
angular distance from the equator (geography)

Examples

The city of Cairo is situated at a latitude of roughly 30 degrees north.
Sailors used the stars to determine their latitude while navigating across the ocean.
As you move towards higher latitude, the climate generally becomes colder.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: longitude - the angular distance east or west of the prime meridian

Etymology

From Latin latitudo (breadth, width), from latus (wide).
