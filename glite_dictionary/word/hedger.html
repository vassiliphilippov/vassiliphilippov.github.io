hedger

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɛdʒər / (AmE), / ˈhɛdʒə / (BrE)
a person who trims and maintains hedges, especially as a gardener
gardener who trims hedges

Examples

The hedger arrived early to trim the overgrown bushes along the driveway.
As a skilled hedger, she shaped the shrubs into elegant patterns every spring.
They hired a professional hedger to maintain the formal gardens at the estate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gardener - a person who tends and cultivates a garden as an occupation or for pleasure

Etymology

From 'hedge' + '-er', indicating a person who performs the action.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɛdʒɚ / (AmE), / ˈhɛdʒə / (BrE)
a person or company that reduces financial risk by making counterbalancing investments or bets to protect against potential loss
person who reduces financial risk by counterbalancing

Examples

The company's hedger limited potential losses by investing in offsetting markets.
As a professional hedger, she routinely uses futures contracts to manage risk.
Many commodity producers hire a hedger to protect against price fluctuations.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: speculator - person who invests in stocks, property, etc. in the hope of profit

Etymology

From the verb 'hedge', meaning to limit risk (originally 'enclose with a hedge'); financial meaning recorded from the 17th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈhɛdʒər / (AmE), / ˈhɛdʒə / (BrE)
A person who avoids giving a clear or direct answer, especially to evade commitment or responsibility.
someone who avoids direct answers

Examples

The politician was known as a hedger who rarely gave straightforward answers during interviews.
During the meeting, he acted like a hedger, always dodging the tough questions.
It's difficult to trust a hedger when you want clear decisions or commitments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equivocator - a person who uses ambiguous language to conceal the truth
  • Antonym: straight-talker - someone who speaks plainly and honestly

Etymology

Derived from the verb 'hedge,' meaning to avoid giving a clear answer, originally from Old English 'hecg' (fence, boundary).
