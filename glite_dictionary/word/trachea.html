trachea

1. NOUN

/ ˈtreɪkiə / (AmE), / ˈtreɪkɪə / (BrE)
each of the small tubes forming part of the respiratory system of insects and many arachnids, through which air passes to the tissues
air tube in insects/arachnids
medical

Examples

Oxygen enters an insect's body through openings connected to the trachea.
The trachea in arachnids delivers air directly to their internal organs.
Scientists studied how dust particles move through the trachea of a beetle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trachea - the main airway (windpipe) in vertebrates including humans

Etymology

From Greek *tracheia* (windpipe), feminine of *tracheios* (rough).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtreɪ.ki.ə / (AmE), / ˈtræki.ə / (BrE)
the tube in the throat with cartilaginous rings that carries air from the larynx to the bronchi in the lungs
airway tube in the throat
medical

Examples

The doctor examined the patient's trachea for signs of injury.
Air passes through the trachea before reaching the lungs.
A blockage in the trachea can make breathing difficult.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: windpipe - another term for the trachea
  • Degree Lower: bronchus - major air passages branching from the trachea into the lungs

Etymology

From Greek 'tracheia' (windpipe), from 'trachys' (rough).
