to vegetate

1. VERB

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of a plant or fungus) to grow or develop in the manner of a plant.
to grow or develop like a plant

Examples

The mushrooms vegetate rapidly in this damp cellar.
This species of algae vegetates best in shallow water.
The mold vegetated vigorously on the decaying wood.

Synset Examples

This fungus usually vegetates vigorously

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to grow - to undergo natural development by increasing in size and changing physically

Etymology

From Latin vegetatus, past participle of vegetare (to enliven, quicken), from vegetus (lively, vigorous).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00232651-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈviːdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of land or fields) to produce vegetation or grow plants, especially with vigor.
to grow vegetation (land, fields)

Examples

After the rainy season, the barren land began to vegetate rapidly.
These fertile soils allow crops to vegetate throughout the year.
If properly irrigated, the fields will vegetate more vigorously each spring.

Synset Examples

The fields vegetate vigorously

Etymology

From Latin vegetatus, past participle of vegetare (to enliven, stimulate, quicken).

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)

to establish vegetation on land

4. VERB

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to spend time in a passive and inactive way, often relaxing without much mental effort; to be idle or unproductive
be idle or engage in passive relaxation
informal

Examples

After a long week at work, I just want to vegetate on the couch all weekend.
Sometimes it's good to vegetate and binge-watch your favorite shows without any guilt.
She spent her holiday vegetating at home with a pile of novels and takeout food.

Synset Examples

After a hard day's work, I vegetate in front of the television

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to laze - to spend time being lazy or idle
  • Synonym: idle - to spend time doing nothing; to be inactive

Etymology

From Latin vegetatus, past participle of vegetare (‘to enliven’), later used figuratively in English from 'vegetable'.

6. VERB

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of an organism) to reproduce or propagate by asexual means, especially by vegetative methods such as budding, fragmentation, or division.
asexually reproduce (biology)

Examples

Under favorable conditions, the fungus will vegetate by producing new hyphae asexually.
The bacterial colony vegetated across the nutrient medium, forming a visible layer.

Synset Examples

7. VERB

/ ˈvɛdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to spend time in a dull, inactive, or unchallenging way, without using one's body or mind actively
lead a passive, unchallenging life

Examples

After his retirement, he refused to vegetate in front of the television all day.
Without any goals or hobbies, she began to vegetate and felt her days slipping by.
If you vegetate over the summer, you'll find it hard to get back into a routine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to laze - to spend time in a relaxed, lazy way
  • Synonym: to stagnate - to stop developing or progressing
  • Antonym: to thrive - to grow, develop, or be successful

Etymology

From Latin 'vegetatus', past participle of 'vegetare' meaning 'to animate, enliven'.
