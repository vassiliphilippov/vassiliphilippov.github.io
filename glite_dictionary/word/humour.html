humour

1. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
the quality of being amusing or comical, or the ability to express things in an amusing way; that which provokes laughter or amusement
the quality of being amusing or comical

Examples

His dry humour always made meetings more enjoyable.
British humour is often based on understatement and irony.
She used her sense of humour to diffuse tense situations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wit - the ability to use words and ideas in a quick and inventive way to create humour
  • Antonym: seriousness - the quality of being solemn or earnest, lack of humour

Etymology

From Old French *humor*, from Latin *humor* (moisture, fluid), later associated with temperament and wit.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
(archaic) Any of the fluid substances in the body, especially as classified in ancient and medieval physiology.
(archaic) body fluid, esp. in ancient physiology
archaic

Examples

The ancient Greeks believed that the balance of bodily humours affected a person's health and temperament.
According to medieval medicine, an excess of any humour could cause disease.
The eye contains two main humours: the aqueous humour and the vitreous humour.

Etymology

From Latin 'humor' meaning 'moisture, body fluid'; originally from Proto-Indo-European root *gheu- (to pour, pour a libation).

5. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
(historical) In ancient and medieval medicine, any of the four bodily fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile) once believed to determine a person's health and temperament.
one of four bodily fluids in medieval medicine
archaic british

Examples

In medieval times, doctors believed that a person's health depended on the balance of the four humours.
Bloodletting was once used to restore the body's humours to their proper proportions.
The theory of humours influenced medical practice well into the Renaissance.

Synset Examples

the humors are blood and phlegm and yellow and black bile

Etymology

From Latin 'humor' (moisture, fluid), via Old French 'humour'.
