dissimilation

1. NOUN

/ dɪˌsɪməˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the process in language by which similar sounds in a word become less alike, often to make pronunciation easier
a linguistic process where similar sounds become less alike

Examples

In some dialects, the dissimilation of similar consonants creates new word forms.
The word 'colonel' in English is a result of historical dissimilation.
Linguists study dissimilation to understand how languages change over time.

Synset Examples

the Old French ‘marbre’ became the English ‘marble’ by dissimilation

Etymology

From Latin 'dissimulatio' meaning 'making unlike'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɪs.ɪ.məˈleɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪs.ɪ.mɪˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process in living organisms by which complex substances are broken down into simpler ones, often accompanied by the release of energy
breakdown of substances in organisms with energy release
medical

Examples

During intense exercise, dissimilation provides the necessary energy for muscle contraction.
Scientists study dissimilation to understand how cells obtain energy from nutrients.
Many metabolic diseases interfere with normal dissimilation processes in the body.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: assimilation - the biological process of incorporating substances into the body (opposite of dissimilation)

Etymology

From Latin dissimilatio, from dissimilare 'to become unlike'.
