logic

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɑːdʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɒdʒɪk / (BrE)
the quality of being reasonable and based on sound judgment; clear or valid reasoning
reasoned or sound judgment

Examples

Her argument had a certain logic, even if I didn't agree with it.
You can't argue with the logic of his conclusion.
There is a clear logic behind this decision.

Synset Examples

it made a certain kind of logic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reasoning - the action of thinking about something in a logical, sensible way
  • Antonym: irrationality - the quality of being illogical or unreasonable

Etymology

From Greek logikē (the art of reasoning), via Latin logica.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɑːdʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɒdʒɪk / (BrE)
the set of principles or operations by which a computer processes information and carries out instructions, forming the basis of computational functions and decision-making
rules or operations in computers

Examples

The computer's internal logic determines how it processes data and performs calculations.
Good programming requires an understanding of both software and hardware logic.
Digital logic circuits form the foundation of modern electronic devices.

Etymology

From Greek *logikē* (science of reasoning), via Latin *logica*.

Synset POS: n

/ ˈlɑː.dʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈlɒdʒ.ɪk / (BrE)

  • Antonym: illogic - lack of logical reasoning; absence of logic

Etymology
