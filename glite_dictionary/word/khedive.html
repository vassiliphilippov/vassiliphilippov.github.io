Khedive

1. NOUN

/ kəˈdiːv / (AmE), / kɪˈdiːv / (BrE)
The title of the Turkish viceroy who governed Egypt from 1867 to 1914, under the nominal authority of the Ottoman Empire.
viceroy of Egypt (1867–1914)

Examples

The Khedive of Egypt played a crucial role in the country's modernization during the late nineteenth century.
A British official met with the Khedive to discuss political reforms in Cairo.
After the defeat in World War I, the title of Khedive was abolished and replaced by Sultan.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: viceroy - a ruler exercising authority in a colony on behalf of a sovereign

Etymology

From French 'khédive', from Ottoman Turkish 'hidiv', from Persian 'khidīw' (prince, ruler).
