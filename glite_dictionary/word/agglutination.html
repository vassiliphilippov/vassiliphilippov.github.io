agglutination

1. NOUN

/ əˌɡluːtəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌɡluːtɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which suspended small particles or cells clump together to form larger masses, especially in a liquid
clumping of particles in a liquid
medical

Examples

The scientist observed the agglutination of red blood cells under the microscope.
When the antibody is present, agglutination occurs, causing visible clumps in the solution.
Proper identification of blood type prevents unwanted agglutination during transfusions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulation - the process by which a liquid, especially blood, changes to a thick or semi-solid state
  • Antonym: dispersion - the process of distributing particles more widely in a liquid, the opposite of coalescence

Etymology

From Latin agglutinatio (a bonding together), from ad- (to) + gluten (glue).

2. NOUN

/ əˌɡluːtəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌɡluːtɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
a clumping together of bacteria or red blood cells caused by antibodies binding to their surface.
clumping of cells due to antibodies
medical

Examples

The laboratory test revealed the agglutination of red blood cells, indicating an immune response.
When exposed to the matching antibody, the bacteria underwent rapid agglutination.
Doctors observed agglutination in the blood sample, which helped confirm the diagnosis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulation - the process of a liquid, especially blood, changing to a solid or semi-solid state

Etymology

From Latin agglutinatio (a sticking to).

3. NOUN

/ əˌɡluː.t̬əˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / əˌɡluː.tɪˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process in linguistics of forming words by combining morphemes, each retaining its original form and meaning
word formation by joining morphemes

Examples

Turkish is often cited as an example of a language that uses agglutination to form words.
The study of agglutination reveals how different languages construct complex meanings efficiently.
In agglutination, morphemes retain their shape and meaning as they are joined together.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inflection - modification of a word to express grammatical features
  • Antonym: fusion - word formation by merging morphemes, often losing distinct boundaries

Etymology

From Latin 'agglutinatio', from 'agglutinare' (to glue to).
