to orient

1. VERB

/ ˈɔːriənt / (AmE), / ˈɔːriənt / (BrE)
to align or position something or oneself in a particular direction or relative to a particular point; to determine the position or direction of something
align or position in a direction

Examples

Before construction, the architect will orient the building to maximize natural sunlight.
When you camp, it's important to orient your tent so the door faces away from the wind.
The map helped her orient herself after she got lost in the forest.

Synset Examples

The weather vane points North
the dancers toes pointed outward

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to align - to place in a straight line or in correct relative positions

Etymology

From Latin *orientem* (the east, rising sun), participle of *oriri* (to rise).

2. VERB

/ ˈɔːriˌɛnt, ˈɔr- / (AmE), / ˈɔːriənt / (BrE)
to find or determine one's position in relation to one's surroundings or to another reference point
find one's position relative to surroundings

Examples

It took us several minutes to orient ourselves after emerging from the subway.
The hikers paused to orient the map before continuing down the trail.
She tried to orient herself in the unfamiliar city streets.

Synset Examples

We had to orient ourselves in the forest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to locate - to identify or discover the exact place of something

Etymology

From French 'orienter', from Latin 'orient-', meaning 'rising (of the sun), east'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɔːriˌɛnt / (AmE), / ˈɔːriənt / (BrE)
to tailor or adapt something to meet specific needs, interests, or conditions
adapt; tailor toward needs or market

Examples

The company decided to orient its products toward eco-conscious consumers.
New courses have been oriented to meet the evolving needs of students.
She helped orient the website for an international audience.

Synset Examples

a magazine oriented towards young people
tailor your needs to your surroundings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tailor - to make or adapt for a particular purpose or person
  • Synonym: to adapt - to modify something to suit new conditions or uses

Etymology

From Latin orient-, oriri meaning 'to rise' (as in sunrise), originally referring to the east; later figuratively extended to 'direct' or 'align'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

5. VERB

to position or align something in a particular direction, especially in relation to the points of a compass
align or position in a direction

Examples

Before building, the architect will orient the house to maximize sunlight.
Pilots must orient the aircraft towards the runway for a safe landing.
You should orient your desk so that you face away from distractions.

Synset Examples

Orient the house towards the West

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to align - to place or arrange things in a straight line or in correct relative positions

Etymology

From Latin 'orient-', present participle of 'oriri' (to rise), referring originally to the rising sun (east).
