slapstick

1. NOUN

/ ˈslæp.stɪk / (AmE), / ˈslæp.stɪk / (BrE)
comedy that involves exaggerated physical actions, collisions, and practical jokes, often resulting in humorous mishaps
broad, boisterous physical comedy

Examples

The old silent movies are full of slapstick that still makes audiences laugh today.
The comedian is famous for his use of slapstick in stage performances.
Children especially enjoy slapstick humor with its clumsy falls and silly chases.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: farce - a light dramatic work marked by broadly satirical comedy and improbable plot

Etymology

From the Italian 'batacchio', a device used by actors to make a slapping sound on stage.

2. NOUN

/ ˈslæpˌstɪk / (AmE), / ˈslæp.stɪk / (BrE)
A device made of two flat pieces of wood joined by a hinge, used in theatrical performances to produce a loud slapping noise, especially to simulate a blow without causing injury.
theatrical noise-making device

Examples

The actor used a slapstick to create the sound of a punch during the comedy scene.
A traditional slapstick can make a surprisingly loud noise when struck together.
Stagehands prepared the slapstick before the performance to ensure the sound effects were convincing.

Etymology

Named for the literal stick used in Italian commedia dell'arte to make a slapping sound.

3. ADJ

/ ˈslæpˌstɪk / (AmE), / ˈslæp.stɪk / (BrE)
involving exaggerated physical activity, horseplay, or boisterous humor, typically intended to provoke laughter
involving exaggerated physical comedy

Examples

The comedian's slapstick routines left the audience in fits of laughter.
Many classic silent films rely heavily on slapstick humor to entertain viewers.
She prefers clever wordplay to the slapstick antics typical of some sitcoms.

Synset Examples

slapstick style of humor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: farce - comedy that uses improbable situations and exaggeration
  • Antonym: subtle - not obvious or showy in approach or style

Etymology

From the name of a prop (Italian 'batacchio' or 'slap stick') used in 16th-century Italian commedia dell'arte to produce a slapping sound.
