hype

1. VERB

/ haɪp / (AmE), / haɪp / (BrE)
to promote or publicize something in an exaggerated or misleading way, often generating more excitement than is justified
promote in an exaggerated way
informal

Examples

The company decided to hype the new smartphone with flashy ads and celebrity endorsements.
Critics accused the studio of hyping the movie far beyond its actual quality.
He hyped his skills during the interview, which made him seem more qualified than he really was.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to promote - encourage or support the publicity of something

Etymology

Probably a back-formation from hyperbole or hype (noun, swindle).

2. NOUN

/ haɪp / (AmE), / haɪp / (BrE)
Exaggerated or sensational publicity or promotion of something, often to attract attention or boost interest.
exaggerated or sensational promotion
informal

Examples

The movie failed to live up to its hype.
There's a lot of hype surrounding the new phone release.
Despite all the hype, the product was disappointing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: publicity - attention given to someone or something by the media
  • Antonym: understatement - the presentation of something as being smaller or less important than it really is

Etymology

Probably shortened from 'hyperbole' or from 1920s slang 'hyp' (to stimulate, from 'hypodermic', as in hypodermic needle).

3. NOUN

/ haɪp / (AmE), / haɪp / (BrE)
exaggerated or intensive publicity or promotion, often intended to excite interest
exaggerated publicity or promotion

Examples

The hype around the new phone made people stand in line overnight.
Don't believe all the hype until you've tried it yourself.
After all the hype, the movie was actually disappointing.

Synset Examples

The hype for new games is huge before GTA 6.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: publicity - the attention given to someone or something by the media
  • Synonym: buzz - excited interest or attention, especially as generated by discussion or media
  • Antonym: understatement - presentation of something as being smaller or less important than it actually is

Etymology

Probably a shortened form of 'hyperbole' or influenced by 'hypo' (from Greek, under).
