lobby

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɑː.bi / (AmE), / ˈlɒb.i / (BrE)
a group of people who try to influence politicians or public officials on a particular issue
group seeking to influence legislation

Examples

The tobacco lobby spent millions of dollars to oppose new regulations.
Environmental groups formed a lobby to push for stricter pollution controls.
There has been criticism of the influence wielded by the tech lobby in government decision-making.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pressure group - organization that tries to influence public policy
  • Synonym: interest group - group organized to promote a particular interest or cause

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'lobia', meaning a covered walk or portico, later extended in English to refer to groups gathering in lobbies to influence politicians.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɑː.bi / (AmE), / ˈlɒb.i / (BrE)
a large entrance or reception area inside a building, especially at the front
large entrance or reception area in a building

Examples

Please wait for me in the lobby before the concert begins.
The hotel lobby was decorated with fresh flowers and comfortable chairs.
She met her friends in the lobby after checking out of her room.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foyer - an entrance hall or other open area in a building
  • Synonym: vestibule - a small entryway within a building

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *lobia*, *laubia* (covered walk or hall), probably of Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɑːbi / (AmE), / ˈlɒbi / (BrE)
a group of people who try to influence politicians or public officials on a particular issue or in support of a specific cause or interest
group seeking to influence policy

Examples

The environmental lobby is pushing for stricter emissions regulations in parliament.
A powerful gun rights lobby poured millions into the election campaign.
Consumer advocates accused the tobacco lobby of misleading the public about health risks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interest group - an organized group seeking to influence public policy
  • Synonym: pressure group - a group trying to influence public policy in its favor

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *lobia* (covered walk, gallery); sense as pressure group developed in 19th-century US politics.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
