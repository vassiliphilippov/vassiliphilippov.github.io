to pressurise

1. VERB

/ ˈprɛʃəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəraɪz / (BrE)
to increase the pressure in or of something, especially by means of a mechanical system
increase the pressure in something
british

Examples

The engineers need to pressurise the system before testing for leaks.
To maintain comfort, airlines pressurise the cabins during flight.
He pressurised the container to simulate deep-sea conditions.

Synset Examples

The captain will pressurize the cabin for the passengers' comfort

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pressurize - increase the pressure in or of (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'pressure' + '-ise', modeled after the American 'pressurize'.

2. VERB

/ ˈprɛʃəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəraɪz / (BrE)
to maintain or increase the pressure in a closed space or vessel, especially to a level higher than atmospheric pressure
maintain or increase pressure in a closed space
british

Examples

The engineers need to pressurise the chamber before anyone can enter.
Modern aircraft automatically pressurise the cabin at high altitudes.
They pressurised the space suit to ensure the astronaut's safety.

Synset Examples

the airplane cabin is pressurized
pressurize a space suit

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pressurize - maintain a certain pressure (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'pressure' + '-ise', modelled after similar technical terms.

3. VERB

/ ˈprɛʃəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈprɛʃəraɪz / (BrE)
to increase the pressure in a gas or liquid, especially by means of a mechanical device
increase pressure in gas or liquid
british

Examples

The engineer had to pressurise the tank before starting the experiment.
Modern aircraft cabins are designed to pressurise automatically at high altitudes.
It is important to pressurise the system to test for any leaks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pressurize - to increase the pressure in a gas or liquid (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'pressure' + '-ise', a suffix used in British English to form verbs.
