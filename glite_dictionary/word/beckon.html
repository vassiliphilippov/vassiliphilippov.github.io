to beckon

1. VERB

/ ˈbɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈbɛk.ən / (BrE)
to make a gesture with the hand, head, or other part of the body to encourage someone to come nearer or follow
summon by a gesture

Examples

She beckoned to the waiter to bring the bill.
The old man beckoned me over with a gentle wave of his hand.
Standing at the door, she beckoned her friend inside.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gesture - a movement of part of the body to express an idea or meaning
  • Synonym: signal - an action that conveys information or instructions

Etymology

From Old English *bececnian* (to make a sign).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈbɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈbɛk.ən / (BrE)
to appear inviting or attractive, suggesting that someone should approach or take an interest
appear inviting or attractive

Examples

The warm glow of the fireplace beckoned us to come inside and escape the cold.
A promising future beckons those who work hard and stay dedicated to their goals.
The aroma of freshly baked bread beckoned from the open kitchen door.

Synset Examples

The shop window decorations beckoned

Related Terms

  • Synonym: invite - to encourage someone to come or take part
  • Antonym: to repel - to drive away or discourage from approaching

Etymology

From Old English *bececnian* (to make a sign), related to 'beacon'.

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈbɛk.ən / (BrE)
to make a gesture with the hand, arm, or head to encourage someone to come nearer or follow
gesture to signal someone to approach

Examples

The teacher beckoned the students to gather around her desk.
He beckoned to his friend from across the crowded room.
She stood at the door, beckoning for us to enter.

Synset Examples

She waved to her friends
He waved his hand hospitably

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gesture - a movement of part of the body to express an idea or meaning
  • Synonym: wave - move one's hand to signal or greet

Etymology

From Old English *béċnian* (to signify), related to 'beacon'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01043287-v
Synset POS: v
