to nauseate

1. VERB

/ ˈnɔziˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˈnɔːzieɪt / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel disgust or a strong sense of aversion, especially in a moral or figurative sense
cause moral disgust or aversion

Examples

Many people nauseate at the very thought of animal cruelty.
The politician's blatant lies nauseated the public and fueled outrage.
She found his lack of empathy so troubling that it truly nauseated her.

Synset Examples

The pornographic pictures sickened us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to offend - to cause displeasure or insult by violating social or moral norms
  • Synonym: disgust - to cause a feeling of revulsion or profound disapproval

Etymology

From Latin nauseare (to feel seasick), from nausea (seasickness).

2. VERB

/ ˈnɔːziˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˈnɔːzieɪt / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel sick to their stomach or to feel disgusted
cause to feel sick or disgusted

Examples

The strong smell of bleach can nauseate some people instantly.
Seeing the rotten food in the fridge was enough to nauseate her.
He was so nauseated by the graphic scenes that he had to leave the room.

Synset Examples

The smell of the food turned the pregnant woman's stomach
The mold on the food sickened the diners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sicken - to make or become ill or disgusted
  • Synonym: disgust - to cause a strong feeling of dislike or revulsion

Etymology

From Latin nauseāre ('to feel sick'), from nausea ('seasickness, sickness').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02199637-v
Synset POS: v
