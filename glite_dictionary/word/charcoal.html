charcoal

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʃɑːrˌkoʊl / (AmE), / ˈtʃɑːkəʊl / (BrE)
a very dark grey color, similar to the color of burned wood
very dark grey color

Examples

The designer chose a charcoal suit for a sophisticated look.
She painted her living room walls a subtle charcoal to create a modern atmosphere.
The sky turned a deep charcoal before the storm began.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: gray - a color between black and white
  • Degree Higher: black - the darkest color

Etymology

From Old French 'charcal', from 'char' (charred) + 'cole' (coal). Originally referred to the substance before describing the color.

4. NOUN

/ ˈtʃɑrˌkoʊl / (AmE), / ˈtʃɑːkəʊl / (BrE)
a black, carbon-rich material made by heating wood or other organic substances in the absence of air, often used as fuel or for drawing.
black carbon material from burned wood

Examples

He drew a portrait using only charcoal and paper.
The steaks were cooked over a charcoal grill, giving them a smoky flavor.
Artists often use charcoal for sketching because of its rich, dark lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coal - a combustible black or dark brown rock formed from fossilized plant matter, used as fuel

Etymology

From Old French 'charboucle' (charred coal), from Late Latin 'carbunculus', diminutive of 'carbo' (coal).
