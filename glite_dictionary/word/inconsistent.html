inconsistent

1. ADJ

/ ˌɪnkənˈsɪstənt / (AmE), / ˌɪnkənˈsɪstənt / (BrE)
not able to be made consistent or in agreement; lacking harmony or compatibility
not able to be consistent or harmonious

Examples

The witness gave inconsistent testimony throughout the trial.
Their plans were inconsistent with each other, leading to confusion.
You cannot base your argument on inconsistent data.

Synset Examples

inconsistent accounts

Related Terms

  • Antonym: compatible - able to exist or work together without conflict
  • Antonym: consistent - acting or done in the same way over time; not self-contradictory

Etymology

From Latin 'in-' (not) + 'consistens', present participle of 'consistere' (to stand together).

2. ADJ

/ ˌɪnkənˈsɪstənt / (AmE), / ˌɪnkənˈsɪstənt / (BrE)
not staying the same throughout; lacking in regularity, logic, or agreement.
lacking consistency or agreement

Examples

Her explanations for the mistake were inconsistent and raised many questions.
The data collected from the experiment were inconsistent with previous research findings.
His inconsistent attendance at meetings made it difficult to rely on him.

Synset Examples

inconsistent statements cannot both be true at the same time
inconsistent with the roadmap

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consistent - always behaving or happening in a similar way; in agreement
  • Synonym: contradictory - mutually opposed or inconsistent

Etymology

From Latin in- (not) + consistentem (standing firm).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00580271-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ˌɪn.kənˈsɪstənt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.kənˈsɪstənt / (BrE)
not in agreement; lacking harmony or compatibility between parts or elements.
not in agreement; lacking harmony

Examples

Her statements about the project were inconsistent, causing confusion among the team.
The witness gave inconsistent answers throughout the trial.
The results of the experiment were inconsistent with previous findings.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consistent - in agreement; acting or done in the same way over time

Etymology

From Latin 'in-' (not) + 'consistent', from 'consistere' (to stand firm).
