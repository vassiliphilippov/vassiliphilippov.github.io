to etymologise

1. VERB

/ ˌɛtɪˈmɑlədʒaɪz / (AmE), / ˌɛtɪˈmɒlədʒaɪz / (BrE)
to explain the origin and historical development of a word, or to suggest its derivation
explain the origin of a word
british

Examples

The linguist spent hours trying to etymologise rare dialect words.
Many amateurs like to etymologise even if their theories are not always accurate.
She enjoys etymologising words during conversations about language history.

Synset Examples

The linguist probably etymologized the words incorrectly
Although he is not trained in this, his hobby is etymologizing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to etymologize - to explain the etymology of a word; American spelling

Etymology

From Greek etymologein (to give the etymology of a word), via French and Latin.

2. VERB

/ ˌɛtɪˈmɑːlədʒaɪz / (AmE), / ˌɛtɪˈmɒlədʒaɪz / (BrE)
to trace or study the origin and historical development of words
trace word origins
british

Examples

Linguists often etymologise new words to understand their origins and cultural contexts.
Some scholars etymologise terms incorrectly by relying on folk etymology rather than historical evidence.
She enjoys reading about how experts etymologise words by analyzing ancient languages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to etymologize - American spelling of 'etymologise'; to trace word origins

Etymology

From Greek etumologein (to give the true sense of a word), via French étymologiser and Medieval Latin etymologizare. Spelling is chiefly British, with American equivalent 'etymologize'.
