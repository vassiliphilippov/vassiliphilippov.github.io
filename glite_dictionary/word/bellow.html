bellow

1. VERB

/ ˈbɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɛləʊ / (BrE)
(of a person or animal) to make a loud, deep roar, typically in pain or anger.
to make a loud, deep roar

Examples

The bull bellowed loudly in the field, startling everyone nearby.
She bellowed at her children to come inside before it got dark.
The wounded lion bellowed in pain, echoing through the savannah.

Synset Examples

The bull bellowed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roar - to make a deep, loud, prolonged sound
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak very softly

Etymology

From Middle English 'belwen', of imitative origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɛləʊ / (BrE)
a very loud and deep shout or roar, similar to the sound made by a large animal
a loud, deep roar or shout

Examples

His bellow echoed off the walls, startling everyone in the room.
With a furious bellow, the coach called the players back to the field.
The lion's bellow could be heard throughout the entire savanna.

Synset Examples

his bellow filled the hallway

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roar - a loud, deep, prolonged sound
  • Antonym: whisper - a very quiet or soft spoken utterance

Etymology

From Middle English 'belen', probably imitative of the sound.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07136069-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈbɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɛləʊ / (BrE)
to shout loudly and without restraint, often in anger or pain
to shout loudly and forcefully

Examples

He bellowed at his team to hurry up during the last few minutes of the game.
She bellowed with laughter, startling everyone nearby.
The teacher bellowed instructions to the noisy students across the playground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yell - to shout loudly, especially in anger, fear, or excitement
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak very softly using the breath, without vocal cords

Etymology

From Middle English 'belwen', from Old English 'bylgan' (to roar), related to the sound made by animals.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01050441-v
Synset POS: v
