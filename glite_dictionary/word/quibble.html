quibble

1. NOUN

/ ˈkwɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈkwɪbəl / (BrE)
a minor objection or irrelevant argument, especially one used to avoid the main point
minor or irrelevant objection

Examples

The lawyer raised a quibble about a minor detail, hoping to delay the proceedings.
During the meeting, his only contribution was a single quibble about wording.
She was tired of every quibble he made instead of addressing the real issue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: objection - an expression or feeling of disapproval or opposition

Etymology

From Latin *quivis* (whoever) via obsolete sense of 'quibble' as a pun or play on words.

2. VERB

/ ˈkwɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈkwɪbəl / (BrE)
to argue or raise objections about a trivial matter
argue over trivial matters

Examples

There's no need to quibble about the exact details; let's focus on the main point.
They quibbled for hours over the smallest expenses in the budget.
Stop quibbling and just agree on a restaurant for dinner.

Synset Examples

Let's not quibble over pennies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bicker - to argue about petty and trivial matters

Etymology

From Latin *quibus*, a legal term used in ambiguities.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00776101-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkwɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈkwɪbəl / (BrE)
to evade the main point of an argument by raising trivial or irrelevant objections
evade by making trivial objections

Examples

Rather than address the main issue, he chose to quibble about minor details.
The lawyer tried to quibble over the wording instead of answering the question directly.
Let's not quibble about insignificant points when the real problem needs attention.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cavil - raise petty or unnecessary objections
  • Synonym: to evade - avoid giving a direct answer

Etymology

From Latin *quibus* (the dative or ablative plural of 'qui', meaning 'who'), used in legal documents, later transferred to minor objections in legal arguments.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00812047-v
Synset POS: v
