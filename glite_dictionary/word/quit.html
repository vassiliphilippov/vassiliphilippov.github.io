to quit

1. VERB

/ kwɪt / (AmE), / kwɪt / (BrE)
to stop doing something or put an end to a state or activity
stop doing something; cease

Examples

You should quit worrying about things you can’t control.
He finally decided to quit smoking after years of trying.
Please quit making so much noise while I’m on the phone.

Synset Examples

Quit teasing your little brother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stop - to cease an activity or action
  • Antonym: resume - to begin again after stopping

Etymology

From Latin 'quietare' (make quiet), via Old French 'quiter' (to release, free).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02686624-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kwɪt / (AmE), / kwɪt / (BrE)
to leave, resign, or give up a position or occupation
to resign or leave a position

Examples

After ten years as CEO, she decided to quit her job and travel the world.
He quit his position as manager after a disagreement with the board.
Many employees are choosing to quit for better opportunities elsewhere.

Synset Examples

The Secretary of the Navy will leave office next month
The chairman resigned over the financial scandal

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to resign - to give up a job or position formally
  • Synonym: to retire - to leave a job permanently, usually due to age

Etymology

From Old French *quiter* (to release), from Latin *quietare* (to calm, to quit).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02387509-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kwɪt / (AmE), / kwɪt / (BrE)
to stop trying or accept defeat, especially when faced with difficulty or hopelessness
to stop trying or give up

Examples

After losing the first set badly, he refused to quit and fought back.
Many people quit when faced with failure, but she kept going.
Though the task was tough, Sam would not quit until he succeeded.

Synset Examples

In the second round, the challenger gave up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to give up - to stop trying; admit defeat
  • Antonym: to persevere - to continue attempting despite difficulties

Etymology

From Old French *quiter* (to release, acquit), from Latin *quietare* (to make quiet).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01085437-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ kwɪt / (AmE), / kwɪt / (BrE)
to leave a place or to go away, usually permanently
leave or go away

Examples

After the argument, he decided to quit the house and never return.
Many settlers had to quit their homeland in search of a better life.
She quit the noisy party early because she was feeling unwell.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to depart - to go away from a place
  • Antonym: to remain - to stay or not leave a place

Etymology

From Old French *quiter* (to release, free), from Latin *quietare* (to calm, make quiet), from *quietus* (quiet).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02012411-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ kwɪt / (AmE), / kwɪt / (BrE)
To stop doing or give up something, especially a habit, activity, or pursuit.
to stop or give up something

Examples

After years of smoking, he finally decided to quit for the sake of his health.
Many people find it hard to quit unhealthy habits once they've begun.
She wanted to quit her job and pursue her passion for painting.

Synset Examples

I am foreswearing women forever

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to give up - to stop doing or abandon something
  • Synonym: to renounce - to formally declare abandonment of a claim, right, or possession
  • Antonym: to continue - to persist in an activity or process

Etymology

From Old French *quiter* (to release, discharge), from Latin *quietare* (to make quiet).
