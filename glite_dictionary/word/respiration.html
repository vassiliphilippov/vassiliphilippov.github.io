respiration

1. NOUN

/ ˌrɛspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
A single complete act of breathing in and out.
a single breath in and out
medical

Examples

The nurse counted the patient's respiration rate carefully.
During exercise, your respiration becomes quicker and deeper.
There was a noticeable pause after each respiration.

Synset Examples

thirty respirations per minute

Related Terms

  • Synonym: breath - the air taken into or expelled from the lungs in a single act

Etymology

From Latin respiratio (breathing), from respirare (to breathe again).

2. NOUN

/ ˌrɛspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
The biological process by which organisms obtain energy from organic molecules, producing carbon dioxide and transferring energy within cells.
process of energy release in living cells
medical

Examples

During aerobic respiration, cells use oxygen to break down glucose and release energy.
Scientists measured the rate of respiration in the plant leaves under different light conditions.
Proper respiration is essential for the survival of all complex organisms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: breathing - the process of taking air into and expelling it from the lungs

Etymology

From Latin respiratio (breathing), from respirare (to breathe out).

3. NOUN

/ ˌrɛspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process in living organisms of taking in oxygen from the air and releasing carbon dioxide, involving inhalation and exhalation
the process of breathing (inhalation and exhalation)

Examples

During exercise, respiration increases to supply more oxygen to the muscles.
The doctor listened to the child’s respiration for signs of difficulty.
Plants carry out both photosynthesis and respiration to sustain life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: breathing - the process of taking air into and expelling it from the lungs

Etymology

From Latin 'respiratio' (breathing out), from 'respirare' (to breathe out).
