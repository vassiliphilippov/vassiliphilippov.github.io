scour

1. VERB

/ ˈskaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈskaʊə / (BrE)
to clean something by rubbing it hard, often with an abrasive material, to remove dirt or stains
clean by hard rubbing

Examples

He used steel wool to scour the burnt pan until it was spotless.
After the barbecue, we had to scour the grill grates to get rid of the grease.
The cleaning crew scoured every corner of the kitchen before the inspection.

Synset Examples

She scrubbed his back

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scrub - to clean by rubbing hard, often with a brush
  • Degree Lower: polish - to make something smooth and shiny by rubbing

Etymology

From Old French 'escurer', from Latin 'excurare' meaning 'to clean out'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01253792-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈskaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈskaʊə / (BrE)
an area that has been eroded or hollowed out, especially by the action of running water
area eroded by running water

Examples

After the heavy rain, a deep scour formed at the base of the hillside.
Fish often gather in the scours below river bends, where the water runs fastest.
Engineers studied the scour around the bridge piers to assess stability.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erosion - the process by which something is gradually worn away
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08665032-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈskaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈskaʊə / (BrE)
to clean, rinse, or remove something from a surface or container by using a liquid, often with force
clean or empty with a liquid

Examples

The surgeon will scour the wound with a sterile solution before stitching it up.
You must scour the fuel tank to remove any traces of dirt or debris.
The chef scoured the pan with hot water to get rid of burnt food.

Synset Examples

flush the wound with antibiotics
purge the old gas tank

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flush - to clean with a flow of water or other liquid
  • Synonym: purge - to rid something of unwanted contents

Etymology

From Old French *escurer* (to clean, polish), from Latin *excurare* (to clean out).
