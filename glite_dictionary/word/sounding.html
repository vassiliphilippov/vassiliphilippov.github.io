sounding

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊndɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊndɪŋ / (BrE)
a measurement of the depth of water, typically made with a line or other instrument
measurement of water depth

Examples

The crew took a sounding every hour to ensure the ship stayed in safe waters.
Early explorers relied on soundings to navigate uncharted coastlines.
Before anchoring, the fisherman checked the sounding to avoid running aground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: depth - distance from the surface downward, especially of water

Etymology

From Middle English 'sounden' (to measure the depth of water), from Old French 'sonder'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊndɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊndɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of measuring the depth of water, typically with a line or sounding device
measuring water depth

Examples

The crew took a sounding every hour to avoid running aground.
Early explorers relied on rope and weights for each sounding they made along the coast.
The captain ordered a sounding to determine the depth before anchoring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: depth sounding - measuring the depth of water

Etymology

From the verb 'sound' (to measure depth) + -ing; 'sound' in this sense comes from Old French 'sondier', from sonde 'sounding line'.
