restive

1. ADJ

/ ˈrɛstɪv / (AmE), / ˈrɛstɪv / (BrE)
unable to remain still, silent, or submissive, especially because of dissatisfaction or impatience; tense or uneasy.
tense or unable to keep still

Examples

The audience grew increasingly restive as the speaker continued to delay the announcement.
After hours of waiting in the crowded room, the children became noticeably restive.
The stock market has been restive amid rumors of an impending recession.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restless - unable to rest or relax; constantly moving
  • Antonym: calm - free from agitation or excitement

Etymology

From French *restif*, from Latin *restare* (to resist, to remain).

2. ADJ

/ ˈrɛstɪv / (AmE), / ˈrɛstɪv / (BrE)
unable to remain still or silent, especially because of impatience, dissatisfaction, or boredom under restriction or delay
impatient or difficult to control under restriction

Examples

The soldiers became increasingly restive as they waited for orders that never came.
He grew restive during the long meeting and kept glancing at his watch.
After weeks of strict lockdown, the population grew restive and demanded some relaxation of the rules.

Synset Examples

the government has done nothing to ease restrictions and manufacturers are growing restive

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impatient - easily annoyed by delay or unwilling to wait
  • Antonym: composed - calm and in control of oneself

Etymology

From French *restif* (resting, resisting), from Latin *restare* (to hold back).

WordNet ID: oewn-01741752-s
Synset POS: s
