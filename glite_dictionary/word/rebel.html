rebel

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈbɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈbɛl / (BrE)
a person who rises in opposition or armed resistance against an established government or authority, often in the hope of bringing about change
person fighting against authority or government

Examples

The rebel forces captured several key government buildings during the night.
He was labeled a rebel after joining the uprising against the dictatorship.
After many years, the rebel leader agreed to peace talks with the government.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insurgent - a person who rises in forcible opposition to lawful authority
  • Antonym: loyalist - a person who remains loyal to the established ruler or government

Etymology

From Latin rebellis (waging war again), from re- (again) + bellum (war).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈbɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈbɛl / (BrE)
to oppose or fight against authority, control, or tradition, especially in a deliberate and active way
rise in opposition; resist authority

Examples

The citizens decided to rebel against the unfair new law.
Teenagers often rebel when they feel misunderstood by adults.
If conditions do not improve, the workers may rebel against management.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: revolt - to take violent action against authority or government
  • Antonym: to submit - to accept or yield to a superior force

Etymology

From Latin 'rebellare' (to wage war again, revolt).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈbɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈbɛl / (BrE)
a person who shows great independence in thought or action and resists authority, control, or tradition
a person who resists authority or convention

Examples

Even as a child, Sarah was known as a rebel who questioned every rule.
The great innovators throughout history are often described as rebels in their fields.
He admired her for being a rebel, never afraid to follow her own path.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nonconformist - a person who does not conform to established customs, attitudes, or norms
  • Antonym: conformist - a person who adheres to conventional standards or customs

Etymology

From Latin rebellis (warring again), from re- (again) + bellum (war).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10323321-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ rɪˈbɛl / (AmE), / rɪˈbɛl / (BrE)
to refuse to obey rules, authority, or established customs; to resist or act against control or tradition
refuse to obey rules or authority

Examples

Teenagers often rebel against their parents by questioning family traditions.
She decided to rebel after years of following strict workplace routines.
Artists sometimes rebel against conventional styles to create something entirely new.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to conform - to comply with rules, standards, or laws

Etymology

From Latin rebellāre (to renew war), from re- (again) + bellāre (to wage war).
