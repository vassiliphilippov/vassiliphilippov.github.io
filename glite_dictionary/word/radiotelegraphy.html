radiotelegraphy

1. NOUN

/ ˌreɪdioʊtəˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / ˌreɪdiəʊtɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
The sending of telegrams or messages by radio waves rather than by wire.
sending messages by radio instead of wire

Examples

The development of radiotelegraphy revolutionized long-distance communication for ships at sea.
Before the invention of voice radio, messages were sent using radiotelegraphy in Morse code.
Early explorers relied on radiotelegraphy to maintain contact with their home countries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegraphy - the transmission of messages by telegraph equipment

Etymology

From radio- (relating to electromagnetic waves) + telegraphy (from Greek tele- ‘far’ + -graphy ‘writing’).

2. NOUN

/ ˌreɪdioʊtəˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / ˌreɪdiəʊtɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
the transmission of telegraphic messages by radio waves, typically using Morse code
sending telegrams by radio waves

Examples

In the early twentieth century, radiotelegraphy was the main method for ships to communicate long distances.
The invention of radiotelegraphy made it possible to send messages across the ocean without physical wires.
With the decline of Morse code, radiotelegraphy is now mostly of historical interest.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegraphy - the transmission of messages over distance using coded signals

Etymology

Formed from 'radio' + 'telegraphy'; 'telegraphy' from Greek 'tele-' (far) and 'graphein' (to write).
