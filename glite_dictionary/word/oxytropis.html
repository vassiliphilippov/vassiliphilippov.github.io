Oxytropis

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑk.sɪˈtroʊ.pɪs / (AmE), / ˌɒk.sɪˈtrəʊ.pɪs / (BrE)
A large, widely distributed genus of evergreen shrubs or subshrubs in the pea family, characterized by odd-pinnate leaves and racemose or spicate clusters of pea-like flowers.
genus of evergreen shrubs or subshrubs in the pea family

Examples

Several species of Oxytropis are known for their beautiful purple flowers and are found in alpine meadows.
The toxic properties of some Oxytropis plants pose a risk to grazing livestock.
Researchers are studying Oxytropis to understand adaptations to harsh, cold environments.

Etymology

From Neo-Latin, from Greek oxys (sharp) and tropis (keel), referring to the pointed keel of the flower.

2. NOUN

/ ɑkˈsaɪtrəpɪs / (AmE), / ɒkˈsɪtrəpɪs / (BrE)
A large genus of evergreen shrubs or subshrubs of the pea family, widely distributed, with odd-pinnate leaves and racemose or spicate clusters of clawed, pea-like flowers.
genus of flowering plants in the pea family

Examples

Several species of Oxytropis are commonly known as locoweeds due to their toxicity to livestock.
The dry prairies are home to vibrant patches of Oxytropis in bloom during spring.
Botanists identified the specimen as belonging to the genus Oxytropis based on its distinctive clawed petals.

Etymology

From Greek 'oxy-' meaning 'sharp' and 'tropis' meaning 'keel', referring to the sharp keel of the flower.
