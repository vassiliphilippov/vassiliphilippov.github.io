sensation

1. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a general feeling of excitement and heightened interest, often caused by anticipation or something remarkable
a feeling of excitement or heightened interest

Examples

The news of the discovery created a sensation in the scientific community.
She felt a strange sensation of excitement every time she walked on stage.
Winning the competition gave him a sensation unlike anything he had experienced before.

Synset Examples

anticipation produced in me a sensation somewhere between hope and fear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrill - a sudden feeling of excitement and pleasure
  • Antonym: boredom - a state of being weary and uninterested due to lack of excitement

Etymology

From Latin sensatio (perception, feeling), from sentire (to feel).

2. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a physical feeling or perception resulting from something that happens to or comes into contact with the body
basic physical feeling or perception

Examples

The cold breeze gave me a sudden sensation on the back of my neck.
After touching the icy water, he felt a prickling sensation in his fingers.
She lost all sensation in her foot after sitting awkwardly for too long.

Synset Examples

a sensation of touch

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feeling - an emotional or physical sensation
  • Synonym: perception - the process of becoming aware through the senses

Etymology

From Latin sensationem (perception, feeling), from sentire (to feel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05720023-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a person who is dazzlingly skilled or has made a great impact in a particular field, often attracting public attention
remarkably skilled or impactful person

Examples

The young pianist became a sensation overnight after her stunning performance.
He is a basketball sensation known for his extraordinary skills on the court.
She was hailed as a fashion sensation at her first runway show.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prodigy - a young person with exceptional abilities

Etymology

From Latin sensatio (feeling, perception), sense extended in 19th c. to exceptional people who cause a strong impression.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)

widespread public excitement

Examples

Her performance created a sensation in the art world.

5. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a faculty by which the body perceives an external stimulus, such as sight, hearing, smell, taste, or touch
faculty for perceiving external stimuli

Examples

When vision was lost, his sense of sensation in his fingertips became much more important.
Doctors tested her sensation to determine if her nerves were functioning properly.
Loss of sensation in the legs can be a symptom of nerve damage.

Synset Examples

in the dark he had to depend on touch and on his senses of smell and hearing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sense - a faculty by which the body perceives an external stimulus

Etymology

From Latin sensatio, from sentire (to feel).
