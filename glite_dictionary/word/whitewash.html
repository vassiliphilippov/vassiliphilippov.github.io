whitewash

1. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪtˌwɑʃ / (AmE), / ˈwaɪtˌwɒʃ / (BrE)
a liquid mixture of lime and water used to whiten walls and other surfaces
lime-based wall paint

Examples

The old cottage was given a fresh coat of whitewash to brighten it up.
He noticed that the fence was covered with flaking whitewash.
They applied whitewash to the cellar walls for a clean, traditional look.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: paint - colored substance spread over a surface for protection or decoration

2. VERB

/ ˈwaɪtˌwɑʃ / (AmE), / ˈwaɪtwɒʃ / (BrE)
to cover a surface, especially a wall or building, with a white liquid mixture used for whitening and disinfecting
cover with a whitening mixture (walls)

Examples

They decided to whitewash the old fence to make it look fresh and clean.
The caretaker whitewashed the walls before the grand reopening of the hall.
In preparation for the festival, locals spent days whitewashing every building in the village.

Synset Examples

whitewash walls

Related Terms

  • Synonym: paint - to cover a surface with paint

Etymology

Whitewash: from 'white' + 'wash', referring to the practice of washing surfaces with a white mixture.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪtˌwɑʃ / (AmE), / ˈwaɪtwɒʃ / (BrE)
a defeat in which the losing person or team fails to score any points, especially in games or sports.
defeat with no points scored by loser

Examples

The team suffered a whitewash in the finals, failing to score a single goal.
It was their third consecutive whitewash this season, much to the disappointment of their fans.
The visitors were handed a humiliating whitewash by the home team.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shutout - a game in which one side prevents the other from scoring

Etymology

Figurative extension from 'whitewash' (to cover up flaws), adapted in sports to mean a complete shutout.

4. VERB

/ ˈwaɪtwɑʃ / (AmE), / ˈwaɪtwɒʃ / (BrE)
to attempt to hide or minimize faults, misdemeanors, or unpleasant facts, often in order to make something appear better or more acceptable than it really is
cover up faults or wrongdoing

Examples

The politician tried to whitewash the scandal by releasing a vague public statement.
We should not whitewash the company's mistakes, but address them openly.
The report was criticized for attempting to whitewash the real issues.

an attempt to conceal faults, errors, or unpleasant facts, often in a misleading or deceptive way
an attempt to cover up faults or failures

Examples

Related Terms

From white (the color) + wash, originally meaning a white liquid used for painting walls and later extended metaphorically.

Examples

  • Antonym: to expose - to reveal the truth about something or someone

Etymology

Originally from the noun 'whitewash', meaning a white liquid used as a cheap paint, figuratively extended to mean concealing faults.
