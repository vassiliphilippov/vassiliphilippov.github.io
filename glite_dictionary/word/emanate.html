to emanate

1. VERB

/ ˈɛm.əˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛməneɪt / (BrE)
(of something abstract but perceptible) to come out from a source; to issue or spread out from a particular place.
to come out from a source

Examples

A strange scent seemed to emanate from the old chest in the attic.
Laughter and warmth emanate from their home during family gatherings.
Brilliant ideas often emanate from creative discussions and brainstorming sessions.

Synset Examples

Water emanates from this hole in the ground

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to originate - to have a specified beginning, to start from a source
  • Synonym: radiate - to send out rays or waves, especially from a central point

Etymology

From Latin *emanare* (to flow out), from *e-* (out) + *manare* (to flow).

2. VERB

/ ˈɛm.əˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛm.ə.neɪt / (BrE)
to produce or send out something such as a smell, sound, or feeling from a source
to send out, emit (smell, light, etc.)

Examples

A faint aroma seemed to emanate from the freshly baked bread.
Warmth emanates from the fireplace during winter evenings.
An eerie light emanated from behind the old door, making us curious.

Synset Examples

The chimney exhales a thick smoke

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to emit - to send out gas, heat, light, sound, etc.
  • Synonym: to exude - to give off a liquid or smell slowly and steadily

Etymology

From Latin 'emanare', meaning 'to flow out'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00004605-v
Synset POS: v
