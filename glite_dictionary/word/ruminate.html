to ruminate

1. VERB

/ ˈruːməˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈruːmɪneɪt / (BrE)
(of ruminant animals) to chew the cud, i.e., to regurgitate partly digested food and chew it again.
chew the cud (of animals)

Examples

Cows often ruminate after grazing in the fields for several hours.
Sheep ruminate as part of their normal digestive process.
When disturbed, the cattle briefly stopped grazing and then returned to ruminate.

Synset Examples

cows ruminate

Etymology

From Latin ruminatus, past participle of ruminare (to chew the cud).

2. VERB

/ ˈruː.mɪ.neɪt / (AmE), / ˈruː.mɪ.neɪt / (BrE)
to think deeply and carefully about something, often for a prolonged period
think deeply about something

Examples

She would often ruminate on her choices before making important decisions.
After the meeting, he continued to ruminate about the potential consequences.
Instead of acting impulsively, Mia preferred to ruminate over every detail.

Synset Examples

I mulled over the events of the afternoon
philosophers have speculated on the question of God for thousands of years
The scientist must stop to observe and start to excogitate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to contemplate - to think about something carefully for a long time
  • Synonym: to ponder - to consider something deeply, especially before making a decision
  • Antonym: to ignore - to intentionally pay no attention to something

Etymology

From Latin ruminare (to chew the cud, turn over in the mind), from rumen (throat, gullet).
