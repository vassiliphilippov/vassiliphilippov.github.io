scallywag

1. NOUN

/ ˈskæliˌwæɡ / (AmE), / ˈskælɪwæɡ / (BrE)
A mischievous or dishonest person, especially a child; a scoundrel or rogue.
mischievous or dishonest person

Examples

My little brother can be a real scallywag when he wants to hide my things.
The group of scallywags caused chaos in the town square before running off laughing.
Don't trust that scallywag with your secrets—he's known for spreading rumors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scalawag - a dishonest or mischievous person
  • Synonym: rascal - a mischievous or cheeky person
  • Synonym: villain - a wicked or criminal person

Etymology

Probably an alteration of 'scalawag,' of unknown origin (19th century American English).

2. NOUN

/ ˈskæliˌwæɡ / (AmE), / ˈskælɪwæɡ / (BrE)
(US historical) a white Southerner who collaborated with Northern Republicans during Reconstruction, often perceived as acting out of self-interest.
white Southerner supporting Reconstruction (US history)

Examples

During Reconstruction, many Northerners distrusted the motives of every local scallywag.
The politician was accused of being a scallywag for supporting radical reforms.
History books discuss the role of scallywags in post-Civil War Southern society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carpetbagger - a Northerner who moved to the South after the Civil War for personal gain

Etymology

Variant of 'scalawag', origin uncertain, early 19th century American English, originally meaning 'worthless person or animal'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈskæliˌwæɡ / (AmE), / ˈskælɪwæɡ / (BrE)
a person, especially a child, who behaves in a mischievous, playful, or cheeky way
playfully mischievous person

Examples

That little scallywag hid my keys again!
The teacher smiled at the scallywag who had swapped the chalk for soap.
Don't let that scallywag fool you with his innocent face.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rascal - a mischievous or cheeky person, often a child
  • Synonym: rogue - a dishonest or unprincipled but likable person

Etymology

Possibly an alteration of 'scalawag,' of uncertain origin, first recorded in American English in the 19th century.
