unobjectionable

1. ADJ

/ ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃənəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃənəbl̩ / (BrE)
not likely to cause objection; acceptable or harmless.
not likely to cause objection; acceptable

Examples

Her comments during the meeting were entirely unobjectionable and did not upset anyone.
The film was so unobjectionable that it was suitable for all ages.
Most people found the proposal unobjectionable and quickly agreed to move forward.

Synset Examples

the ends are unobjectionable; it's the means that one can't accept

Related Terms

  • Antonym: objectionable - likely to cause dislike or disapproval; offensive

Etymology

From un- (not) + objectionable.

2. ADJ

/ ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃ(ə)nəbl / (AmE), / ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃənəbl / (BrE)
not likely to cause disapproval; acceptable or unobtrusive.
not likely to cause disapproval

Examples

Her proposal was considered completely unobjectionable by everyone at the meeting.
Only unobjectionable materials are permitted for display in the public library.
The film received positive reviews for its unobjectionable content and family-friendly themes.

Synset Examples

confined himself to innocuous generalities
unobjectionable behavior

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innocuous - not harmful or offensive
  • Antonym: objectionable - causing disapproval or offense

Etymology

3. ADJ

/ ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃənəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˌʌnəbˈdʒɛkʃ(ə)nəbl̩ / (BrE)
not likely to offend or cause disapproval; acceptable to most people, especially regarding behavior or language.
not likely to offend; acceptable

Examples

The film was considered unobjectionable and suitable for viewers of all ages.
Her comments at the meeting were completely unobjectionable and did not upset anyone.
He told an unobjectionable joke that made everyone laugh without causing offense.

Synset Examples

good clean fun
a clean joke

Related Terms

  • Antonym: objectionable - likely to cause offense or disapproval

Etymology

From un- (not) + objectionable, based on Latin objectio (an objection).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00425527-a
Synset POS: a
