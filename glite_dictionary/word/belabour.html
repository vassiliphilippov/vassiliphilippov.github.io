to belabour

1. VERB

/ bɪˈleɪbɚ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to argue or elaborate on (a subject) in excessive detail; to repeat or dwell on at unnecessary length
to dwell on at excessive length

Examples

The professor tended to belabour minor points during his lectures, making them longer than necessary.
Let's not belabour the issue; everyone already understands the main argument.
She often belabours her explanations, repeating the same details over and over.

Synset Examples

belabor the obvious

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dwell on - to think, speak, or write at length about a particular subject
  • Synonym: to overemphasize - to give too much emphasis or stress to something
  • Antonym: skim - to deal with or read superficially or quickly

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + labour, from Old French labourer (to work).

2. VERB

/ bɪˈleɪbər / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to criticize someone or something harshly and repeatedly
criticize harshly and repeatedly
british

Examples

The journalist continued to belabour the government for its handling of the crisis.
There is no need to belabour the obvious mistakes in his argument.
She felt unfairly belaboured by the critics after her debut performance.

Synset Examples

She was belabored by her fellow students

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to criticize - to express disapproval of someone or something
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + labour; Middle English.

3. VERB

/ bɪˈleɪbər / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
To beat someone or something forcefully and repeatedly.
to beat soundly or repeatedly
archaic british

Examples

The villain began to belabour his helpless victim with a stick.
Angry at the thief, the crowd proceeded to belabour him before the police arrived.
In the old tales, warriors would belabour their enemies in fierce combat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beat - to strike repeatedly
  • Synonym: assault - to attack violently
  • Antonym: caress - to touch or stroke gently

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + labour, meaning 'to exert oneself strenuously'.
