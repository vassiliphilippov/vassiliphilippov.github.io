brail

1. NOUN

/ breɪl / (AmE), / breɪl / (BrE)
a small net used to draw fish into a boat
small net for drawing fish into a boat

Examples

The fisherman used a brail to lift the catch from the water.
He tossed the brail overboard to gather the fish near the surface.
After casting the net, they pulled it in with a brail to haul the fish onto the boat.

2. VERB

/ breɪl / (AmE), / breɪl / (BrE)
to haul fish aboard (a boat) using brails (nets or ropes)
haul fish aboard with nets

Examples

The fishermen skillfully brailed the catch onto the deck using large nets.
We watched as the crew brailed hundreds of sardines at sunrise.
They needed extra help to brail the heavy load of tuna aboard the ship.

Etymology

From Middle English 'braile', from Old French 'braiel' (strap, belt), possibly from Latin 'braca' (trousers, pants).

3. VERB

/ breɪl / (AmE), / breɪl / (BrE)
(of a sail) to gather in or take up with ropes for furling or securing, especially using a brail.
gather a sail in with ropes

Examples

The sailors began to brail the mainsail as the storm approached.
You must brail the sail quickly to avoid damage in high winds.
He brailed the canvas tightly before leaving the deck.

Etymology

From Middle English 'brayle', from Old French 'braille' (strap, thong), likely from Latin 'braculum' (thong, strap).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ breɪl / (AmE), / breɪl / (BrE)
a small rope used to draw in or gather up a sail on a ship
small rope for gathering a sail

Examples

The sailor pulled on the brail to gather up the sail before the storm.
Several brails were frayed and needed to be replaced before the next voyage.
With a practiced hand, she loosened the brail to let the sail fall.

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'braeile' or Middle Low German 'brāle', meaning 'hoisting rope'.
