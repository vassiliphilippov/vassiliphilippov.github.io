to induce

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈdus / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to persuade or influence someone to do something; to cause someone to act in a particular way
persuade or influence to do something

Examples

The promise of a bonus induced several employees to work overtime.
She was induced to accept the offer despite her initial hesitation.
Nothing could induce him to change his mind about the decision.

Synset Examples

The ads induced me to buy a VCR
My children finally got me to buy a computer
My wife made me buy a new sofa

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to persuade - to cause someone to do something through reasoning or argument
  • Antonym: to dissuade - to persuade someone not to take a particular action

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in), from in- (in) + ducere (to lead).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00772482-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈdus / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to produce an electric current in a conductor by the influence of a changing magnetic or electric field.
produce electric current by magnetic or electrostatic means

Examples

A moving magnet can induce a current in a coil of wire.
Alternating current is often induced by rotating a conductor in a magnetic field.
Physicists demonstrated how changing the magnetic field would induce electricity in the circuit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to generate - to produce electricity or energy
  • Antonym: to suppress - to prevent or stop (as in suppressing a current)

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01741298-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈdus / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to derive or establish (a general principle) from specific observations or facts, especially by the method of induction
reason or establish by induction

Examples

From the pattern in the data, we can induce a general law of behavior.
Scientists often induce theories by observing repeated phenomena and identifying common features.
If we induce that all metals conduct electricity from our experiments, we're applying inductive reasoning.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deduce - to reach a conclusion by reasoning from general principles to specific cases

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in), from in- (into) + ducere (to lead).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00638103-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈdus / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to cause (something, especially a process or feeling) to happen rapidly or bring about suddenly
cause to occur rapidly

Examples

The doctor used medication to induce labor when the baby was overdue.
Exposure to the allergen can induce a severe reaction within minutes.
High stress levels sometimes induce headaches very quickly.

Synset Examples

the infection precipitated a high fever and allergic reactions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precipitate - to cause to happen suddenly or sooner than expected
  • Synonym: to provoke - to cause a reaction or feeling to occur suddenly
  • Antonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in), from in- (into) + ducere (to lead).

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ ɪnˈdus / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to cause (something) to happen or arise, especially as a result of a particular action or situation.
cause (something) to happen

Examples

The government's new policy may induce inflation in the coming months.
His harsh words could induce anxiety in some listeners.
Exposure to cold temperatures can sometimes induce illness.

Synset Examples

induce a crisis

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cause - to make something happen, especially something negative

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in, bring in), from in- (in) + ducere (to lead).
