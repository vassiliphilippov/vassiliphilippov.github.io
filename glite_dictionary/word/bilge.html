bilge

1. NOUN

/ bɪldʒ / (AmE), / bɪldʒ / (BrE)
the enclosed area on each side of the bottom of a ship's hull where water often collects
enclosed lowest area inside a ship's hull

Examples

Water often accumulates in the bilges and must be pumped out regularly for safety.
After the storm, the crew inspected the bilges for any signs of leakage or damage.
Routine maintenance includes cleaning out oil and debris from the bilges.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: keel - the central structural base of a ship running along the bottom from front to back

Etymology

From Middle French 'bilge' meaning 'a bulge or swelling,' possibly of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ bɪldʒ / (AmE), / bɪldʒ / (BrE)
the curved area on the bottom of a ship or boat where the sides meet the base
curved bottom part of a vessel

Examples

Water collected in the bilge after the storm, and the crew had to pump it out.
Inspecting the bilge is a routine part of ship maintenance.
The damage to the bilge caused the vessel to list to one side.

Etymology

From Middle French *bilge*, from Old French *bille* (baulk of timber).

3. NOUN

/ bɪldʒ / (AmE), / bɪldʒ / (BrE)
water that collects in the lowest part of a ship's hull, often from leaks or internal spillage
water collected in a ship's lowest part

Examples

The crew pumped the bilge to prevent water from accumulating in the lower decks.
A strong odor was coming from the bilge, alerting the captain to a possible leak.
Routine maintenance includes checking for excess water in the bilge of the vessel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ballast - heavy material placed in a ship's hold for stability

Etymology

From Middle English 'bulge', from Old French 'bouge' (leather bag), later nautical usage for the bulging part of a ship’s bottom.

4. VERB

/ bɪldʒ / (AmE), / bɪldʒ / (BrE)
(of a vessel) to spring a leak or be made to leak, especially by having the bottom or sides damaged.
to cause a vessel to leak

Examples

The collision bilged the old ship, leaving it taking on water quickly.
Flood debris struck the hull and bilged the fishing boat.
Engineers warned that a sharp rock could bilge the vessel in these dangerous waters.

Synset Examples

the collision bilged the vessel

Etymology

From the noun 'bilge,' meaning the lowest compartment of a ship, where water may collect.

5. VERB

/ bɪldʒ / (AmE), / bɪldʒ / (BrE)
(of a ship or boat) to take in water at the lowest part of the hull (the bilge), usually due to damage or leakage.
(of a vessel) take in water at the bilge

Examples

After striking the rocks, the old fishing boat began to bilge rapidly and had to be abandoned.
Engineers patched the hull to prevent the tanker from continuing to bilge during the storm.
The lifeboat bilged after hours adrift, forcing the crew to bail out the water.

Synset Examples

the tanker bilged

Etymology

Related to the noun 'bilge', from Middle English 'bilge' (the bulging part of a cask or barrel).
