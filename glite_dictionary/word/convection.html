convection

1. NOUN

/ kənˈvɛkʃən / (AmE), / kənˈvɛkʃən / (BrE)
the transfer of heat or other properties through a fluid (such as air or water) by the movement of masses within the fluid, especially vertically in the atmosphere
transfer of heat by fluid movement

Examples

Warm air rises in the atmosphere due to convection, leading to cloud formation.
Most thunderstorms are powered by the convection of moist air from the ground upward.
The meteorologist explained how convection currents drive weather patterns.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: conduction - the transfer of heat through direct contact
  • Antonym: radiation - the transfer of energy by electromagnetic waves

Etymology

From Latin 'convectionem' (a bringing together), from 'convehere' (to carry together).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈvɛk.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˈvɛk.ʃən / (BrE)
the transfer of heat through a fluid (such as a liquid or gas) by the movement of its molecules
heat transfer in fluids by movement

Examples

Warm air rises in a room due to convection, spreading heat throughout the space.
The weather report explained how convection currents influence local wind patterns.
Cooking soup relies on convection to circulate heat and cook the ingredients evenly.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: conduction - the transfer of heat through direct contact between substances
  • Antonym: radiation - the transfer of energy as electromagnetic waves or particles

Etymology

From Latin 'convectio' (a bringing together, a carrying), from 'convehere' (to carry together).
