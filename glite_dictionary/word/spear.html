spear

1. VERB

/ spɪr / (AmE), / spɪə / (BrE)
to rise or thrust up sharply or straight, like a spear
to thrust up like a spear

Examples

The pine trees seemed to spear up through the misty morning air.
Shards of glass speared upwards after the explosion.
Wildflowers spear through the snow as spring begins.

Synset Examples

The branch speared up into the air

2. NOUN

/ spɪr / (AmE), / spɪə / (BrE)
A weapon with a long shaft and a pointed tip, used for thrusting or throwing.
long pointed weapon or tool

Examples

The hunter threw his spear at the charging animal.
Museum visitors admired the iron-tipped spear from the Bronze Age.
He caught fish using a homemade spear in the shallow river.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lance - a long weapon for thrusting, similar to a spear but typically used by cavalry
  • Degree Lower: javelin - a light spear designed primarily to be thrown

Etymology

Old English *spere*, related to Old Norse *spjōr* and German *Speer*.

/ spɪr / (AmE), / spɪə / (BrE)
to pierce or kill with or as if with a spear
to pierce or kill with a spear

Examples

Divers are not allowed to spear certain types of protected fish.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

4. NOUN

/ spɪr / (AmE), / spɪə / (BrE)
a tool with a long shaft and a barbed point, used for catching fish by thrusting or throwing
tool with pointed shaft for catching fish

Examples

The fisherman used a spear to catch salmon in the shallow stream.
Ancient people often fashioned a spear from wood and bone to hunt for fish.
With a quick thrust of his spear, he secured dinner for his family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harpoon - a barbed spear used for hunting large fish or marine mammals

Etymology

Old English *spere*, from Proto-Germanic *sperō* (spear, lance).
