to conform

1. VERB

/ kənˈfɔrm / (AmE), / kənˈfɔːm / (BrE)
to change your behavior or beliefs so that they are more similar to those of others or so that you fit in with new or different conditions
adapt to fit in with conditions or norms

Examples

New employees are expected to conform to the company's dress code.
He found it difficult to conform after moving to such a different culture.
Students often feel pressure to conform to the expectations of their peers.

Synset Examples

We must adjust to the bad economic situation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adapt - to change to suit new conditions
  • Antonym: to resist - to refuse to comply or adapt to changes

Etymology

From Latin 'conformare' (to shape, fashion, form), from 'con-' (together) + 'formare' (to form).

2. VERB

/ kənˈfɔrm / (AmE), / kənˈfɔːm / (BrE)
to behave or think in a way that is the same as or very similar to other people or to a set of standards or expectations
to behave or be similar to standards/rules

Examples

The new design must conform to international safety standards.
All recommendations should conform with the official guidelines provided by the committee.
Employees are expected to conform to the company dress code at all times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to comply - to act in accordance with a wish or command
  • Antonym: deviate - to depart from an established course or norm

Etymology

From Latin 'conformare' (to form, shape), from 'con-' (together) + 'formare' (to form).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
