burrow

1. VERB

/ ˈbɜːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbʌrəʊ / (BrE)
to move through something by digging or making a tunnel, typically like an animal in soil or undergrowth
move or dig through (like an animal)

Examples

The rabbit managed to burrow beneath the fence and escape into the garden.
During winter, some animals burrow through the snow to find shelter.
The children tried to burrow through the pile of autumn leaves.

Synset Examples

burrow through the forest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dig - to break up and move earth with a tool, hands, or animal's snout

Etymology

From Middle English 'borow', from Old English 'burh' (a shelter, dwelling).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɜroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbʌrəʊ / (BrE)
a hole or tunnel dug in the ground by an animal for shelter or habitation
animal's shelter dug in ground

Examples

The rabbit disappeared into its burrow when it heard us approaching.
Foxes often sleep in a burrow during the day to stay hidden from predators.
After hours of digging, the badger finally finished its new burrow under the hedge.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: den - the home of some wild animals, usually larger and more enclosed than a burrow
  • Synonym: nest - a structure or place made or chosen by an animal to lay eggs or give birth

Etymology

From Old English *burh* (dwelling, shelter, fortified place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09252948-n
Synset POS: n
