Phlebotomus

1. NOUN

/ fləˈbɑtəˌməs / (AmE), / flɪˈbɒtəməs / (BrE)
A genus of small bloodsucking sand flies that resemble moths, some species of which can transmit diseases to humans and animals.
genus of small bloodsucking sand flies
medical

Examples

Phlebotomus flies are known vectors of leishmaniasis in tropical regions.
Researchers collected several species of Phlebotomus for entomological studies.
The presence of Phlebotomus in the area raised concerns about potential disease transmission.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sand fly - small biting flies of subfamily Phlebotominae

Etymology

From Greek 'phlebotomos', meaning 'vein-cutting', from 'phleps' (vein) + 'temnein' (to cut).

2. NOUN

/ fləˈbɑːtə məs / (AmE), / flɪˈbɒtəməs / (BrE)
A genus of small, bloodsucking sand flies that resemble moths, many species of which can transmit diseases to humans and animals.
a genus of bloodsucking sand flies
medical

Examples

Scientists identified the Phlebotomus species responsible for the outbreak of leishmaniasis in the region.
The Phlebotomus fly is notorious for its ability to transmit parasitic diseases to mammals.
Researchers are studying how Phlebotomus sand flies adapt to different environmental conditions.

Etymology

From Greek *phleps* (vein) + *tomos* (cutting), referring to vein-cutting or bloodsucking.
