literal

1. ADJ

/ ˈlɪt̬.ɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈlɪt.ər.əl / (BrE)
used to emphasize that something is exactly true and not exaggerated or embellished; without exaggeration.
exactly true; not exaggerated

Examples

When she said she was starving, she didn't mean it was the literal truth.
The judge asked for the literal facts, without any exaggeration.
His account was the literal version of what happened, unembellished and direct.

Synset Examples

it's the literal truth

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figurative - not literal; using metaphorical or symbolic meaning

Etymology

From Latin 'litteralis' (of or relating to letters).

2. ADJ

/ ˈlɪt̬.ɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈlɪt.ər.əl / (BrE)
Taking words or representations in their usual or most basic sense, without interpretation or embellishment.
not figurative; exact or factual

Examples

Her account of the accident was a literal description of what happened.
This passage should be understood in its literal sense, not as a metaphor.
The reporter gave a literal translation of the president's statement.

Synset Examples

a literal depiction of the scene before him

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figurative - not literal; using metaphor or symbolism

Etymology

From Late Latin 'litteralis', from Latin 'littera' (letter).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00918097-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈlɪtəɹəl / (AmE), / ˈlɪtərəl / (BrE)
being or reflecting the true or essential nature of something; genuine or actual.
reflecting the true or essential character

Examples

He experienced a literal solitude during his months in the remote cabin.
Her literal motive for leaving was concern for her family, not ambition.
The landscape offered a literal beauty rarely seen elsewhere.

Synset Examples

her actual motive
a literal solitude like a desert
a genuine dilemma

Related Terms

  • Synonym: genuine - truly what something is said to be; authentic
  • Synonym: actual - existing in fact; real and not imagined
  • Antonym: figurative - departing from a literal use of words; metaphorical

Etymology

From Late Latin literal(is), from Latin littera (letter).

4. ADJ

/ ˈlɪt̬.ɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈlɪt.ər.əl / (BrE)
Taking words in their usual or most basic sense, without metaphor or exaggeration.
taking words in basic, non-figurative sense

Examples

A literal translation can sometimes sound awkward in English.
The judge insisted on a literal interpretation of the law.
Children often have a literal understanding of idioms, leading to confusion.

Synset Examples

a literal translation

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figurative - departing from a literal use of words; metaphorical
  • Synonym: exact - accurate, not approximate or metaphorical

Etymology
