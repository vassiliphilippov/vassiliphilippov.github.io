sourness

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊrnəs / (AmE), / ˈsaʊənəs / (BrE)
a sullen, moody, or resentful attitude or disposition
sullen or resentful disposition

Examples

There was a noticeable sourness in her tone when she spoke about her colleague.
His sourness seemed to infect the whole office, making everyone tense.
Despite their success, a lingering sourness remained between the two former friends.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bitterness - resentful or cynical attitude
  • Antonym: sweetness - pleasant or agreeable quality or disposition

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French *sur*, from Latin *sūrus*, meaning 'sour.'

2. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊrnəs / (AmE), / ˈsaʊənəs / (BrE)
the quality or state of having a sharp, acidic taste or character
the quality of being sour or acidic

Examples

The sourness of the lemonade made her lips pucker.
A hint of sourness in the wine indicated it had gone bad.
He prefers yogurt with just the right amount of sourness for a refreshing taste.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acidity - the quality of being acid; sourness
  • Antonym: sweetness - the quality of being sweet in taste or character

Etymology

From the adjective 'sour' + suffix '-ness', forming a noun indicating a state or quality.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊrnəs / (AmE), / ˈsaʊənɪs / (BrE)
The quality of having a sharp, acidic taste, like that of lemon juice or vinegar.
sharp, acidic taste quality

Examples

The sourness of the lemon made my mouth pucker immediately.
She likes the sourness of yogurt more than its sweetness.
The sourness in the sauce balanced the richness of the meat.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: bitterness - the quality of having a harsh, sharp taste, less acidic than sourness
  • Synonym: acidity - the degree to which a substance is sour; often used more technically in chemistry and food science

Etymology

From 'sour' + '-ness', Middle English 'sour' from Old English 'sūr', meaning 'sour, acidic'.
