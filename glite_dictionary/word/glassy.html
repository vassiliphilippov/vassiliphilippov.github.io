glassy

1. ADJ

/ ˈɡlæsi / (AmE), / ˈɡlɑːsi / (BrE)
(of ceramics) having a shiny, nonporous surface produced by fusing a vitreous coating to it.
having a shiny, nonporous ceramic surface

Examples

The vase had a glassy finish that made it smooth and reflective.
After firing, the pottery developed a glassy coating, making it both attractive and waterproof.
This tile’s glassy surface resists stains and is easy to clean.

Synset Examples

glassy porcelain
hard vitreous china used for plumbing fixtures

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vitreous - having the nature or appearance of glass

Etymology

From 'glass' + '-y', describing characteristics similar to glass.

2. ADJ

/ ˈɡlæsi / (AmE), / ˈɡlɑːsi / (BrE)
smooth, shiny, and slick like glass; resembling glass in appearance or texture
smooth and shiny like glass

Examples

After the storm, the glassy roads reflected the city lights brilliantly.
She admired the glassy finish of the newly polished marble floor.
The pond was so still in the morning that its surface looked glassy.

Synset Examples

the glassy surface of the lake
the pavement was … glassy with water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lustrous - having a gentle sheen or soft glow
  • Antonym: dull - lacking brightness or sheen

Etymology

From 'glass' + '-y', meaning 'like glass'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02245505-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈɡlæsi / (AmE), / ˈɡlɑːsi / (BrE)
(of eyes) showing no expression or liveliness; dull and unresponsive in appearance, often with a shiny surface.
dull, unfocused, or lifeless eyes

Examples

Her glassy eyes stared into the distance, betraying no emotion.
After hours of studying, his gaze became glassy and unfocused.
The patient’s glassy stare concerned the nurse, who called for the doctor.

Synset Examples

empty eyes
a glassy stare
his eyes were glazed over with boredom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glazed - (of eyes) having a fixed, shiny, and expressionless appearance
  • Synonym: lifeless - without life or energy
  • Antonym: alert - attentive and responsive in appearance

Etymology

From Middle English glasen, from Old English glæs (glass), referring to a glass-like appearance.

Source: WordNet
