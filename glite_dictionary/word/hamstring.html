hamstring

1. NOUN

/ ˈhæmˌstrɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈham.strɪŋ / (BrE)
one of the tendons at the back of the knee, or the group of muscles at the back of the thigh that bends the knee and straightens the hip
tendon/muscle at back of knee or thigh

Examples

After tearing his hamstring, he was unable to finish the race.
The athlete spent months in rehab for a hamstring injury.
Stretching before exercise helps prevent hamstring strains.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: quadriceps - the large muscle group at the front of the thigh

Etymology

From Old English ham ('back of the thigh') + string ('cord, tendon').

2. VERB

/ ˈhæmˌstrɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈhæm.strɪŋ / (BrE)
to cripple or severely disable by cutting the hamstring tendon, especially in the leg of a person or animal
cripple by cutting the hamstring

Examples

The invaders would sometimes hamstring their captives to prevent escape.
The cruel practice of hamstringing horses rendered them useless for work or war.
In medieval times, traitors might be hamstrung as punishment for their disloyalty.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cripple - to cause someone to become unable to walk or move properly

Etymology

From Middle English ham (back of the knee) + string (cord, tendon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00091603-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈhæmˌstrɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈhæm.strɪŋ / (BrE)
to make someone or something powerless or ineffective, especially by restricting their activity or capability
to make powerless or ineffective

Examples

New rules may hamstring small businesses trying to compete with larger firms.
The team's progress was hamstrung by budget cuts and staff shortages.
Bureaucratic red tape continues to hamstring efforts to improve public services.

Synset Examples

The teachers were hamstrung by the overly rigid schedules

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cripple - to severely limit the functionality, efficiency, or power of something
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to make stronger, more powerful, or more effective

Etymology

Originally from 'hamstring' (noun: a tendon in the back of the thigh); figurative use derives from the idea of crippling by cutting the hamstring.
