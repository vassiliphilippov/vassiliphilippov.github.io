whistling

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɪs.lɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɪs.lɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of producing musical sounds with the lips, usually as a tune
the act of producing a tune by whistling

Examples

I heard his cheerful whistling coming from the kitchen as he prepared breakfast.
Her whistling filled the empty street with lively echoes in the early morning.
The whistling stopped abruptly when he saw someone approaching.

Synset Examples

his cheerful whistling indicated that he enjoyed his work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: singing - the act of producing musical tones with the voice

Etymology

From Middle English 'whistlen', from Old English 'hwistlian', imitative of the sound.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪslɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɪslɪŋ / (BrE)
a high-pitched sound produced by something moving rapidly or by air or steam escaping through a narrow opening
high-pitched sound of air or movement

Examples

The whistling of the wind kept her awake all night.
We could hear the whistling of the train as it sped past the station.
Suddenly, a sharp whistling came from the kettle as the water began to boil.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whistle - a clear, high-pitched sound or the act of producing such a sound
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07414756-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪslɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɪslɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of signaling or attracting attention by making a whistling sound, especially by blowing a whistle
signaling using a whistle or whistling

Examples

The referee's whistling signaled a foul and stopped the play.
Her whistling brought the dog running back to her side.
There was a loud whistling at the end of the match as the coach called the players off the field.

Synset Examples

the whistle signalled the end of the game

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whistle - a device that produces a sound when air is blown through it

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'whistle' + suffix '-ing'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06818956-n
Synset POS: n
