stigmatic

1. NOUN

/ stɪgˈmætɪk / (AmE), / stɪgˈmatɪk / (BrE)
a person whose body is marked by religious stigmata, such as wounds resembling those of the crucified Christ
person exhibiting religious stigmata

Examples

The medieval church investigated several supposed stigmatics for signs of fraud.
Many pilgrims journeyed to see the famous stigmatic display her wounds.
The priest believed the wounds on the stigmatic were a sign of divine favor.

Etymology

From Greek 'stigma' (mark, tattoo) via Medieval Latin 'stigmaticus'.

2. ADJ

/ stɪgˈmætɪk / (AmE), / stɪgˈmatɪk / (BrE)
(of an optical system or surface) having the property that light rays converge to a single focal point, not suffering from astigmatism.
having no astigmatism (in optics)

Examples

The lens is stigmatic and produces a clear, sharp image.
A stigmatic optical system is essential for high-quality photography.
Unlike astigmatic mirrors, stigmatic mirrors focus all rays to a single point.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: astigmatic - having or relating to astigmatism

Etymology

From Greek *stigma* (mark, point) via Medieval Latin *stigmaticus*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00109406-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ stɪgˈmætɪk / (AmE), / stɪgˈmætɪk / (BrE)
(of a lens or optical system) free from astigmatism, able to produce precise point images.
free from astigmatism (optics)

Examples

The new telescope features a stigmatic lens system for clearer, sharper images.
To correct for distortion, the optical engineer designed a highly stigmatic camera lens.
A perfectly stigmatic focus is essential in high-precision microscopy.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: astigmatic - not sharply focused due to astigmatism (optics)

Etymology

From Greek stigma (a mark, spot) via Late Latin 'stigmaticus'.

4. ADJ

/ stɪgˈmætɪk / (AmE), / stɪgˈmatɪk / (BrE)
Relating to or resembling stigmata; having marks or characteristics similar to stigmata.
relating to or resembling stigmata

Examples

Some saints are described as stigmatic, displaying mysterious wounds similar to those of Christ.
The stigmatic appearance of her hands drew the attention of the local church.
In the Middle Ages, individuals believed to be stigmatic often attracted both admiration and suspicion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stigmatized - marked by stigma or social disapproval

Etymology

From Late Latin stigmaticus, from Greek stigma (mark, tattoo).
