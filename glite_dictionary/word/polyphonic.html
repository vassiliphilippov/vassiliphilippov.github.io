polyphonic

1. ADJ

/ ˌpɑːliˈfɑːnɪk / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈfɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of a letter or symbol) having two or more possible pronunciations or phonetic values.
having two or more pronunciations (linguistics)

Examples

The letter 'a' in English is often polyphonic, with different pronunciations in words like 'cat' and 'cake'.
In linguistics, a polyphonic letter requires context to determine its sound value.
Some alphabets contain polyphonic characters that change pronunciation based on surrounding letters.

Synset Examples

polyphonic letters such as ‘a’

Etymology

From Greek poluphōnos, from poly- (many) + phōnē (sound).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02867009-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˌpɑːliˈfɑːnɪk / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈfɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of music) having two or more independent melodic voices that are harmonically related and sounding together.
music with multiple independent melodic lines

Examples

The choir performed a polyphonic piece from the Renaissance era.
My new synthesizer can produce polyphonic sounds, allowing for more complex arrangements.
He is studying polyphonic textures in Baroque music for his thesis.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monophonic - music consisting of a single melodic line

Etymology

From Greek 'polyphōnos', from 'poly-' (many) + 'phōnē' (sound, voice).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01548336-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ˌpɑliˈfɑnɪk / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈfɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of music or a musical texture) consisting of two or more independent melodic lines performed simultaneously.
having two or more simultaneous melodies

Examples

The choir performed a polyphonic composition from the Renaissance period.
Her latest album explores polyphonic textures using both traditional and electronic instruments.
Many Baroque pieces are known for their intricate polyphonic structures.

Synset Examples

polyphonic traditions of the baroque

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monophonic - consisting of a single melodic line without harmonies
  • Synonym: polyphony - music with two or more simultaneous independent melodic parts

Etymology

From Greek polyphōnos, from poly- (many) + phōnē (sound, voice).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02866642-a
Synset POS: a
