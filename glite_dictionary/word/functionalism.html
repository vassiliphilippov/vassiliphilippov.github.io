functionalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈfʌŋkʃənəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈfʌŋkʃənəlɪzm / (BrE)
a theory in psychology that considers mental processes as serving adaptive purposes, helping individuals adjust to their environment
psychological theory: mental processes serve adaptation

Examples

Functionalism emphasizes how mental activities help an organism adapt to its environment.
Many early American psychologists were strong advocates of functionalism in opposition to structuralism.
In discussing psychological theories, she presented functionalism as the foundation for modern applied psychology.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: structuralism - theory in psychology focusing on the structure of the mind

Etymology

From function + -al + -ism, ultimately from Latin 'functio' (performance) and Greek '-ismos' (practice, system).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfʌŋkʃənəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈfʌŋkʃənəlɪzəm / (BrE)
the belief or doctrine that the function or purpose of something should be the focus, especially in philosophy, architecture, and the social sciences.
doctrine focusing on function or utility

Examples

Many modern buildings reflect functionalism by emphasizing practicality over decoration.
In sociology, functionalism examines how social institutions fulfill useful roles within society.
Critics argue that strict functionalism sometimes ignores the emotional or cultural aspects of design.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: functionality - the quality of being suited to serve a purpose well; practicality

Etymology

From 'function' + '-alism', modeled on similar terms for philosophical doctrines.
