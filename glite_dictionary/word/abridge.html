to abridge

1. VERB

/ əˈbrɪdʒ / (AmE), / əˈbrɪdʒ / (BrE)
to make a book, play, text, or speech shorter by removing parts while preserving its essential meaning
make shorter while keeping the main parts

Examples

The author decided to abridge the novel for younger readers.
The legal document was abridged to make it easier to understand.
He is abridging the lengthy speech to fit the time limit.

Synset Examples

The manuscript must be shortened

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to shorten - to make something shorter in length, duration, or extent
  • Antonym: to extend - to make something longer or larger in scope

Etymology

From Old French 'abregier', from Latin 'abbreviare' (to shorten).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ əˈbrɪdʒ / (AmE), / əˈbrɪdʒ / (BrE)
to reduce, shorten, or curtail something, especially rights or privileges.
reduce or shorten; curtail

Examples

The new rules may abridge individual rights.
The policy was designed not to abridge any freedoms guaranteed by law.
Some argue that the proposal would abridge the scope of public debate.

Synset Examples

the new law might abridge our freedom of expression

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to curtail - to reduce in extent or quantity; restrict
  • Synonym: to diminish - to make or become less
  • Antonym: to expand - to increase in size, number, or importance

Etymology

From Old French *abregier*, from Late Latin *abbreviare* (to shorten).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00237729-v
Synset POS: v
