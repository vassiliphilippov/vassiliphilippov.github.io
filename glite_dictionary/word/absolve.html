to absolve

1. VERB

/ əbˈzɑlv / (AmE), / əbˈzɒlv / (BrE)
to officially declare someone free from blame, responsibility, or guilt
declare free from blame or responsibility

Examples

The priest promised to absolve him of his sins.
The committee voted to absolve the chairman from any wrongdoing.
You cannot simply absolve yourself of responsibility for the damage caused.

Synset Examples

I absolve you from this responsibility

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exonerate - to officially clear someone from blame or fault
  • Antonym: to condemn - to officially pronounce someone to be guilty or at fault

Etymology

From Latin absolutus, past participle of absolvere (to set free, acquit).

2. VERB

/ əbˈzɑlv / (AmE), / əbˈzɒlv / (BrE)
(especially in a religious context) to formally free someone from blame or responsibility for a sin, obligation, or wrongdoing.
formally free from blame or sin

Examples

The priest promised to absolve her of all her sins if she confessed sincerely.
The church has the authority to absolve those who truly repent.
He hoped that apologizing would absolve him from any blame.

Synset Examples

The priest absolved him and told him to say ten Hail Marys

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to forgive - to stop feeling angry or resentful toward someone for an offense or mistake
  • Antonym: to condemn - to declare someone to be guilty of wrongdoing

Etymology

From Latin absolvere (to set free, acquit), from ab- (from) + solvere (to loosen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00905665-v
Synset POS: v
