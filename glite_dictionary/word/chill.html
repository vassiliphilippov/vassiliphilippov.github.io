chill

1. NOUN

/ tʃɪl / (AmE), / tʃɪl / (BrE)
a sudden feeling of fear, excitement, or anticipation, often experienced as a slight shiver
a sudden feeling of fear or excitement

Examples

A chill ran down her spine as she entered the dark house.
He felt a chill of anticipation before the curtain lifted.
The ghost story sent a chill through everyone in the room.

Synset Examples

a frisson of surprise shot through him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frisson - a sudden strong feeling of excitement or fear; a thrill

2. VERB

/ tʃɪl / (AmE), / tʃɪl / (BrE)
to make (something) cool or colder, especially by refrigeration
make cool or colder

Examples

Please chill the drinks before serving them at the party.
You should chill the dough in the refrigerator for at least an hour.
After boiling the eggs, chill them in cold water to stop the cooking process.

Synset Examples

Chill the food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cool - make or become less warm

Etymology

From Old English ciele (cold, chill); related to Old Norse kaldi (cold).

6. NOUN

/ tʃɪl / (AmE), / tʃɪl / (BrE)
A feeling of coldness, often caused by a drop in temperature or a cold environment.
coldness; a sensation of being cold

Examples

A sudden chill filled the room when the window was opened.
She wrapped her coat tighter to protect herself from the evening chill.
There was a noticeable chill in the air as autumn approached.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: warmth - a state or sensation of being warm
  • Degree Higher: frost - extreme cold, often causing freezing
  • Degree Lower: coolness - a state or sensation of being slightly cold

Etymology

From Old English 'ciele' (cold, chill) and 'ceald' (cold).

to discourage or depress

Examples

Rumors of layoffs chilled the previously optimistic staff.

Related Terms
