Caryophyllidae

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɛriəˌfaɪəˈlɪdi.i / (AmE), / ˌkarɪəˌfɪlɪˈdiː / (BrE)
a former subclass of flowering plants that included families such as Caryophyllaceae, Amaranthaceae, and Cactaceae, characterized by mostly basal or central placentation and trinucleate pollen
subclass of flowering plants (includes carnations, cacti, etc.)

Examples

The Caryophyllidae were once classified as a major subgroup of flowering plants, including carnations and cacti.
Modern taxonomists often discuss the reclassification of Caryophyllidae within new molecular frameworks.
Features such as trinucleate pollen were key in distinguishing Caryophyllidae from other plant groups.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Caryophyllaceae - the carnation or pink family of flowering plants
  • Synonym: Cactaceae - the cactus family of flowering plants

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Caryophyllaceae (the carnation family), from Greek 'karyophyllon' (clove).

2. NOUN

/ ker.i.oʊˌfɪl.ɪˈdiː / (AmE), / kar.ɪ.əʊˌfɪl.ɪˈdiː / (BrE)
a botanical subclass comprising several families of mostly flowering plants, characterized by basal or central placentation and trinucleate pollen
a subclass of flowering plants

Examples

The family of cacti is included in the subclass Caryophyllidae.
Researchers found that plants in Caryophyllidae often have trinucleate pollen grains.
Several important agricultural crops are classified within the Caryophyllidae.

Etymology

From Modern Latin, based on genus Caryophyllus (carnation) + Greek -idae (descending from).
