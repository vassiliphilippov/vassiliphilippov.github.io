Baroque

1. NOUN

/ bəˈroʊk / (AmE), / bəˈrəʊk / (BrE)
the period in European history, art, and music from about 1600 to 1750 characterized by ornate detail and expressive style
European cultural period, 1600–1750

Examples

The Baroque was a time of dramatic artistic, musical, and architectural innovation throughout Europe.
Many cathedrals built during the Baroque feature intricately decorated interiors.
Handel and Bach were two of the greatest composers of the Baroque era.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Renaissance - the period of European history immediately preceding the Baroque, roughly the 14th–16th centuries
  • Degree Higher: Classical period - the period in art and music following the Baroque, roughly 1750–1820

Etymology

From French baroque, from Portuguese barroco (irregular pearl), later applied to ornate art and architecture.

2. ADJ

/ bəˈroʊk / (AmE), / bəˈrɒk / (BrE)
relating to the highly ornate and extravagant style of European art, architecture, and music from the early 17th to mid-18th centuries
highly ornate European art style (1600–1750)

Examples

The palace is decorated in a Baroque style, with elaborate gold leaf and ornate ceilings.
Many churches built during the 17th century feature stunning Baroque architecture.
She enjoys listening to Baroque music, especially the works of Bach and Handel.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Rococo - a later, even more ornate artistic style
  • Antonym: Classical - relating to the artistic style after Baroque, emphasizing simplicity and symmetry

Etymology

From French 'baroque', probably from Portuguese 'barroco' (irregular pearl).
