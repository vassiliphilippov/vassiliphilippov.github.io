plodder

1. NOUN

/ ˈplɑːdər / (AmE), / ˈplɒdə / (BrE)
a person who moves or works slowly and steadily, often without excitement or enthusiasm
person who moves or works slowly

Examples

Despite his lack of speed, the plodder finished the marathon with determination.
She was a plodder at work, always reliable, if never especially quick or innovative.
The teacher encouraged the plodder in her class to keep going and not get discouraged.

Synset Examples

in England they call a slowpoke a ‘slowcoach’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slowpoke - person who moves or acts slowly
  • Synonym: laggard - person who makes slow progress and falls behind others

Etymology

From 'plod', of uncertain origin, meaning to walk heavily or move laboriously.

2. NOUN

/ ˈplɑːdər / (AmE), / ˈplɒdə / (BrE)
A person who walks in a slow, heavy, and laborious way.
person who walks laboriously

Examples

The plodder made his way down the muddy lane, leaving deep footprints behind.
Unlike the sprinters, he was a plodder, taking his time over every step.
Though considered a plodder, she reached the finish line with steady determination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trudger - a person who walks slow and wearily
  • Antonym: strider - a person who walks with long, decisive steps

Etymology

From the verb 'plod', of uncertain origin, meaning to walk heavily or laboriously.

3. NOUN

/ ˈplɑːdər / (AmE), / ˈplɒdə / (BrE)
a person who works steadily but slowly and often without much imagination or enthusiasm
one who works slowly and steadily

Examples

Although he was a plodder, his reliability made him an asset to the company.
The novelist was often described as a plodder rather than a creative genius.
Even though she's a plodder, she always gets the job done in the end.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drudge - a person who does dull, menial, or tedious work
  • Synonym: grinder - a person who works hard, especially at tedious work
  • Antonym: dynamo - an energetic, hardworking person

Etymology

From the verb 'plod' (to walk or work with slow, heavy steps) + '-er'.
