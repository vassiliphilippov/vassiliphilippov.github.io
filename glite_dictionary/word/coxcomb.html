coxcomb

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑksˌkoʊm / (AmE), / ˈkɒks.kəʊm / (BrE)
the fleshy red crest on the head of a domestic rooster and other related birds
red crest on rooster's head

Examples

The rooster's coxcomb was bright red and stood out against its feathers.
With pride, the bird strutted about, its coxcomb held high.
A healthy coxcomb is often a sign of a well-nourished chicken.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comb - the crest on the head of a bird, especially a rooster

Etymology

From Middle English cokescombe (‘fool’s cap’), later used for the bird’s crest due to the resemblance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑkskoʊm / (AmE), / ˈkɒkskəʊm / (BrE)
A cap worn by court jesters, typically adorned with a strip of red fabric or feathers.
cap worn by jesters, often red

Examples

The jester entered the hall wearing a bright coxcomb on his head.
His coxcomb, tipped with tiny bells, jingled with every move he made.
In medieval paintings, the coxcomb is a symbol of folly and jest.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jester - a professional entertainer in medieval times, usually wearing distinctive costume

Etymology

From Middle English 'coxcomb', meaning 'cock's comb', referring to the comb of a rooster.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑksˌkoʊm / (AmE), / ˈkɒks.kəʊm / (BrE)
an overly conceited man, especially one excessively concerned with his appearance or accomplishments; a vain dandy
vain, self-important man
archaic

Examples

The nobleman was known as a coxcomb, admired for his wardrobe but mocked for his vanity.
He strutted through the ballroom like an utter coxcomb, drawing disapproving looks from the other guests.
In Restoration comedies, the character of the coxcomb often provides comic relief through his foolish pride.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dandy - a man who places particular importance upon physical appearance and style
  • Synonym: fop - a man who is excessively concerned with his clothes and appearance

Etymology

From Middle English 'cokkes comb' (literally ‘cock’s comb’), probably from the resemblance of a jester’s cap to a rooster’s comb; later referring derisively to vain men.
