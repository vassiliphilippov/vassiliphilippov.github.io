placebo

1. NOUN

/ pləˈsiboʊ / (AmE), / pləˈsiːbəʊ / (BrE)
a substance with no therapeutic effect, often given to a patient or used as a control in testing new drugs.
inactive substance given as treatment

Examples

The patients in the control group received a placebo instead of the active medication.
Some people experience improvement in symptoms simply because they believe the placebo is an actual drug.
The new drug was tested against a placebo to determine its real effectiveness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nocebo - a substance that causes negative effects, opposite of placebo

Etymology

From Latin 'placebo', meaning 'I shall please'.

2. NOUN

/ pləˈsiboʊ / (AmE), / pləˈsiːbəʊ / (BrE)
(historical) In the Roman Catholic Church, the vespers service as part of the Office for the Dead.
vespers for the dead (historical, Catholic)
archaic

Examples

The family attended the placebo at the church on the eve of All Souls' Day.
In medieval times, monks would chant the placebo for the souls of the departed.
The placebo was an important part of Catholic funeral rites centuries ago.

Etymology

From Latin 'placebo' (I shall please), the first word of the antiphon traditionally sung at this service.
