dominant

1. ADJ

/ ˈdɑːmɪnənt / (AmE), / ˈdɒmɪnənt / (BrE)
(of a gene) producing the same observable trait whether paired with an identical or a different allele.
gene that masks the effect of others
medical

Examples

In pea plants, the dominant allele determines flower color, even if only one copy is present.
Brown eyes are typically a dominant genetic trait.
A child only needs one dominant gene from either parent to express the trait.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: recessive - (of a gene) producing its effect only when paired with an identical allele

Etymology

From Latin dominans, dominant-, present participle of dominari (to rule, dominate).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɑːmɪnənt / (AmE), / ˈdɒmɪnənt / (BrE)
(Music) The fifth note or degree of a diatonic scale, which is important in establishing the key and harmony of a piece of music.
fifth note of a diatonic scale (music)

Examples

In the key of C major, the dominant is the note G.
The melody moved from the tonic to the dominant before resolving.
Many classical pieces use the dominant to create tension before returning to the home key.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tonic - the first note of a diatonic scale, serving as the home note
  • Degree Lower: subdominant - the fourth note of a diatonic scale, below the dominant

Etymology

From Latin dominans, dominant-, present participle of dominari 'to rule'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈdɑːmənənt / (AmE), / ˈdɒmɪnənt / (BrE)
An allele that produces the same observable effect whether paired with an identical or a different allele.
genetic allele always determining trait
medical

Examples

The brown eye color gene acts as a dominant in humans.
If one parent has a dominant for curly hair, the child may inherit curly hair as well.
In a Punnett square, a dominant masks the effect of a recessive allele.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: recessive - an allele that only produces an effect when paired with an identical allele

Etymology

From Latin 'dominari' (to rule).
