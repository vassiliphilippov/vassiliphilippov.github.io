grudge

1. VERB

/ ɡrʌdʒ / (AmE), / ɡrʌdʒ / (BrE)
to give, allow, or admit something unwillingly or with reluctance
to do something reluctantly; give or allow unwillingly

Examples

She grudged every minute she had to spend on paperwork.
He gave his consent, but he clearly grudged it.
They grudge paying so much for basic services.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to begrudge - to give, allow, or admit something with reluctance or resentment

Etymology

From Middle English 'grucchen', from Old French 'grouchier' (to grumble).

2. VERB

/ ɡrʌdʒ / (AmE), / ɡrʌdʒ / (BrE)
to feel or show that you are unwilling to give or allow something, often because of resentment or ill feeling
to resent or be unwilling to give

Examples

He still grudges his brother's success after all these years.
She grudged him the recognition he deserved.
They grudge every penny spent on necessary repairs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to resent - to feel bitterness or indignation at a circumstance, action, or person

Etymology

From Middle English 'gruggen' (to murmur, grumble), of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01777926-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ɡrʌdʒ / (AmE), / ɡrʌdʒ / (BrE)
a persistent feeling of ill will or resentment resulting from a past insult or injury, often strong enough to provoke thoughts of retaliation
a persistent feeling of resentment

Examples

She had held a grudge against her former friend for years after their argument.
It's not healthy to harbor a grudge over something so minor.
Despite his boss's harsh words, Tom tried not to develop a grudge.

Synset Examples

holding a grudge
settling a score

Related Terms

  • Synonym: resentment - a feeling of anger or displeasure about someone or something unfair
  • Antonym: forgiveness - the act of forgiving or pardoning an offense

Etymology

From Middle English 'grucchen' (to murmur or complain), from Old French 'grouchier'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
