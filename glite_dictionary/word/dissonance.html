dissonance

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪs.ə.nəns / (AmE), / ˈdɪs.ə.nəns / (BrE)
A lack of agreement or harmony between people, opinions, or actions.
lack of agreement; conflict

Examples

There was obvious dissonance among the team members regarding the next steps for the project.
Political dissonance increased as the candidates presented opposing views.
The dissonance between his words and his actions made people question his sincerity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discord - disagreement or conflict between people
  • Antonym: harmony - agreement; a state of peaceful coexistence

Etymology

From Latin dissonantia, from dissonare (dis- ‘apart’ + sonare ‘to sound’).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɪs.ə.nəns / (AmE), / ˈdɪs.ə.nəns / (BrE)
A combination of sounds that are harsh or unpleasant to hear; a lack of harmony.
harsh or discordant sounds

Examples

The piece ends with a burst of dissonance that surprises the audience.
Many modern composers make use of dissonance to create tension in their music.
He winced at the sudden dissonance coming from the orchestra's rehearsal.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consonance - a combination of sounds that are pleasant or harmonious

Etymology

From Latin dissonantia, from dissonare (to sound apart), from dis- (apart) + sonare (to sound).

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɪsənəns / (AmE), / ˈdɪsənəns / (BrE)
A combination of sounds that are harsh, jarring, or lack harmony, often creating a sense of tension in music or unpleasantness to the ear.
harsh or jarring sound; lack of harmony

Examples

The dissonance in the experimental piece made some listeners cover their ears.
His music is full of dissonance and unexpected chord changes.
To emphasize the story’s tension, the composer used sudden dissonance in the background score.

Synset Examples

modern music is just noise to me

Related Terms

  • Antonym: consonance - a combination of notes that are harmonious or pleasing to the ear

Etymology

From Latin 'dissonantia', from 'dissonare' (to sound apart, disagree).
