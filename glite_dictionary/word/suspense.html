suspense

1. NOUN

/ səˈspɛns / (AmE), / səˈspɛns / (BrE)
a feeling of excited or anxious uncertainty about what may happen
nervous anticipation about what will occur

Examples

There was a feeling of suspense in the air as everyone waited for the results.
The movie kept audiences in suspense until the very last scene.
Her announcement put us all in suspense about her future plans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anticipation - a feeling of looking forward to an event
  • Antonym: relief - absence of anxiety after uncertainty

Etymology

From Old French 'suspens' (delay, uncertainty), from Latin 'suspensus', past participle of 'suspendere' (to hang up, keep in doubt).

2. NOUN

/ səˈspɛns / (AmE), / səˈspɛns / (BrE)
A feeling of excited or anxious uncertainty about what may happen next, especially before a climax or important event.
state of anxious anticipation

Examples

The movie maintained a high level of suspense until the very end.
The author creates suspense by revealing clues one at a time.
We waited in suspense for the results of the competition.

Synset Examples

the play kept the audience in suspense

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anticipation - expectation or looking forward to something
  • Antonym: certainty - the state of being sure or confident

Etymology

From Old French 'suspens', from Latin 'suspensus' (suspended, doubtful, uncertain).

3. NOUN

/ səˈspɛns / (AmE), / səˈspɛns / (BrE)
A state or feeling of uncertainty and anxious anticipation about what may happen.
uncertainty or anxious anticipation

Examples

The movie kept the audience in suspense until the very last scene.
She waited in suspense for the results of the competition.
The matter remained in suspense for several years.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anticipation - the feeling of looking forward expectantly to something
  • Antonym: certainty - the state of being sure or confident about something

Etymology

From Old French 'suspens' (uncertain, doubtful), from Latin 'suspensus' (suspended, in doubt).
