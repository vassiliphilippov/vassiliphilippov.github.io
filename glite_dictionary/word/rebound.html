rebound

1. NOUN

/ ˈriˌbaʊnd / (AmE), / ˈriːbaʊnd / (BrE)
An act of gaining possession of the basketball after a missed shot.
basketball: act of gaining possession after a missed shot

Examples

She had ten rebounds in last night's basketball game.
After missing the shot, he jumped up and grabbed the rebound.
Securing the defensive rebound is crucial in a close game.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to retrieve - to get or bring (something) back; regain possession
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00140932-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ rɪˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / rɪˈbaʊnd / (BrE)
to spring back or bounce away after hitting a surface or object
to spring back after impact

Examples

The ball hit the wall and quickly rebounded across the room.
Her enthusiasm rebounded after hearing the good news.
Basketballs often rebound off the rim if the shot isn’t accurate.

Synset Examples

The rubber ball bounced
These particles do not resile but they unite after they collide

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bounce - to spring back after hitting a surface

Etymology

From Old French *rebondir* (to bounce back), from Latin *re-* (again) + *bondir* (to leap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01896097-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈriˌbaʊnd / (AmE), / ˈriːbaʊnd / (BrE)
a sudden movement back after hitting something; a reaction when force causes something to return to its original position or direction
movement back after impact

Examples

The basketball made a loud rebound off the backboard before dropping into the net.
After striking the wall, the ball took a sharp rebound toward the players.
The car's tire suffered a rebound after hitting the curb, causing a slight jolt.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bounce - the movement of something springing back after hitting a surface

Etymology

From Old French 'rebondir', from re- (again) + bondir (to leap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07364792-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ riˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / ˈriːbaʊnd / (BrE)
a period or process of reacting emotionally, especially recovering, after a setback, crisis, or frustration, often following the end of a relationship
emotional recovery after a setback

Examples

After his divorce, he dated several people while still on the rebound.
She made some major life decisions on the rebound from losing her job.
Being on the rebound can make people act impulsively.

Synset Examples

he is still on the rebound from his wife's death

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recovery - the process of becoming well again after an illness or setback

Etymology

From Old French rebondir (to bounce back), from re- (again) + bondir (to leap).

5. VERB

/ riˈbaʊnd / (AmE), / rɪˈbaʊnd / (BrE)
to return to a previous good condition after experiencing difficulties or decline
recover or return to former condition

Examples

After several months of hardship, the company finally began to rebound and regain its market share.
He struggled with sadness after the breakup but managed to rebound quickly and felt hopeful again.
Many economies rebound after recessions, although the pace of recovery can vary.

Synset Examples

The jilted lover soon rallied and found new friends
The stock market rallied

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to recover - to return to a normal state after trouble, illness, or setback
  • Antonym: relapse - to fall back into a previous state, often worse than before; opposite of rebound in the recovery context

Etymology

From Old French rebondir, from re- (again) + bondir (to bounce).
