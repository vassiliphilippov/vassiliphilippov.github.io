precedent

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɛsɪdənt / (AmE), / ˈprɛsɪdənt / (BrE)
(in law) a previous case or legal decision that may be or (in common law) must be followed in subsequent similar cases.
an earlier legal decision serving as authority
legal

Examples

The court relied on the precedent set by an earlier ruling when making its decision.
Lawyers searched for a precedent that would support their client's case.
Setting a new precedent can shape future interpretations of the law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: example - something that serves as a model or reference for others
  • Synonym: authority - a source able to supply or verify information, or having the power to influence or decide

Etymology

From Old French precedent (preceding), from Latin praecedent-, praecedere (to go before).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɛsɪdənt / (AmE), / ˈprɛsɪdənt / (BrE)
An earlier event or action that is regarded as an example or guide to be considered in subsequent similar circumstances.
earlier example guiding future actions

Examples

The court's decision set a significant precedent for future legal cases.
There is no precedent for such a rapid technological change in history.
Lawyers often cite precedent to support their arguments in court.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: example - something that serves as a pattern or model
  • Antonym: following - coming after or next

Etymology

From Old French 'precedent' and Latin 'praecedent-', from 'praecedere' meaning 'to go before'.
