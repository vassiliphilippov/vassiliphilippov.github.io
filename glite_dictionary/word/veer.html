to veer

1. VERB

/ vɪr / (AmE), / vɪə / (BrE)
to change direction suddenly or sharply
change direction suddenly

Examples

The cyclist had to veer left to avoid the pothole.
As the storm approached, the boat began to veer off course.
He suddenly veered into the next lane without signaling.

Synset Examples

The car cut to the left at the intersection
The motorbike veered to the right

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swerve - to turn aside sharply from a straight course
  • Synonym: turn - to move or cause to move in a different direction
  • Antonym: to plow ahead - to continue moving straight ahead

Etymology

From French 'virer' (to turn), possibly from Old Provençal 'virar'.

2. VERB

/ vɪr / (AmE), / vɪə / (BrE)
(of the wind or another moving force) to change direction, especially to shift in a clockwise direction.
to change direction, especially clockwise

Examples

As the storm approached, the wind began to veer sharply to the north.
During the afternoon, the breeze will veer from west to north-east.
The captain noticed that the currents would often veer suddenly, affecting the ship’s course.

Synset Examples

the wind veered

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction (general sense)
  • Antonym: back - (of wind) to change direction in a counterclockwise direction

Etymology

From French 'virer' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00561841-v
Synset POS: v
