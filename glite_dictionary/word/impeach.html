to impeach

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpitʃ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpiːtʃ / (BrE)
to formally accuse a public official or person of wrongdoing, especially in a legal or official context
formally accuse or charge

Examples

The committee voted to impeach the governor for corruption.
Some historians argue that few leaders have actually been impeached throughout history.
Congress began impeaching the official after receiving new evidence.

Synset Examples

The neighbors accused the man of spousal abuse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accuse - to charge someone with an offense or crime
  • Antonym: to exonerate - to officially declare someone not guilty

Etymology

From Old French *empechier*, from Latin *impedicare* (to entangle).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpitʃ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpiːtʃ / (BrE)
to formally charge a public official with misconduct or an offense committed while in office
charge a public official with misconduct
legal

Examples

The House of Representatives voted to impeach the governor for abusing his powers.
Several lawmakers debated whether to impeach the judge after the scandal came to light.
He was the third president in history to be impeached by Congress.

Synset Examples

The President was impeached

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to acquit - to formally declare not guilty of criminal charges
  • Antonym: convict - to find guilty of a criminal offense

Etymology

From Old French *empechier* (to hinder), from Latin *impedicare* (to fetter).

3. VERB

/ ɪmˈpitʃ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpiːtʃ / (BrE)
to challenge or call into question the honesty, integrity, or validity of someone or something, especially a witness or their testimony
challenge the honesty or validity of
legal

Examples

The defense attorney attempted to impeach the key witness during cross-examination.
Her testimony could be impeached if new evidence is presented.
The opposing lawyer worked to impeach the reliability of her statement.

Synset Examples

the lawyers tried to impeach the credibility of the witnesses

Related Terms

  • Synonym: challenge - to question the truth or validity of something
  • Antonym: to affirm - to declare or state as true, often the opposite of challenge

Etymology

From Old French *empechier* (to hinder), from Latin *impedicare* (to fetter).
