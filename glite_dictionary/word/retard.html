to retard

1. VERB

/ rɪˈtɑrd / (AmE), / rɪˈtɑːd / (BrE)
to make the progress, development, or growth of something slower; to delay or hinder
to delay or slow down

Examples

The new regulations could retard economic growth in the region.
Low temperatures may retard the fermentation process.
Efforts to retard the spread of the disease have been only partly successful.

Synset Examples

The brain damage will retard the child's language development

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delay - to make something happen later than planned or expected
  • Antonym: to accelerate - to increase the speed or rate of something

Etymology

From Old French 'retarder', from Latin 'retardare' (to make slow, delay).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈtɑrd / (AmE), / rɪˈtɑːd / (BrE)
to make something happen more slowly than normal or to delay its progress
to delay or slow down

Examples

The thick walls help to retard the spread of fire throughout the building.
Low temperatures can retard the growth of certain crops.
Special coatings are used to retard corrosion on metal surfaces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delay - to make something late or slow its progress

Etymology

From Latin 'retardare' (to make slow, delay), from 're-' (back) + 'tardare' (to slow, make late).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02758442-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈtɑrd / (AmE), / rɪˈtɑːd / (BrE)
to slow down the development, progress, or speed of something; to cause to move or proceed more slowly
to slow down or delay

Examples

The engineer designed a feature to retard the engine in case of overheating.
Certain medications can retard the progress of the disease.
Heavy traffic can retard your journey, making you late for appointments.

Synset Examples

The car decelerated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to decelerate - to reduce speed; to slow down
  • Antonym: to accelerate - to move or cause to move faster

Etymology

From Latin retardare, from re- (back) + tardare (to make slow), from tardus (slow).
