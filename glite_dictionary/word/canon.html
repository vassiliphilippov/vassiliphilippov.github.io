canon

1. NOUN

/ ˈkænən / (AmE), / ˈkanən / (BrE)
the collection of official or recognized books, especially those accepted as part of a holy scripture such as the Bible
collection of recognized sacred books

Examples

Some early Christian communities debated which texts belonged in the biblical canon.
The Church officially recognized the New Testament canon in the fourth century.
Scholars still discuss the history of the Hebrew canon and its formation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: apocrypha - biblical or related writings not forming part of the accepted canon of Scripture

Etymology

From Greek *kanōn* (rule, standard), via Latin *canon* (rule, model).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.ən / (AmE), / ˈkan.ən / (BrE)
a general rule, principle, or standard by which something is judged, especially in a particular field such as art, literature, or philosophy
accepted rule or standard in a field

Examples

The canon of Western literature includes works by Shakespeare and Dickens.
Her theory challenged the accepted canon of modern art.
Social etiquette in the club follows a strict canon of behavior.

Synset Examples

the neoclassical canon
canons of polite society

Related Terms

  • Synonym: principle - a fundamental truth or proposition that serves as the foundation for a system of belief or behavior

Etymology

From Greek kanōn (rule, measuring rod) via Latin canon (rule).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.ən / (AmE), / ˈkan.ən / (BrE)
a contrapuntal piece of music in which a melody introduced by one part is successively imitated by other parts.
musical composition with imitative parts

Examples

The choir performed a canon in which each voice entered at different intervals.
Bach's music includes several beautifully constructed canons that showcase his mastery of counterpoint.
Learning to compose a canon is an important skill for advanced music students.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fugue - a complex polyphonic composition with thematic imitation

Etymology

From Latin 'canon' (rule), via Old French, relating to a rule or standard in music.

5. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.ən / (AmE), / ˈkæn.ən / (BrE)
a deep narrow valley with steep sides, typically formed by river erosion in an arid or semi-arid region; variant spelling of 'canyon'
deep narrow valley with steep sides

Examples

The Colorado River flows through the vast canon carved over millions of years.
We hiked along the rim of a spectacular canon in the desert.
In regions with little rain, a river may still form a dramatic canon over time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canyon - a deep gorge, typically with a river; American English spelling

Etymology

6. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.ən / (AmE), / ˈkæn.ən / (BrE)
a priest who is a member of the chapter of clergy serving a cathedral or certain collegiate churches
a member of a cathedral chapter (priest)

Examples

The newly appointed canon was welcomed into the cathedral chapter.
As a canon, she was responsible for overseeing some of the administrative work of the church.
He served as a canon for over twenty years before retiring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dean - the head of a chapter of canons in a cathedral
  • Antonym: bishop - a senior member of the Christian clergy, usually in charge of a diocese

Etymology

From Latin 'canonicus', from Greek 'kanonikos' (according to rule).
