commute

1. VERB

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
to travel regularly between home and work or another place, especially over some distance
travel regularly between two places

Examples

She commutes to the city by train every weekday.
Many people now commute long distances for work.
He used to commute over an hour each way to his office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: travel - to go from one place to another, especially over a distance

Etymology

From Latin 'commutare', meaning 'to change altogether', via French 'commuter'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02065774-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
a regular journey between one's home and place of work, especially over some distance
regular journey between home and work

Examples

My daily commute takes about an hour each way by train.
She uses the time during her commute to catch up on reading.
The long commute is starting to affect his work-life balance.

Synset Examples

there is standing room only on the high-speed commute

Etymology

From the verb 'commute', originally meaning to change or exchange; noun sense developed in the 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00308281-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
(of a punishment) to reduce a penalty to a less severe one, especially officially by authority.
reduce a punishment to something less severe

Examples

The president decided to commute her life sentence to twenty years.
The judge has the power to commute a death sentence if new evidence emerges.
After a thorough review, the governor chose to commute the prisoner's punishment to time served.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to mitigate - to make a punishment or penalty less severe

Etymology

From Latin 'commutare' (to change, exchange).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00161795-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
(of two operations or elements) to be able to exchange places or order without affecting the result or value.
have positions exchanged without altering the result (maths/physics)

Examples

In mathematics, two matrices are said to commute if their product is independent of the order.
The new operators introduced in quantum mechanics do not necessarily commute with each other.
If these matrices commute, their eigenvectors can be simultaneously determined.

Synset Examples

These operators commute with each other

Related Terms

  • Synonym: commutative - pertaining to an operation where order does not affect the result

Etymology

From Latin commutare (to change, exchange), via French commuter.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
