Stoic

1. NOUN

/ ˈstoʊɪk / (AmE), / ˈstəʊɪk / (BrE)
A member of the ancient Greek school of philosophy founded by Zeno, which taught that virtue and self-control lead to happiness and advocated acceptance of fate.
member of Greek philosophical school (Stoicism)

Examples

As a Stoic, he believed in enduring hardship without complaint.
Many Roman philosophers were followers of the Stoic tradition.
The Stoic viewed virtue as the highest good.

Synset Examples

a Stoic achieves happiness by submission to destiny

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Epicurean - a member of an ancient Greek school of philosophy that taught that pleasure is the greatest good

Etymology

From Greek stoikos (of the Stoa), referring to the Stoa Poikile where Zeno taught.

2. ADJ

/ ˈstoʊɪk / (AmE), / ˈstəʊɪk / (BrE)
Relating to Stoicism or its followers; characteristic of the ancient Greek school of philosophy that taught the development of self-control and fortitude as a means of overcoming destructive emotions.
of or relating to Stoicism or its followers

Examples

His Stoic beliefs guided his reaction to personal setbacks.
She composed a paper on Stoic philosophy for her philosophy class.
The Stoic approach emphasizes virtue and rationality above all.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Stoical - showing endurance of pain or hardship without showing feelings

Etymology

From Greek 'Stōïkos', from 'Stoa' (a porch in Athens where Zeno taught).

Synset POS: a
