premature

1. ADJ

/ ˌpriːməˈtʃʊr / (AmE), / ˌpriːməˈtjʊə / (BrE)
(of a baby) born before the end of the normal period of pregnancy.
born before full term of pregnancy
medical

Examples

The hospital has a special unit for premature babies.
A premature birth can lead to various health complications.
She was worried when her daughter delivered a premature infant.

Synset Examples

a premature infant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preterm - born or occurring before the expected time, especially referring to childbirth
  • Antonym: full-term - born after the normal length of pregnancy

Etymology

From Latin praematurus, from prae- 'before' + maturus 'ripe, mature'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˌpriməˈtʃʊr / (AmE), / ˌpriːməˈtʃʊə / (BrE)
Happening or done before the usual or proper time; too soon or too hasty.
occurring too soon; too hasty

Examples

Making such a decision now would be premature.
His premature announcement caused confusion among the team.
It was premature to celebrate victory before the final results were in.

Synset Examples

our condemnation of him was a bit previous
a premature judgment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: early - happening or done before the expected time
  • Antonym: belated - happening later than expected or usual

Etymology

From Latin praematurus, from prae (before) + maturus (ripe, mature).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00818796-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˌpriːməˈtʃʊr / (AmE), / ˌpriːməˈtʃʊə / (BrE)
happening or done before the usual or expected time; occurring too soon
too early; before expected time

Examples

The doctor warned that celebrating the results now would be premature.
Many investors made a premature decision to sell their shares when the market dipped.
Her premature departure from the company surprised her coworkers.

Synset Examples

illness led to his premature death
alcohol brought him to an untimely end

Related Terms

  • Synonym: early - occurring before the usual or expected time
  • Antonym: late - occurring after the expected or usual time

Etymology

From Latin praematurus, from prae (before) + maturus (ripe, mature).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00818569-s
Synset POS: s
