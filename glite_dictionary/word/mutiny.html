mutiny

1. NOUN

/ ˈmjuːtəni / (AmE), / ˈmjuːtəni / (BrE)
an open rebellion against the proper authorities, especially by soldiers or sailors against their officers
open rebellion against authority

Examples

The crew staged a mutiny against the ship's captain due to poor conditions.
There was talk of mutiny among the soldiers after several unpopular orders were given.
Harsh discipline sometimes led to mutiny in naval fleets during the 18th century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebellion - an act of violent or open resistance to an established government or ruler
  • Synonym: insurrection - a violent uprising against an authority or government
  • Antonym: obedience - compliance with an order, request, or law; submission to another's authority

Etymology

From Latin *mutinari* (to revolt), from *mutinus* (mutinous).

2. VERB

/ ˈmjuːtəni / (AmE), / ˈmjuːtɪni / (BrE)
(of a group of people, especially soldiers or sailors) to refuse to obey orders from a person in authority, often using violence or open rebellion.
refuse to obey authority; rebel

Examples

The sailors threatened to mutiny if their demands for better conditions were not met.
Fearing harsh punishment, the crew decided not to mutiny against their captain.
When soldiers mutiny, discipline and order in the army quickly collapse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: revolt - to take violent action against authority or government
  • Antonym: to obey - to do what you are told or expected to do by a person in authority

Etymology

From Latin mutinari (to revolt), from mutinus (mutinous).
