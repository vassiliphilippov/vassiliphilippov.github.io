radiator

1. NOUN

/ ˈreɪdiˌeɪt̬ər / (AmE), / ˈreɪdieɪtə / (BrE)
A device consisting of a series of pipes or sections, used to circulate steam or hot water to heat rooms or buildings.
room heater using hot water or steam

Examples

We turned on the radiator to warm up the living room during the cold evening.
The old apartment still had cast-iron radiators under the windows.
Please do not place wet clothes on the radiator to dry them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heater - a device that produces heat, especially for warming a room or building

Etymology

From Latin 'radiare' (to emit rays), referring to emitting heat.

2. NOUN

/ ˈreɪ.diˌeɪ.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈreɪ.dɪˌeɪ.tə / (BrE)
a device, often part of a vehicle or heating system, consisting of a metal structure through which hot fluids are circulated to transfer heat to the surrounding air
device for transferring heat from fluids

Examples

The car overheated because the radiator was leaking coolant.
He replaced the old radiator in his living room with a modern, efficient model.
Steam rises from the radiator as the central heating system warms the house.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heater - device for generating heat

Etymology

From Latin 'radiatus' (having rays), referring to radiating heat.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈreɪ.diˌeɪ.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈreɪ.diˌeɪ.tə / (BrE)
an object or device that emits energy, especially in the form of heat or electromagnetic waves
object that emits energy

Examples

The sun acts as a powerful radiator, sending energy throughout the solar system.
In physics, a blackbody is considered an ideal radiator because it emits energy at all wavelengths.
The heated metal plate serves as a radiator in the experiment, releasing thermal energy into the room.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: absorber - an object that absorbs energy rather than emitting it
  • Synonym: emitter - something that sends out energy, especially light, heat, or radiation

Etymology

From Latin 'radiare' (to emit rays) via 'radiatus' and English '-or'.
