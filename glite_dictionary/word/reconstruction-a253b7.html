reconstruction

1. NOUN

/ ˌriː.kənˈstrʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌriː.kənˈstrʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of building something again after it has been damaged or destroyed
the act of rebuilding

Examples

The city began the reconstruction of the historic bridge after the flood.
After the earthquake, reconstruction efforts focused on rebuilding homes and schools.
The reconstruction of the museum took nearly two years to complete.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restoration - the action of returning something to a former condition

Etymology

From French 'reconstruction', from Latin 're-' (again) + 'construction' (building).

2. NOUN

/ ˌriː.kənˈstrʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌriː.kɒnˈstrʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
an interpretation or account created by assembling fragments of evidence, facts, or remembered details
interpretation assembled from evidence

Examples

The archaeologists gave a detailed reconstruction of the ancient city's layout based on their findings.
His reconstruction of the events relied mainly on eyewitness testimony and a few preserved letters.
Historians debated the accuracy of her reconstruction of medieval court life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recreation - a re-creation or imitation of something, especially a past event

Etymology

From French 'reconstruction', from Latin 're-' (again) + 'constructio' (a building up).

3. NOUN

/ ˌriː.kənˈstrʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌriː.kənˈstrʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
The process of recalling a memory by using stored abstract features to construct the original experience, rather than retrieving it exactly as it was.
memory recall by constructing from features

Examples

Psychologists believe that reconstruction explains why people often remember events differently over time.
Eyewitness testimony is sometimes unreliable due to the reconstruction involved in memory recall.
In cognitive science, reconstruction refers to building a memory from fragments rather than retrieving an exact copy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recall - the act of remembering or recollecting information
  • Antonym: recognition - the identification of something as having been previously seen or known

Etymology

From Latin reconstrūctiō (a rebuilding), from re- (again) + construere (to build).
