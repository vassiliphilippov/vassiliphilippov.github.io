dribble

1. NOUN

/ ˈdrɪb.əl / (AmE), / ˈdrɪb.əl / (BrE)
A small, slow flow of liquid in drops; the action or result of liquid dripping slowly.
small flow or fall of drops of liquid

Examples

A dribble of rainwater ran down the windowpane during the storm.
There was only a dribble of sauce left at the bottom of the bottle.
She wiped the dribble of milk from her baby's chin.

Synset Examples

there's a drip through the roof

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drip - the act or sound of falling drops of liquid

Etymology

Probably from frequentative of 'drib', a dialect word related to 'drip'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07447159-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈdrɪb.əl / (AmE), / ˈdrɪb.əl / (BrE)
to let or cause (a liquid) to fall in small drops; to trickle slowly.
let liquid fall in drops

Examples

The chef began to dribble olive oil over the roasted vegetables.
Tears dribble down her cheek as she listens to the sad news.
You should dribble honey onto the pancakes, not pour it.

Synset Examples

dribble oil into the mixture

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trickle - to flow in a small stream or in drops

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound or movement of drops falling.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01614420-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈdrɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈdrɪbəl / (BrE)
the act of moving a ball forward by repeatedly tapping, bouncing, or kicking it, especially in sports such as soccer or basketball
moving a ball by repeated taps or kicks

Examples

His impressive dribble past three defenders led to a goal.
The coach praised her quick dribble up the court.
A well-timed dribble can break through a tight defense.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: pass - an act of moving the ball to another player
  • Degree Higher: drive - a strong, forceful kick or hit (in sports)
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00479658-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈdrɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈdrɪbəl / (BrE)
saliva flowing lightly or slowly from the mouth, often unintentionally
saliva trickling from the mouth

Examples

The baby had a little dribble on her chin after her nap.
He wiped the dribble from his mouth with a handkerchief.
A small dribble of saliva escaped as he slept in the chair.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drool - let saliva flow from the mouth uncontrollably

Etymology

From Middle English driblen, related to 'drip' and 'drivel'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05424022-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈdrɪb.əl / (AmE), / ˈdrɪb.əl / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to flow or fall slowly in small drops or in a thin, unsteady stream.
to flow slowly in drops or a thin stream

Examples

Tears began to dribble down her cheeks as she listened to the sad news.
Blood dribbled from the wound, staining his shirt.
Information about the new product started to dribble out before the official announcement.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trickle - to flow in a small stream or in drops
  • Antonym: gush - to flow out quickly and in large amounts

Etymology

Perhaps an alteration of 'drivel', from Old English 'drēflian' (to slaver, make foul).
