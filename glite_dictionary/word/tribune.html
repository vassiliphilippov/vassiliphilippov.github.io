tribune

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪb.jun / (AmE), / ˈtrɪb.juːn / (BrE)
the semicircular area (apse) in a Christian church where the bishop's throne is situated
apse with bishop's throne in a church

Examples

The bishop delivered his sermon from the tribune at the far end of the cathedral.
Restorers carefully preserved the mosaic decorations above the tribune.
The tribune is considered the spiritual heart of the basilica.

Etymology

From Latin *tribuna*, meaning a raised platform or dais.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪˌbjuːn / (AmE), / ˈtrɪ.bjuːn / (BrE)
In ancient Rome, an official elected by the plebeians to protect their interests and rights.
Roman official elected by plebeians

Examples

The tribune spoke passionately on behalf of the plebeians in the assembly.
As a tribune, he had the power to veto decisions harmful to the common people.
The Roman Senate often clashed with the tribunes over policy matters.

Etymology

From Latin 'tribunus' (head of a tribe), from 'tribus' (tribe).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪb.juːn / (AmE), / ˈtrɪb.juːn / (BrE)
a person who defends, speaks on behalf of, or advocates for the rights and interests of others, often in a political or social context
advocate for others' rights

Examples

He was seen as a tribune for the working class, tirelessly fighting for their interests.
As a tribune for women's rights, she led several successful campaigns for gender equality.
Throughout his career, he acted as a tribune for the marginalized, ensuring their voices were heard in parliament.

Synset Examples

He served as a tribune for the people's rights.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advocate - a person who publicly supports or recommends a particular cause or policy

Etymology

From Latin tribunus, the title of a magistrate in ancient Rome representing the people.
