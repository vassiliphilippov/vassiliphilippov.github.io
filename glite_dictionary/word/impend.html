to impend

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɛnd / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɛnd / (BrE)
(of an event or situation) to be about to happen, especially in a way that causes worry or suspense.
to be imminent or about to occur

Examples

A sense of doom seemed to impend over the entire community.
As the dark clouds gathered, a storm appeared to impend.
Economic crisis may impend if the current policies continue.

Synset Examples

Changes are impending

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loom - to appear as a large or threatening event about to happen
  • Synonym: approach - to draw near in time or space
  • Antonym: to recede - to move back or withdraw, especially from an imminent state

Etymology

From Latin 'impendēre', meaning 'to hang over'.

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɛnd / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɛnd / (BrE)
to be about to happen or likely to occur very soon; to be imminent
to be about to happen; to be imminent

Examples

Dark clouds suggest that a storm may impend over the town tonight.
A sense of disaster seemed to impend as the stock market began to tumble.
They cancelled the trip because danger appeared to impend on the mountain roads.

Synset Examples

Change is about to impend in the organization.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: approach - to come near in time or space
  • Synonym: to threaten - to be likely to happen soon (of something bad)
  • Antonym: to recede - to move further away; to become more distant (opposite in temporal sense)

Etymology

From Latin 'impendere', meaning 'to hang over'.
