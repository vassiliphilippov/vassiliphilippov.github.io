to destabilize

1. VERB

/ diˈsteɪbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diˈsteɪbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to become unstable or to make something become less stable or secure
become or make unstable

Examples

There are fears that the political changes could destabilize the entire region.
After the withdrawal of foreign investment, the currency quickly began to destabilize.
Sudden leadership changes may destabilize the organization during a crucial period.

Synset Examples

The economy destabilized rapidly

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to stabilize - make or become stable

Etymology

From de- (removal, reversal) + stabilize.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00272976-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ diˈsteɪbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diˈsteɪbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make something less stable or likely to fail or collapse
make unstable; undermine stability

Examples

The economic crisis threatened to destabilize the entire region.
Misinformation can destabilize public trust in institutions.
Their efforts to reform the system did not destabilize the government as critics feared.

Synset Examples

Terrorism destabilized the government

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to stabilize - make stable or more stable

Etymology

From de- (removal) + stabilize, formed in English in the mid-20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00272510-v
Synset POS: v
