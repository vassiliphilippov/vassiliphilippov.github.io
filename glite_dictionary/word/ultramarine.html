ultramarine

1. ADJ

/ ˌʌltrəˈməˌrin / (AmE), / ˌʌltrəˈmɑːriːn / (BrE)
having a brilliant pure blue to purplish blue color
brilliant blue or purplish-blue color

Examples

The artist selected an ultramarine background that made the other colors stand out vividly.
Her dress shimmered in an ultramarine shade that caught everyone's attention at the party.
They painted the door in a striking ultramarine to give the house a Mediterranean feel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: azure - bright blue in color, like a clear sky
  • Synonym: cobalt blue - a bright blue pigment

Etymology

From Latin 'ultramarinus' (beyond the sea), referring to the lapis lazuli imported from Asia for the pigment.

2. NOUN

/ ˌʌl.trə.məˈrin / (AmE), / ˌʌl.trə.məˈriːn / (BrE)
a brilliant blue pigment originally made by grinding lapis lazuli into powder
brilliant blue pigment

Examples

The artist chose ultramarine to capture the vivid blue of the sky in her painting.
In the Renaissance, ultramarine was more valuable than gold due to its rarity.
He mixed ultramarine with white to create a softer shade for the background.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cobalt blue - a bright blue pigment made from cobalt compounds
  • Antonym: vermilion - a bright red pigment made from mercury sulfide

Etymology

From Latin 'ultramarinus' (beyond the sea), as lapis lazuli was imported to Europe from Asia.

3. NOUN

/ ˌʌltrəməˈrin / (AmE), / ˌʌltrəməˈriːn / (BrE)
a vivid blue or purple-blue color
vivid blue color

Examples

The artist chose ultramarine for the sky in her painting.
Her dress was a striking shade of ultramarine, vibrant and bold.
They mixed white with ultramarine to achieve a lighter blue hue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cobalt blue - a bright blue pigment or color
  • Degree Lower: cerulean - a deep sky-blue color
  • Degree Higher: navy blue - a very dark shade of blue

Etymology

From Medieval Latin ultramarinus, meaning 'beyond the sea', originally referring to lapis lazuli imported from Asia.
