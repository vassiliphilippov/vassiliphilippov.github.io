to pulsate

1. VERB

/ ˈpʌlˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈpʌlseɪt / (BrE)
to expand and contract with a steady rhythm, or to beat in a regular, rhythmic way
expand and contract rhythmically

Examples

The crowd felt the music pulsate through the floor during the concert.
Bright lights pulsated above the dance floor, creating an electric atmosphere.
His temples pulsated with pain after the stressful meeting.

Synset Examples

The baby's heart was pulsating again after the surgeon massaged it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - to beat or sound with a strong, regular rhythm
  • Degree Lower: flutter - to move or beat rapidly and lightly

Etymology

From Latin pulsatus, past participle of pulsare, meaning ‘to beat, strike repeatedly’.

2. VERB

/ ˈpʌlˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈpʌlseɪt / (BrE)
(of a signal or apparatus) to produce, transmit, or modulate in a series of short bursts or pulses, especially of electromagnetic waves.
produce or transmit pulses (esp. waves)

Examples

The radio transmitter can pulsate signals at regular intervals.
Modern radars pulsate electromagnetic waves to detect objects at long ranges.
Engineers designed the system to pulsate bursts of energy rather than emit a continuous signal.

Synset Examples

pulse waves
a transmitter pulsed by an electronic tube

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - to emit or modulate in pulses

Etymology

From Latin 'pulsatus', past participle of 'pulsare' (to strike repeatedly).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01626064-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈpʌlˌseɪt / (AmE), / ˈpʌlseɪt / (BrE)
to move, beat, or flow with a steady, regular rhythm or movement
move or beat in a regular rhythm

Examples

The lights in the club pulsated to the rhythm of the music.
Her heart began to pulsate rapidly as she waited for the results.
The city streets pulsate with energy during the annual festival.

Synset Examples

the city pulsated with music and excitement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - to beat or sound with a strong, regular rhythm
  • Synonym: quiver - to tremble or shake with a slight rapid motion

Etymology

From Latin pulsatus, past participle of pulsare (to beat, strike), from pellere (to drive, push).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01883572-v
Synset POS: v
