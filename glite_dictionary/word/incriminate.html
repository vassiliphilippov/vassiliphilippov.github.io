to incriminate

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to make someone appear guilty of a crime or wrongdoing; to strongly suggest guilt
make someone appear guilty

Examples

The evidence found at the scene seemed to incriminate the suspect.
He refused to answer the question, fearing it might incriminate him.
Her testimony could potentially incriminate her colleagues in the scheme.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accuse - to say that someone has done something wrong or illegal
  • Antonym: to exonerate - to show or state that someone is not guilty

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *incriminatus*, past participle of *incriminare* (to accuse of a crime), from Latin *in-* (in) + *crimen* (crime).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to make someone appear guilty of a crime or wrongdoing; to accuse or present evidence against someone
make appear guilty; accuse of a crime

Examples

The evidence could easily incriminate an innocent person if misinterpreted.
She refused to answer the question, fearing it might incriminate her.
His statement seemed designed to incriminate his business partner.

Synset Examples

The neighbors accused the man of spousal abuse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accuse - to charge someone with an offence or crime
  • Antonym: to exonerate - to officially state that someone is not guilty

Etymology

From Medieval Latin incriminatus, past participle of incriminare, from Latin in- (into) + crimen (crime).
