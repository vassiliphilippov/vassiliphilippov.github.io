Mohican

1. NOUN

/ moʊˈhiːkən / (AmE), / məʊˈhiːkən / (BrE)
the now-extinct Algonquian language formerly spoken by the Mohican people of the northeastern United States
the Algonquian language of the Mohican

Examples

Few written records remain of the Mohican language, which was spoken along the Hudson River.
Scholars have tried to reconstruct the vocabulary of Mohican using historical documents.
The last fluent speakers of Mohican died in the early twentieth century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Algonquian - a family of Native American languages spoken in North America

Etymology

From the name Mohican, derived from Mahican, the self-designation of the people.

2. NOUN

/ moʊˈhiːkən / (AmE), / məʊˈhiːkən / (BrE)
a member of an Algonquian-speaking Native American people formerly living in the Hudson River valley and eastward to the Housatonic River
member of a Native American people of the Hudson valley

Examples

The Mohican tribe once occupied vast areas along the Hudson River.
He traced his ancestry back to a Mohican woman born in the 18th century.
Many Mohican descendants now live in Wisconsin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Mohawk - A member of another Native American people of the northeastern US

Etymology

From the Mahican people's name for themselves, Mohikaneuw (meaning 'people of the waters that are never still')
