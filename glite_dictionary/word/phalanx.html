phalanx

1. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪlæŋks / (AmE), / ˈfælæŋks / (BrE)
A body of soldiers standing or moving closely together in a rectangular formation; especially used in ancient Greek warfare.
close military formation

Examples

The ancient Greek army famously fought in a phalanx to defend their territory.
As the enemy advanced, the soldiers formed a tight phalanx with their shields locked together.
Historians admire the discipline required to maintain a phalanx during battle.

Etymology

From Greek phalanx (variant phalang-, phalangos) meaning 'line of battle, battle array.'

2. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪ.læŋks / (AmE), / ˈfæl.æŋks / (BrE)
a large group of people or things closely packed together and acting as a unified force
closely packed group; unified crowd

Examples

A phalanx of reporters waited outside the courthouse for a statement.
The protesters advanced in a solid phalanx towards the city center.
Security guards formed a phalanx at the entrance to control the crowd.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throng - a large, densely packed crowd of people
  • Synonym: mob - a large, disorderly crowd of people

Etymology

From Greek phalanx (line of battle, formation of soldiers).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪ.læŋks / (AmE), / ˈfæl.æŋks / (BrE)
Any of the bones in the fingers or toes of the hands and feet.
bone in a finger or toe
medical

Examples

The surgeon carefully examined the broken phalanx in his patient's finger.
Humans have three phalanx bones in each finger, except for the thumb, which has two.
An X-ray revealed that the athlete had fractured a phalanx in his big toe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: phalanges - Plural form; bones of the fingers or toes

Etymology

From Greek 'phalanx' (log, line of battle, bone of the finger or toe).
