to animalise

1. VERB

/ ˈænɪməˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈænɪməlaɪz / (BrE)
to represent or depict something in the form of an animal
represent as an animal

Examples

The ancient Egyptians would often animalise their gods by giving them animal heads.
Some artists animalise human figures in their paintings to convey symbolic meanings.
The cartoonist skillfully animalised the famous politician in his latest illustration.

Etymology

From Latin animalis (animal) with the English suffix -ise/-ize.

2. VERB

/ ˈænɪməˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈænɪməlaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something brutal, unfeeling, or inhuman; to deprive of civility or refinement
make brutal or inhuman

Examples

The harsh conditions of war can animalise even the most gentle people.
Some believe that violence in society can animalise its members over time.
He feared that the relentless cruelty of the system would animalise his spirit.

Synset Examples

Life in the camps had brutalized him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to brutalize - make someone cruel, violent, or insensitive
  • Antonym: to civilize - bring out of a savage or unrefined state

Etymology

From Latin animalis (having breath), with English suffix -ise.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00113860-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈæn.ɪ.məˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈæn.ɪ.mə.laɪz / (BrE)
(of a person or their behavior) to become brutal, insensitive, or lacking in human feeling.
become brutal or insensitive

Examples

He began to animalise after years of hardship and neglect.
The cruel conditions inside the prison animalized many of its inmates.
War can animalise even the most compassionate individuals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to brutalize - to make or become cruel, harsh, or insensitive

Etymology

From Latin 'animalis' (animal) + English suffix '-ize'.

Synset POS: v
