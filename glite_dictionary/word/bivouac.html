bivouac

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɪvuˌæk / (AmE), / ˈbɪvuæk / (BrE)
A place where people, especially those traveling or camping, can set up a temporary shelter such as a tent for the night.
temporary camping site

Examples

We found a perfect bivouac near the lake for our overnight stay.
After a long hike, the group set up their bivouac in a clearing in the woods.
The weather was kind, making the bivouac a pleasant and memorable experience.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: campsite - a place used for camping
  • Synonym: campground - an area where tents or campers can be set up

Etymology

From French 'bivouac', originally from Swiss German 'Biwuache' (night guard).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɪvuˌæk / (AmE), / ˈbɪv.u.æk / (BrE)
A temporary camp without tents or cover, used especially by soldiers or mountaineers.
temporary military or outdoor camp

Examples

The soldiers set up a bivouac in the forest before nightfall.
After a long day's climb, the team established a bivouac on the mountain slope.
The refugees constructed a makeshift bivouac using tarpaulins and sticks.

Synset Examples

wherever he went in the camp the men were grumbling

Related Terms

  • Synonym: camp - temporary lodging, especially for soldiers or outdoors

Etymology

From French 'bivouac', likely from Swiss German 'biwacht' (night guard).

3. VERB

/ ˈbɪv.u.æk / (AmE), / ˈbɪv.wæk / (BrE)
to stay or camp temporarily in a simple shelter, especially outdoors; to live as if in a tent, often without permanent facilities
camp or stay in a temporary shelter

Examples

After the long hike, we decided to bivouac under the stars for the night.
During the expedition, the team bivouacked in a makeshift shelter at the base of the mountain.
They often bivouac in the forest when conducting field research.

Synset Examples

Can we go camping again this summer?
The circus tented near the town
The houseguests had to camp in the living room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: camp - to stay in a tent or temporary shelter outdoors

Etymology

From French 'bivouac', originally from Swiss German 'biwacht' (night watch), later referring to temporary encampments.
