yaw

1. NOUN

/ jɑ / (AmE), / jɔː / (BrE)
an erratic or side-to-side movement away from the intended course, especially used in navigation and aviation
sideways deviation from course

Examples

The pilot corrected the plane's yaw after encountering strong crosswinds.
During the storm, the ship experienced a dangerous yaw to starboard.
The missile's sudden yaw caused it to miss the target.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction suddenly

Etymology

From Old Norse jaga (to show defiance).

2. VERB

/ jɑ / (AmE), / jɔː / (BrE)
(of a moving vehicle, especially a ship or aircraft) to swerve off its intended course, especially momentarily.
swerve off course (vehicles, especially ships or planes)

Examples

As the strong gust hit the plane, it began to yaw to the left.
The damaged rudder caused the fishing boat to yaw dangerously in heavy seas.
If not corrected swiftly, a sudden yaw can lead to loss of control.

Synset Examples

the ship yawed when the huge waves hit it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swerve - to change or cause to change direction abruptly

Etymology

Probably of Low German or Dutch origin, related to Dutch 'jagen' (to chase, to drive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02037849-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ jɑ / (AmE), / jɔː / (BrE)
(of a ship, aircraft, or vehicle) to swerve or deviate temporarily from a straight course, especially erratically.
to deviate from a set course

Examples

The ship began to yaw as the storm grew stronger.
The pilot noticed that the plane would yaw slightly during turbulence.
Improper cargo loading can cause a truck to yaw on sharp turns.

Synset Examples

the yawing motion of the ship

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swerve - to change direction suddenly, especially to avoid something
  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction suddenly; to turn aside from a course or direction

Etymology

Probably from Low German *jagen* (to chase, hunt); use in navigation dates from the late 16th century.
