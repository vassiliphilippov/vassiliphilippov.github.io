gut

1. VERB

/ ɡʌt / (AmE), / ɡʌt / (BrE)
to remove the internal parts of something completely, especially the inside of a building or an animal
remove internal parts completely

Examples

The company plans to gut the old warehouse and turn it into apartments.
Renovators had to gut the house before rebuilding the interior.
Firefighters managed to save the walls, but the fire completely gutted the inside of the building.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to demolish - to destroy a building or structure

Etymology

From Old English guttas (plural), related to Latin gutta (drop), originally referring to intestines.

2. NOUN

/ ɡʌt / (AmE), / ɡʌt / (BrE)
a strong, thin cord made from the intestines of sheep or other animals, used especially in surgery for sutures.
cord from animal intestines used in surgery
medical

Examples

The surgeon selected a length of gut to use for the internal stitches.
Early tennis racquets were strung with natural gut from sheep or cows.
Synthetic alternatives have largely replaced gut sutures in modern medical procedures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: catgut - a type of cord made from animal intestines, used for stringing musical instruments and in surgery

Etymology

From Old English guttas (plural), related to Old Norse guth (bowels, entrails).

5. NOUN

/ ɡʌt / (AmE), / ɡʌt / (BrE)
the part of the digestive tract between the stomach and the anus, especially the intestines
part of digestive tract; intestines

Examples

During the operation, the surgeon carefully examined the patient's gut for any signs of disease.
Some animals have a very long gut to help digest tough plant materials.
Scientists study the bacteria in the human gut to understand how they affect health.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intestine - the long tube in the body that helps digest food and absorb nutrients

Etymology

From Old English 'guttas' (bowels, entrails), related to Middle Dutch 'gut' (bowel).
