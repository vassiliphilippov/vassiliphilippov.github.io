heuristic

1. ADJ

/ hjuˈrɪstɪk / (AmE), / hjʊəˈrɪstɪk / (BrE)
(of a method or process) enabling or serving to discover or learn something for oneself, especially by allowing for intuitive judgment or trial and error rather than following strict rules.
enabling discovery; guiding investigation

Examples

A heuristic approach can sometimes find solutions faster than strict algorithms in complex problems.
He used a heuristic method to generate new ideas instead of following a set process.
Medical diagnosis often involves heuristic reasoning based on experience and observation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: algorithmic - following fixed rules or procedures, opposite of heuristic

Etymology

From Greek heuriskein (to find, discover).

WordNet ID: oewn-00093786-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ hjuˈrɪstɪk / (AmE), / hjʊəˈrɪstɪk / (BrE)
a rule or method for problem-solving, learning, or discovery that employs practical, experience-based techniques rather than guaranteed logical procedures
problem-solving rule or method

Examples

The programmer used a heuristic to speed up the search process.
Doctors often apply heuristics when diagnosing patients with complex symptoms.
A simple heuristic can help you choose the shortest route to your destination.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: algorithm - a step-by-step procedure for calculations or problem-solving
  • Synonym: rule of thumb - a broadly accurate principle based on experience or practice

Etymology

From Greek heuriskein (to find, discover).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05856483-n
Synset POS: n
