broadside

1. NOUN

/ ˈbrɔdˌsaɪd / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːd.saɪd / (BrE)
the simultaneous firing of all the guns on one side of a warship
naval: all guns fired on one side

Examples

The old battleship fired a broadside that shook the enemy fleet.
He admired the skill required to coordinate a perfect broadside attack.
During the battle, both ships exchanged broadsides at close range.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: salvo - a simultaneous discharge of artillery or firearms

Etymology

From broad (wide) + side, originally referring to the side of a ship.

8. NOUN

/ ˈbrɔdˌsaɪd / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdˌsaɪd / (BrE)
a strongly worded or vehement verbal attack; a speech or piece of writing that criticizes someone or something harshly and publicly
a forceful verbal or written attack

Examples

The senator delivered a broadside against the administration's foreign policy.
The article launched a fierce broadside at the company's business practices.
He ended his speech with a sudden broadside aimed at his opponents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tirade - a long, angry speech of criticism or accusation
  • Synonym: invective - insulting, abusive, or highly critical language
  • Antonym: panegyric - a public speech or published text in praise of someone or something

Etymology

Originally a nautical term from 'broad side' (the side of a ship where guns are mounted); figurative use for attacks dates from the 18th century.
