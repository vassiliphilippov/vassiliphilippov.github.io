imitative

1. ADJ

/ ˈɪm.əˌteɪ.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈɪm.ɪ.tə.tɪv / (BrE)
copying or tending to copy someone or something; marked by imitation.
tending to imitate or copy

Examples

Children are naturally imitative, often copying the behavior of adults around them.
Her paintings are highly imitative of famous Impressionist artists.
The dancers performed in an imitative style, echoing traditional folk movements.

Synset Examples

acting is an imitative art
man is an imitative being

Related Terms

  • Antonym: original - not derived from something else; fresh and inventive
  • Synonym: mimetic - relating to, characterized by, or exhibiting imitation or mimicry

Etymology

From Latin imitativus (pertaining to imitation), from imitari (to imitate).

2. ADJ

/ ˈɪmɪˌteɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈɪmɪtətɪv / (BrE)
(of words) formed by imitating or reproducing natural sounds, typically through onomatopoeia.
(of words) formed by imitating sounds

Examples

Some animal names, like imitative words such as 'cuckoo', closely reflect natural sounds.
Linguists note that imitative terms often arise independently in multiple languages.
Many comic books use imitative sound effects like 'bang' or 'buzz' to convey action.

Synset Examples

onomatopoeic words are imitative of noises
it was independently developed in more than one place as an onomatopoetic term

Related Terms

  • Synonym: onomatopoeic - (of words) formed to imitate natural sounds

Etymology

From Latin imitativus, from imitari (to imitate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01220250-a
Synset POS: a
