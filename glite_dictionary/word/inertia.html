inertia

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈnɜrʃə / (AmE), / ɪˈnɜːʃə / (BrE)
(physics) The tendency of an object to remain at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
resistance to change in motion (physics)

Examples

Because of inertia, a stationary object will stay still unless something pushes or pulls it.
Once the car started moving, inertia kept it rolling even after the driver released the gas pedal.
The concept of inertia explains why passengers are thrown forward when a vehicle stops suddenly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: momentum - the quantity of motion an object has, related to its mass and velocity

Etymology

From Latin inertIA (inactivity), from iners (idle, inactive).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈɜrʃə / (AmE), / ɪnˈɜːtɪə / (BrE)
a tendency to remain inactive or unchanged; a lack of willingness or ability to move or change
tendency to remain inactive or unchanged

Examples

After the long holiday, his inertia made it hard to start working again.
The committee's inertia prevented any real progress on the issue.
She finally overcame her inertia and began exercising regularly.

Synset Examples

he had to overcome his inertia and get back to work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: apathy - lack of interest, enthusiasm, or concern
  • Antonym: activity - the state of being active; doing something

Etymology

From Latin 'iners' (inactive, idle) + '-ia' (suffix forming nouns).
