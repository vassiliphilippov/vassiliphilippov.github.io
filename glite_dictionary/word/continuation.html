continuation

1. NOUN

/ kənˌtɪn.juˈeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˌtɪn.juˈeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(Psychology) The tendency to perceive a visual line or pattern as continuing in its original direction, even when interrupted.
psychological principle of visual continuity

Examples

According to the principle of continuation, we naturally follow lines and curves in a picture.
Designers use continuation to guide the viewer’s eye through a composition.
The Gestalt law of continuation explains why we perceive intersecting lines as two continuous paths.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: closure - the Gestalt principle of perceiving incomplete figures as complete

Etymology

From Latin continuatio (a continuing, continuation), from continuare (to continue).

3. NOUN

/ kənˌtɪn.juˈeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / kənˌtɪn.juˈeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the act or result of something being made to last longer or proceed without stopping
act or result of prolonging something

Examples

The continuation of the talks was agreed upon by all parties after a short break.
She was grateful for the continuation of her scholarship for another year.
Unforeseen events forced the continuation of the project beyond its original deadline.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extension - the action of making something longer or larger
  • Antonym: interruption - a break in continuity or duration

Etymology

From Latin continuatio (a continuing), from continuare (to make continuous).
