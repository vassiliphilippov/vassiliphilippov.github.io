workhouse

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɜrkˌhaʊs / (AmE), / ˈwɜːkˌhaʊs / (BrE)
a county jail or institution where prisoners are held for short periods, historically also used for confining the poor or those convicted of minor offenses
county jail or institution for short-term prisoners
archaic legal

Examples

The judge sentenced him to six months in the workhouse for petty theft.
Many 19th-century towns maintained a workhouse for the poor and the convicted.
After his arrest, he spent several weeks in the local workhouse.

Etymology

From Middle English 'werkhous', formed from 'work' + 'house'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɝːkˌhaʊs / (AmE), / ˈwɜːkˌhaʊs / (BrE)
(historical) A place where those unable to support themselves were provided with accommodation and made to work, particularly in Britain.
historical poorhouse where inmates work
archaic british

Examples

Hundreds of the city's destitute ended up in the workhouse during the harsh winter.
The novel describes the grim conditions of children living in a Victorian workhouse.
After losing his job, he feared he would be sent to the workhouse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poorhouse - a building where poor people receive board and lodging in return for work

Etymology

From Middle English 'werkhous' (house for work), from 'work' + 'house'.
