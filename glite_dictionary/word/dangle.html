to dangle

1. VERB

/ ˈdæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈdaŋɡl̩ / (BrE)
to hang or swing loosely, especially so as to be able to move freely
hang or swing loosely

Examples

Colorful paper lanterns dangled from the ceiling, casting a festive glow on the room.
She let her feet dangle over the edge of the dock while she watched the sunset.
A single earring dangled from her left ear, catching the light as she turned.

Synset Examples

the ornaments dangled from the tree
The light dropped from the ceiling

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hang - be suspended or hold something so as to allow free movement

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *dangel*, of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02723347-v

2. VERB

/ ˈdæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈdæŋɡl̩ / (BrE)
to cause to hang or swing loosely, usually so as to move freely in the air
cause to hang loosely

Examples

She dangled her keys above the dog's head to get its attention.
The fisherman dangled a worm from his line over the water.
He dangled the puppet in front of the children, making them laugh.

Synset Examples

He dangled the ornaments from the Christmas tree

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hang - to suspend or be suspended by attachment from above

Etymology

Of uncertain origin; possibly related to Swedish *dängla* (to dangle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01484915-v
Synset POS: v
