to overdress

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈdrɛs / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdrɛs / (BrE)
to put on more clothing than necessary, especially so as to be too warm
wear too many clothes; dress too warmly

Examples

Children often overdress in winter and end up feeling too hot indoors.
I tend to overdress for autumn hikes, then have to remove layers as I warm up.
If you overdress your baby, she might become uncomfortable and fussy.

Synset Examples

You should not overclothe the child — she will be too hot

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underdress - to wear too little clothing for the conditions

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'over-' meaning 'excessive' and 'dress'.

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈdrɛs / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdrɛs / (BrE)
to put on clothes that are too formal, elaborate, or showy for a particular occasion
wear excessively formal or elaborate clothes

Examples

He tends to overdress for casual gatherings, making everyone else feel underdressed.
Try not to overdress for the interview; business casual is appropriate.
She realized she had overdressed when she saw everyone else in jeans and T-shirts.

Synset Examples

She never dresses up, even when she goes to the opera
The young girls were all fancied up for the party

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dress up - to put on formal or smart clothes
  • Antonym: to underdress - to wear clothes that are too casual or not formal enough for an occasion

Etymology

From 'over-' (excessively) + 'dress' (to clothe).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00043880-v
Synset POS: v
