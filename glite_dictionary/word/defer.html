to defer

1. VERB

/ dɪˈfɜr / (AmE), / dɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to yield to someone else's wishes, judgment, or opinion, especially out of respect or recognition of their authority
yield to someone else's wishes or opinion

Examples

Sometimes it is wise to defer to experts when making complicated decisions.
He chose to defer to his manager's judgment on the issue.
She rarely defers to other people's opinions in meetings.

Synset Examples

The government bowed to the military pressure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to acquiesce - to accept or comply reluctantly but without protest
  • Antonym: to resist - to withstand, oppose, or refuse to comply

Etymology

From Latin 'deferre' (to bring down, submit), from 'de-' (down) + 'ferre' (to carry).

2. VERB

/ dəˈfɝː / (AmE), / dɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to postpone something or delay it until a later time
postpone to a later time

Examples

The committee decided to defer the meeting until next week.
We had to defer payment because of unforeseen expenses.
Due to heavy rain, the match was deferred to the following day.

Synset Examples

let's postpone the exam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to postpone - to arrange for something to take place at a later time
  • Antonym: advance - to move forward in time or make something happen sooner

Etymology

From Latin 'differre', meaning 'to postpone, delay'.
