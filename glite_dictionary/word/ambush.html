ambush

1. NOUN

/ ˈæm.bʊʃ / (AmE), / ˈæm.bʊʃ / (BrE)
the act of hiding and waiting in order to attack someone by surprise
a surprise attack from hiding

Examples

The soldiers walked into an ambush set by the enemy near the river.
Police officers fell victim to an ambush while responding to a routine call.
He narrowly escaped an ambush thanks to a warning from a local guide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - an aggressive and violent action against a person or place
  • Synonym: assault - a sudden, violent attack

Etymology

From Old French *embusche* (an ambush), from *embuschier* (to lie in wait), ultimately from Medieval Latin *imboscare* (to put in a wood).

2. VERB

/ ˈæm.bʊʃ / (AmE), / ˈam.bʊʃ / (BrE)
to hunt a person or animal by lying in wait and launching a surprise attack
to hunt by lying in wait and attacking

Examples

The jaguar would ambush its prey by silently waiting in the dense undergrowth.
Poachers ambush animals near the watering hole, making it dangerous for wildlife.
Predators often ambush unsuspecting birds that come too close to the ground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to waylay - to stop or attack unexpectedly as someone passes by
  • Synonym: attack - to take aggressive action against someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'embusche' (act of lying in wait), from 'embusquer' (to place in a bush or wood).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈæmˌbʊʃ / (AmE), / ˈæm.bʊʃ / (BrE)
to make a surprise attack on someone or something from a hidden position
make a surprise attack from hiding

Examples

The soldiers planned to ambush the enemy convoy at dawn.
Rebels often ambush patrols passing through this region.
The police managed to ambush the suspects as they left the building.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - to take aggressive action against
  • Antonym: to defend - to protect from attack

Etymology

From French 'embuscher' (to lie in wait), from Old French 'en-' (in) + 'busche' (wood).
