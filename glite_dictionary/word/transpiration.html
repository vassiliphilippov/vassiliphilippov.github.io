transpiration

1. NOUN

/ ˌtrænspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the movement of gases through narrow tubes or pores because of differences in pressure or temperature
movement of gases through tubes due to pressure or temperature

Examples

In laboratory apparatus, transpiration can occur when gases move through capillary tubes.
Changes in temperature can significantly affect the rate of transpiration in porous materials.
Engineers must account for transpiration when designing systems that involve the flow of gases.

2. NOUN

/ ˌtrænspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which water vapor is given off through the skin, mucous membranes, or the surface of leaves
release of water vapor through skin or leaves

Examples

Plants regulate water loss through the process of transpiration.
During exercise, transpiration can help cool the human body.
Humidity affects the rate of transpiration in both plants and animals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: perspiration - the process of sweating; excretion of moisture by the skin

Etymology

From French 'transpiration' or Latin 'transpirare' (to breathe through).

3. NOUN

/ ˌtrænspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which water vapor is released from the leaves of plants into the atmosphere
release of water vapor from plant leaves

Examples

During the summer, transpiration can cause significant water loss in plants.
Humidity levels often rise due to the transpiration of forests.
The process of transpiration is essential for moving nutrients from the soil to plant leaves.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evaporation - the process of turning from liquid into vapor

Etymology

From French transpiration, from Latin transpiratio, from transpirare (to breathe through).
