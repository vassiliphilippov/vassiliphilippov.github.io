Dunkirk

1. PROPN

/ ˌdʌnˈkɜrk / (AmE), / ˌdʌnˈkɜːk / (BrE)
A seaport in northern France on the North Sea, known as the site of the evacuation of British forces in 1940 during World War II.
French seaport; site of WWII evacuation

Examples

Thousands of soldiers waited at Dunkirk to be rescued during the historic evacuation in 1940.
Many families in Britain had relatives who were evacuated from Dunkirk.
The beach at Dunkirk remains a symbol of hope and resilience for many.

Etymology

From French 'Dunkerque', meaning 'church of the dunes'.

2. PROPN

/ ˈdʌnˌkɜrk / (AmE), / ˈdʌn.kɜːk / (BrE)
the evacuation of Allied troops from the beaches of Dunkirk, France, during World War II in 1940, conducted under enemy attack
WWII Allied evacuation from Dunkirk, France (1940)

Examples

The evacuation at Dunkirk was a turning point in World War II history.
Many soldiers credited their survival to the massive rescue operation at Dunkirk.
The film vividly recreates the intensity of the Dunkirk evacuation.

Etymology

Named after Dunkirk (French: Dunkerque), the port city in northern France where the evacuation took place.

3. NOUN

/ ˈdʌnˌkɜrk / (AmE), / ˈdʌn.kɜːk / (BrE)
a situation or event in which a desperate, determined effort is made as the only alternative to defeat or disaster
a desperate last-ditch effort to avoid defeat

Examples

The team staged a Dunkirk when they fought back in the final minutes of the game.
After months of setbacks, the company had to pull a Dunkirk to avoid bankruptcy.
Facing overwhelming odds, the activists launched a Dunkirk to stop the demolition of the historic building.

Synset Examples

the Russians had to pull off a Dunkirk to get out of there

Related Terms

  • Synonym: last-ditch effort - a final attempt to achieve something when everything else has failed

Etymology

From the evacuation of Dunkirk (Dunkerque), France, in 1940, when Allied forces were rescued in a desperate operation during World War II.
