analogue

1. ADJ

/ ˈænəˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈænəlɒɡ / (BrE)
(of a circuit or device) having an output that varies continuously and is proportional to the input signal, rather than being digital or discrete.
output varies continuously with input (not digital)

Examples

This old analogue synthesizer produces a warmer sound than most digital ones.
Many radio stations still broadcast in analogue signals in some regions.
The analogue output provides a continuous voltage that tracks the sensor's input.

Synset Examples

analogue device
linear amplifier

Related Terms

  • Antonym: digital - relating to a system that uses discrete values, often binary, instead of continuous variation

Etymology

From French 'analogue' (analogous), from Greek 'analogos' (proportionate).

2. NOUN

/ ˈænəˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈænəlɒg / (BrE)
a thing that is similar to or can be compared with something else in certain respects, typically in a way that makes clearer the nature of the things compared
thing similar to another

Examples

In biology, a bat's wing is an analogue of a bird's wing, serving a similar function.
Some people view the electric car as an analogue to earlier technological breakthroughs.
The exhibition compared ancient maps with their modern analogues to show advancements in cartography.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterpart - a person or thing that corresponds to another in a different place or situation
  • Synonym: equivalent - something that is equal to or interchangeable with something else
  • Antonym: opposite - a person or thing that is totally different from or the reverse of someone or something else

Etymology

From French 'analogue', from Latin 'analogus', from Greek 'analogos' (proportionate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04753670-n
Synset POS: n
