sixfold

1. ADV

/ ˈsɪksˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈsɪksfəʊld / (BrE)
by six times as much; by a factor of six.
by a factor of six

Examples

The population grew sixfold within a decade due to the mining boom.
Profits increased sixfold after introducing the new product line.
Her workload expanded sixfold when her company merged with a rival.

Synset Examples

the population of this town increased sixfold when gold was found in the surrounding hills

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: threefold - by three times as much; by a factor of three
  • Degree Higher: tenfold - by ten times as much; by a factor of ten

Etymology

From Middle English, from six + fold ('times, multiplied by').

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɪksˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈsɪksfəʊld / (BrE)
Having six parts, elements, or times as much as something else.
having six parts or times as many

Examples

Profits increased sixfold during the company's best year.
The new system consists of a sixfold array of sensors.
There was a sixfold rise in the number of applications this month.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: sevenfold - having seven parts or times as much
  • Degree Lower: fivefold - having five parts or times as much

Etymology

Formed from the number 'six' and the suffix '-fold' (meaning 'times as much'), which comes from Old English 'feald'.

3. ADJ

/ ˈsɪksˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈsɪksfəʊld / (BrE)
Having six parts, units, or elements; multiplied by six.
consisting of six parts or units

Examples

The company reported a sixfold increase in profits this year.
His responsibilities became sixfold after the department was reorganized.
The risk of infection was sixfold higher in the untreated group.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: threefold - having three parts or units
  • Degree Higher: twelvefold - having twelve parts or units

Etymology

Formed from 'six' + 'fold,' where 'fold' means 'times as many.'
