projectile

1. NOUN

/ prəˈdʒɛktʃəl / (AmE), / prəˈdʒɛkt.aɪl / (BrE)
an object, especially a weapon, that is fired, thrown, or otherwise propelled towards a target, but is not self-propelled
object fired or thrown at a target

Examples

The soldier loaded a projectile into his slingshot before aiming at the target.
A stone can become a dangerous projectile when hurled with enough force.
During the demonstration, the scientist explained how the angle affects the path of a projectile.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: missile - an object that is forcibly propelled at a target
  • Antonym: rocket - a self-propelled missile or vehicle

Etymology

From Latin 'proiectus', past participle of 'proicere' (to throw forth).

2. NOUN

/ prəˈdʒɛk.tɪl / (AmE), / prəˈdʒɛk.taɪl / (BrE)
A vehicle or object propelled through space by a rocket engine, typically as a weapon or for scientific purposes.
object or vehicle propelled by rocket engine

Examples

The military launched a projectile into the upper atmosphere for testing purposes.
Engineers designed a new projectile that could travel greater distances with improved stability.
The museum displayed a historical projectile used in early rocket experiments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: missile - an object that is forcibly propelled at a target, typically by a rocket engine

Etymology

From Latin 'proiectus', past participle of 'proicere', meaning to throw forward.

3. ADJ

/ prəˈdʒɛktʃaɪl / (AmE), / prəˈdʒɛktʃaɪl / (BrE)
(of a force or object) impelling or impelled forward; propelled onward.
impelling or impelled forward

Examples

The missile followed a projectile path toward its target.
The invention used a projectile mechanism to accelerate particles.
He described the comet's projectile movement through space.

Synset Examples

a projectile force
a projectile missile

Related Terms

  • Synonym: propulsive - tending to propel or push forward
  • Antonym: stationary - not moving, not intended to be moved

Etymology

From Latin projectilis, from proicere (to throw forward).
