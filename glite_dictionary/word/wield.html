to wield

1. VERB

/ wiːld / (AmE), / wiːld / (BrE)
to have and use (power, authority, or influence); to exercise or control something effectively
have and exercise power, authority, etc.

Examples

The prime minister continues to wield significant influence over party decisions.
She wielded power with a firm but fair hand throughout her career.
They wield authority within the company, guiding its major projects.

Synset Examples

wield power and authority

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exert - to apply or use (especially power or effort)
  • Synonym: to brandish - to wave or swing (something, especially a weapon) as a threat or in anger or excitement

Etymology

From Old English *wieldan* (to control, command), related to Old High German *waltan* (to rule).

2. VERB

/ wild / (AmE), / wiːld / (BrE)
to hold and use (a tool, weapon, or other object) effectively; to have and exercise (authority, influence, etc.)
handle or use with skill

Examples

The knight expertly wielded his sword in the tournament.
She wields significant influence in the company despite her young age.
The chef wielded his knife with impressive precision.

Synset Examples

The burglar wielded an axe
The young violinist didn't manage her bow very well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to brandish - to wave or swing (especially a weapon) in a threatening or excited manner
  • Synonym: to manipulate - to handle or control (a tool, mechanism, etc.) skillfully

Etymology

From Old English *wieldan* (to control, govern, possess), related to German *walten* (to rule) and Old Norse *velda* (to have power).

Synset POS: v
