discriminate

1. ADJ

/ dɪˈskrɪmɪnət / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪnət / (BrE)
having or showing the ability to recognize fine distinctions or subtle differences
able to make fine distinctions

Examples

The critic's discriminate taste in literature was widely respected.
A discriminate ear can detect even the slightest differences between the two singers.
Hiring requires a discriminate evaluation of each candidate's strengths and weaknesses.

Synset Examples

discriminate judgments
discriminate people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discerning - having or showing good judgment; able to perceive subtle differences

Etymology

From Latin discriminatus, past participle of discriminare 'to divide, distinguish'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00777138-a
Synset POS: a

2. VERB

/ dɪˈskrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to recognize or perceive a difference between things and act accordingly
to recognize a difference

Examples

Children can easily discriminate between the voices of their parents and other adults.
He struggled to discriminate the subtle differences between the two wines.
Advanced equipment helps scientists discriminate one species from another based on genetic material.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to distinguish - to recognize or treat something as different

Etymology

From Latin discriminatus, past participle of discriminare (to divide, distinguish), from discrimen (distinction).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00651669-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈskrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to treat a person or group differently, especially in a worse way, because of their sex, race, or other characteristic
treat unfairly based on group identity

Examples

It is illegal to discriminate against someone based on their race or gender.
Many companies have strict policies not to discriminate in hiring practices.
She felt that the club discriminated against women by refusing them membership.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to segregate - to separate groups of people based on race or other differences
  • Antonym: favor - to treat someone better than others, often unfairly

Etymology

From Latin discriminare (to divide, distinguish), from discrimen (distinction).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02517971-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪˈskrɪmɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to recognize or identify a difference between two or more things; to distinguish
distinguish between things

Examples

Most people can discriminate between subtle shades of color.
The machine is able to discriminate among various types of metals.
She struggled to discriminate the individual instruments in the orchestra.

Synset Examples

I could not discriminate the different tastes in this complicated dish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to distinguish - to recognize or treat someone or something as different

Etymology

From Latin discriminatus, past participle of discriminare (to divide, distinguish).
