analog

1. ADJ

/ ˈænəˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈænəlɒg / (BrE)
(of a circuit or device) using signals or information represented by a continuously variable physical quantity, such that output is proportional to input.
using continuously variable signals, not digital

Examples

Traditional vinyl records produce an analog sound that many audiophiles prefer.
This old camera uses analog film rather than storing images digitally.
The temperature was displayed by an analog gauge, not an electronic screen.

Synset Examples

analogue device
linear amplifier

Related Terms

  • Antonym: digital - relating to using discrete numerical values or signals

Etymology

From Greek 'analogos' (proportionate), through French 'analogue'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00111308-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ ˈænəˌlɔɡ / (AmE), / ˈanəlɒɡ / (BrE)
a person or thing seen as comparable to another; something that is analogous to something else
something comparable to another thing

Examples

In art, stained glass is often seen as an analog to painting due to its use of color and light.
The wings of a bird and the wings of an airplane are considered analogs in function.
His recent work serves as an analog to developments in modern architecture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: analogue - something comparable to another thing; chiefly British spelling
  • Synonym: equivalent - a person or thing equal to another in value, function, or meaning
  • Antonym: opposite - something that is completely different or contrary

Etymology

From Greek 'analogos' meaning 'proportionate'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04753670-n
Synset POS: n
