justice

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌstɪs / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌstɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being just, fair, or morally right
fairness; quality of being just

Examples

She dedicated her life to fighting for justice in her community.
The protesters marched to demand justice for the victims.
Everyone deserves to be treated with justice and respect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fairness - the quality of treating people equally or in a way that is right or reasonable
  • Antonym: injustice - lack of fairness or justice

Etymology

From Old French justice, from Latin justitia (righteousness, equity), from justus (just)

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌs.tɪs / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌs.tɪs / (BrE)
a judge, especially a judge of a higher court such as the Supreme Court or a court of appeal
a judge, especially of a high court

Examples

The justice delivered the court's opinion in a calm and authoritative manner.
Several justices of the Supreme Court attended the ceremonial event.
After a long career as a lawyer, she was appointed as a justice on the state’s highest court.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: judge - a public official with authority to decide cases in a court of law

Etymology

From Old French *justice*, from Latin *justitia* (righteousness, equity), from *justus* (just).

3. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌstɪs / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌstɪs / (BrE)
The process or principle of determining what is right and fair, especially in regard to the assignment of rewards and punishments in a legal or moral context.
fairness; law or moral judgment

Examples

She spent her career fighting for justice and equal rights for all citizens.
Many people believe that the court's verdict was a triumph of justice.
The organization strives to promote justice in communities that have long been neglected by the legal system.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: injustice - lack of fairness or justice
  • Synonym: fairness - impartial and just treatment or behavior

Etymology

From Old French *justice*, from Latin *justitia* (righteousness, equity), from *justus* (just).
