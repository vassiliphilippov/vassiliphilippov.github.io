subjectivism

1. NOUN

/ səbˈdʒɛktɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / səbˈdʒɛktɪvɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
(philosophy) the doctrine that knowledge, truth, and values are dependent on individual subjective experience rather than objective reality.
belief that knowledge or value is subjective

Examples

The philosopher argued that subjectivism undermines the possibility of universal moral truths.
Modern art critics sometimes embrace subjectivism to explain varying interpretations of a single painting.
Her approach to ethics is rooted in subjectivism, focusing on individual perspectives rather than fixed rules.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: objectivism - the belief that knowledge and values are independent of personal feelings or opinions

Etymology

From 'subjective' + '-ism', influenced by French 'subjectivisme'.

2. NOUN

/ səbˈdʒɛktɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / səbˈdʒɛktɪˌvɪzəm / (BrE)
the doctrine that knowledge and value judgments are shaped by personal feelings, tastes, or opinions rather than objective reality
doctrine prioritizing personal experience or feelings

Examples

Many critics accuse modern media of promoting subjectivism over factual reporting.
His philosophical writings strongly advocate subjectivism as the basis for all human understanding.
In art, subjectivism values the individual experience of the viewer above all else.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: objectivism - the belief that certain things, especially moral truths, exist independently of human knowledge or perception
  • Synonym: subjectivity - the quality of being based on or influenced by personal feelings or opinions

Etymology

From French subjectivisme, from 'subjectif' (subjective) + '-isme' (-ism).
