to denaturalise

1. VERB

/ diˈnætʃərəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diˈnatʃ(ə)rəlʌɪz / (BrE)
to make something less natural or deprive it of its natural qualities
make less natural; deprive of natural qualities

Examples

Attempts to denaturalise traditional art forms have sparked much debate among critics.
Excessive processing can denaturalise the taste of fresh food products.
Urban planning often denaturalises landscapes, replacing greenery with concrete.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to denature - to alter the natural qualities of something

Etymology

From de- (expressing removal) + naturalise, after French dénaturaliser.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00413785-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ diˈnætʃrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diˈnatʃ(ə)rəlʌɪz / (BrE)
to officially remove someone's rights and status as a citizen of a country
remove citizenship status

Examples

After the investigation, the government decided to denaturalise the individual for lying on his citizenship application.
Several war criminals were denaturalised in the 1980s as new evidence emerged about their past.
The government has strict rules about when it can denaturalise someone.

Synset Examples

The former Nazi was denaturalized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to denationalize - to deprive of national rights or characteristics; often used synonymously

Etymology

From Latin 'de-' (removal) + 'naturalise', meaning to make a citizen; modelled on 'naturalise'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00413436-v
Synset POS: v
