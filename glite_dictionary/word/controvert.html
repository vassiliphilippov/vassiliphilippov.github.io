to controvert

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːntrəˌvɜrt / (AmE), / ˈkɒntrəˌvɜːt / (BrE)
to prove something to be false or incorrect; to dispute or oppose in argument
prove false; dispute

Examples

The scientist attempted to controvert the widely accepted theory with new evidence.
Her findings controvert the conclusions drawn in previous studies.
Many historians have controverted the accuracy of the ancient manuscript.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refute - to prove a statement or theory is wrong or false
  • Antonym: support - to back up or confirm the truth of something

Etymology

From Latin 'controvertere', from 'contra-' (against) + 'vertere' (to turn).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːn.trə.vɝːt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.trə.vɜːt / (BrE)
to dispute or oppose by argument; to deny the truth of (a statement or proposition)
to dispute or oppose by argument

Examples

Legal scholars continue to controvert the interpretation of the new law.
The scientists did not controvert the findings, but rather built upon them.
His claims were quickly controverted by expert witnesses during the trial.

Synset Examples

The board opposed his motion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oppose - to actively resist or refuse to comply
  • Antonym: to concede - to admit as true, to yield

Etymology

From Latin 'controvertere', from 'contra' (against) + 'vertere' (to turn).
