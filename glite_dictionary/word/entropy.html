entropy

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛn.trə.pi / (AmE), / ˈɛn.trə.pi / (BrE)
In information theory, a numerical measure of the uncertainty or unpredictability of the possible outcomes in a set of messages.
uncertainty measure in information theory

Examples

The entropy of the compressed file is significantly lower than that of the original data.
A random sequence of letters has higher entropy than a predictable phrase.
Shannon's concept of entropy revolutionized digital communication.

Synset Examples

the signal contained thousands of bits of information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: information - facts provided or learned about something

Etymology

From Greek entropia (a turning towards), introduced in this sense by Claude Shannon in 1948 after the thermodynamic term.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛntrəpi / (AmE), / ˈɛntrəpi / (BrE)
a measure of the unavailable energy in a thermodynamic system, often interpreted as the degree of disorder or randomness
measure of unavailable energy; disorder in thermodynamics
medical

Examples

According to the second law of thermodynamics, entropy in a closed system will always increase over time.
As ice melts, the entropy of the water molecules increases due to greater movement.
Physicists study how energy becomes less available for work as entropy rises in a process.

Synset Examples

entropy increases as matter and energy in the universe degrade to an ultimate state of inert uniformity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: order - organized or structured arrangement, in contrast to randomness or disorder
  • Synonym: disorder - state of confusion or lack of order, related to increasing entropy

Etymology

From Greek 'entropia' (transformation), from 'en-' (in) + 'trope' (a turning, transformation).
