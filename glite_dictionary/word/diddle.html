to diddle

1. VERB

/ ˈdɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈdɪd.əl / (BrE)
to cheat or swindle someone out of something by deceit
cheat or swindle by deceit
informal

Examples

The con artist tried to diddle elderly people out of their savings.
He was furious when he discovered he'd been diddled in the deal.
They accused the company of diddling customers by charging hidden fees.

Synset Examples

He swindled me out of my inheritance
She defrauded the customers who trusted her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swindle - to cheat someone out of money or property
  • Synonym: to defraud - to illegally obtain money or property by deception
  • Synonym: scam - a dishonest scheme or fraud

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly imitative or from dialectal daddle (to walk unsteadily).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪd.əl / (AmE), / ˈdɪd.əl / (BrE)
to move, handle, or manipulate something, especially absent-mindedly or without purpose
handle or move something absent-mindedly

Examples

Stop diddling with the remote and pay attention to the movie.
She absent-mindedly diddled the pen cap while thinking about her essay.
He likes to diddle with gadgets he finds around the house.

Synset Examples

She played nervously with her wedding ring
Don't fiddle with the screws
He played with the idea of running for the Senate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fiddle - to touch or manipulate something restlessly or absent-mindedly
  • Synonym: toy - to handle or play with something in a casual or absent-minded way

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly imitative or a variant of 'daddle' (to walk unsteadily).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01589199-v
Synset POS: v
