tram

1. NOUN

/ træm / (AmE), / træm / (BrE)
A four-wheeled wagon that runs on tracks, used especially in mines to transport materials.
mine wagon running on tracks

Examples

The miners loaded the coal into the tram before sending it to the surface.
Each tram could carry up to a ton of ore along the underground tracks.
The old photograph showed a row of trams lined up deep inside the mine.

Synset Examples

a tramcar carries coal out of a coal mine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tramcar - a railroad car that runs on tracks in a mine or in the street

Etymology

Probably from Scottish 'tram' (beam of wood, shaft of a barrow).

2. NOUN

/ træm / (AmE), / træm / (BrE)
a vehicle for carrying passengers or goods in cabins suspended from cables and supported by towers, often used at ski resorts or across valleys
cable-suspended passenger or freight vehicle

Examples

We took the tram up the mountain for a breathtaking view.
The ski resort installed a new tram to carry visitors to the summit.
During the summer, the tram offers rides across the wide valley.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gondola - an enclosed cabin suspended from a cable, used to transport passengers
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04477048-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ træm / (AmE), / træm / (BrE)
A passenger vehicle that runs on tracks, typically along urban streets, and is powered by electricity.
electric passenger vehicle on tracks

Examples

The city has an extensive tram network connecting all major neighborhoods.
She caught the tram to work every morning instead of driving.
A new tram line is being built to reduce traffic congestion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: streetcar - an electric passenger vehicle running on rails in city streets
  • Synonym: trolley - an electric vehicle that runs on rails, usually in a city (North American usage)
  • Antonym: bus - a road vehicle for transporting many passengers, usually on scheduled routes

Etymology

From Scottish and Northern English dialect 'tram' (beam, shaft), possibly of Scandinavian origin.
