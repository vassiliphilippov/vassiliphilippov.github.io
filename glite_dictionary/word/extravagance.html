extravagance

1. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (AmE), / ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (BrE)
The practice of spending more money than is necessary or wise; excessive or wasteful expenditure.
excessive or wasteful spending

Examples

The family's extravagance soon led them into debt.
Critics blame government extravagance for the rise in taxes.
He regretted his youthful extravagance once he saw his empty bank account.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wastefulness - the quality of using more than is needed
  • Antonym: frugality - careful use of resources, avoiding waste

Etymology

From French *extravagance*, from Medieval Latin *extravagantia*, from *extravagari* (to wander outside bounds).

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (AmE), / ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (BrE)
the quality of exceeding what is reasonable, accepted, or probable, especially in behavior, description, or claim
quality of exceeding reasonable limits

Examples

The extravagance of his promises made them hard to believe.
Some critics accused the novel of narrative extravagance and fanciful details.
Her stories are charming, but sometimes their extravagance stretches the truth.

Synset Examples

we were surprised by the extravagance of his description

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exaggeration - representation of something as greater than it really is
  • Antonym: moderation - avoiding excess or extremes; restraint

Etymology

From French extravagance, from Latin extra (outside) + vagari (to wander).

3. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (AmE), / ɪkˈstrævəɡəns / (BrE)
the quality of spending money in an excessive or wasteful way
excessive or wasteful spending

Examples

His extravagance quickly depleted the family fortune.
The company's extravagance on luxury offices raised questions about its priorities.
Learning to avoid extravagance can help you save money and manage your budget better.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: frugality - the quality of being economical with money
  • Synonym: profligacy - reckless extravagance or wastefulness

Etymology

From Medieval Latin extravagantia (a wandering), from Latin extravagari (to wander outside).
