/ ˈkɑːn.dʒəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.dʒəˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
(of a pinnate leaflet) having only one pair of leaflets.

Examples

The leaves of this species are conjugate, with only a single pair of leaflets per leaf.

6. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːn.dʒə.ɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.dʒʊ.ɡeɪt / (BrE)
(of an organic compound) having two or more double bonds each separated by a single bond, allowing for resonance and delocalization of electrons.
having alternating double and single bonds (chemistry)

Examples

Benzene is a classic example of a conjugate hydrocarbon, with alternating single and double bonds forming a ring.
The dye molecule relies on a conjugate system to absorb visible light and produce color.
Chemists study conjugate dienes because their structure affects reactivity and stability.
