libel

1. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈlaɪbəl / (BrE)
In law, a written statement filed by a plaintiff in a defamation case, explaining their complaint and the relief sought.
written legal statement in defamation case
legal

Examples

The court required the plaintiff to submit a detailed libel outlining the alleged defamation.
Her attorney drafted a libel that thoroughly described how the article damaged her reputation.
The libel included both the facts of the case and the damages the plaintiff sought.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: complaint - legal document stating a plaintiff's case

Etymology

From Old French *libelle*, from Latin *libellus* (little book, pamphlet), diminutive of *liber* (book).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪ.bəl / (AmE), / ˈlaɪ.bəl / (BrE)
a published false statement that is damaging to a person's reputation; written defamation
written defamation; damaging false publication
legal

Examples

The celebrity sued the magazine for publishing a libel against her.
It is essential to verify facts before printing any story to avoid accusations of libel.
He was found guilty of libel after a court determined his article was both false and malicious.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slander - spoken false statement damaging to a person's reputation
  • Synonym: defamation - the action of damaging the good reputation of someone; slander or libel

Etymology

From Old French *libelle*, from Latin *libellus* (little book, document).

3. VERB

/ ˈlaɪ.bəl / (AmE), / ˈlaɪ.bəl / (BrE)
to publish a false and defamatory statement about someone that damages their reputation
publish a defamatory statement
legal

Examples

The celebrity sued the magazine for having libeled him in their latest article.
Journalists must be careful not to libel anyone, as it can lead to lawsuits.
The book was withdrawn after the author was accused of libeling a local official.

Synset Examples

The newspaper was accused of libeling him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defame - to damage someone's reputation by making false statements
  • Synonym: slander - to make false spoken statements damaging to a person's reputation

Etymology

From Latin 'libellus' (little book, petition), diminutive of 'liber' (book).
