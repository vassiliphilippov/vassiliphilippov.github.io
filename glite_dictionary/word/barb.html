barb

1. NOUN

/ bɑrb / (AmE), / bɑːb / (BrE)
a sharp projection with a backward-facing point, attached to an arrowhead or spear, making it difficult to remove once embedded
sharp backward point on arrow or spear

Examples

The ancient hunters designed their arrows with a barb to ensure the prey could not escape.
Once the fish was hooked, the barb made it nearly impossible to free it easily.
Removing a barb from the wound required great care to avoid further injury.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prong - a pointed projection, similar in shape to a barb

Etymology

From Latin 'barba' (beard), referring to bristle-like projections.

3. NOUN

/ bɑrb / (AmE), / bɑːb / (BrE)
a remark intended to hurt or criticize someone in a sharp, direct way
hurtful or critical remark

Examples

She couldn't resist adding a barb about his punctuality during the meeting.
His barb at her expense left everyone in the room silent.
Even in his farewell speech, he slipped in a final barb directed at his rivals.

4. NOUN

/ bɑrb / (AmE), / bɑːb / (BrE)
A sharp, pointed projection, especially one found on barbed wire designed to catch or prevent movement through it.
sharp point on barbed wire

Examples

The barb on the wire tore his sleeve as he climbed over the fence.
Make sure you avoid the barb when handling barbed wire to prevent injury.
Each barb was sharp enough to deter animals from crossing the boundary.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spike - a sharp pointed object or projection
  • Synonym: prong - a pointed projecting part

Etymology

From Old French *barbe*, from Latin *barba* (beard), referring to something that sticks out like a beard hair.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

5. NOUN

/ bɑrb / (AmE), / bɑːb / (BrE)
one of the slender, parallel filaments that project from the main shaft of a feather and interlock to form the feather's vane
filament projecting from a feather shaft

Examples

Each feather consists of a central shaft with hundreds of barbs on either side.
The barbs of a bird's feather help hook together, giving the feather its smooth surface.
When examined under a microscope, the tiny barbs are clearly visible along the feather's shaft.

Etymology

From Latin 'barba' meaning 'beard', referring to hair-like structures.

Source: WordNet
