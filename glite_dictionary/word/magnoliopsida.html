Magnoliopsida

1. NOUN

/ mæɡˌnoʊliəˈpsaɪdə / (AmE), / mæɡˌnəʊliəˈpsɪdə / (BrE)
A class of flowering plants (angiosperms) characterized by seeds with two cotyledons (dicotyledons), net-veined leaves, and comprising several subclasses including Magnoliidae, Hamamelidae, Caryophyllidae, Dilleniidae, Rosidae, and Asteridae.
a class of dicotyledonous flowering plants
medical

Examples

Most trees and shrubs you see in temperate regions belong to the Magnoliopsida.
The classification of Magnoliopsida includes a wide variety of flowering plant families.
Botanists debate on the precise boundaries of the Magnoliopsida class.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dicotyledon - a plant with two seed leaves (cotyledons)
  • Antonym: monocotyledon - a plant with one seed leaf (cotyledon)

Etymology

From modern Latin, from the genus name 'Magnolia' and Greek 'opsida' (appearance).

2. NOUN

/ mæɡˌnoʊliˈɒpsɪdə / (AmE), / maɡˌnəʊliˈɒpsɪdə / (BrE)
A major class of flowering plants (angiosperms) that produce seeds with two cotyledons (dicotyledons) and typically have net-veined leaves; includes many of the familiar broad-leaved plants.
a class of dicotyledonous flowering plants

Examples

Most trees and shrubs in temperate regions belong to the Magnoliopsida.
The class Magnoliopsida includes roses, oaks, and sunflowers.
Researchers are studying the evolutionary history of Magnoliopsida to better understand plant diversity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dicotyledon - a plant whose seed has two embryonic leaves or cotyledons
  • Antonym: monocotyledon - a plant whose seed has one embryonic leaf or cotyledon

Etymology

From New Latin, combining Magnolia (a genus of plants) and '-opsida' (used in botanical Latin to denote classes).
