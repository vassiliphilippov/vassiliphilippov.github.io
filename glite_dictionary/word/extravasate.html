to extravasate

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈstrævəˌseɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstrævəseɪt / (BrE)
(of a fluid, especially blood or lymph) to force out or cause to escape from a vessel or its normal channel into surrounding tissue.
leak or force fluid out of a vessel
medical

Examples

If injected too quickly, the fluid may extravasate into the surrounding tissue.
The dye began to extravasate, causing discoloration at the injection site.
Injury can cause blood to extravasate from the vessels, leading to swelling.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leak - to flow out of a container or through a hole
  • Synonym: to infiltrate - (of a liquid) to permeate into something

Etymology

From Latin ex- (out of) + vas, vasis (vessel).

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈstrævəˌseɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstrævəseɪt / (BrE)
(of molten material or a liquid, especially lava or blood) to force or cause to flow out of its usual channel or vessel.
to force liquid (e.g. lava) to flow out

Examples

Intense heat can extravasate magma from the depths of a volcano to the surface.
Engineers observed that the pressure was sufficient to extravasate molten metal from the containment vessel.
If the rock is porous enough, lava may extravasate through hidden cracks and fissures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pour - to cause a liquid to flow rapidly, especially in a steady stream
  • Synonym: to erupt - to burst forth, especially referring to volcanoes or molten material

Etymology

From Latin extravasatus (poured out), from extra- (outside) + vas (vessel).
