to uproot

1. VERB

/ ʌpˈruːt / (AmE), / ʌpˈruːt / (BrE)
to pull a plant or something out of the ground by its roots; to remove completely or force to leave a place
pull out by the roots

Examples

Heavy rains helped to uproot several trees along the riverbank.
The gardener tried to uproot all the weeds from the flower beds.
They were forced to uproot their family and move to another country.

Synset Examples

uproot the vine that has spread all over the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pull up - to remove something from the ground by pulling

Etymology

From the prefix 'up-' and 'root', meaning to pull upwards by the roots.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01569697-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ʌpˈrut / (AmE), / ʌpˈruːt / (BrE)
to remove people forcibly from their homeland or usual environment, making them settle elsewhere
force to leave homeland

Examples

The civil war uprooted thousands of families from their ancestral homes.
She was uprooted from her village and forced to start over in a foreign country.
Economic hardship can sometimes uproot entire communities, scattering residents far from their homeland.

Synset Examples

The war uprooted many people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to displace - to force someone to leave their home, especially because of war or disaster

Etymology

From up- (meaning 'out of') + root, originally used for plants and later extended metaphorically to people.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02544219-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ʌpˈɹut / (AmE), / ʌpˈɹuːt / (BrE)
to remove or destroy completely, especially something deeply established or ingrained
destroy or remove completely

Examples

The new government set out to uproot corruption from every level of the administration.
Decades-old customs were suddenly uprooted by rapid modernization in the country.
Activists are working hard to uproot the causes of inequality in the community.

Synset Examples

the vestiges of political democracy were soon uprooted
root out corruption

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eradicate - to destroy or get rid of something completely
  • Synonym: to eliminate - to remove or get rid of something
  • Antonym: preserve - to keep something in its original state

Etymology

From up- + root, originally referring to pulling a plant up by the roots.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01666012-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ʌpˈruːt / (AmE), / ʌpˈruːt / (BrE)
to pull something, especially a plant or tree, out of the ground; to remove someone or something completely from their usual environment or situation
remove from ground or usual environment

Examples

The gardener had to uproot all the weeds before planting vegetables.
After the factory closed, many families had to uproot and seek work elsewhere.
Strong winds can sometimes uproot even the largest trees.

Synset Examples

She decided to uproot her life and move abroad.
We need to uproot the old plants before replanting.
The storm could uproot trees and cause damage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eradicate - to completely destroy or remove something
  • Antonym: plant - to place a seed, bulb, or plant in the ground to grow

Etymology

From up + root, meaning 'to root up.'
