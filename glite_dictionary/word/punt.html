punt

1. NOUN

/ pʌnt / (AmE), / pʌnt / (BrE)
the former basic unit of money in Ireland, equal to 100 pence, replaced by the euro in 2002
former Irish currency unit

Examples

The Irish punt was replaced by the euro in 2002.
Collectors can still find old punt notes and coins in Ireland.
He exchanged his punts for euros at a local bank.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: euro - currency that replaced the punt in Ireland

Etymology

From Irish 'punt', derived from the English 'pound', itself from Latin 'pondo' (by weight).

2. VERB

/ pʌnt / (AmE), / pʌnt / (BrE)
(in sports) to kick the ball by dropping it from the hands and striking it before it touches the ground, especially in football or rugby.
kick the ball before it hits the ground

Examples

The quarterback decided to punt the ball on fourth down.
She watched the player expertly punt during the rugby match.
They punted the ball deep into the opponent's territory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kick - to strike with the foot

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly related to earlier sense of 'punting' (propel a boat with a pole).

3. NOUN

/ pʌnt / (AmE), / pʌnt / (BrE)
a long, narrow, flat-bottomed boat with square ends, used in shallow water and propelled with a long pole by pushing against the riverbed
flat-bottomed boat for shallow waters

Examples

They hired a punt to explore the quiet backwaters of the river.
Tourists enjoyed gliding past willows in a punt on the Cam.
He learned to steer a punt using the long pole.

Etymology

From Middle English *ponte* (flat-bottomed boat), of uncertain origin.
