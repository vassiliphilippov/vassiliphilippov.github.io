dwarf

1. NOUN

/ dwɔrf / (AmE), / dwɔːf / (BrE)
(in folklore and mythology) a small, often bearded, creature resembling an old man, believed to live underground and guard hidden treasures.
legendary small, old man-like creature

Examples

In Norse mythology, a dwarf is known for crafting powerful magical items.
The dwarf guarded the treasure deep within the mountain.
Many fairy tales feature a dwarf living underground and protecting hidden gold.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elf - a small, often mischievous, supernatural creature of folklore
  • Antonym: giant - a being of great size and strength in mythology

Etymology

From Old English 'dweorg', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'dwerg' and German 'Zwerg'.

2. VERB

/ dwɔrf / (AmE), / dwɔːf / (BrE)
to make someone or something seem small by comparison
make seem small by comparison

Examples

The new skyscraper dwarfs all the surrounding buildings in the city center.
Her intelligence dwarfs that of her classmates.
This gigantic oak tree dwarfs everything else in the park.

Synset Examples

This year's debt dwarfs that of last year

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to overshadow - to appear more important or impressive than (something or someone else)

Etymology

From Old English 'dweorg' (mythical being of small stature), later used metaphorically.

3. VERB

/ dwɔrf / (AmE), / dwɔːf / (BrE)
to make something appear smaller or less significant by comparison, or to check the growth or development of something
make appear small; check growth

Examples

The new skyscrapers dwarf the old church in the city center.
Lack of water can dwarf the growth of tomato plants in the garden.
Her achievements in the field dwarf those of her contemporaries.

Synset Examples

the lack of sunlight dwarfed these pines

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stunt - to prevent from growing or developing normally

Etymology

From Old English *dweorg* (dwarf) and related to Old Norse *dvergr*; figurative verb sense developed later.

4. NOUN

/ dwɔrf / (AmE), / dwɔːf / (BrE)
a person of unusually short stature, often due to a medical condition; in modern usage, considered insensitive or offensive by some, with 'person of short stature' or 'person with dwarfism' preferred
a person of very short stature
offensive

Examples

The story centers around a dwarf who overcomes prejudice in his village.
Medical professionals now prefer the term 'person with dwarfism' instead of calling someone a dwarf.
A famous dwarf actor played memorable roles in several blockbuster movies.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: giant - a person of extremely large size

Etymology

From Old English 'dweorg', related to Old Norse 'dvergr'.

5. NOUN

/ dwɔrf / (AmE), / dwɔːf / (BrE)
A plant or animal that is much smaller than is typical for its species or kind.
an unusually small plant or animal

Examples

The bonsai is a dwarf tree that fits comfortably on a desk.
Scientists discovered a dwarf variety of the shrimp in the lake.
That unusual flower is a dwarf compared to others of its species.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: giant - an exceptionally large plant or animal
  • Synonym: pygmy - an unusually small animal or plant, especially one of a species distinguished by small size

Etymology

From Old English 'dweorg', related to Old Norse 'dvergr' (dwarf, small being).
