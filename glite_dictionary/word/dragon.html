dragon

1. NOUN

/ ˈdræɡən / (AmE), / ˈdræɡən / (BrE)
a small tropical Asian lizard capable of gliding by spreading winglike membranes on each side of the body
small gliding Asian lizard

Examples

The dragon launched itself from the tree, gliding gracefully between branches.
Biologists discovered a new species of dragon in the dense forests of Borneo.
Unlike other lizards, the dragon can escape predators by gliding through the air.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdræɡən / (AmE), / ˈdraɡən / (BrE)
A large imaginary creature, typically represented as a reptilian monster with wings, claws, and the ability to breathe fire, found in the mythologies and folklore of Europe and Asia.
mythical fire-breathing reptilian creature

Examples

The knight set out to slay the fierce dragon that terrorized the village.
According to legend, the ancient dragon guarded a mountain of gold.
Children listened in awe as their grandfather described a battle with a fire-breathing dragon.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: wyvern - mythical two-legged dragon with wings

Etymology

From Old French 'dragon', from Latin 'draco', from Greek 'drakōn' (serpent, dragon).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈdræɡən / (AmE), / ˈdræɡən / (BrE)
a fiercely vigilant and unpleasant woman, often domineering or difficult to deal with
domineering or unpleasant woman

Examples

Everyone is a little afraid of the office dragon, who scolds anyone for being late.
My friend's mother was a real dragon about keeping the house spotless.
People referred to the headmistress as the school dragon because of her strict rules.

Etymology

From the legendary dragon, a large, fierce mythical creature; metaphorical use for a fierce woman dates to the 19th century.
