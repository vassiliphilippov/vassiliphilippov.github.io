to criminate

1. VERB

/ ˈkrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈkrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
formally accuse someone of a crime or wrongdoing
formally accuse of a crime

Examples

The lawyer did not wish to criminate her client without substantive evidence.
He was careful not to criminate anyone when discussing the incident.
Evidence emerged that could potentially criminate the officials involved in the case.

Synset Examples

The neighbors accused the man of spousal abuse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accuse - to say that someone has done something wrong or illegal
  • Antonym: to exonerate - to officially state that someone is not to blame or is free of guilt

Etymology

From Medieval Latin criminatus, past participle of criminari (to accuse), from Latin crimen (crime).

2. VERB

/ ˈkrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈkrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
To accuse or charge someone with a wrongdoing, especially formally or in a legal context.
formally accuse or charge

Examples

He refused to criminate himself when questioned by the authorities.
The witness was careful not to criminate anyone without concrete evidence.
Her testimony could potentially criminate several senior officials implicated in the case.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accuse - to charge someone with a fault or offense
  • Antonym: to exonerate - to officially declare someone not guilty of wrongdoing

Etymology

From Latin criminari (to accuse), from crimen (crime, accusation).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00825330-v
Synset POS: v
