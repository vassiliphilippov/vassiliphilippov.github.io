a posteriori

1. ADV

/ ˌeɪ pɑːˌstɪəriˈɔːraɪ / (AmE), / ˌeɪ pɒˌstɪəriˈɔːraɪ / (BrE)
(of reasoning or knowledge) derived from actual observation or experience, rather than from theory or pure logic.
based on observed facts or experience

Examples

Scientific hypotheses are tested a posteriori through experiments and observations.
He argued a posteriori that the theory aligns with all the available evidence.
The validity of the claim was established a posteriori, after collecting sufficient data.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: a priori - based on theoretical deduction rather than empirical observation

Etymology

From Latin 'a posteriori', meaning 'from the latter'; used in philosophy to refer to reasoning from effects to causes.

3. ADJ

/ ˌeɪ pɑːˌstɪəriˈɔːraɪ / (AmE), / ˌeɪ pɒˌstɪəriˈɔːraɪ / (BrE)
(of knowledge or reasoning) dependent on observation or experience for validation, rather than derived from theory or pure logic.
requiring evidence or experience

Examples

The scientist rejected the hypothesis because it lacked a posteriori evidence.
Our conclusions must be based on a posteriori observations, not just theoretical speculation.
Historically, much of science has relied on a posteriori reasoning to validate claims.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: a priori - independent of experience; based on theory or logic

Etymology

From Latin, literally 'from the latter', referring to reasoning based on observed facts.
