jib

1. VERB

/ dʒɪb / (AmE), / dʒɪb / (BrE)
(of a sail or boom) to shift abruptly from one side of a ship to the other, especially when sailing before the wind.
to shift a sail or boom sharply from side to side (nautical)

Examples

The mainsail suddenly jibbed as the wind direction changed.
Watch out when the boom jibs so you don't get hit.
As the yacht accelerated, the headsail jibbed and flapped violently in the wind.

Synset Examples

The sail jibbed wildly

2. VERB

/ dʒɪb / (AmE), / dʒɪb / (BrE)
to suddenly refuse to do something or to comply, especially because of reluctance or objection
refuse or hesitate to comply

Examples

The horse jibbed at the jump and refused to go any further.
When asked to speak at the event, she jibbed at the last moment.
He tends to jib whenever new responsibilities are mentioned.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to comply - to act in accordance with a wish or command
  • Synonym: balk - to hesitate or be unwilling to accept an idea or undertaking

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English 'gibyng' (mocking, jeering), origin uncertain.

3. NOUN

/ dʒɪb / (AmE), / dʒɪb / (BrE)
a triangular sail set ahead of the foremast of a sailing vessel
triangular sail in front of foremast

Examples

The crew quickly raised the jib as the wind began to pick up.
He adjusted the jib to catch more wind and increase the yacht's speed.
The old schooner carried both a mainsail and a jib on her voyage.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: genoa - a large jib that overlaps the mainsail

Etymology

Probably from a Dutch dialect word *gibbe* (sail).

4. VERB

/ dʒɪb / (AmE), / dʒɪb / (BrE)
to suddenly hesitate or refuse to proceed or do something; to show reluctance or objection
hesitate or refuse to proceed

Examples

She began to jib at the idea of working overtime without extra pay.
He will jib if you ask him to speak in public unexpectedly.
The horse started to jib at the unfamiliar jump.

Synset Examples

Don't jib on the test; study hard!
It's easy to jib if you're not prepared.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to hesitate - be slow to act because of uncertainty
  • Antonym: to comply - to act in accordance with a wish or command

Etymology

Originally a nautical term from late 18th century, possibly related to the verb 'gib' meaning 'to start or balk'.
