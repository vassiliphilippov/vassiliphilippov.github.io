to mobilise

1. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to prepare and organize troops or resources for war or military action
prepare troops for war
british

Examples

The government decided to mobilise its armed forces in response to the growing threat.
It took only a few days to mobilise two divisions for active duty.
After the declaration of war, the country had to quickly mobilise all available resources.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to demobilise - to disband or stand down troops after military service
  • Synonym: to mobilize - American spelling of 'mobilise'

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile' (movable), related to 'mobilis' in Latin.

2. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to prepare and organize troops or resources for active military service or action
organize for military action

Examples

The government decided to mobilise thousands of troops in response to the border crisis.
After the declaration of war, the country mobilised its armed forces within days.
As tensions rose, the military began to mobilise for possible conflict.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to demobilise - to disband troops or end military mobilization

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile', from Latin 'mobilis' (movable).

3. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to cause something or someone to move or be put into motion; to make ready for movement or action
to cause to move or set in motion

Examples

The government decided to mobilise resources to help the victims of the disaster.
Doctors were able to mobilise the patient after several weeks of physical therapy.
The campaign aims to mobilise volunteers across the country for environmental clean-up projects.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: move - to change position; to go from one place to another

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile' (movable), from Latin 'mobilis'.

4. VERB

/ ˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to prepare and organize resources, people, or troops for a purpose, especially for action or use
prepare for action or use
british

Examples

The government had to mobilise emergency services after the earthquake struck.
Community leaders are working to mobilise volunteers for the clean-up campaign.
She managed to mobilise enough support to launch the new initiative.

Synset Examples

marshal resources

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to demobilise - to disband or release from a state of readiness or active service
  • Synonym: to mobilize - alternative (American) spelling: to make ready for action or use

Etymology

From French 'mobiliser', from 'mobile' (movable, ready to move), from Latin 'mobilis' (movable).
