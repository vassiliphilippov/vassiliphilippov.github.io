overhang

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊ.vɚˈhæŋ / (AmE), / ˌəʊ.vəˈhæŋ / (BrE)
to extend, project, or hang over the edge of something
project or extend over

Examples

The cliff overhangs the winding river below, creating a dramatic view.
A large tree branch overhangs the roof, providing shade in the summer.
Be careful when walking under the balcony that overhangs the sidewalk.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to protrude - to stick out from a surface
  • Antonym: to recede - to move back or withdraw

Etymology

From Old English 'oferhangian', with 'over' + 'hang'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02719623-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈhæŋ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈhæŋ / (BrE)
to be suspended over or extend outward above something
extend or hang over

Examples

Tall pine trees overhang the riverbank, shading the water below.
The balcony overhangs the sidewalk, giving shelter during rain.
A large boulder overhung the narrow trail, making it seem dangerous to pass under.

Synset Examples

This huge rock beetles over the edge of the town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jut - to stick out, especially beyond the surface
  • Synonym: project - to extend outward beyond a surface

Etymology

From over- (above) + hang (from Old English 'hangan', to be suspended).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02703277-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌhæŋ / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəhæŋ / (BrE)
a part of something that sticks out or extends over something else, especially a structure or edge projecting beyond its support
a projecting part extending over something

Examples

The cliffs had a dangerous overhang that could break off at any moment.
The roof's overhang protected us from the rain while we waited outside.
A large overhang shaded the windows from the midday sun.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: projection - a part that juts out from a surface

Etymology

From over- (above) + hang.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03869894-n
Synset POS: n
