underlying

1. ADJ

/ ˌʌndɚˈlaɪɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈlaɪɪŋ / (BrE)
existing beneath the surface or as a fundamental, though not immediately obvious, part of something
present but not immediately obvious

Examples

The doctor tried to identify the underlying cause of her persistent headaches.
Although they seemed happy, there was an underlying tension in the conversation.
The success of the business depended on underlying economic factors that were not immediately visible.

Synset Examples

shortcomings inherent in our approach
an underlying meaning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inherent - existing as a natural or permanent part of something
  • Antonym: superficial - existing or occurring at or on the surface

Etymology

From underlie + -ing; underlie from Old English underlicgan ('to lie under').

2. ADJ

/ ˌʌndɚˈlaɪɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈlaɪɪŋ / (BrE)
situated beneath or below something else; existing under the surface.
situated beneath or below

Examples

The roots of the tree are hidden in the underlying soil.
Archaeologists discovered ancient ruins in the underlying layers of earth.
The technician examined the underlying structure to ensure it was stable.

Etymology

From underlie + -ing, first attested in the 17th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02358143-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈʌndɚˌlaɪɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈʌndəˌlaɪɪŋ / (BrE)
forming the basis or foundation of something; fundamental but not always immediately obvious.
basic; fundamental; foundational

Examples

The scientists are trying to understand the underlying causes of the disease.
Trust is the underlying foundation of any good relationship.
Her calm behavior hides an underlying sense of anxiety.

Synset Examples

the fundamental laws of the universe
a fundamental incompatibility between them
these rudimentary truths
underlying principles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fundamental - serving as an essential part or foundation
  • Synonym: basic - forming an essential foundation or starting point
  • Antonym: superficial - existing or occurring at or on the surface; not deep or foundational

Etymology

Formed from 'under-' (beneath) + 'lie' (to exist or be situated).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01862358-s
Synset POS: s
