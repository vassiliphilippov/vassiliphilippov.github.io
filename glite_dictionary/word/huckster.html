huckster

1. VERB

/ ˈhʌkstər / (AmE), / ˈhʌkstə / (BrE)
to sell or offer goods for sale in a petty, aggressive, or itinerant manner; to peddle goods from place to place
to peddle goods from place to place

Examples

The vendors would huckster their wares in every village they passed through.
He huckstered homemade jewelry at fairs across the region last summer.
Street performers often huckster small items to tourists along the boardwalk.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to peddle - to travel about selling goods

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *hokester* (pedlar, haggle), related to *hok* (corner, stall).

WordNet ID: oewn-02307797-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈhʌkstɚ / (AmE), / ˈhʌkstə / (BrE)
to bargain or wrangle, especially over the price or terms of a sale or agreement
to bargain or wrangle over price or terms

Examples

The vendor tried to huckster with tourists over the price of souvenirs.
They spent hours huckstering before finally reaching an agreement.
She refused to huckster for a better rate, finding it tiresome.

Synset Examples

Let's not haggle over a few dollars

Related Terms

  • Synonym: haggle - to dispute or bargain persistently, especially over the cost of something

Etymology

From the noun 'huckster', originally Middle English, from Middle Dutch 'hokester' (petty dealer).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02264354-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈhʌk.stɚ / (AmE), / ˈhʌk.stə / (BrE)
a person who sells goods, often of questionable value or quality, in an aggressive or dishonest way
dishonest or aggressive seller

Examples

The huckster shouted loudly in the marketplace, trying to attract buyers for his cheap wares.
Some viewed the salesman as a clever entrepreneur, while others called him a mere huckster.
The town passed new regulations to prevent hucksters from deceiving customers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peddler - a person who sells goods in public places
  • Degree Higher: merchant - a person involved in trade, especially one dealing with goods in large amounts
  • Antonym: customer - a person who buys goods or services

Etymology

From Middle English *huccester* (female peddler), from Old English *huxster*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10210135-n
Synset POS: n
