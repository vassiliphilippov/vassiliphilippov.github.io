dentin

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛn.tɪn / (AmE), / ˈdɛntɪn / (BrE)
The hard, dense, calcareous tissue beneath the enamel of a tooth, forming the bulk of the tooth's structure.
hard tissue beneath tooth enamel
medical

Examples

The dentin can become sensitive if the protective enamel is worn away.
Dentists often check the condition of the dentin during an examination.
Cavities that reach the dentin can cause pain and require prompt treatment.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: enamel - the hard, outer surface layer of a tooth
  • Degree Lower: pulp - the soft, innermost part of a tooth

Etymology

From Latin 'dens, dentis' (tooth), via French 'dentine'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɛn.tɪn / (AmE), / ˈdɛn.tɪn / (BrE)
The hard, dense, bony tissue forming the bulk of a tooth beneath the enamel and surrounding the pulp cavity.
hard tissue beneath tooth enamel
medical

Examples

The dentist explained that a cavity could expose the dentin and cause sensitivity.
Fluoride treatments help to protect both the enamel and the dentin of your teeth.
Damage to the dentin can lead to pain when consuming hot or cold foods.

Etymology

From Latin 'dens, dentis' (tooth), with -in suffix for substances.
