frantic

1. ADJ

/ ˈfræn.tɪk / (AmE), / ˈfræn.tɪk / (BrE)
extremely anxious, worried, or upset, often resulting in hurried or chaotic behavior; distraught with fear or other intense emotion.
extremely agitated or distraught

Examples

She made a frantic phone call to her mother when she lost her keys.
The crowd became frantic after hearing the shocking news.
He searched the house in a frantic attempt to find his missing wallet.

Synset Examples

frantic with anger and frustration
frenetic screams followed the accident
a frenzied look in his eye

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frenzied - wildly excited or uncontrolled
  • Antonym: calm - not agitated; free from excitement or disturbance

Etymology

From Latin 'phreneticus' via Old French 'frentik', influenced by Greek 'phrenētikos' (delirious).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfræn.tɪk / (AmE), / ˈfræn.tɪk / (BrE)
showing uncontrolled excitement, fear, or worry; wild or hurried in a way that is difficult to control
wild with excitement or emotion

Examples

The crowd grew frantic as the final seconds of the game ticked away.
She made a frantic search for her missing keys before her appointment.
His frantic phone calls finally paid off when someone answered.

Synset Examples

a crowd of delirious baseball fans
something frantic in their gaiety
a mad whirl of pleasure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delirious - showing wild excitement or uncontrolled emotion
  • Antonym: calm - not showing excitement or agitation; peaceful

Etymology

From Middle English *frantike*, from Old French *frantique*, from Latin *phreneticus* (‘delirious’).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02399292-s
Synset POS: s
