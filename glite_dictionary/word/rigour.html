rigour

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɪɡər / (AmE), / ˈrɪɡə / (BrE)
the strictness or thoroughness in attention to detail, standards, or accuracy
strictness or thoroughness
british

Examples

His research was praised for its scientific rigour and meticulous methodology.
The rigour of the school's admission process ensures only dedicated students are accepted.
She questioned the rigour with which the experiment was conducted.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rigor - the strictness or severity (American spelling)
  • Antonym: leniency - being less strict or showing tolerance

Etymology

From Latin rigor (stiffness, harshness) via Old French rigueur.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɪɡər / (AmE), / ˈrɪɡə / (BrE)
the quality of being extremely strict, severe, or harsh, especially in discipline or punishment
sternness or harsh strictness
british

Examples

The army training program is known for the rigour of its discipline and routines.
Parents sometimes criticized the school's rigour in enforcing its policies.
He was admired for his fairness, but some felt his rigour bordered on cruelty.

Synset Examples

severity of character

Related Terms

  • Synonym: severity - harshness or strictness, especially of treatment or rules
  • Antonym: leniency - the quality of being more merciful or tolerant

Etymology

From Latin rigor (stiffness, severity), via Old French rigour.

3. NOUN

/ ˈrɪɡər / (AmE), / ˈrɪɡə / (BrE)
severe or harsh conditions that are difficult to endure; hardship or harshness.
harsh or severe conditions; hardship
british

Examples

The rigour of the high-altitude climate challenged even the most experienced explorers.
During the long war, civilians endured the rigour of daily shortages and curfews.
Training for the marathon exposed him to the rigour of early morning runs in the winter.

Synset Examples

the asperity of northern winters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hardship - severe suffering or difficulty
  • Synonym: harshness - the quality of being unpleasantly rough or severe
  • Antonym: mildness - the quality of being not severe or harsh (contrasting property)

Etymology

From Latin rigor (stiffness, hardness), via Old French rigour.
