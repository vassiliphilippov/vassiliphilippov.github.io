X-ray

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛks.reɪ / (AmE), / ˈɛks.reɪ / (BrE)
a type of electromagnetic radiation of very short wavelength, produced when high-speed electrons strike a solid target, used especially in medical imaging and scientific research
type of electromagnetic radiation

Examples

The doctor ordered an X-ray to examine the patient's broken arm.
Airport security uses X-ray machines to scan luggage for prohibited items.
Recent advances have improved the clarity of medical X-ray images.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: gamma ray - a type of electromagnetic radiation with a shorter wavelength than X-rays
  • Degree Lower: ultraviolet - electromagnetic radiation with a longer wavelength than X-rays

Etymology

From 'X' (meaning 'unknown') + 'ray', coined by German physicist Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛksˌreɪ / (AmE), / ˈɛksˌreɪ / (BrE)
a photograph or image produced using X rays, typically to view the inside of the body for medical diagnosis
image produced using X rays
medical

Examples

The doctor reviewed the X-ray to check for any broken bones.
A dental X-ray can reveal cavities that are not visible during a routine exam.
After the accident, she had an X-ray taken of her wrist.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: radiograph - an image produced on a sensitive plate or film by X rays

Etymology

From X (unknown) and ray, as coined by Wilhelm Röntgen in 1895 to describe the mysterious new rays he discovered.

4. NOUN

/ ˈɛks ˌreɪ / (AmE), / ˈɛks ˌreɪ / (BrE)
a photographic image produced by the passage of X-rays through an object or body, used especially for medical examination and diagnosis
medical photographic image using X-rays
medical

Examples

The doctor examined the X-ray to check for any broken bones.
After my fall, I had an X-ray of my ankle at the hospital.
The X-ray revealed a small fracture that was not visible externally.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: radiograph - an image produced on a sensitive plate or film by X-rays, gamma rays, or similar radiation
  • Antonym: MRI - medical imaging using magnetic resonance

Etymology

From X (symbol for an unknown quantity) + ray, named by Wilhelm Conrad Röntgen in 1895 for the unknown type of radiation he discovered.
