cocoon

1. NOUN

/ kəˈkun / (AmE), / kəˈkuːn / (BrE)
a silky covering spun by the larvae of many insects to protect themselves during the pupal stage, or by spiders to protect their eggs
silky envelope spun by insect larvae or spiders

Examples

The silkworm spins a cocoon of fine silk around itself.
Spiders wrap their eggs in a protective cocoon to shield them from predators.
We found a cocoon hanging from a branch in the garden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chrysalis - the pupal stage of butterflies; often inside a cocoon

Etymology

From French 'cocon', from Occitan 'coucoun' (egg shell, cocoon).

2. VERB

/ kəˈkun / (AmE), / kəˈkuːn / (BrE)
to wrap or envelop (someone or something) protectively as if in a cocoon
wrap protectively as if in a cocoon

Examples

During the blizzard, she cocooned herself in blankets to stay warm.
The puppy cocooned in a pile of towels, safe and snug.
I like to cocoon on the couch with a good book when it rains.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to envelop - to surround or cover completely
  • Synonym: shield - to protect from danger or harm

Etymology

From French 'cocon', meaning "egg-case of insects", ultimately from Occitan.

3. VERB

/ kəˈkun / (AmE), / kəˈkuːn / (BrE)
to retreat into a protected, safe, or isolated state, often to avoid discomfort or danger
retreat into a safe or protected state

Examples

After a long, stressful week, she likes to cocoon at home with a good book.
During the pandemic, many people chose to cocoon instead of going out.
He cocooned himself in his apartment, avoiding all social contact.

Synset Examples

Families cocoon around the T.V. set most evenings
She loves to stay at home and cocoon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to withdraw - to move back or away, especially into privacy or safety
  • Synonym: to isolate - to separate oneself from others or the environment
  • Antonym: to socialize - to interact with others in a social setting

Etymology

From the noun 'cocoon', which comes from French cocon (silk case of an insect larva).
