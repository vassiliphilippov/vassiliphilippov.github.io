to accompany

1. VERB

/ əˈkʌmpəni / (AmE), / əˈkʌmpəni / (BrE)
to play a musical accompaniment for a singer or another musician
play music to support another performer

Examples

The pianist will accompany the soloist during the concert.
Maria loves to accompany her sister on the guitar while she sings.
Would you be able to accompany me at the recital next week?

Synset Examples

The orchestra could barely follow the frequent pitch changes of the soprano

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to provide musical backing for a main performer

Etymology

From Old French accompagner (to go with), from Latin ad- (to) + companio (companion).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01732234-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ əˈkʌmpəni / (AmE), / əˈkʌmpəni / (BrE)
to go somewhere with someone as a companion or escort
go with someone as a companion

Examples

May I accompany you to the museum this afternoon?
She decided to accompany her friend on the trip to Paris.
Children under 12 must accompany an adult when entering the library.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: escort - to go with someone to give protection or assistance
  • Antonym: to follow - to come after or move behind someone

Etymology

From Old French accompagner, from Late Latin *accompagnare*, from ad- (to) + companio (companion).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02722642-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ əˈkʌmpəni / (AmE), / əˈkʌmpəni / (BrE)
to be present or occur at the same time as something, or to exist together with something else
be present or occur together with

Examples

Loud thunder often accompanies lightning during a storm.
Fatigue frequently accompanies a high fever in many illnesses.
A sense of excitement accompanied the announcement of the new project.

Synset Examples

French fries come with the hamburger
heart attacks are accompanied by destruction of heart tissue
fish usually goes with white wine
this kind of vein accompanies certain arteries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to attend - to be present at; go with as an attendant or companion
  • Degree Lower: to follow - to come after or as a result of something

Etymology

From Old French accompagner, based on Latin ad- ‘to’ + companis ‘companion’.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02722040-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ əˈkʌmpəni / (AmE), / əˈkʌmpəni / (BrE)
to go somewhere with someone as a companion; to travel along with someone
go with someone as a companion

Examples

The nurse always accompanies the patient to her doctor's appointments.
Would you like me to accompany you to the airport?
He was accompanied by his sister on the trip.

Synset Examples

The nurse accompanied the old lady everywhere

Related Terms

  • Synonym: escort - to go with someone to protect or show respect
  • Antonym: to follow - to go or come after someone

Etymology

From Middle French accompagner, from Late Latin *accompagnare*, from ad- (to) + companion (companion).
