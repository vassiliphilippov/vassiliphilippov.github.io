Gasteromycetes

1. NOUN

/ ˌɡæstəroʊmaɪˈsiːtiːz / (AmE), / ˌɡastərəʊmʌɪˈsiːtiːz / (BrE)
a former class of fungi in which the spore-bearing structure (hymenium) is enclosed until after the spores have matured, including puffballs, earth stars, and stinkhorns
a group of fungi with enclosed spore structures
medical

Examples

Many members of the Gasteromycetes are found in temperate forests during the autumn season.
The Gasteromycetes include puffballs and earth stars, which release spores when disturbed.
Mycologists once classified all fungi with enclosed spore bodies under Gasteromycetes.

Etymology

From Greek 'gaster' (stomach) + 'mykēs' (fungus), referring to the stomach-like spore structure.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɡæstərəˈmaɪsiːts / (AmE), / ˌɡæstərəˈmaɪsiːtiːz / (BrE)
a former class of fungi in which the hymenium is enclosed until after the spores have matured, including puffballs, earth stars, and stinkhorns
former class of fungi (puffballs, stinkhorns)

Examples

Scientists studied the unique spore release mechanisms of Gasteromycetes in temperate woodlands.
Puffballs and stinkhorns are both classified as Gasteromycetes in traditional fungal taxonomy.
The characteristic feature of Gasteromycetes is their enclosed hymenium, which protects developing spores.

Etymology

From Greek gaster (stomach) + mykēs (fungus), referring to the enclosed spore-bearing structure.
