opacity

1. NOUN

/ oʊˈpæs.ə.ti / (AmE), / əʊˈpæs.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the quality or state of not allowing electromagnetic radiation, such as light, to pass through
impenetrability to radiation or light

Examples

The opacity of the material prevents X-rays from passing through.
Scientists measured the opacity of the atmosphere to determine how much sunlight reaches the surface.
In astrophysics, opacity plays an important role in understanding the behavior of stars.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transparency - the property of allowing light or radiation to pass through
  • Degree Lower: translucency - the property of allowing some light to pass through but diffusing it

Etymology

From Latin 'opacitas', from 'opacus' (shaded, dark).

2. NOUN

/ oʊˈpæsəti / (AmE), / əʊˈpæsɪti / (BrE)
the quality of being difficult to understand because of unclear or obscure meaning
obscurity or incomprehensibility of meaning

Examples

The opacity of the legal document made it almost impossible to determine its true meaning.
Many critics complained about the opacity of the novel's language and symbolism.
Her explanation was marked by such opacity that few listeners could follow her logic.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand
  • Synonym: obscurity - the state of being unclear or difficult to understand

Etymology

From Latin 'opacitas', from 'opacus' (shaded, dark).

3. NOUN

/ oʊˈpæsəti / (AmE), / əʊˈpæsɪti / (BrE)
the quality or degree to which something is opaque, especially in reducing the passage of light
degree to which something is opaque

Examples

The opacity of the frosted glass prevented us from seeing into the room.
Medical imaging uses dye to increase the opacity of blood vessels on an X-ray.
The artist adjusted the opacity of each layer in the digital painting.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transparency - the quality of being able to see through; opposite of opacity

Etymology

From Latin opacitas (darkness), from opacus (shady).
