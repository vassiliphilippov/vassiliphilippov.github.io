sophistic

1. ADJ

/ səˈfɪstɪk / (AmE), / səˈfɪstɪk / (BrE)
Relating to sophists or to the use of fallacious or deceptive reasoning, especially in arguments.
relating to sophists or deceptive reasoning

Examples

The philosopher criticized the sophistic arguments for their deceptive logic.
Some debates descend into sophistic reasoning when clarity is lost.
He dismissed her objections as mere sophistic tricks rather than valid criticism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sophistical - relating to sophistry or sophists; using subtle or fallacious reasoning

Etymology

From Latin sophisticus, from Greek sophistikos (of or pertaining to a sophist).

2. ADJ

/ səˈfɪstɪk / (AmE), / səˈfɪstɪk / (BrE)
Seemingly plausible but actually fallacious or misleading, especially in reference to reasoning or arguments.
misleadingly plausible (esp. of arguments)

Examples

The lawyer's sophistic arguments swayed the jury, despite their misleading logic.
Many political debates are filled with sophistic reasoning designed to confuse the public.
She quickly saw through his sophistic explanations and demanded the truth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sophistical - characterized by clever but false reasoning
  • Synonym: specious - superficially plausible but actually wrong
  • Antonym: valid - logically sound and acceptable

Etymology

From Greek 'sophistikos', relating to the Sophists, who were known for their use of plausible but deceptive reasoning.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02510685-s
Synset POS: s
