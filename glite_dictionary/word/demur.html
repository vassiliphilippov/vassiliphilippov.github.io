demur

1. VERB

/ dɪˈmɝː / (AmE), / dɪˈmjʊə / (BrE)
(law) to raise an objection or show reluctance, especially by formally entering a demurrer in a court of law.
raise a formal legal objection
legal

Examples

The defense lawyer decided to demur, arguing that the complaint lacked sufficient grounds.
If you wish to challenge the plaintiff's claim, you must demur before the trial proceeds.
The attorney chose not to demur at this stage of the hearing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: object - to express or feel disapproval or opposition

Etymology

From Latin *demorari* meaning 'to delay'.

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈmɜr / (AmE), / dɪˈmɜː / (BrE)
(Law) A formal objection raised in response to an opponent's pleading, often arguing that even if the facts alleged are true, they do not support a legal claim.
(law) formal objection to a pleading
legal

Examples

Counsel entered a demur to the plaintiff's complaint, challenging its legal sufficiency.
The judge overruled the demur and allowed the case to proceed to trial.
Filing a demur can delay proceedings if there are substantive questions about the pleadings.

Etymology

From Old French *demorer* (to delay), from Latin *demorari* (to delay), related to 'morari' (to delay).

3. VERB

/ dɪˈmɝ / (AmE), / dɪˈmjʊə / (BrE)
to raise objections or show reluctance, especially on the grounds of conscience or principle
raise objections or show reluctance

Examples

She demurred politely when asked to lead the meeting.
Although he wanted to help, he demurred at the last minute.
Several board members demurred when the proposal was put to a vote.

Synset Examples

he demurred at my suggestion to work on Saturday

Related Terms

  • Synonym: object - to express or feel disapproval, opposition, or dissent
  • Antonym: to agree - to have the same opinion or to consent

Etymology

From Latin 'demorari' (to delay), from 'de-' (away, from) + 'morari' (to delay).
