to extirpate

1. VERB

/ ˈɛk.stɚˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛk.stɪˌpeɪt / (BrE)
to remove (an organ or tissue) entirely by surgery
surgically remove (an organ or tissue)
medical

Examples

The surgeon decided to extirpate the tumor to prevent its spread.
After the diagnosis, the medical team extirpated the diseased kidney.
In rare cases, doctors may need to extirpate an organ to save the patient's life.

Etymology

From Latin extirpāre (to root out, destroy), from ex- (out) + stirps (root).

2. VERB

/ ˈɛk.stɚˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛk.stɪˌpeɪt / (BrE)
to remove or destroy completely; to pull up by the roots
remove or destroy completely

Examples

The invasive weeds were extirpated from the field by hand.
Efforts to extirpate the disease have been largely successful.
The government launched a campaign to extirpate corruption from public offices.

Synset Examples

uproot the vine that has spread all over the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to uproot - remove completely by pulling up by the roots
  • Synonym: to eradicate - destroy completely, especially something harmful

Etymology

From Latin extirpāre, from ex- (out) + stirps (root, stock).

3. VERB

/ ˈɛk.stɚˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛk.stə.peɪt / (BrE)
to destroy or remove completely; to eradicate something thoroughly, especially by eliminating the root cause or source
destroy or remove completely

Examples

The government took several measures to extirpate organized crime from the region.
Conservationists are working to extirpate invasive species from the national park.
The dictator attempted to extirpate all traces of opposition from the country.

Synset Examples

the vestiges of political democracy were soon uprooted
root out corruption

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eradicate - destroy or put an end to something completely

Etymology

From Latin 'extirpatus', past participle of 'extirpare' (to root out), from 'ex-' (out) + 'stirps' (root, stem).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
