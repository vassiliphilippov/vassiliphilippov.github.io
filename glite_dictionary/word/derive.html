to derive

1. VERB

/ dəˈraɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈraɪv / (BrE)
to have a specified origin; to come from or be descended from a particular source, often used with reference to ancestry or lineage
to come from a source, esp. ancestry

Examples

Her family derives from Scottish nobility on her mother's side.
The language derives from an ancient Indo-European root.
Many modern traditions derive from older religious customs.

Synset Examples

She was descended from an old Italian noble family
he comes from humble origins

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to descend - to be born from a particular ancestor
  • Synonym: to originate - to have a specified beginning or source

Etymology

From Latin 'derivare' (to draw off, divert), from 'de-' (down, away) + 'rivus' (stream).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02743062-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈraɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈraɪv / (BrE)
to obtain something from a specified source or as a result of a process
obtain from a source

Examples

She managed to derive great satisfaction from helping others.
Many medicines are derived from plants found in the rainforest.
Children often derive their attitudes from their parents.

Synset Examples

derive pleasure from one's garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to obtain - to get or acquire something
  • Synonym: to receive - to be given or presented with something
  • Antonym: to lose - to be deprived of or cease to have something

Etymology

From Latin derivare (to draw off), from de- (from) + rivus (stream).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02297086-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dəˈraɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈraɪv / (BrE)
to obtain or reach (a result or conclusion) by reasoning or deduction
obtain by reasoning; deduce

Examples

From these axioms, we can derive the fundamental laws of motion.
She managed to derive the answer after several complex calculations.
Philosophers often derive ethical principles from general theories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deduce - reach a conclusion by reasoning

Etymology

From Latin derivare ‘to draw off (water), derive’, from de- ‘from’ + rivus ‘stream’.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00638236-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪˈraɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈraɪv / (BrE)
(of something) to develop or evolve from a latent or potential state; to emerge or be formed from something that exists in a non-obvious or undeveloped way.
develop or evolve from a potential state

Examples

The plant hormone is thought to derive from a compound that is inactive in the early stages.
Their theory proposes that language skills derive from innate neural structures present at birth.
Modern ecosystems derive largely from changes that occurred after the last Ice Age.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evolve - to develop gradually, especially from a simple to a more complex form
  • Synonym: to originate - to have a specified beginning or origin

Etymology

From Latin *derivare* (to draw off, divert).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00252501-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ dɪˈraɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈraɪv / (BrE)
to obtain or receive something from a specified source
obtain from a source

Examples

Many English words derive from Latin roots.
His confidence derives from years of experience.
The idea derived from a casual conversation with a friend.

Synset Examples

The present name derives from an older form

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to originate - to have a specified beginning; to come from a source
  • Synonym: to descend - to come down or be derived from a source

Etymology

From Latin derivare (to lead or draw off, to derive), from de- (down, away) + rivus (stream).
