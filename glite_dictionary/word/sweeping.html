sweeping

1. NOUN

/ ˈswipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈswiːpɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of cleaning a floor or surface with a broom or similar brush
the act of cleaning with a broom

Examples

The sweeping of the kitchen takes only a few minutes each morning.
She finished the sweeping before the guests arrived.
Daily sweeping keeps the classroom tidy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sweep - to clean something using a broom
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. ADJ

/ ˈswipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈswiːpɪŋ / (BrE)
covering or affecting a wide area; moving over or including a broad range
covering a wide area or range

Examples

The new law is expected to have sweeping effects on the entire industry.
She made a sweeping gesture that took in the whole room.
His sweeping generalizations often miss important details.

Synset Examples

a sweeping glance
a wide-sweeping view of the river

Related Terms

  • Synonym: broad - covering a large area or scope
  • Antonym: narrow - covering a small area or limited range

Etymology

Derived from the verb 'sweep,' originally from Old English 'swāpan' (to sweep, rush, move swiftly).

3. ADJ

/ ˈswipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈswiːpɪŋ / (BrE)
(of statements or actions) affecting many things or people and tending to ignore individual differences; too general or extensive.
extensive and ignoring differences

Examples

Many critics dismissed his theory as a sweeping generalization lacking nuance.
The government announced sweeping reforms that would impact all sectors of society.
Avoid making sweeping statements that ignore important differences between groups.

Synset Examples

sweeping generalizations
wholesale destruction

Related Terms

  • Synonym: general - not specific; including or affecting all or most people, things, or places
  • Antonym: specific - clearly defined or identified; precise; opposite of general

Etymology

Present participle of 'sweep', from Middle English 'swepen' (to sweep).
