buckle

1. NOUN

/ ˈbʌkəl / (AmE), / ˈbʌkəl / (BrE)
A shape that has become distorted by twisting or folding, typically referring to a structural deformation.
a distorted shape caused by twisting or folding

Examples

The metal beam developed a small buckle under the heavy load.
After the car accident, there was a visible buckle in the door frame.
The ship’s hull showed a buckle where it struck the rock.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warp - a twist or distortion in something that was once flat or straight
  • Synonym: bend - a curve or angle formed by something bending
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13908393-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈbʌk.əl / (AmE), / ˈbʌk.əl / (BrE)
to fasten or secure (something) with a buckle or buckles
to fasten with a buckle

Examples

She quickly buckled her seatbelt before the car started moving.
Make sure you buckle your shoes tightly so they don’t come off.
He buckled the straps on his backpack and set out for his hike.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unbuckle - to undo or release a buckle

Etymology

From Old French *bocle* (buckle, boss of a shield), from Latin *buccula* (cheek strap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01551072-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbʌk.əl / (AmE), / ˈbʌk.əl / (BrE)
to bend or collapse under pressure, heat, or force; to give way, especially by folding
to collapse or bend under pressure

Examples

The bridge began to buckle under the intense heat of the fire.
When faced with immense stress, she felt as if her resolve would buckle.
The heavy snow caused the roof to buckle and eventually collapse.

Synset Examples

His knees buckled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: collapse - to fall down or give way suddenly
  • Synonym: yield - to give way under pressure
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01281789-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ ˈbʌk.əl / (AmE), / ˈbʌk.əl / (BrE)
A clasp or fastener, typically made of metal or plastic, used to join together the ends of a belt, strap, or similar item, often featuring a movable prong.
belt or strap fastener

Examples

He adjusted the buckle on his belt before stepping on stage.
The strap wouldn't stay tight because the buckle was broken.
Her new shoes have a shiny silver buckle on each strap.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clasp - a device with interlocking parts used for fastening things together

Etymology

From Old French *bocle* (buckle, boss), from Latin *buccula* (cheek strap of a helmet).

5. VERB

/ ˈbʌkəl / (AmE), / ˈbʌkəl / (BrE)
to bend or collapse out of shape, especially under pressure, heat, or strain
bend or give way under pressure or heat

Examples

The intense heat caused the railroad tracks to buckle.
The flimsy bridge started to buckle under the weight of the heavy truck.
With age, the old pipes may buckle during extreme temperature changes.

Synset Examples

The highway buckled during the heat wave

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warp - to become bent or twisted out of shape
  • Degree Higher: collapse - to fall down or cave in suddenly

Etymology

From Old French bocler (to arch, curve), from boucle (buckle), ultimately from Latin buccula (cheek strap).
