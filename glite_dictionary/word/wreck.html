wreck

1. NOUN

/ rɛk / (AmE), / rɛk / (BrE)
a ship that has been destroyed, sunk, or badly damaged at sea
destroyed or sunken ship

Examples

Divers explored the coral growing around the wreck off the coast.
The wreck was discovered by fishermen after a storm.
Treasure hunters hoped to find gold in the old Spanish wreck.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shipwreck - destruction or loss of a ship at sea, or the remains thereof

Etymology

From Old English *wrecan* (to drive out, punish), from Proto-Germanic *wrekaną*, related to driving or forcing out (in reference to ships being driven ashore).

2. NOUN

/ rɛk / (AmE), / rɛk / (BrE)
a person or thing that is badly damaged, ruined, or in a state of disrepair
damaged person or thing

Examples

The abandoned car was a total wreck after years in the junkyard.
After the stressful week, he felt like an emotional wreck.
The hurricane left the coastal town a complete wreck.

Synset Examples

the house was a wreck when they bought it
thanks to that quack I am a human wreck

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ruin - the physical destruction or disintegration of something
  • Synonym: disaster - a sudden event causing great damage or loss
  • Antonym: treasure - something of great value, often found in good condition

Etymology

From Middle English *wrek*, from Old Norse *wrek* (wreckage, flotsam).

3. NOUN

/ rɛk / (AmE), / rɛk / (BrE)
a serious accident, especially one involving vehicles such as cars, trains, or planes
serious vehicle accident

Examples

The highway was closed for hours after the massive wreck involving five cars.
He suffered minor injuries in yesterday's train wreck but is expected to recover fully.
Investigators arrived at the scene of the plane wreck early this morning.

Synset Examples

they are still investigating the crash of the TWA plane

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crash - a sudden and violent accident, especially between vehicles
  • Synonym: collision - an event in which two or more objects hit each other violently

Etymology

From Old English *wrecan* (to drive out, punish); modern meaning influenced by nautical use referring to shipwrecks.

4. NOUN

/ rɛk / (AmE), / rɛk / (BrE)
an accident in which a ship is destroyed or badly damaged at sea
ship destroyed in an accident at sea

Examples

The storm caused a terrible wreck off the rocky coast.
Divers explored the wreck of a 19th-century sailing ship at the bottom of the bay.
After the wreck, survivors clung to debris awaiting rescue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shipwreck - the destruction or loss of a ship at sea

Etymology

From Middle English wrek, from Old Norse *wrek* (something driven ashore), related to the verb 'to wreck'.

5. VERB

/ rɛk / (AmE), / rɛk / (BrE)
to seriously damage or destroy something, especially by smashing or breaking it forcefully
to destroy or damage severely

Examples

The storm wrecked hundreds of homes along the coast.
He accidentally wrecked his bike by riding into a tree.
Careless driving can easily wreck a brand-new car.

Synset Examples

The kid busted up the car

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to destroy - to put an end to the existence of something by damaging or attacking it
  • Synonym: ruin - to harm something so badly that it loses all value, enjoyment, or usefulness
  • Antonym: repair - to fix or mend something that is damaged or broken

Etymology

From Old English *wrecan* (‘to drive, pursue, avenge’) and Middle English *wrec* (‘shipwreck’).
