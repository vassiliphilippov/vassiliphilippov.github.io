separatism

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɛpɚəˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsɛpərətɪzəm / (BrE)
The advocacy or practice of keeping two groups, institutions, or principles—such as church and state—strictly separate from each other.
doctrine of complete separation (e.g. church and state)

Examples

Many supporters of separatism argue for a strict division between religious organizations and government.
The country's constitution is based on the principle of separatism between church and state.
Debates about separatism frequently arise whenever religious influence is seen in public education policies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separationism - the belief in keeping things separate, especially church and state

Etymology

From French séparatisme, from séparer (to separate).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɛpəˌrætɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsepərətɪzəm / (BrE)
the belief in or practice of people separating from a larger group, country, or organization, often for reasons of ethnicity, religion, or politics.
the policy or practice of separation from a larger group

Examples

Economic disparities fueled separatism among several regions of the country.
Religious separatism sometimes leads to the formation of new communities or schools.
Government leaders debated how to manage rising separatism within the border provinces.

Synset Examples

separatism is a serious problem in Quebec
demands for some form of separatism on grounds of religion have been perceived as a threat to mainstream education

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secession - the action of formally withdrawing from a group or organization
  • Antonym: integration - the action of incorporating into a whole, especially in social or political contexts

Etymology

From French séparatisme, from séparer (to separate), based on Latin separare.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɛp.ə.rəˌtɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈsɛp.ər.ə.tɪz.əm / (BrE)
The policy or practice of maintaining separate institutions or facilities for different groups, especially for minority groups within a society.
the policy or practice of separation for minority groups

Examples

The government was criticized for its support of separatism in the education system.
Civil rights activists campaigned against institutionalized separatism in public facilities.
Modern societies generally reject separatism and seek greater social integration.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: integration - the process of combining or incorporating different groups into a unified whole

Etymology

From French séparatisme, from séparer (to separate).
