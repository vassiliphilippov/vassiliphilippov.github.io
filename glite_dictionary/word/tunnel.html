tunnel

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
an underground or underwater passageway through or under something, especially for trains, cars, or pedestrians
underground passageway

Examples

The new tunnel under the river makes commuting much faster.
Engineers are working to reinforce the walls of the old railway tunnel.
We drove through a long tunnel carved into the mountain.

Synset Examples

the tunnel reduced congestion at that intersection

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bridge - a structure built to span a physical obstacle for passage
  • Synonym: underpass - a road or footpath passing under another road or railway

Etymology

From Old French 'tonnelle' (funnel, tube), diminutive of 'tonne' (cask, tun)

2. NOUN

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
a passage or hole dug by an animal, usually for shelter or movement underground
animal's underground passage or hole

Examples

The rabbit quickly disappeared into its tunnel beneath the hedgerow.
Earthworms create a network of tunnels that help aerate the soil.
A fox used the old badger tunnel as a shelter from predators.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burrow - a hole or tunnel dug by a small animal for habitation

Etymology

From Old French 'tonnel' (funnel, tube), ultimately from Latin 'tubinus' (tube).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09252948-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
to move through or create a passage by digging, especially underground or through an obstruction
move or dig through as by creating a passage

Examples

The moles tunnel under our garden every spring.
Engineers tunnel through the mountains to build new railways.
Workers had to tunnel for weeks before reaching the buried chamber.

Synset Examples

burrow through the forest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burrow - to make a hole or tunnel, especially for habitation or protection

Etymology

From earlier noun 'tunnel', from Old French 'tonnelle' (funnel, tube), from Old Provençal 'tonel' (wine cask).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02045912-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
to make or force a way through or under something, typically by digging or boring
force a way through, usually by digging

Examples

Rescuers tunneled through the debris to reach the trapped survivors.
The moles have tunnelled under the garden all spring.
Engineers managed to tunnel beneath the river to lay new cables.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bore - to make a hole or passage, especially with a tool or by digging

Etymology

From French *tonnel* (subterranean passage), from Old French *tonnelle* (arched passage).

Source: WordNet
