bifurcate

1. ADJ

/ ˈbaɪ.fər.keɪt / (AmE), / ˈbɪ.fə.keɪt / (BrE)
divided into two parts or branches; forked
divided into two branches

Examples

The river became bifurcate as it flowed around the island.
The plant's root system was bifurcate, spreading in two distinct directions.
You could tell the species by its bifurcate antennae.

Synset Examples

the biramous appendages of an arthropod
long branched hairs on its legs on which pollen collects
a forked river
a forked tail
forked lightning
horseradish grown in poor soil may develop prongy roots

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forked - divided into two or more branches
  • Antonym: unbranched - not divided into branches or forks

Etymology

From Latin bifurcatus, past participle of bifurcare (to divide into two forks), from bis (twice) + furca (fork).

2. VERB

/ ˈbaɪ.fɚ.keɪt / (AmE), / ˈbɪ.fə.keɪt / (BrE)
to divide into two parts or branches; to split into two separate paths
divide into two branches

Examples

At the river delta, the main channel bifurcates into two smaller streams.
The discussion bifurcated into two competing viewpoints during the meeting.
If the path bifurcates ahead, take the left fork for the scenic route.

Synset Examples

The road bifurcated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: split - to separate or be divided into parts
  • Antonym: to merge - to combine or join together to form one

Etymology

From Medieval Latin bifurcatus, from Latin bi- (two) + furca (fork).

3. VERB

/ ˈbaɪ.fɚˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈbɪ.fɜː.keɪt / (BrE)
to split or divide into two branches or parts
divide into two parts

Examples

The river will bifurcate into two distinct streams at this point.
Urban planners decided to bifurcate the main road to ease traffic congestion.
If left unchecked, the path may bifurcate and confuse hikers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: split - to break or divide into parts, especially along a natural line
  • Antonym: to merge - to combine or join together into a single thing

Etymology

From Medieval Latin bifurcatus, past participle of bifurcare (to divide into two forks), from Latin bi- (two) + furca (fork).
