to burke

1. VERB

/ bɝːk / (AmE), / bɜːk / (BrE)
to murder (someone) in a way that leaves no visible mark; to suffocate or kill without leaving evidence of violence, especially by smothering
to murder without visible marks

Examples

The criminal attempted to burke his victim to avoid leaving any evidence behind.
Some historians believe the gang would burke unsuspecting lodgers in their sleep.
It's a grim story about two men who set out to burke people for profit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to smother - to kill by preventing breath, often by suffocation

Etymology

From the name of William Burke (1792–1829), who killed victims in this manner in Edinburgh, Scotland.

2. VERB

/ bɜrk / (AmE), / bɜːk / (BrE)
to suppress, silence, or get rid of something quietly or indirectly, especially to avoid discussion or attention
suppress or silence (an issue, etc.)

Examples

The committee decided to burke the report to avoid public controversy.
Some managers attempt to burke complaints rather than address them directly.
It was clear that they tried to burke the issue during the meeting.

Synset Examples

burke an issue

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to forcibly put an end to; to subdue
  • Synonym: to conceal - to keep from sight; to hide
  • Antonym: address - to deal with or discuss openly

Etymology

From the name of William Burke, a 19th-century murderer who suffocated his victims, giving rise to the sense of suppressing or quietly eliminating.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00463807-v
Synset POS: v
