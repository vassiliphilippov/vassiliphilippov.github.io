anthrax

1. NOUN

/ ˈænˌθræks / (AmE), / ˈænθræks / (BrE)
a highly infectious disease affecting animals, especially cattle and sheep, which can also be transmitted to humans
infectious disease (affects animals, can infect humans)
medical

Examples

Authorities confirmed an outbreak of anthrax among livestock in the rural region.
The laboratory is equipped to handle dangerous pathogens, including anthrax spores.
Farmers were vaccinated to reduce the risk of anthrax transmission to humans.

Etymology

From Greek anthrax (coal), probably referring to the black skin lesions seen in infection.

2. NOUN

/ ˈænˌθræks / (AmE), / ˈæn.θræks / (BrE)
a serious, often fatal infectious disease caused by the bacterium Bacillus anthracis, usually affecting livestock and occasionally humans
infectious disease caused by Bacillus anthracis
medical

Examples

There was an outbreak of anthrax among the livestock in the region.
Inhalation of anthrax spores can be fatal if not treated promptly.
Doctors quickly isolated the patient after diagnosing a case of anthrax.

Etymology

From Greek anthrax (coal), referring to the black lesions characteristic of the disease.
