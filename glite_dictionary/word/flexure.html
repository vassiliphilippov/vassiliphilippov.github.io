flexure

1. NOUN

/ ˈflɛk.ʃɚ / (AmE), / ˈflɛk.ʃə / (BrE)
the act of bending a joint, especially so that the angle between the bones of a limb is reduced
bending of a joint or limb
medical

Examples

The flexure of the elbow allows the hand to move closer to the shoulder.
Arthritis can limit the flexure of affected joints, causing discomfort.
During physical therapy, exercises often focus on improving flexure in the knees and hips.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extension - straightening a limb at a joint so that the angle is increased

Etymology

From Latin flexura (a bending), from flectere (to bend).

2. NOUN

/ ˈflɛkʃər / (AmE), / ˈflɛkʃə / (BrE)
the state or condition of being bent, especially the bending of a limb or other structure in the body
the state of being bent or flexed

Examples

The flexure of the elbow allows the arm to move smoothly during exercise.
Studying the flexure of the spine can help diagnose certain back conditions.
The doctor observed the flexure at the patient's knee during the physical examination.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extension - the straightening of a joint or limb

Etymology

From Latin 'flexura' (a bending), from 'flectere' (to bend).

3. NOUN

/ ˈflɛk.ʃɚ / (AmE), / ˈflɛk.ʃə / (BrE)
a bend, fold, or curve, especially one made by bending or folding something; in anatomy, a curved part of an organ or structure.
a bend or fold; especially in anatomy

Examples

The colon contains a sharp flexure known as the splenic flexure.
Engineers designed the bridge to accommodate natural flexure caused by temperature changes.
A visible flexure appeared in the thin metal rod after repeated stress.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bend - a curve or angle formed by bending
  • Synonym: fold - a crease or plication created by doubling over

Etymology

From Latin 'flexura' meaning 'a bending, a turning', from 'flectere' (to bend).
