bridgehead

1. NOUN

/ ˈbrɪdʒˌhɛd / (AmE), / ˈbrɪdʒˌhɛd / (BrE)
a fortified position at the end of a bridge nearest to the enemy, used as a defensive stronghold during military operations
military defensive position at a bridge

Examples

The troops quickly established a bridgehead before advancing further into enemy territory.
Securing the bridgehead was essential to allow reinforcements to cross safely.
The general ordered his forces to hold the bridgehead at any cost.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beachhead - a defended position on a beach taken from the enemy

Etymology

Formed from 'bridge' + 'head'; popularized in military use during the 19th and 20th centuries.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbrɪdʒˌhɛd / (AmE), / ˈbrɪdʒˌhɛd / (BrE)
an area in enemy territory that has been captured and is held to allow the arrival of additional troops or equipment
secured position in enemy territory

Examples

The army established a bridgehead to prepare for the next phase of the operation.
Paratroopers secured a bridgehead across the river during the early hours of the battle.
Defending the bridgehead was crucial to maintaining supply lines.

Synset Examples

an attempt to secure a bridgehead behind enemy lines
the only foothold left for British troops in Europe was Gibraltar

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foothold - a secure position from which further progress can be made
  • Synonym: beachhead - a defended position on a beach, taken to begin an invasion

Etymology

Formed from 'bridge' + 'head', originally referring to a position secured at the end of a bridge.
