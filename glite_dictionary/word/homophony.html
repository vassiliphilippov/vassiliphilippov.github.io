homophony

1. NOUN

/ həˈmɑːfəni / (AmE), / həˈmɒfəni / (BrE)
Music in which one melody is dominant and all accompanying parts move together harmonically (in a homophonic style).
musical texture with one dominant voice

Examples

The choir performed a piece rich in homophony, allowing the soprano line to shine.
Many popular songs favor homophony over complex counterpoint.
Baroque composers often balanced homophony and polyphony within their choral works.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: polyphony - musical texture with two or more independent melodies
  • Antonym: monophony - musical texture with a single melodic line without harmony

Etymology

From Greek homophōnia, from homos (same) + phōnē (sound, voice).

2. NOUN

/ hoʊˈmɑːfəni / (AmE), / həˈmɒfəni / (BrE)
The state of two or more words having the same pronunciation but different meanings and origins.
same pronunciation for different words

Examples

The homophony of 'knight' and 'night' often amuses students learning English.
French contains much homophony, so words like 'ver' and 'vers' sound the same.
Poets may use homophony to create puns and play with the meaning of words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: homonym - a word that is spelled or pronounced like another word but has a different meaning
  • Synonym: homograph - a word spelled the same as another but with different meaning and sometimes pronunciation
  • Degree Lower: polysemy - the coexistence of many possible meanings for a word or phrase

Etymology

From Greek homophōnos, from homos (same) + phōnē (sound).
