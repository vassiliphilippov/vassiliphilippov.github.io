cirrus

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪr.əs / (AmE), / ˈsɪr.əs / (BrE)
A slender, threadlike or coiled structure, such as a plant tendril or an appendage of an animal, often used for attachment or sensory functions.
slender, coiled or threadlike structure

Examples

The barnacle attaches itself to rocks using a cirrus to anchor securely.
Many climbing plants have a specialized cirrus that helps them hold onto supports.
Under the microscope, the worm's tiny cirrus was clearly visible as a coiled appendage.

Etymology

From Latin 'cirrus' meaning 'curl' or 'tuft'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪr.əs / (AmE), / ˈsɪr.əs / (BrE)
a slender, flexible appendage found on certain animals such as barnacles, crinoids, or insects, often serving a tactile function
slender, flexible animal appendage

Examples

The barnacle uses its cirri to filter food particles from the water.
A crinoid extends each cirrus to anchor itself to rocks on the ocean floor.
Some insects have a sensitive cirrus that helps them feel their surroundings.

Etymology

From Latin *cirrus* (a curl, tuft, or tendril).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɪr.əs / (AmE), / ˈsɪr.əs / (BrE)
A type of thin, wispy white cloud that forms at high altitudes, typically composed of ice crystals.
thin, wispy high-altitude cloud

Examples

On clear days, you can sometimes see cirrus clouds far above the other clouds.
The jet left a trail that soon turned into a thin strip of cirrus.
Meteorologists predict fair weather when only cirrus clouds are present in the sky.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cumulus - a dense, fluffy cloud with a flat base
  • Antonym: stratus - a low, gray cloud layer covering the sky

Etymology

From Latin 'cirrus' meaning 'curl', 'fringe', or 'lock of hair', referring to its wispy appearance.
