spirant

1. ADJ

/ ˈspaɪrənt / (AmE), / ˈspaɪərənt / (BrE)
(of a speech sound) produced by forcing air through a narrow channel, leading to audible friction; fricative.
fricative speech sound

Examples

In English, the spirant sounds include 'f', 'v', 's', and 'z'.
Some children initially replace spirant consonants with stops in early speech development.
The transition from a stop to a spirant is called spirantization in phonology.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fricative - a speech sound produced by forcing air through a narrow channel—synonymous with 'spirant' in phonetics

Etymology

From Latin 'spirant-', present participle of 'spirare' (to breathe).

2. NOUN

/ ˈspaɪrənt / (AmE), / ˈspaɪrənt / (BrE)
a consonant sound produced by breath forcing its way through a narrow opening in the vocal tract; a fricative
a type of fricative consonant

Examples

The sound of 'f' in 'fun' is a classic example of a spirant.
Linguists classify both 's' and 'z' as spirant consonants.
Some languages have more spirant sounds than others, making pronunciation challenging for learners.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fricative - a consonant produced by forcing air through a narrow passage (such as 'f' or 's')

Etymology

From Late Latin 'spirant-', present participle of 'spirare' (to breathe).
