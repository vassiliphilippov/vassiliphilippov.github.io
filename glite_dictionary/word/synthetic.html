synthetic

1. ADJ

/ sɪnˈθɛtɪk / (AmE), / sɪnˈθɛtɪk / (BrE)
(of a language) characterized by the systematic combining of root and modifying elements into single words, often resulting in words that express several grammatical meanings at once.
characterized by combining elements into single words (linguistics)

Examples

Finnish is considered a highly synthetic language due to its complex word formation.
Many indigenous American languages are more synthetic than English, expressing more information within single words.
In linguistic typology, synthetic languages contrast with analytic languages like Chinese.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: analytic - (of a language) tending to use separate words rather than inflection

Etymology

From Greek sunthetikos ('skilled in putting together'), from suntithenai ('to put together').

Synset POS: a

5. ADJ

/ sɪnˈθɛtɪk / (AmE), / sɪnˈθɛtɪk / (BrE)
(of a proposition) having its truth or falsity dependent on observation or factual evidence, rather than purely on meanings of words or logic.
(logic) empirical proposition; not true by definition

Examples

The statement 'all bachelors are unmarried' is analytic, while 'all bachelors are unhappy' is a synthetic claim.
Logical positivists distinguished between analytic and synthetic propositions in their philosophy.
To verify a synthetic statement, one must consult empirical facts, not just meanings.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: analytic - (of a proposition) true by virtue of meaning or logic alone

Etymology

From Greek sunthetikos (‘skilled in putting together’), from suntithenai (‘to put together’).
