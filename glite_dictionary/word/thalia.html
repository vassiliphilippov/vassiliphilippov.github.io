Thalia

1. PROPN

/ ˈθeɪliə / (AmE), / ˈθeɪliə / (BrE)
In Greek mythology, the Muse of comedy and pastoral poetry.
Muse of comedy and pastoral poetry

Examples

According to ancient Greek myth, Thalia inspired poets to write joyful and humorous verses.
A statue of Thalia can be found in the city’s main art museum, holding a comic mask.
In literature, Thalia is often depicted surrounded by scenes of laughter and pastoral life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: muse - one of the nine goddesses of the arts in Greek mythology
  • Degree Lower: Clio - the Muse of history in Greek mythology
  • Degree Higher: Melpomene - the Muse of tragedy in Greek mythology

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Θάλεια (Tháleia), meaning 'the joyous' or 'abundance'.

2. PROPN

/ ˈθeɪliə / (AmE), / ˈθeɪlɪə / (BrE)
In Greek mythology, one of the three Graces, known as the one who blossoms or brings prosperity.
one of the three Graces in Greek mythology

Examples

Many classical paintings depict Thalia alongside her sisters, Aglaea and Euphrosyne.
As one of the three Graces, Thalia symbolized blossoming and abundance in Greek myth.
Poets often invoked Thalia as a muse of fertility and festivity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Aglaea - one of the three Graces, representing beauty and splendor
  • Synonym: Euphrosyne - one of the three Graces, representing joy and mirth

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Θάλεια (*Tháleia*), meaning 'to blossom' or 'bloom.'
