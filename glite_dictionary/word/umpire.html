umpire

1. NOUN

/ ˈʌm.paɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈʌm.paɪ.ə / (BrE)
a person who is appointed to judge and make decisions in a dispute, especially in sports or legal cases
official judge or decision-maker

Examples

The umpire made a controversial decision that determined the outcome of the match.
As an umpire, she remained impartial throughout the heated arbitration.
They agreed to let an experienced umpire resolve the complex business dispute.

Synset Examples

the critic was considered to be an arbiter of modern literature
the arbitrator's authority derived from the consent of the disputants
an umpire was appointed to settle the tax case

Related Terms

  • Synonym: arbitrator - person appointed to settle a dispute
  • Synonym: referee - official supervising a game, especially in sports

Etymology

From Old French 'nonper' meaning 'not equal', later influenced by Latin 'impar' (unequal).

2. NOUN

/ ˈʌm.paɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈʌm.paɪə / (BrE)
an official who watches a game, especially baseball, and makes sure that the rules are followed and makes decisions on plays
official at a sports game

Examples

The umpire called the runner out at home plate.
Each umpire wears a protective mask and chest guard during a baseball game.
The crowd booed when the umpire made a controversial decision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: referee - an official who supervises a game and enforces the rules, especially in sports like football and basketball

Etymology

From Middle English *oumpere*, from Old French *nonper* (odd person, arbiter).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10755641-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈʌmˌpaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈʌmpaɪə / (BrE)
to act as an umpire or referee in a sports competition, making judgments on the rules of the game
act as a referee in sport

Examples

It was his turn to umpire the baseball game this weekend.
She often umpires youth tennis matches in her community.
After years of playing, Brian decided to start umpiring local softball leagues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: referee - to officiate as a referee in a game or match

Etymology

From Middle English *umpere*, from Old French *nonper* (not equal, arbiter), from Latin *non* (not) + *par* (equal).
