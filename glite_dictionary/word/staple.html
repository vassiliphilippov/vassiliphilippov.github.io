staple

1. NOUN

/ ˈsteɪpəl / (AmE), / ˈsteɪpəl / (BrE)
a small U-shaped metal wire used to fasten sheets of paper together, usually with a stapler
U-shaped wire for fastening paper

Examples

I need a staple to attach these forms together.
There are only a few staples left in the stapler.
Remove the staple before shredding the documents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clip - a device, often metal or plastic, used to hold papers together
  • Synonym: fastener - something used to attach or hold things together
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈsteɪpəl / (AmE), / ˈsteɪpəl / (BrE)
a short U-shaped wire fastener used for binding papers or attaching materials together, usually driven by a stapler or hammer
short U-shaped wire fastener

Examples

The documents are held together with a staple in the upper left corner.
He removed a staple from the stack of papers before copying them.
The staple got jammed in the stapler and had to be replaced.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: paperclip - a small piece of bent wire used for holding sheets of paper together

Etymology

From Old English stapol (post, pillar), sense extended to the fastener in the 19th century.

4. NOUN

/ ˈsteɪpl̩ / (AmE), / ˈsteɪpəl / (BrE)
A basic or principal item, especially a commodity that is in constant demand and forms an important part of something, such as a diet or economy.
basic commodity in constant demand

Examples

Rice and wheat are considered staples in many countries around the world.
Milk is a staple of the household grocery list.
The store specializes in staples such as bread, sugar, and flour.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: essential - something absolutely necessary
  • Antonym: luxury - an inessential, often expensive item

Etymology

From Middle English stapel (marketplace, trading post), probably from Middle Low German stapel (pillar, emporium, warehouse).

5. NOUN

/ ˈsteɪpəl / (AmE), / ˈsteɪpəl / (BrE)
A natural fiber, such as raw cotton, wool, hemp, or flax, that can be twisted together to form yarn.
textile fiber used for yarn

Examples

Cotton with a longer staple is generally considered better for making fine yarn.
The quality of the wool is determined by the length and strength of its staple.
Textile manufacturers often blend different types of staple to achieve specific fabric properties.

Synset Examples

staple fibers vary widely in length

Etymology

From Middle English stapel (market, emporium); meaning related to fiber developed in the textile context.
