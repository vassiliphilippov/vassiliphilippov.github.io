dictation

1. NOUN

/ dɪkˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪkˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of speaking words aloud for someone else to write down, or the words spoken for this purpose
speaking for someone else to write down

Examples

The students had trouble keeping up with the dictation during the language lesson.
She gave a dictation to her secretary every morning to prepare her correspondence.
Accurate dictation is an important skill for administrative assistants.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transcription - a written or printed version of material originally presented in another medium

Etymology

From Latin 'dictatio', from 'dictare' (to say often).

2. NOUN

/ dɪkˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪkˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
a piece of writing that has been dictated and transcribed; a dictated passage
dictated and transcribed passage

Examples

The manager reviewed the dictation before signing the official letter.
After the meeting, the secretary typed up the dictation from her notes.
She saved each dictation in a separate folder for easy reference.

Synset Examples

he signed and mailed his dictation without bothering to read it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dictate - to say or read aloud for someone else to write down

Etymology

From Latin dictatio (a dictating), via dictare (to dictate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06398975-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ dɪkˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪkˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
an authoritative order or instruction given by someone in authority
authoritative order or instruction

Examples

The rules are subject to the dictation of the company's board of directors.
He acted under the dictation of his superior.
The project was halted by the dictation of government officials.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: order - an instruction to do something, especially given by someone in authority
  • Synonym: command - an authoritative order

Etymology

From Latin dictatio (a saying, dictating), from dictare (to dictate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07182782-n
Synset POS: n
