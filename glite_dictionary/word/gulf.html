gulf

1. NOUN

/ ɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɡʌlf / (BrE)
A large area of sea or ocean that is partly enclosed by land, typically larger than a bay.
large sea area partly enclosed by land

Examples

The Persian Gulf has been a major trade route for centuries.
A powerful storm swept across the Gulf of Mexico last night.
Many species of fish thrive in the warm waters of this gulf.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: bay - a broad inlet of the sea where the land curves inward, typically smaller than a gulf

Etymology

From Old French golfe, from Italian golfo, from Greek kolpos (bosom, gulf).

2. NOUN

/ ɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɡʌlf / (BrE)
a large difference or separation between two people, groups, or things, especially in ideas, feelings, or understanding
large difference or separation between groups/ideas

Examples

After years of conflict, there was a growing gulf between the two communities.
Her strict upbringing created a gulf of misunderstanding with her more liberal friends.
The gulf between the rich and the poor has widened in recent decades.

Synset Examples

he felt a gulf between himself and his former friends
there is a vast disconnect between public opinion and federal policy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: divide - a wide separation or difference between people or groups
  • Synonym: chasm - a profound gap or division between two people, groups, or viewpoints

Etymology

From Old French 'golfe', from Italian 'golfo', from Greek 'kolpos' (bosom, bay, gulf).

3. NOUN

/ ɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɡʌlf / (BrE)
a deep, wide gap or chasm, either physical or figurative, especially one that separates places, groups, or ideas
a deep, wide gap or chasm

Examples

After years of conflict, a gulf remained between the two communities.
There was a gulf of misunderstanding between the generations.
The earthquake created a massive gulf in the landscape.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chasm - a deep fissure in the earth, rock, or another surface
  • Synonym: gap - an open or empty space in or between things

Etymology

From Old French 'golfe', from Italian 'golfo', from Greek 'kolpos' (bosom, bay).
