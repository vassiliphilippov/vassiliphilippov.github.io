suppressor

1. NOUN

/ səˈprɛsɚ / (AmE), / səˈprɛsə / (BrE)
a person who suppresses people, information, or actions, often by force or authority
person who suppresses others or things

Examples

The dictator was known as a ruthless suppressor of dissent in his country.
History often condemns the suppressor while praising those who fight for freedom.
He was seen as a suppressor by civil rights activists for his strict policies.

Synset Examples

dictators are suppressors of free speech

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oppressor - a person who keeps others in subservience and hardship, especially by the unjust exercise of authority
  • Antonym: liberator - a person who frees others from oppression or confinement

Etymology

From Latin 'supprimere' (to press down) via 'suppress' + '-or' suffix.

2. NOUN

/ səˈprɛsər / (AmE), / səˈprɛsə / (BrE)
a gene that suppresses the phenotypic expression of another gene, especially a mutant gene
gene that suppresses another gene's effect
medical

Examples

The suppressor gene counteracts the effects of a harmful mutation.
Researchers identified a suppressor that restored normal cell function in the experiment.
A defect in the suppressor may amplify the impact of certain mutations.

Etymology

From Latin 'suppressor', from 'supprimere' meaning 'to press down' or 'suppress'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05449044-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ səˈprɛsɚ / (AmE), / səˈprɛsə / (BrE)
an electrical device used to prevent or reduce unwanted electrical currents, especially noise or interference
device that reduces unwanted electrical currents

Examples

The technician installed a suppressor to prevent interference from other electronic devices.
A power surge damaged the suppressor in the amplifier circuit.
Without a suppressor, you might hear a buzzing noise from the speakers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: filter - device that removes unwanted signals from an electrical circuit

Etymology

From Latin 'suppressor', from 'supprimere' meaning 'to press down'.
