transudation

1. NOUN

/ ˌtræn.sjuˈdeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌtræn.sjʊˈdeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process by which fluid slowly passes through a membrane or vessel wall, especially from blood vessels, due to pressure differences rather than inflammation.
slow passage of fluid through membranes
medical

Examples

The presence of protein-poor fluid indicated that transudation rather than inflammation was occurring.
During heart failure, transudation leads to accumulation of fluid in the lungs.
Doctors analyzed the fluid to determine whether it was caused by transudation or exudation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: exudation - the process of oozing out fluid, typically as a result of inflammation

Etymology

From Latin 'transudare' (to sweat through).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtræn.suˈdeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌtræn.sjuːˈdeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A fluid that passes through a membrane or tissue due to hydrostatic or osmotic pressure, especially one that has filtered out of blood vessels but is not the result of inflammation.
fluid passing through a membrane or tissue
medical

Examples

The physician collected a sample of transudation from the patient's pleural cavity for analysis.
Low-protein transudation can occur in cases of heart failure.
Unlike an exudate, transudation is typically clear and low in cellular content.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: exudate - fluid that seeps out of blood vessels, often due to inflammation

Etymology

From Latin transudare (to ooze through), from trans- (across) + sudare (to sweat).
