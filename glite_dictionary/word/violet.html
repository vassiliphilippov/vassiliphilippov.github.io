violet

1. ADJ

/ ˈvaɪ.ələt / (AmE), / ˈvaɪ.əlɪt / (BrE)
of a color intermediate between red and blue, similar to that of the violet flower
of a purplish-blue color

Examples

She wore a beautiful violet dress to the party.
The artist chose violet hues to capture the twilight sky.
His eyes had a subtle violet tint in the morning light.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: purple - of a color intermediate between red and blue, but generally darker and stronger than violet
  • Degree Lower: blue - of the color between green and violet in the spectrum; the color of a clear sky
  • Degree Higher: indigo - of a deep purplish-blue color; between blue and violet in the spectrum

Etymology

From Old French violette, from Latin viola (the violet flower).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvaɪ.əl.ət / (AmE), / ˈvaɪ.əl.ɪt / (BrE)
A color at the end of the visible spectrum, between blue and ultraviolet, typically described as a bluish-purple.
a bluish-purple color

Examples

Her dress was a striking shade of violet that caught everyone's attention.
The artist blended blue and red to create a deep violet for the sunset.
In the spectrum, violet is found beyond blue and before ultraviolet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: purple - a color intermediate between red and blue
  • Degree Lower: blue - a color between green and violet in the spectrum
  • Degree Higher: ultraviolet - light beyond the violet end of the visible spectrum

Etymology

From Old French *violete*, diminutive of *viole* (violet flower), from Latin *viola*.

3. NOUN

/ ˈvaɪ.əl.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈvaɪ.əl.ɪt / (BrE)
A small low-growing plant of the genus Viola, typically having purple, blue, or white flowers and heart-shaped leaves.
small plant with purple or blue flowers

Examples

In the spring, violet flowers carpeted the woodland floor.
She picked a violet from the garden and put it in her hair.
Many perfumes are scented with the delicate fragrance of violet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pansy - a cultivated plant of the genus Viola with large, rounded flowers

Etymology

From Old French violete, diminutive of viole (violet), from Latin viola.
