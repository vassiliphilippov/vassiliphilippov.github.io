circumlocution

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɝːkəmloʊˈkjuːʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɜːkəmləˈkjuːʃən / (BrE)
the use of an unnecessarily large number of words to express an idea, often in an indirect or roundabout way
indirect or roundabout expression

Examples

The politician's answer was pure circumlocution, never getting to the main point.
Instead of addressing the issue, she used circumlocution to avoid giving a direct response.
Legal documents are often criticized for their excessive use of circumlocution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: periphrasis - the use of indirect or circumlocutory speech or writing
  • Antonym: conciseness - the quality of being brief and to the point

Etymology

From Latin circumlocutio (a roundabout expression), from circum- (around) + loqui (to speak).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɝːkəmloʊˈkjuːʃn̩ / (AmE), / ˌsɜːkəmləˈkjuːʃən / (BrE)
The use of many words where fewer would do, especially in a deliberate attempt to be vague or evasive; an indirect or roundabout way of saying something.
an indirect or roundabout expression

Examples

The politician's circumlocution frustrated reporters who wanted a direct answer to their question.
Instead of telling the truth, he used circumlocution to avoid admitting his mistake.
Legal language is sometimes criticized for its unnecessary circumlocution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: periphrasis - the use of indirect or roundabout language
  • Antonym: conciseness - the quality of being brief and to the point

Etymology

From Latin circumlocutio ('a roundabout expression'), from circum- ('around') + loqui ('to speak').
