to seethe

1. VERB

/ sið / (AmE), / siːð / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to move about actively with bubbles or foam, often as a result of boiling or agitation.
move or foam as if boiling

Examples

The water began to seethe in the pot as it reached a rolling boil.
Molten lava could be seen seething inside the volcano's crater.
The cauldron seethed with a thick, bubbling potion.

Synset Examples

a seething liquid

Etymology

From Old English *seōþan* (to boil, seethe), related to Old Norse *sjōða* (to boil).

2. VERB

/ siːð / (AmE), / siːð / (BrE)
to be filled with intense but unexpressed anger or agitation
show intense, unexpressed anger or agitation

Examples

She tried to remain calm, but inside she was seething with frustration.
Although he appeared composed, his mind continued to seethe over the unfair decision.
After the meeting, several employees were quietly seething at the manager's remarks.

Synset Examples

The customer was seething with anger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: simmer - stay almost at the point of boiling, to feel anger or excitement that is kept hidden
  • Synonym: boil - to become very angry or agitated

Etymology

From Old English 'seþan' (to boil, to seethe).

3. VERB

/ sið / (AmE), / siːð / (BrE)
(of a place) to be filled with intense, often noisy activity or movement; to be in a state of turmoil.
be noisy or turbulent with activity

Examples

The city streets seethe with energy during the morning rush hour.
The market seethed with shoppers searching for deals.
On election night, the campaign headquarters seethed with anticipation and movement.

Synset Examples

This office is buzzing with activity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buzz - to be full of busy movement or excitement
  • Synonym: hustle - to be bustling with energetic activity
  • Antonym: calm - absence of noise or movement; peaceful

Etymology

From Old English *sēothan* (to boil), related to boiling or bubbling movement.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ sið / (AmE), / siːð / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to bubble or boil vigorously; to be in a state of rapid agitation.
to boil or bubble vigorously

Examples

The pot began to seethe as the soup reached a full boil.
The cauldron seethed and emitted clouds of steam in the dim light.
The river was so agitated after the storm that it seemed to seethe with energy.

Synset Examples

The liquid was seething
The water rolled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boil - to reach the temperature at which a liquid turns to vapor
  • Degree Lower: simmer - to stay just below boiling point while bubbling gently

Etymology

Old English *sēothan* (to boil, seethe), from Proto-Germanic *seuthaną*.
