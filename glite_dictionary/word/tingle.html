tingle

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɪŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪŋɡəl / (BrE)
a slight, often pleasurable feeling of excitement or fear, especially one that produces a physical sensation in the body
slight sensation of excitement or fear

Examples

He felt a tingle of excitement as he waited to go on stage.
A slight tingle ran down her spine at the eerie sound.
The horror movie gave him a tingle that was both frightening and enjoyable.

Synset Examples

a frisson of surprise shot through him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frisson - a sudden strong feeling of excitement or fear; a thrill
  • Synonym: shiver - a slight shaking movement or feeling, often from cold or fear
  • Antonym: dullness - a lack of excitement or feeling

Etymology

From Middle English *tinglen*, frequentative of *tinen* (to prick, tickle).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɪŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪŋɡl / (BrE)
a slight, prickling or stinging sensation, often experienced as from many small points or as a subtle vibration in the skin or body
slight prickling or stinging sensation

Examples

The icy wind sent a tingle down my spine as I walked outside.
She felt a pleasant tingle in her fingers after holding the snowball.
A strange tingle ran through his arm after he bumped his funny bone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prickle - a slight, sharp, tingling sensation in the skin
  • Antonym: itch - an irritating feeling that produces a desire to scratch

Etymology

From Middle English *tinglen*, frequentative of *tingen* (to ring, tingle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05730815-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈtɪŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪŋɡəl / (BrE)
to cause or experience a slight stinging or prickling sensation, often accompanied by excitement or anticipation
feel or cause a sharp, prickling sensation

Examples

The cold wind made her cheeks tingle as she walked home.
His fingers tingled after touching the icy metal railing.
Excitement made my skin tingle as I waited for the announcement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prickle - to experience a slight stinging or tingling sensation

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'tinglen', frequentative of 'tinen' (to kindle), influenced by Old Norse 'tingla'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02127448-v
Synset POS: v
