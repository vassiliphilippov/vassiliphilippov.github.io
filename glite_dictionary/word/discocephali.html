Discocephali

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɪskoʊˈsɛfəli / (AmE), / ˌdɪskəʊˈsɛfəli / (BrE)
A small order of fishes comprising the remoras, known for having a disk-like structure on the head used for attaching to larger marine animals.
order of fishes including remoras

Examples

The Discocephali are notable for the specialized suction disks found on the heads of remoras.
Biologists recently studied the diversity of species within the order Discocephali.
The unique reproductive strategies of Discocephali make them an interesting subject in marine biology.

Etymology

From Greek 'diskos' (disk) + 'kephalē' (head), referring to the disk-shaped head of remoras.

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɪskoʊˈsɛfəli / (AmE), / ˌdɪskəʊˈsɛfəli / (BrE)
A small taxonomic order of fishes comprising the remoras, known for their sucker-like heads used to attach to larger marine animals.
taxonomic order of fishes (remoras)

Examples

The Discocephali are best known for their attachment to sharks and other large marine animals.
Scientists study Discocephali to understand their unique adaptation for hitchhiking in the ocean.
Members of the Discocephali order use a modified dorsal fin as a sucker to cling to hosts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remora - a fish of the family Echeneidae, known for attaching to larger animals

Etymology

From Greek 'diskos' (disc) + 'kephalē' (head), referring to the disc-shaped structure on the fishes' heads.
