scapular

1. NOUN

/ ˈskæpjələr / (AmE), / ˈskæpjʊlə / (BrE)
a long, wide piece of woolen cloth with an opening for the head, worn over the shoulders as part of a monastic habit; also, a similar devotional garment in certain Christian traditions
religious shoulder garment; part of monastic habit

Examples

The monk wore his scapular as part of his daily habit.
Pilgrims were given a scapular to wear as a sign of devotion.
She explained the significance of the brown scapular to the visitors.

Etymology

From Late Latin 'scapularis' (of the shoulders), from Latin 'scapulae' (shoulders).

3. NOUN

/ ˈskæpjələr / (AmE), / ˈskapjʊlə / (BrE)
a feather or group of feathers covering the shoulder area of a bird, often aiding in streamlining and protection
feather covering shoulder of a bird

Examples

The eagle ruffled its scapular feathers as it perched on the branch.
The distinctive white scapular makes the heron easy to identify from a distance.
During molting season, some birds lose their scapular feathers first.

Etymology

From Late Latin scapularis, from Latin scapula (shoulder blade).
