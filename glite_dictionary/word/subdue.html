to subdue

1. VERB

/ səbˈdu / (AmE), / səbˈdjuː / (BrE)
to bring under control, especially by force or authority
bring under control by force or authority

Examples

The government deployed troops to subdue the violent protests in the capital.
She struggled to subdue her anger during the heated argument.
Firefighters worked hard to subdue the blaze before it could spread further.

Synset Examples

suppress a nascent uprising
stamp down on littering
conquer one's desires

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to something, especially by force
  • Synonym: to conquer - to take control of a place or people by force
  • Antonym: release - to allow something or someone to go free

Etymology

From Latin 'subduere', meaning 'to draw from below'; related to 'sub-' (under) and 'duco' (lead).

2. VERB

/ səbˈdu / (AmE), / səbˈdjuː / (BrE)
to bring under control, especially by punishment or discipline; to restrain or curb someone or something
bring under control by discipline

Examples

The teacher had to subdue the disruptive student with extra assignments.
The rebellion was finally subdued through strict discipline and firm leadership.
He used time-outs to subdue his son's unruly behavior.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discipline - to train or control by punishment and instruction
  • Synonym: to punish - to inflict a penalty for wrongdoing
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support, confidence, or hope to someone

Etymology

From Latin 'subdere', meaning 'to put under', from 'sub-' (under) + 'dere' (to put).

  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support or confidence to
