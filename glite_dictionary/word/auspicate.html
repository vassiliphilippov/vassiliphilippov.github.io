to auspicate

1. VERB

/ ˈɔːspɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɔːspɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to indicate or predict (an event) by signs or omens
foretell by signs or omens
archaic

Examples

The ancient priests would auspicate the city's fate from the flight of birds.
Some believed that solar eclipses auspicate important changes in the world.
He attempted to auspicate the outcome by carefully interpreting the omens.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: augur - to foretell events by omens
  • Synonym: to predict - state or estimate that a specific thing will happen

Etymology

From Latin auspicator, from auspici-, stem of auspicium (augury, omen).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈɔːspɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɔːspɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to begin or inaugurate (an activity or event) in a way believed to bring good luck
initiate with the intent to bring good luck

Examples

The couple decided to auspicate their marriage by planting a tree in their garden.
Some ancient societies would auspicate military campaigns with elaborate rituals.
They auspicated the opening of the new store by breaking a bottle of wine at the entrance.

Synset Examples

They auspicated the trip with a bottle of champagne

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to inaugurate - to begin or introduce (a system, policy, or period)

Etymology

From Latin auspicatus, past participle of auspicare (to take auspices, begin under good omens).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00348074-v
Synset POS: v
