flute

1. VERB

/ fluːt / (AmE), / fluːt / (BrE)
to make long, narrow grooves or channels in a surface, especially as a decorative or functional feature
to make grooves or channels in a surface

Examples

The carpenter skillfully fluted the columns to add a classical touch to the building.
You can flute the pie crust edges to give them a decorative finish.
Special tools are needed to flute the surface of metal pipes evenly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groove - to make a long, narrow cut or indentation

2. NOUN

/ fluːt / (AmE), / fluːt / (BrE)
a high-pitched woodwind instrument consisting of a slender tube closed at one end, with finger holes and an opening near the closed end across which the breath is blown
high-pitched woodwind instrument

Examples

She played a beautiful melody on her flute during the concert.
The haunting sound of the flute echoed through the ancient hall.
Children in the orchestra were excited to try the silver flute for the first time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recorder - a woodwind musical instrument similar to the flute but with a mouthpiece
  • Synonym: clarinet - a single-reed woodwind musical instrument with a cylindrical tube
  • Synonym: oboe - a woodwind instrument with a double-reed mouthpiece and a slender tubular body

Etymology

From Old French 'flaüte', from Occitan 'flaüt', of imitative origin.

3. NOUN

/ fluːt / (AmE), / fluːt / (BrE)
a shallow, concave groove or furrow, especially one of several parallel grooves on the shaft of a column or in materials such as cloth
a shallow groove or furrow (esp. in columns)

Examples

The ancient Greek columns were decorated with narrow flutes running vertically along the shafts.
The craftsman carved delicate flutes into the wooden moldings for added elegance.
Some fabrics have subtle flutes that create a textured appearance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groove - a long, narrow cut or depression in a surface
  • Antonym: ridge - a long, narrow raised part or bump (opposite of groove)

Etymology

From Old French 'fleute', from Latin 'flatus' (flow, blowing), likely transferred by resemblance to the musical instrument's tubular shape.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ fluːt / (AmE), / fluːt / (BrE)
a tall, narrow wineglass, typically used for serving champagne or sparkling wines
tall, narrow wineglass

Examples

He raised his flute in a toast to the happy couple.
The waiter poured champagne into a chilled flute.
She accidentally knocked over a crystal flute during the party.

Etymology

From Old French 'fleute'; this sense relates to the resemblance in shape between the glass and the musical instrument.
