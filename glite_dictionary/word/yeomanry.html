yeomanry

1. NOUN

/ ˈjoʊmənri / (AmE), / ˈjəʊmənri / (BrE)
a British volunteer cavalry force, originally formed in 1761 for home defense and later incorporated into the Territorial Army
British volunteer cavalry force (historical)
british

Examples

The yeomanry played a vital role in defending the British homeland during the Napoleonic Wars.
Many members of the yeomanry were recruited from the rural gentry and landowners.
Today, some regiments trace their origins back to the original yeomanry units established in the 18th century.

Etymology

From 'yeoman' (small landowner/farmer) + -ry (forming collective nouns), reflecting the social class of early members.

2. NOUN

/ ˈjoʊmənri / (AmE), / ˈjəʊmənri / (BrE)
A class of small landowners who cultivated their own land, especially in England; historically, a social class between the gentry and the laborers.
class of small freeholding cultivators
archaic

Examples

The English yeomanry played a vital role in the country’s agricultural development.
Many members of the yeomanry owned their land outright, unlike tenant farmers.
In medieval times, the yeomanry often served as skilled archers in the king's army.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peasantry - class of rural agricultural laborers or small farmers
  • Degree Higher: gentry - people of good social position just below the nobility

Etymology

From 'yeoman' (small landholder) + '-ry' (collective suffix).
