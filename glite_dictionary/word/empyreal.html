empyreal

1. ADJ

/ ˌɛmˈpaɪriəl / (AmE), / ˌɛmˈpɪəriəl / (BrE)
relating to the sky or the heavens, especially in a celestial or sublime sense
of the heavens; celestial

Examples

The poet described the angels as empyreal beings shining above the clouds.
Painted with empyreal light, the sky looked almost otherworldly at dawn.
Her dreams soared to empyreal heights, untouched by earthly concerns.

Synset Examples

the empyrean sphere

Related Terms

  • Synonym: celestial - relating to the sky or heavens; heavenly
  • Antonym: terrestrial - relating to the earth rather than the sky or heavens

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'empyreus', from Greek 'empyrios' (in or on the fire), relating to the highest heaven, originally thought to be made of fire.

2. ADJ

/ ˌɛm.pɪˈriː.əl / (AmE), / ˌɛm.pɪˈrɪə.l / (BrE)
inspiring awe by being sublime or heavenly; related to the highest heavens
sublime, heavenly, awe-inspiring

Examples

The cathedral's architecture reached for the empyreal, filling visitors with a sense of awe.
He described her singing as having an empyreal quality that left the audience silent.
Beneath the empyreal sky, he felt profoundly inspired by the beauty of nature.

Synset Examples

well-meaning ineptitude that rises to empyreal absurdity
empyrean aplomb
the sublime beauty of the night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sublime - of such excellence or beauty as to inspire awe
  • Antonym: earthly - relating to the world rather than to heaven; mundane

Etymology

From Medieval Latin empyreus, from Ancient Greek empyros (in or of the fire), referring to the highest heaven.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01125519-s
Synset POS: s
