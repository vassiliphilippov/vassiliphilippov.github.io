proverbial

1. ADJ

/ prəˈvɝbiəl / (AmE), / prəˈvɜːbiəl / (BrE)
(of a person or thing) widely referred to, especially as an example of a particular characteristic; well known through common sayings or reputation.
widely referred to; well known

Examples

She is famous for her proverbial punctuality, never arriving late to a single meeting.
The proverbial elephant in the room was finally addressed by the manager.
It was his proverbial sense of humor that kept the mood light during difficult times.

Synset Examples

her proverbial lateness
the proverbial absentminded professor
your proverbial dizzy blonde

Related Terms

  • Synonym: well-known - widely recognized or familiar

Etymology

From Latin 'proverbialis', from 'proverbium' (proverb).

2. ADJ

/ prəˈvɝːbiəl / (AmE), / prəˈvɜːbiəl / (BrE)
referred to in a proverb or well-known saying; well-known, especially so as to be stereotypical or commonly mentioned.
of, or like, a proverb; widely known

Examples

Everyone laughed when he used the proverbial phrase, 'the early bird catches the worm.'
The proverbial needle in a haystack was exactly what they were searching for.
She referenced the proverbial grasshopper to make her point about planning ahead.

Synset Examples

he kicked the proverbial bucket
the proverbial grasshopper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: well-known - known by many people; familiar
  • Synonym: famous - known about by many people; celebrated

Etymology

From Latin 'proverbialis', from 'proverbium' (proverb).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03001772-a
Synset POS: a
