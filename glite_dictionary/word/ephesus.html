Ephesus

1. PROPN

/ ˈɛfɪsəs / (AmE), / ˈɛfɪsəs / (BrE)
an ancient Greek city located on the western coast of Asia Minor (now Turkey), famous for the Temple of Artemis and its significance in early Christianity
ancient Greek city in Asia Minor

Examples

Many tourists visit Ephesus to see the ruins of the Temple of Artemis.
Ephesus was once one of the largest cities in the ancient world.
Paul the Apostle famously wrote a letter to the Christians in Ephesus.

Etymology

From Latin Ephesus, from Ancient Greek Ἔφεσος (Éphesos).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛfəsəs / (AmE), / ˈɛfɪsəs / (BrE)
the third ecumenical council of the Christian church, held in 431, which declared Mary as the Mother of God and condemned Pelagianism.
Christian ecumenical council in 431 AD

Examples

The Council of Ephesus played a pivotal role in shaping early Christian doctrine.
At Ephesus, church officials debated the nature of Christ and the status of Mary.
Many historians study the outcomes of Ephesus to understand theological disputes of the fifth century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Council of Nicaea - the first ecumenical council of the Christian church, held in 325
  • Antonym: Pelagianism - the doctrine condemned at the Council of Ephesus

Etymology

From the ancient city of Ephesus, where the council was held.
