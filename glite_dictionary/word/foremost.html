foremost

1. ADJ

/ ˈfɔrˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməʊst / (BrE)
most important or outstanding in rank, position, or importance
most important; leading

Examples

She is regarded as the foremost expert in her field.
Innovation is the company’s foremost priority.
As one of the foremost scientists of his generation, he received many awards.

Synset Examples

was first in her class
the foremost figure among marine artists

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leading - most important or influential
  • Synonym: principal - most important; main
  • Antonym: minor - of lesser importance or rank

Etymology

From Old English 'formest', superlative of 'forma' (first).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfɔrˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməʊst / (BrE)
Situated at the very front; in the leading position or most prominent location.
at the very front; leading position

Examples

She stood at the foremost edge of the crowd, eager for a better view.
The foremost part of the plane sustained the most damage in the accident.
The general led his troops from the foremost position on the battlefield.

Synset Examples

the foremost compartment of the ship

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hindmost - farthest back in position

Etymology

From Old English 'formest', superlative of 'forma' (first).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00200202-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈfɔrˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməʊst / (BrE)
(of an object or compartment) situated at the front, especially nearest the bow of a ship.
at or near the front (esp. of a ship)

Examples

The crew stored equipment in the foremost compartment to balance the ship's weight.
Passengers are not allowed in the foremost section during docking maneuvers.
The foremost cabins offer the best views ahead as the vessel moves forward.

Synset Examples

the foremost compartment of the ship

Related Terms

  • Antonym: aft - toward or near the stern (rear) of a ship

Etymology

From Old English *formest*, superlative of 'fore' (before, in front), influenced by analogous forms in later English.
