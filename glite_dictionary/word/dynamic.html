dynamic

1. ADJ

/ daɪˈnæmɪk / (AmE), / daɪˈnæmɪk / (BrE)
Relating to dynamics, such as forces and motion in physics or varying degrees of loudness in music.
of or relating to dynamics

Examples

The dynamic behavior of the system was analyzed using Newton's laws.
In music, dynamic markings indicate how loudly or softly a piece should be played.
Understanding the dynamic properties of materials is essential in engineering.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: static - lacking in movement, relating to bodies at rest or forces in equilibrium

Etymology

From Greek dynamikos (powerful), from dynamis (power).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02991423-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ daɪˈnæmɪk / (AmE), / daɪˈnæmɪk / (BrE)
(of a person, process, or thing) full of energy, enthusiasm, and new ideas; characterized by constant change or activity.
full of energy or constantly changing

Examples

She is a dynamic leader who inspires her team to achieve great results.
The company's dynamic approach to innovation sets it apart from competitors.
Our teacher is incredibly dynamic, making every lesson lively and engaging.

Synset Examples

a dynamic market
a dynamic speaker
the dynamic president of the firm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: energetic - showing or involving great activity or vitality
  • Antonym: static - lacking in movement, action, or change

Etymology

From Greek dunamikos (powerful), from dunamis (power, force).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00811761-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ daɪˈnæmɪk / (AmE), / daɪˈnæmɪk / (BrE)
(Linguistics) describing a verb or adjective that expresses action or process, rather than a state of being.
linguistics: expressing action or process

Examples

Only dynamic verbs can be used in the present continuous tense.
The word 'running' in 'running water' is a dynamic participial adjective.
In grammar, dynamic verbs contrast with stative verbs, which describe states rather than actions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stative - (Linguistics) denoting a verb or adjective that expresses a state rather than an action

Etymology

From Greek dynamikos (powerful), from dynamis (power).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00041840-a
Synset POS: a

4. NOUN

/ daɪˈnæmɪk / (AmE), / daɪˈnæmɪk / (BrE)
a force or factor that stimulates change or progress within a system or process
a motivating force or factor

Examples

The rise in consumer demand was the main dynamic behind the company's growth.
A strong leader can serve as a positive dynamic within a struggling organization.
The new policy introduced a fresh dynamic to the team, motivating everyone to work harder.

Synset Examples

they hoped it would act as a spiritual dynamic on all churches

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incentive - something that motivates or encourages one to do something
  • Synonym: impetus - the force that makes something happen or happen more quickly
  • Antonym: deterrent - something that discourages action or prevents progress

Etymology

From Greek dunamikos (powerful), from dunamis (power, force).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09202859-n
Synset POS: n
