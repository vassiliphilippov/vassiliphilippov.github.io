fleabane

1. NOUN

/ ˈfliˌbeɪn / (AmE), / ˈfliːˌbeɪn / (BrE)
a common plant of the daisy family with small, yellow or white flowers, often considered a weed in North America and other temperate regions; sometimes placed in the genus Erigeron
a weed with small daisy-like flowers

Examples

Gardeners often struggle to remove fleabane from their flower beds.
The meadow was dotted with patches of fleabane and wild grasses.
Some herbal remedies historically used fleabane to try to repel insects.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Erigeron - a genus of plants commonly known as fleabanes

Etymology

Probably from the belief it repelled fleas, combining 'flea' and 'bane' (something that causes death or destruction).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfliˌbeɪn / (AmE), / ˈfliːbeɪn / (BrE)
A hairy perennial plant of the daisy family, with yellow or white flowers, once believed to repel fleas.
plant of the daisy family reputed to repel fleas

Examples

The garden was full of fleabane growing among the roses and daisies.
Historically, people believed that fleabane could protect their homes from fleas.
Wild fleabane adds a splash of color to meadows in late summer.

Etymology

From Middle English flebane, from Old English fleoge (flea) + bane (something that causes death or destruction).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfliˌbeɪn / (AmE), / ˈfliːˌbeɪn / (BrE)
a North American plant of the genus Erigeron, having small daisylike flowers and once believed to repel fleas
North American plant with daisylike flowers

Examples

Folk tradition held that hanging dried fleabane in the house would ward off pests.
Wild fleabane grew in abundance along the roadside, its tiny flowers brightening the ditch.
Gardeners appreciate fleabane for its hardiness and its ability to attract pollinators.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Erigeron - the plant genus to which fleabane belongs

Etymology

From 'flea' + 'bane' (an archaic word for 'killer' or 'slayer'), referring to its former use as a flea repellent.
