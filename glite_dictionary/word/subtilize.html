to subtilize

1. VERB

/ ˈsʌt̬əˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌtɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to make or become more subtle, refined, or delicate
make or become more subtle

Examples

The critic's efforts to subtilize the argument only confused the readers further.
She tried to subtilize her approach to discussing the sensitive issue.
Years of experience had subtilized his sense of taste and judgement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refine - make something more pure or improved

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subtilizare (to make subtle), from Latin subtilis (fine, delicate).

2. VERB

/ ˈsʌbtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌbtɪlʌɪz / (BrE)
To make or perceive fine distinctions or subtleties, especially in reasoning or language.
make fine distinctions; perceive subtleties

Examples

She tends to subtilize every argument, focusing on the smallest differences in meaning.
In philosophical debates, experts often subtilize distinctions that seem trivial to others.
The critic's ability to subtilize between related concepts impressed her colleagues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discriminate - to recognize or make distinctions between things
  • Antonym: to generalize - to form general ideas or principles instead of making fine distinctions

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'subtilizare', from Latin 'subtilis' (fine, delicate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00651886-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈsʌt.əlˌaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌt.ɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
To make (the senses or understanding) more keen or refined.
make senses or mind more keen

Examples

Years of training served to subtilize her sense of taste far beyond the ordinary.
Meditation can subtilize one’s perception of faint sounds and subtle emotions.
The artist sought to subtilize his visual perception to better capture the nuances of color.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sharpen - to make or become sharp or sharper

Etymology

From Late Latin subtilizare, from Latin subtilis (fine, delicate).
