skeleton

1. NOUN

/ ˈskɛlətn̩ / (AmE), / ˈskɛlɪtən / (BrE)
the internal supporting structure that gives strength or shape to an object, such as a building or machine
internal supporting structure (of an object)

Examples

The architect designed the skeleton of the bridge using reinforced concrete.
Even before the walls went up, the skeleton of the skyscraper was clearly visible against the sky.
The car's skeleton must be strong enough to protect passengers during a crash.

Synset Examples

the building has a steel skeleton

Related Terms

  • Synonym: framework - a supporting structure around which something can be built

Etymology

From Greek *skeletos* (dried up, mummy), adopted into Late Latin *skeleton* (bones of the body).

2. NOUN

/ ˈskɛlətən / (AmE), / ˈskɛlɪtən / (BrE)
the rigid structure of bones and cartilage that supports and gives shape to the body of an animal or human
the bone structure of an animal or human

Examples

The human skeleton consists of 206 bones.
A giraffe’s long neck is supported by a unique skeleton.
Archaeologists discovered an ancient skeleton buried beneath the site.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skeleton key - a key designed to open many different locks

Etymology

From Greek *skeletos* (dried up) via Latin *skeleton* (mummy, skeleton).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05593033-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈskɛlətən / (AmE), / ˈskɛlɪtən / (BrE)
a reduced or basic form of something, containing only its most essential parts
basic or minimal form

Examples

We only had the skeleton of the proposal ready by the deadline.
The organization was reduced to a skeleton after budget cuts.
She drafted the skeleton of her book before filling in the details.

Synset Examples

the battalion was a mere skeleton of its former self
the bare skeleton of a novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: outline - the main features or general shape of something without detail
  • Synonym: framework - a basic structure underlying a system, concept, or text
  • Antonym: fullness - a state of being complete or abundant

Etymology

From Greek *skeletos* (dried up, mummy), via Latin *skeleton* (bones of a body).
