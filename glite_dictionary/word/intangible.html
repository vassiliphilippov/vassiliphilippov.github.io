intangible

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtændʒəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtændʒɪbəl / (BrE)
difficult or impossible to define, grasp, or identify; not having a physical presence.
not easily defined or grasped

Examples

There was an intangible tension in the room that no one could explain.
She had an intangible sense that something was about to go wrong.
The value of a brand often lies in its intangible qualities.

Synset Examples

an intangible feeling of impending disaster

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tangible - able to be touched or clearly defined

Etymology

From Latin 'intangibilis', from 'in-' (not) + 'tangibilis' (that can be touched).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01276564-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtændʒəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtændʒəb(ə)l / (BrE)
(of something, especially business assets) not having a physical form or substance; unable to be touched or physically measured.
not having physical substance

Examples

Goodwill and brand reputation are considered intangible assets on the company's balance sheet.
The value of customer loyalty is often an intangible benefit that is hard to quantify.
Patents fall under the category of intangible property in most business environments.

Synset Examples

intangible assets such as good will

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tangible - having physical form; able to be touched or measured

Etymology

From Medieval Latin intangibilis, from in- (not) + tangibilis (that can be touched).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02401221-a
Synset POS: a
