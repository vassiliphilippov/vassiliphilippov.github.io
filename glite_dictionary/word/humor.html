humor

1. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
(historical) In medieval physiology, any of the four bodily fluids—blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile—whose relative proportions were believed to affect health, temperament, and emotions.
one of four bodily fluids in medieval physiology

Examples

Doctors once believed that an imbalance of humors could cause illness or changes in personality.
In Shakespeare's plays, references to the four humors reflect popular medical beliefs of his era.
According to medieval medicine, each humor was associated with a particular temperament and element.

Synset Examples

the humors are blood and phlegm and yellow and black bile

Etymology

From Latin humor (moisture, fluid), later adopted in medieval medical theory.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
A bodily fluid, especially one of the four fluids (blood, phlegm, black bile, and yellow bile) formerly believed to determine a person's health and temperament.
bodily fluid, esp. one of the four ancient "humors"
archaic medical

Examples

Early physicians believed that an imbalance of the humors could cause disease.
Medieval medicine was based on the theory of the four humors.
It was once thought that personality traits depended on the proportions of different humors in the body.

Etymology

From Latin 'humor' (moisture, fluid), from 'humere' (to be moist).
