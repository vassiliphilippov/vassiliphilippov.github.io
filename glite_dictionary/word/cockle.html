cockle

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːkəl / (AmE), / ˈkɒkəl / (BrE)
to stir up water so as to form ripples
make ripples by stirring water

Examples

The boat's movement cockled the surface of the calm lake.
A sudden breeze cockled the puddles left by the rain.
Children waded in the pond and their splashing cockled the still water.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːkəl / (AmE), / ˈkɒk(ə)l / (BrE)
to gather or cause to gather into small wrinkles or folds
gather into wrinkles or folds

Examples

The heat caused the paper to cockle as it dried.
After washing, the fabric began to cockle around the edges.
If you spill water on it, the cardboard will cockle and lose its smoothness.

Synset Examples

She puckered her lips

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pucker - to gather or contract into wrinkles or folds
  • Antonym: smooth - to make the surface flat and without wrinkles

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːkəl / (AmE), / ˈkɒkəl / (BrE)
a small, edible, burrowing bivalve mollusk, found chiefly in Europe, with a strong, rounded shell and radiating ribs
small edible European shellfish

Examples

Fishermen gathered cockle from the sandy shores at low tide.
The restaurant served fresh cockle in a garlic and white wine sauce.
Children enjoyed searching for cockle shells along the beach.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clam - an edible burrowing bivalve mollusk similar to cockle
  • Synonym: oyster - an edible bivalve mollusk with a rough shell, often eaten raw

Etymology

From Old French *coquille* (shell) or Latin *conchylium* (shellfish).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɑkəl / (AmE), / ˈkɒkəl / (BrE)
a small, edible marine bivalve mollusc with a deeply ribbed shell, commonly found on sandy coasts of Europe.
edible European marine bivalve mollusc

Examples

Fishermen gathered cockle from the sandy shores early in the morning.
The dish featured steamed cockle served with garlic and parsley.
You can often find cockle shells scattered along British beaches.

Etymology

From Old French *coquille* (shell), from Latin *conchylia* (shellfish).
