magic

1. ADJ

/ ˈmædʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈmadʒɪk / (BrE)
possessing or using supernatural powers; characteristic of or appropriate to supernatural forces, especially in stories or legends.
relating to supernatural powers

Examples

The children were thrilled by the magic castle in the storybook.
She wore a magic ring that was said to make wishes come true.
The show featured magic potions and mysterious spells.

Synset Examples

charming incantations
magic signs that protect against adverse influence
a magical spell
'tis now the very witching time of night
wizard wands
wizardly powers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magical - having or relating to magical powers or qualities

Etymology

From Old French magique, from Latin magicus, from Greek magikos (relating to magic).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmӕdʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈmadʒɪk / (BrE)
the art of producing effects or controlling events by supernatural means, often involving spells, rituals, or special knowledge
the art of using supernatural powers

Examples

The ancient priest claimed to possess powerful magic that could heal the sick.
In many cultures, magic is performed by shamans or witches.
The story is about a kingdom where magic is forbidden by law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sorcery - the use of magic, especially with the help of evil spirits
  • Synonym: witchcraft - the practice of magic, typically involving spells and rituals
  • Antonym: science - the systematic study of the natural world through observation and experiment

Etymology

From Old French *magique*, from Latin *magica*, from Greek *magikē* (the art of the Magi).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05976844-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmædʒɪk / (AmE), / ˈmadʒɪk / (BrE)
the power of apparently influencing events using mysterious or supernatural forces, or a performance using tricks that deceive the senses
power or tricks involving the supernatural or illusion

Examples

The magician performed an incredible piece of magic that amazed everyone in the room.
For those who don't know the secret, the trick seems like real magic.
To a child, technology can be just as astonishing as magic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: illusion - a deceptive appearance or impression, especially achieved by tricks
  • Synonym: sorcery - the use of magic, especially that involving supposed supernatural powers
  • Antonym: reality - the state of things as they actually exist, as opposed to an idealistic or notional idea

Etymology

From Old French magique, from Latin magicus, from Greek magikos (relating to the Magi, wise men of the East).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00100771-n
Synset POS: n
