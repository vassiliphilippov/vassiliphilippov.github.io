vice

1. NOUN

/ vaɪs / (AmE), / vaɪs / (BrE)
immoral or wicked behavior; a specific form of wrongdoing or moral failing
immoral or wicked behavior

Examples

Gambling was considered a vice in many early societies.
He struggled to overcome the vice of dishonesty throughout his life.
The novel explores how vice can corrupt even the most upright individuals.

Synset Examples

vice offends the moral standards of the community

Related Terms

  • Antonym: virtue - behavior showing moral goodness or excellence

Etymology

From Latin vitium (fault, defect, crime).

2. NOUN

/ vaɪs / (AmE), / vaɪs / (BrE)
weakness of character or behavior that is considered immoral or wicked
immoral weakness or bad habit

Examples

He struggled for years to overcome the vice of gambling.
Generosity was her best virtue, while laziness remained her greatest vice.
The book examines the relationship between vice and virtue in human nature.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: virtue - behavior showing high moral standards

Etymology

From Latin 'vitium' (fault, defect, crime).

3. NOUN

/ vaɪs / (AmE), / vaɪs / (BrE)
a metal tool with two jaws, attached to a workbench, used to hold an object firmly while work is done on it
tool for holding objects tightly

Examples

The carpenter tightened the vice to hold the wooden plank steady.
Be careful not to damage the metal when securing it in the vice.
A small vice is useful for holding jewelry pieces during repair.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clamp - a device used for holding things tightly together

Etymology

From Old French *vis*, from Latin *vitis* (vine, tendril), used for winding; influenced by the tool's winding screw.
