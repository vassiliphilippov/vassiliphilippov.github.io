scablands

1. NOUN

/ ˈskæbˌlændz / (AmE), / ˈskæb.lændz / (BrE)
(in geology) Flat, elevated land with poor soil and sparse vegetation, marked by dry channels created by glacial floods, particularly found in eastern Washington.
flat elevated land scarred by dry glacial channels

Examples

The Columbia Plateau is famous for its scablands, formed by massive ice age floods.
Scientists study the scablands to understand the effects of glacial outburst floods on the landscape.
Few plants can survive in the arid scablands due to the poor, rocky soil.

Etymology

From 'scab' (a rough bare patch) + 'land'; refers to scarred, barren terrain.
