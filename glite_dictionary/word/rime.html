rime

1. NOUN

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
correspondence in the ending sounds of words or lines of verse, especially in poetry; an alternative spelling of 'rhyme'
correspondence of sounds in poetry

Examples

The poet carefully crafted each stanza to create a perfect rime at the end of every line.
This sonnet follows a strict rime scheme that unifies the poem.
Without a strong rime, the lyrics lose some of their musicality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhyme - correspondence of sound between words or endings of words, especially in poetry

Etymology

From Old French rime, from Latin rythmus, from Greek rhythmos (measured flow or movement).

2. VERB

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
(archaic) To have or end with the same sound, especially at the end of a word; to rhyme.
archaic verb meaning to rhyme
archaic

Examples

The words 'light' and 'flight' rime in this poem.
In his verse, every line rimed perfectly with the next.
The poet skillfully rimes each ending word in her sonnet.

Synset Examples

‘hat’ and ‘cat’ rhyme

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhyme - to have or end with the same sound, especially at the end of a word

Etymology

From Middle English *rime*, from Old French *rime*, related to 'rhyme.'

4. NOUN

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
A white, icy deposit formed by the rapid freezing of water droplets on cold surfaces, especially outdoors.
white deposit of ice crystals

Examples

The trees were covered with a delicate rime after the cold, foggy night.
After the blizzard, a layer of rime coated the telephone wires.
We woke to find the windows glazed with rime, sparkling in the morning sun.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frost - a thin layer of ice formed on surfaces when water vapor freezes
  • Synonym: hoarfrost - a type of frost that forms feathery white ice crystals

Etymology

From Old English rīm (frost), related to Old Norse hrīm, German Reif (hoarfrost).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
