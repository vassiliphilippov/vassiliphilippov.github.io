to gnaw

1. VERB

/ nɔ / (AmE), / nɔː / (BrE)
(of something abstract, such as feelings or conditions) to gradually wear away, erode, or deteriorate.
to gradually wear away or deteriorate

Examples

Years of criticism began to gnaw at his self-esteem.
Doubt continued to gnaw at her mind until she found proof.
Persistent anxiety can gnaw away at your peace of mind.

Synset Examples

Her confidence eroded

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to erode - to gradually destroy or be destroyed by natural forces; to wear away
  • Synonym: to deteriorate - to become worse or of less value

Etymology

From Old English *gnagan*, related to Old Norse *gnaga* and German *nagen*, meaning 'to bite'.

2. VERB

/ nɔ / (AmE), / nɔː / (BrE)
to bite or chew something repeatedly with the teeth, especially making it slowly smaller
bite or chew with the teeth

Examples

The mouse began to gnaw on the cheese left in the trap.
He watched his dog gnaw on a bone for hours.
The child tried to gnaw a tough piece of bread.

Synset Examples

gnaw an old cracker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chew - to crush or grind with the teeth
  • Synonym: munch - to eat or chew with a steady grinding noise

Etymology

Old English *gnagan* (to gnaw), of Germanic origin.
