wafer

1. NOUN

/ ˈweɪfər / (AmE), / ˈweɪfə / (BrE)
a small, thin disk of adhesive paste used to seal letters or documents
adhesive seal for letters

Examples

He pressed a red wafer onto the envelope to seal it closed.
In the 18th century, letters were often sealed with an adhesive wafer rather than wax.
She collected antique wafer seals from old family correspondence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seal - something used to close or secure a letter or document

Etymology

From Middle English 'wafer', from Old French 'wafre', originally referring to a thin cake.

2. NOUN

/ ˈweɪ.fɚ / (AmE), / ˈweɪ.fə / (BrE)
A thin disk of unleavened bread, especially one used in the Christian celebration of the Eucharist or other religious services.
thin disk of bread used in religious ceremonies

Examples

During Mass, the priest distributed the sacred wafer to the congregation.
She received the wafer on her tongue as part of the Eucharist ceremony.
The church ordered special wafers for use in its communion services.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: host - the consecrated bread used in the Eucharist

Etymology

From Middle English wafer, from Old Northern French waufre, related to Middle Dutch wafel (‘waffle’).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈweɪfər / (AmE), / ˈweɪfə / (BrE)
a very thin, crisp cake or cookie, often sweet and sometimes used in desserts or as a garnish
a thin, crisp cake or cookie

Examples

She placed a wafer on top of the ice cream sundae for decoration.
The bakery sold chocolate-filled wafers that were light and crunchy.
He enjoyed his coffee with a vanilla wafer on the side.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: biscuit - a small baked unleavened cake, typically crisp, sometimes soft (Brit.)
  • Synonym: cookie - a small sweet baked treat, crisp or chewy (Am.)

Etymology

From Old French 'wafre', from Middle Low German or Middle Dutch 'wâfel' (honeycomb, cake).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
