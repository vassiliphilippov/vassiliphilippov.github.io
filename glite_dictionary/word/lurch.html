lurch

1. NOUN

/ lɝtʃ / (AmE), / lɜːtʃ / (BrE)
a sudden, uncontrolled movement, especially forward or sideways
sudden uncontrolled movement

Examples

The train came to a sudden lurch as it halted at the station.
She felt a lurch in her stomach when the roller coaster dropped.
With a lurch, the boat tipped to one side in the rough sea.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jerk - a quick, sudden movement
  • Synonym: stagger - an unsteady, swaying movement
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00291120-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ lɝtʃ / (AmE), / lɜːtʃ / (BrE)
A sudden, unsteady, and uneven movement or gait, especially while walking or moving.
an unsteady, uneven movement or gait

Examples

After spinning too quickly, he walked with a noticeable lurch toward the door.
The drunkard's lurch down the street drew curious glances from passersby.
At the sight of the cat, the old dog began a slow, uneven lurch across the yard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stagger - an unsteady or reeling walk

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English lurchen (to dodge, to lurk), of uncertain origin.

3. NOUN

/ lɝːtʃ / (AmE), / lɜːtʃ / (BrE)
a decisive defeat in a game, especially one in which the loser fails to score any points, used particularly in cribbage
decisive defeat in a game (esp. cribbage)

Examples

He suffered a lurch in the final round, failing to score a single point.
Losing by such a lurch was a rare humiliation in the club's cribbage tournament.
If you don’t play carefully, you might end up with a lurch against you.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: victory - win or success in a contest or game
  • Synonym: defeat - the act of being beaten in a contest or game

Etymology

Possibly from French *l'ourche* (the pool or game), used in gaming contexts.
