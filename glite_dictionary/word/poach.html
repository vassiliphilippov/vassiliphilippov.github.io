to poach

1. VERB

/ poʊtʃ / (AmE), / pəʊtʃ / (BrE)
To cook an egg, fish, or other food by simmering it gently in water or another liquid.
cook food gently in simmering liquid

Examples

She likes to poach her eggs for breakfast instead of frying them.
The chef chose to poach the salmon in a lemon-infused broth for a delicate flavor.
Apricots can be poached in syrup to make a delicious dessert.

Synset Examples

poached apricots

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: boil - to cook food by immersing it in water at 100°C
  • Synonym: simmer - to cook food in liquid at a temperature just below boiling

2. VERB

/ poʊtʃ / (AmE), / pəʊtʃ / (BrE)
to hunt or catch animals, birds, or fish illegally, especially on someone else's land or in a protected area
to hunt or catch animals illegally

Examples

The rangers patrol the park to prevent people from poaching endangered animals.
Several villagers were arrested for poaching deer in the protected forest.
He was caught poaching fish in the river without a permit.

Synset Examples

people are poaching elephants for their ivory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hunt - to chase and kill wild animals for food or sport

Etymology

From Middle English *pocchen* (to poke, thrust), possibly from Old French *pochier* (to bag, poke).

3. VERB

/ poʊtʃ / (AmE), / pəʊtʃ / (BrE)
to illegally hunt, capture, or kill wild animals, especially on land where this is not allowed
illegally hunt or capture animals

Examples

Authorities arrested three men who tried to poach rhinos in the national park.
It is a crime to poach wildlife in many countries worldwide.
Some animals are endangered because people continue to poach them for their fur and tusks.

Synset Examples

It's illegal to poach wildlife in protected areas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hunt - to pursue and kill animals for sport or food

Etymology

Probably from Middle English pocchen (to poke, thrust), related to Old French pochier (to enclose in a bag).

4. VERB

/ poʊtʃ / (AmE), / pəʊtʃ / (BrE)
to illegally or unfairly take someone else's ideas, work, or information and use them as your own
take or steal someone's ideas

Examples

The company was accused of trying to poach ideas from its rivals during the conference.
She felt betrayed when her colleague began to poach her research ideas for his own presentation.
Some startups are notorious for attempting to poach ideas from more established firms.

Synset Examples

He plans to poach ideas from competitors for his project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steal - to take something without permission

Etymology

From Middle English 'pocchen' (to poke), later adapted to mean illegal hunting, and figuratively extended to ideas.
