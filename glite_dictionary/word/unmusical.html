unmusical

1. ADJ

/ ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (AmE), / ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (BrE)
Not having melody or musical quality; lacking ability to produce or appreciate music.
lacking musical quality or ability

Examples

The singer's voice was surprisingly unmusical despite her years of training.
He found the constant banging of the radiator rather unmusical and annoying.
Her performance was criticized as being flat and unmusical compared to her peers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tuneless - lacking a tune or melody
  • Antonym: musical - having a pleasant melody, or related to music

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'un-' (not) and 'musical,' from Latin 'musicus' (of music).

2. ADJ

/ ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (AmE), / ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (BrE)
not having interest in or talent for music; not musical.
lacking musical talent or interest

Examples

Although her family played several instruments, she remained completely unmusical.
It was obvious from his singing that he was rather unmusical.
His unmusical background made learning the piano especially challenging.

Synset Examples

too unmusical to care for concerts
it is unfortunate that her children were all nonmusical

Related Terms

  • Antonym: musical - having talent for or an interest in music

Etymology

From un- (not) + musical; patterned after 'musical'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01509465-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (AmE), / ʌnˈmjuːzɪkəl / (BrE)
not having a pleasant or harmonious sound; lacking melody or musical quality
not musical; lacking melody or harmony

Examples

The bird's unmusical cry startled everyone in the garden.
Her unmusical voice made the song difficult to enjoy.
Despite his efforts, the melody remained strangely unmusical and discordant.

Synset Examples

the unmusical cry of the bluejay

Related Terms

  • Antonym: musical - having the qualities of music; melodious or harmonious

Etymology

From the prefix un- (not) + musical.
