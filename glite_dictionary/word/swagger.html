swagger

1. NOUN

/ ˈswæɡər / (AmE), / ˈswæɡə / (BrE)
A proud, confident, and sometimes arrogant manner of walking.
a proud, confident walk

Examples

He entered the room with a noticeable swagger, drawing everyone's attention.
Her confident swagger made it clear she was in charge.
There was a touch of swagger in his steps after winning the award.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strut - to walk in a proud, stiff way
  • Degree Lower: walk - to move at a regular pace by lifting and setting down each foot in turn

Etymology

Probably from Scots swagger (to sway, lurch), of imitative origin.

Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈswæɡər / (AmE), / ˈswæɡə / (BrE)
to walk in a confident and sometimes arrogant or boastful way, often to impress others
walk confidently to impress

Examples

He tried to swagger into the meeting, pretending to be in control.
The actors swagger across the stage, embodying their confident characters.
She couldn't help but swagger a little after winning the award.

Synset Examples

He struts around like a rooster in a hen house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strut - to walk with a stiff, erect, and proud gait
  • Degree Lower: saunter - to walk in a slow, relaxed manner, without hurry or effort

Etymology

Probably imitative of the act of swaying or lurching, or an alteration of 'swag'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01920627-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈswæɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈswæɡə / (BrE)
to walk, behave, or speak in an overly confident and arrogant way, often to impress others
walk or act with arrogant confidence

Examples

He likes to swagger into meetings as if he owns the company.
The young athlete swaggered across the field, soaking up the applause.
She was swaggering about her recent promotion at every opportunity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strut - to walk with a proud or vain gait
  • Antonym: humble - to act in a modest or unassuming way

Etymology

Probably from earlier 'swag' meaning to sway or lurch, possibly influenced by Norwegian 'svagga' (to sway) or related Scandinavian sources.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00012427-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈswæɡər / (AmE), / ˈswæɡə / (BrE)
to discourage or frighten someone with threats or a domineering manner; to intimidate
discourage or intimidate with threats

Examples

The villain tried to swagger the witnesses into silence with his menacing glare.
She refused to let anyone swagger her out of telling the truth in court.
They attempted to swagger their way through the negotiations, but the opposition stood firm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intimidate - to frighten or overawe, especially to make someone do what one wants
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support, confidence, or hope to someone

Etymology

Probably from Scots 'swagger' (to sway, lurch), of Scandinavian origin.

Source: WordNet
