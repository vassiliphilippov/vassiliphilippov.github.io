fancy

1. ADJ

/ ˈfæn.si / (AmE), / ˈfæn.si / (BrE)
Elaborate, decorative, or ornamented in style; not plain or simple.
decorative or ornamented

Examples

She wore a fancy dress covered in sequins to the party.
They served dessert on fancy china plates reserved for special occasions.
His handwriting is so fancy that it's hard to read.

Synset Examples

fancy handwriting
fancy clothes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: plain - not decorated or elaborate; simple in design

Etymology

From Middle English 'fantasie', from Old French 'fantaisie', from Latin 'phantasia' (imagination), later specialized in sense.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01798849-a
Synset POS: a

2. VERB

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
to imagine or think of something as possible; to conceive in the mind
imagine or conceive of
british

Examples

I can't fancy him ever admitting he was wrong.
Do you fancy yourself living in Paris someday?
She fancied she heard a voice calling her name.

Synset Examples

I can't see him on horseback!
I can see what will happen
I can see a risk in this strategy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imagine - to form a mental image of something; suppose
  • Synonym: to envision - to picture something in one's mind
  • Antonym: doubt - to feel uncertain about; lack belief in

Etymology

From Middle English 'fance', contraction of 'fantasy' from Old French 'fantasie', from Latin 'phantasia' (imagination).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01638974-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
a liking, fondness, or preference for someone or something, often fleeting or whimsical
a liking or preference (often brief)

Examples

He developed a sudden fancy for learning to play the piano.
Her fancy for exotic foods faded after her trip to Asia.
It was only a passing fancy, but it left a lasting impression on him.

Synset Examples

he had a fondness for whiskey
she had dismissed him quite brutally, relegating him to the status of a passing fancy, or less

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fondness - a strong liking or affection for someone or something
  • Antonym: aversion - a strong dislike or disinclination

Etymology

From Middle English 'fantasie', from Old French, from Latin 'phantasia', from Greek 'phantasia' (appearance, imagination).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07512937-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
Imagination or fantasy; a faculty of forming mental images or concepts not present to the senses, considered by some writers as more superficial than true imagination.
imagination or fantasy, esp. superficial

Examples

His wild fancy conjured up entire new worlds.
The poet's fancy was captured by the beauty of the landscape.
Coleridge distinguished between fancy and true imagination in his writings.

Synset Examples

never had the wildest flights of fancy imagined such magnificence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imagination - The faculty of forming new ideas, concepts, or images not present to the senses
  • Synonym: fantasy - The faculty or activity of imagining impossible or improbable things

Etymology

From Middle English 'fancy', contraction of 'fantasy', from Old French 'fantaisie', from Latin 'phantasia'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05640055-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
to want or feel a liking for something; to desire, especially in a spontaneous or passing way
to want or desire (something)
british

Examples

Do you fancy a cup of tea?
I didn't really fancy going out last night, so I stayed in.
She really fancies that new restaurant down the street.

Synset Examples

She fancied a necklace that she had seen in the jeweler's window

Related Terms

  • Synonym: want - to desire something
  • Degree Higher: to crave - to have a strong desire for
  • Antonym: dislike - to not like or wish for something

Etymology

From Middle English 'fanten' (to fantasize), from 'fantasy'.
