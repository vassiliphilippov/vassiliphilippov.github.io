Petromyzon

1. NOUN

/ ˌpɛtrəˈmaɪzən / (AmE), / ˌpɛtrəˈmɪzɒn / (BrE)
A genus of jawless fish known as lampreys, having elongated bodies and a sucker-like mouth, found in freshwater and coastal seas.
a genus of lampreys (jawless fish)

Examples

The Petromyzon is known for its circular, sucker-like mouth used to attach to other fish.
Biologists have studied Petromyzon to understand the evolution of vertebrates.
Larval stages of Petromyzon can spend several years buried in riverbeds before maturing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lamprey - any of several jawless fish of the family Petromyzontidae

Etymology

From Greek 'petra' (rock) + 'myzon' (to suck), referring to their habit of attaching to rocks or fish.

2. NOUN

/ ˌpɛtroʊˈmaɪzɒn / (AmE), / ˌpiːtrəˈmɪzɒn / (BrE)
A genus of jawless fish, commonly known as lampreys, found in temperate rivers and streams.
genus of jawless lamprey fish

Examples

The Petromyzon is often studied for its primitive nervous system.
While fishing, they spotted a Petromyzon clinging to a rock in the river.
Researchers observed the migration patterns of Petromyzon in the Great Lakes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lamprey - a jawless, eel-like parasitic fish

Etymology

From Greek 'petra' (stone) + 'myzon' (to suck), referring to the lamprey's habit of attaching to rocks or fish.
