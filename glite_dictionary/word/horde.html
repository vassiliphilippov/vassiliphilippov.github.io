horde

1. NOUN

/ hɔrd / (AmE), / hɔːd / (BrE)
A large group of people or things, especially one moving together or considered as an unruly crowd.
a large, moving crowd or group

Examples

A horde of tourists flooded the streets during the summer festival.
The store was overrun by a horde of bargain hunters on Black Friday.
A horde of locusts swept across the field, devouring everything in their path.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - a large group of people gathered together
  • Synonym: throng - a large, densely packed crowd of people
  • Synonym: mob - a large, disorderly crowd of people

Etymology

From Turkish *ordu* (camp, army), via Russian *orda* and Polish *horda*.

2. NOUN

/ hɔrd / (AmE), / hɔːd / (BrE)
A large group of people, especially one that moves quickly and is often perceived as unruly or threatening.
large, moving crowd

Examples

A horde of fans rushed towards the stage after the concert ended.
Every summer, a horde of tourists descends upon the small coastal town.
The store was swarmed by a horde of shoppers on Black Friday.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - A large number of people gathered together
  • Synonym: throng - A densely packed crowd of people or animals
  • Synonym: mob - A large and disorderly crowd

Etymology

From Turkish 'ordu' (camp, army), via Russian 'orda'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ hɔrd / (AmE), / hɔːd / (BrE)
A large group or tribe, especially one of nomads in Central Asia or Eastern Europe, typically moving together as a community.
nomadic community or tribe

Examples

In ancient times, the Mongol horde swept across much of Asia and Europe.
The historians studied the movement of each horde across the steppes.
Legend tells of a horde that established a great empire by uniting rival clans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tribe - a social group consisting of people with the same culture, language, or ancestor

Etymology

From Turkish 'ordu' (camp, army), via Russian 'orda'.
