suckling

1. NOUN

/ ˈsʌk.lɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsʌk.lɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of feeding an infant by allowing it to suck at the breast
feeding an infant at the breast

Examples

The mother devoted many hours each day to the suckling of her newborn.
Proper suckling is vital for providing nutrients and building infant immunity.
He observed the bond formed between mother and child during suckling.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: breastfeeding - the act of feeding a baby with milk from the breast

Etymology

From Middle English *sokelyng*, from Old English *sucan* (to suck) + -ling (diminutive suffix).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌklɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsʌklɪŋ / (BrE)
an infant or young animal that is still being fed with its mother's milk or, for humans, an infant who has not yet been weaned.
infant or animal still nursing

Examples

The mother gently cradled her suckling in her arms as she sang a lullaby.
The lioness kept her suckling cubs close to protect them from danger.
In ancient times, the health of a suckling was often closely linked to the mother's well-being.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: weanling - a young animal or infant that has just been weaned

Etymology

From Middle English 'sokelyng', formed from 'suckle' (to nurse) + '-ing'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10387990-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈsʌk.lɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsʌk.lɪŋ / (BrE)
a young mammal that is still feeding on its mother's milk and has not yet been weaned
young mammal not yet weaned

Examples

The farmer kept the sucklings separate from the adult sheep for their own protection.
A lioness is fiercely protective of her suckling cubs.
The vet checked the health of the sucklings in the barn.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: weanling - a young animal recently weaned from its mother

Etymology

From the verb 'suckle' + -ing, meaning one that is being suckled.
