vulgarisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌvʌlɡərəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvʌlɡəraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of making something simpler or more attractive so that it is accessible to the general public, often by removing technical details
making something accessible to the public
british

Examples

The vulgarisation of science has helped more people understand complex research.
She criticized the vulgarisation of classical literature in modern adaptations.
Many educational programs aim at the vulgarisation of difficult topics for younger audiences.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: popularization - the act of making something popular or accessible to the public; U.S. spelling

Etymology

From French vulgarisation, from Latin vulgaris (common, of the people).

2. NOUN

/ ˌvʌlɡərəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvʌlɡəraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of making something coarse, less refined, or more commonplace, often leading to a loss of original quality or sophistication
making something coarse or unrefined

Examples

Many critics argue that the vulgarisation of classical art diminishes its cultural significance.
The vulgarisation of public discourse has been a subject of concern for educators.
Some believe that reality television has led to the vulgarisation of modern entertainment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coarsening - the process of making something less delicate or refined
  • Antonym: refinement - the process of making something more elegant or cultured

Etymology

From French 'vulgarisation' and Latin 'vulgaris' (common, of the crowd).
