to undertake

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to agree formally or contractually to do something; to enter into an obligation or responsibility
formally agree to do something

Examples

The company will undertake to complete the project by the end of the year.
She agreed to undertake the role of legal advisor, signing the contract yesterday.
Before we undertake any new commitments, let's review the existing agreements.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commit - to pledge or bind to a certain course or policy

Etymology

From Middle English 'undertaken', from under- + taken (to take), influenced by Old Norse 'taka' (to take).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to formally promise or commit to do something or take responsibility for it
promise or commit to do

Examples

The company has undertaken to provide free repairs for any faulty products.
I undertake to complete the project by the agreed deadline.
She undertook to organize the conference herself.

Synset Examples

guarantee to free the prisoners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: promise - to assure someone that one will do something
  • Synonym: guarantee - to give a formal assurance or undertaking

Etymology

From Middle English 'undertaken', from under + take, influenced by Old English 'under-' (among, between) and 'tacan' (to take).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to begin or take responsibility for an activity, project, or task; to commit oneself to doing something
begin or take on a task or responsibility

Examples

She decided to undertake the responsibility of organizing the team's annual trip.
The company will undertake a major expansion next year.
Before you undertake such a big project, make sure you have a clear plan.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commence - to begin or start something
  • Antonym: finish - to bring a task or activity to an end

Etymology

From Middle English 'undertaken', meaning to take on, from under + take.

to agree to do something challenging, especially a task or responsibility

Examples

Etymology

5. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to agree to do or be responsible for something, especially a task, job, or responsibility
accept responsibility for a task or job

Examples

She agreed to undertake the difficult project despite her reservations.
The company will undertake a full investigation into the matter.
I cannot undertake such a large responsibility on my own.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to take on - to assume responsibility for something
  • Antonym: refuse - to decline to do or accept something

Etymology

From Middle English 'undertaken', from under- + taken (to take).
