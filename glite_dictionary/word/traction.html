traction

1. NOUN

/ ˈtræk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˈtræk.ʃən / (BrE)
(medicine) the application of a sustained pulling force to a limb or bone, especially to realign or immobilize it during healing.
medical pulling force for healing bones
medical

Examples

After the accident, his broken leg was placed in traction for two weeks to ensure it healed correctly.
The doctor recommended traction to realign the patient’s fractured femur.
Children with certain bone injuries may require long-term traction in hospital settings.

Synset Examples

his leg was in traction for several days

Etymology

From Latin tractio (a drawing or pulling), from trahere (to draw, pull).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtræk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˈtræk.ʃən / (BrE)
the friction between a moving body and the surface it moves on, allowing it to move or stay in place, especially in reference to vehicles
friction between a moving object and its surface

Examples

The car lost traction on the icy road and began to slide.
Good tires can improve traction in wet or slippery conditions.
Mountain bikes are designed to provide better traction on rough terrain.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: friction - the resistance that one surface or object encounters when moving over another

Etymology

From Latin tractio (a drawing, pulling), from trahere (to pull).
