to dramatize

1. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to present or represent something in a dramatic or theatrical way; to make something appear more exciting or important than it really is
make something more dramatic or theatrical

Examples

The film dramatizes the struggles of the early suffragettes in Britain.
She tends to dramatize minor disagreements to get attention.
The report dramatized the impact of the crisis to increase public concern.

Synset Examples

These events dramatize the lack of social responsibility among today's youth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - represent something as larger, better, or worse than it really is

Etymology

From Greek drama, meaning 'action' or 'play', via French dramatiser.

WordNet ID: oewn-00990319-v

2. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to adapt (a story, novel, or real event) into a play, film, or other dramatic form
convert into dramatic form

Examples

The director decided to dramatize the true events for the movie adaptation.
They plan to dramatize the classic novel for Broadway next year.
Several authors have dramatized historical incidents to appeal to modern audiences.

Synset Examples

adopt a book for a screenplay

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adapt - to make something suitable for a new use or purpose
  • Degree Lower: to novelize - to convert into the form of a novel

Etymology

From Greek dramatizein, from drama (action, play).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01705170-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to make something more vivid, exciting, or exaggerated by adding details, often to attract greater attention or interest
make more vivid or exaggerated

Examples

Reporters often dramatize the facts to create a more compelling story.
She tends to dramatize minor problems, making them seem much worse than they are.
The movie dramatizes historical events by adding fictional characters and dialogue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - to represent as greater or more intense than it really is
  • Antonym: to downplay - to make something appear less important or significant

Etymology

From Greek drama (action, play) + -ize, via Late Latin dramatizare.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00958437-v
Synset POS: v
