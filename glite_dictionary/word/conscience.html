conscience

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːn.ʃəns / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.ʃəns / (BrE)
a person's inner sense of right and wrong that guides their actions and produces feelings of guilt or shame when they do something immoral
inner sense of right and wrong

Examples

He couldn't escape his conscience after lying to his best friend.
Her strong conscience prevented her from cheating on the exam.
A guilty conscience often keeps people awake at night.

Synset Examples

he has no conscience about his cruelty

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guilt - a feeling of responsibility or remorse for a real or imagined offense
  • Synonym: scruple - a feeling of doubt or hesitation about the morality of an action
  • Antonym: shamelessness - lack of shame or moral restraint

Etymology

From Latin 'conscientia' (knowledge within, sense of right and wrong), from 'conscire' (to know with).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːnʃəns / (AmE), / ˈkɒnʃəns / (BrE)
A part of the mind that tells a person whether what they are doing is morally right or wrong; an inner sense of what is right or wrong in one's conduct or motives.
inner sense of right and wrong

Examples

Her conscience told her it was wrong to lie to her friend.
He struggled with his conscience after making a difficult decision at work.
I couldn't ignore my conscience any longer and confessed the truth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: morality - principles concerning the distinction between right and wrong or good and bad behavior
  • Degree Lower: guilt - a feeling of having committed wrong or failed in an obligation

Etymology

From Latin 'conscientia' (knowledge within oneself, sense of right), from 'con-' (with) + 'scire' (to know).
