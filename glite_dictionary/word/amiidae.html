Amiidae

1. NOUN

/ ˌæmiˈaɪɪdi / (AmE), / ˌæmɪˈaɪɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of freshwater fishes known as bowfins, native to North America
family of freshwater fishes (bowfins)

Examples

The Amiidae are characterized by a single living species, the bowfin.
Recent studies have explored the evolutionary history of the Amiidae family.
Fossil records indicate that Amiidae once had a much greater diversity than today.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bowfin - a long-bodied predatory freshwater fish native to North America

Etymology

From modern Latin Amiidae, from Amia (the type genus) + -idae (family suffix in zoological taxonomy).

2. NOUN

/ ˌeɪ.miˈɪ.di.i / (AmE), / ˌæm.iˈɪ.diː.iː / (BrE)
A family of freshwater fish known as the bowfins, found mainly in North America.
family of freshwater fish (bowfins)

Examples

The Amiidae are considered living fossils among modern freshwater fish.
There is only one extant species in the Amiidae, known as the bowfin.
Paleontologists have found Amiidae fossils dating back to the Jurassic period.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bowfin - a primitive freshwater fish of North America, the only living member of the family Amiidae

Etymology

From Neo-Latin Amiidae, from genus Amia + -idae (family suffix in zoological taxonomy).
