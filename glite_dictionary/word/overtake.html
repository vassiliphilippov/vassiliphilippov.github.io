to overtake

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to go past another vehicle or person that is moving in the same direction
go past (esp. a vehicle/person)

Examples

She watched the motorcycle overtake the slow-moving truck on the highway.
It can be dangerous to overtake another car on a sharp bend.
As the runner tired, two competitors managed to overtake him before the finish line.

Synset Examples

The sports car passed all the trucks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pass - to go past another vehicle on the road

Etymology

From Old English ofertācan (over + take), meaning 'to overtake, come up with.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02053645-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈteɪk / (BrE)
to catch up with and then move ahead of someone or something, especially while traveling in the same direction
catch up with and pass

Examples

The sports car managed to overtake the truck on the highway.
She tried to overtake her rival in the final lap of the race.
We had to speed up to overtake the slow-moving tractor ahead of us.

Synset Examples

The Rolls Royce caught us near the exit ramp

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pass - to go past or move ahead of

Etymology

From Old English ofertēacan, from ofer (over) + tēacan (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01157556-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈteɪk / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈteɪk / (BrE)
(of emotions or sensations) to affect someone strongly and suddenly, so that they are unable to control their reaction.
be overcome by (emotion or feeling)

Examples

A sudden wave of panic overtook her as she entered the dark alley.
He was overtaken by grief when he heard the news.
As exhaustion overtook him, he could barely keep his eyes open.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to overwhelm - to have a strong emotional effect on

Etymology

From Old English 'overtacan', from 'over' + 'tacan' (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01813288-v
Synset POS: v
