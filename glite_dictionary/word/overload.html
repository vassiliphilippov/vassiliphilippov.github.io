overload

1. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌloʊd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvələʊd / (BrE)
An electrical load that exceeds the available amount of electrical power, causing a risk of damage or system failure.
excessive electrical load

Examples

An overload can trip the circuit breaker to prevent electrical fires.
If you plug too many devices into one outlet, you risk causing an overload.
The technician traced the power failure back to an overload in the main supply line.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overcurrent - an excessive current in an electrical circuit

Etymology

From over- (exceeding) + load (burden to be carried or supplied).

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈləʊd / (BrE)
to put too much of something into or on something, so that it cannot function properly
fill too much; impair function

Examples

Please do not overload the washing machine as it may stop working.
She overloaded her mind with information and ended up feeling confused.
The website crashed because too many users overloaded the server at once.

Synset Examples

Fear clogged her mind
The story was clogged with too many details

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overburden - give (someone) too much work or responsibility
  • Antonym: underload - not making full use of capacity or ability

Etymology

From over- (excessive) + load (burden or weight).

5. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvərˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈləʊd / (BrE)
an excessive amount or load that exceeds what is normal, safe, or manageable; an excessive burden
excessive burden or load

Examples

The electrical circuit failed due to an overload of appliances being used at once.
After taking on three new projects, he was feeling the overload at work.
Mental overload can lead to increased stress and burnout.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burden - a load, typically a heavy one; something carried or endured
  • Synonym: to overwhelm - to give too much of something; to overpower or overload
  • Antonym: underload - a load that is less than normal or desired

Etymology

From over- (excessive) + load (something carried), first attested in the 19th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03870532-n
Synset POS: n
