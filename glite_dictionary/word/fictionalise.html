to fictionalise

1. VERB

/ ˈfɪkʃənəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɪkʃənəlaɪz / (BrE)
to change a real event or situation so that it is partly or wholly made up, often in the style of a novel or story
make into fiction or a novel
british

Examples

The director chose to fictionalise the events to make the film more dramatic.
Many biographers fictionalise parts of their subjects' lives to fill in historical gaps.
The novel fictionalises actual crimes from the 1920s, blending fact and invention.

Synset Examples

The author novelized the historical event

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fictionalize - convert into a fictional form (US spelling)
  • Synonym: to novelize - to turn into or adapt as a novel

Etymology

From 'fiction' + '-alise', modelled on words like 'realise'.

2. VERB

/ ˈfɪkʃənəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɪkʃənəlaɪz / (BrE)
to present a real person, event, or situation as fiction by altering or inventing details
make something into fiction
british

Examples

The author decided to fictionalise her childhood experiences for her debut novel.
Many films fictionalise historical events to create a more engaging story.
He was accused of having fictionalised the facts in his memoir.

Synset Examples

The writer fictionalized the lives of his parents in his latest novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fictionalize - make into fiction (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'fiction' + '-alise', the British spelling of the suffix corresponding to American '-alize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01638718-v
Synset POS: v
