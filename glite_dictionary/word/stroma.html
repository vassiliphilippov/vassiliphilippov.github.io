stroma

1. NOUN

/ ˈstroʊmə / (AmE), / ˈstrəʊmə / (BrE)
The connective and supporting tissue of an organ, as distinguished from the functional tissue (parenchyma).
supporting tissue of an organ
medical

Examples

The stroma provides the structural framework for the kidney.
In some tumors, an abundance of stroma can affect the progression of the disease.
The function of the stroma is distinct from that of the parenchyma.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: parenchyma - the functional tissue of an organ as opposed to the supporting or connective tissue

Etymology

From Greek *strōma* (bed, mattress, coverlet), meaning something spread out.

2. NOUN

/ ˈstroʊ.mə / (AmE), / ˈstrəʊ.mə / (BrE)
The colorless, dense, fluid-filled framework inside a chloroplast that supports its structure and contains enzymes and other molecules needed for photosynthesis.
colorless framework in a chloroplast
medical

Examples

The stroma contains the enzymes essential for the light-independent reactions of photosynthesis.
Scientists studied how molecules move within the stroma of spinach chloroplasts.
During photosynthesis, sugars are produced in the stroma by the Calvin cycle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chloroplast - a plastid containing chlorophyll where photosynthesis takes place

Etymology

From Greek 'stroma' (covering, mattress, bed).

3. NOUN

/ ˈstroʊmə / (AmE), / ˈstrəʊmə / (BrE)
A mass of fungal tissue in which spore-bearing structures are embedded or on which they are borne.
fungal tissue mass with spore structures

Examples

The fungus developed a dense stroma that supported the formation of fruiting bodies.
Microscopic examination revealed that the spore sacs were embedded in the stroma.
Researchers studied the morphology of the stroma to classify the new fungal species.

Etymology

From Greek *stroma* meaning 'mattress' or 'bed', referring to a supporting layer.
