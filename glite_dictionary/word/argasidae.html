Argasidae

1. NOUN

/ ɑrˈɡæsɪdi / (AmE), / ɑːˈɡæsɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of soft-bodied ticks distinguished by their flexible bodies and absence of a hard dorsal shield, many of which act as parasites on birds and mammals.
family of soft-bodied ticks

Examples

The Argasidae include several species that transmit diseases to livestock.
Unlike hard ticks, members of the Argasidae have soft, leathery bodies and lack a scutum.
Scientists studied the feeding habits of Argasidae to better understand their role as disease vectors.

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'argas' (a kind of tick) with the suffix '-idae' denoting a zoological family.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɑːrɡəˈsaɪdiː / (AmE), / ˌɑːɡəˈsiːdiː / (BrE)
a family of soft ticks characterized by their leathery bodies and lack of a hard dorsal shield, often found as parasites of birds and mammals
family of soft ticks
medical

Examples

The Argasidae are noteworthy for their lack of a hard dorsal shield, distinguishing them from other tick families.
Several species within the Argasidae family are known to transmit diseases to livestock and humans.
Unlike hard ticks, members of the Argasidae often inhabit bird nests and bat roosts.

Etymology

From New Latin 'Argasidae', based on the genus 'Argas' and the standard zoological suffix '-idae' indicating a family.
