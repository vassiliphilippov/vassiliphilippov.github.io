flagellate

1. NOUN

/ fləˈdʒɛlət / (AmE), / fləˈdʒɛlɪət / (BrE)
a single-celled organism, often a protozoan, that moves using one or more whip-like appendages called flagella; some are free-living, while others can cause disease in humans and animals
protozoan with whip-like appendages

Examples

Giardia is a well-known flagellate that can cause intestinal infections in humans.
Researchers observed the movement of a flagellate under the microscope.
Some flagellates are important indicators of water quality in aquatic environments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protozoan - a single-celled microscopic organism

Etymology

From Latin 'flagellum' (whip) + '-ate' (forming adjectives and nouns).

2. ADJ

/ ˈflædʒəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈflædʒɪlət / (BrE)
having or resembling a lash or whip, especially in reference to structures like a flagellum.
having or resembling a whip-like structure

Examples

Some protists are flagellate, using whip-like appendages for movement.
The flagellate structure of certain bacteria enables them to swim efficiently.
Under the microscope, the flagellate cells were easy to identify by their distinctive tails.

Etymology

From Latin flagellum (whip, scourge), diminutive of flagrum (whip).

3. VERB

/ ˈflædʒəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈflædʒɪleɪt / (BrE)
to whip or flog someone or oneself, typically as a form of punishment or religious discipline
to whip or flog

Examples

During the ceremony, some devotees flagellate themselves as a sign of penance.
In medieval times, monks would flagellate themselves to atone for their sins.
The fanatic flagellated himself daily as part of his religious rituals.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whip - to strike with a flexible instrument such as a lash
  • Synonym: to flog - to beat with a whip or stick as punishment

Etymology

From Latin flagellatus, past participle of flagellare (to whip), from flagellum (whip).
