embolism

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛm.bə.lɪ.zəm / (AmE), / ˈɛm.bə.lɪ.zəm / (BrE)
the insertion of a day or month into a calendar, such as in leap years
insertion of a day or month into a calendar

Examples

The ancient Romans used embolism to keep their lunar calendar in sync with the solar year.
A calendar embolism often involves adding a leap day or a leap month.
The reform introduced a system of embolism to address inconsistencies in the old dating method.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intercalation - insertion of days or months into a calendar

Etymology

From Greek embolismos (intercalation), from emballein (to throw in)

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛmbəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈɛmbəlɪzəm / (BrE)
the sudden blockage of a blood vessel by an embolus, such as a blood clot, air bubble, or other particle carried in the bloodstream
blockage of a blood vessel by an embolus
medical

Examples

The patient suffered an embolism that blocked the artery in his leg.
Doctors explained that an embolism can quickly become life-threatening if not treated promptly.
Air bubbles entering the bloodstream during surgery may cause an embolism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrombosis - formation of a blood clot inside a blood vessel
  • Synonym: embolus - a mass, such as a clot or air bubble, that causes an embolism
  • Degree Lower: ischemia - insufficient blood supply to an organ or part of the body

Etymology

From Greek embolismos (insertion), from emballein (to throw in).
