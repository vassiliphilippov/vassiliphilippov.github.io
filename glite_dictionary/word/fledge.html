to fledge

1. VERB

/ flɛdʒ / (AmE), / flɛdʒ / (BrE)
to care for and rear (young birds) until they are ready to fly
rear young birds for flight

Examples

The parents will fledge their chicks over the next few weeks before they leave the nest.
Birdwatchers observed the robins as they fledged their young in the spring.
Many species fledge their offspring with impressive devotion and patience.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rear - to care for and raise (children, young animals, or plants)

Etymology

Probably from Old English *flycge* meaning 'ready to fly'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02545848-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ flɛdʒ / (AmE), / flɛdʒ / (BrE)
(of a young bird) to develop feathers necessary for flight.
grow feathers (of a young bird)

Examples

The chicks will fledge in about three weeks after hatching.
As soon as the sparrows fledged, they left the safety of the nest.
Many bird species fledge earlier in warmer climates than in colder ones.

Synset Examples

The young sparrows are fledging already

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fledgling - a young bird that has just developed feathers; an inexperienced person

Etymology

From Old English *flycge* (having feathers or fit to fly).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00094066-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ flɛdʒ / (AmE), / flɛdʒ / (BrE)
to decorate or provide (something, especially an arrow) with feathers
decorate with feathers, esp. for arrows

Examples

The craftsman skillfully fledged each arrow to ensure perfect flight.
In traditional archery, it is important to fledge the arrows properly for accuracy.
She decided to fledge her costume mask with bright, colorful feathers.

Synset Examples

fledge an arrow

Etymology

From Old English 'flēogan' (to fly), related to feathers for flying and their use on arrows.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01673687-v
Synset POS: v
