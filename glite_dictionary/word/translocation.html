translocation

1. NOUN

/ ˌtrænzloʊˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænzləʊˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the movement of dissolved substances from one part of a plant to another, especially through the phloem
transport of dissolved material within a plant

Examples

The process of translocation ensures that sugars produced in the leaves are distributed to other parts of the plant.
Scientists studied the rate of translocation to understand how nutrients move during plant growth.
A blockage in the phloem can affect translocation and harm the wellbeing of the entire plant.

Etymology

From Latin translocare (to move from one place to another), from trans- (across) + locare (to place).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtrænsloʊˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌtrænzləʊˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Genetics) an exchange of parts between nonhomologous chromosomes, which can result in a genetic abnormality.
exchange of chromosome parts (genetics)
medical

Examples

A chromosomal translocation was identified in the patient's genetic analysis.
Certain types of leukemia can be caused by a specific translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22.
The scientist explained how a translocation might disrupt gene function and cause disease.

Synset Examples

translocations can result in serious congenital disorders

Etymology

From Latin 'translocare' (to move to a different place), from 'trans-' (across) + 'locare' (to place).
