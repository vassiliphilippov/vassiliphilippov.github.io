fosterage

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɑːstərɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfɒstərɪdʒ / (BrE)
The act of encouraging or aiding the development and growth of something.
encouragement; aiding development

Examples

The university provided considerable fosterage for research projects in renewable energy.
Her fosterage of young artists led to a vibrant arts community in the city.
The government’s fosterage of innovation has resulted in numerous technological advancements.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: encouragement - The action of giving someone support, confidence, or hope.

Etymology

From Old English *fostrian* (to nourish, foster) and the suffix -age, indicating action or process.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɑːstərɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfɒstərɪdʒ / (BrE)
the practice of bringing up a child that is not one's own, often with the aim of helping them grow up as an accepted member of the community
bringing up a non-biological child

Examples

In many ancient cultures, fosterage was a common way to establish alliances between families.
The concept of fosterage often involved not just care, but also instruction in community values.
Debates about nature versus nurture sometimes mention practices like fosterage in historical societies.

Synset Examples

they debated whether nature or nurture was more important

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adoption - the act of legally taking a child to be the same as one's own
  • Synonym: nurture - the process of caring for and encouraging the growth or development of someone

Etymology

From Old English 'fostrian' (to nourish, rear).
