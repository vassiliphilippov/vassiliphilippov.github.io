to smother

1. VERB

/ ˈsmʌðɚ / (AmE), / ˈsmʌðə / (BrE)
To cover something completely, especially with a thick substance or in a way that makes it difficult to see or access.
cover completely, envelop

Examples

She likes to smother her pancakes in syrup every morning.
The chef will smother the vegetables in a rich, creamy sauce.
Snow began to smother the garden, hiding all signs of the plants underneath.

Synset Examples

smother the meat in gravy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cover - to put something over or on top of something else
  • Antonym: to uncover - to remove a covering from something

Etymology

From Middle English 'smotheren' (to suffocate).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈsmʌðɚ / (AmE), / ˈsmʌðə / (BrE)
to extinguish (a fire) by depriving it of oxygen, typically by covering it
put out a fire by cutting off oxygen

Examples

The quickest way to smother a small kitchen fire is to cover it with a metal lid.
Firefighters worked to smother the flames before they could spread to the next building.
You should never use water to smother a grease fire; cover it instead.

Synset Examples

smother fires

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to extinguish - to cause a fire or light to stop burning

Etymology

From Middle English smotheren, related to Middle English smother (thick smoke, suffocating vapor).

5. VERB

/ ˈsmʌðɚ / (AmE), / ˈsmʌðə / (BrE)
to deprive (someone) of air and prevent them from breathing, typically causing death by suffocation
deprive of air; suffocate

Examples

He tried to smother the flames, but nearly suffocated from the smoke.
She was accused of attempting to smother her husband while he slept.
The villain in the novel plotted to smother his victim with a cushion.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suffocate - to die or cause to die from lack of air

Etymology

From Middle English *smotheren*, from *smother* (thick smoke), of uncertain origin.
