deduction

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the process of reaching a conclusion about something based on general principles, applying them to specific cases; reasoning from the general to the particular
reasoning from general to specific

Examples

Through careful deduction, the detective determined who the real culprit was.
Mathematics often relies on deduction to prove theorems from established axioms.
Her deduction that the weather would worsen was based on the darkening clouds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: induction - reasoning from specific cases to general principles

Etymology

From Latin deductio (a leading away, conclusion), from deducere (to lead down).

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the act of subtracting an amount from a total, especially from money legally owed or due
subtraction of a part from a whole, often money

Examples

There will be a deduction for late submission of the assignment.
The company made a deduction for his missed days of work.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

4. NOUN

/ dɪˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈdʌkʃ(ə)n / (BrE)

Examples

Her deduction that the meeting was canceled proved to be correct when no one arrived.
After analyzing the data, we came to the deduction that the experiment had failed.
