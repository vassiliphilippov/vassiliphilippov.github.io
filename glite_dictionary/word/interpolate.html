to interpolate

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtɜːr.pə.leɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɜː.pə.leɪt / (BrE)
to insert words or passages into a text, often in a way that alters its original meaning or authenticity
insert words into a text

Examples

The author was accused of having interpolated several passages into the original manuscript.
Some lines were clearly interpolated by later editors, changing the meaning of the poem.
Historians often debate whether certain phrases in ancient texts were interpolated after the fact.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put or introduce into something

Etymology

From Latin interpolare (to alter, refurbish), from inter- (between) + polire (to polish).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00201771-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtɜːr.pə.leɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɜː.pə.leɪt / (BrE)
To estimate or calculate a value within two known values in a sequence of values.
estimate a value within known values

Examples

We can interpolate the missing data points using the values from before and after the gap.
Given two temperature readings, she decided to interpolate the value at noon.
The software can automatically interpolate intermediate frames to create smoother animations.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to extrapolate - to estimate a value outside the known range

Etymology

From Latin 'interpolare' (to polish or alter), from 'inter-' (between) + 'polire' (to polish).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
