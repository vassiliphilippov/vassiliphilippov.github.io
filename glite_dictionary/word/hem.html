hem

1. VERB

/ hɛm / (AmE), / hɛm / (BrE)
to fold the edge of cloth over and sew it down to prevent unravelling or to finish the edge
fold and sew the edge of cloth

Examples

Could you hem this dress for me by Friday?
She learned to hem her own skirts in sewing class.
The tailor will hem your trousers to the right length.

Synset Examples

hem my skirt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sew - to join or attach with needle and thread

Etymology

From Old English *hemm* (a border, hem of a garment).

2. NOUN

/ hɛm / (AmE), / hɛm / (BrE)
a sound like clearing the throat, used to attract attention, show hesitation, fill a pause, or express mild embarrassment
throat-clearing sound to signal hesitation or attract attention

Examples

She gave a discreet hem before beginning her speech, signaling she was nervous.
There was an awkward hem from the back of the room as he tried to get the teacher's attention.
He filled the silence with a quiet hem, unsure how to respond to the question.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ahem - the utterance 'ahem' to attract attention or express hesitation

3. VERB

/ hɛm / (AmE), / hɛm / (BrE)
to make a short sound such as 'hem' or 'ahem', often to attract attention or clear the throat
to make a sound like 'ahem'

Examples

He hemmed loudly to let everyone know he had something to say.
Whenever she was nervous, she would hem before speaking.
The lecturer paused and hemmed, searching for the right words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ahem - an interjection used to attract attention or clear one's throat

4. NOUN

/ hɛm / (AmE), / hɛm / (BrE)
the edge of a piece of cloth, especially where it has been folded under and sewn to prevent unravelling
finished edge of cloth

Examples

She noticed that the hem of her skirt was coming loose during the meeting.
The tailor carefully measured the hem before making adjustments to the pants.
A bit of lace was sewn along the hem to add a decorative touch.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seam - a line where two pieces of fabric are sewn together

Etymology

Old English *hemm* (border, hem), of Germanic origin.
