to enchant

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtʃænt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtʃɑːnt / (BrE)
to attract someone strongly and make them feel delight or admiration
attract, captivate someone

Examples

The singer's voice managed to enchant everyone in the audience.
Children are often enchanted by stories of magical lands and brave heroes.
Her warm smile and kind words enchanted all who met her.

Synset Examples

She captured all the men's hearts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to captivate - to attract and hold the interest of someone
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel uninterested or dull

Etymology

From Old French enchanter, from Latin incantare (to chant a magic spell).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtʃænt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtʃɑːnt / (BrE)
to greatly delight or fascinate someone, often as if by magic; to hold someone's attention completely
to fascinate or hold spellbound

Examples

The magician's performance managed to enchant the entire audience.
The fairy tale continues to enchant children around the world.
She was enchanted by the beauty of the ancient forest.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to captivate - attract and hold the interest of someone
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel uninterested or dull

Etymology

From Old French 'enchanter', from Latin 'incantare' (to chant or cast a spell).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01821281-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈtʃænt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtʃɑːnt / (BrE)
to cast a spell on someone or something; to magically charm or bewitch
cast a magical spell on

Examples

The witch tried to enchant the prince with a powerful spell.
Stories tell of wizards who could enchant objects to make them move.
The sorcerer enchanted the forest, making it come alive at night.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bewitch - to cast a magic spell over someone
  • Antonym: curse - to call upon a supernatural power to inflict harm

Etymology

From Old French enchanter, from Latin incantare (to chant a magic spell).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00779033-v
Synset POS: v
