quail

1. NOUN

/ ˈkweɪl / (AmE), / ˈkweɪl / (BrE)
the flesh of a quail, eaten as food, especially when roasted or broiled if young
edible flesh of a quail

Examples

The restaurant's special was grilled quail served with wild rice and vegetables.
She prepared roasted quail with a honey glaze for the holiday dinner.
He prefers quail over chicken because of its richer flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chicken - flesh of a domestic fowl as food
  • Synonym: duck - flesh of a duck as food

Etymology

From Old French *quaille*, from Medieval Latin *quaccula* (quail, the bird).

2. VERB

/ kweɪl / (AmE), / kweɪl / (BrE)
to feel or show fear; to shrink back in fear or apprehension
shrink back in fear

Examples

Despite his bold words, he began to quail at the thought of facing his accuser.
She did not quail in the face of danger, but met the crisis with courage.
Many students quail before their first major public presentation.

Synset Examples

she flinched when they showed the slaughtering of the calf

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flinch - to make a quick, nervous movement as a reaction to fear or pain
  • Synonym: wince - to make a slight involuntary movement due to pain or distress
  • Antonym: brace - to prepare (oneself) for something difficult or unpleasant

Etymology

From Middle English quailen, perhaps from Old French quaillir (to waver, retreat).

3. NOUN

/ kweɪl / (AmE), / kweɪl / (BrE)
a small, short-tailed game bird with a rounded body, found chiefly in Europe, Asia, and North America, and often hunted for sport or food.
a small game bird

Examples

The hunter spotted a quail darting through the underbrush early in the morning.
She ordered roasted quail served with wild rice at the restaurant.
A flock of quail settled in the field just beyond the farmhouse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: partridge - a medium-sized game bird related to the quail
  • Degree Higher: pheasant - a larger game bird often hunted for sport

Etymology

From Middle Dutch quāel or Old French quaille, both meaning 'quail'.
