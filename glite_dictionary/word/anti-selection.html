anti-selection

1. NOUN

/ ˌænti səˈlɛkʃən / (AmE), / ˌanti sɪˈlɛkʃən / (BrE)
A situation in which one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other, often leading to unfavorable outcomes for the less informed party, especially in insurance and finance.
asymmetric information in transactions

Examples

The insurance company raised premiums to protect themselves against the effects of anti-selection.
Actuaries study anti-selection risks to develop better policy terms for clients.
Anti-selection can lead to higher costs and instability in financial markets.

Synset Examples

The insurance company investigators realized new policy-holder Louis was an adverse selection shortly after he was insured, when a slew of illnesses suddenly sent him to the hospital numerous times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adverse selection - the tendency of those in dangerous jobs or high-risk lifestyles to get insurance

Etymology

From prefix anti- (against) + selection, referring to the process where the selection process works against the interests of one party.
