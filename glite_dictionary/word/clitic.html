clitic

1. NOUN

/ ˈklɪtɪk / (AmE), / ˈklɪtɪk / (BrE)
A word or particle that is pronounced as part of a neighboring word, often lacking its own stress and sometimes contracted or attached to another word.
word pronounced as part of another

Examples

In spoken French, the pronoun 'je' is a clitic that often merges with the following verb.
The particle 'n't' in English, as in 'can't,' is considered a clitic form of 'not.'
Linguists study how a clitic attaches to a host word in different languages.

Synset Examples

Most languages can adopt theme-rheme structure idiosyncratically — as for English, we often use as for theme constructions — but topic-prominent languages use systematic changes in syntax or even dedicated morpological elements such as the Japanese clitic particle ‘-wa’ to mark themes and to set them apart from rhemes.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: affix - a morpheme added to a word to modify its meaning
  • Degree Higher: word - a single distinct meaningful element of speech or writing

Etymology

From Greek *klitikos* (able to lean), from *klinein* (to lean).
