motivation

1. NOUN

/ ˌmoʊ.t̬ɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌməʊ.tɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the reason or reasons behind a person's actions or behavior; the desire or willingness to do something
reason for acting; drive

Examples

She lost her motivation to exercise after the race was over.
Good teachers know how to inspire motivation in their students.
His main motivation for volunteering was to help others.

Synset Examples

we did not understand his motivation
he acted with the best of motives

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incentive - something that encourages or motivates someone to take action
  • Antonym: apathy - lack of motivation or interest

Etymology

From Latin 'motivus' (moving) via French 'motivation'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmoʊ.t̬ɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌməʊ.tɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the state or condition of being motivated, especially having enthusiasm or a strong reason to act or work
state of being motivated

Examples

After the holiday break, her motivation to exercise regularly was much higher.
Teachers often try new methods to boost students' motivation in the classroom.
His motivation to learn the language came from wanting to travel abroad.

Synset Examples

his motivation was at a high level

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drive - strong motivation to achieve something
  • Antonym: apathy - lack of interest, enthusiasm, or motivation

Etymology

From Latin motivus (moving), related to 'motive', plus the noun-forming suffix -ation.

3. NOUN

/ ˌmoʊ.tɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌməʊ.tɪˈveɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of encouraging someone to act or achieve something, especially by providing a reason or incentive
the act of motivating or providing incentive

Examples

The coach's speech gave the players the motivation they needed to win the game.
Teachers often use praise as a form of motivation to inspire their students.
She lacked the motivation to finish the project until there was a reward involved.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incentive - something that encourages someone to do something
  • Antonym: discouragement - the act of making someone less confident or willing to do something

Etymology

From Latin 'motivus' (moving), from 'movere' (to move), with '-ation' forming a noun.

Source: WordNet
