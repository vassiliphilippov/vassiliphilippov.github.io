foliaceous

1. ADJ

/ foʊliˈeɪʃəs / (AmE), / fəʊliˈeɪʃəs / (BrE)
having numerous leaves or being leaf-like in appearance, especially in a botanical context.
having many leaves or leaf-like

Examples

The foliaceous branches of the shrub created dense shade in the garden.
This plant is known for its foliaceous stems, which resemble large green leaves.
Some algae have foliaceous structures that allow them to absorb sunlight efficiently.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leafy - having many leaves
  • Antonym: leafless - lacking leaves

Etymology

From Latin 'foliaceus' (of leaves), from 'folium' (leaf).

2. ADJ

/ ˌfoʊliˈeɪʃəs / (AmE), / ˌfəʊliˈeɪʃəs / (BrE)
(of a plant part) having the nature or appearance of a leaf; resembling or pertaining to a leaf.
having the characteristics of a leaf

Examples

The foliaceous appendages help the mantis blend in with the surrounding vegetation.
Some algae display foliaceous forms, appearing flat and leaf-like under the microscope.
The mineral exhibited a foliaceous texture, resembling thin sheets similar to leaves.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leafy - having many leaves or covered with leaves

Etymology

From Latin folium (leaf) + -aceous (having the nature of).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02740838-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ foʊliˈeɪʃəs / (AmE), / fəʊliˈeɪʃəs / (BrE)
(especially of rocks or minerals) having thin, leaflike layers or strata; resembling or composed of thin leaves.
having thin, leaflike layers or strata

Examples

The metamorphic rock displayed a distinctly foliaceous structure visible in its thin, parallel layers.
Some minerals in the collection were classified as foliaceous due to their leaflike sheets.
The geologist explained that the foliaceous texture is typical of certain schists and gneisses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: laminar - composed of thin layers; layered in form

Etymology

From Latin 'foliaceus' (leafy), from 'folium' (leaf).
