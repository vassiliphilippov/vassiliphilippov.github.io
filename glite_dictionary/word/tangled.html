tangled

1. ADJ

/ ˈtæŋɡəld / (AmE), / ˈtæŋɡəld / (BrE)
twisted together in a confused or disordered mass, making it difficult to separate or untangle
twisted together in a confused mass

Examples

Her tangled hair took a long time to brush in the morning.
The old fisherman struggled with the tangled nets on the boat.
Branches and vines formed a tangled barrier across the narrow path.

Synset Examples

pushed back her tangled hair
the tangled ropes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: matted - twisted or sticking together in a thick mass, especially hair or wool

Etymology

From the verb 'tangle', likely of Scandinavian origin (compare Swedish 'tangla').

2. ADJ

/ ˈtæŋɡəld / (AmE), / ˈtaŋɡəld / (BrE)
Extremely complicated or difficult to understand; intricate and sometimes deceitful in nature.
complicated; intricate

Examples

The negotiations became so tangled that no one could follow the discussions any longer.
The novel's plot is full of tangled relationships and surprising twists.
She tried to explain the tangled rules, but it only made things more confusing.

Synset Examples

the Byzantine tax structure
Byzantine methods for holding on to his chairmanship
convoluted legal language
convoluted reasoning
the plot was too involved
a knotty problem
got his way by labyrinthine maneuvering
Oh, what a tangled web we weave
tortuous legal procedures
tortuous negotiations lasting for months

Related Terms

  • Synonym: complex - not simple; made up of many interconnected parts
  • Antonym: simple - easily understood or done; not complicated

Etymology

From Middle English tanglen (to entangle), frequentative of tangen (to tangle), of Scandinavian origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02184401-s
Synset POS: s
