galvanisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɡæl.və.nəˈzeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɡæl.və.naɪˈzeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of coating iron or steel with a layer of zinc to protect it from rusting, or the process of applying a protective metal coating using a galvanic current
coating metal with zinc or by galvanic current

Examples

The galvanisation of steel beams is essential to prevent rust in humid environments.
After galvanisation, the metal surface appeared shiny and resistant to corrosion.
Modern construction relies heavily on galvanisation to extend the lifespan of iron products.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: galvanization - Alternative spelling, chiefly American English

Etymology

From French 'galvanisation', from Italian scientist Luigi Galvani, whose work with electricity led to the term.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɡælvənaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɡælvənaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the stimulation of living tissue with a galvanic (direct) electric current, especially for medical or physiological purposes
stimulation with an electric current
medical

Examples

The physiotherapist applied galvanisation to stimulate muscle contraction in the patient's leg.
Early experiments with galvanisation demonstrated the effects of electricity on nerve cells.
Doctors once used galvanisation as a treatment for paralysis and other nervous disorders.

Etymology

From French *galvanisation*, after Luigi Galvani (1737–1798), Italian physicist.

3. NOUN

/ ˌɡælvəˌnaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɡælvənaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
Stimulation or shock that causes people to take lively or rapid action.
arousing stimulation or incitement to action

Examples

The unexpected speech acted as a galvanisation for the movement's supporters.
Her words were a much-needed galvanisation that revived our enthusiasm for the project.
After months of inaction, the crisis served as a galvanisation for change.

Synset Examples

the unexpected news produced a kind of galvanization of the whole team

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stimulus - something that causes a reaction or activity
  • Synonym: incitement - the act of urging one to action

Etymology

From French galvanisation, based on the name of Luigi Galvani (1737–1798), Italian scientist.
