singsong

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪŋˌsɔŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋˌsɒŋ / (BrE)
an informal gathering where people sing popular songs together
informal group singing event

Examples

After dinner, everyone gathered by the fire for a cheerful singsong.
The pub was filled with laughter and singsong late into the night.
At camp, they held a singsong under the stars every Friday.

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɪŋˌsɔŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋˌsɒŋ / (BrE)
spoken or pronounced with a monotonous, rhythmic, or chantlike cadence.
monotonous, chantlike speech

Examples

She recited the poem in a singsong voice that put the children to sleep.
His explanation came out in a singsong tone, making it hard to take seriously.
The tour guide spoke with a singsong rhythm that was oddly hypnotic.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɪŋˌsɔŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋˌsɒŋ / (BrE)
A regular and monotonous rising and falling intonation in speech, often lacking natural variation.
monotonous rising and falling intonation

Examples

The teacher read the story in a singsong that made the students drowsy.
His voice took on a singsong when he recited the poem aloud.
Children often chant rhymes in a playful singsong during recess.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monotone - a continuing sound, especially of a person's voice, that is unchanging in pitch
Source: WordNet

4. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋˌsɔŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋˌsɒŋ / (BrE)
to speak, chant, or recite with a rhythmic, musical, and repetitive tone, often with little variation in pitch
speak or chant in a musical, repetitive tone

Examples

She singsonged the nursery rhymes to soothe the children.
The teacher singsongs the alphabet as part of the lesson.
He was singsonging the poem, making it sound almost like a lullaby.

Etymology

From 'sing' + 'song', originally as a noun meaning repetitive musical intonation.
