Infusoria

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fjuˈzɔr.i.ə / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fjuˈzɔː.ri.ə / (BrE)
(historical) Minute aquatic microorganisms, especially ciliates, found in decaying organic matter or infusions, once classified as a distinct group in older biological taxonomy.
minute aquatic microorganisms (historical term)
archaic

Examples

Under the microscope, the drop of pond water was teeming with Infusoria.
In the nineteenth century, scientists debated the classification of Infusoria.
Fish fry often feed on Infusoria in aquariums during their early growth stages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ciliate - a type of protozoan with hair-like organelles called cilia

Etymology

From New Latin infusoria, referring to organisms found in infusions; from Latin infusio (a pouring in).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪnˌfjuzˈɔriə / (AmE), / ˌɪnˌfjʊˈzɔːrɪə / (BrE)
A former taxonomic group of single-celled microscopic organisms, especially those with cilia, commonly found in decaying organic matter.
microscopic ciliated organisms (obsolete taxonomy)

Examples

Infusoria are often observed under the microscope in samples of pond water.
The biologist explained how Infusoria were once considered a distinct group of microorganisms.
Historically, Infusoria included various species now classified elsewhere in protozoan taxonomy.

Etymology

From New Latin, from Latin infusus (infused) + -oria.
