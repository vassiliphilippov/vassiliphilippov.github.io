ejector

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈdʒɛktər / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
A device or pump that uses a jet of fluid, typically steam or water, to induce the flow of another fluid.
device that draws fluid using a jet

Examples

The steam ejector was used to create a vacuum in the condenser.
A ejector pump can move fluids without moving parts in contact with the liquid.
Technicians installed a new ejector to improve the efficiency of the cooling system.

Synset Examples

Depending on the specific application, an injector can take the form of an eductor-jet pump, a water eductor, a vacuum ejector, a steam-jet ejector, or an aspirator.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: injector - device that introduces fluid into something, often under pressure

Etymology

From Latin ‘eiectus’, past participle of ‘eicere’ (to throw out).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈdʒɛktər / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
a mechanism in a firearm that expels the empty shell casing after a round is fired
mechanism that expels fired shell casings

Examples

The ejector failed to expel the spent casing, causing a jam in the rifle.
After firing, the ejector automatically pushes out the empty shell from the chamber.
A broken ejector can prevent a gun from working reliably.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extractor - a device in a firearm that withdraws the cartridge case from the chamber

Etymology

From Latin 'ejectus', past participle of 'eicere' (to throw out), combined with the English agentive suffix '-or'.

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈdʒɛktər / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
a person who forces someone else out of a position, place, or role; someone who ousts or supplants another
person who ousts or supplants another

Examples

The board relied on an outside ejector to resolve the leadership dispute.
He became the ejector of his predecessor, replacing her as CEO.
In ancient times, the victor often acted as the ejector of the previous ruler.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evictor - person who evicts someone, especially from property
  • Synonym: to oust - to remove or force out of a position

Etymology

From Latin 'ejectus', past participle of 'eicere' (to throw out) + English '-or'
