taro

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːrəʊ / (BrE)
A tropical plant with large leaves, cultivated in warm regions for its starchy tuberous root, which is eaten as a vegetable.
tropical starchy root vegetable

Examples

Many traditional dishes in Hawaii use taro as a staple ingredient.
She bought fresh taro from the Asian market to make a creamy soup.
The soft, sweet flavor of taro makes it popular in desserts and beverages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yam - another edible starchy tuber

Etymology

From Polynesian (Hawaiian "kalo", Samoan "talo") or Pacific origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːrəʊ / (BrE)
a tropical plant with large, glossy leaves, grown for its starchy, edible root, and also as an ornamental plant
tropical plant with edible root

Examples

Taro is a staple food in many Pacific Island cultures, often boiled or mashed.
We bought some taro roots from the Asian market to try in a new recipe.
The large, heart-shaped leaves of the taro plant add a lush look to gardens.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: colocasia - another name for taro or the genus it belongs to

Etymology

From Polynesian, cf. Hawaiian 'kalo', Samoan 'talo'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːrəʊ / (BrE)
A tropical plant grown for its large, starchy edible root, used as a staple food in many parts of the world; also, the root itself.
tropical starchy root vegetable

Examples

Taro is a staple food in many Pacific Islands, where the taro root is boiled or steamed.
The chef prepared a delicious curry using freshly grated taro.
Farmers harvested the muddy taro fields at the end of the rainy season.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yam - starchy tuber similar to taro but from a different plant family
  • Synonym: sweet potato - another starchy edible root commonly used as food

Etymology

From Polynesian (Samoan, Maori, Hawaiian) 'talo', adopted into English by the late 18th century.
