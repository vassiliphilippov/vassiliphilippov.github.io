poleaxe

1. VERB

/ ˈpoʊˌlæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊlˌæks / (BrE)
to strike down or knock someone to the ground as if with a heavy weapon
to strike down forcefully

Examples

The boxer was poleaxed by a powerful right hook.
The news of his dismissal poleaxed him into silence.
She felt as though she had been poleaxed by the shocking announcement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fell - to knock down (someone or something)

Etymology

From 'poleaxe,' a medieval weapon combining an ax and hammer mounted on a long handle.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpoʊlˌæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊlˌæks / (BrE)
a medieval weapon consisting of a long-handled axe with a blade and often a spike or hammer head, used in battle and sometimes in executions
medieval long-handled battle axe

Examples

The knight carried a poleaxe into battle to break through enemy armor.
A well-preserved poleaxe was found in the ruins of the ancient fortress.
In medieval reenactments, the poleaxe is often used to demonstrate historical combat techniques.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: halberd - a combined spear and battle-axe used especially in the 15th and 16th centuries

Etymology

From Middle English 'poleax', from 'poll' (head) + 'axe'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpoʊlˌæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊlˌæks / (BrE)
A type of axe with a hammer on the side opposite the blade, traditionally used for slaughtering cattle.
axe used in slaughterhouses

Examples

The butcher used a poleaxe to quickly slaughter the cattle.
Historical records show that the poleaxe was a common tool in old abattoirs.
Inspectors found an antique poleaxe among the ancient farming equipment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: axe - a tool with a heavy bladed head used for chopping

Etymology

From Old French *poleis*, from poll (head) + axe; originally referring to a weapon for striking the head.

4. VERB

/ ˈpoʊlˌæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊlˌæks / (BrE)
to strike or affect with a sudden, devastating blow or shock, especially to one's emotions or expectations.
to deliver a sudden emotional shock

Examples

The sudden layoff poleaxed everyone in the department, leaving them in disbelief.
His confidence was poleaxed by the harsh criticism from the judges.
She was poleaxed when she heard the unexpected bad news.

Synset Examples

The sudden news felt like a poleaxe to her hopes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to devastate - to cause great shock or pain to someone
  • Synonym: to stun - to shock or surprise someone very much

Etymology

From Middle English 'polax', from Old French 'poleis', originally referring to a type of battle axe.
