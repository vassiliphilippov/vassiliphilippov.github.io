disillusion

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɪsɪˈluʒən / (AmE), / ˌdɪsɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
the process or state of being freed from false beliefs or illusions, especially resulting in disappointment
freedom from false beliefs or illusions

Examples

His disillusion with politics began when he realized promises were rarely kept.
Traveling abroad was a source of disillusion as her idealized views were challenged by reality.
After years of optimism, the harsh facts brought about a painful disillusion.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: illusion - a false idea or belief
  • Synonym: enlightenment - the act of gaining knowledge and truth, often bringing freedom from illusion

Etymology

From French 'désillusion' (disappointment on learning the truth), based on Latin 'illusio' (mockery, illusion).

2. VERB

/ ˌdɪsɪˈluːʒən / (AmE), / ˌdɪsɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
to cause someone to realize that a belief or ideal is false or mistaken, removing their illusions or enchantment
to free from false belief or illusion

Examples

The harsh reality of war finally disillusioned him.
She was disillusioned by the company’s broken promises and left soon after.
Parents sometimes disillusion their children when they tell them the truth about fairy tales.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disabuse - to persuade someone that a belief or idea is mistaken
  • Antonym: to enchant - to charm or fill with delight; to put under a spell

Etymology

From French désillusionner, from dés- (dis-) + illusion (illusion).
