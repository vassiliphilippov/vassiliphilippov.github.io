deference

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛf.ɚ.əns / (AmE), / ˈdɛf.ər.əns / (BrE)
polite respect shown for someone else's opinions, wishes, or feelings
respectful or polite regard

Examples

The staff spoke to the judge with great deference.
He gave up his seat out of deference to the elderly woman.
In deference to her parents' wishes, she postponed the trip.

Synset Examples

in deference to your wishes
out of respect for his privacy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration or esteem
  • Antonym: disregard - lack of consideration or respect

Etymology

From French 'déférence', from Latin 'deferre' (to yield, to bring down).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɛfərəns / (AmE), / ˈdɛfərəns / (BrE)
polite submission and respect for someone or something
respectful submission or regard

Examples

Out of deference to the elderly guests, everyone waited for them to be seated first.
She spoke with deference to the panel of experts during the interview.
In deference to his teacher's wishes, he changed his research topic.

Synset Examples

his deference to her wishes was very flattering
be sure to give my respects to the dean

Related Terms

  • Synonym: respect - a feeling or expression of admiration or regard
  • Antonym: disrespect - lack of respect or courtesy

Etymology

From French déférence, from Latin deferre (to yield).

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɛfərəns / (AmE), / ˈdɛfərəns / (BrE)
polite or respectful submission and yielding to the judgment, wishes, or opinion of another
respectful submission or yielding

Examples

The soldiers bowed in deference to their commanding officer.
She showed deference to her elders during the meeting.
In deference to his wishes, we postponed the discussion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something elicited by their abilities, qualities, or achievements
  • Antonym: disobedience - refusal to comply with rules or someone in authority

Etymology

From Latin 'deferre' (to bring down, yield), via French 'déférence' (respect, regard).
