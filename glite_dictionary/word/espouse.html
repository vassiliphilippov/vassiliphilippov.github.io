to espouse

1. VERB

/ ɪˈspaʊz / (AmE), / ɪˈspaʊz / (BrE)
to adopt or support a cause, theory, or belief
adopt or support a cause or belief

Examples

The company decided to espouse a new environmental policy to reduce its carbon footprint.
She openly espouses equal rights for all citizens, regardless of background.
Many politicians choose to espouse popular causes during their campaigns to gain support.

Synset Examples

She followed the feminist movement
The candidate espouses Republican ideals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adopt - to take up or start to use an idea or method
  • Antonym: reject - to refuse to accept, believe in, or agree with something

Etymology

From Old French 'espouser' (to marry), from Latin 'sponsare' (to betroth).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈspaʊz / (AmE), / ɪˈspaʊz / (BrE)
to adopt or support a cause, belief, or way of life as one's own
adopt or support a cause or belief

Examples

She decided to espouse the environmental movement after learning about climate change.
Many philosophers espouse rationality as the primary basis for ethics.
The company has espoused a strict policy of sustainability in all its operations.

Synset Examples

She embraced Catholicism
They adopted the Jewish faith

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adopt - to take up or start to use something, such as a practice or belief
  • Antonym: reject - to refuse to accept, believe in, or agree with something

Etymology

From Old French 'espouser' (to marry), from Latin 'sponsare', from 'spondere' (to pledge).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00603217-v
Synset POS: v
