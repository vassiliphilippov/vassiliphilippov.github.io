hornpipe

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɔrnˌpaɪp / (AmE), / ˈhɔːnˌpaɪp / (BrE)
a traditional British solo dance, often associated with sailors, characterized by lively steps and distinctive rhythms
a British dance performed by sailors
british

Examples

The sailor expertly performed a hornpipe on the deck, to the delight of the crew.
During the festival, children competed in a lively hornpipe contest.
Many folk dance groups include the hornpipe in their repertoire of traditional dances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jig - a lively folk dance, usually in compound meter
  • Synonym: reel - a lively folk dance type from the British Isles

Etymology

From hornpipe (the musical instrument) originally used to accompany the dance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɔrnˌpaɪp / (AmE), / ˈhɔːnˌpaɪp / (BrE)
a lively piece of music written for the hornpipe dance, characterized by its distinctive rhythm.
music for the hornpipe dance

Examples

The band played a lively hornpipe that got everyone on their feet.
She practiced the hornpipe on her violin for the upcoming dance competition.
The concert featured several traditional folk tunes, including a spirited hornpipe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reel - a type of lively folk dance and its music
  • Synonym: jig - a lively folk dance and its music
  • Antonym: waltz - a slow ballroom dance and its music in triple time

Etymology

From Middle English hornpipe, originally a musical instrument made of horn and pipe; later referring to the dance and its music.

3. NOUN

/ ˈhɔrnˌpaɪp / (AmE), / ˈhɔːnˌpʌɪp / (BrE)
an ancient single-reed woodwind instrument, usually made of bone or horn, now obsolete
obsolete ancient woodwind instrument
archaic

Examples

The shepherd played a hornpipe as the sun set over the hills.
Archaeologists discovered a hornpipe at the ancient settlement site.
References to the hornpipe can be found in several medieval manuscripts.

Etymology

From horn + pipe, referring to the materials from which the instrument was traditionally made.
