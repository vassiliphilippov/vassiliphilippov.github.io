to cultivate

1. VERB

/ ˈkʌl.tɪˌveɪt / (AmE), / ˈkʌl.tɪ.veɪt / (BrE)
to prepare and use land for crops or gardening, or to grow a plant by caring for it
prepare land or grow plants

Examples

Farmers have to cultivate the land before planting crops each spring.
She learned how to cultivate wild herbs in the garden behind her house.
Over many years, early settlers cultivated large areas of previously untouched land.

Synset Examples

domesticate oats
tame the soil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to domesticate - to tame or adapt plants or animals for human use

Etymology

From Latin 'cultivare', from 'cultus' (to till, care for).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkʌltɪˌveɪt / (AmE), / ˈkʌltɪveɪt / (BrE)
to develop, improve, or refine (a quality, taste, or skill) through education or training
develop or refine taste, skill, or quality

Examples

She tried to cultivate a taste for classical music in her children.
Reading widely can help you cultivate a critical mind.
He has cultivated an appreciation for fine art over the years.

Synset Examples

Cultivate your musical taste
Train your tastebuds
She is well schooled in poetry

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to develop - to cause something to grow or become more advanced
  • Synonym: to improve - to make or become better
  • Antonym: neglect - to fail to care for or pay attention to

Etymology

From Latin 'cultivare' (to till), from 'cultus' (care, cultivation).

4. VERB

/ ˈkʌl.təˌveɪt / (AmE), / ˈkʌl.tɪ.veɪt / (BrE)
to prepare and work land in order to grow crops
prepare land for crops

Examples

Farmers must cultivate their fields each spring before planting seeds.
The land was carefully cultivated to ensure a good harvest.
They spent hours cultivating the soil to prepare it for vegetables.

Synset Examples

Work the soil
cultivate the land

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plow - to turn up the earth with a plow, especially before sowing

Etymology

From Latin 'cultivare', from 'cultus' (tilled, cultivated).
