luff

1. NOUN

/ lʌf / (AmE), / lʌf / (BrE)
the act of sailing a boat as close to the wind as possible without losing forward movement
sailing close to the wind

Examples

During the regatta, the skipper executed a sharp luff to gain a better position.
Proper timing of the luff can help sailors avoid losing speed while changing direction.
An unexpected luff caught the inexperienced crew off guard, causing the sails to flap.

Etymology

From Middle English 'luff' (part of a ship), from Old French 'lof' (windward side of a ship).

2. VERB

/ lʌf / (AmE), / lʌf / (BrE)
(of a sailing vessel) to turn its head toward the wind so that the sails flap and lose effective propulsion; to sail closer to the wind.
to turn a sailing vessel toward the wind

Examples

The captain ordered the crew to luff as the wind shifted suddenly.
If you luff too much, the sails will lose their power and begin to flap.
She skillfully luffed the yacht to avoid hitting the buoy.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to bear away - to steer a sailing vessel away from the wind

Etymology

From Middle English 'lof' (the weather side of a ship), probably from Middle Dutch or Low German.

3. VERB

/ lʌf / (AmE), / lʌf / (BrE)
(of a sail) to flap or flutter when the wind blows equally on both sides, usually because it is turned too far into the wind.
(of a sail) to flap in the wind

Examples

When the wind shifted suddenly, the mainsail began to luff near the harbor entrance.
If you point the boat too far into the wind, the jib will luff and lose power.
The skipper shouted for the crew to trim the sails before they started to luff.

Synset Examples

the sails luffed

Etymology

From Middle English *lof* (the weather side of a ship), from Middle Dutch *loef*.

Source: WordNet

4. NOUN

/ lʌf / (AmE), / lʌf / (BrE)
(nautical) The forward edge of a fore-and-aft sail, nearest to the mast.
forward edge of a sail near the mast

Examples

The sailor adjusted the luff to catch more wind.
You must keep the luff smooth for optimal sailing performance.
Damage to the luff can affect the sail's efficiency.

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *loef* (weather side of a ship, luff), adopted into nautical English in the 14th century.
