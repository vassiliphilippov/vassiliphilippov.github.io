to perforate

1. VERB

/ ˈpɜr.fəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɜː.fər.eɪt / (BrE)
to make a line of small holes in something, especially to make it easier to tear
make holes for easy separation

Examples

The tickets were perforated so they could easily be torn apart.
Many notebooks have perforated pages for quick removal.
Please perforate the form along the dotted line before handing it in.

Synset Examples

perforate the sheets of paper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: puncture - to pierce or make a small hole in something

Etymology

From Latin 'perforatus', past participle of 'perforare' (to bore through), from 'per-' (through) + 'forare' (to bore).

2. VERB

/ ˈpɜr.fəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɜː.fəˌreɪt / (BrE)
to make a hole or holes through something, especially by passing through or piercing it
make a hole through; pierce

Examples

The surgeon needed to perforate the membrane to relieve the pressure.
Certain bacteria can perforate cell walls, causing infection.
The artist used a needle to perforate intricate patterns onto the paper.

Synset Examples

The bullet penetrated her chest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pierce - to penetrate or make a way through something
  • Synonym: to penetrate - to succeed in entering or passing into or through something

Etymology

From Latin 'perforatus', past participle of 'perforare', from 'per-' (through) + 'forare' (to bore, pierce).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
