buoyancy

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɔɪ.ən.si / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪ.ən.si / (BrE)
the ability or tendency of an object to float in water or another fluid
ability to float in fluid

Examples

The buoyancy of the boat kept it safely above the water's surface.
Salt water increases the buoyancy of swimmers, making it easier to float.
Submarines control their buoyancy to dive or rise in the ocean.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: floatation - the process or capability of floating on water or other liquid

Etymology

From French *bouée* (buoy) and -ancy, or based on 'buoyant'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɔɪ.ən.si / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪ.ən.si / (BrE)
A cheerful and optimistic attitude that is lively and uplifting.
cheerful, optimistic attitude

Examples

Even after the disappointing result, her buoyancy helped the whole team stay positive.
His natural buoyancy made him a joy to work with in difficult times.
Despite many setbacks, she always maintained a sense of buoyancy that inspired others.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cheerfulness - the state of being noticeably happy and optimistic
  • Antonym: gloom - a state of depression or sadness

Etymology

From French *bouée* (buoy) and -ancy, ultimately from Latin *boia* (a fetter for the feet).

4. NOUN

/ ˈbɔɪənsi / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪənsi / (BrE)
the ability of something to float in water or air, or remain uplifted; the quality of being light or insubstantial
ability to float or be light

Examples

The buoyancy of the balloon allowed it to rise gently into the sky.
Helium gives the blimp enough buoyancy to float above the stadium.
She faced her challenges with the same buoyancy that always kept her spirits high.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: floatation - the act, process, or state of floating or causing to float
  • Antonym: density - the degree of compactness of a substance, often contrasted with buoyancy

Etymology

From French *buée* (steam, vapor), from Old French *buée* (damp, vapor), related to 'buoy'.
