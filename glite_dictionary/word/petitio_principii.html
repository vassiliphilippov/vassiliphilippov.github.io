petitio principii

1. NOUN

/ pəˌtɪtioʊ prɪnˈsɪpi.aɪ / (AmE), / pɛˌtɪti.əʊ prɪnˈkɪpi.aɪ / (BrE)
A logical fallacy in which the conclusion to be proved is assumed implicitly or explicitly in the premises; also called begging the question.
logical fallacy: assuming the conclusion

Examples

The philosopher accused his opponent of petitio principii for building the conclusion into the premise.
Many circular arguments are examples of petitio principii in logical reasoning.
It's important in debate to avoid petitio principii, as it undermines the soundness of your position.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: begging the question - The logical fallacy of assuming what needs to be proved

Etymology

From Latin 'petitio principii' (assuming the initial point).
