neutralization

1. NOUN

/ ˌnutʃrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnjuːtrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of counteracting or nullifying the effect of something, especially an earlier action or force
the act of counteracting something

Examples

The new policy was a neutralization of the previous administration's more aggressive stance.
Chemical neutralization is often necessary to safely dispose of certain industrial wastes.
His unexpected apology acted as a neutralization of the anger that had built up between them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counteraction - the act of working against or neutralizing a force

Etymology

From French 'neutralisation', from 'neutraliser' (to neutralize), based on Latin 'neutralis' (neutral).

2. NOUN

/ ˌnuːtrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnjuːtrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the chemical reaction in which an acid and a base react to form a salt and water, usually resulting in a solution that is neither strongly acidic nor basic
reaction of acid and base to form salt and water

Examples

The neutralization of hydrochloric acid with sodium hydroxide produces salt and water.
During a titration, the point of neutralization is indicated by a color change in the indicator.
Industrial waste treatment often relies on the neutralization of acidic or basic effluents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acid-base reaction - a chemical reaction between an acid and a base
  • Synonym: to neutralize - to make (an acidic or basic substance) chemically neutral

Etymology

From French 'neutralisation', from 'neutraliser' (to neutralize), from Latin 'neutralis' (neutral).

3. NOUN

/ ˌnuːtrələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnjuːtrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
(euphemism) The removal of a threat by killing or destroying it, especially during a covert or military operation.
military euphemism for killing or destroying a threat

Examples

The military reported the neutralization of several enemy targets during the raid.
Intelligence agencies often use the term neutralization to refer to the elimination of high-level threats.
The operation's success hinged on the swift neutralization of the opposition's leadership.

Etymology

From neutralize + -ation; "neutralize" from French "neutraliser", based on Latin "neuter" (neither).

4. NOUN

/ ˌnuːtrəlɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnjuːtrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the action or process of making a country neutral in a conflict or excluding it from involvement in war, especially by diplomatic or political means
making a country neutral in war or politics

Examples

The neutralization of Belgium was a key concern for European diplomats in the early 20th century.
International treaties sometimes call for the neutralization of certain territories to promote peace.
During the conflict, the neutralization of smaller states was often discussed as a strategy to prevent escalation.
