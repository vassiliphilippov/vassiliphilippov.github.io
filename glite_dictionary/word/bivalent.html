bivalent

1. ADJ

/ ˈbaɪ.və.lənt / (AmE), / ˈbaɪ.və.lənt / (BrE)
(of homologous chromosomes) paired together during synapsis in meiosis.
chromosomes paired in meiosis
medical

Examples

During meiosis, each bivalent consists of two homologous chromosomes joined together.
The scientist observed the formation of a bivalent under the microscope.
Improper pairing of chromosomes can result in an abnormal bivalent configuration.

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two) + 'valentia' (strength, capacity).

2. ADJ

/ ˈbaɪ.vəˌlɛnt / (AmE), / ˈbʌɪ.və.lənt / (BrE)
(of an atom, ion, or molecule) having a valence of two; capable of forming two bonds.
having a valence of two
medical

Examples

Oxygen is a bivalent element because it forms two chemical bonds in most compounds.
The vaccine is bivalent, targeting two different strains of the virus.
In some reactions, the ion acts as a bivalent species, forming two covalent bonds.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: monovalent - having a valence of one
  • Degree Higher: trivalent - having a valence of three

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two) + 'valentia' (strength, capacity), referring to chemical valence.
