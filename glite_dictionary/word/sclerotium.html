sclerotium

1. NOUN

/ skləˈroʊtiəm / (AmE), / sklɪəˈrəʊtiəm / (BrE)
A form genus of sterile, imperfect fungi known for forming hard masses (sclerotia); some species cause plant diseases.
genus of fungi forming sclerotia
medical

Examples

The soil was infected with sclerotium, causing the crops to wilt and die.
Researchers studied the life cycle of sclerotium to understand how it spreads in plants.
Certain plants are particularly vulnerable to sclerotium infestations, which can devastate harvests.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fungus - a group of spore-producing organisms feeding on organic matter

Etymology

From Greek *sklērotos* (hardened), referencing the hardened structures formed by these fungi.

2. NOUN

/ skləˈroʊʃiəm / (AmE), / skləˈrəʊtɪəm / (BrE)
A compact, usually dark mass of hardened fungal mycelium that serves as a vegetative food-storage body; it detaches when mature and can produce new growth.
hardened fungal food-storage structure
medical

Examples

When conditions become unfavorable, some fungi form a sclerotium to survive.
The sclerotium can remain dormant in the soil for years before germinating.
Scientists studied the development of the sclerotium in pathogenic fungi.

Etymology

From Greek 'sklērotos' (hardened), related to 'sklērós' (hard).
