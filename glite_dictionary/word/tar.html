tar

1. NOUN

/ tɑr / (AmE), / tɑː / (BrE)
an informal or archaic term for a sailor, especially in the British or American navy
informal/archaic term for sailor
informal archaic

Examples

The old tar told us fascinating stories about life at sea.
After twenty years as a tar, he finally decided to retire from the navy.
Each tar aboard the vessel had his own unique tale of adventure.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sailor - a person who works on a ship as a member of the crew
  • Synonym: mariner - a person who navigates or assists in navigating a ship

Etymology

Possibly from tarpaulin, a nickname for sailors because of their tarred clothing.

2. VERB

/ tɑr / (AmE), / tɑː / (BrE)
to cover or coat (something) with tar, especially as a waterproofing or protective measure
to coat with tar

Examples

Every summer, workers tar the main road to prevent cracks and potholes.
To repair the old shed, we decided to tar the roof for extra protection.
After the storm, the city crew had to tar several damaged sections of the street.

Synset Examples

tar the roof
tar the roads

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a black, sticky substance used for surfacing roads, similar in function to tar
  • Synonym: seal - to cover a surface to make it waterproof

3. NOUN

/ tɑr / (AmE), / tɑː / (BrE)
a thick, dark, sticky substance obtained as a residue from the distillation of organic materials such as coal or wood, used especially for waterproofing, paving roads, and sealing surfaces
dark, sticky residue from distillation

Examples

Roads are often paved with a mixture of gravel and tar to provide a durable surface.
The workers applied a thick layer of tar to seal the roof against rain.
Smelling the heated tar, I knew the construction crew was repairing the street again.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a black, sticky substance similar to tar used in road surfacing

Etymology

Old English *teoru*; related to Old High German *zeor* (tar), Old Norse *tjara*.
