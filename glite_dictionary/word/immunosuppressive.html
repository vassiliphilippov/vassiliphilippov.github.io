immunosuppressive

1. NOUN

/ ɪˌmjunəsoʊˈprɛsɪv / (AmE), / ɪˌmjuːnəʊsəˈprɛsɪv / (BrE)
A drug that reduces the body's normal immune response, especially to prevent organ rejection or treat autoimmune diseases.
drug that reduces immune response
medical

Examples

Patients who receive organ transplants are often prescribed an immunosuppressive to prevent rejection.
Prolonged use of an immunosuppressive can increase the risk of infections.
Doctors carefully monitor the dosage of each immunosuppressive to minimize side effects.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: immunosuppressant - a drug that suppresses the immune system

Etymology

From immune + suppress + -ive, reflecting its function of suppressing immune activity.

2. ADJ

/ ɪˌmjunəsoʊsəˈprɛsɪv / (AmE), / ɪˌmjuːnəʊsəˈprɛsɪv / (BrE)
(of a drug, agent, or process) reducing or inhibiting the body's normal immune response.
reducing immune response
medical

Examples

Doctors may prescribe immunosuppressive drugs to prevent organ transplant rejection.
Long-term use of immunosuppressive therapy can increase the risk of infections.
The patient responded well to the immunosuppressive treatment regimen.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: immunostimulant - a substance that stimulates the immune response

Etymology

From Latin 'immunis' (exempt) + English 'suppress' + '-ive' (adjective suffix).
