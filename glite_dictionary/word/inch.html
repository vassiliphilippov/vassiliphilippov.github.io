inch

1. NOUN

/ ɪntʃ / (AmE), / ɪntʃ / (BrE)
a unit of length equal to one twelfth of a foot, or 2.54 centimetres
unit of length (2.54 cm)

Examples

He measured the desk and found it was precisely forty-eight inches long.
The plant had grown only an inch since last week, but it was noticeable.
Please leave at least an inch of space between the paintings on the wall.

Etymology

From Latin 'uncia' (twelfth part), via Old French 'unche' and Middle English 'inche'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪntʃ / (AmE), / ɪntʃ / (BrE)
a unit of length equal to 1/12 of a foot, or approximately 2.54 centimeters.
unit of length (2.54 cm)

Examples

He measured the shelf and found it was exactly twelve inches wide.
The plant grew almost an inch overnight after the rain.
The snow was six inches deep by morning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: centimeter - a metric unit of length equal to one hundredth of a meter

Etymology

From Latin *uncia* (twelfth part, ounce, inch).

3. NOUN

/ ɪntʃ / (AmE), / ɪntʃ / (BrE)
A unit of linear measurement equal to 2.54 centimeters, often used to specify the size or area of space in printed advertisements.
unit of length, esp. for ad space

Examples

The advertisement occupied a full six inches of space in the newspaper.
Rates for inch columns in the magazine have increased this year.
Each classified ad is charged by the inch, so longer ads cost more.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: centimeter - a metric unit of length, equal to one hundredth of a meter
  • Degree Higher: foot - a unit of linear measurement equal to 12 inches

Etymology

From Latin *uncia* (a twelfth part, inch).

4. VERB

/ ɪntʃ / (AmE), / ɪntʃ / (BrE)
to move or progress very slowly or gradually in a particular direction
move or progress slowly

Examples

Traffic began to inch forward as the accident was cleared.
She inched her chair closer to the fire for warmth.
He inched down the narrow ledge, afraid to make any sudden moves.

Synset Examples

He edged towards the car

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creep - to move slowly and carefully in order to avoid being noticed
  • Antonym: rush - to move with urgent haste

Etymology

From the noun 'inch', originally from Latin 'uncia' (a twelfth part, an inch).
