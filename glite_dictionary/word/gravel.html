gravel

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡrævəl / (AmE), / ˈɡravəl / (BrE)
Small, loose rock fragments and pebbles, often used to cover roads, paths, or as a construction material.
small stones and pebbles

Examples

The path was covered with gravel to make it easier to walk on.
After the rain, tiny pieces of gravel washed onto the road.
They laid fresh gravel in the driveway to improve drainage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pebble - a small, rounded stone
  • Degree Lower: sand - fine particles smaller than gravel
  • Degree Higher: cobble - a stone larger than gravel but smaller than a boulder

Etymology

From Old French *gravele*, diminutive of *grave* (gravel), of uncertain origin.

4. VERB

/ ˈɡrævəl / (AmE), / ˈɡravəl / (BrE)
to confuse or bewilder someone; to perplex or puzzle
confuse or perplex

Examples

The complicated explanation graveled everyone in the meeting, leaving them with more questions than answers.
Her unexpected response completely graveled the interviewer.
The professor's riddle has graveled students for years.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to perplex - to confuse or puzzle someone
  • Synonym: baffle - to confuse completely and make unable to understand
  • Antonym: to enlighten - to make someone understand; give clarity

Etymology

Originally from the noun 'gravel'; figuratively extended in American English from the 17th century sense 'to bring to a standstill, puzzle (a witness)' possibly from the image of being stuck in gravel.
