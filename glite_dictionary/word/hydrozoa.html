Hydrozoa

1. NOUN

/ ˌhaɪdrəˈzoʊə / (AmE), / ˌhʌɪdrəˈzəʊə / (BrE)
A class of mostly marine coelenterate animals, typically showing alternation of generations, and including hydras and certain jellyfish.
a class of coelenterate animals (hydras, jellyfish)

Examples

The Hydrozoa include both solitary hydras and colonial jellyfish-like creatures.
Scientists study the reproductive cycles of Hydrozoa to understand their complex life histories.
Most members of the Hydrozoa exhibit both polyp and medusa stages during their lifecycle.

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek 'hydro-' (water) + 'zōion' (animal).

2. NOUN

/ ˌhaɪdrəˈzoʊə / (AmE), / ˌhʌɪdrəʊˈzəʊə / (BrE)
a class of mostly marine coelenterates, including hydras and some jellyfish, characterized by a life cycle typically involving a colonial hydroid stage and a free-swimming medusa stage
a class of aquatic animals including hydras and some jellyfish

Examples

Many Hydrozoa species show both polyp and medusa stages in their life cycle.
The biologist studied the unique colonial structures of Hydrozoa in the laboratory.
Some familiar freshwater Hydrozoa include the well-known hydra.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Cnidaria - the animal phylum including jellyfish, corals, and sea anemones

Etymology

From Greek hydra (water serpent) + zoon (animal).
