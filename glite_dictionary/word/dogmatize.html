to dogmatize

1. VERB

/ ˈdɔɡməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡmətaɪz / (BrE)
to state opinions or beliefs as if they are certainly correct and cannot be doubted; to speak or present ideas in a dogmatic way
state opinions dogmatically

Examples

She tends to dogmatize during discussions, leaving little room for differing views.
The professor was criticized for dogmatizing his theories in his lectures.
It is important to present ideas thoughtfully rather than simply dogmatize them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pontificate - to speak or express opinions in a dogmatic way
  • Antonym: to speculate - to form a theory or conjecture without firm evidence

Etymology

From French dogmatiser, from Late Latin dogmatizare, from dogma (opinion, tenet).

2. VERB

/ ˈdɔɡməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡmətaɪz / (BrE)
to state or assert opinions or beliefs as if they are absolute truths, without consideration for evidence or other viewpoints
to assert as an absolute truth

Examples

The professor tends to dogmatize on issues where the evidence is far from conclusive.
If we dogmatize every detail of the policy, we risk ignoring valuable contributions.
She warned her team not to dogmatize and to remain open to new perspectives.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pronounce - to declare or state formally
  • Synonym: to assert - to state a fact or belief confidently and forcefully
  • Antonym: question - to express doubt or request more information

Etymology

From Greek *dogmatizein* (to lay down an opinion), from *dogma* (opinion, decree).
