to entitle

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (BrE)
to give someone the right to do or have something
give the right to

Examples

The new benefits policy entitles employees to extra vacation days.
Holding a valid ticket entitles you to enter the event.
Membership in the club entitles you to use all the facilities.

Synset Examples

The Freedom of Information Act entitles you to request your FBI file

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to authorize - to give official permission for something
  • Antonym: to disqualify - to take away someone's right to do something

Etymology

From Old French *entituler*, from Latin *titulus* (title).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02452235-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (BrE)
to give a rank or title to someone, especially by making them a member of the nobility
to give a title of nobility

Examples

The king decided to entitle his loyal knight, making him a duke.
Upon her marriage, she was entitled as the Countess of Warwick.
For his bravery, the soldier was entitled to a position within the aristocracy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ennoble - to make someone a member of the nobility or to give a noble title

Etymology

From Old French 'entituler', from Latin 'titulus' (title).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02403605-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪtəl / (BrE)
to give a title to a book, work of art, or other creation
give a title to something

Examples

The artist decided to entitle her latest painting 'Dawn'.
The author chose to entitle his memoir 'A Life Remembered'.
They will entitle the new film after the main character.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: name - to give a specific name to something

Etymology

From Old French 'entituler', from Late Latin 'entitulare' (give a title to).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01031533-v
Synset POS: v
