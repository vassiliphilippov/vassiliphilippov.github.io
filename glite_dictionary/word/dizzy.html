dizzy

1. ADJ

/ ˈdɪzi / (AmE), / ˈdɪzi / (BrE)
(of a person) silly, scatterbrained, or lacking seriousness; frivolous or flighty.
frivolous or scatterbrained (person)

Examples

Her dizzy remarks made everyone laugh at the meeting.
He tended to act a little dizzy when the discussion turned serious.
The movie portrays a dizzy heiress who doesn't care about responsibilities.

Synset Examples

a dizzy blonde
light-headed teenagers
silly giggles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: silly - showing a lack of good sense or judgment; frivolous
  • Antonym: serious - thoughtful, solemn, not playful or silly
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02128108-s
Synset POS: s

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪzi / (AmE), / ˈdɪzi / (BrE)
to make someone feel unsteady or confused, as if they might lose their balance
to make feel unsteady or confused

Examples

The flashing lights dizzied her until she had to sit down.
The constant spinning of the merry-go-round dizzies most children.
He found the sheer amount of information dizzying at first.

Synset Examples

a dizzying pace

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bewilder - to cause someone to become confused

Etymology

From Old English 'dysig' (foolish, stupid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00533408-v
Synset POS: v

3. ADJ

/ ˈdɪzi / (AmE), / ˈdɪzi / (BrE)
having a sensation of spinning around and losing one's balance; likely to fall or feel faint
sensation of spinning or unsteadiness

Examples

She stood up too quickly and suddenly felt dizzy, grabbing the table for support.
Looking down from the tall tower made me feel a little dizzy and uneasy.
After spinning around in circles, the children laughed and fell over, dizzy from the game.

Synset Examples

had a dizzy spell
a dizzy pinnacle
had a headache and felt giddy
a giddy precipice
feeling woozy from the blow on his head
a vertiginous climb up the face of the cliff

Related Terms

  • Synonym: giddy - having a sensation of whirling and tendency to fall
  • Synonym: woozy - feeling weak or unsteady, as if about to lose consciousness
  • Synonym: vertiginous - causing or relating to a sensation of dizziness or whirling

Etymology

Old English dysig (foolish, stupid), with the sense evolved to 'having a whirling sensation'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02554818-s
Synset POS: s
