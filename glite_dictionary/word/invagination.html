invagination

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪnˌvædʒəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˌvadʒɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the folding in of an outer layer to form a pouch or pocket, especially in a biological structure
folding in to form a pocket
medical

Examples

During development, the embryo undergoes invagination as tissues start to differentiate.
The invagination of the cell membrane is an early step in endocytosis.
Surgeons observed an abnormal invagination in the patient’s intestinal wall.

Synset Examples

the invagination of the blastula

Related Terms

  • Synonym: infolding - the act of folding inward, especially of a membrane or tissue

Etymology

From Latin 'in-' (into) + 'vagina' (sheath).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪnˌvædʒəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪnˌvædʒɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the condition or process of being folded inward to form a sheath or cavity, especially in biological tissues
folding inward to form a sheath or cavity
medical

Examples

During embryonic development, the invagination of cells forms the early gut structure.
Surgeons were concerned about the invagination seen in the patient’s intestinal wall.
The process of invagination is vital for the formation of certain organs in animals.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: evagination - the condition of being turned outward

Etymology

From Latin 'invaginare' (to put into a sheath), from 'in-' (in) + 'vagina' (sheath).
