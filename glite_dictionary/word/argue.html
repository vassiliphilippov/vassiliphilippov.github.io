to argue

1. VERB

/ ˈɑrgju / (AmE), / ˈɑːgjuː / (BrE)
to give reasons or cite evidence in support of or against an idea, action, or theory; to discuss or debate an issue logically
present reasons or debate

Examples

She tried to argue her point using clear and logical reasons.
The lawyer will argue the case before the court tomorrow.
Scientists often argue that more research is necessary before drawing conclusions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: debate - to discuss a question by considering opposed arguments
  • Synonym: to persuade - to cause someone to do something through reasoning or argument

Etymology

From Latin 'arguere' (to make clear, to prove).

2. VERB

/ ˈɑrɡju / (AmE), / ˈɑːɡjuː / (BrE)
(of evidence or facts) to give indication or provide support for something; to suggest or imply by evidence.
(of evidence) to suggest or indicate

Examples

The new data argue against the previous theory.
Her calm demeanor argues a strong sense of self-control.
These facts argue for immediate action by the authorities.

Synset Examples

The evidence argues for your claim
The results indicate the need for more work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to indicate - to point out or show evidence for something
  • Synonym: to suggest - to imply or put forward an idea indirectly

Etymology

From Latin arguere (to make clear, prove, accuse).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00774685-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɑrɡju / (AmE), / ˈɑːɡjuː / (BrE)
to speak angrily with someone because you disagree; to have an argument about something
to have a verbal disagreement

Examples

The children began to argue about whose turn it was to play with the toy.
They often argue over politics at family dinners.
I didn't want to argue, so I just let her have her way.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quarrel - to have an angry argument or disagreement
  • Degree Lower: to discuss - to talk about something in detail (less confrontational)
  • Antonym: to agree - to have the same opinion; the opposite of to argue

Etymology

From Latin 'arguere' (to make clear, prove), through Old French and Middle English.
