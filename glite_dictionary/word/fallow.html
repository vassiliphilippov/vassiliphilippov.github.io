fallow

1. ADJ

/ ˈfæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈfæləʊ / (BrE)
(of farmland) left unplowed and unseeded during a growing season to restore its fertility.
(of land) left unplanted for a season

Examples

The farmer decided to leave the eastern field fallow this year to improve soil quality.
After several seasons of intensive agriculture, the land was left fallow to recover its nutrients.
Rotating crops and leaving some plots fallow are traditional farming practices.

Synset Examples

fallow farmland

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cultivated - land prepared and used for growing crops

Etymology

From Old English *fealgian* (to plow land), related to Germanic roots.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈfæləʊ / (BrE)
Land that has been ploughed and left unseeded for one or more growing seasons to restore its fertility.
uncultivated ploughed farmland

Examples

The farmer left the field fallow for a season to improve the soil quality.
Crop rotation often includes a year when the land remains fallow.
The lush meadow was once a fallow awaiting next year's planting.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: arable - land that can be used for growing crops

Etymology

From Old English *fealh* (ploughed land), related to German *Feld* (field).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09301201-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈfæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈfæləʊ / (BrE)
(of a resource, opportunity, or potential) not currently active or developed but capable of being used in the future.
undeveloped but potentially useful

Examples

The company decided to invest in a fallow sector of the market with high growth potential.
After years of inactivity, her fallow talents began to flourish again.
The real estate remained fallow, awaiting redevelopment by investors.

Synset Examples

a fallow gold market

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inactive - not currently active
  • Synonym: dormant - temporarily inactive but capable of being activated

Etymology

Originally referring to land left unsown after plowing; from Old English *fealgian* (to plow).
