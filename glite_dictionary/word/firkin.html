firkin

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɜrkɪn / (AmE), / ˈfɜːkɪn / (BrE)
a British unit of capacity, particularly for liquids such as beer or ale, equal to 9 imperial gallons (approximately 41 litres)
British unit of capacity (9 imperial gallons)
british archaic

Examples

The brewery delivered a firkin of ale to the local pub for the festival.
Historically, a firkin was a common measurement for both beer and butter in Britain.
The old recipe calls for a firkin of cider, which is much more than a modern bottle.

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *vierdekijn* (a fourth part), via Middle English *ferdekyn*.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɜrkɪn / (AmE), / ˈfɜːkɪn / (BrE)
A small wooden barrel or keg, typically used for storing liquids such as beer or butter.
small wooden barrel or keg

Examples

They stored ale in a firkin in the cellar.
The museum displayed a nineteenth-century firkin used for transporting butter.
A single firkin was enough to supply the workers for a week.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barrel - a large cylindrical container, typically for liquids
  • Synonym: keg - a small barrel, especially one for beer

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *vierdekijn* (little fourth), from *vierde* (fourth), referring to one-fourth of a barrel.
