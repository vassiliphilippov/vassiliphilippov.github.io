dibble

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪb.əl / (AmE), / ˈdɪb.əl / (BrE)
A small hand tool with a pointed end, used to make holes in the ground for planting seeds or bulbs.
pointed hand tool for planting

Examples

The gardener used a dibble to make holes for the tulip bulbs.
Please pass me the dibble so I can plant these seeds more evenly.
A wooden dibble is ideal for making precise holes in loose soil.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dibber - alternative term for dibble, especially in British English

Etymology

From Middle English *dybylle*, probably from Latin *dibapha* (a kind of vat), possibly influenced by related Old Norse words.

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪb.əl / (AmE), / ˈdɪb.əl / (BrE)
to make holes in the ground with a pointed tool, especially for planting seeds or bulbs
make holes in soil with a tool

Examples

In spring, the gardener would dibble small holes for the young seedlings.
Farmers often dibble rows to ensure seeds are evenly spaced in their fields.
She dibbled into the wet earth, preparing for this year’s tulip bulbs.

Synset Examples

dibble the ground

Etymology

From Middle English *debyl*, probably related to Old English *dībel* (pointed stick for making holes).

3. VERB

/ ˈdɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈdɪbəl / (BrE)
to plant seeds or bulbs in the ground using a pointed tool, or to make holes in soil with such a tool in preparation for planting
plant with a pointed gardening tool

Examples

Gardeners often dibble small holes in the soil before setting the seeds.
It is best to dibble tulip bulbs several inches apart for optimal growth.
She carefully dibbled the young plants into the freshly prepared beds.

Synset Examples

dibble Spring bulbs

Etymology

From the noun 'dibble', from Middle English, possibly a diminutive of 'dib', meaning 'to poke or thrust'.
