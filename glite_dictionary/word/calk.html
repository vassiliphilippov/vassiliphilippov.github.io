calk

1. VERB

/ kɑːk / (AmE), / kɔːk / (BrE)
to provide (a horse's shoe) with calks to give better grip
fit with calks (on shoes)

Examples

The blacksmith will calk the new set of horseshoes before attaching them.
It is important to calk horseshoes properly for icy conditions.
She watched as the farrier calked the shoes during the winter preparation.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shoe - to fit a horse with horseshoes

2. NOUN

/ kɔk / (AmE), / kɔːk / (BrE)
a metal projection or cleat on the bottom front of a horseshoe, designed to prevent slipping
metal cleat on a horseshoe

Examples

The blacksmith carefully fitted a calk to the horseshoe to provide extra traction on icy ground.
Each calk must be properly aligned to prevent the horse from stumbling.
Replacing a worn calk can extend the life of a horseshoe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caulk - to fill or close seams or crevices (different spelling, related pronunciation)
  • Synonym: horseshoe - a U-shaped piece of metal nailed to the bottom of a horse's hoof

Etymology

Possibly from Old North French cauc (heel), related to Latin calx (heel).

3. VERB

/ kɑk / (AmE), / kɔːk / (BrE)
to seal a joint or seam, especially in windows, doors, or boats, with a waterproof filler or caulking material
to seal with caulking

Examples

The workers will calk the edges of the windows to prevent drafts.
You should calk around the bathtub to stop water from leaking.
He calked the seams of the wooden boat before launching it.

Synset Examples

caulk the window

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caulk - to seal a joint or seam with waterproof material

Etymology

From Old Northern French *calker* (to tread, press), from Latin *calcare* (to tread, press with the feet).

4. VERB

/ kɑk / (AmE), / kɔːk / (BrE)
(of a horse) to injure by striking with a calk, the sharp projection on a horseshoe.
injure with a horseshoe calk

Examples

The horse calked its mate during the scramble in the pasture.
If a horse is not careful, it could calk another while turning sharply.
The farrier warned us to watch for injuries if one horse accidentally calks another.

Etymology

From Middle English 'calken', from Old North French 'caucher' (to tread), relating to the projection on a horseshoe; see also 'calcar' (spur) in Latin.
