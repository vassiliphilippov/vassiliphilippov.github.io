blitzkrieg

1. NOUN

/ ˈblɪtsˌkriɡ / (AmE), / ˈblɪtskriːɡ / (BrE)
a swift and intense military attack, especially one using air and ground forces to achieve a quick victory
swift, intense military attack

Examples

The German army used blitzkrieg tactics during the early years of World War II.
Many historians study the effectiveness of blitzkrieg in changing the nature of modern warfare.
The general planned a blitzkrieg to quickly overwhelm enemy defenses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - an aggressive and violent action against a target
  • Antonym: defense - resistance against attack

Etymology

From German 'Blitzkrieg', meaning 'lightning war' (Blitz 'lightning' + Krieg 'war').

2. VERB

/ ˈblɪtsˌkriɡ / (AmE), / ˈblɪtsˌkriːɡ / (BrE)
to conduct a military campaign or attack using swift, surprise tactics designed for rapid victory
attack rapidly and unexpectedly

Examples

The general planned to blitzkrieg the enemy's defenses before they could organize a response.
In business, the company tried to blitzkrieg the market with its new product launch.
The football team's strategy was to blitzkrieg their opponents in the first quarter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - take aggressive military action

Etymology

From German 'Blitzkrieg', meaning 'lightning war'. Popularized during World War II.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01095776-v
Synset POS: v
