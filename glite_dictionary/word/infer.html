to infer

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɜr / (AmE), / ɪnˈfəː / (BrE)
to reach a conclusion based on evidence and reasoning, rather than explicit statements
deduce or conclude from evidence

Examples

From her silence, I inferred that she was not pleased with the decision.
The researcher inferred a connection between the two phenomena based on the data.
We can infer from the evidence that the suspect left before midnight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deduce - to arrive at a fact or conclusion by reasoning; draw as a logical conclusion
  • Antonym: to imply - to suggest or indicate something without stating it directly (the counterpart of inference)

Etymology

From Latin inferre (to bring in, deduce), from in- (in) + ferre (to carry).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɜr / (AmE), / ɪnˈfəː / (BrE)
to form an opinion or guess that something is true based on available information or evidence
deduce or conclude from evidence

Examples

Based on the evidence presented, the jury could infer that the defendant was present at the scene.
From her tone of voice, I inferred that she was upset about the decision.
Scientists often infer the existence of planets by observing changes in starlight.

Synset Examples

I understand you have no previous experience?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deduce - to reach a conclusion using logic
  • Antonym: to imply - to suggest something without saying it directly

Etymology

From Latin inferre (to bring in, deduce), from in- (in) + ferre (to bear, carry).

Examples

Related Terms

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɜr / (AmE), / ɪnˈfɜː / (BrE)
to reach a general conclusion based on evidence from specific cases or facts
draw a general conclusion from evidence

Examples

From the repeated results, scientists infer a consistent pattern across experiments.
We can infer from his silence that he disagrees with the proposal.
Teachers often infer students’ understanding from their questions and comments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deduce - arrive at a conclusion by reasoning; often used interchangeably with 'infer'
  • Synonym: to extrapolate - to extend or project known information to unknown areas

From Latin inferre (to bring in, deduce), from in- (in) + ferre (to carry).
