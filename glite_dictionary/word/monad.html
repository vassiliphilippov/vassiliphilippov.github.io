monad

1. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊ.næd / (AmE), / ˈmɒ.nad / (BrE)
(Biology) A single-celled microorganism, especially one that is a flagellate protozoan.
single-celled microorganism, esp. protozoan
medical

Examples

Under the microscope, the monad moved swiftly using its slender flagellum.
Researchers identified a rare monad in the pond water sample.
Flagellated monads play an important role in aquatic ecosystems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protozoan - single-celled microscopic animal, eukaryotic organism

Etymology

From Greek monas, monad-, meaning 'unit' or 'single thing'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊnæd / (AmE), / ˈməʊnæd / (BrE)
(in philosophy) A singular metaphysical entity, considered indivisible and fundamental, from which all material properties are derived.
indivisible metaphysical entity (philosophy)

Examples

Some philosophers argue that the soul is a monad, indivisible and eternal.
According to Leibniz, every monad reflects the entire universe in its own way.
The concept of the monad has fascinated metaphysicians for centuries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atom - the smallest particle of a chemical element
  • Antonym: plurality - a state of being numerous or multiple

Etymology

From Greek 'monas' (unit, alone), from 'monos' (alone, single).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13764980-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊnæd / (AmE), / ˈməʊnæd / (BrE)
(Chemistry) An atom or radical with a valence of one; a monovalent atom or group.
chemistry: atom with valence of one
medical

Examples

In sodium chloride, the sodium atom acts as a monad, forming a single bond with the chloride ion.
Hydrogen is the simplest example of a chemical monad in many compounds.
The chemist explained that a monad has only one combining site in reactions.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: divalent - an atom or radical with a valence of two
  • Degree Higher: trivalent - an atom or radical with a valence of three

Etymology

From Greek 'monas', 'monados' (unit, alone), from 'monos' (alone).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
