sampling

1. NOUN

/ ˈsæmplɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɑːmplɪŋ / (BrE)
the process of measuring the amplitude of a continuously varying signal at regular intervals, typically to convert it into digital form
measuring signals at intervals for digital conversion

Examples

Modern audio systems use sampling to convert analog music into digital files.
The quality of the digital recording depends largely on the sampling rate.
In signal processing, sampling allows continuous waveforms to be represented digitally.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: digitization - the process of converting information into a digital format

2. NOUN

/ ˈsæmp.əl.ɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɑːm.plɪŋ / (BrE)
In statistics, the process of selecting a representative subset from a larger population for the purpose of analysis or study.
selecting a sample for study (statistics)
medical

Examples

Sampling is a crucial step in conducting reliable survey research.
The accuracy of the results depends heavily on the sampling method used.
Statisticians use various sampling techniques to avoid bias in data collection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sample - a part or quantity taken from a larger whole for testing or analysis

Etymology

From 'sample' plus the suffix '-ing'; 'sample' from Old French *essample*, from Latin *exemplum* (example).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsæmplɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɑːmplɪŋ / (BrE)
a group or set of items selected at random from a larger population and used to draw conclusions or test hypotheses about that population
selection of items from a population for analysis

Examples

The researchers drew a sampling of 500 students to measure eating habits across the university.
A random sampling from the survey revealed surprising satisfaction levels.
Quality control relies on the sampling of products during each batch to ensure standards are met.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sample - a small part or quantity intended to show what the whole is like

Etymology

From the verb 'sample' + '-ing', in use since the 17th century relating to statistics and testing.
