helter-skelter

1. ADJ

/ ˌhɛltɚˈskɛltɚ / (AmE), / ˌhɛltəˈskɛltə / (BrE)
In a state of disorder or confusion; lacking visible order or organization.
in disorder or confusion

Examples

After the sudden announcement, the meeting ended in a helter-skelter rush.
She organized her notes in a helter-skelter fashion, making them hard to review.
The kitchen looked helter-skelter after the family finished baking cookies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chaotic - completely disordered and unpredictable
  • Antonym: organized - orderly and structured

Etymology

Reduplicative formation from 'skelter' (to hurry), first attested in the 16th century.

2. ADV

/ ˌhɛltərˈskɛltər / (AmE), / ˌhɛltəˈskɛltə / (BrE)
Involving hurried, disorderly, or confused activity; done with undue haste and confusion.
in hurried and confused disorder

Examples

The children ran helter-skelter down the hallway when the bell rang.
In the storm, papers flew helter-skelter around the office.
They packed their bags helter-skelter before fleeing the house.

Synset Examples

a helter-skelter kind of existence with never a pause
a pell-mell dash for the train

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pell-mell - in a confused, disordered, or rushed manner
  • Antonym: orderly - organized and systematic, not confused

Etymology

Originated in 16th century English as a rhyming compound of uncertain origin, possibly imitative of disorderly motion.

3. ADV

/ ˌhɛltɚˈskɛltɚ / (AmE), / ˌhɛltəˈskɛltə / (BrE)
In a haphazard, disorderly, or chaotic manner.
in a disorderly or chaotic way

Examples

The children raced helter-skelter across the playground, laughing all the way.
Papers were scattered helter-skelter over the desk after the busy meeting.
During the storm, people ran helter-skelter for shelter from the heavy rain.

Synset Examples

the books were piled up helter-skelter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chaotically - in a completely confused or disordered way
  • Antonym: orderly - in a neat and organized manner

Etymology

Reduplicative formation from 'skelter', origin uncertain, attested since the 17th century.
