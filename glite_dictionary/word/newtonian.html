Newtonian

1. ADJ

/ nuːˈtoʊniən / (AmE), / njuːˈtəʊniən / (BrE)
relating to or derived from the scientific principles and theories of Sir Isaac Newton, especially in physics
relating to Isaac Newton's science

Examples

Modern physics has challenged some traditional Newtonian ideas about motion and gravity.
The experiment demonstrated behavior consistent with Newtonian mechanics.
In a Newtonian universe, time and space are considered absolute.

Synset Examples

Newtonian physics

Etymology

From the name of Sir Isaac Newton (1642–1727), English mathematician and physicist, with the adjectival suffix -ian.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03047528-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ nuːˈtoʊniən / (AmE), / njuːˈtəʊniən / (BrE)
A person who follows the ideas or principles of Isaac Newton, especially in science or philosophy.
follower of Isaac Newton

Examples

As a prominent Newtonian, he defended the laws of motion in scientific debates.
The Newtonians revolutionized physics by emphasizing empirical evidence and mathematics.
Many philosophers in the 18th century identified themselves as Newtonians.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Newtonian - relating to the theories or discoveries of Isaac Newton

Etymology

From the name Isaac Newton (1642–1727), English mathematician and physicist, with the suffix -ian denoting association.
