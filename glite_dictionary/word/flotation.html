flotation

1. NOUN

/ floʊˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / fləʊˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of financing a company by issuing shares or bonds to raise capital
raising capital by issuing shares or bonds

Examples

The company's flotation on the stock exchange was met with great investor interest.
They hired a team of financial advisors to manage the flotation of their new business venture.
After the flotation, the firm quickly expanded its operations overseas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: issue - the action of offering securities for sale to the public

Etymology

From float + -ation, referring to launching a company or its shares on a stock market.

2. NOUN

/ floʊˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / fləʊˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the action or process of remaining on the surface of a liquid without sinking
remaining on the surface of a liquid

Examples

The principle of flotation explains why ships can stay afloat despite their weight.
Oil spills threaten the flotation of many aquatic animals, making it harder for them to survive.
Life jackets are designed to provide extra flotation in emergency situations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buoyancy - the ability or tendency to float in water or other fluid

Etymology

From Latin *fluctuare* (to float), via French *flottaison*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11548418-n
Synset POS: n
