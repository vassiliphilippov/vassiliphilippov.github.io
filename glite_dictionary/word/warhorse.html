warhorse

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɔrˌhɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːˌhɔːs / (BrE)
a horse trained and used for fighting in wars, especially in historical times
horse trained for war

Examples

The knight rode his warhorse into battle, its armor gleaming under the sun.
Medieval armies relied on powerful warhorses to carry soldiers and supplies.
During the parade, a descendant of a famous warhorse led the procession.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: charger - a horse trained for battle; a steed used by cavalry

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɔrˌhɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːhɔːs / (BrE)
a person who is experienced and has served for a long time, especially in a particular field or job
experienced veteran, long-serving person

Examples

The senator is a political warhorse who has seen many battles in Congress.
As a legal warhorse, she has argued hundreds of cases in her career.
That old football coach is a warhorse who never gives up on his team.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: veteran - a person with long experience in a particular field, especially military service

Etymology

Originally referred to a horse used in battle; by metaphorical extension to seasoned people.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɔrˌhɔrs / (AmE), / ˈwɔːˌhɔːs / (BrE)
A work of art, such as a musical composition or play, that is part of the standard repertoire but has become overused or stale due to frequent performance.
well-worn, overused artistic work

Examples

That symphony is a warhorse of orchestral concerts, performed almost every season.
He decided not to program another warhorse and opted for a lesser-known opera instead.
Critics described the play as a theatrical warhorse, familiar to audiences for decades.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: classic - A work of art recognized as outstanding and of lasting value
  • Synonym: cliché - An overused expression or idea
  • Antonym: novelty - Something new or unusual

Etymology

Originally referred to a horse used in war; figuratively applied to anything, especially an artwork, that is used repeatedly.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
