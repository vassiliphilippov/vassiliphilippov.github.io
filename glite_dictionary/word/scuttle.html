scuttle

1. NOUN

/ ˈskʌt̬l̩ / (AmE), / ˈskʌt.əl / (BrE)
A metal container with a sloping lid or lip, used for holding and pouring coal onto a fire.
coal container with a sloping lip

Examples

She brought another scuttle of coal to keep the fire burning.
The ornate brass scuttle stood beside the old fireplace.
He filled the scuttle in the cellar before carrying it upstairs.

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'scutel' (dish, platter), from Old French 'escutel', from Latin 'scutella' (dish).

2. NOUN

/ ˈskʌt̬l̩ / (AmE), / ˈskʌt.əl / (BrE)
an entrance in a ship or building fitted with a hatch, especially a covered opening in a ship's deck providing access between decks
hatchway in a ship or building

Examples

The sailor climbed up through the scuttle to reach the deck above.
Emergency drills require quick access to the lifeboats via the nearest scuttle.
Heavy rain forced them to close every scuttle on the lower deck.

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English *scutel* (a dish), or from Old French *escoutille* (hatchway), ultimately from Spanish *escotilla*.

3. VERB

/ ˈskʌt̬l̩ / (AmE), / ˈskʌt.l̩ / (BrE)
to move quickly with short, hurried steps, often with the suggestion of being unnoticed or afraid
move quickly in a hurried manner

Examples

The children scuttled across the playground when the bell rang.
A mouse scuttled along the baseboard and disappeared into a hole.
As soon as the lights came on, the insects scuttled into the cracks.

Synset Examples

so terrified by the extraordinary ebbing of the sea that they scurried to higher ground

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scurry - to move quickly and with short steps, especially because you are frightened or trying to get away
  • Antonym: to dawdle - to move or act too slowly

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from earlier dialectal meaning 'to hurry', related to the noun 'scuttle' meaning a shallow basket.
