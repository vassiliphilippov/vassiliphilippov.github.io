to equate

1. VERB

/ ɪˈkweɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈkweɪt / (BrE)
(in mathematics) To be or show as equal or equivalent in value, amount, function, or meaning.
be or show as equal, esp. in math

Examples

In this equation, we equate x to 10 to find the solution.
Mathematicians often equate two expressions to prove they are identical.
You must equate both sides of the equation to solve for y.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equal - being the same in quantity, size, degree, or value

Etymology

From Latin 'aequatus', past participle of 'aequare' (to make equal), from 'aequus' (equal).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈkweɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈkweɪt / (BrE)
to consider or describe one thing as being the same as or similar to another
consider as equal or similar

Examples

It is misleading to equate intelligence with academic achievement.
Some people equate happiness with wealth, but they are not the same.
She equated the feeling of freedom with driving her own car for the first time.

Synset Examples

We can compare the Han dynasty to the Romans
You cannot equate success in financial matters with greed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compare - to examine similarities or differences
  • Antonym: contrast - to show differences when compared

Etymology

From Latin 'aequatus', past participle of 'aequare' (to make equal).

3. VERB

/ ɪˈkweɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈkweɪt / (BrE)
to consider or treat (one thing) as equal to another, or to make things equal or equivalent
consider or make equal

Examples

It would be wrong to equate wealth with happiness.
Some people equate success with having a lot of money.
The law equates this behavior with negligence.

Synset Examples

let's equalize the duties among all employees in this office
The company matched the discount policy of its competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compare - to examine two or more things to note similarities and differences
  • Antonym: to differentiate - to recognize or state the difference between things

Etymology

From Latin aequatus, past participle of aequare (to make equal).
