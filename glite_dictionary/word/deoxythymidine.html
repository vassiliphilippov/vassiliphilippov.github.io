deoxythymidine

1. NOUN

/ diˌɑksiˈθaɪməˌdin / (AmE), / diˌɒksiˈθɪmɪˌdiːn / (BrE)
a nucleoside formed by the combination of deoxyribose and thymine, found as a component of DNA
a nucleoside component of DNA
medical

Examples

The incorporation of deoxythymidine into DNA is a key step in cellular replication.
Scientists often use labeled deoxythymidine to track DNA synthesis in cells.
A lack of deoxythymidine can disrupt normal DNA replication and cell division.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thymidine - a nucleoside composed of thymine and ribose (not deoxyribose)

Etymology

From 'deoxy-' (denoting loss of oxygen) + 'thymidine', itself from 'thymine' + '-idine'.
