barratry

1. NOUN

/ ˈbærətri / (AmE), / ˈbærətri / (BrE)
the offense of persistently instigating groundless lawsuits or quarrels, especially for personal gain; vexatious litigation.
offense of inciting lawsuits
legal

Examples

The lawyer was disbarred after repeated accusations of barratry in the courts.
Historically, barratry was considered a serious offense to prevent the abuse of legal systems.
She was charged with barratry for repeatedly pursuing unfounded lawsuits against her neighbors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: litigation - the process of taking legal action

Etymology

From Old French barraterie, from barrat (deceit, fraud).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbæˌrætɹi / (AmE), / ˈbær.ə.tɹi / (BrE)
the buying or selling of ecclesiastical offices or church appointments; the traffic in church preferments
buying or selling church offices
archaic

Examples

The medieval church condemned barratry as a grave sin against its integrity.
He was accused of barratry after attempting to purchase a prominent ecclesiastical position.
The practice of barratry undermined trust in the church during the Middle Ages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: simony - the buying or selling of church offices or sacred things

Etymology

From Old French 'baraterie' (deceit, trickery), from 'barat' (fraud).

3. NOUN

/ ˈbær.ə.tri / (AmE), / ˈbær.ə.tri / (BrE)
The crime of a judge whose decisions are improperly influenced by bribery or corruption.
judicial corruption through bribery
legal

Examples

The judge was accused of barratry after evidence of accepting bribes was uncovered.
Laws preventing barratry are crucial to maintaining trust in the legal system.
Historical records detail several cases of judicial barratry in the 18th century.

Etymology

From Old French *baraterie* (deceit, trickery), from *barater* (to cheat), of uncertain origin.

4. NOUN

/ ˈbær.əˌtri / (AmE), / ˈbær.ə.tri / (BrE)
(in maritime law) fraudulent breach of duty by the master or crew of a ship resulting in injury to the owner or cargo, such as theft, intentional sinking, abandonment, or embezzlement.
fraud by a ship's captain or crew
legal

Examples

The insurer refused to pay due to evidence of barratry by the ship's captain.
Maritime insurance policies often exclude losses resulting from barratry.
Acts of barratry can include intentionally damaging the ship or stealing its cargo.

Etymology

From Old French *barraterie* (deceit, fraud), from *baratter* (to cheat), probably of Italian origin.
