difficulty

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪfɪˌkʌlti / (AmE), / ˈdɪfɪkəlti / (BrE)
the state or condition of needing extra effort or being inconvenient to do or accomplish something
requiring effort or inconvenience

Examples

She managed to finish the task, but only with great difficulty.
He had difficulty understanding the instructions at first.
Crossing the river posed less difficulty than we expected.

Synset Examples

I went to a lot of trouble
he won without any trouble
had difficulty walking
finished the test only with great difficulty

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ease - the absence of difficulty or effort
  • Degree Higher: hardship - severe suffering or privation; more serious than ordinary difficulty

Etymology

From Latin 'difficultas', from 'difficilis' (not easy, hard to do).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɪfɪkəlti / (AmE), / ˈdɪfɪkəlti / (BrE)
the quality or state of being hard to do, deal with, or understand, often requiring great effort
state of being hard or taxing

Examples

The difficulty of the mountain trail made the hike much more challenging than we expected.
She underestimated the difficulty of learning a new language as an adult.
Due to the difficulty of the task, the project took several weeks to complete.

Synset Examples

they agreed about the difficulty of the climb

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ease - the absence of difficulty, effortlessness
  • Synonym: challenge - something that tests one's abilities or requires great effort

Etymology

From Old French *dificulte*, from Latin *difficultas*, from *difficilis* (hard to do).
