enticement

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (BrE)
the quality or feature of something that attracts or tempts by seeming to promise a reward or benefit
attractive quality that tempts or lures

Examples

The enticement of higher salaries attracted many skilled workers to the city.
No advertising enticement could persuade her to buy the product.
Holiday packages often include special enticements such as spa treatments or free meals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allurement - the power to attract or lure
  • Synonym: temptation - the desire to do something, especially something wrong or unwise
  • Antonym: repulsion - a feeling of intense distaste or disgust

Etymology

From Old French 'entisier' (to entice), from Vulgar Latin *intitiāre.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (BrE)
something that attracts or tempts someone to do something, especially by offering pleasure or advantage; the act or process of enticing.
something that tempts or attracts

Examples

The promise of higher pay was a strong enticement for workers to switch companies.
He couldn't resist the enticement of freshly baked bread wafting from the kitchen.
Advertising often relies on the enticement of glamour and excitement to attract customers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: temptation - the desire to do something, especially something wrong or unwise
  • Antonym: repellent - something that repels or drives away

Etymology

From Old French *entecement*, from *entecer* 'to entice'.

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtaɪsmənt / (BrE)
the act of attracting someone to do something by exciting hope or desire
the act of luring or attracting

Examples

The enticement of a high salary convinced her to take the job abroad.
There was little enticement for the children to eat their vegetables without any sauce.
The advertisement used bright colors as an enticement to attract new customers.

Synset Examples

his enticements were shameless

Related Terms

  • Synonym: temptation - the desire to do something, especially something wrong or unwise
  • Antonym: repulsion - the act of driving someone away or causing aversion

Etymology

From Old French *enticier* (to incite), from Vulgar Latin *intitiare*.
