radiotelegraph

1. NOUN

/ ˌreɪdiˌoʊˈtɛlɪɡræf / (AmE), / ˌreɪdɪəʊˈtɛlɪɡrɑːf / (BrE)
the transmission of telegraphic messages by means of radio waves, especially using Morse code
telegraphic messages sent by radio

Examples

The Titanic sent distress signals using the radiotelegraph to nearby ships.
Early expeditions relied on the radiotelegraph for communication when traveling in remote areas.
With the invention of the radiotelegraph, global communication became possible over long distances without physical wires.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: radiotelegraphy - the system or process of transmitting telegraphic messages by radio waves
  • Synonym: wireless telegraphy - sending telegraphic messages without wires, usually by radio
  • Antonym: radiotelephone - a system for transmitting speech over radio waves

Etymology

From radio- (relating to radio waves) + telegraph (from Greek 'tele' far + 'graphein' to write).

2. NOUN

/ ˌreɪdioʊˈtɛləˌɡræf / (AmE), / ˌreɪdiəʊˈtelɪˌɡrɑːf / (BrE)
a system of telegraphy that transmits messages using radio waves instead of wire connections
telegraphy using radio waves

Examples

The invention of the radiotelegraph made it possible to send messages across oceans without wires.
Early ships were equipped with a radiotelegraph to communicate with shore stations.
The operator sent the emergency signal via the radiotelegraph during the storm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegraph - a system for transmitting messages from a distance along a wire

Etymology

From radio- (referring to radio waves) + telegraph.
