undesirable

1. NOUN

/ ˌʌndɪˈzaɪərəbəl / (AmE), / ˌʌndɪˈzaɪərəbəl / (BrE)
a person who is considered unwanted, unpleasant, or objectionable in a particular place or group.
an unwanted or objectionable person

Examples

The police were criticized for targeting so-called undesirables in the city center.
Some clubs have strict door policies to keep undesirables away.
After the incident, those labeled as undesirables were barred from entering the event.

Synset Examples

rounding up vagrants and drunks and other undesirables

Related Terms

  • Synonym: outcast - a person who is rejected by society or a group
  • Antonym: welcome - a person whose presence is gladly received

Etymology

From prefix 'un-' (not) + 'desirable' (from Latin desiderabilis).

2. ADJ

/ ˌʌndɪˈzaɪərəbl / (AmE), / ˌʌndɪˈzaɪərəbəl / (BrE)
not wanted or worthy of being chosen; unpleasant or objectionable.
not wanted or not worthy of being chosen

Examples

The committee rejected all undesirable candidates for the position.
Many parents consider smoking an undesirable habit in teenagers.
She feared being labeled as undesirable in her small community.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: desirable - wanted or worthy of being chosen
  • Synonym: unwanted - not desired; not wanted

Etymology

From un- (not) + desirable, from Latin desiderabilis (worth seeking).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00856652-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˌʌndɪˈzaɪɚəbəl / (AmE), / ˌʌndɪˈzaɪərəbəl / (BrE)
not wanted because it may be harmful, unpleasant, or inconvenient
not wanted; potentially harmful or unpleasant

Examples

The report identified several undesirable side effects of the new medication.
There are undesirable elements in every community that authorities try to control.
Too much noise is considered undesirable in library settings.

Synset Examples

undesirable impurities in steel
legislation excluding undesirable aliens
removed the unwanted vegetation

Related Terms

  • Antonym: desirable - wanted or worth having

Etymology

From un- (not) + desirable (worth having); 'desirable' from Old French 'desirabler', from Latin 'desiderabilis'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00737448-a
Synset POS: a
