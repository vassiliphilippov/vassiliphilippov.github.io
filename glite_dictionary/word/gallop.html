gallop

1. VERB

/ ˈɡæl.əp / (AmE), / ˈɡæl.əp / (BrE)
to cause a horse or other animal to move at a full gallop
make a horse move at full speed

Examples

He expertly galloped the horse across the field to impress the judges.
She taught her daughter how to gallop the pony along the fence line.
Did you gallop the horse just now, or was it trotting?

Synset Examples

Did you gallop the horse just now?

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: canter - to make a horse move at a moderate speed between a trot and a gallop

Etymology

From Old French *galoper* (to gallop), from Frankish *walalaupan* (to run, leap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01964155-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡæl.əp / (AmE), / ˈɡæl.əp / (BrE)
A fast running gait of a horse or other quadruped, in which all four feet leave the ground simultaneously during each stride.
fast gait of a horse

Examples

The horse broke into a gallop as it approached the finish line.
She watched the herd move across the plain at a full gallop.
Suddenly, the sound of hoofbeats at a gallop echoed through the valley.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: canter - a moderate gait of a horse between a trot and a gallop
  • Degree Lower: trot - a gait of a horse between a walk and a canter

Etymology

From French *galoper* (to gallop), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00289515-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈɡæləp / (AmE), / ˈɡæləp / (BrE)
(of a horse or other animal, or its rider) to move or ride at the fastest pace, with all the feet off the ground together in each stride.
to ride or move at a fast pace

Examples

The rider urged his horse to gallop across the open field.
She loves to gallop through the countryside on weekends.
As the storm approached, they began to gallop back toward the stable.

Synset Examples

He was galloping down the road

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: trot - to move at a pace faster than a walk but slower than a gallop
  • Degree Lower: canter - to move at a pace between a trot and a gallop

Etymology

From Old French *galoper*, of Germanic origin, related to Middle Dutch *waloperen* (to run, leap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01963786-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈɡæl.əp / (AmE), / ˈɡæl.əp / (BrE)
(of a horse or other quadruped) to run at full speed with all the feet off the ground simultaneously at each stride.
run fast (horses or animals)

Examples

The horses galloped across the open field, their manes flying in the wind.
As soon as the gate opened, the rider urged her horse to gallop ahead of the others.
Children watched in awe as the ponies galloped past the fence during the race.

Synset Examples

The horse was galloping along

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: trot - to go at a moderate speed (horses or riders)

Etymology

From Old French 'galoper', of Germanic origin, related to 'gallop' (noun).
