papyrus

1. NOUN

/ pəˈpaɪrəs / (AmE), / pəˈpʌɪrəs / (BrE)
a tall aquatic plant of the Nile valley (Cyperus papyrus) whose stem was used especially by ancient Egyptians to make paper-like material and ropes.
plant used for making ancient paper

Examples

The ancient Egyptians harvested papyrus from the banks of the Nile to make scrolls.
Large stems of papyrus plants can still be found growing in marshy areas today.
Researchers examined fragments of papyrus to learn about early writing techniques.

Etymology

From Latin papyrus, from Greek papyros (papyrus plant or paper).

2. NOUN

/ pəˈpaɪrəs / (AmE), / pəˈpaɪərəs / (BrE)
A material similar to thick paper made from the pith of the papyrus plant, used especially by ancient Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans for writing.
ancient writing material from papyrus plant

Examples

The ancient scroll was written on papyrus and preserved for centuries.
Scholars have discovered many original texts on fragments of papyrus.
Making papyrus required skilled craftsmanship in cutting and pressing the plant sheets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parchment - material made from animal skin used for writing
  • Synonym: vellum - fine parchment made from calf skin

Etymology

From Latin papyrus, from Ancient Greek πάπυρος (papyros), the name of the plant.

3. NOUN

/ pəˈpaɪrəs / (AmE), / pəˈpʌɪərəs / (BrE)
an ancient document or manuscript written on sheets made from the papyrus plant
ancient document written on papyrus

Examples

The museum displays an ancient papyrus containing hieroglyphs from Egypt.
Scholars studied a papyrus to learn about life in classical Alexandria.
The papyrus was carefully unrolled to reveal a lost Greek poem.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manuscript - a handwritten or typed document, especially before printing was common

Etymology

From Latin papyrus, from Greek papyros (the papyrus plant).
