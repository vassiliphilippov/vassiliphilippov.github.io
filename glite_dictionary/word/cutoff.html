cutoff

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌtˌɔf / (AmE), / ˈkʌtɒf / (BrE)
a road, path, or route that provides a shorter alternative to the usual way
shorter alternative route

Examples

We took the cutoff through the hills to avoid the morning traffic.
The map shows a cutoff that saves drivers nearly fifteen miles.
Local residents use a dirt cutoff to get to the main highway faster.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shortcut - a route shorter than the usual one

Etymology

From the verb phrase 'cut off', referring to something that shortens a journey by eliminating part of the normal route.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkʌtˌɔf / (AmE), / ˈkʌtɒf / (BrE)
a designated limit or point at which something must stop, ceases to operate, or is no longer valid
designated stopping point or limit

Examples

There's a strict cutoff time for submitting applications to the program.
If your income is above the cutoff, you are not eligible for assistance.
The power company issues a cutoff notice if your bill is overdue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deadline - a latest time or date by which something should be completed
  • Synonym: threshold - the level or point at which an effect or change occurs

Etymology

From the phrasal verb 'cut off,' meaning to stop or interrupt, by nominalization.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13781154-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈkʌtˌɔf / (AmE), / ˈkʌtɒf / (BrE)
a device that stops or terminates the flow of a liquid or gas in a pipe
device for stopping flow in a pipe

Examples

The plumber installed a cutoff to quickly stop the water supply in case of a leak.
Always know where the main cutoff is located before starting plumbing repairs.
If you smell gas, immediately turn the cutoff to the off position and call for help.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: valve - a device that controls the passage of fluid through a pipe

Etymology

From cut + off, indicating the function of stopping or interrupting the flow.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03158251-n
Synset POS: n
