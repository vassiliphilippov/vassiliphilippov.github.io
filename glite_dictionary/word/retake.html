retake

1. VERB

/ riˈteɪk / (AmE), / riˈteɪk / (BrE)
to photograph, film, or record something again, especially after an unsatisfactory first attempt
to photograph or film again

Examples

The photographer decided to retake the group picture after noticing someone had blinked.
We had to retake the shot because the lighting wasn't quite right.
After reviewing the footage, the director asked the actors to retake the scene.

Synset Examples

Please retake that scene

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reshoot - to shoot (a scene, photograph, etc.) again

Etymology

From re- (again) + take (to capture), reflecting the act of taking again.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01005633-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ riˈteɪk / (AmE), / riːˈteɪk / (BrE)
to take back possession of something by force, especially after it has been lost in battle or conflict
take back by force (esp. in battle)

Examples

The army was able to retake the city after a fierce battle.
Rebels tried to retake the region that had been occupied for months.
It took several days for the forces to retake the border post from the enemy.

Synset Examples

The military forces managed to recapture the fort

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recapture - to capture again, especially after having lost it

Etymology

From re- (again) + take; patterned after similar Latin-derived formations.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02308993-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈriːˌteɪk / (AmE), / ˈriːˌteɪk / (BrE)
an instance of shooting a scene, photograph, or shot again in film or photography, usually to correct an error or improve quality
a repeated shot or scene in film or photography

Examples

The director called for a retake after noticing a mistake in the background.
Due to equipment failure, the actors had to do several retakes.
Everyone was relieved when the scene was perfect and no retake was needed.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: take - an attempt at filming a scene without interruption

Etymology

From re- (again) + take (to capture on film), first used in cinematic contexts in early 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00909990-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ riˈteɪk / (AmE), / rɪˈteɪk / (BrE)
to capture or take possession of something or someone again, especially after it has been lost or seized.
to capture again

Examples

The army managed to retake the city after days of fierce fighting.
Police worked quickly to retake control of the building from the intruders.
They planned a mission to retake the hostages from their captors.

Synset Examples

recapture the escaped prisoner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recapture - to capture again after an escape or loss

Etymology

From re- (again) + take.
