to flout

1. VERB

/ flaʊt / (AmE), / flaʊt / (BrE)
to openly disregard or show contempt for (a law, rule, or convention)
openly disregard (rules, laws)

Examples

They chose to flout the city's noise regulations during the festival.
Some companies blatantly flout environmental laws to increase profits.
By not wearing a uniform, she was seen as flouting school policy.

Synset Examples

flout the rules

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defy - to openly resist or refuse to obey
  • Antonym: to obey - to comply with a command, rule, or law

Etymology

Probably from Middle English flouten (to play the flute, jeer), of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ flaʊt / (AmE), / flaʊt / (BrE)
to openly disregard or mock, especially by treating with obvious contempt or scorn
to show contempt by openly disobeying or mocking

Examples

The protesters chose to flout the new regulations by gathering publicly.
She continued to flout convention with her unique sense of style.
His remarks seemed to flout all sense of propriety and respect.

Synset Examples

The crowd jeered at the speaker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to tease or laugh at in a scornful manner
  • Antonym: respect - to admire deeply as a result of their abilities or qualities

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'flouten', related to Middle Dutch 'fluyten' (to play the flute, jeer).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00851872-v
Synset POS: v
