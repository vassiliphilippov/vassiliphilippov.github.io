cohort

1. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.hɔrt / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.hɔːt / (BrE)
A military unit of the ancient Roman army, typically consisting of 300–600 soldiers; more generally, a group or band of warriors.
Roman military unit; band of warriors

Examples

The Roman cohort marched steadily toward the enemy's camp.
Each cohort of the legion had its own banner and commander.
In battle, a cohort formed a strong defensive line against the opposing forces.

Etymology

From Latin cohors, cohortis (enclosure, company of soldiers).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.hɔrt / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.hɔːt / (BrE)
A group of people who share a common characteristic or are supporters of a particular person, cause, or activity.
group of companions or supporters

Examples

The senator arrived with a loyal cohort of advisors.
She and her cohort worked tirelessly to complete the project on time.
A small cohort of fans waited outside the venue to catch a glimpse of the band.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: group - a number of people or things that are together or share something in common

Etymology

From Latin 'cohors', meaning 'enclosure, company of soldiers'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.hɔrt / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.hɔːt / (BrE)
A group of people who share a particular characteristic, often age, and are treated as a group for study purposes.
group sharing a characteristic, often age

Examples

The study tracked a cohort of children born in 2010 over ten years.
Each cohort in the survey represented a different decade of birth.
Researchers compared the health outcomes of the 1980s cohort to those born in the 1990s.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: generation - all people born and living at about the same time, regarded collectively

Etymology

From Latin 'cohors' (yard, retinue, band of soldiers).
