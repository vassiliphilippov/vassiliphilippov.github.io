garner

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑrnər / (AmE), / ˈɡɑːnə / (BrE)
a storehouse for threshed grain or animal feed, especially in older usage
storehouse for threshed grain or feed
archaic

Examples

The old stone garner at the edge of the farm was used to store wheat for the winter.
During the harvest, each garner was filled with barley to feed the livestock.
The villagers gathered in the garner to weigh and distribute the season's grain.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: granary - a storehouse for threshed grain

Etymology

From Old French garnier (to store, protect), from Latin granarium (granary).

2. VERB

/ ˈɡɑrnər / (AmE), / ˈɡɑːnə / (BrE)
to collect or obtain something, especially after effort; to acquire or deserve by one's actions
to acquire or collect, especially by effort

Examples

Her research has garnered widespread recognition in academic circles.
He worked hard to garner support for the new policy.
The novel garnered praise from critics and readers alike.

Synset Examples

its beauty won Paris the name ‘City of Lights’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to acquire - to gain or obtain something
  • Synonym: to earn - to receive or deserve as a result of effort
  • Antonym: to lose - to fail to keep or maintain; to have taken away from you

Etymology

From Middle English *garneren*, from Old French *gerner*, from Latin *granarium* (a place for grain).

3. VERB

/ ˈɡɑrnər / (AmE), / ˈɡɑːnə / (BrE)
to collect or gather something, especially information, support, or achievements, often over a period of time
collect; gather (especially information or support)

Examples

She managed to garner enough support to launch her new project.
The movie garnered critical acclaim from reviewers worldwide.
He worked hard to garner valuable experience in the field over several years.

Synset Examples

gather some stones
pull your thoughts together

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gather - to bring things or people together in one place
  • Synonym: collect - to bring things together from different places or people

Etymology

From Old French 'gerner' (to store grain), from Latin 'granarium' (granary, storehouse for grain).
