californium

1. NOUN

/ ˌkæləˈfɔrniəm / (AmE), / ˌkælɪˈfɔːniəm / (BrE)
a radioactive transuranic chemical element with the symbol Cf and atomic number 98, used mainly in scientific research and as a neutron source
radioactive chemical element (Cf)

Examples

Researchers used californium as a source of neutrons in their experiments.
The element californium was first synthesized in 1950 by bombarding curium with alpha particles.
Because of its radioactivity, californium must be handled with extreme care in laboratory settings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: element - a substance that cannot be broken down chemically into simpler substances

Etymology

Named after the state of California, where it was first synthesized.

2. NOUN

/ ˌkæləˈfɔrniəm / (AmE), / ˌkælɪˈfɔːniəm / (BrE)
a radioactive chemical element (symbol Cf), produced artificially by bombarding curium with alpha particles; used in nuclear research and as a neutron source.
a radioactive chemical element

Examples

Californium is commonly used as a neutron source in scientific research.
Scientists first created californium by bombarding curium with alpha particles.
The radioactive nature of californium requires strict safety precautions when handling it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: element - a substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means
  • Degree Lower: curium - the chemical element used to produce californium

Etymology

Named after the state of California and the University of California, where it was first produced.
