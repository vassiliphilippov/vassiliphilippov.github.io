Crossopterygii

1. NOUN

/ krɒˌsɑptəˈrɪdʒiaɪ / (AmE), / krɒˌsɒptəˈrɪdʒɪaɪ / (BrE)
an extinct group of lobe-finned fishes, having paired fins that resemble limbs and considered ancestral to amphibians
group of extinct lobe-finned fishes

Examples

Fossils of Crossopterygii provide key evidence for the evolution of land-dwelling vertebrates.
The Crossopterygii had robust, limb-like fins that helped them move in shallow waters.
Scientists often study the Crossopterygii to understand how aquatic life adapted to terrestrial environments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lobe-finned fish - fish with fleshy, lobed, paired fins; class Sarcopterygii
  • Degree Higher: amphibian - cold-blooded vertebrate animals including frogs, toads, and salamanders

Etymology

From Greek 'krossos' (fringe) + 'pteryx' (fin, wing), referring to their fringed fins.

2. NOUN

/ krɑːsˌɑptəˈrɪdʒiːˌaɪ / (AmE), / krɒsˌɒptəˈrɪdʒiːˌaɪ / (BrE)
A subclass of ancient bony fishes with paired, fleshy fins, considered ancestral to amphibians.
subclass of lobe-finned fishes

Examples

The extinct Crossopterygii played a key role in the evolution of land vertebrates.
Fossils of Crossopterygii reveal their unique fleshy, limb-like fins.
Researchers studied the skeletal features of Crossopterygii to understand the origins of amphibians.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lobe-finned fish - fish with lobed, fleshy fins
  • Antonym: actinopterygian - ray-finned fish, contrasted with lobe-finned fish

Etymology

From Greek 'krosos' (fringe) + 'pteryx' (fin, wing).
