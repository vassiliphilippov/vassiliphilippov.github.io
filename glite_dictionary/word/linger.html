to linger

1. VERB

/ ˈlɪŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈlɪŋɡə / (BrE)
to remain or move about a place without any apparent purpose, often aimlessly or idly
remain in a place aimlessly

Examples

Several teenagers lingered outside the cinema long after the movie ended.
She likes to linger in the park even when her friends have gone home.
Security guards asked the man not to linger near the building entrance.

Synset Examples

The high school students like to loiter in the Central Square
Who is this man that is hanging around the department?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to loiter - to stand or wait around without apparent purpose
  • Antonym: to depart - to leave a place

Etymology

From Latin *linguāre* via Old French *linguer* (to delay, to remain).

2. VERB

/ ˈlɪŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈlɪŋɡə / (BrE)
to stay in a place longer than necessary, often because of reluctance to leave; to leave slowly and hesitantly
stay longer than necessary; leave slowly

Examples

After everyone else had left, she chose to linger in the empty hall for a moment longer.
He lingered at the door, unsure if he should leave just yet.
Tourists often linger by the fountains, reluctant to end their visit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dawdle - to move slowly and waste time
  • Antonym: hurry - to move or act with speed

Etymology

From Middle English 'lengeren', frequentative of 'lengthen', originally from Old English 'lengan' (to lengthen).

3. VERB

/ ˈlɪŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈlɪŋɡə / (BrE)
to stay in a place longer than is necessary, usually because you do not want to leave
stay somewhere longer than necessary

Examples

After the meeting ended, several colleagues lingered in the hallway to chat.
The scent of her perfume lingered long after she had left the room.
He likes to linger over his morning coffee, even on busy days.

Synset Examples

The shy student lingered in the corner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dawdle - to move or act slowly, wasting time
  • Antonym: to depart - to leave a place

Etymology

From Old English 'lengan' (to lengthen, prolong), related to Old Norse 'lengja' (to prolong).

4. VERB

/ ˈlɪŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈlɪŋɡə / (BrE)
to remain in a place or situation for a longer time than expected, especially as something gradually fades or disappears
remain present while fading

Examples

The fragrance of fresh bread lingered in the kitchen long after breakfast was over.
A sense of unease lingered even after the storm had passed.
The memory of that day lingers in his mind, bittersweet and vivid.

Synset Examples

Her perfume lingered on

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to persist - to continue to exist or endure
  • Antonym: fade - to gradually disappear or lose intensity

Etymology

From Middle English 'lengeren' (to dwell, delay), frequentative of Old English 'lengan' (to prolong, lengthen).

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ ˈlɪŋɡər / (AmE), / ˈlɪŋɡə / (BrE)
to stay in a place or remain for a long time, often because of reluctance to leave; to proceed slowly or take one's time
stay or proceed slowly; remain longer than necessary

Examples

He chose to linger at the café, enjoying his coffee and watching people pass by.
Tourists often linger at the museum, taking their time to see every exhibit.
She lingered in the doorway, reluctant to say goodbye.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dawdle - to waste time or move slowly
  • Antonym: hurry - to move or act with speed

Etymology

From Old English 'lingeren', frequentative of 'longian' (to dwell, to long for).
