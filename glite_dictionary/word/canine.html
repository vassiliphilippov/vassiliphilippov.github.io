canine

1. NOUN

/ ˈkeɪˌnaɪn / (AmE), / ˈkeɪnʌɪn / (BrE)
a mammal of the dog family (Canidae), such as a dog, wolf, or fox, typically having nonretractile claws and a long muzzle
dog-like mammal; member of the dog family

Examples

Wolves, foxes, and domestic dogs are all considered canines.
The park ranger studied the tracks left by a canine near the riverbank.
Fossils of ancient canines have been discovered in this region.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: feline - a mammal of the cat family (Felidae)

Etymology

From Latin caninus (of the dog), from canis (dog).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkænaɪn / (AmE), / ˈkeɪnaɪn / (BrE)
one of the four pointed, conical teeth in mammals, located between the incisors and the premolars, used for tearing food
pointed tooth between incisors and premolars
medical

Examples

The dentist explained that my canine was slightly crooked and might need adjustment.
Dogs have especially prominent upper canine teeth for gripping and tearing food.
She lost a canine tooth after falling from her bicycle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cuspid - another term for canine tooth in dentistry
  • Degree Lower: incisor - the type of tooth at the front of the mouth for cutting
  • Degree Higher: premolar - the tooth just behind the canine used for grinding

Etymology

From Latin *caninus* (of a dog), from *canis* (dog), as the shape of these teeth resembles those of a dog's fangs.

4. ADJ

/ ˈkeɪnaɪn / (AmE), / ˈkeɪnaɪn / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of a pointed conical tooth, especially one of the pointed teeth situated between the incisors and premolars in mammals.
relating to a pointed conical tooth
medical

Examples

The dentist noted that her upper right canine was slightly longer than average.
Some animals have very large canine teeth for hunting and tearing meat.
A broken canine tooth can cause significant discomfort and dental issues.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: incisor - a narrow-edged tooth at the front of the mouth, adapted for cutting
  • Degree Higher: molar - a tooth at the back of the mouth used for grinding

Etymology

From Latin caninus (of the dog), referring to the pointed shape of these teeth, similar to a dog's.
