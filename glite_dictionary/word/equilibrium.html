equilibrium

1. NOUN

/ ˌikwəˈlɪbriəm / (AmE), / ˌiːkwɪˈlɪbrɪəm / (BrE)
a state in which opposing forces or influences are balanced, resulting in stability or equality in distribution
state of balanced distribution

Examples

The government aims to achieve equilibrium between supply and demand in the economy.
When the concentrations on both sides are equal, the system has reached equilibrium.
Social justice depends on an equilibrium in the distribution of wealth and opportunity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: balance - a condition in which different elements are equal or in the correct proportions
  • Antonym: disequilibrium - a loss or absence of equilibrium or stability

Etymology

From Latin aequilibrium, from aequus (equal) + libra (balance).

3. NOUN

/ ˌikwəˈlɪbriəm / (AmE), / ˌiːkwɪˈlɪbrɪəm / (BrE)
A state in a chemical reaction in which the forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate, so that the concentrations of the reactants and products remain constant over time.
state with balanced chemical reaction rates

Examples

At chemical equilibrium, the concentrations of all reactants and products remain constant over time.
When a reaction reaches equilibrium, the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal.
Le Chatelier's principle explains how a system at equilibrium responds to external changes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: disequilibrium - a state where reaction rates are not balanced in a chemical process

Etymology

From Latin 'aequilibrium' (equal balance), from 'aequus' (equal) + 'libra' (balance).

the sense of balance that enables an organism to detect the orientation and movement of the head, maintained by structures in the inner ear
sense of balance in the inner ear

Examples

Damage to the inner ear can affect a person's equilibrium and cause dizziness.
The labyrinth in the ear plays a key role in maintaining equilibrium.
Astronauts can experience loss of equilibrium in zero gravity environments.
