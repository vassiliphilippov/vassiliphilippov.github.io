primate

1. NOUN

/ ˈpraɪˌmeɪt / (AmE), / ˈpraɪ.meɪt / (BrE)
a member of the highest order of mammals, including lemurs, tarsiers, monkeys, apes, and humans
order of mammals including apes, monkeys, and humans

Examples

Humans, chimpanzees, and gorillas are all classified as primates.
Many primates are known for their complex social behaviors and high intelligence.
Some species of primates live in large groups, while others are more solitary.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: mammal - warm-blooded vertebrate animal with hair or fur, and females secrete milk

Etymology

From Latin 'primates', plural of 'primas' (of the first rank); originally used in a religious sense, later extended to zoology.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpraɪ.meɪt / (AmE), / ˈpraɪ.meɪt / (BrE)
a mammal of the order Primates, including humans, apes, monkeys, and prosimians, characterized by good eyesight and flexible hands and feet
a mammal of the order Primates

Examples

The lemur is a type of primate found only in Madagascar.
Humans are classified as primates in biological taxonomy.
The study focused on the behavior of various primate species in the wild.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ape - a tailless primate of the family Hominoidea
  • Synonym: monkey - a primate of the order Primates, generally with a tail

Etymology

From Latin *prīmātes*, plural of *prīmās* (of the first rank), used in taxonomy to denote the order's high rank.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpraɪˌmeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɪːmət / (BrE)
a senior bishop or archbishop in certain Christian churches, often holding authority over other bishops in a region or country
chief bishop or archbishop in some churches

Examples

The primate of the Church of England is the Archbishop of Canterbury.
After his appointment as primate, he presided over all the churches in the region.
The primate issued a statement addressing the concerns of the clergy and laity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: archbishop - a bishop of the highest rank, in charge of an archdiocese or province
  • Degree Lower: bishop - a senior member of the Christian clergy with authority over a diocese

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *prīmās, prīmāt-* (principal leader), from Latin *primus* (first).
