recession

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈsɛʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛʃən / (BrE)
a small hollow or indentation in a surface; a concavity
a small hollow or indentation

Examples

A tiny recession in the wall collected water after heavy rain.
The dentist examined a recession on the gumline near the molar.
Archaeologists noted a recession in the ancient stone, possibly caused by centuries of erosion.

Etymology

From Latin recessio(n-) (a going back, retreat), from recedere (to recede).

/ rɪˈsɛʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛʃən / (BrE)

the act of returning something ceded

Examples

During the negotiations, both parties discussed the recession of certain exclusive rights.
Historical documents confirmed the recession of the island to its original owners.

From Latin recessio, from recedere (to go back, withdraw).

/ rɪˈsɛʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛʃən / (BrE)
the act of moving back or becoming more distant from a previous position
the act of becoming more distant

The recession of the coastline over centuries has left the old port far inland.

4. NOUN

/ rɪˈsɛʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
a period of temporary economic decline during which trade and industrial activity are reduced, generally identified by a fall in GDP and employment lasting at least six months
period of economic decline

Examples

The country entered a recession after two consecutive quarters of negative growth.
During the last recession, unemployment rates rose sharply across all sectors.
Many businesses were forced to close during the recession due to declining demand.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: depression - a prolonged and severe downturn in economic activity
  • Antonym: boom - a period of rapid economic growth

Etymology

From Latin recessio (a going back, retreat), from recedere (to go back, withdraw).
