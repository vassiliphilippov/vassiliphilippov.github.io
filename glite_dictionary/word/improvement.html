improvement

1. NOUN

/ ɪmˈpruvmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpruːvmənt / (BrE)
the act or process of making something better, or the state of being made better
the act of improving something

Examples

The company saw significant improvement in its sales figures this quarter.
Regular exercise can lead to a noticeable improvement in your health.
We made several improvements to the house before selling it.

Synset Examples

their improvements increased the value of the property

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enhancement - the process of increasing or improving in value or quality
  • Antonym: deterioration - the process of becoming worse

Etymology

From Latin 'improbare' (to make better), via Old French 'emprovement'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪmˈpruːvmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpruːvmənt / (BrE)
A change or development that makes something better or a state in which something becomes better than before.
a change making something better

Examples

The new healthcare policy is a significant improvement over the previous system.
There has been a noticeable improvement in her attitude this semester.
We saw an improvement in the team's performance after hiring the new coach.

Synset Examples

the new school represents a great improvement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advancement - progress or improvement in development
  • Antonym: decline - a worsening or deterioration in condition

Etymology

From Middle English 'emprowement', from Anglo-French 'emprowement', from 'emprower' (to improve).

3. NOUN

/ ɪmˈpruvmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpruːvmənt / (BrE)
a change or addition that makes something better or increases its value, quality, or effectiveness.
an instance of making something better

Examples

There has been a dramatic improvement in air quality over the past decade.
The new software update brought considerable improvement to system performance.
We noticed a steady improvement in her grades after she started studying with a tutor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: progress - forward movement toward a better or more advanced state
  • Antonym: decline - a gradual and continuous loss of strength, numbers, quality, or value

Etymology

From Middle English 'emprowement', influenced by Old French 'emprouvement', from 'emprover' (to improve).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07371988-n
Synset POS: n
