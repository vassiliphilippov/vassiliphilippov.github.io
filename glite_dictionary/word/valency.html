valency

1. NOUN

/ ˈveɪlənsi / (AmE), / ˈveɪlənsi / (BrE)
(Biology) The relative capacity of a biological molecule, such as an antigen or substrate, to react or interact with another molecule.
capacity to interact in biological processes
medical

Examples

The valency of an antibody determines how effectively it can bind to antigens.
Researchers studied the valency of viral proteins to assess their interactions in the host.
A change in valency may alter the biological response to a particular medication.

Etymology

From Latin valentia (strength, capacity), via chemical usage.

2. NOUN

/ ˈveɪlənsi / (AmE), / ˈveɪlənsi / (BrE)
(Chemistry) The capacity of an atom or a radical to combine with other atoms, measured by the number of hydrogen atoms or equivalents it can displace or combine with.
combining capacity of an atom in chemistry
medical

Examples

The valency of carbon is four, allowing it to form four bonds with other atoms.
Oxygen has a valency of two, which explains why water is H₂O.
Chemists determine the valency of elements to predict how they will react.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: valence - alternative spelling; combining capacity of atoms in chemistry

Etymology

From Latin *valentia* (strength, capacity).

3. NOUN

/ ˈveɪlənsi / (AmE), / ˈveɪlənsi / (BrE)
the property of an element that determines the number of chemical bonds it can form with other elements
chemical bonding capacity of an element
medical

Examples

The valency of carbon allows it to form four bonds with other atoms.
Oxygen has a valency of two, which means it can form two chemical bonds.
In chemistry class, students learned how to determine the valency of various elements.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: valence - alternative spelling, particularly in American English; same meaning in chemistry

Etymology

From Latin 'valentia' (strength, capacity), via Medieval Latin 'valentia' (combining power, especially in chemistry).
