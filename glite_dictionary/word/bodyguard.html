bodyguard

1. VERB

/ ˈbɑdiˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈbɒdiˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
to accompany and protect someone from physical harm, usually as a bodyguard
accompany and protect from harm

Examples

Two security officers bodyguarded the celebrity as she left the hotel.
He hired a professional to bodyguard him during the conference.
They often bodyguard diplomats to ensure their safety abroad.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to protect - to keep someone or something safe from harm or injury

Etymology

Formed from the noun 'bodyguard' (a person who protects another), itself from 'body' + 'guard'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02030714-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɑdiˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈbɒdiˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
a person employed to protect someone important from danger or attack, often by accompanying them in public
person who protects someone important

Examples

The celebrity hired a bodyguard to keep her safe during public appearances.
His bodyguard intervened quickly when the crowd became unruly.
As a bodyguard, he was trained to anticipate security threats.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protector - a person who protects someone or something

Etymology

Formed from 'body' + 'guard' in English, indicating its literal meaning.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09882437-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɑdiˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈbɒdiˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
a group of people whose job is to protect an important person from danger or harm
a group protecting an important person

Examples

The president's bodyguard formed a protective ring around him during the event.
A squad of bodyguard accompanied the ambassador to every public appearance.
The celebrity relies on a trusted bodyguard when attending large gatherings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: escort - a person or group accompanying and protecting another

Etymology

From 'body' + 'guard', first used in the 17th century for a person or persons who protect.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08232722-n
Synset POS: n
