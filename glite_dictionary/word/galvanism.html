galvanism

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡælvəˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈɡal.və.nɪ.zəm / (BrE)
Electricity generated by chemical reactions, especially as observed in a cell or battery.
electricity from chemical reaction

Examples

Scientists in the 18th century were fascinated by galvanism and its potential to animate muscle.
The battery operates by means of galvanism, converting chemical energy into electrical energy.
Mary Shelley was inspired by galvanism when she wrote about reanimating life in her novel 'Frankenstein'.

Etymology

From Italian 'galvanismo', after Luigi Galvani, an Italian scientist who studied bioelectricity.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡælvəˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈɡal.və.nɪ.zəm / (BrE)
the therapeutic use of electricity in the treatment of disease, especially the application of electric currents to stimulate muscles or nerves
use of electricity for medical therapy
medical

Examples

In the nineteenth century, galvanism was considered a promising treatment for paralysis.
Early experiments with galvanism led to important discoveries about how nerves and muscles respond to electricity.
Many doctors were fascinated by the apparent healing powers of galvanism in restoring movement to paralyzed limbs.

Etymology

From Italian 'galvanismo', named after Luigi Galvani, an Italian physician and physicist
