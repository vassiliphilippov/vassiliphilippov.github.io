fortissimo

1. ADJ

/ fɔrˈtɪsɪˌmoʊ / (AmE), / fɔːˈtɪsɪməʊ / (BrE)
(in music) very loud and strong; used as a direction to play or sing with the utmost volume.
very loud (musical direction)

Examples

The composer marked this section as fortissimo to create a dramatic effect.
During the performance, the orchestra suddenly played fortissimo, startling the audience.
She ended the song with a fortissimo note that echoed across the hall.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: forte - (in music) loud; a less intense dynamic than fortissimo
  • Antonym: pianissimo - (in music) very soft; the opposite dynamic of fortissimo

Etymology

From Italian 'fortissimo', superlative of 'forte' (loud).

2. NOUN

/ fɔrˈtɪsɪmoʊ / (AmE), / fɔːˈtɪsɪməʊ / (BrE)
a passage in a musical composition that is to be played or sung very loudly
very loud musical passage

Examples

The orchestra reached a stunning fortissimo at the climax of the symphony.
In this section, the conductor signaled for a fortissimo to emphasize the dramatic shift.
As the music swelled to fortissimo, the audience felt the intensity of the performance.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: forte - loud (in music)
  • Antonym: pianissimo - very soft, in music

Etymology

From Italian, superlative of 'forte' (loud), from Latin 'fortis' (strong).

3. ADV

/ fɔrˈtɪsɪˌmoʊ / (AmE), / fɔːˈtɪsɪməʊ / (BrE)
(in music) very loudly; as a direction, to be played very loudly.
(music) very loudly

Examples

The orchestra burst into fortissimo during the final movement, filling the hall with sound.
At the conductor’s signal, the choir sang the chorus fortissimo, creating a powerful climax.
The composer marked the passage fortissimo to build excitement in the music.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: pianissimo - (music) very softly; in a very quiet manner
  • Degree Lower: forte - (music) loudly; to be played loudly

Etymology

From Italian fortissimo (‘very strong, very loud’), superlative of forte (‘loud’).
