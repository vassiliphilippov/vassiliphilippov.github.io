supernumerary

1. NOUN

/ ˌsuː.pɚˈnuː.məˌrɛr.i / (AmE), / ˌsuː.pəˈnjuː.mə.rə.ri / (BrE)
a minor actor in a play, opera, or film who appears in crowd scenes and does not have speaking parts
minor actor in crowd scenes

Examples

He worked as a supernumerary in several opera productions before landing larger roles.
The casting director is looking for supernumeraries to fill the crowd scenes in the film.
As a supernumerary, she enjoyed being part of the theatre world without the pressure of speaking lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extra - a minor actor who appears in a non-speaking role, usually part of a crowd scene

Etymology

From Latin supernumerarius (exceeding the number), from super (above) + numerus (number).

2. ADJ

/ ˌsuːpərˈnuːməˌrɛri / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəˈnjuːmərəri / (BrE)
present in excess of the normal or required number; more than is needed or usual, especially used in technical or formal contexts
more than the required number

Examples

The patient was born with a supernumerary toe on her right foot.
Many large theatres keep a few supernumerary actors on call for crowd scenes.
The report found several supernumerary staff members whose roles overlapped with existing positions.

3. NOUN

/ ˌsuː.pɚˈnuː.məˌrɛr.i / (AmE), / ˌsuː.pəˈnjuː.mə.rər.i / (BrE)
a person who is present but has no essential or assigned role; an extra or surplus person
person serving no apparent function

Examples

The company found it hard to dismiss any supernumerary without disrupting team morale.
In the film industry, a supernumerary often appears in crowd scenes without a specific role.
During the reorganization, several employees were classified as supernumerary staff.

Synset Examples

reducing staff is difficult because our employees include no supernumeraries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extra - a person engaged temporarily, especially in a performance, with no speaking part
  • Antonym: essential - a person or thing absolutely necessary

Etymology

From Latin supernumerarius, from super- (above) + numerarius (of numbers).
