twinge

1. NOUN

/ twɪndʒ / (AmE), / twɪndʒ / (BrE)
a sudden, sharp pain or feeling, often emotional or physical
a sudden sharp pain or emotion

Examples

She felt a twinge of guilt as she remembered her promise.
A sudden twinge in his knee forced him to stop running.
He couldn't help but feel a twinge of envy watching his friend's success.

Synset Examples

pangs of regret
she felt a stab of excitement
twinges of conscience

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pang - a sudden sharp pain or distressing feeling
  • Synonym: stab - a sudden, brief pain or sensation

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English twengen (to pinch, tweak), related to Old Norse thvinga (to force).

2. VERB

/ twɪndʒ / (AmE), / twɪndʒ / (BrE)
to feel a sudden, sharp, and usually brief pain in a specific part of the body
feel a sudden sharp pain

Examples

He suddenly twinged in his lower back as he bent to pick up the box.
Occasionally, I twinge in my knee after a long run.
She twinged with anxiety every time she heard the phone ring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sting - to feel a sharp, sudden pain
  • Degree Lower: ache - to feel a continuous or prolonged pain

Etymology

From Middle English 'twengen' (to pinch, tug), of Germanic origin; related to Old High German 'zwengen' (to constrain).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02126310-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ twɪndʒ / (AmE), / twɪndʒ / (BrE)
a sudden, sharp feeling of pain or distress
sudden sharp pain or discomfort

Examples

He felt a sudden twinge in his back as he lifted the box.
A twinge of guilt made her hesitate before speaking.
Every now and then, a twinge of pain shot through his knee.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pang - a sudden sharp pain or emotion

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English *twengen* (to pinch, pain), related to Old English *twengan* (to pinch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05733214-n
Synset POS: n
