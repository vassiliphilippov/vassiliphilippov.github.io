bath

1. NOUN

/ bæθ / (AmE), / bɑːθ / (BrE)
a vessel or container filled with liquid in which something is immersed for a specific purpose, such as cleaning, processing, or maintaining temperature
container of liquid for immersing objects

Examples

The metal parts were cleaned in a bath of solvent before assembly.
She immersed the sample in an ice-water bath to stop the reaction.
The photographic print was placed in a chemical bath for development.

Synset Examples

she soaked the etching in an acid bath

Etymology

From Old English bæth (immersion in water), related to bathe.

2. NOUN

/ bæθ / (AmE), / bɑːθ / (BrE)
an ancient Hebrew unit of liquid capacity, approximately equal to 10 gallons or 37 liters
ancient Hebrew liquid measure (~10 gallons)

Examples

The priest poured a bath of oil as required by the ancient law.
According to the Old Testament, a bath was used to measure large quantities of wine.
The vessel could hold about one bath of water, suitable for ritual washing.

Etymology

From Hebrew *bat* (measure of capacity).

3. NOUN

/ bæθ / (AmE), / bɑːθ / (BrE)
an act of washing the body by immersing oneself in water, typically in a bathtub
act of washing the body in water

Examples

I like to take a relaxing bath after a long day at work.
The children splashed water everywhere during their evening bath.
She drew a hot bath and added some lavender oil to the water.

Synset Examples

he has a good bath every morning

Related Terms

  • Antonym: shower - an act of washing oneself with a spray of water

Etymology

From Old English 'bæth', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'bad', German 'Bad'.
