sheldrake

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛlˌdreɪk / (AmE), / ˈʃɛl.dreɪk / (BrE)
An Old World duck slightly larger than a mallard, with mostly black-and-white plumage and a red bill.
large Old World duck, black-and-white plumage

Examples

A flock of sheldrakes settled on the lake, their striking plumage catching the sunlight.
The sheldrake is known for its bold coloring and loud, honking call.
We spotted a pair of sheldrakes near the estuary during our birdwatching trip.

Etymology

From Middle English 'sheld' (piebald) + 'drake' (male duck).

2. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛlˌdreɪk / (AmE), / ˈʃɛl.dreɪk / (BrE)
a large, crested, fish-eating diving duck with a slender, hooked bill that has serrated edges, belonging to the genus Tadorna or Mergus.
large crested fish-eating duck

Examples

The bright plumage of the sheldrake made it easy to spot among the reeds.
A pair of sheldrakes dived gracefully for fish in the shallow estuary.
Unlike many other ducks, the sheldrake prefers saltwater habitats along the coast.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: merganser - a similar fish-eating diving duck
  • Degree Higher: duck - a type of waterbird with a broad blunt bill

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *sheldrak*, from 'sheld' (variegated) + 'drake' (male duck).
