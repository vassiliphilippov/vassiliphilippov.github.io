gall

1. NOUN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
A sore or wound on the skin, especially one caused by rubbing or chafing.
sore caused by chafing

Examples

The harness caused a painful gall on the horse's shoulder.
After the hike, she discovered a gall where her backpack had rubbed against her skin.
Proper padding can help prevent a gall from forming during long rides.

Etymology

From Old English 'gealla', meaning 'bile', later extended to sores likened to corrosive effects.

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
(of skin or body part) to become or make sore through rubbing or friction.
become or make sore from rubbing

Hiking for hours in wet boots can gall your feet.

3. NOUN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
A bitter yellowish fluid secreted by the liver and stored in the gallbladder, which helps to digest fats.
digestive fluid from the liver
medical

Examples

Doctors discovered an excess of gall in her system during the medical exam.
Gall is essential for the digestion and absorption of dietary fats.
The liver continuously produces gall, which is stored in the gallbladder until needed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bile - the same digestive fluid, especially in technical or medical contexts

Etymology

From Old English 'galla', from Latin 'gallē' (bile).

5. NOUN

/ ɡɔl / (AmE), / ɡɔːl / (BrE)
An abnormal growth or swelling of plant tissue, typically caused by insects, fungi, bacteria, or injury.
abnormal plant tissue swelling

Examples

The oak tree was covered in galls caused by tiny wasps.
Scientists studied the formation of galls to understand plant-insect interactions.
Some galls can be mistaken for fruit, but they are actually abnormal tissue growths.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tumor - an abnormal mass of tissue; analogous term for animals

Etymology

From Old English 'galla', related to swelling or sore, influenced by Latin 'galla' (oak-apple, gall).
