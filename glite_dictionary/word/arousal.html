arousal

1. NOUN

/ əˈraʊzəl / (AmE), / əˈraʊzəl / (BrE)
the act of awakening a response, feeling, or emotion in someone or something
the act of awakening a response or emotion

Examples

The arousal of curiosity is essential for effective learning.
The film was designed for the arousal of suspense and excitement.
Psychologists study the arousal of different emotions in various situations.

Synset Examples

the purpose of art is the arousal of emotions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stimulation - the action of encouraging something to happen or develop
  • Antonym: suppression - the act of stopping or restraining something

Etymology

From 'arouse' + '-al', formed in English.

2. NOUN

/ əˈraʊzl̩ / (AmE), / əˈraʊzəl / (BrE)
a state in which there is increased physiological or psychological activity, such as excitement or alertness
heightened physiological activity

Examples

The loud noise triggered a sudden arousal in the laboratory rats.
Physical arousal is necessary for peak athletic performance.
Stress can lead to increased arousal and difficulty sleeping.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alertness - the quality of being alert or awake
  • Antonym: calmness - the state of being calm and relaxed

Etymology

From English verb 'arouse' + suffix '-al'.

Examples

Light touching and kissing can lead to increased arousal between partners.
Foreplay is important for fostering arousal and intimacy in a relationship.

4. NOUN

/ əˈraʊzl̩ / (AmE), / əˈraʊzl̩ / (BrE)
the process of waking from sleep or becoming alert and responsive after a period of rest
awakening from sleep
medical

Examples

The patient experienced frequent arousal during the night, disrupting her sleep cycle.
Sudden arousal can occur due to loud noises or other disturbances in the environment.
Children may be difficult to wake if their level of arousal is low during deep sleep.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wakefulness - the state of being awake
  • Antonym: sleep - a natural recurring state of rest

Etymology

From the verb 'arouse', from Middle English, from Old French 'arouser', ultimately from Latin 'ad-' (to) + 'rosa' (dew, metaphor for awakening).
