enlargement

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈlɑrdʒmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈlɑːdʒmənt / (BrE)
a photographic print that is made larger than the original negative or image
a large photographic print

Examples

She ordered an enlargement of her favorite wedding photo.
The enlargement brought out details that were not visible in the original snapshot.
He framed the enlargement and hung it in the living room.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: print - a photographic image produced on paper

Etymology

From enlarge + -ment; 'enlarge' from Old French enlargier, based on Latin largus (large).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈlɑrdʒmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈlɑːdʒmənt / (BrE)
the state or process of becoming larger; an increase in size, extent, or amount.
the state of being enlarged

Examples

The recent enlargement of the company resulted in the hiring of many new employees.
After surgery, there was a noticeable enlargement of the affected lymph nodes.
The bank approved funds for the enlargement of the community center.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expansion - the act or process of becoming larger or more extensive
  • Antonym: reduction - the act or process of making something smaller

Etymology

From Old French *enlargement* or Medieval Latin *enlargamentum*, from 'enlarge' + '-ment'.

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈlɑrdʒmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈlɑːdʒmənt / (BrE)
the act or process of making something larger, or the state of being made larger
the act of making larger

Examples

The doctor recommended an enlargement of the image so the details could be examined more closely.
There has been a recent enlargement of the park to include a new playground area.
Joining the European Union marked a significant enlargement of the organization's scope.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expansion - the act of becoming greater in size, number, or importance
  • Antonym: reduction - the act of making something smaller or less in amount

Etymology

From Old French enlargement, from enlarger (to make large), from en- + large.
