professed

1. ADJ

/ prəˈfɛst / (AmE), / prəˈfɛst / (BrE)
stated or claimed, often falsely or with the intention to deceive; alleged rather than genuine.
claimed or alleged (often falsely)

Examples

Despite his professed concern for the environment, he continued to invest in oil companies.
Her professed innocence did little to convince the skeptical jury.
They questioned the company's professed commitment to improving working conditions.

Synset Examples

his professed intentions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alleged - stated without proof, supposed
  • Antonym: genuine - real, authentic, not false or pretended

Etymology

Past participle of 'profess', from Latin 'professus' (to declare publicly).

2. ADJ

/ prəˈfɛst / (AmE), / prəˈfɛst / (BrE)
Stated or claimed openly, especially without necessarily being genuine or qualified.
claimed or declared (often falsely)

Examples

He is a professed expert, but many doubt his actual experience.
The professed goals of the organization did not match its real intentions.
Although she was a professed skeptic, she attended every lecture on the topic.

Synset Examples

a professed philosopher

Related Terms

  • Synonym: so-called - commonly or supposedly named, but not necessarily so

Etymology

From Latin professus, past participle of profiteri (to declare publicly).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01875208-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ prəˈfɛst / (AmE), / prəˈfɛst / (BrE)
(of a quality, feeling, or belief) openly declared or claimed, especially when this is not necessarily true.
openly declared or claimed

Examples

He is a professed expert in the subject, though his credentials are unclear.
Despite her professed interest in art, she rarely visits galleries.
The suspect was a professed supporter of the organization.

Synset Examples

an avowed enemy
her professed love of everything about that country
McKinley was assassinated by a professed anarchist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: avowed - openly declared or acknowledged
  • Antonym: actual - genuine; existing in fact, not just in name or claim

Etymology

Past participle of 'profess', from Latin 'professus'.
