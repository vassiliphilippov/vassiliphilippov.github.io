recovery

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈkʌvəri / (AmE), / rɪˈkʌvəri / (BrE)
the act of regaining possession of or saving something that was lost or at risk of being lost
the act of regaining something lost

Examples

The team's unexpected recovery of the stolen painting surprised the community.
Quick recovery of the lost data prevented a major setback for the company.
Efforts are underway for the recovery of artifacts from the sunken ship.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: loss - the state of no longer having something or someone

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French 'recoverer' (to recover), from Latin 'recuperare'.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈkʌvəri / (AmE), / rɪˈkʌvəri / (BrE)
the process of returning to a normal state of health, mind, or strength after illness, injury, or difficulties
process of returning to health after illness or injury

Examples

She made a remarkable recovery after her operation and was back at work in a month.
The doctors monitored his recovery carefully, making sure he followed their advice.
Many patients require physical therapy as part of their recovery from serious injuries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: convalescence - the gradual recovery of health and strength after illness
  • Antonym: relapse - a return of a disease or symptoms after recovery

Etymology

From Old French *recovrer* (recover), from Latin *recuperare* (to recover, regain).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈkʌvəri / (AmE), / rɪˈkʌvəri / (BrE)
the process of returning to a normal or original state after a setback, loss, or damage
return to original state

Examples

The recovery of the economy following the recession took longer than expected.
Full recovery of the patient is expected within a few weeks.
Scientists monitored the recovery of wildlife in the area after the oil spill.

Synset Examples

the recovery of the forest after the fire was surprisingly rapid

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restoration - the act of bringing back to a former position or condition
  • Antonym: deterioration - the process of becoming worse

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French 'recovrer' (to recover), from Latin 'recuperare'.
