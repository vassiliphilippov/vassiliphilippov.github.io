posture

1. VERB

/ ˈpɑstʃɚ / (AmE), / ˈpɒstʃə / (BrE)
to adopt an artificial or exaggerated manner or attitude, especially to impress others
behave in an affected way to impress

Examples

He tends to posture in meetings to seem more knowledgeable than he is.
The candidate accused his opponent of merely posturing for the cameras instead of stating real policies.
She postured as a socialite at the party, hoping to impress the guests.

Synset Examples

Don't pay any attention to him — he is always posing to impress his peers!
She postured and made a total fool of herself

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pose - to assume a particular attitude in order to impress others
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02524849-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈpɑstʃər / (AmE), / ˈpɒstʃə / (BrE)
to assume or arrange one's body in a particular position, especially for a photograph or an artist
assume a specific bodily position, often for art

Examples

The model was asked to posture gracefully for the portrait session.
For the sculpture class, students had to posture in dynamic poses for several minutes.
She postured elegantly on stage during the ballet performance.

Synset Examples

We don't know the woman who posed for Leonardo so often

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pose - to assume a particular attitude or position, especially for artistic purposes

Etymology

From Latin 'positura' (position, attitude), via French 'posture'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːstʃər / (AmE), / ˈpɒstʃə / (BrE)
a particular mental attitude or approach deliberately adopted towards something, often for a specific purpose
adopted mental attitude or stance

Examples

The company adopted a tough posture towards negotiations with the union.
His political posture shifted as public opinion changed.
She criticized the government's posture on climate change as too passive.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attitude - a settled way of thinking or feeling about something
  • Synonym: stance - a publicly stated opinion or attitude
  • Synonym: approach - a way of dealing with something

Etymology

From Latin postura (position, attitude), from ponere (to place).
