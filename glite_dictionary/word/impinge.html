to impinge

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɪndʒ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɪndʒ / (BrE)
to advance beyond the usual or proper limit, often having a negative or intrusive effect
go beyond the usual limit

Examples

The new building's design may impinge on the neighboring park's open space.
Researchers must be careful not to impinge upon the participants' privacy.
Rising costs could eventually impinge on our plans for expansion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encroach - to intrude gradually or stealthily, often on rights or territory

Etymology

From Latin 'impingere', from 'in-' (into) + 'pangere' (to fasten).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01997384-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɪndʒ / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɪndʒ / (BrE)
to have an effect or impact, especially a negative one, typically used with 'on' or 'upon'
have an impact or effect on

Examples

Noise from the construction site began to impinge on the residents' ability to concentrate.
Managers must ensure their decisions do not impinge upon employees' legal rights.
Her personal problems started to impinge on her performance at work.

Synset Examples

This impinges on my rights as an individual
This matter entrenches on other domains

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infringe - to actively break the terms of a law or agreement; to encroach upon
  • Synonym: to encroach - to intrude on a person's territory, rights, etc.

Etymology

From Latin 'impingere' (to strike against), from 'in-' (into) + 'pangere' (to fix).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
