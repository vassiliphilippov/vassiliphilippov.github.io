401(k)

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɔr oʊ ˈwʌn keɪ / (AmE), / ˌfɔːr əʊ ˈwʌn keɪ / (BrE)
A tax-advantaged retirement savings plan in the United States, funded by employee contributions and often matched by the employer, with contributions made before taxes and investment growth tax-deferred until withdrawal.
U.S. tax-advantaged retirement savings plan
american

Examples

Many companies offer their employees a 401(k) plan to help them save for retirement.
She decided to increase her 401(k) contributions after receiving a raise.
If you leave your job, you can roll over your 401(k) into an IRA without penalty.

Etymology

Named after section 401(k) of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code.
