to oxidise

1. VERB

/ ˈɑksɪdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɒksɪdaɪz / (BrE)
to add oxygen to a substance or combine with oxygen, especially as a chemical reaction
combine with oxygen (chemistry)
british

Examples

If iron is exposed to air and moisture, it will slowly oxidise and form rust.
Copper pipes can oxidise when they come into contact with certain chemicals in water.
Fruit slices often oxidise and turn brown when left out in the open.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oxidize - add oxygen to or combine with oxygen (US spelling)
  • Antonym: to reduce - chemically remove oxygen from or add electrons to a substance

Etymology

From oxygen + -ise, patterned after French 'oxyder' or after 'oxide'.

2. VERB

/ ˈɑksɪˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɒksɪdaɪz / (BrE)
(of a substance) to combine or react with oxygen, or to be changed into an oxide.
combine with oxygen; become an oxide

Examples

Iron quickly begins to oxidise when exposed to air and moisture.
If the copper surface is left untreated, it will gradually oxidise over time.
Some metals do not oxidise as easily as others, making them valuable for certain applications.

Synset Examples

This metal oxidizes easily

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to reduce - to cause (a chemical substance) to undergo reduction; the opposite chemical process of gaining electrons or losing oxygen
  • Synonym: to oxidize - alternate American spelling for oxidise; to combine with oxygen or become an oxide

Etymology

From French oxyder (to oxidise), from oxide, itself from oxygen.
