pigeonhole

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɪdʒənˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈpɪdʒɪnhəʊl / (BrE)
a small compartment, typically one of a set, used for sorting or storing papers, letters, or small items
small compartment for storage or sorting

Examples

Each staff member has a pigeonhole in the staff room for internal mail.
The manager placed the documents in the appropriate pigeonhole for filing.
Her letters accumulated in the dusty pigeonhole at the front desk.

Etymology

From the resemblance of small compartments to holes where pigeons nest.

2. VERB

/ ˈpɪdʒənˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈpɪdʒɪnhəʊl / (BrE)
to put or store something in a small compartment or designated slot, especially in a desk or shelving unit
put into a small compartment or slot

Examples

She carefully placed each letter into its own pigeonhole in the organizer.
The librarian pigeonholed the returned books for sorting later.
He pigeonholes notes and receipts in the tiny compartments of his desk.

Etymology

From 'pigeon' and 'hole', originally referring to compartments that resemble nesting holes for pigeons.

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɪdʒənˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈpɪdʒ.ən.həʊl / (BrE)
a category, especially an overly narrow or simplistic one, into which someone or something is placed or classified
an overly simple category

Examples

It's unfair to put people into a pigeonhole based on their appearance alone.
The committee refused to assign the proposal to a single pigeonhole and considered it from multiple angles.
Writers often dislike being placed in a particular pigeonhole because it limits creative freedom.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: category - a class or division of people or things regarded as having particular shared characteristics
  • Synonym: stereotype - a widely held but oversimplified idea of a particular type of person or thing
  • Synonym: generalization - a general statement or concept obtained by inference from specific cases

Etymology

Originally from the literal compartments used to house pigeons; by extension, used for small compartments and then as a metaphor for restrictive categories.

4. VERB

/ ˈpɪdʒᵊnˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈpɪdʒᵊnhəʊl / (BrE)
to unfairly classify or judge someone or something as belonging to a particular category, especially by ignoring individual differences
classify unfairly; stereotype

Examples

It's unfair to pigeonhole people based on their appearance alone.
She didn't want her work to be pigeonholed as just another romance novel.
The committee was accused of pigeonholing innovative ideas without proper consideration.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stereotype - to judge or categorize someone based on oversimplified ideas
  • Synonym: to typecast - to always cast or regard a person as having only one type of character

Etymology

From 'pigeonhole' (noun), originally referring to small compartments for pigeons, then for organizing papers; figurative sense as verb from the 19th century.

5. VERB

/ ˈpɪdʒənˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈpɪdʒ.ən.həʊl / (BrE)
to assign someone or something to a particular category, especially in a way that is too rigid or restrictive
categorize in a restrictive way

Examples

It's unfair to pigeonhole children based on their early academic performance.
Some critics pigeonhole his work as only suitable for young audiences.
She hates being pigeonholed as just a romantic comedian.

Synset Examples

He decided to pigeon his friends into groups.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: label - to describe or classify in a simplistic way
  • Synonym: to classify - to arrange in categories
  • Synonym: to generalize - to make broad or overly simplistic judgments

Etymology

From the noun 'pigeonhole', originally referring to a small compartment, likened to the holes for pigeons; the verb is derived from the metaphorical use of sorting into compartments.
