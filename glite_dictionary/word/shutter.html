shutter

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃʌtər / (AmE), / ˈʃʌtə / (BrE)
a mechanical device in a camera that opens and closes to control the length of time light reaches the film or sensor
camera device controlling exposure time

Examples

The camera's shutter opened for half a second to capture the night scene.
A fast shutter speed is necessary to photograph moving objects clearly.
He heard the distinct click of the shutter as he took the picture.

Etymology

From Middle English 'shuteren', related to 'shut', meaning to close.

2. VERB

/ ˈʃʌt̬ər / (AmE), / ˈʃʌtə / (BrE)
to close (a window, door, or building) with shutters
to close with shutters

Examples

During the storm, we shuttered all the windows to protect the glass from flying debris.
Most shops shutter their doors and windows overnight for security reasons.
She quickly shuttered the cottage before leaving for the city.

Synset Examples

We shuttered the window to keep the house cool

Related Terms

  • Antonym: open - to move something so that it is no longer closed or covered

3. NOUN

/ ˈʃʌt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈʃʌtə / (BrE)
a solid covering, usually hinged, that can be closed over a window to keep out light or protect against weather
hinged covering for a window

Examples

She closed the shutter to keep the storm out.
The old house had wooden shutters painted green.
He opened the shutter to let in some sunlight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blind - a covering for a window, often made of slats, cloth, or other material, used to block light

Etymology

From Old English scyttan (to shut, bolt).
