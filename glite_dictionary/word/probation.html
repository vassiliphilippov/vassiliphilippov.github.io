probation

1. NOUN

/ proʊˈbeɪʃən / (AmE), / prəˈbeɪʃən / (BrE)
a period during which someone's suitability for a job or membership is tested before they are given full acceptance or permanent status
trial period to test suitability

Examples

After being hired, she was placed on probation for three months to assess her performance.
New members must pass a six-week probation before gaining full club privileges.
His probation period ended successfully, and he became a permanent employee.

Etymology

From Latin probatio (testing, proof), from probare (to test, approve).

2. NOUN

/ proʊˈbeɪʃən / (AmE), / prəʊˈbeɪʃən / (BrE)
A period of supervision ordered by a court as an alternative to imprisonment, during which an offender must comply with certain conditions.
court-ordered supervised period for offenders
legal

Examples

After the trial, the judge sentenced him to one year of probation instead of jail time.
If she violates the conditions of her probation, she could be sent to prison.
The court-appointed officer will supervise his activities during the period of probation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parole - conditional release of a prisoner before the end of their sentence

Etymology

From Latin probatio (a proving, trial), from probare (to test, prove).

3. NOUN

/ proʊˈbeɪʃən / (AmE), / prəʊˈbeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Law) A period during which an offender is allowed to remain in the community instead of going to prison, subject to conditions set by a court and under supervision.
legal sentence allowing supervised freedom
legal

Examples

The judge sentenced him to five years of probation instead of prison time.
If she violates the terms of her probation, she could be sent to jail.
During his probation, he must report regularly to his officer.

Synset Examples

probation is part of the sentencing process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parole - the conditional release of a prisoner before the end of a sentence, with supervision

Etymology

From Latin 'probatio' (proof, test), from 'probare' (to prove).
