Myriapoda

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɪriˈæpədə / (AmE), / ˌmɪrɪˈapədə / (BrE)
A large group of arthropods, including millipedes, characterized by bodies divided into many segments, each bearing two pairs of legs.
a group of segmented arthropods, e.g. millipedes

Examples

The Myriapoda include familiar creatures such as millipedes, which can be found in moist soil.
Scientists study the Myriapoda to better understand terrestrial arthropod evolution.
Although centipedes are not part of the Myriapoda, they are often confused with millipedes.

Etymology

From Greek 'myria' (countless) + 'pous, pod-' (foot).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɪriˈæpədə / (AmE), / ˌmɪrɪˈapədə / (BrE)
a class of arthropods comprising millipedes and similar creatures, characterized by bodies divided into many segments, each with two pairs of legs
a class of arthropods with many-legged segments

Examples

Scientists recently discovered a new species in the Myriapoda class in the Amazon rainforest.
Members of the Myriapoda are easily recognized by their segmented, many-legged bodies.
Unlike insects, organisms in the Myriapoda class have two pairs of legs per segment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: millipede - a type of myriapod with many body segments and legs

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek myrias (ten thousand) + pous, podos (foot).

3. NOUN

/ ˌmɪriˈæpədə / (AmE), / ˌmɪrɪˈapədə / (BrE)
A former taxonomic superclass of arthropods that includes the millipedes, centipedes, and sometimes pauropods and symphylans; now more commonly used to refer only to millipedes.
group of arthropods: millipedes, centipedes, etc.

Examples

The superclass Myriapoda includes creatures such as centipedes and millipedes in biological classification.
Some scientists once classified both Pauropoda and Symphyla within Myriapoda, though this usage has become less common.
Research on the evolutionary origins of Myriapoda has provided new insights into arthropod relationships.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Diplopoda - class of arthropods including all millipedes
  • Synonym: Chilopoda - class of arthropods including all centipedes

Etymology

From Greek myria- (ten thousand, countless) + pous (foot).
