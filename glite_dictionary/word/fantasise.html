to fantasise

1. VERB

/ ˈfæntəˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfæntəsaɪz / (BrE)
to imagine or think about something that is unlikely or impossible, often as a form of wishful thinking
to imagine unlikely or impossible things
british

Examples

Sometimes I fantasise about moving to a tropical island and starting over.
As a child, she would often fantasise about becoming a famous singer.
People may fantasise about winning the lottery, even if they rarely buy tickets.

Synset Examples

he is fantasizing when he says he plans to start his own company

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fantasize - US spelling of fantasise
  • Synonym: dream - to imagine or hope for something, especially something unlikely

Etymology

From Greek phantasia (appearance, imagination), via Medieval Latin and Old French.

2. VERB

/ ˈfæn.tə.saɪz / (AmE), / ˈfæn.tə.saɪz / (BrE)
to imagine something or someone in a way that is not grounded in reality, often as an ideal or wishful vision
to imagine unreal situations or people
british

Examples

As a child, she would often fantasise about living in a castle.
He tends to fantasise scenarios where everything turns out perfectly for him.
Writers sometimes fantasise entire worlds before putting pen to paper.

Synset Examples

he is fantasizing the ideal wife

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fantasize - to imagine unreal situations or people (American spelling)
  • Synonym: to imagine - to form a mental image or concept
  • Synonym: daydream - to indulge in pleasant, wishful thoughts

Etymology

From fantasy + -ise; patterned after 'fantasize' in American English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01640401-v
Synset POS: v
