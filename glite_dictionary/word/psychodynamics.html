psychodynamics

1. NOUN

/ ˌsaɪkoʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (AmE), / ˌsaɪkəʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (BrE)
The interrelation of conscious and unconscious mental or emotional processes, especially as they influence personality and motivation.
interaction of mental processes affecting personality
medical

Examples

Modern therapy often explores the psychodynamics underlying a patient's behavior.
Understanding the psychodynamics of a group can improve teamwork and communication.
Many theories of personality are rooted in the study of psychodynamics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: psychoanalysis - A set of therapeutic and theoretical approaches to understanding the unconscious mind
  • Antonym: behaviorism - A psychological approach focusing on observable behaviors

Etymology

From psycho- (mind) + dynamics (forces; from Greek dynamis, meaning 'power, force')

2. NOUN

/ ˌsaɪkoʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (AmE), / ˌsaɪkəʊdaɪˈnæmɪks / (BrE)
the branch of psychology concerned with the interplay of mental, emotional, and motivational forces, especially those operating unconsciously, that influence human behavior and personality
branch of psychology studying unconscious mental forces
medical

Examples

The therapist employed techniques based on psychodynamics to explore her patient's unconscious conflicts.
An understanding of psychodynamics can help explain certain patterns of behavior in relationships.
He specialized in psychodynamics, focusing on the emotional drivers behind human motivation.

Etymology

From psycho- (mind) + dynamics (the study of forces).
