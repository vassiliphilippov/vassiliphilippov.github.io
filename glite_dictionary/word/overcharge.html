overcharge

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈtʃɑrdʒ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtʃɑːdʒ / (BrE)
to place too heavy a load or burden on; to cause to bear more than is proper or safe
place too heavy a load or burden

Examples

If you overcharge the truck, it could damage the suspension system.
The elevator stopped working after being overcharged with too many people.
Engineers warn not to overcharge the crane, as it exceeds safety limits.

Synset Examples

don't overload the car

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overload - to load with too great a burden

Etymology

From over- (too much) + charge (to load or burden), first recorded in the late Middle English period.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01491184-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvɚˈtʃɑrdʒ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtʃɑːdʒ / (BrE)
to charge someone more than the correct or fair price for something
charge too much (verb)

Examples

Tourists often worry that local shops will overcharge them for souvenirs.
The mechanic overcharged me for a simple repair job.
Some companies have been accused of overcharging customers for basic services.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: undercharge - to charge too little for something

Etymology

From over- + charge; 'charge' from Old French 'charger' (to load).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02324030-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvərˈtʃɑrdʒ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtʃɑːdʒ / (BrE)
an amount of money that is charged in excess of a fair or correct price
excessive charge or fee

Examples

The hotel imposed an overcharge for the late checkout, which I refused to pay.
If you notice an overcharge on your bill, contact customer service immediately.
Many customers complained about the frequent overcharge on their electricity invoices.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: undercharge - an amount charged that is less than the correct price
  • Synonym: surcharge - an additional charge or payment

Etymology

From over- (above, excessive) + charge, meaning to ask for too high a price.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13328806-n
Synset POS: n
