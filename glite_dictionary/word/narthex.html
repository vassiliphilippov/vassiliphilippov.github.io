narthex

1. NOUN

/ ˈnɑrˌθɛks / (AmE), / ˈnɑːθɛks / (BrE)
an enclosed porch or vestibule at the western entrance of an early Christian church or basilica, often separated from the nave by a wall or screen
entrance porch of a church

Examples

The ancient basilica featured a spacious narthex leading into the main worship area.
Guests gathered in the narthex before entering the sanctuary for the wedding ceremony.
In early Christian architecture, the narthex served as a transitional space for catechumens.

Etymology

From Greek narthex (giant fennel, case for unguent, by extension, vestibule, portico).

2. NOUN

/ ˈnɑːr.θɛks / (AmE), / ˈnɑː.θɛks / (BrE)
an entrance hall or vestibule leading into the nave of a church, typically separated from the main area by a screen or doors
vestibule at the entrance of a church

Examples

The couple waited quietly in the narthex before the ceremony began.
Visitors gathered in the narthex, admiring the intricate mosaics on the walls.
Rain-soaked parishioners took shelter in the narthex before entering the church.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vestibule - an antechamber or hall next to the outer door of a building

Etymology

From Ancient Greek 'narthēx', meaning 'giant fennel', later used for 'casket' or 'box', referring to the entrance compartment of a church.
