deconstructivism

1. NOUN

/ diˌkɑnˈstrʌktɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / diˌkɒnˈstrʌktɪˌvɪzəm / (BrE)
A style or school of architecture characterized by fragmentation, non-linear processes of design, and an apparent deconstruction of traditional forms, influenced by the philosophy of deconstruction.
architectural style influenced by deconstruction

Examples

The museum is an example of deconstructivism, featuring striking, non-rectilinear shapes and fragmented geometry.
Many architects in the late 20th century were inspired by the principles of deconstructivism.
Critics argue that deconstructivism challenges the conventions of what traditional structures should look like.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deconstruction - A philosophical approach concerned with unravelling and questioning traditional assumptions, especially in literature or philosophy

Etymology

From 'deconstruction' + '-ism'; 'deconstruction' from French 'déconstruction', popularized by philosopher Jacques Derrida.
