mitre

1. NOUN

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
the beveled surface at the end of a piece of material, such as wood, where a miter joint is formed
beveled end surface for miter joint

Examples

Before attaching the pieces, he applied glue to the mitre for a secure bond.
Always check that the mitre is perfectly cut to ensure a tight joint.
Excess glue can squeeze out along the mitre when clamping the frame together.

Synset Examples

he covered the miter with glue before making the joint

Related Terms

  • Synonym: miter joint - a joint made by joining two pieces with beveled ends

Etymology

From Old French *mitre*, from Latin *mitra* (headband, turban), originally from Greek *mítrā*, later used for the angular joint shape resembling a bishop's mitre.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
a joint that forms a corner, usually with both adjoining surfaces cut at an angle (typically 45 degrees) to form a 90-degree corner
a carpentry joint forming a corner

Examples

The picture frame's corners were joined with a perfect mitre.
He used a saw to cut the wood at a 45-degree angle for the mitre joint.
A well-made mitre offers a seamless and attractive finish for mouldings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bevel - an edge cut at an angle other than a right angle

Etymology

From Old French *mitre*, from Latin *mitra* (cap, headband), likely via figurative sense of 'head'; technical sense from resemblance to a bishop's mitre.

3. NOUN

/ ˈmaɪtər / (AmE), / ˈmaɪtə / (BrE)
A tall, folding headdress, usually pointed at the front and back, worn by bishops and certain other clergy during ceremonies.
ceremonial headdress for bishops

Examples

The bishop donned his elaborate mitre for the Easter service.
During the procession, the golden mitre gleamed under the cathedral lights.
She crafted a model mitre for the church display.

Etymology

From Latin 'mitra', from Greek 'mítra' (headband, turban).
