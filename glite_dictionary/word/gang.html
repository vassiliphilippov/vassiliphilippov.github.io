gang

1. NOUN

/ ɡæŋ / (AmE), / ɡæŋ / (BrE)
a group of workers who work together, especially on manual or industrial tasks
group of workers

Examples

The road repair gang started work at dawn to avoid the traffic.
He led a gang of electricians during the factory renovation project.
A gang of builders was busy laying bricks at the new site.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crew - a group of people who work together, especially on a ship or aircraft

Etymology

From Old English *gang* (a going, journey, way), related to Old Norse *gangr*, German *Gang* (a going, walk).

2. NOUN

/ ɡæŋ / (AmE), / ɡæŋ / (BrE)
A tool consisting of a group of implements connected or arranged to work together for a particular function.
combination tool; group of working implements

Examples

The farmer used a gang of plows to cover more ground efficiently.
A gang of saws was employed to cut multiple boards at once.
The engineer repaired the gang of wrenches designed for synchronized operation.

Etymology

From Old English 'gang' (a going, a passage, company), related to Old Norse 'gangr'.

3. NOUN

/ ɡæŋ / (AmE), / ɡæŋ / (BrE)
an informal group of friends who regularly spend time together
informal group of friends

Examples

The whole gang met up at Lisa's house for pizza and games.
After moving to the new city, she quickly found a gang of friends to hang out with.

4. NOUN

/ ɡæŋ / (AmE), / ɡæŋ / (BrE)
A group of people who engage in criminal activities together.
group of criminals

Examples

The authorities are determined to dismantle the notorious gang responsible for recent robberies.
A rival gang moved into the neighborhood and tensions quickly escalated.
He was arrested for his involvement with a violent street gang.

Synset Examples

police tried to break up the gang
a pack of thieves

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mob - a large, disorderly group often engaged in criminal activity
  • Synonym: crew - a group working or acting together, often casually or for crime
  • Antonym: police - the authorities opposing criminal groups

Etymology

From Old English 'gang' (a going, journey, way), related to 'go'; meaning shifted in the 19th century to groups of criminals.

5. VERB

/ ɡæŋ / (AmE), / ɡæŋ / (BrE)
(of people or things) to move or act together as an organized group.
to act as an organized group

Examples

The children tended to gang together during recess for games.
Several small companies might gang up to compete against larger corporations.
The tools ganged together performed the task more efficiently.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: group - to form a group or act collectively

Etymology

From Old English ‘gang’ (“a going, a journey”), later generalized to group sense.
