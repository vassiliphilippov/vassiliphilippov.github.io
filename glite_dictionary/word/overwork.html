overwork

1. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvərˈwɜrk / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈwɜːk / (BrE)
excessive or prolonged work, especially to the point of physical or mental harm
excessive or prolonged work

Examples

After months of overwork, she finally decided to take a vacation.
Many health problems can result from chronic overwork and lack of rest.
The manager warned his team about the dangers of overwork before the busy season began.

Synset Examples

he became ill from overwork

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burnout - state of physical or mental collapse from overwork or stress
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00624610-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊ.vɚˈwɝːk / (AmE), / ˌəʊ.vəˈwɜːk / (BrE)
to cause someone or oneself to work too much or too hard
work too hard; work excessively

Examples

Employees who overwork are at greater risk of burnout and health problems.
She tends to overwork herself before important deadlines.
If you overwork the dough, the bread will turn out tough.

Synset Examples

he is exploiting the students

Related Terms

  • Antonym: underwork - to work less than is expected or required

Etymology

Formed from 'over-' (excessively) + 'work'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02414278-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌoʊ.vɚˈwɝːk / (AmE), / ˌəʊ.vəˈwɜːk / (BrE)
to use something too much or for too long, especially so that it loses its quality or value
use something excessively

Examples

Critics argue that the director overworked the original story, making it less impactful.
If you overwork the dough, the bread will become tough and dry.
The essay has been overworked with too many complicated ideas.

Synset Examples

This play has been overworked

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overuse - to use something too much
  • Antonym: underuse - to use something less than is necessary or desirable

Etymology

From over- (excessively) + work (to use or operate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01673069-v
Synset POS: v
