aback

1. ADV

/ əˈbæk / (AmE), / əˈbak / (BrE)
(of a ship's sails) with the wind pressing against the forward side, causing the ship to slow or stop; said of sails.
of sails, having wind against the front

Examples

The sudden squall caught us off guard, and soon the sails were aback.
If the helmsman turns too quickly, the jib will be aback, causing the vessel to stall.
With all yards aback, progress was halted until the crew could adjust the rigging.

Synset Examples

the ship came up into the wind with all yards aback

Etymology

From Old English 'on bæc' meaning 'at or on the back'.

2. ADV

/ əˈbæk / (AmE), / əˈbak / (BrE)
by surprise; unexpectedly, often used in the phrase 'taken aback' to describe being surprised or shocked
by surprise, unexpectedly

Examples

She was completely aback when he suddenly proposed to her.
The manager was taken aback by the team's unexpected resignation.
His blunt response caught everyone aback during the meeting.

Synset Examples

taken aback by the caustic remarks

Etymology

From Middle English abak (back, away), from Old English on bæc ('on back').
