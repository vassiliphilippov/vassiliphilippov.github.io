voluntary

1. ADJ

/ ˈvɑːlənˌteri / (AmE), / ˈvɒlənt(ə)ri / (BrE)
Done, given, or acting of one's own free will, without being forced or paid.
done by choice; not forced

Examples

Attendance at the seminar was completely voluntary, so participation was not required.
He made a voluntary donation to the charity after hearing about their work.
His confession was voluntary, as the police had not coerced him in any way.

Synset Examples

man is a voluntary agent
participation was voluntary
voluntary manslaughter
voluntary generosity in times of disaster
voluntary social workers
a voluntary confession

Related Terms

  • Antonym: involuntary - not done by choice; done without one's will
  • Synonym: optional - not compulsory; left to choice

Etymology

From Latin voluntarius (of one's free will), from voluntas (will, wish).

2. ADJ

/ ˈvɑːlənˌteri / (AmE), / ˈvɒlənˌtri / (BrE)
(of an action or movement) controlled by a person's conscious will; done by choice rather than automatically or involuntarily.
controlled by conscious will

Examples

The doctor explained that voluntary muscles are those we can control consciously.
Learning to play piano requires precise voluntary finger movements.
His recovery included regaining voluntary control over his leg muscles.

Synset Examples

voluntary motions
voluntary muscles

Related Terms

  • Antonym: involuntary - not under conscious control; automatic

Etymology

From Latin voluntarius, from voluntas (will, wish).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02532426-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ ˈvɑːlənˌtɛri / (AmE), / ˈvɒl.ən.tər.i / (BrE)
A musical composition, often improvised, for a solo instrument (especially the organ) played before, during, or after a religious service and not forming a regular part of the service.
musical piece for solo organ, often improvised

Examples

The organist performed an exquisite voluntary at the end of the service.
She composed a voluntary to showcase her skills on the church organ.
The concert opened with a Baroque voluntary that set a majestic tone.

Etymology

From Latin 'voluntarius' (of one's free will); used in music since the 17th century for free-form organ pieces.
