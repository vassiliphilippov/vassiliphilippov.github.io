Pygopus

1. NOUN

/ ˈpaɪɡəpəs / (AmE), / ˈpɪɡəpəs / (BrE)
A genus of snake-shaped, limbless or near-limbless lizards belonging to the family Pygopodidae, characterized by the absence of forelimbs and only rudimentary hind limbs.
genus of legless lizards

Examples

Scientists discovered a new species within the genus Pygopus in Australia.
The Pygopus lizard resembles a snake due to its elongated, limbless body.
Unlike true snakes, Pygopus retains rudimentary hind limbs.

Etymology

From Greek 'pygē' (rump) + 'pous' (foot), referring to the vestigial hind limbs.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpaɪɡəpəs / (AmE), / ˈpɪɡəpəs / (BrE)
A genus of snake-like lizards in the family Pygopodidae, characterized by the absence of forelimbs and only rudimentary hind limbs.
genus of snake-like legless lizards

Examples

The Pygopus genus includes several species of legless lizards native to Australia.
Biologists studied the unique locomotion of Pygopus to understand its adaptation to a limbless lifestyle.
Unlike snakes, members of the genus Pygopus have external ear openings and remnants of hind limbs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Pygopodidae - the family to which Pygopus belongs

Etymology

From Greek 'pygē' (rump) and 'pous' (foot), referring to the reduced limbs.
