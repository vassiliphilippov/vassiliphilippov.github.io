cluster

1. VERB

/ ˈklʌstɚ / (AmE), / ˈklʌstə / (BrE)
to come together or gather in a close group or bunch
gather closely together

Examples

After the concert, fans clustered around the stage, hoping for an autograph.
Bees cluster around the hive entrance on warm afternoons.
During the emergency, people clustered near the exits, waiting for instructions.

Synset Examples

The poets constellate in this town every summer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gather - to come together or assemble in a group
  • Antonym: to disperse - to scatter in different directions

Etymology

From Old English 'clyster' (cluster, bunch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02028855-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈklʌstɚ / (AmE), / ˈklʌstə / (BrE)
a group of similar things or people positioned or occurring closely together
group of similar things close together

Examples

The grapes grew in a tight cluster on the vine.
There was a small cluster of houses at the end of the road.
After the concert, a cluster of fans waited by the stage door.

Synset Examples

a bunch of trees
a cluster of admirers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: group - a number of people or things that are together or in the same place
  • Synonym: collection - a group of things that have been collected; an accumulation
  • Antonym: scattering - a small, spread-out number or amount

Etymology

From Old English *clyster* (cluster, bunch), related to Middle Dutch *closter* (cluster, bundle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07976557-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈklʌstɚ / (AmE), / ˈklʌstə / (BrE)
to come together in a group or to form a cluster, or to cause things or people to do this
to come together in a group

Examples

Wildflowers cluster along the edge of the forest each spring.
Tourists often cluster around famous landmarks for photos.
The children clustered together to look at the strange insect.

Synset Examples

She bunched her fingers into a fist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gather - to come together in one place; to collect things or people
  • Antonym: scatter - to disperse or move apart in different directions

Etymology

From Middle English 'clustren', from Old English 'clyster' (cluster, heap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01487029-v
Synset POS: v
