bowdlerization

1. NOUN

/ ˌbaʊdlɚəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌbaʊdlərəˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of removing or altering parts of a text considered offensive or inappropriate, especially by making changes that diminish its quality or meaning
removal of offensive material from texts

Examples

Many critics condemned the bowdlerization of classic literature for removing important themes.
The bowdlerization of the story made it suitable for children, but lost much of the original impact.
Historians often debate whether bowdlerization helps or harms our understanding of past societies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: censorship - the suppression or prohibition of speech or writing considered obscene or politically unacceptable

Etymology

Named after Thomas Bowdler (1754–1825), who published an expurgated edition of Shakespeare.

2. NOUN

/ ˌboʊdlərəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌbəʊdləraɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or result of removing or altering parts of a text considered offensive or inappropriate, often resulting in a less effective or less authentic version
removal of offensive parts from a text

Examples

The bowdlerization of the novel sparked criticism from literary scholars.
Fans were disappointed by the bowdlerization of the classic film for television broadcast.
Many argue that bowdlerization can distort the original intent of the author.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: censorship - the suppression or prohibition of parts of books, films, etc.
  • Synonym: expurgation - the action of removing objectionable parts from a text

Etymology

From the name of Thomas Bowdler (1754–1825), who published expurgated editions of Shakespeare.
