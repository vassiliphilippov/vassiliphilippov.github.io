to distract

1. VERB

/ dɪˈstrækt / (AmE), / dɪˈstrakt / (BrE)
to make someone feel anxious, uneasy, or disturbed in mind; to cause worry or alarm
to disturb or worry someone

Examples

The troubling headlines distract him from focusing on his work.
Bad news late at night can easily distract you from sleep.
Her friend's illness distracted her and made it hard to concentrate.

Synset Examples

She was rather perturbed by the news that her father was seriously ill

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to perturb - to disturb or make anxious or uneasy
  • Antonym: calm - free from agitation or disturbance

Etymology

From Latin distractus, past participle of distrahere (to pull apart, perplex).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈstrækt / (AmE), / dɪˈstrækt / (BrE)
to prevent someone from giving full attention to something by drawing their attention away
draw someone's attention away

Examples

The loud noise outside distracted me from my work.
She tried not to let her phone distract her during the meeting.
He distracted the guards so his friend could sneak past.

Synset Examples

The thief distracted the bystanders
He deflected his competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to divert - to cause someone or something to change course or direction; to distract
  • Antonym: focus - to concentrate attention or effort; the opposite of distract

Etymology

From Latin distractus, past participle of distrahere (to pull apart, distract).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01795785-v
Synset POS: v
