appellative

1. ADJ

/ əˈpɛlətɪv / (AmE), / əˈpɛlətɪv / (BrE)
(Linguistics) Relating to or functioning as a common noun, as opposed to a proper noun.
used as or relating to a common noun

Examples

In English, the word 'city' is an appellative whereas 'London' is a proper noun.
An appellative noun refers to any member of a class rather than a unique entity.
Some words can function as either appellative or proper nouns depending on context.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: proper - denoting a proper noun

Etymology

From Latin appellativus (relating to naming), from appellare (to call, name).

2. ADJ

/ əˈpɛlətɪv / (AmE), / əˈpɛlətɪv / (BrE)
relating to the act of naming or giving names; serving to name or designate.
relating to naming or designation

Examples

The appellative function of language is especially evident in how we name new inventions.
In early childhood, the appellative faculty develops as children learn to assign words to objects.
Some rituals have an appellative purpose, symbolically bestowing names or titles during the ceremony.

Synset Examples

the appellative faculty of children
the appellative function of some primitive rites

Etymology

From Latin appellativus (pertaining to a name), from appellare (to call, name).

3. NOUN

/ əˈpɛlətɪv / (AmE), / əˈpɛlətɪv / (BrE)
a name or title used to identify and distinguish a person or thing
name or title identifying someone or something

Examples

His formal appellative is Dr. Smith, but most students simply call him Professor.
The city acquired its appellative from an ancient legend.
A child's appellative often carries personal or familial significance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: name - the word or set of words by which a person or thing is known
  • Synonym: title - a word or phrase that describes a person's position or job

Etymology

From Latin appellativus (pertaining to naming), from appellare (to call, address).
