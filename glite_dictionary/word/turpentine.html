turpentine

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɝː.pənˌtaɪn / (AmE), / ˈtɜː.pənˌtaɪn / (BrE)
an oleoresin obtained from coniferous trees, especially pines, used in making varnishes, solvents, and as a paint thinner.
oleoresin from pines, used as solvent

Examples

The artists used turpentine to clean their brushes after painting with oils.
Workers collected turpentine from the pine trees in the forest.
Some varnishes are made with natural turpentine as a key ingredient.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rosin - a solid form of resin obtained from pines, related to turpentine production

Etymology

From Old French *terebentine*, from Latin *terebinthina* (resin from the terebinth tree).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɜːrpənˌtaɪn / (AmE), / ˈtɜːpənˌtaɪn / (BrE)
A volatile liquid distilled from the resin of pine trees, used as a paint thinner, a solvent, and sometimes in medicine.
volatile pine resin solvent

Examples

He used turpentine to clean his paintbrushes after finishing the portrait.
The strong smell of turpentine filled the workshop as the artist worked.
In earlier centuries, turpentine was sometimes prescribed in herbal medicine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solvent - a liquid that dissolves other substances
  • Synonym: paint thinner - a substance for thinning oil-based paint

Etymology

From Middle English, via Old French 'terebentine', from Latin 'terebinthina', from Greek 'terebinthos' (turpentine tree).
