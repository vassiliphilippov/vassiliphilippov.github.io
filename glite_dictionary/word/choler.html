choler

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːlər / (AmE), / ˈkɒlə / (BrE)
a state or feeling of anger or irritability; wrath
anger or irritability
archaic

Examples

He struggled to control his choler when confronted with the unfair decision.
A fit of choler overtook her, causing her to lash out unexpectedly.
His choler was evident in his sharp words and flushed face.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anger - a strong feeling of displeasure or hostility
  • Antonym: calm - a state of peacefulness or tranquility, not angry

Etymology

From Latin 'cholera' (bile), from Greek 'kholē' (bile), once believed to cause anger in humoral theory.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːlər / (AmE), / ˈkɒlə / (BrE)
anger or irritable feeling, especially as expressed in response to a grievance; historically, one of the four bodily humors believed to cause such moods.
anger; irritable feeling

Examples

He could barely control his choler when confronted with the accusation.
Her choler rose quickly during the heated argument.
Literature from that era often describes heroes with uncontrollable choler.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anger - a strong feeling of displeasure or hostility
  • Antonym: calm - a state of being free from anger or strong emotion

Etymology

From Old French *colere* and Latin *cholera* (bile), related to the ancient belief in bodily humors.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːlər / (AmE), / ˈkɒlə / (BrE)
(historical) In pre-modern medicine, one of the four bodily humors, believed to be secreted by the liver and to cause irritability and anger.
an old medical humor linked to anger
archaic

Examples

The excess of choler was once thought to make a person quick-tempered and irritable.
Physicians of the Middle Ages believed that balancing one's choler was essential for good health.
His rash behavior was often attributed to an abundance of choler in his system.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anger - strong feeling of annoyance, displeasure, or hostility
  • Degree Lower: phlegm - another of the four bodily humors, believed to cause sluggishness

Etymology

From Old French *colere*, from Latin *cholera* (bilious disease, bile), from Greek *kholē* (bile).
