tarmac

1. VERB

/ ˈtɑːr.mæk / (AmE), / ˈtɑː.mæk / (BrE)
To cover or surface (a road, path, etc.) with tarmacadam.
surface with tarmacadam

Examples

They plan to tarmac the road leading to the new housing estate this summer.
The company tarmacked the driveway to give it a smooth, durable finish.
Workers are tarmacking the parking lot to improve access for customers.

Synset Examples

macadam the road

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pave - to cover a surface, especially a road or path, with flat stones or bricks

Etymology

Shortened from tarmacadam, after John Loudon McAdam, a Scottish engineer.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːrˌmæk / (AmE), / ˈtɑːmæk / (BrE)
a road or other surface made of compressed layers of broken stone held together with tar, or the material used for this purpose
paved surface with tar and crushed stone

Examples

The plane waited on the tarmac for an hour before taking off.
Workers repaired the cracks in the tarmac after the storm.
Children rode their bikes across the smooth tarmac in the school playground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a similar dark bituminous substance used for paving

Etymology

Shortened from trade name 'tarmacadam', invented in the early 20th century: from tar + macadam (after John Macadam).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04401723-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːrˌmæk / (AmE), / ˈtɑːmæk / (BrE)
A material used for paving roads and runways, made by mixing tar with crushed stone.
paving material made from tar and stone

Examples

The long driveway was resurfaced with tarmac to make it smoother.
Planes lined up on the tarmac waiting for takeoff.
Fresh tarmac was laid on the high street overnight, causing a pleasant, new smell in the morning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a similar black tarry substance used for paving

Etymology

From trademark Tarmac, originally from 'tar' + 'macadam' (a type of road surfacing).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-15001747-n
Synset POS: n
