bland

1. ADJ

/ blænd / (AmE), / blænd / (BrE)
lacking strong flavor or taste; uninteresting or insipid in flavor.
lacking flavor or taste

Examples

The soup was disappointingly bland, so I added more salt and spices.
He followed a bland diet after his surgery, avoiding anything too spicy or rich.
Many people find hospital food to be rather bland and unappetizing.

Synset Examples

a bland diet
insipid hospital food
flavorless supermarket tomatoes
vapid beer
vapid tea

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insipid - lacking flavor; dull
  • Synonym: flavorless - without taste or flavor
  • Synonym: tasteless - having little or no flavor

Etymology

From Latin blandus (charming, mild, smooth).

2. ADJ

/ blænd / (AmE), / blænd / (BrE)
Lacking strong features or characteristics and therefore uninteresting.
uninteresting; lacking distinctive features

Examples

The critic described the film as bland and forgettable.
Her speech was polite but rather bland, lacking any real passion.
Many people find the decor in chain hotels to be quite bland.

Synset Examples

a bland little drama
a flat joke

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dull - lacking interest or excitement
  • Synonym: boring - not interesting; tedious
  • Antonym: exciting - causing great enthusiasm and eagerness

Etymology

From Latin blandus (charming, mild).

3. ADJ

/ blænd / (AmE), / blænd / (BrE)
smoothly agreeable, polite, and courteous, often with a degree of sophistication or lack of strong emotion
polite and pleasantly agreeable

Examples

Her bland response calmed the situation and reassured everyone in the meeting.
He offered a bland compliment, careful not to offend anyone present.
The diplomat was admired for his bland and sophisticated manner during tense negotiations.

Synset Examples

he was too politic to quarrel with so important a personage
the manager pacified the customer with a smooth apology for the error

Related Terms

  • Synonym: smooth - having a suave or agreeable manner
  • Degree Lower: insipid - lacking distinctiveness or strong character

Etymology

From Latin 'blandus' (smooth, pleasant).
