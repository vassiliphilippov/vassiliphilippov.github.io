to undergird

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈɡɜrd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈɡɜːd / (BrE)
to support or strengthen from beneath; to make secure underneath
to support or reinforce from below

Examples

Large steel beams undergird the foundation of the new building.
Honesty and trust undergird every successful relationship.
Crews worked tirelessly to undergird the sinking vessel with strong cables.

Synset Examples

undergird the ship

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to bear the weight of; to hold up

Etymology

From Old English 'gehyrdan' (to gird, encircle), influenced by 'under-' (beneath).

WordNet ID: oewn-00222144-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndɚˈɡɝd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈɡɜːd / (BrE)
To provide moral, emotional, or structural support to something or someone.
to give support; reinforce or uphold

Examples

Strong ethical values undergird the mission of the organization.
The Constitution has long undergirded democratic institutions in the country.
Her unshakable faith undergirded her determination to keep going during hard times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance or backing to
  • Antonym: to undermine - to weaken or lessen the effectiveness of

Etymology

From Old English 'under' (below) and 'gyrdan' (to gird), meaning to secure or strengthen from beneath.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02562555-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈɡɝd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈɡɜːd / (BrE)
to provide support, strength, or a firm basis for something, especially an idea or plan
to support or strengthen

Examples

Strong ethical principles undergird our company's mission and decisions.
These new reforms are designed to undergird economic stability for future generations.
The research findings undergird the proposal for long-term policy changes.

Synset Examples

The new policies will undergird our long-term strategy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to hold up or strengthen something
  • Antonym: to undermine - to weaken or damage something, especially gradually

Etymology

From Old English 'geard' (yard, enclosure), influenced by gird, meaning to encircle or secure.
