to sugarcoat

1. VERB

/ ˈʃʊɡɚˌkoʊt / (AmE), / ˈʃʊɡəkəʊt / (BrE)
to cover or coat something with a sweet, sugary substance, such as a hard sugar glaze
cover with a sweet sugar coating

Examples

She decided to sugarcoat the cookies to make them more appealing to the children.
The chef will sugarcoat the pastries with a glossy glaze for the party.
After baking, he sugarcoated the almonds for a delicious snack.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glaze - to cover food with a smooth, shiny coating

Etymology

Formed from 'sugar' + 'coat', meaning to cover with sugar.

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈʃuɡɚˌkoʊt / (AmE), / ˈʃʊɡəkəʊt / (BrE)
to make something seem more pleasant or acceptable than it really is, especially by omitting unpleasant details
make something seem more pleasant than it is

Examples

There is no need to sugarcoat the feedback; honesty is important.
She tried to sugarcoat the bad news so it wouldn't upset her friend.
The report does not sugarcoat the seriousness of the environmental damage.

Synset Examples

The mayor did not sugarcoat the reality of the tax cuts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sweeten - make something more agreeable; to improve the appeal of something
  • Antonym: to downplay - to make something appear less important or serious than it really is

Etymology

From 'sugar' + 'coat', originally meaning to coat with sugar; later used metaphorically.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01035814-v
Synset POS: v
