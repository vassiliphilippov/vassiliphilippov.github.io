boundary

1. NOUN

/ ˈbaʊndəri / (AmE), / ˈbaʊndəri / (BrE)
a line that marks the limits or edge of an area; a dividing line
line marking the edge of an area

Examples

The river forms the natural boundary between the two countries.
They put up a fence to mark the boundary of their property.
Crossing the boundary into the restricted area is prohibited.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: border - the outer edge or boundary of something
  • Synonym: limit - the point or edge beyond which something does not or may not extend or pass
  • Antonym: center - the middle point of something, opposite of the edge or boundary

Etymology

From Middle English 'boundarie', from 'bound' + '-ary'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbaʊndɛri / (AmE), / ˈbaʊndəri / (BrE)
the greatest or furthest limit or degree that something can reach
greatest or furthest limit of something

Examples

She refused to push herself beyond the boundary of her endurance.
There is a clear boundary between professional and personal life for him.
The discussion tested the boundary of what was considered acceptable.

Synset Examples

what he did was beyond the bounds of acceptable behavior
to the limit of his ability

Related Terms

  • Synonym: limit - the point or level beyond which something does not or may not extend or pass
  • Synonym: extent - the degree to which something has spread; scope or range
  • Synonym: end - the final part or limit of something

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *boundaria*, from *bounda* (limit, boundary).

3. NOUN

/ ˈbaʊndɛri / (AmE), / ˈbaʊndəri / (BrE)
a line that marks the limit or edge of an area; a dividing line between places or things
a dividing line or limit

Examples

The river forms the boundary between the two countries.
Please respect the boundary of my personal space.
The fence marks the boundary of the property.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: border - the boundary line or the area immediately inside the boundary
  • Synonym: limit - the furthest point, degree, or amount allowed
  • Antonym: center - the middle point of something

Etymology

From English 'bound' (limit) + '-ary', patterned after 'boundary' in the 17th century.
