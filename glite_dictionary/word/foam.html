foam

1. NOUN

/ foʊm / (AmE), / fəʊm / (BrE)
a lightweight material consisting of a mass of small gas bubbles trapped in a solid or liquid, often used for cushioning, insulation, or packaging
lightweight material with gas bubbles, used for cushioning or insulation

Examples

The mattress is made of memory foam for extra comfort.
We used foam insulation to keep the house warm in winter.
He packed the glassware carefully in foam to prevent it from breaking during shipping.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sponge - a soft, porous material similar to foam, often used for cleaning

2. VERB

/ foʊm / (AmE), / fəʊm / (BrE)
to produce or become covered with a mass of small bubbles; to become frothy or bubbly
produce or become covered with bubbles

Examples

The waves began to foam as they crashed against the rocks.
The chemicals reacted, causing the liquid to foam rapidly.
He watched the milk foam as he steamed it for his coffee.

Synset Examples

The boiling soup was frothing
The river was foaming
sparkling water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: froth - to form or produce a mass of small bubbles; to become foamy
  • Synonym: bubble - to form or emit bubbles

Etymology

From Old English 'fām' (foam, froth), related to Old Norse 'fǫm'.

3. NOUN

/ foʊm / (AmE), / fəʊm / (BrE)
A mass of small bubbles formed in or on the surface of a liquid, often as a result of agitation or the presence of certain substances.
mass of small bubbles on liquid

Examples

The barista carefully spooned the foam onto the top of the cappuccino.
Waves crashing against the rocks produced a thick foam along the shoreline.
He popped the soap bubbles in the foam while taking a bath.

Synset Examples

the beer had a thick head of foam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: froth - a mass of small bubbles formed on a liquid
  • Synonym: scum - a layer of froth or bubbles on liquid, often with impurities

Etymology

From Old English *fām* (foam, froth), of Germanic origin.
