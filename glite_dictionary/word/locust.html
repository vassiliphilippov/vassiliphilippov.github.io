locust

1. NOUN

/ ˈloʊ.kəst / (AmE), / ˈləʊ.kəst / (BrE)
A large, mainly tropical grasshopper with short antennae that migrates in vast swarms and can cause extensive damage to crops.
migratory grasshopper that forms swarms

Examples

A swarm of locusts devoured the farmers' entire wheat crop in just a few hours.
During periods of drought, locust plagues can become a serious threat to agriculture.
The villagers tried to protect their fields from the oncoming locust swarm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grasshopper - plant-eating insect with long hind legs for jumping

Etymology

From Latin 'locusta' (locust, lobster).

2. NOUN

/ ˈloʊ.kəst / (AmE), / ˈləʊ.kəst / (BrE)
The hard, durable wood obtained from any of various locust trees, especially used in construction and carpentry.
hardwood from the locust tree

Examples

The fence posts were made from sturdy locust to resist rot and insects.
Carpenters prize locust for its strength and durability in outdoor furniture.
He split the locust logs for firewood, noting how dense the wood was.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: locust tree - a tree species such as black locust or honey locust

3. NOUN

/ ˈloʊ.kəst / (AmE), / ˈləʊ.kəst / (BrE)
a type of hardwood tree in the family Leguminosae, especially the black locust and honey locust, known for durable timber and compound leaves
type of hardwood tree; black or honey locust

Examples

The wood from the locust tree is prized for its exceptional strength and resistance to rot.
A row of locust trees lines the main street, providing shade in summer.
Farmers sometimes plant locust for windbreaks and erosion control due to its hardy nature.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: black locust - a North American locust tree with fragrant white flowers
  • Synonym: honey locust - a North American locust tree with long seed pods and sweet pulp

Etymology

From Latin *locusta* ('locust, grasshopper'), later applied to these trees due to the resemblance of their pods to insect bodies.
