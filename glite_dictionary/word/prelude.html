prelude

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɛl.jud / (AmE), / ˈprɛl.juːd / (BrE)
a short piece of music, often serving as an introduction to another, such as a fugue or an act in an opera
introductory musical piece

Examples

The orchestra performed a stirring prelude before the main opera began.
Chopin’s prelude in E minor is one of his most famous piano pieces.
Each act of the play was introduced by a brief musical prelude.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overture - a musical introduction to an opera or longer musical work

Etymology

From Latin 'praeludium' (something played before), from 'prae-' (before) + 'ludere' (to play).

2. VERB

/ ˈprɛljuːd, prəˈluːd / (AmE), / ˈprɛljuːd, ˈprɛljuːd / (BrE)
to serve as a prelude or introduction to something, often marking the beginning of an event or action.
serve as an introduction or opening to

Examples

A sudden storm preluded the evening's festivities, setting a dramatic tone.
The short poem preludes the main story, giving readers a taste of what's to come.
Soft music preluded the speaker's entrance, creating a calm atmosphere.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to introduce - to present or bring into use; to begin
  • Antonym: to conclude - to bring something to an end, to finish

Etymology

From Latin 'praeludium' (prelude, preface), from 'prae-' (before) + 'ludere' (to play).

3. NOUN

/ ˈprɛl.jud / (AmE), / ˈprɛl.juːd / (BrE)
an action or event that happens before and leads to or introduces a more important event or situation
something that comes before another event

Examples

The months of negotiations were merely a prelude to the bigger announcement that followed.
The thunderstorm seemed to be a prelude to the heavy rain that drenched the city.
His speech was intended as a prelude to the evening's festivities.

As the pianist preluded, the guests settled into their seats, awaiting the concert.

  • Antonym: to conclude - to bring to an end; to finish
