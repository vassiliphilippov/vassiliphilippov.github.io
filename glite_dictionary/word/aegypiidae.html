Aegypiidae

1. NOUN

/ iːˈdʒɪpiˌɪdiː / (AmE), / iːˌdʒɪpɪˈaɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of large Old World vultures in some classifications, usually regarded as part of Accipitridae.
family of Old World vultures

Examples

The Aegypiidae are distinguished from New World vultures by their lack of a keen sense of smell.
Some ornithologists still classify Old World vultures under the family Aegypiidae.
Unlike their American relatives, members of the Aegypiidae have strong feet adapted for walking.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Accipitridae - the family of birds including hawks, eagles, and most vultures

Etymology

From scientific Latin, based on Greek aigyps (‘vulture’) + -idae (family suffix in taxonomy).

2. NOUN

/ iːˌdʒɪˈpɪɪdiː / (AmE), / iːˌdʒɪˈpɪɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of large birds of prey known as the Old World vultures, native to Africa, Europe, and Asia, sometimes classified as a separate family or included in Accipitridae.
Old World vulture family (birds of prey)

Examples

The Aegypiidae are distinguished by their featherless heads and large size.
Many species in the Aegypiidae are crucial for cleaning up carrion in their habitats.
Recent research debates whether to classify Old World vultures under Aegypiidae or Accipitridae.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Accipitridae - the family of birds of prey including hawks, eagles, and sometimes vultures

Etymology

From New Latin Aegypiidae, based on Aegypius, a type genus of vulture, from Greek aigyps (vulture).
