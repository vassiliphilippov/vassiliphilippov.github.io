adenosine diphosphate

1. NOUN

/ əˈdɛnəˌsin daɪˈfɑsfət / (AmE), / əˈdiːnəˌsiːn daɪˈfɒsfət / (BrE)
a compound consisting of adenosine and two phosphate groups, formed as an intermediate in the conversion of ATP to store and release energy in cells
a molecule involved in cellular energy transfer
medical

Examples

During cellular respiration, adenosine diphosphate is converted into ATP by the addition of a phosphate group.
The breakdown of ATP to adenosine diphosphate releases energy needed for muscle contraction.
Biologists often refer to adenosine diphosphate simply as ADP in textbooks and research papers.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: adenosine triphosphate - a molecule with three phosphate groups, used as an energy carrier in cells
  • Degree Lower: adenosine monophosphate - a molecule with one phosphate group, involved in cellular processes

Etymology

From adenosine (a nucleoside) + diphosphate (two phosphate groups).
