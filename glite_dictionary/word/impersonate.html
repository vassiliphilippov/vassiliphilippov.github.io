to impersonate

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɜrsəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɜːsəneɪt / (BrE)
to pretend to be another person, especially to deceive others
pretend to be another person

Examples

It is illegal to impersonate a police officer.
He earned a living by impersonating famous celebrities on stage.
She tried to impersonate her professor in a prank call.

Synset Examples

She posed as the Czar's daughter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imitate - to copy someone’s actions or speech
  • Synonym: masquerade - to pretend to be someone you are not

Etymology

From Latin 'persona' (person, character), via 'impersonare' (to invest with a personality).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɜːrsəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɜːsəneɪt / (BrE)
to pretend to be or act the part of another person, often to entertain or deceive
pretend to be another person

Examples

The comedian expertly impersonated several famous politicians during the show.
He tried to impersonate a police officer, but was quickly discovered.
In the play, she impersonates a historical queen with remarkable accuracy.

Synset Examples

She impersonates Madonna
The actor portrays an elderly, lonely man

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imitate - to copy someone's actions, speech, or mannerisms
  • Synonym: mimic - imitate someone, often in a mocking or playful way
  • Synonym: impersonator - a person who impersonates others

Etymology

From Latin 'personare', meaning 'to represent a person'.

3. VERB

/ ɪmˈpɜːrsəˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɜːsəneɪt / (BrE)
to pretend to be another person, especially by copying their appearance, voice, or behaviour, often for entertainment or comic effect
pretend to be another person, often comically

Examples

The comedian loves to impersonate famous politicians on stage.
During the talent show, Sarah decided to impersonate her favourite pop star.
He was able to impersonate his boss so well that everyone burst out laughing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mimic - to imitate someone's voice or actions, often for entertainment
  • Synonym: to imitate - to copy the speech or behaviour of someone

Etymology

From Latin *personatus* (impersonated), from *persona* (character, person).
