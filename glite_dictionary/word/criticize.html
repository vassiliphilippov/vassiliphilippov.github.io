to criticize

1. VERB

/ ˈkrɪtɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkrɪtɪsʌɪz / (BrE)
to express disapproval of someone or something by pointing out faults or shortcomings
express disapproval; point out faults

Examples

She tends to criticize her colleagues for minor mistakes.
The teacher did not hesitate to criticize the student's essay.
It's easy to criticize others, but harder to admit your own faults.

Synset Examples

Those who criticize others often are not perfect, either

Related Terms

  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From Greek 'kritikos' (able to discern), via French 'critiquer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01098508-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkrɪtɪˌsaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkrɪtɪsʌɪz / (BrE)
to indicate faults or shortcomings in something or someone; to point out or express disapproval of perceived problems
to express disapproval or point out faults

Examples

She never hesitates to criticize her colleagues when she thinks they are wrong.
The professor publicly criticized the report for its lack of thorough research.
If you constantly criticize a child, it can damage their confidence.

Synset Examples

The paper criticized the new movie
Don't knock the food — it's free

Related Terms

  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something
  • Synonym: censure - to express severe disapproval of someone or something, typically in a formal statement

Etymology

From Greek kritikós (able to discern), via French and Latin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00828170-v
Synset POS: v
