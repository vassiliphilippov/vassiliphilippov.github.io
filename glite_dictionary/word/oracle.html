oracle

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔr.ə.kəl / (AmE), / ˈɒr.ə.kəl / (BrE)
a person with great knowledge or authority who is believed to be able to predict the future or give wise advice, especially in ancient times
person who claims to foretell the future

Examples

In ancient Greece, people traveled great distances to consult the oracle at Delphi.
She was seen by her followers as a modern oracle because of her uncanny predictions.
The king relied on the oracle for guidance before making important decisions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prophet - a person regarded as an inspired teacher or proclaimer of the future
  • Synonym: seer - someone who claims to see into the future
  • Synonym: soothsayer - a person who predicts the future by magical or intuitive means

Etymology

From Latin 'oraculum' (divine announcement), from 'orare' (to speak, pray).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɔːrəkl̩ / (AmE), / ˈɒrəkəl / (BrE)
A shrine where people consulted a god for advice or prophecy in ancient times.
ancient shrine for divine consultation

Examples

The ancient Greeks traveled long distances to consult the oracle at Delphi.
Priests interpreted the signs at the oracle, believing them to be messages from the gods.
Fantastic tales grew around the mysterious oracle located deep within the forest temple.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: oracle - a person through whom a deity speaks; a prophet

Etymology

From Latin 'oraculum', from 'orare' (to speak, pray), originally meaning 'that which is spoken' (by a god).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɔr.ə.kəl / (AmE), / ˈɒr.ə.kəl / (BrE)
a prophecy or message, often obscure or allegorical, revealed by a priest or priestess and believed to be infallible
prophecy revealed by priest or priestess

Examples

The priestess delivered the oracle in enigmatic verses that puzzled everyone.
Many ancient leaders consulted the oracle before making important decisions.
The villagers interpreted the oracle as a sign of prosperity to come.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prophecy - a prediction of what will happen in the future
  • Synonym: divination - the practice of seeking knowledge of the future by supernatural means

Etymology

From Latin 'oraculum' (divine announcement), from 'orare' (to speak, pray).
