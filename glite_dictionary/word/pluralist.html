pluralist

1. NOUN

/ ˈplʊr.əl.ɪst / (AmE), / ˈplʊə.rə.lɪst / (BrE)
a cleric who holds more than one benefice at the same time
cleric holding multiple benefices

Examples

In the 17th century, many high-ranking clergy were known as pluralists, holding several parishes at once.
The bishop condemned the practice of pluralist clergy neglecting their pastoral duties.
He gained a reputation as a pluralist, managing multiple church incomes simultaneously.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: benefice - an ecclesiastical office to which the revenue from an endowment is attached

Etymology

From 'plural' + '-ist'; 'plural' from Old French, from Latin 'pluralis'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈplʊrəlɪst / (AmE), / ˈplʊərəlɪst / (BrE)
a person, especially a philosopher, who believes that reality consists of multiple fundamental substances or principles and that no single explanation can account for all phenomena
philosopher believing in multiple explanations of reality

Examples

As a pluralist, she argued that no single theory could fully explain the complexity of the universe.
Some philosophers are pluralists, emphasizing the existence of many different kinds of reality.
The debate between pluralists and monists shaped much of nineteenth-century philosophy.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monist - a philosopher who believes that all things can be explained by a single principle

Etymology

From Latin pluralis (of or belonging to more than one), via French pluraliste.

3. NOUN

/ ˈplʊrəlɪst / (AmE), / ˈplʊərəlɪst / (BrE)
a person who believes that different ethnic, cultural, or religious groups can coexist within a single society
person who supports coexistence of groups

Examples

As a committed pluralist, she advocated for religious tolerance in her community.
The political party appeals to pluralist values by promoting diversity and inclusion.
A true pluralist recognizes the importance of dialogue between different cultural groups.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: multiculturalist - a person who supports the presence of multiple cultures in a society

Etymology

From Latin 'pluralis' (more than one) + English '-ist.'
