hindrance

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɪndr(ə)ns / (AmE), / ˈhɪndrəns / (BrE)
the act of making it more difficult for someone to do something or for something to happen; an obstacle or impediment
the act of hindering; obstacle

Examples

Traffic was a major hindrance to getting to the airport on time.
The lack of funds proved to be a serious hindrance to the project's success.
Her injury became a hindrance during the competition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obstacle - something that blocks or impedes progress
  • Synonym: impediment - something that hinders or obstructs progress
  • Antonym: assistance - the action of helping or supporting

Etymology

From Middle English hinderaunce, from hinder (to impede) + -ance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɪndr(ə)ns / (AmE), / ˈhɪndr(ə)ns / (BrE)
A thing that makes progress or movement more difficult; an obstacle or impediment.
something that impedes or obstructs

Examples

Heavy traffic is often a serious hindrance to getting to work on time.
His lack of experience proved to be a hindrance during the interview process.
Without her support, every task felt like a greater hindrance than before.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obstacle - a thing that blocks one's way or prevents progress
  • Synonym: impediment - something that hinders or obstructs progress
  • Antonym: help - something that makes progress easier

Etymology

From Middle English 'hinderance', from 'hinder' + '-ance'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈhɪndrəns / (AmE), / ˈhɪndrəns / (BrE)
a thing that delays or obstructs someone or something; an impediment.
something that delays or obstructs

Examples

Procrastination can be a serious hindrance to achieving your goals.
The lack of resources proved to be a major hindrance to the project's success.
His injured knee was a hindrance during the race.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impediment - something that obstructs or hinders progress
  • Synonym: obstacle - a thing that blocks one's way or prevents progress
  • Antonym: help - something that makes it easier to do a job or achieve a goal

Etymology

From Middle English hinderaunce, from Old English hindrian (‘to injure, to hinder’).
