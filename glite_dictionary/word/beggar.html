beggar

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɛɡər / (AmE), / ˈbɛɡə / (BrE)
a person, typically poor, who lives by asking others for money or food.
a person who begs for money or food

Examples

A beggar sat on the steps, asking passersby for spare change.
She offered her lunch to the beggar standing outside the bakery.
The city has programs to support homeless beggars during the colder months.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: panhandler - someone who begs for money in public places
  • Antonym: donor - a person who gives money or goods, opposite of beggar

Etymology

From Middle English 'beggere', from 'beggen' (to beg).

2. VERB

/ ˈbɛɡər / (AmE), / ˈbɛɡə / (BrE)
To reduce someone to poverty; to make someone so poor that they have to beg for a living.
reduce to poverty

Examples

The collapse of the company beggared hundreds of workers and their families.
Continuous war had beggared the once rich nation beyond recognition.
Such reckless spending could easily beggar even the wealthiest of individuals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to impoverish - to make someone poor

Etymology

From Old French 'begart', meaning a member of a mendicant order, later generalizing to someone who begs.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02322950-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbɛɡər / (AmE), / ˈbɛɡə / (BrE)
(used figuratively) to be beyond what can be adequately described or believed; to defy or exhaust the limits of something (especially description or belief).
to defy or exhaust description or belief

Examples

The scale of the disaster simply beggars description.
His generosity beggars belief; no one expected such kindness.
The brutality of the attack beggared imagination and shocked the whole community.

Synset Examples

This beggars description!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defy - to resist or refuse to obey; to be impossible or very difficult to describe or explain

Etymology

From Middle English 'beggere' (noun); verb usage developed later by extension from 'beggar' meaning 'to make poor.'

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
