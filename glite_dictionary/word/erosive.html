erosive

1. ADJ

/ ɪˈroʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊsɪv / (BrE)
causing gradual destruction or wearing away, especially by friction or the action of natural forces such as water, wind, or chemicals
causing gradual wear or destruction

Examples

The erosive force of the river has carved deep canyons into the rock over thousands of years.
Frequent sandstorms have an erosive effect on exposed surfaces in the desert.
The doctor warned that the erosive nature of the acid could damage tooth enamel over time.

Synset Examples

the erosive effects of waves on the shoreline

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrosive - tending to cause corrosion; able to destroy by chemical action

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *erosivus*, from Latin *erodere* (to gnaw away).

2. ADJ

/ ɪˈroʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊsɪv / (BrE)
Having the quality of causing erosion; able to wear away or destroy gradually, especially by chemical action.
able to cause erosion or destruction

Examples

The scientist handled the erosive acid with extreme care.
Coastal cliffs are vulnerable to the erosive power of the sea.
Some cleaning agents are too erosive for delicate surfaces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrosive - causing or capable of causing gradual destruction, especially by chemical action

Etymology

From Medieval Latin erosivus, from Latin erodere (to gnaw away).
