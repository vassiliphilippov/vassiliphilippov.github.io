incarnate

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈkɑrnət / (AmE), / ɪnˈkɑːnət / (BrE)
(of a spirit or quality) possessed or manifested in bodily form; embodied in flesh.
embodied in bodily or physical form

Examples

Some cultures believe the gods can become incarnate as animals or humans.
She was kindness incarnate, always ready to help anyone in need.
The villain was described as evil incarnate in the novel.

Synset Examples

what seemed corporal melted as breath into the wind
an incarnate spirit
‘corporate’ is an archaic term

Etymology

From Latin 'incarnatus', past participle of 'incarnare' (to make flesh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00633307-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈkɑrˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈkɑː.nət / (BrE)
(especially of a deity or spirit) embodied in human or animal form; given bodily shape or substance.
embodied in bodily form

Examples

Many cultures considered their ruler to be a god incarnate, worthy of worship.
She saw evil incarnate in the dictator who oppressed the nation.
For the artist, beauty incarnate appeared in the form of a graceful dancer.

Synset Examples

a monarch … regarded as a god incarnate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embodied - given a tangible or visible form; made concrete

Etymology

From Latin incarnatus, past participle of incarnare (to make flesh), from in- (in) + caro (flesh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00631042-s
Synset POS: s

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈkɑːrˌneɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈkɑːneɪt / (BrE)
to make something abstract or intangible real or concrete; to embody or represent in a physical form
make something real or concrete; embody

Examples

The artist's work brilliantly incarnates the spirit of freedom through bold colors and dynamic shapes.
She hoped her new project would incarnate her ideals in practical ways.
The company aims to incarnate its values in every product they create.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embody - to give a tangible or visible form to an idea or quality
  • Synonym: to materialize - to make something become actual or real
  • Antonym: to disembody - to divest of a body, to make immaterial

Etymology

From Latin incarnare ‘to make flesh’, from in- ‘into’ + caro, carn- ‘flesh’.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01648699-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈkɑːr.neɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈkɑː.nət / (BrE)
to represent or embody (an abstract concept, quality, or idea) in bodily or physical form; to give tangible form to something nonmaterial.
to embody in physical form

Examples

Some people believed she incarnated compassion and kindness.
The monument incarnates the nation's hopes for peace and unity.
His character incarnates the struggle between good and evil within us all.

Synset Examples

He embodies all that is evil wrong with the system
The painting substantiates the feelings of the artist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embody - to give a concrete form to an idea or quality
  • Synonym: to personify - to attribute human form or nature to something nonhuman

Etymology

From Latin 'incarnare' (to make flesh), from 'in-' (into) + 'caro, carnis' (flesh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02704285-v
Synset POS: v
