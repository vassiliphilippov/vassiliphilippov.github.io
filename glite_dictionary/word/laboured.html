laboured

1. ADJ

/ ˈleɪbɚd / (AmE), / ˈleɪbəd / (BrE)
(of movement, speech, writing, etc.) requiring a lot of effort and therefore slow, awkward, or not natural or easy.
requiring noticeable effort; awkward or forced

Examples

After running up the stairs, her laboured breathing was obvious to everyone in the room.
The professor's lecture felt laboured and difficult to follow for most students.
His apology sounded laboured, as if he was forcing the words out of obligation.

Synset Examples

heavy breathing
the subject made for labored reading

Related Terms

  • Antonym: effortless - achieved with little or no difficulty or effort
  • Synonym: strained - forced or produced with effort, not natural

Etymology

Past participle of the verb 'labour', from Latin 'laborare' (to toil, work).

2. ADJ

/ ˈleɪbɚd / (AmE), / ˈleɪbəd / (BrE)
(of speech, writing, movement, etc.) not natural, relaxed, or spontaneous; showing effort that makes it seem dull, artificial, or awkward.
not natural or spontaneous; forced

Examples

Her speech sounded laboured and failed to engage the audience.
Despite his efforts, the essay felt laboured and lacked originality.
He adopted a laboured tone, making the conversation feel uncomfortable.

Synset Examples

a labored style of debating

Related Terms

  • Antonym: natural - done in a relaxed, easy, or unforced way
  • Synonym: awkward - lacking grace or ease

Etymology

From Middle English 'laboured' (worked at, fatigued), from Old French 'labourer', from Latin 'laborare' (to work).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s
