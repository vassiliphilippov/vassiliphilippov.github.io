aisle

1. NOUN

/ aɪl / (AmE), / aɪl / (BrE)
a long, narrow passage or corridor, especially one found in natural settings such as caves or woods
long narrow passage (e.g. in caves or woods)

Examples

The explorers made their way through a narrow aisle between two towering rock walls.
A sudden crack of light revealed an aisle winding deeper into the cave.
Thick roots hung from the ceiling of the forest aisle, brushing against their heads as they walked.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: passage - a way or corridor through which one can pass

Etymology

From Old French 'ele' (wing), later influenced by 'isle' due to similar spelling.

2. NOUN

/ aɪl / (AmE), / aɪl / (BrE)
a passage between rows of seats in a building such as a church or theater, an aircraft, or a train, or between shelves in a store
passage between rows or shelves

Examples

Please keep the aisle clear for safety in case of an emergency.
She walked quickly down the aisle to greet her friends.
The supermarket aisle was crowded with shoppers looking for deals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corridor - long passage in a building
  • Synonym: hallway - passage in a building often connecting rooms

Etymology

From Old French 'aile' (wing), later influenced by Middle English spelling.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02696719-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ aɪl / (AmE), / aɪl / (BrE)
(in a church) a part parallel to the nave, separated from it by rows of pillars or columns.
side part of church by nave

Examples

Tourists admired the stained glass windows along the north aisle of the cathedral.
Choir members walked down the aisle as the organ played.
The church restoration revealed original stonework in the southern aisle.

Etymology

From Old French 'ele' (currently 'aile'), meaning 'wing', referring to the wing of a building.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
