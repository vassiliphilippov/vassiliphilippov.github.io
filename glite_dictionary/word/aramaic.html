Aramaic

1. NOUN

/ ˌærəˈmeɪɪk / (AmE), / ˌarəˈmeɪɪk / (BrE)
an alphabetic or syllabic script used since the 9th century BC to write the Aramaic language, from which many other scripts were later derived
ancient script for Aramaic language

Examples

Scholars have studied ancient Aramaic inscriptions found in the ruins.
The Hebrew script evolved from Aramaic during the Second Temple period.
Some biblical texts were originally written in Aramaic characters.

Etymology

From Late Latin Aramaicus, from Aramaeus (Aramaean).

3. NOUN

/ ˌærəˈmeɪɪk / (AmE), / ˌærəˈmeɪɪk / (BrE)
a Semitic language originally spoken by the ancient Arameans, later used by various peoples in southwestern Asia and still spoken by some communities today
ancient Semitic language of southwestern Asia

Examples

The sacred texts were originally written in Aramaic before being translated.
Some small communities in the Middle East still speak Aramaic today.
Many ancient inscriptions in the region are found in Aramaic.

Etymology

From Latin Aramaicus, from Ancient Greek Aramai͂os (an Aramean), from Aram (the name of an ancient region).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07000967-n
Synset POS: n
