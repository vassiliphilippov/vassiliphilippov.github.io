dogfight

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɔɡˌfaɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡˌfaɪt / (BrE)
a violent close-range aerial battle between fighter aircraft
aerial battle between fighter planes

Examples

The two pilots were engaged in a fierce dogfight over enemy territory.
Several dogfights broke out during the intense aerial campaign.
He became an ace after downing five enemy planes in dogfights.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air battle - a battle fought in the air between aircraft

Etymology

Originally referred to fights between dogs; applied to aerial battles during World War I due to their chaotic, close combat nature.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00976310-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɔgˌfaɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɒg.faɪt / (BrE)
a fierce, disorderly fight, especially an aerial battle between fighter aircraft
fierce, disorderly fight (esp. aerial battle)

Examples

The two pilots became engaged in a dogfight high above the city.
A violent dogfight broke out between rival gangs downtown last night.
The movie featured an intense dogfight scene with vintage fighter planes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skirmish - a minor or brief fight
  • Degree Higher: battle - a sustained fight between organized armed forces

Etymology

Compound of 'dog' and 'fight,' originally referencing actual fighting dogs, later applied to chaotic battles.

6. VERB

/ ˈdɔɡˌfaɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡˌfaɪt / (BrE)
(of fighter planes) to engage in a close-range aerial battle with enemy aircraft.
to engage in an aerial battle (aircraft)

Examples

The two squadrons began to dogfight high above the battlefield as the ground troops advanced.
Many ace pilots became famous for their ability to dogfight during World War II.
Young aviators train for years before they are allowed to dogfight in real combat missions.

Etymology

Derived from the noun 'dogfight' (aerial battle), from the image of two dogs fighting; sense extended in early 20th-century aviation.
