antilogism

1. NOUN

/ ˌæn.tɪˈlɔː.dʒɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˌæn.tɪˈlɒ.dʒɪz.əm / (BrE)
A set of three statements in logic, of which any two imply the negation of the third; specifically, two are the premises of a valid syllogism and the third is the contradiction of the conclusion.
inconsistent triad of logical propositions

Examples

A logical puzzle may ask you to identify which part of the argument forms an antilogism.
If you spot an antilogism in a set of propositions, you know they are inconsistent.
Every valid syllogism can be reformulated as an antilogism by negating its conclusion.

Synset Examples

Diagram the premises and the negation of the conclusion; the argument is valid iff the resulting diagram is an antilogism (i.e. it's inconsistent).

Related Terms

  • Antonym: syllogism - a form of reasoning with two premises leading to a conclusion

Etymology

From Greek anti- (against) + logos (word, reason) + -ism.
