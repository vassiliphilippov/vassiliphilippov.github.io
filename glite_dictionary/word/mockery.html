mockery

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɑkəri / (AmE), / ˈmɒkəri / (BrE)
the act of ridiculing or making fun of someone or something; scornful or contemptuous language or behavior.
ridicule, scornful imitation or derision

Examples

His speech was met with mockery from the audience, who laughed at every mistake.
They made a mockery of the rules by blatantly ignoring them.
The cruel mockery in her voice made him blush with embarrassment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridicule - the act of making someone or something look foolish
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'mocquerie', from 'moquer' (to mock).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɑːkəri / (AmE), / ˈmɒkəri / (BrE)
a literary or artistic composition that imitates or misrepresents someone's style, usually for humorous or satirical effect
imitation or parody of someone's style

Examples

The play was a clever mockery of classical tragedy, filled with exaggerated characters and absurd dialogue.
Critics viewed the film as a mockery of the original novel, ridiculing its style and themes.
His poem was a brilliant mockery of the poet laureate's sentimental verses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parody - a humorous or satirical imitation
  • Synonym: satire - use of humor or exaggeration to criticize or mock

Etymology

From Old French moquerie (derision), from moquer (to mock).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɑkəri / (AmE), / ˈmɒkəri / (BrE)
the act of imitating or mimicking someone or something in a way that is intended to be funny or satirical, often to criticize or ridicule
humorous or satirical mimicry

Examples

His speech was met with mockery from the audience.
The comedian's mockery of politicians drew both laughter and criticism.
Their mockery of celebrity culture was both amusing and insightful.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parody - an imitation with deliberate exaggeration for comic effect
  • Synonym: satire - the use of humor or exaggeration to criticize people or ideas
  • Antonym: praise - expression of approval or admiration

Etymology

From Middle French 'mocquerie', from 'moquer' (to mock).
