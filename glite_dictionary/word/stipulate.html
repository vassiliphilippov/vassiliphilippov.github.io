to stipulate

1. VERB

/ ˈstɪpjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈstɪpjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to state clearly and formally that something must be done, or that something is necessary as part of an agreement or contract
specify as a formal condition

Examples

The contract stipulates that all materials must be delivered by June 1st.
The agreement stipulated that both parties would share the costs equally.
Her promotion was stipulated on completing the required training.

Synset Examples

The will stipulates that she can live in the house for the rest of her life
The contract stipulates the dates of the payments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to specify - to state something clearly and exactly
  • Synonym: to require - to make something necessary as part of a rule or agreement

Etymology

From Latin 'stipulatus', past participle of 'stipulari' (to demand or specify a guarantee).

2. VERB

/ ˈstɪpjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈstɪpjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to demand or state something clearly and formally as a condition of an agreement; to promise or guarantee formally
to set a formal condition or requirement

Examples

The contract stipulates that payment must be made by the end of the month.
They stipulated that the work be completed before the deadline.
The judge stipulated the conditions for the defendant's release.

Synset Examples

They stipulated to release all the prisoners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to specify - to state explicitly as a condition
  • Synonym: to require - to make something necessary as a condition
  • Antonym: to waive - to refrain from insisting on or demanding

Etymology

From Latin stipulatus, past participle of stipulari (to demand a formal promise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01021481-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈstɪpjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈstɪpjʊleɪt / (BrE)
(legal) To make an agreement or contract, especially orally and in the form of question and answer, that has legal force.
formally agree or contract (legal)
legal

Examples

The parties stipulate the main terms in response to each question from the judge.
During the hearing, the lawyers stipulated the facts of the case orally.
You must stipulate your agreement clearly for it to have legal standing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to agree - to have the same opinion or come to a mutual arrangement

Etymology

From Latin stipulatus, past participle of stipulari (to demand a formal promise).

Synset POS: v
