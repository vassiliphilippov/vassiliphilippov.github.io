clasp

1. VERB

/ klæsp / (AmE), / klɑːsp / (BrE)
to hold something or someone tightly with your hands or arms
hold tightly with hands or arms

Examples

She clasped the letter to her chest and smiled.
He clasped my hand tightly as we crossed the street together.
The old woman sat by the fire, clasping a faded photograph.

Synset Examples

The child clasped my hands

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grip - to hold something tightly
  • Antonym: release - to let go of something held

Etymology

From Middle English *claspen*, probably from Old English *clæppan* (to clap, embrace).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ klæsp / (AmE), / klɑːsp / (BrE)

fasten with a buckle

Related Terms

5. NOUN

/ klæsp / (AmE), / klɑːsp / (BrE)
a device, such as a buckle or hook, used to fasten or hold two things together
fastening device or hook

Examples

She fastened the necklace with a delicate clasp at the back of her neck.
The briefcase has a sturdy metal clasp that keeps it securely closed.
He struggled for a moment with the tiny clasp on his watch band.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buckle - fastening device for joining two ends, often on a belt
  • Synonym: hook - curved device used for catching, holding, or pulling
  • Synonym: fastener - device that fastens things together

Etymology

From Old French *clasp* (hook, buckle), related to Middle English *clapse*.

6. VERB

/ klæsp / (AmE), / klɑːsp / (BrE)
to fasten or secure (something) with a clasp or similar fastening, as one would with a brooch
fasten with a clasp

Examples

She carefully clasped the necklace around her mother’s neck.
He clasped the medal to his jacket for the ceremony.
The brooch clasps securely, ensuring it won’t fall off.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unclasp - to open the clasp of; to release

Etymology

From Old English *clyppan* (to hold, embrace) and Middle English *claspe* (a fastening).
