to vocalise

1. VERB

/ ˈvoʊkəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvəʊkəlaɪz / (BrE)
to express or state something clearly in words, especially aloud
express or state clearly in words

Examples

She struggled at first, but soon learned how to vocalise her thoughts in meetings.
The teacher encouraged students to vocalise their concerns during class discussions.
It's important to vocalise your feelings instead of keeping them hidden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: articulate - to express an idea or feeling fluently and coherently
  • Antonym: mumble - speak in a quiet and indistinct way, making it hard to hear clearly

Etymology

From French 'vocaliser', from Latin 'vocalis' (vocal).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00980401-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈvoʊ.kə.laɪz / (AmE), / ˈvəʊ.kə.laɪz / (BrE)
to sing each note of a scale or melody using the same vowel sound, often as a vocal exercise
sing notes using a single vowel sound

Examples

The choir was asked to vocalise the melody on 'ah' for the warm-up.
Before rehearsing the song, the soprano spent several minutes vocalising to loosen her voice.
He vocalised each note of the scale smoothly during his lesson.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vocalize - to sing or utter with the voice; US spelling variant

Etymology

From French 'vocalise', from Italian 'vocalizzo', ultimately from Latin 'vocālis' (vocal).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01708095-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈvoʊkəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvəʊkəlaɪz / (BrE)
to produce speech sounds using the voice
produce speech sounds
british

Examples

Infants begin to vocalise long before they utter real words.
He tried to vocalise his thoughts clearly during the meeting.
Some birds can vocalise complex sounds to communicate with their flock.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to speak - to utter words or articulate sounds with the ordinary voice; talk
  • Degree Higher: to sing - to produce musical tones with the voice

Etymology

From French 'vocaliser', from 'vocal', from Latin 'vocalis' (vocal).

4. VERB

/ ˈvoʊkəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvəʊkəlaɪz / (BrE)
to produce sound with the voice, especially by vibrating the vocal cords; to utter or sing using the voice
produce sound with the voice

Examples

She began to vocalise a gentle tune as she worked in the kitchen.
The baby is starting to vocalise and experiment with different sounds.
During the warm-up, the choir members vocalised simple scales to prepare their voices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to speak - to utter words with the voice
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak very softly using breath but not the vocal cords

Etymology

From French 'vocaliser', from Latin 'vocalis' (vocal).

5. VERB

/ ˈvoʊkəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈvəʊkəlaɪz / (BrE)
(of a consonant or sound) to pronounce or articulate as a vowel; to change into a vowel sound.
to pronounce as a vowel

Examples

In some dialects, the letter 'l' may be vocalised at the end of a word.
Linguists have studied how certain consonants are vocalised between vowels.
During speech development, some sounds naturally vocalise over time.

Synset Examples

Related Terms
