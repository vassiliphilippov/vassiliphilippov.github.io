to recuse

1. VERB

/ rɪˈkjuz / (AmE), / rɪˈkjuːz / (BrE)
(of a judge or official) to disqualify oneself from participating in a case or decision because of a potential conflict of interest or lack of impartiality.
disqualify oneself (legal, esp. judge)
legal

Examples

The judge decided to recuse herself from the trial due to a personal connection to the defendant.
If the prosecutor has a conflict of interest, he must recuse himself from the case.
There were calls for the committee chair to recuse herself to ensure an impartial decision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disqualify - to declare someone unfit or unqualified; to remove from eligibility

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *recusare* (to refuse, reject).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈkjuz / (AmE), / rɪˈkjuːz / (BrE)
(of a judge or official) to disqualify oneself or be disqualified from participating in a legal case, usually due to potential bias or a conflict of interest.
disqualify oneself in a legal case
legal

Examples

The judge decided to recuse herself from the case due to a potential conflict of interest.
If you feel you cannot be impartial, you must recuse yourself from the proceedings.
The defense attorney filed a motion to recuse the presiding judge.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disqualify - to officially declare someone unfit for a role or activity

Etymology

From Medieval Latin recūsāre (to refuse, object).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00688150-v
Synset POS: v
