to presume

1. VERB

/ prɪˈzum / (AmE), / prɪˈzjuːm / (BrE)
to suppose that something is true without having proof, often as a basis for action or belief
suppose something without proof

Examples

We cannot presume innocence or guilt until more evidence is presented.
She presumed the meeting had been canceled because nobody showed up.
It would be unwise to presume that the weather will stay nice all week.

Synset Examples

I assume his train was late

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to suppose something to be true without proof
  • Degree Lower: guess - to estimate or suppose without sufficient information

Etymology

From Latin 'praesumere' meaning 'to take in advance'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00633898-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ prɪˈzum / (AmE), / prɪˈzjuːm / (BrE)
to act with unwarranted confidence or take liberties beyond what is considered appropriate
act with unwarranted confidence

Examples

I wouldn't presume to tell you how to do your job.
He tends to presume on our hospitality without asking.
Don't presume that you're welcome to join the meeting without an invitation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to take on a role or responsibility, often without right
  • Synonym: to overstep - to go beyond what is proper or allowed
  • Antonym: respect - to regard with deference; behave appropriately within limits

Etymology

From Latin praesumere, from prae- (before) + sumere (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00011544-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ prɪˈzum / (AmE), / prɪˈzjuːm / (BrE)
to act without permission or authority; to take liberty often in a way considered inappropriate or overstepping bounds
to act without authority or permission

Examples

I would not presume to speak for the whole group without their consent.
You presume too much when you make decisions for others unasked.
He presumed to enter her office without knocking, which annoyed her.

Synset Examples

How dare you call my lawyer?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to take upon oneself without permission or justification
  • Synonym: dare - have the courage to do something, often without permission

Etymology

From Latin praesumere (to take beforehand), from prae- (before) + sumere (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02380015-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ prɪˈzum / (AmE), / prɪˈzjuːm / (BrE)
to provide a reasonable basis for believing something to be true; to imply or suggest as probable based on evidence
to suggest as probable based on evidence

Examples

The evidence presumes the defendant's involvement in the case.
A signed contract presumes full understanding of the agreement.
Her silence presumes consent to the proposed changes.

Synset Examples

A restaurant bill presumes the consumption of food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imply - to suggest something is true without stating it directly
  • Synonym: to suggest - to cause one to think that something exists or is the case
  • Antonym: to prove - to establish the truth or validity of something

Etymology

From Latin 'praesumere' (to take beforehand), from 'prae' (before) + 'sumere' (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01017686-v
Synset POS: v
