to decompose

1. VERB

/ ˌdiː.kəmˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌdiː.kəmˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to separate or break down a substance into its constituent elements or parts, especially by chemical means
break down into basic parts or elements

Examples

Bacteria help to decompose dead plant material in the soil.
Scientists use heat to decompose the compound into its separate elements.
The process will decompose the waste into harmless substances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disintegrate - to break into small parts or lose cohesion
  • Antonym: combine - to join or unite to form a single substance or group

Etymology

From Latin decomponere, from de- (down) + componere (to put together).

2. VERB

/ ˌdiː.kəmˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌdiː.kəmˈpəʊz / (BrE)
(of organic matter) to decay or break down into simpler substances, especially through the action of bacteria or fungi.
to decay or break down (especially by bacteria)

Examples

After several weeks in the compost bin, the food scraps began to decompose.
Heat and moisture cause dead leaves to decompose quickly in the rainforest.
Scientists studied how different bacteria help substances to decompose in the soil.

Synset Examples

The bodies decomposed in the heat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decay - to rot or undergo decomposition
  • Synonym: rot - to become bad or decomposed through natural processes
  • Antonym: preserve - to keep something from decaying or decomposing

Etymology

From French 'décomposer', from Latin 'componere' (to put together) with prefix 'de-' indicating reversal.

Source: WordNet
