mahagua

1. NOUN

/ məˈhɑːɡwə / (AmE), / məˈhɑːɡwə / (BrE)
An upright forest tree native to Cuba and Jamaica, known for its variably hairy leaves, orange-yellow or orange-red flowers, and moderately dense timber used in cabinetwork and gunstocks.
tropical tree of Cuba and Jamaica; timber source

Examples

The craftsman preferred mahagua for its durability when making furniture.
In some parts of the Caribbean, mahagua trees are valued for their beautiful blossoms.
The cabinetwork in the old house was made from locally sourced mahagua wood.

Etymology

From Spanish 'majagua', probably of Taíno origin.

2. NOUN

/ məˈhɑːɡwə / (AmE), / məˈhɑːɡwə / (BrE)
A tropical shrubby tree widely found along shores, known for its light, tough wood used in canoe outriggers and its fiber used for making cordage and caulking; often grown as an ornamental plant.
tropical tree used for wood and fiber

Examples

The villagers used mahagua wood to build sturdy outriggers for their canoes.
Because of its attractive foliage, mahagua is often planted as an ornamental tree.
Ropes and caulking were traditionally made from the fiber of the mahagua tree.

Etymology

From Spanish 'mahagua', originally from Taíno 'mahagua', referring to the tree.
