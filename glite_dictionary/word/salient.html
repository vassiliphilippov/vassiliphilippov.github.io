salient

1. ADJ

/ ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (AmE), / ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (BrE)
(Heraldry) Depicted as leaping or springing forward, with the front legs raised together and the body tilted forward.
(heraldry) leaping or springing figure

Examples

The lion was depicted salient on the family's crest, symbolizing courage in action.
On the ancient shield, a stag salient represented swiftness and agility.
The herald described the animal as salient, distinguishing it from rampant or couchant poses.

Etymology

From Latin 'salire' (to leap).

2. ADJ

/ ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (AmE), / ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (BrE)
(of an angle or part of a structure) projecting outward at an angle of less than 180 degrees.
projecting outward (angle, structure)

Examples

The fort's salient angle gave defenders a clear view of the approaching enemy.
In geometry, a salient angle is one that points outward, not inward.
The wall forms a salient at the northeast corner for extra protection.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: re-entrant - (of angles) pointing inward, opposite of salient

Etymology

From Latin salient-, saliens, present participle of salīre (to leap, spring).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02602211-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (AmE), / ˈseɪ.li.ənt / (BrE)
(military) a part of a defensive line or fortification that projects outward towards the enemy, making it more exposed to attack.
military position projecting toward enemy

Examples

The regiment held the salient despite heavy enemy fire.
Troops stationed at the salient were at greater risk of encirclement.
The general ordered reinforcements to defend the vulnerable salient in the line.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: re-entrant - an inwardly pointing angle on a defensive line, opposite of salient

Etymology

From Latin saliens, salientis (leaping), present participle of salire (to leap), referring to a part that 'leaps out'.

Source: WordNet
