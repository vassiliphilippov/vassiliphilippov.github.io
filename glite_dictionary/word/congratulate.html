to congratulate

1. VERB

/ kənˈɡrætʃəˌleɪt / (AmE), / kənˈɡratjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to express pleasure or praise to someone because of their success or good fortune
express praise or good wishes

Examples

We should congratulate Sarah on her promotion at work.
He called to congratulate me after I passed my exam.
I want to congratulate you on your outstanding achievement.

Synset Examples

He complimented her on her last physics paper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: praise - to express approval or admiration
  • Synonym: compliment - to express praise or admiration to someone

Etymology

From Latin 'congratulatus', past participle of 'congratulare' (to wish joy), from 'con-' (together) + 'grātulārī' (to rejoice).

2. VERB

/ kənˈɡrætʃəˌleɪt / (AmE), / kənˈɡratjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to express praise or pleasure to someone because of an achievement or special occasion
express praise for an achievement

Examples

We all congratulate her on winning the award last night.
He called to congratulate me after I passed my exams.
They congratulated the team on their impressive comeback.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: praise - to express approval or admiration
  • Synonym: to commend - to praise formally or officially
  • Antonym: to criticize - to express disapproval or point out faults

Etymology

From Latin 'congratulatus', past participle of 'congratulare' meaning 'wish joy'.

3. VERB

/ kənˈɡrætʃəˌleɪt / (AmE), / kənˈɡratjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to express pleasure or pride for someone's achievement or special occasion; sometimes used reflexively to show personal pride in one's own achievement
express pleasure for achievement or occasion

Examples

She congratulated herself on finishing the marathon despite the difficult weather.
It's important to congratulate your colleagues on their successes.
He turned to congratulate his team after the project was completed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: praise - to express approval or admiration
  • Synonym: to commend - to formally praise or express approval
  • Antonym: to criticize - to express disapproval or point out faults

Etymology

From Latin 'congratulatus', past participle of 'congratulāri' (to wish joy).
