belay

1. NOUN

/ bəˈleɪ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪ / (BrE)
a point or object to which a climber's rope is securely fastened during an ascent, providing safety support
anchor point for a climber's rope

Examples

Before climbing further, she searched for a solid belay to secure her rope.
The guide checked each belay carefully for stability and safety.
We set up a belay on the ledge before attempting the next pitch.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anchor - a point of security, often for a rope

Etymology

From obsolete English 'belay' (to secure), from Middle Low German 'beleien' (to cover, overlie), used in sailing and climbing.

2. VERB

/ bəˈleɪ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪ / (BrE)
To fasten a boat to a bitt, pin, or cleat using a rope or line, especially in nautical contexts.
secure a boat by tying it to something

Examples

The sailor quickly belayed the boat to the dock before unloading the cargo.
Always belay the rope securely to prevent the vessel from drifting away.
They belayed the yacht to a sturdy cleat on the pier.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moor - to secure a boat by attaching it to a fixed object

Etymology

From Old English *belecgan* (to lay on, cover), related to nautical practice.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01345318-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bəˈleɪ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪ / (BrE)
to secure a rope by winding it around an object or person, especially to support or anchor someone in climbing or sailing
secure a rope by winding it around something

Examples

The climber carefully belayed the rope around the sturdy rock to ensure safety.
You must always belay your partner properly before they start to climb.
She belaying the rope quickly as her friend ascended the cliff.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secure - to make something safe or fixed in place
  • Synonym: to fasten - to attach or tie something firmly

Etymology

From Old English *belecgan* (to lay around, cover), related to putting or laying something around.
