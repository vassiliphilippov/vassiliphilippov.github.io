crusade

1. NOUN

/ kruˈseɪd / (AmE), / kruˈseɪd / (BrE)
any of a series of military expeditions by European Christians in the 11th to 13th centuries intended to recover the Holy Land from Muslim rule.
medieval Christian military expedition

Examples

Many Europeans joined the Crusade in hopes of reclaiming Jerusalem from Muslim control.
The First Crusade began in 1096 after a call by Pope Urban II.
Historians debate the motivations behind the various Crusades throughout the Middle Ages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: holy war - a war declared or fought for religious reasons

Etymology

From Medieval Latin crusata (marked with a cross), from Latin crux (cross).

/ kruˈseɪd / (AmE), / kruˈseɪd / (BrE)
A vigorous campaign for social, political, or religious change, typically involving passionate and sustained effort.
vigorous campaign for a cause

Examples

The organization launched a crusade against plastic pollution in the oceans.
He became known for his lifelong crusade to promote equality and social justice.

4. VERB

/ kruˈseɪd / (AmE), / kruːˈseɪd / (BrE)
to engage in or take part in a vigorous campaign or fight, especially for a cause or against an injustice; originally, to participate in a holy war
engage in a vigorous campaign or holy war

Examples

She decided to crusade for environmental protection after witnessing widespread pollution.
Throughout history, many have crusaded against social injustice, hoping to bring about reform.
He is crusading against corruption in the local government.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: campaign - to work in an organized and active way toward a goal

Etymology

From Old French *croisade*, from *croiser* (to mark with a cross), ultimately from Latin *crux* (cross); originally referring to medieval religious wars.
