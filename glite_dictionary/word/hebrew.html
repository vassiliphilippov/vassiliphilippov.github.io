Hebrew

1. NOUN

/ ˈhiːbruː / (AmE), / ˈhiːbruː / (BrE)
the ancient Semitic language of the Hebrews, revived as the modern official language of Israel
ancient and modern official language of Israel

Examples

He studied ancient Hebrew texts to better understand the origins of the Bible.
Modern Hebrew is spoken by millions of people in Israel and around the world.
She is fluent in both English and Hebrew.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Arabic - major Semitic language of the Arab world
  • Synonym: Yiddish - language historically spoken by Ashkenazi Jews

Etymology

From Old French Ebreu, from Latin Hebraeus, from Ancient Greek Hebraios, from Hebrew ‘Ivri.

4. NOUN

/ ˈhiːbruː / (AmE), / ˈhiːbruː / (BrE)
a member of an ancient people descended from Jacob, or a person connected by cultural or religious ties to this group; historically, often used to refer to the Jewish people, especially in biblical contexts
a member of the ancient Hebrew people

Examples

The ancient Hebrews are believed to have migrated from Mesopotamia to Canaan.
Moses is considered one of the greatest leaders of the Hebrew people.
In the Old Testament, the term Hebrew is often used to refer to the descendants of Jacob.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Jew - a member of the people and cultural community whose traditional religion is Judaism

Etymology

From Old French Ebreu, from Latin Hebraeus, from Ancient Greek Hebraios, from Hebrew ʿIvri.
