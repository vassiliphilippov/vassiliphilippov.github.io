barge

1. VERB

/ bɑrdʒ / (AmE), / bɑːdʒ / (BrE)
to transport goods or people by barge on a body of water
transport by barge

Examples

The company barges coal down the river to the power plant.
During the flood, emergency supplies were barged to the isolated town.
They barged heavy machinery across the canal to the construction site.

Etymology

From Old French *barge*, from Vulgar Latin *barga* (boat).

2. NOUN

/ bɑrdʒ / (AmE), / bɑːdʒ / (BrE)
a large, flat-bottomed boat used for carrying heavy goods, especially on rivers or canals
large flat-bottomed boat for heavy loads

Examples

The barge slowly made its way down the canal, loaded with coal.
Workers used a barge to transport building materials along the river.
A tourist could take a leisurely cruise on a traditional barge through the countryside.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boat - a small vessel for traveling over water
  • Degree Lower: canoe - a narrow, light boat pointed at both ends and moved with a paddle
  • Degree Higher: ship - a large vessel for transporting people or goods by sea

Etymology

From Old French *barge*, from Medieval Latin *barga* (boat).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02795575-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ bɑrdʒ / (AmE), / bɑːdʒ / (BrE)
to push or move forcefully and rudely, often into a place or situation without permission or invitation
push one's way rudely

Examples

He didn't knock; he just barged into my office without warning.
People often barge past others when the train doors open.
She apologized for barging in on their conversation, but she needed urgent help.

Synset Examples

she barged into the meeting room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intrude - to enter without invitation or permission, especially rudely
  • Degree Lower: to enter - to go or come into a place

Synset POS: v
