hypozeuxis

1. NOUN

/ haɪpoʊˈzuːksɪs / (AmE), / haɪpəˈzjuːksɪs / (BrE)
A rhetorical device in which a series of parallel clauses each have their own subject and predicate, as in 'I came, I saw, I conquered.'
rhetorical device of parallel clauses

Examples

The ancient speech employed hypozeuxis to emphasize each action taken by the hero.
Writers often use hypozeuxis for clarity and dramatic effect in persuasive texts.
An English teacher pointed out the use of hypozeuxis in the famous phrase 'I came, I saw, I conquered.'

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hyperzeuxis - use of a linking word with several clauses, opposite of hypozeuxis

Etymology

From Greek hypozeuxis (subordinate joining), from hypo- (under) + zeugnunai (to yoke).
