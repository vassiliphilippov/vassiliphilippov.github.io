Lancastrian

1. NOUN

/ lænˈkæstriən / (AmE), / laŋˈkæstrɪən / (BrE)
a person who is a native or inhabitant of Lancaster, especially in northern England
person from Lancaster, England

Examples

As a proud Lancastrian, she always supports her local football team.
Many Lancastrians still observe traditions unique to Lancashire.
The author, a lifelong Lancastrian, often writes about his hometown.

Etymology

From Lancaster, the city in northwestern England, with the suffix -ian forming a demonym.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09723453-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ lænˈkæstriən / (AmE), / laŋˈkæstriən / (BrE)
a member or supporter of the house of Lancaster, especially during the Wars of the Roses in England
supporter of Lancaster house (historical)

Examples

During the Wars of the Roses, every Lancastrian fought fiercely to restore their house to the throne.
He identified as a proud Lancastrian and believed in the cause of Henry VI.
The castle was a stronghold for Lancastrian forces in the fifteenth century.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Yorkist - a member or supporter of the house of York

Etymology

From Lancaster, the name of the English royal house, with the suffix -ian denoting membership or affiliation.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ lænˈkæstriən / (AmE), / laŋˈkæstriən / (BrE)
of or relating to the English royal house of Lancaster or its supporters, especially during the Wars of the Roses.
relating to the house of Lancaster

Examples

During the War of the Roses, many noble families were staunchly Lancastrian in their allegiance.
The Lancastrian army suffered a decisive defeat at the Battle of Towton.
Some historians analyze the Lancastrian influence on English culture during the 15th century.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Yorkist - of or relating to the royal house of York or their supporters

Etymology

From Lancaster, a city in northwest England, center of the House of Lancaster, plus the English suffix -ian.
