complementation

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmpləˌmɛnˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒmplɪmɛnˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the grammatical relationship in which a word or phrase completes the meaning of a predicate in a sentence
grammar: predicate-completing relation

Examples

The complementation of the verb 'consider' often involves a clause, as in 'I consider him a friend.'
English verbs show different patterns of complementation, such as taking direct objects or infinitives.
Understanding complementation helps in constructing complex sentences accurately.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: complement - a word or phrase that completes the meaning of a predicate

Etymology

From complement + -ation, based on Latin 'complementum' (that which fills up).

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmpləˌmenˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒmplɪmenˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
(Linguistics) The occurrence of related speech sounds or forms so that each appears only in different contexts and never in exactly the same environment.
linguistic distribution of forms in exclusive contexts
medical

Examples

The phenomenon of complementation explains why certain allophones never occur in the same phonetic environment.
In English, nasal and oral vowels show complementation in some dialects.
Phonologists study complementation to understand how languages organize similar sounds.

Etymology

From Latin 'complementum' (that which fills up or completes) + '-ation'.
