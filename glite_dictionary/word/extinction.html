extinction

1. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (AmE), / ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (BrE)
the act of putting out a fire, light, or flame; the process of causing something to stop burning or shining
the act of extinguishing a fire or light

Examples

Following the ceremony, the extinction of the candles marked the end of the event.
The sudden extinction of all the lights left the audience in darkness.
The firefighter was praised for his quick extinction of the kitchen blaze.

Synset Examples

the extinction of the lights

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extinguishing - the act of causing something to stop burning

Etymology

From Latin 'extinctio', from 'extinguere' (to put out, quench).

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (AmE), / ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (BrE)
the decrease in the intensity of radiation, such as light, as it passes through a medium due to absorption and scattering
decrease in radiation intensity by absorption/scattering

Examples

The extinction of starlight by interstellar dust must be accounted for in astronomical observations.
Atmospheric extinction causes distant mountains to appear hazy or less distinct.
Ultraviolet extinction can significantly reduce the amount of radiation that reaches the Earth's surface.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attenuation - weakening in force or intensity, especially of a signal or beam

Etymology

From Latin extinctio (putting out, quenching), from extinguere (to extinguish).

6. NOUN

/ ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (AmE), / ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən / (BrE)
In psychology, the gradual weakening and eventual disappearance of a conditioned response when the reinforcer is removed or the conditioned stimulus is repeatedly presented without the unconditioned stimulus.
psychology: disappearance of conditioned response

Examples

After repeated trials without food, the dog's salivation response underwent extinction.
Therapists may use extinction techniques to reduce unwanted behaviors in children.
The process of extinction can take several sessions, depending on the strength of the conditioned response.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conditioning - a learning process in which an organism's behavior becomes dependent on environmental stimuli

Etymology

From Latin 'extinctio' (a quenching, extinguishing), from 'extinguere' (to extinguish).
