cultivated

1. ADJ

/ ˈkʌltɪˌveɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈkʌltɪveɪtɪd / (BrE)
(of land or fields) prepared and used for growing crops, often by plowing, fertilizing, or other agricultural methods.
prepared for raising crops (of land)

Examples

The cultivated fields stretched as far as the eye could see.
After years of hard work, the once-barren land became fully cultivated.
Rice is grown on cultivated land near the riverbanks.

Synset Examples

cultivated land

Related Terms

  • Antonym: uncultivated - (of land) not prepared or used for growing crops
  • Synonym: arable - (of land) suitable for growing crops

Etymology

Past participle of the verb 'cultivate', from Latin 'cultivatus', past participle of 'cultivare' (to till, cultivate).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkʌltɪˌveɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈkʌltɪveɪtɪd / (BrE)
having or showing good education, manners, and refinement in taste
refined and polished in taste or manners

Examples

He spoke with a cultivated accent that impressed everyone at the dinner party.
Her cultivated manners made her stand out in any social gathering.
The author's cultivated taste in literature was evident from his eloquent reviews.

Synset Examples

cultivated speech
cultured Bostonians
cultured tastes
a genteel old lady
polite society

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cultured - having good education and refined tastes
  • Synonym: genteel - polite, refined, or respectable in an affected or ostentatious way
  • Antonym: uncouth - lacking good manners, refinement, or grace

Etymology

From Latin 'cultivatus', the past participle of 'cultivare' (to till or tend), later used figuratively for personal development.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01954376-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈkʌltɪˌveɪtɪd / (AmE), / ˈkʌltɪˌveɪtɪd / (BrE)
(of plants or land) no longer in a natural state, having been developed or improved by human effort, especially for use in agriculture or horticulture.
grown or developed by humans rather than wild

Examples

The cultivated fields stretched for miles beyond the village.
Only cultivated mushrooms are sold in most supermarkets.
These cultivated varieties yield more fruit than their wild relatives.

Synset Examples

cultivated roses
cultivated blackberries

Related Terms

  • Antonym: wild - growing or living in a natural state, not cultivated or domesticated

Etymology

Past participle of the verb 'cultivate', from Latin 'cultivare' (to till).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02397164-s
Synset POS: s
