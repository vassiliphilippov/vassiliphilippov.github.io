runaway

1. NOUN

/ ˈrʌnəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈrʌnəweɪ / (BrE)
an easy or decisive victory, especially by a large margin
an easy or overwhelming victory

Examples

The team won in a runaway, beating their rivals by twenty points.
The election turned into a runaway after early results showed a huge lead.
Many predicted a close game, but it ended up as a runaway victory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: landslide - an overwhelming victory, especially in an election
  • Antonym: close call - an outcome where the result was nearly even or undecided
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07490247-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈrʌnəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈrʌnəˌweɪ / (BrE)
a person who has left a place or situation secretly and unexpectedly, often to escape trouble or difficulties
someone who flees from a bad situation

Examples

The police found the young runaway hiding in a nearby train station.
After weeks of searching, her parents finally located the missing runaway.
Many runaways seek shelter in the city to escape difficult family situations.

Synset Examples

fugitives from the sweatshops

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fugitive - a person who has escaped from captivity or is in hiding

Etymology

From the verb phrase 'run away', first recorded in Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10134739-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈrʌnəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈrʌnəweɪ / (BrE)
(of a situation or process) completely out of control or happening faster than expected.
out of control; unchecked

Examples

Economists are concerned about runaway inflation affecting the cost of living.
The company struggled to control runaway spending during the busy holiday season.
A runaway fire swept through the forest, destroying hundreds of acres.

Synset Examples

runaway inflation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unchecked - not restrained or controlled
  • Antonym: contained - kept under control; restrained

Etymology

From the verb phrase 'run away', later used as noun and adjective.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00603794-s
Synset POS: s
