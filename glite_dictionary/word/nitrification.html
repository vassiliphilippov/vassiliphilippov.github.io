nitrification

1. NOUN

/ ˌnaɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnʌɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which soil bacteria convert ammonium compounds into nitrates and nitrites, making nitrogen available to plants
conversion of ammonium to nitrates by bacteria

Examples

Soil health depends on the efficiency of nitrification to supply plants with essential nutrients.
The use of fertilizers can accelerate nitrification in agricultural fields.
Scientists study nitrification to better understand the nitrogen cycle in ecosystems.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: denitrification - the process by which bacteria convert nitrates back into nitrogen gas

Etymology

From French *nitrification* and Latin *nitrum* (natron, soda), plus the suffix '-fication' (making, causing).

2. NOUN

/ ˌnaɪ.trɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌnaɪ.trɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the chemical process by which a nitro group is introduced into an organic compound, or substituted for another group; also, the process by which ammonia is converted into nitrites and then nitrates by bacteria in soil.
chemical process adding a nitro group or converting ammonia
medical

Examples

Nitrification is a key step in the nitrogen cycle, converting ammonia into nitrites and nitrates.
High rates of nitrification in soils can lead to groundwater contamination.
Chemical nitrification is used in the production of explosives and fertilizers.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: denitrification - the process by which nitrates are reduced to nitrogen gas, typically by bacteria

Etymology

From French nitrification, from nitrate + -ification, influenced by Latin nitrum (niter).
