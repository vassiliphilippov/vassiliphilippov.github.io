moralisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɔːrələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɒrələˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of making something moral or more moral, often by introducing moral principles or values
the act of making more moral

Examples

The moralisation of media content often sparks debate about freedom of expression.
Her lectures focused on the moralisation of contemporary art and culture.
Some critics argue that excessive moralisation can stifle creativity and open discussion.

Synset Examples

for years she worked toward the moralization of English literature

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moralization - alternative spelling (US English) for 'moralisation'

Etymology

From French 'moralisation', from Latin 'moralis' (moral).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɔːrələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɒrələˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of expressing moral judgments or offering explanations based on a particular moral code, often in a way that is overly simplistic or preachy
expressing moral judgments or codes

Examples

Her speech was full of moralisation that left little room for differing opinions.
The movie avoids overt moralisation, allowing viewers to draw their own conclusions.
Too much moralisation can sometimes alienate an audience instead of inspiring them.

Synset Examples

his constant moralizing drove me mad

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moralizing - making moral pronouncements in speech or writing
  • Synonym: sermonizing - giving moral advice in a preachy manner

Etymology

From French 'moralisation', from 'moraliser' (to moralize), ultimately from Latin 'moralis' (moral).
