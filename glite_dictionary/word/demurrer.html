demurrer

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈmɜrər / (AmE), / dɪˈmɜːrə / (BrE)
(law) an objection that challenges the legal sufficiency of the opponent's pleadings in a court case, without addressing the facts of the case.
legal objection to opponent's pleadings
legal

Examples

The defendant filed a demurrer arguing that the plaintiff's complaint failed to state a valid legal claim.
After considering the demurrer, the judge concluded that there was enough basis for the case to proceed.
The court granted the demurrer, dismissing the lawsuit without addressing the merits of the facts.

Etymology

From Old French *demorer* (to delay), from Latin *demorari* (to linger).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈmɜrər / (AmE), / dɪˈmʌrə / (BrE)
(Law) A formal objection raised in a court of law, asserting that even if the facts presented by the opposing party are true, they are insufficient to establish a legal case.
formal legal objection to pleadings
legal

Examples

The defense filed a demurrer to challenge the sufficiency of the plaintiff's complaint.
After considering the demurrer, the judge ruled that the case lacked legal grounds to proceed.
A demurrer can be an effective way to dismiss unsubstantiated lawsuits at an early stage.

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French *demourer* (to delay, linger), ultimately from Latin *demorari* (to delay).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈmɜːrər / (AmE), / dɪˈmʌrə / (BrE)
a formal objection that a point in a legal case is irrelevant or invalid, without admitting the truth or falsity of the charge
formal legal objection
legal

Examples

The defense filed a demurrer to challenge the sufficiency of the plaintiff's complaint.
The judge overruled the demurrer, allowing the case to proceed to trial.
After reviewing the facts, the lawyer recommended entering a demurrer rather than answering the complaint.

Etymology

From Old French *demorrer* (to delay), from Latin *demorari* (to linger, delay).
