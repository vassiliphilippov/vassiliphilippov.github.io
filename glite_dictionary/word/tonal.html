tonal

1. ADJ

/ ˈtoʊ.nəl / (AmE), / ˈtəʊ.nəl / (BrE)
(of a language) employing variations in pitch to distinguish meanings of words that otherwise have similar sounds.
using pitch to distinguish word meanings

Examples

Mandarin Chinese is a tonal language, where pitch differences change the meaning of words.
Vietnamese and Thai are also well-known examples of tonal languages.
Speakers of non-tonal languages may find it challenging to hear subtle pitch distinctions in words.

Synset Examples

Chinese is a tonal language

Related Terms

  • Antonym: non-tonal - (of a language) not using variations in pitch to distinguish word meanings

Etymology

From tone + -al, relating to or characterized by tone.

2. ADJ

/ ˈtoʊ.nəl / (AmE), / ˈtəʊ.nəl / (BrE)
relating to or having tonality; characterized by tones and chords arranged around a central key or tonic
relating to musical tonality

Examples

The composer preferred to write tonal music rather than experimental pieces.
Many classic symphonies are highly tonal in their structure and harmony.
Her latest album explores both tonal and atonal elements, blending tradition with innovation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: atonal - music lacking a central key or tonal center

Etymology

From French "tonal," from "ton" (tone), ultimately from Latin "tonus."

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a
