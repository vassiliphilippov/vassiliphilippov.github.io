flit

1. VERB

/ flɪt / (AmE), / flɪt / (BrE)
to move swiftly and lightly from place to place; to dart or skim quickly
move lightly and quickly

Examples

The butterfly seemed to flit from one flower to another without resting.
Her thoughts would often flit to memories of her childhood during quiet moments.
A shadow flitted across the wall as the bird passed the window.

Synset Examples

The hummingbird flitted among the branches

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dart - move quickly and suddenly in a particular direction
  • Antonym: to linger - stay in a place longer than necessary

Etymology

From Old Norse *flytjask* (to move oneself).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01903884-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ flɪt / (AmE), / flɪt / (BrE)
a sudden quick movement, especially as of something darting or fluttering
sudden quick movement

Examples

A sudden flit of movement caught my eye in the shadows.
With a flit, the bird disappeared into the trees.
She noticed the flit of butterflies above the flowers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dart - a sudden, swift movement
  • Synonym: dash - an act of moving suddenly and quickly

Etymology

From Middle English flitten, from Old Norse flytja (to remove, convey).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00335182-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ flɪt / (AmE), / flɪt / (BrE)
an act of secretly leaving one's home or lodgings to avoid paying debts or being detected
secret departure to evade debts

Examples

The tenants did a flit in the middle of the night, leaving the rent unpaid.
Rumors spread quickly after the shopkeeper's sudden flit from town.
Faced with mounting debts, he considered making a flit to start anew elsewhere.

Synset Examples

they did a moonlight flit

Related Terms

  • Synonym: runaway - a person who has run away, especially to avoid trouble or payment
  • Antonym: eviction - the action of expelling someone from a property

Etymology

From Old Norse *flytja* (to remove, convey, move house).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00169732-n
Synset POS: n
