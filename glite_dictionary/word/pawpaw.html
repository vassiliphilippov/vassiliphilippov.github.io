pawpaw

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːˌpɑː / (AmE), / ˈpɔːˌpɔː / (BrE)
A fruit with yellow flesh, native to North America and related to the custard apple, known for its sweet flavor.
fruit with yellow flesh, related to custard apple

Examples

The ripe pawpaw was sweet and creamy, perfect for dessert.
Farmers in the region often grow pawpaw trees in their orchards.
I've never tasted a pawpaw before, but I've heard it's delicious.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: custard apple - a tropical fruit with creamy flesh, related to pawpaw

Etymology

From the Spanish 'papaya' via Caribbean languages; adopted in English for several unrelated tropical fruits.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɔˌpɔ / (AmE), / ˈpɔːˌpɔː / (BrE)
a tropical shrub or small tree native to America, with large deeply divided leaves and oblong yellow fruit
tropical American tree; yellow fruit

Examples

The ripe pawpaw fell from the tree and landed softly on the ground.
We enjoyed slices of fresh pawpaw for breakfast while visiting the Caribbean.
She planted a pawpaw tree in her backyard to enjoy its shade and fruit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: papaya - another tropical fruit, sometimes called pawpaw in some regions

Etymology

Possibly from Carib papaya, referring to the fruit; sometimes distinct from 'papaya' in different English varieties.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɑˌpɔ / (AmE), / ˈpɔːˌpɔː / (BrE)
A small deciduous tree native to the eastern United States, with large oblong leaves and edible, fleshy fruit; also, the fruit of this tree.
North American fruit-bearing tree

Examples

We found a pawpaw tree while hiking in the woods.
The pawpaw produces large, sweet fruit that ripen in late summer.
She made a delicious dessert from fresh pawpaws she picked herself.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: papaya - Tropical fruit or tree with sweet orange flesh, also called pawpaw in some regions

Etymology

From Powhatan or another Algonquian language, probably via Spanish *papaya*, but refers to a different fruit from tropical papaya.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
