Ceratodontidae

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛr.ə.toʊˈdɒn.tɪˌdi / (AmE), / ˌsɛr.ə.təʊˈdɒn.tɪˌdiː / (BrE)
a family of lungfishes distinguished by hornlike ridges on their teeth, found in certain freshwater habitats
family of lungfishes with hornlike tooth ridges

Examples

The Ceratodontidae family is represented today by only a few living species in Australia.
Fossil records show that Ceratodontidae once had a much wider distribution.
Scientists studied the unique teeth structure of Ceratodontidae to better understand their evolutionary history.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lungfish - a type of freshwater fish capable of breathing air

Etymology

From Greek *keras* (horn) + *odous* (tooth) + -idae (family suffix in taxonomy).

2. NOUN

/ sɛˌræt̬əˈdɒn.tɪˌdiː / (AmE), / sɛˌræt.əˈdɒn.tɪˌdiː / (BrE)
a family of lungfishes characterized by hornlike ridges on the teeth, including some ancient and living species
family of lungfishes with hornlike dental ridges

Examples

The Ceratodontidae are most notably represented today by the Queensland lungfish of Australia.
Fossils indicate that members of the Ceratodontidae once inhabited a wider geographic area.
Researchers study the teeth structure of Ceratodontidae to understand evolutionary adaptations in lungfishes.

Etymology

From New Latin Ceratodontidae, based on the type genus Ceratodus (from Greek keras, kerat- 'horn' + odous, odont- 'tooth').
