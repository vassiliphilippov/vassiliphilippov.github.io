to inflate

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪt / (BrE)
to fill something with air or gas so that it becomes larger or rounder
fill with air or gas

Examples

Please inflate the air mattress before our guests arrive.
She watched as the clown began to inflate a bright red balloon.
You need a pump to easily inflate your bicycle tires.

Synset Examples

inflate a balloon

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deflate - to release air or gas from and make smaller or less swollen

Etymology

From Latin inflare, from in- (into) + flare (to blow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00264920-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪt / (BrE)
to make something seem more important or impressive than it really is, especially by exaggerating its size, value, or significance
exaggerate or make bigger (figurative)

Examples

Some companies inflate their earnings reports to attract investors.
The coach warned the players not to inflate their achievements in interviews.
Reporters accused the government of inflating the statistics to make the situation look better.

Synset Examples

The charges were inflated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - to represent something as being larger, better, or worse than it really is

Etymology

From Latin inflare, meaning 'to blow into, inflate', from in- 'into' + flare 'to blow'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00265272-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪt / (BrE)
to increase the amount or availability of something, especially money or currency, often leading to a rise in prices or value
to increase quantity, esp. money, causing rise in value or prices

Examples

The central bank decided to inflate the currency to stimulate economic growth.
If the government continues to inflate the money supply, inflation will become a serious problem.
Speculators can inflate the value of an asset by driving up demand.

Synset Examples

inflate the currency

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deflate - to reduce the amount or availability, especially of money, often lowering prices or value

Etymology

From Latin inflatus, past participle of inflare (to blow into, puff up)

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00563600-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪt / (BrE)
to become larger by filling with air or gas
become filled with air or gas

Examples

After a few moments, the parachute began to inflate and slow the descent.
The party balloons will inflate quickly once you start blowing air into them.
When placed in water, the self-inflating raft will inflate automatically.

Synset Examples

The sails ballooned

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deflate - to release air or gas from something
  • Synonym: to expand - to increase in size or volume, become larger

Etymology

From Latin inflare, from in- (into) + flare (to blow).

5. VERB

/ ɪnˈfleɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪt / (BrE)
to cause prices to rise by increasing the amount of money or credit available in an economy
cause prices to rise by expanding money or credit

Examples

Government policies can sometimes inflate consumer prices by increasing the money supply.
The central bank's decision to print more money started to inflate the nation's economy.
An influx of easy credit helped inflate the value of real estate.

Synset Examples

The war inflated the economy

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deflate - to cause prices to fall by reducing the supply of money or credit

Etymology

From Latin inflatus, past participle of inflare (to blow into, inflate).
