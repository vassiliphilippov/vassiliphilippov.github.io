engraving

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
a print produced by carving a design onto a hard surface and then transferring it to paper
a print made by engraved plate

Examples

The museum displayed a rare engraving by Rembrandt in its main gallery.
She purchased an old engraving of the city to hang in her study.
Many historical documents are illustrated with detailed engravings of past events.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: etching - a print made from a metal plate etched with acid

Etymology

From English 'engrave' + '-ing'; 'engrave' from Middle French 'engraver'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
the art or process of cutting or etching a design onto a hard surface, especially a metal plate, and producing prints from it
the art of making engraved designs and prints

Examples

The museum has a collection of rare engravings from the nineteenth century.
He learned the technique of engraving during his apprenticeship with a master artist.
Modern artists often combine engraving with other printmaking methods to create unique works.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: etching - the process of using acid to cut a design into a metal surface for printing

Etymology

From English 'engrave', from Middle English 'grave' (to carve).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
a block, plate, or other hard surface that has been carved or incised to produce a design, often used for printing images or text
an engraved block or plate

Examples

The artist carefully examined the engraving before inking it for the first print.
This copper engraving was used to produce hundreds of identical illustrations.
The museum display included a nineteenth-century engraving alongside the prints it created.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: etching - a print made from a metal plate that has been etched with acid

Etymology

From engrave + -ing, originally denoting the act or art of engraving before also referring to the engraved surface itself.
