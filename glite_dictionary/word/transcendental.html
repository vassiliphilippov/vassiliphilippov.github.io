transcendental

1. ADJ

/ ˌtræn.sənˈdɛn.tl̩ / (AmE), / ˌtræn.senˈden.tl̩ / (BrE)
existing outside of or beyond the limits of the natural world; supernatural or abstract in nature
existing beyond nature or reality

Examples

Some philosophies seek a transcendental truth that lies beyond the physical world.
Her explanation took on a transcendental tone, suggesting realities beyond ordinary perception.
The poet's work often explored transcendental ideas unrelated to human experience.

Synset Examples

find transcendental motives for sublunary action

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supernatural - attributed to a force beyond scientific understanding or the laws of nature
  • Antonym: natural - existing in or caused by nature; not made or caused by humankind

Etymology

From Medieval Latin transcendentalis, from transcendere (to climb over, surpass).

2. ADJ

/ ˌtrænˌsɛnˈdɛntəl / (AmE), / ˌtræn.senˈden.təl / (BrE)
relating to a system of philosophy that emphasizes intuition and the spiritual above empirical and material evidence, especially as in Transcendentalism.
of philosophy: emphasizing intuition and the spiritual

Examples

Many nineteenth-century poets were inspired by transcendental ideas, seeking to explore the nature of the soul.
Her philosophy takes a transcendental approach, valuing intuition over scientific observation.
Thoreau and Emerson were central figures in the transcendental movement in American thought.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: empirical - based on observation or experience rather than theory or pure logic

Etymology

From Latin transcendere (to climb over or beyond) and Medieval Latin transcendentalis (relating to transcending).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02884663-a
Synset POS: a
