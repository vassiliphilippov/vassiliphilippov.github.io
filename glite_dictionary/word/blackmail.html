blackmail

1. NOUN

/ ˈblækˌmeɪl / (AmE), / ˈblæk.meɪl / (BrE)
the crime of demanding money or another benefit from someone by threatening to reveal damaging or embarrassing information about them
extortion by threatening disclosure

Examples

He was arrested for attempting to blackmail a politician with sensitive photographs.
The company became a victim of blackmail after hackers accessed confidential files.
She lived in constant fear of blackmail after her secret was discovered.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extortion - the act of obtaining something, especially money, through force or threats

Etymology

Originally from Scottish English, 'black' + 'mail' (payment); 'mail' from Old Norse 'mal' meaning 'agreement' or 'payment'.

2. VERB

/ ˈblækˌmeɪl / (AmE), / ˈblæk.meɪl / (BrE)
to obtain money or advantage from someone by threatening to reveal embarrassing, damaging, or harmful information about them
obtain something by threats or coercion

Examples

He tried to blackmail the politician by threatening to release confidential photos.
The company was blackmailed into paying a large sum of money to keep its secrets safe.
They are blackmailing her to force her into signing the documents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to extort - to obtain something by force or threats
  • Synonym: to coerce - to persuade someone to do something by force or threat

Etymology

Originally Scottish, from 'black' + obsolete 'mail' (rent or tribute), from Old Norse 'mál' (agreement, contract).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02246549-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈblækˌmeɪl / (AmE), / ˈblæk.meɪl / (BrE)
to force someone to do something by threatening to reveal secrets or cause harm to them
to threaten for gain

Examples

She attempted to blackmail her boss by threatening to expose his secret.
The politician claimed that his opponents tried to blackmail him into stepping down.
Never allow anyone to blackmail you into doing something unethical.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to extort - to obtain something by force or threats
  • Synonym: to coerce - to persuade or force someone to do something using threats
  • Antonym: to persuade - to make someone do or believe something by giving them a good reason

Etymology

Originally from Scottish English, 'black' + 'mail' (payment), with 'mail' from Old Norse 'mal' (agreement, payment).

Synset POS: v
