to decouple

1. VERB

/ diˈkʌpəl / (AmE), / diˈkʌp(ə)l / (BrE)
to reduce or eliminate the electrical connection or influence between two circuits or parts
separate electrical circuits or parts

Examples

The engineer decided to decouple the two circuits to prevent interference.
You should decouple the receiver from the transmitter for better signal clarity.
In this design, a capacitor is used to decouple the power supply from the amplifier.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disconnect - to separate the connection between two or more things
  • Antonym: couple - to connect together, especially electrically or mechanically

Etymology

From de- (expressing reversal) + couple (from Old French 'coupler', to link together).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00472354-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈkʌpəl / (AmE), / dɪˈkʌpəl / (BrE)
to consider or treat two or more things as separate or unconnected
to separate or disconnect

Examples

Many economists argue we should decouple economic growth from environmental impact.
The company's management tried to decouple its reputation from the scandal.
It's important to decouple emotions from important business decisions.

Synset Examples

you must dissociate these two events!
decouple our foreign policy from ideology

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dissociate - to regard as separate or unconnected
  • Synonym: disconnect - to separate or break the connection between things
  • Antonym: associate - to connect or link things in the mind or in reality

Etymology

From de- (removal, reversal) + couple, based on French découpler.

/ diˈkʌp.əl / (AmE), / diˈkʌp.əl / (BrE)
to disconnect or separate two or more things that have been joined or linked
to disconnect or separate

Examples

Engineers need to decouple the modules before performing maintenance.

  • Antonym: link - to make a connection or association

Etymology

4. VERB

/ diˈkʌpəl / (AmE), / dɪˈkʌpl / (BrE)
to eliminate or reduce the transmission of airborne shock waves from an explosive, often by isolating or separating its effects
eliminate shock waves from an explosive

Examples

Engineers designed the test site to decouple the explosion and prevent shock waves from harming nearby equipment.
Modern demolition techniques aim to decouple explosive blasts from surrounding air, reducing destructive shock.
Special enclosures are used to decouple fireworks explosions, minimizing the risk of injury from shock waves.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to isolate - to separate something from others so as to prevent interaction

Etymology

From de- (expressing reversal) + couple (from Old French 'couple', from Latin 'copula', meaning bond).
