ledger

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɛdʒɚ / (AmE), / ˈlɛdʒə / (BrE)
a book or other record in which financial accounts are recorded and maintained, especially in business or accounting
book of financial accounts

Examples

The accountant entered each transaction into the ledger before closing the books.
At the end of the quarter, the company's ledger showed a significant profit.
Each department is responsible for keeping its own ledger to track expenses.

Synset Examples

they got a subpoena to examine our books

Related Terms

  • Synonym: journal - a daily record of financial transactions in accounting

Etymology

From Middle English 'leger' (a book that lies permanently in a place), from Old French, from Medieval Latin 'legere' (to lie, remain).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɛdʒər / (AmE), / ˈlɛdʒə / (BrE)
a book or other physical record used to keep permanent accounts of financial transactions
accounting book for financial records

Examples

The accountant recorded all expenses in the ledger at the end of each day.
After years of use, the company's old ledger was worn and filled with handwritten numbers.
He carefully balanced the accounts in his ledger before submitting the report.

Synset Examples

he bought a new daybook

Related Terms

  • Synonym: journal - a daily record of business transactions
  • Synonym: daybook - a book in which daily transactions are recorded

Etymology

From Middle English 'leggen' (to lay, place), influenced by Old English and Middle Dutch roots.

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɛdʒər / (AmE), / ˈlɛdʒə / (BrE)
A book or other record in which financial accounts are recorded and summarized.
record book for financial accounts

Examples

Our accountant keeps a detailed ledger to track all business transactions.
The company discovered a discrepancy in the ledger during the annual audit.
She checked the ledger to ensure all credits and debits matched.

Synset Examples

they got a subpoena to examine our books

Related Terms

  • Synonym: account book - a book in which accounts are kept
  • Degree Lower: journal - a daily record, especially of financial transactions

Etymology

From Middle English *leggen* (to lay) and Old English *lecgan*, relating to the sense of 'laid' or 'set down' accounts.
