to colligate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑləˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒlɪˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to consider or link a particular instance as part of a general rule or principle
regard as part of a general rule

Examples

It is common in scientific research to colligate individual observations under general laws.
The theory seeks to colligate diverse phenomena within a single explanatory framework.
Linguists often colligate words into grammatical categories based on shared features.

Etymology

From Latin colligatus, past participle of colligare meaning 'to bind together', from com- 'together' + ligare 'to bind'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.lɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ɪˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to bring together information, facts, or evidence and connect them by a logical or causal relationship
to link facts or evidence logically

Examples

The researcher tried to colligate different findings to form a coherent theory.
It is often difficult to colligate disparate data into a unified explanation.
The detective was unable to colligate the clues into a convincing narrative.

3. VERB

/ kəˈlɪɡeɪt / (AmE), / kəˈlɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to collect or group together (facts, observations, etc.) in a systematic way; to link or connect information logically
group facts or observations systematically

Examples

Researchers must colligate data from multiple sources before drawing conclusions.
The scientist attempted to colligate the results into a unified theory.
In his report, he managed to colligate different observations into a coherent summary.

Synset Examples

From Latin colligare (to bind together), from col- (together) + ligare (to bind).
