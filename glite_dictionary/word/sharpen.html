to sharpen

1. VERB

/ ˈʃɑr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to become sharp or sharper
become sharp or sharper

Examples

As the discussion progressed, opinions began to sharpen on both sides.
The image on the screen slowly sharpened into focus.
Competition in the market tends to sharpen during the holiday season.

Synset Examples

The debate sharpened

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dull - to become less sharp or blunt

Etymology

From sharp + -en, Old English scearp (sharp) plus -en (verb-forming suffix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00393016-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈʃɑːr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to raise the pitch of (a musical note) by a semitone or to make a note sharper.
raise the pitch of a note

Examples

The conductor asked the violinists to sharpen the note for better harmony.
If you sharpen this C, it becomes a C sharp.
Some singers unintentionally sharpen the pitch during a performance.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to flatten - to lower the pitch of a musical note
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00394100-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈʃɑːrpən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑːpən / (BrE)
to make something clearer, more defined, or more precise
make more precise or clear

Examples

The editor asked us to sharpen the focus of our proposal before submission.
In order to win the debate, she needed to sharpen her arguments.
You should sharpen your presentation so that your key points stand out more clearly.

Synset Examples

We had to sharpen our arguments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refine - to improve by making small changes; to make more precise
  • Antonym: blur - to make less clear or distinct

Etymology

From Middle English 'sharpen', from 'sharp' + '-en'.
