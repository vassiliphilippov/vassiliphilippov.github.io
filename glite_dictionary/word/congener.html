congener

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːn.dʒə.nɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.dʒɪ.nə / (BrE)
a minor chemical substance in wine or liquor that contributes to its distinctive character, often affecting flavor or aroma
minor chemical affecting liquor character

Examples

Higher levels of congeners in whiskey are believed to contribute to its complex flavor profile.
Some people find that drinks with fewer congeners result in milder hangovers.
The distillation process can influence the variety and concentration of congeners in spirits.

Etymology

From Latin 'congener' (of the same kind), from 'con-' (together) + 'genus' (kind).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːn.dʒɪ.nɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.dʒɪ.nə / (BrE)
a person or thing of the same kind, class, or category as another
thing or person of the same kind

Examples

The Asian elephant is a congener of the African elephant, both belonging to the same family.
Unlike its North American congener, this species prefers tropical climates.
The wine's European congener has a distinctly different flavor profile.

Synset Examples

lard was also used, though its congener, butter, was more frequently employed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterpart - a person or thing corresponding to another in a different place or situation

Etymology

From Latin congener (of the same kind), from con- (together) + genus (kind, race).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːnˌdʒɛnər / (AmE), / ˈkɒnˌdʒɛnə / (BrE)
an animal or plant belonging to the same genus or closely related to another; a member of the same group sharing a common ancestry
organism of same genus or close relation

Examples

The African lion and the leopard are congeners belonging to the genus Panthera.
Botanists discovered a new congener of the well-known oak tree in the rainforest.
Several species of frogs in this region are congeners sharing a similar habitat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relative - organism connected by common ancestry

Etymology

From Latin 'congener' (of the same kind), from 'con-' (together) + 'genus' (kind, class).
