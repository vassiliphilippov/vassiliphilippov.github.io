involute

1. ADJ

/ ˈɪn.vəˌlut / (AmE), / ˈɪn.və.ljuːt / (BrE)
(of petals or leaves) having the edges or margins rolled inward, especially when in bud.
with edges rolled inward (botany)

Examples

The rosebud’s involute petals protect its delicate interior before blooming.
Some plants have involute leaves that unroll as they mature.
The botanist noted the involute shape of the fern’s young fronds.

Etymology

From Latin involutus, past participle of involvere (to roll in, envelop).

2. ADJ

/ ˈɪn.vəˌlut / (AmE), / ˈɪn.və.ljuːt / (BrE)
(of certain shells) closely coiled so that the axis is hidden or not visible.
closely coiled (shells, axis obscured)

Examples

The ammonite fossil displays an involute shell, with the whorls tightly wound and the center hidden.
Unlike evolute shells, involute shells show no visible central axis.
The involute form makes it difficult to see the inner coils of the sea snail's shell.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: evolute - (of shells) coiled so that the whorls are not closely coiled and the axis is visible

Etymology

From Latin involutus, from involvere ‘to roll in’.
