to co-opt

1. VERB

/ koʊˈɑpt / (AmE), / kəʊˈɒpt / (BrE)
to appoint or elect someone as a member of a group, committee, or organization, often by the decision of existing members rather than by general vote
appoint as a member; elect into a group

Examples

The board decided to co-opt a new member with experience in finance.
She was co-opted onto the advisory committee due to her expertise.
The council may co-opt additional members when necessary.

Synset Examples

The church members co-opted individuals from similar backgrounds to replenish the congregation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to appoint - to assign someone to a position or office
  • Synonym: elect - choose someone for a position by vote
  • Antonym: to exclude - to deny someone membership or participation

Etymology

From Latin cooptare (to choose together, to elect), from co- (together) + optare (to choose).

2. VERB

/ koʊˈɑpt / (AmE), / kəʊˈɒpt / (BrE)
to neutralize, win over, or assimilate individuals or groups by making them part of an established group or system, especially to eliminate opposition or dissent
neutralize by assimilation into a group

Examples

Several youth activists were co-opted onto the board to help guide the agency’s programs.
The regime tried to co-opt its critics by offering them government posts.
Our company often co-opts innovative startups rather than competing with them directly.

Synset Examples

We co-opted the independent minority tribes by pulling them into the Northern Alliance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to absorb - to take in or assimilate, especially into a group or system
  • Antonym: to marginalize - to treat a person or group as insignificant or peripheral

Etymology

From Latin cooptare, meaning 'to choose together or elect as a colleague'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02541764-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ koʊˈɑpt / (AmE), / kəʊˈɒpt / (BrE)
to appoint someone to a role or group summarily, often without the usual procedure or to commandeer for a purpose.
appoint or commandeer someone/something

Examples

The organization decided to co-opt several experts onto the planning committee without a formal vote.
During the crisis, the government attempted to co-opt volunteers into emergency services.
Rebel forces co-opted local resources to support their campaign.

Synset Examples

The army tried to co-opt peasants into civil defence groups

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commandeer - to officially take possession or control of something, especially for military purposes

Etymology

From Latin cooptare (to choose together), from co- (together) + optare (to choose).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02402408-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ koʊˈɑpt / (AmE), / kəʊˈɒpt / (BrE)
to take or adopt (an idea, method, etc.) for one's own use, often without permission or acknowledgment
adopt for one's own use

Examples

Many political movements have tried to co-opt the language of their opponents to gain broader appeal.
The company sought to co-opt innovative startups to expand its portfolio.
She managed to co-opt the original idea and present it as her own.

Synset Examples

He co-opted the criticism and embraced it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: appropriate - take something for one's own use, often without permission
  • Synonym: to usurp - take (a position of power or importance) illegally or by force

Etymology

From Latin cooptare (to elect, choose, or admit), from co- (together) + optare (to choose).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
