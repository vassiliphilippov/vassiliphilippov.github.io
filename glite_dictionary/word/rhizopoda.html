Rhizopoda

1. NOUN

/ raɪˈzɑːpədə / (AmE), / raɪˈzɒpədə / (BrE)
A group of protozoans characterized by their creeping movement and typically including amoebas and foraminifers.
group of creeping protozoans

Examples

Scientists discovered new species within the Rhizopoda while examining soil samples from the rainforest.
Amoebas are well-known members of the Rhizopoda, distinguished by their shape-changing movement.
Fossils of Rhizopoda, such as foraminifers, help geologists date sedimentary rock layers.

Etymology

From Greek rhiza (root) + pous, podos (foot), referring to their root-like projections.

2. NOUN

/ raɪˈzɑːpədə / (AmE), / raɪˈzɒpədə / (BrE)
A subclass of protozoans characterized by their ability to move and capture food by extending pseudopodia, including amoebas and foraminifers.
a subclass of protozoan organisms

Examples

Scientists observed several Rhizopoda under the microscope, noting the extension of their pseudopodia.
The ocean floor is rich with fossils of ancient Rhizopoda, especially foraminifers.
Many introductory biology courses include the study of Rhizopoda as examples of protozoan diversity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amoeba - single-celled protozoan with flexible shape, common member of Rhizopoda
  • Synonym: foraminifer - single-celled marine protozoan with a calcareous shell, member of Rhizopoda

Etymology

From Greek 'rhiza' (root) + 'pous, podos' (foot), referring to root-like pseudopodia.
