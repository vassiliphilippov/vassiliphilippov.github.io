alluvion

1. NOUN

/ əˈluːvi.ən / (AmE), / əˈluːvɪən / (BrE)
The gradual addition of land to a shore or bank, typically by the action of water depositing sediment or by the sea receding.
gradual addition of land by water
legal

Examples

The sudden alluvion created fertile land along the riverbank.
Disputes can arise when alluvion changes property boundaries over time.
The coastline expanded due to the gradual process of alluvion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alluvium - soil or material deposited by running water

Etymology

From Latin 'alluvio', from 'alluere' meaning 'to wash against'.

2. NOUN

/ əˈluːviən / (AmE), / əˈluːvɪən / (BrE)
the overflowing of a body of water onto normally dry land, especially as a natural process
overflowing of water onto land

Examples

The river's alluvion transformed the normally arid fields each spring.
After the alluvion, the banks were covered with nutrient-rich silt.
Certain legal disputes arose over land created by the gradual alluvion of the river.

Synset Examples

plains fertilized by annual inundations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inundation - an overwhelming flood of water
  • Synonym: flood - an overflow of a large amount of water beyond its normal limits
  • Antonym: drought - a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall

Etymology

From Latin alluvio (a washing against, flood), from ad- (to) + luere (to wash).

3. NOUN

/ əˈluːviən / (AmE), / əˈluːviən / (BrE)
Soil, clay, silt, sand, or gravel carried by flowing water and deposited, especially in a riverbed or on a floodplain.
material deposited by flowing water

Examples

During the flood, thick alluvion was deposited onto the fields along the riverbank.
Much of the delta owes its fertility to layers of ancient alluvion.
Construction was delayed because the site was covered in loose alluvion from previous floods.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alluvium - deposited material left by flowing water

Etymology

From Latin alluvio, alluvionis (an overflow, washing against).
