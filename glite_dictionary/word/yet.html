yet

1. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
used after a superlative adjective or adverb to emphasize that something is the most or best so far
used after superlatives for emphasis

Examples

It was the most successful concert yet for the band.
This is their darkest movie yet.
The test results are the highest yet recorded in the lab.

Synset Examples

this is the best so far
the largest drug bust yet

Etymology

From Old English *giet* (still, besides, furthermore).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00028594-r
Synset POS: r

2. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
at some time in the future; eventually or in due course, although it has not happened so far
at some time in the future

Examples

The best part of the book is yet to come.
He hasn't made a decision yet, but he will soon.
Many discoveries are yet to be made in this field.

Synset Examples

he longed for the flowers that were yet to show themselves
sooner or later you will have to face the facts
in time they came to accept the harsh reality

Related Terms

  • Synonym: still - continuing to happen or be the case up to now

Etymology

From Old English *giet*, *ġiet*, meaning 'yet, still'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00048179-r
Synset POS: r

3. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
to a greater degree or extent; used to emphasize a comparative, especially in set phrases such as 'a yet sadder tale' or 'yet another problem'.
to a greater degree (in comparisons)

Examples

The team faced yet another unexpected challenge before the project was complete.
Her voice grew yet softer as she shared the sad news.
It was yet more evidence that the problem was widespread.

Synset Examples

looked sick and felt even worse
an even (or still) more interesting problem
still another problem must be solved
a yet sadder tale

Related Terms

  • Synonym: even - to a greater degree; used with comparisons
  • Synonym: still - to a greater degree; used in comparisons
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00018101-r
Synset POS: r

4. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
Used to introduce a contrast with what has already been mentioned; nevertheless or in spite of that.
in spite of that; nevertheless

Examples

She was tired, yet she stayed up late to finish her project.
The weather was terrible, yet they continued with the picnic.
He has little experience; yet he performed exceptionally well during the interview.

Synset Examples

although I'm a little afraid, however I'd like to try it
while we disliked each other, nevertheless we agreed
he was a stern yet fair master
granted that it is dangerous, all the same I still want to go

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nevertheless - in spite of that; nonetheless
  • Synonym: however - used to introduce a statement that contrasts with or seems to contradict something that has been said previously
  • Synonym: still - nevertheless; in spite of that

Etymology

From Old English giet, gēat, of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00027761-r

5. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
up to the present time; so far; until now
up to now; so far

Examples

I haven't finished the book yet, but I'm almost done.
We are yet to receive confirmation from the organizers.
The decision has yet to be announced by the committee.

Synset Examples

I have yet to see the results
details are yet to be worked out

Related Terms

  • Synonym: still - up to and including the present or the time mentioned
  • Antonym: already - before the present time or earlier than expected

Etymology

From Old English *giet* or *gieta* (yet, still), related to Old Frisian *jit*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00028191-r
Synset POS: r

6. ADV

/ jɛt / (AmE), / jɛt / (BrE)
(used with negatives) up to the present time; so far; at this point in time.
up to now, so far (in negatives)

Examples

He hasn't finished his homework yet, so he's still working on it.
The package has not arrived yet, but it should be here soon.
She hasn't decided on a college yet, but she's considering several options.

Synset Examples

So far he hasn't called
the sun isn't up yet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: still - used to express up to and including the present or a specified time, especially when something continues to happen

Etymology

From Old English *giet*, *ġiet* (still, yet), similar forms appear in other Germanic languages.
