Saracen

1. NOUN

/ ˈsærəsən / (AmE), / ˈsærəs(ə)n / (BrE)
(historically) a member of the nomadic people of the Syrian and Arabian deserts at the time of the Roman Empire, later used in Europe to refer broadly to Muslims, especially during the Middle Ages.
historical term for an Arab or Muslim of the early Middle Ages
archaic

Examples

The medieval chronicles often described battles with the Saracen armies in the Holy Land.
During the Crusades, European knights encountered fierce resistance from Saracen warriors.
Legends of chivalry sometimes feature a noble Saracen as an adversary or ally.

Etymology

From Late Latin Saracenus, from Greek Sarakēnos.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsærəsən / (AmE), / ˈsærəsən / (BrE)
(historical) A member of any of the Arab peoples, especially as referred to by Europeans during the Middle Ages.
historical term for an Arab or Muslim

Examples

Medieval Christian writers often described their enemies as Saracens.
The Crusaders fought against the Saracens for control of the Holy Land.
In the poem, the knight battled both dragons and Saracens.

Etymology

From Latin 'Saracenus', from Greek 'Sarakēnos', of uncertain origin, used by Europeans in the Middle Ages for Arabs and Muslims.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsærəsən / (AmE), / ˈsærəsən / (BrE)
(historically) a member of a group of Muslims, especially those who opposed the Crusaders in the Middle Ages.
historical term for a Muslim during the Crusades
archaic

Examples

Medieval knights fought against the Saracen armies during the First Crusade.
The legend describes a brave Saracen who defended his city from invaders.
In many old texts, the term Saracen was used to refer to Muslim warriors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Muslim - a follower of the religion of Islam

Etymology

From Old French sarrazin, from Late Latin Saracenus, from Greek Sarakēnos.
