to allegorise

1. VERB

/ əˈlɛɡəˌraɪz / (AmE), / əˈlɛɡəraɪz / (BrE)
to interpret or present something as an allegory; to make into an allegory
interpret or make into an allegory
british

Examples

The author chose to allegorise the events of her childhood in the novel.
Many critics allegorise this poem to find a deeper political meaning.
Over centuries, scholars have allegorised these myths to reflect changing values.

Synset Examples

The story was allegorized over time

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to allegorize - to interpret or treat as an allegory; American spelling variant

Etymology

From Greek allēgorein (to speak allegorically), via Latin allegorizare. British spelling variant of 'allegorize'.

2. VERB

/ əˈlɛɡəˌraɪz / (AmE), / əˈlɛɡəraɪz / (BrE)
to interpret or represent something as an allegory; to explain or present by means of allegory
interpret as an allegory
british

Examples

The author chose to allegorise the historical events through the actions of the main characters.
Some readers allegorise ancient myths to find hidden philosophical meanings.
The film can be allegorised as a critique of modern society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to allegorize - interpret as an allegory (American spelling)

Etymology

From Greek 'allēgorein' (to speak allegorically), via late Latin 'allegorizare'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
