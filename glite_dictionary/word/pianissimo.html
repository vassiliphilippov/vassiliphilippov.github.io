pianissimo

1. ADV

/ piːəˈnɪsɪˌmoʊ / (AmE), / piːəˈnɪsɪməʊ / (BrE)
(in music) played or to be played very softly.
very softly (music direction)

Examples

The piece begins pianissimo, creating a delicate and intimate atmosphere.
Please play the final bars pianissimo for a gentle ending.
In the score, the passage marked pianissimo demands great control from the performer.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fortissimo - a direction in music; to be played very loudly
  • Degree Higher: piano - a direction in music; to be played softly

Etymology

Italian, from 'piano' (soft) + superlative suffix '-issimo'.

2. NOUN

/ piˌɑːnɪˈsɪmoʊ / (AmE), / piˌænɪˈsɪməʊ / (BrE)
(in music) a direction to play very softly; the passage of music to be performed at a very low volume.
(music) very soft or quiet passage

Examples

The composer marked this section pianissimo to create a sense of delicate calm.
The orchestra played the final bars pianissimo, barely above a whisper.
The haunting melody begins at pianissimo and gradually grows louder.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: forte - (music) loud or strong; direction to play loudly
  • Degree Higher: piano - (music) softly; a direction to play softly
  • Degree Higher: mezzo-forte - (music) moderately loud

Etymology

From Italian 'pianissimo', superlative of 'piano' (soft).

3. ADJ

/ piˌænɪˈsɪmoʊ / (AmE), / piˌanɪˈsɪməʊ / (BrE)
(in music) very soft or quiet; a direction to play or sing very softly.
very soft (music direction)

Examples

The violinist played the final notes pianissimo, creating a moment of delicate beauty.
Please begin the piece pianissimo and gradually increase the volume.
Her voice dropped to a pianissimo to convey a sense of intimacy in the song.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fortissimo - (in music) very loud; a direction to play or sing very loudly
  • Degree Lower: piano - (in music) soft; a direction to play or sing softly

Etymology

From Italian 'pianissimo', superlative of 'piano' (soft).
