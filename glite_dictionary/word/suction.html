suction

1. NOUN

/ ˈsʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˈsʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of drawing in or removing something by creating a partial vacuum
the act of drawing in by vacuum

Examples

The dentist used suction to remove excess saliva from the patient's mouth.
Strong suction allowed the vacuum cleaner to pick up all the dust.
The octopus attached itself to the glass with powerful suction.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aspiration - the act of drawing in by suction, especially in medical contexts

Etymology

From Latin 'suctio(n-)' from 'sugere' (to suck).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌkʃən / (AmE), / ˈsʌkʃən / (BrE)
the force that draws fluid or objects into a space by creating a lower pressure inside than outside
force caused by a pressure difference

Examples

The surgeon used suction to remove fluids from the wound.
A vacuum cleaner creates suction to pick up dust and dirt.
He felt a slight suction as the cup was pulled away from his skin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vacuum - space entirely devoid of matter, often creates suction

Etymology

From Latin suctio (sucking), from sugere (to suck).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11537927-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈsʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ˈsʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
to empty or clean a cavity or wound by using suction, especially as a medical procedure
remove fluid or material using suction (medical)
medical

Examples

The surgeon suctioned the excess fluid from the patient’s abdomen during the operation.
After the dental procedure, the assistant carefully suctioned the patient's mouth to remove saliva.
Nurses are trained to suction secretions from a patient’s airway to keep it clear.

Synset Examples

suction the uterus in an abortion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aspirate - to draw fluid by suction from a vessel or cavity, especially in medicine

Etymology

From Latin 'suctio', from 'sugere' meaning 'to suck'.
