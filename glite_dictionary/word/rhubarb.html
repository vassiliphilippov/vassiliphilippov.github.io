rhubarb

1. NOUN

/ ˈruːˌbɑrb / (AmE), / ˈruːˌbɑːb / (BrE)
A plant with thick, reddish or green leafstalks that are edible when cooked, while its broad leaves are poisonous.
plant with edible stalks, poisonous leaves

Examples

The chef stewed the rhubarb with sugar to make a delicious tart filling.
Only the stalks of rhubarb should be eaten; the leaves are toxic to humans.
She grows rhubarb in her backyard for pies and jams every spring.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *reubarbarum*, from Greek *rha barbaron* meaning 'foreign rhubarb'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈruː.bɑrb / (AmE), / ˈruː.bɑːb / (BrE)
The long, pinkish, sour stalks of a plant, usually cooked and sweetened for use in pies, desserts, or preserves.
sour, pinkish edible stalk

Examples

My grandmother made a delicious pie using fresh rhubarb from the garden.
You need to add plenty of sugar because rhubarb is naturally tart.
The market sells bunches of bright red rhubarb in early spring.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'reubarbarum', from Greek 'rha barbaron', meaning 'foreign rhubarb'.
