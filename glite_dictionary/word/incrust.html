to incrust

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
to decorate or cover lavishly with a layer of something, especially gems or ornamental material
decorate or cover lavishly

Examples

The gold crown was incrusted with brilliant emeralds and rubies.
The artisan incrusted the box with intricate patterns of mother-of-pearl.
She incrusted her dress in jewels for the gala event.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encrust - to cover or decorate with a hard layer or coating

Etymology

From Latin incrustare (to encrust), from in- (in, on) + crusta (crust).

WordNet ID: oewn-01519987-v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
(of a substance) to form a crust or hard outer layer on a surface.
form a hard outer layer

Examples

Minerals can incrust the walls of the cave, creating sparkling surfaces.
Over time, the pipes began to incrust with layers of limescale.
Salt deposits slowly incrust the shoreline, leaving a white rim along the water.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encrust - to cover or become covered with a hard coating or crust

Etymology

From Latin 'incrustare', from 'in-' (in, on) + 'crusta' (crust).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00255965-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
to cover or coat (something) with a hard outer layer or crust
cover with a hard crust

Examples

The pipes incrust with minerals over time, reducing water flow.
Salt began to incrust the rocks near the shoreline after weeks of drying sun.
Over the years, barnacles incrusted the hull of the old ship.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encrust - to cover or coat with a crust

Etymology

From Latin 'incrustare' (to cover with crust), via French 'incruster' and Italian 'incrostare'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
