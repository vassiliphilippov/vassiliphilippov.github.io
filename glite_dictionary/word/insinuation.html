insinuation

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˌsɪnjuˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˌsɪnjuˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
an indirect or subtle suggestion, often of something unpleasant or discreditable
an indirect, often negative, suggestion

Examples

The article was filled with insinuation rather than clear evidence against the politician.
Her insinuation that he had cheated was deeply hurtful.
He denied the insinuation that his company had acted unethically.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hint - an indirect or subtle suggestion
  • Synonym: innuendo - an allusive or oblique remark or hint, typically suggestive or disparaging
  • Antonym: accusation - a charge or claim that someone has done something illegal or wrong

Etymology

From Latin 'insinuatio', from 'insinuare' (to introduce by windings or curvings, to suggest), related to 'sinuous'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˌsɪnjuˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˌsɪnjʊˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of gaining acceptance or affection for oneself by using subtle or indirect means, often involving flattery or manipulation
subtle method to gain favor

Examples

She viewed his compliments as a form of insinuation to gain her trust.
He advanced in the company through careful insinuation, never open confrontation.
Many politicians are skilled in the art of insinuation to win over supporters.

Synset Examples

she refused to use insinuation in order to gain favor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ingratiation - the act of making oneself favored or acceptable by flattery or trying to please

Etymology

From Latin insinuatio (an introducing, winding in), from insinuare (to wind into, creep in, ingratiate).
