to annul

1. VERB

/ əˈnʌl / (AmE), / əˈnʌl / (BrE)
to declare officially that something, especially a law, agreement, or marriage, is invalid and no longer in effect
declare legally invalid, cancel officially

Examples

The court decided to annul the previous judgment due to new evidence.
They are seeking to annul their marriage in court.
The board can annul any regulation that conflicts with state law.

Synset Examples

He revoked the ban on smoking
lift an embargo
vacate a death sentence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to invalidate - to make something no longer legally valid
  • Synonym: revoke - to officially cancel a law, order, or agreement
  • Antonym: to ratify - to make an agreement officially valid

Etymology

From Latin 'annullare', from 'ad-' (to) + 'nullum' (nothing).

2. VERB

/ əˈnʌl / (AmE), / əˈnʌl / (BrE)
to officially declare something, especially a law, contract, or marriage, to be no longer valid; to cancel formally
declare invalid; cancel officially
legal

Examples

The court decided to annul the marriage due to fraud.
If either party breaches the agreement, we may annul the contract.
The government moved quickly to annul the disputed election results.

Synset Examples

The contract was annulled
void a plea

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to invalidate - to make something invalid or void
  • Synonym: void - to declare something to have no legal force
  • Antonym: to validate - to confirm or make legally valid

Etymology

From Latin *annullare* (to make to nothing), from *ad-* (to) + *nullus* (nothing, none).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02483482-v
Synset POS: v
