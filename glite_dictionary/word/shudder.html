shudder

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃʌdər / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
a sudden, brief feeling of fear, excitement, or pleasure that makes your body shake slightly
brief feeling causing a shake

Examples

A shudder passed through the crowd when the lights suddenly went out.
He felt a shudder of anticipation as the curtain rose.
The memory of the accident sent a shudder down her spine.

Synset Examples

a frisson of surprise shot through him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frisson - a sudden, strong feeling of excitement or fear

Etymology

From Middle English shoderen, from Old English scūderen (to shudder, tremble).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07535351-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈʃʌdɚ / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
to shake or tremble suddenly and violently, often from fear, revulsion, or cold
to shake or tremble suddenly

Examples

She began to shudder as the icy wind blew through the open window.
He couldn't help but shudder at the thought of entering the cold water.
The old house made me shudder with its eerie silence.

Synset Examples

The children are shivering — turn on the heat!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shiver - to tremble typically from cold or fear

Etymology

From Middle English 'shoderen', related to Middle Dutch 'schuderen' (to shake).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00014194-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈʃʌdɚ / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
an involuntary shiver or tremor, often caused by fear, cold, or sudden emotion
an involuntary shiver or tremor

Examples

A sudden shudder ran through him as he stepped into the icy water.
The old house gave a shudder as the wind whipped around it.
She tried to suppress the shudder that crept up her spine at the memory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tremor - an involuntary shaking or trembling movement
  • Synonym: shiver - a sudden tremble or shake, often from cold or fear

Etymology

From Middle English shoderen, related to Middle Dutch schudden (to shake).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00349627-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈʃʌdər / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
to tremble suddenly and uncontrollably, especially as a result of fear, excitement, or disgust
tremble uncontrollably (from emotion or sensation)

Examples

She felt a chill run down her spine and began to shudder with fear.
The audience shuddered at the terrifying scene in the movie.
He couldn't help but shudder when he heard nails scraping on a chalkboard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tremble - to shake involuntarily, especially as a result of anxiety or excitement
  • Synonym: quake - to shake or vibrate, often from shock or fear
  • Antonym: freeze - to become suddenly motionless, often from fear (opposite reaction)

Etymology

Middle English, related to Low German schuddern (to shiver).

Source: WordNet
