to tergiversate

1. VERB

/ tərˈdʒɪvərˌseɪt / (AmE), / təˈdʒɪvəseɪt / (BrE)
to abandon one's beliefs, principles, or allegiances, often in a way that is seen as evasive or indecisive
abandon one's beliefs or allegiances

Examples

Under questioning, the politician began to tergiversate, avoiding a direct answer and sidestepping his earlier commitments.
After years of loyalty, she chose to tergiversate and join the rival faction.
Some critics accused the author of tergiversating on his long-held philosophical views.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to apostatize - to renounce a religious or political belief

Etymology

From Latin tergiversari 'to turn one's back, to shift, to evade responsibility', from tergum 'back' + versare 'to turn'.

2. VERB

/ ˈtɜr.dʒɪ.vɚ.seɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɜː.dʒɪ.və.seɪt / (BrE)
to be deliberately unclear or ambiguous in order to mislead or avoid giving a direct answer
be deliberately ambiguous to mislead

Examples

The politician continued to tergiversate when pressed for a clear answer.
Rather than admit his mistake, he chose to tergiversate during the discussion.
She was known to tergiversate whenever she was asked uncomfortable questions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to prevaricate - to speak or act in an evasive way
  • Synonym: to equivocate - to use ambiguous language to conceal the truth

Etymology

From Latin tergiversari (to turn one's back, to evade).
