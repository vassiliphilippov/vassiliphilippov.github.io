supersonic

1. ADJ

/ ˌsuː.pɚˈsɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / ˌsuː.pəˈsɒ.nɪk / (BrE)
(of a speed or object) greater than the speed of sound in a particular medium, especially air.
faster than the speed of sound

Examples

The jet reached supersonic speeds during its test flight.
A supersonic boom can startle those on the ground when a plane breaks the sound barrier.
Many modern fighter jets are capable of supersonic travel.

Synset Examples

a supersonic bomber flies so fast that it must release its bombs while the target is still over the horizon

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subsonic - slower than the speed of sound
  • Degree Higher: hypersonic - at speeds much greater than the speed of sound

Etymology

From Latin super- (above) + sonic (relating to sound).

2. ADJ

/ ˌsuːpərˈsɑːnɪk / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəˈsɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of a sound or frequency) higher than the upper audible limit of human hearing; ultrasonic.
inaudibly high frequency

Examples

Bats are capable of emitting supersonic frequencies to navigate in the dark.
Medical imaging often relies on supersonic waves to create detailed pictures of the inside of the body.
Certain industrial devices use supersonic vibrations for cleaning delicate items.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ultrasonic - having frequencies above the audible range; beyond human hearing

Etymology

From Latin super (above) + sonic (relating to sound), formed in English based on the model of 'ultrasonic'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00176561-s
Synset POS: s
