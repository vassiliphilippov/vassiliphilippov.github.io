monition

1. NOUN

/ məˈnɪʃən / (AmE), / məˈnɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(Law) A formal summons issued especially in admiralty or ecclesiastical courts, directing all parties concerned to show cause why judgment should not be granted as requested.
legal summons to show cause
legal

Examples

The court issued a monition requiring interested parties to present their claims.
After receiving the monition, the defendants prepared their legal arguments.
Failure to respond to the monition may result in a default judgment.

Etymology

From Latin monitionem (a warning or admonition), from monere (to warn).

2. NOUN

/ məˈnɪʃən / (AmE), / məˈnɪʃən / (BrE)
a formal warning or cautionary advice about something, especially imminent danger or unpleasantness
formal warning; cautionary advice

Examples

The government issued a monition to the public about severe weather conditions arriving over the weekend.
She took his monition seriously and avoided the risky shortcut home.
Ignoring the teacher's monition resulted in several students being caught unprepared.

3. NOUN

/ məˈnɪʃən / (AmE), / məˈnɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
a formal or firm warning; a strong admonition or rebuke, especially in legal or ecclesiastical contexts
formal or strong warning; rebuke

Examples

The judge issued a monition to the witness about the consequences of perjury.
Despite several monitions from her supervisor, she continued to arrive late to work.
The council's monition was clear: failure to comply would result in serious penalties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: admonition - a warning or reprimand
  • Synonym: reprimand - a formal expression of disapproval
  • Antonym: encouragement - the act of giving support, confidence, or hope

Etymology

From Latin 'monitio' (a warning), from 'monere' (to warn).
