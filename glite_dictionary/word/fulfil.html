to fulfil

1. VERB

/ fʊlˈfɪl / (AmE), / fʊlˈfɪl / (BrE)
to make something that has been planned, promised, or expected happen or be done; to carry out or put into effect
carry out or put into effect
british

Examples

It is important to fulfil your promises if you want to build trust.
The government failed to fulfil its election pledges.
She fulfilled her duties as team leader with great dedication.

Synset Examples

carry out a task
execute the decision of the people
He actioned the operation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to carry out - to perform or complete something
  • Synonym: to execute - to put a plan or order into effect

Etymology

From Old English fullfyllan, from full (“full”) + fylian (“to fill, make full”).

2. VERB

/ fʊlˈfɪl / (AmE), / fʊlˈfɪl / (BrE)
to meet or satisfy a requirement, condition, need, or expectation
meet or satisfy a requirement or need
british

Examples

The candidate did not fulfil all the requirements for the scholarship.
The organization aims to fulfil the needs of the local community.
She was proud to fulfil her promise to her parents.

Synset Examples

does this paper meet the requirements for the degree?
This job doesn't match my dreams
meet a need

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to satisfy - to meet the requirements, needs, or expectations of
  • Synonym: meet - to fulfill or satisfy (a requirement, condition, etc.)
  • Antonym: to violate - to break or fail to comply with a rule or requirement

Etymology

From Old English 'fulfyllan', from 'ful' (full) + 'fyllan' (to fill).

3. VERB

/ fʊlˈfɪl / (AmE), / fʊlˈfɪl / (BrE)
to achieve or satisfy the requirements or expectations of something
to meet requirements or expectations

Examples

Her performance did not fulfil the judges' expectations.
The company failed to fulfil its promise to improve working conditions.
He hopes this new job will fulfil his professional ambitions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to satisfy - to meet the requirements or desires of
  • Antonym: to disappoint - to fail to meet expectations or hopes

Etymology

From Old English 'fullfyllan' (to fill full), influenced by Old French 'fulfiller'.
