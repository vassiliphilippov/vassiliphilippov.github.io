motile

1. ADJ

/ ˈmoʊt̬əl / (AmE), / ˈməʊtaɪl / (BrE)
(of cells, spores, or microorganisms) capable of spontaneous movement.
able to move (biology, microbiology)

Examples

The motile sperm cells are responsible for fertilization.
Certain bacteria are motile and use flagella to move through liquids.
Under the microscope, you can see the motile protozoa swimming actively.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: immotile - incapable of movement (biology)

Etymology

From Latin 'motilis' (capable of motion), from 'movere' (to move).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊtəl / (AmE), / ˈməʊtaɪl / (BrE)
A person whose dominant mental imagery is based on bodily movement or inner sensations of action, especially in psychological or educational analysis.
person with kinesthetic mental imagery

Examples

The psychologist found that her client was a motile, often recalling experiences through sensations of movement.
Unlike the visualizers in the group, motiles learned best through hands-on activities.
She identified herself as a motile, explaining that she thinks in terms of physical actions rather than images or sounds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: visualizer - a person whose mental imagery is mainly visual
  • Antonym: audile - a person whose mental imagery is mainly auditory

Etymology

From Latin 'motilis' (capable of movement), from 'movere' (to move); used in psychology since the late 19th century.
