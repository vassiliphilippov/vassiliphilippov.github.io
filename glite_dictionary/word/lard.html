lard

1. VERB

/ lɑrd / (AmE), / lɑːd / (BrE)
to prepare or cook (food) with lard, especially by adding lard as a fat or using it as a cooking medium.
prepare or cook with lard

Examples

The chef decided to lard the roast with strips of bacon for added flavor.
Traditionally, they would lard the pie crust to make it flaky and rich.
Before baking, you should lard the meat to keep it moist during cooking.

Synset Examples

lard meat

Etymology

From Old French 'larde', from Latin 'lardum' (fat, bacon).

2. NOUN

/ lɑrd / (AmE), / lɑːd / (BrE)
soft white fat obtained from hogs, used in cooking and baking
hog fat used in cooking

Examples

She used lard instead of butter to make the pie crust extra flaky.
The old recipe calls for a spoonful of lard melted in the pan.
Cooks once preferred lard for frying because of its high smoke point.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shortening - a fat used in cooking, usually to make pastry crumbly
  • Degree Lower: butter - a dairy product used as a spread or in cooking

Etymology

From Old French ‘larre’, from Latin ‘lardum’ (fat, bacon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07688166-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ lɑrd / (AmE), / lɑːd / (BrE)
to embellish speech, writing, or conversation with additional details, especially in a way that adds flavor or interest
to embellish with details

Examples

She likes to lard her stories with dramatic details for effect.
The professor larded his lecture with historical anecdotes to engage the students.
His speech was larded with technical jargon that only experts could understand.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embellish - to make a story or statement more interesting by adding extra details
  • Degree Higher: to exaggerate - to make something seem larger, better, or worse than it really is

Etymology

Originally from Old French 'larder' (to stuff with lard), figurative use developed for adding embellishments.
