clamor

1. VERB

/ ˈklæmɚ / (AmE), / ˈklæmə / (BrE)
to make loud and insistent demands or protests, especially as a group
make loud demands or protests

Examples

The protestors clamored for better working conditions outside the government building.
Fans clamored to see the celebrity as she arrived at the event.
Reporters clamored for answers as soon as the announcement was made.

Synset Examples

he clamored for justice and tolerance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demand - ask for something forcefully
  • Degree Lower: request - ask for something politely

Etymology

From Latin 'clamare' (to shout, cry out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00776686-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈklæmɚ / (AmE), / ˈklæmə / (BrE)
to compel someone to do something by making persistent and loud demands
demand insistently, especially to persuade

Examples

The protesters clamored the management into reconsidering their decision.
Parents clamored the school board into extending the library hours.
The fans clamored the team into singing one more song after the game.

Synset Examples

They clamored the mayor into building a new park

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demand - to ask for forcefully or insistently
  • Synonym: to coerce - to persuade or force someone to do something by pressure

Etymology

From Latin 'clamare' (to cry out, shout).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02512573-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈklæmɚ / (AmE), / ˈklæmə / (BrE)
a loud and persistent noise or outcry, especially from a group of people expressing their opinions or demands
loud, persistent noise or outcry

Examples

The clamor of the protesters outside the building could be heard for blocks.
Despite the clamor in the auditorium, the speaker continued unperturbed.
The government acted quickly to address the clamor for reform.

Synset Examples

he ignored the clamor of the crowd

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uproar - a state of noisy confusion or excitement
  • Antonym: silence - absence of sound or noise

Etymology

From Latin 'clamor' (a shout, outcry), from 'clamare' (to cry out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07136826-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈklæmɚ / (AmE), / ˈklæmə / (BrE)
A loud and harsh noise; an uproar or outcry, often persistent and confusing.
a loud harsh or strident noise

Examples

The clamor from the construction site made it hard to concentrate on my work.
Suddenly, a clamor erupted in the hallway as the children rushed outside.
The clamor of sirens and shouting filled the city streets late into the night.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: din - a loud, unpleasant, and prolonged noise
  • Synonym: uproar - a loud or impassioned noise or disturbance
  • Antonym: silence - the complete absence of sound

Etymology

From Latin clamorem (shout, outcry), from clamare (to cry out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07392073-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈklæmər / (AmE), / ˈklæmə / (BrE)
to demand, shout, or proclaim something loudly and insistently
demand or shout noisily

Examples

The crowd clamored for the band to return to the stage.
Activists clamored their demands for immediate government action.
Reporters clamored questions at the mayor as she left the building.

Synset Examples

The delegates clamored their disappointment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demand - ask for something forcefully
  • Synonym: shout - utter a loud call or cry

Etymology

From Latin clamare (to cry out) via Old French clamer.
