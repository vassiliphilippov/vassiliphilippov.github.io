write-off

1. NOUN

/ ˈraɪt ˌɔf / (AmE), / ˈraɪt ɒf / (BrE)
the action of cancelling a bad debt or a worthless asset from the accounts; a deduction made when an asset is declared valueless
cancellation of a bad debt or worthless asset

Examples

The company recorded a large write-off after several customers defaulted on their loans.
Following the accident, the damaged vehicle was considered a total write-off by the insurance company.
Last year, the bank announced a write-off of its unpaid credit card balances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bad debt - a debt unlikely to be collected

Etymology

From the phrasal verb 'write off', meaning to remove from accounting records.

2. NOUN

/ ˈraɪt ˌɔf / (AmE), / ˈraɪt ɒf / (BrE)
an amount deducted from the book value of an asset in accounting, usually because it is no longer considered collectible or of value
accounting reduction in asset value

Examples

The company recorded a write-off of several million dollars for obsolete inventory.
After the audit, a large write-off was taken due to uncollectible debts.
The accountant explained the impact of the write-off on the financial statements.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: to write down - reduce the book value of an asset less severely than a write-off

Etymology

From 'write' and 'off,' originally referring to recording something as lost or cancelled in accounts.

Source: WordNet
