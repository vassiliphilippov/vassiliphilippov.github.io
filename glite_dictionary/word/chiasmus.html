chiasmus

1. NOUN

/ kaɪˈæzməs / (AmE), / kaɪˈazməs / (BrE)
a rhetorical or literary figure in which two or more clauses are related to each through a reversal of structures in order to make a larger point; the inversion in the second of two parallel phrases
inverted parallel structure in clauses

Examples

The famous line 'Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country' is a classic example of chiasmus.
In his essay, the writer skillfully employed chiasmus to emphasize his argument.
Understanding chiasmus can enhance one's appreciation for rhetorical devices in speeches and literature.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antimetabole - repetition of words in successive clauses in reverse grammatical order

Etymology

From Greek *chiasmos* (crossing, diagonal arrangement), from *chiazein* (to mark with an X, from Greek letter chi).
