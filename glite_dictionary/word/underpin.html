to underpin

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈpɪn / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈpɪn / (BrE)
to support or strengthen something, especially with evidence or authority; to make more certain or confirm
support or strengthen with evidence

Examples

The report was carefully underpinned by statistical data.
These theories are underpinned by years of scientific research.
Her conclusions were underpinned by solid evidence and clear logic.

Synset Examples

The stories and claims were born out by the evidence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance or backing to something
  • Antonym: to contradict - to assert the opposite, or deny the truth of something

Etymology

From under- (beneath) + pin (as in fastening or securing), originally referring to building supports.

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈpɪn / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈpɪn / (BrE)
to support or strengthen from beneath; to provide a firm foundation or basis for something
support or strengthen from beneath

Examples

The workers had to underpin the building to prevent it from collapsing.
Her thesis is underpinned by extensive research and evidence.
Strong family values often underpin a stable society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to bear the weight of something; to hold up

Etymology

From under + pin, originally in the sense of strengthening a structure by inserting supporting pins or timbers beneath.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01221482-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈpɪn / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈpɪn / (BrE)
to support or justify an idea, argument, or theory with evidence or reasoning
support or justify an idea or argument

Examples

Recent data underpins the claims made in the research paper.
Her analysis underpins the entire argument of the book.
Sound reasoning is needed to underpin your position in a debate.

Synset Examples

Research will underpin our arguments in the report.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance or approval to something; to back up
  • Antonym: to undermine - to weaken or lessen the effectiveness of something

Etymology

From Middle English 'underpynen', literally 'to pin or fasten underneath'.
