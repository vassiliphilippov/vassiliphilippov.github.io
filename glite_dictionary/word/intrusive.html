intrusive

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (BrE)
(of rock) formed by the forcible introduction of molten material into cracks within other rock layers.
geological: rock formed by magma forced into other rocks

Examples

Granite is often classified as an intrusive igneous rock.
The scientist explained how intrusive rocks differ from those formed on the surface.
Geologists found an intrusive layer cutting through the sedimentary strata.

Etymology

From Latin intrudere (to thrust in).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (BrE)
extending or thrusting inward into something; projecting inwards.
thrusting or projecting inward

Examples

An intrusive branch blocked the path through the garden.
The coastline was shaped by several intrusive arms of the sea.
The architect disliked the intrusive wall jutting out into the main hall.

Synset Examples

an intrusive arm of the sea

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extrusive - thrusting outward, projecting outwards

Etymology

From Latin *intrudere* (to thrust in) via Medieval Latin and French.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01355862-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːsɪv / (BrE)
tending to intrude or enter where one is not wanted or invited, especially in a way that disturbs someone’s privacy or peace.
tending to intrude or disturb

Examples

The reporter's intrusive questions made the celebrity uncomfortable.
Some advertisements can feel intrusive when they interrupt your online videos.
She apologized for being intrusive and promised to respect their privacy.

Synset Examples

she felt her presence there was intrusive

Related Terms

  • Synonym: invasive - tending to intrude aggressively or spread undesirably
  • Antonym: discreet - careful not to cause embarrassment or attract much attention

Etymology

From Latin 'intrudere' (to thrust in), via Medieval Latin 'intrusivus'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01354915-a
Synset POS: a
