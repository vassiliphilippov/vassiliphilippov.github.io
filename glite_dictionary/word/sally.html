sally

1. NOUN

/ ˈsæli / (AmE), / ˈsæli / (BrE)
A witty or clever remark, often made spontaneously in conversation.
witty or clever remark

Examples

Her unexpected sally during the meeting made everyone laugh.
He responded to criticism with a sharp sally that caught his opponents off guard.
The dinner was filled with light banter and the occasional sally from their witty host.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quip - a clever or witty remark
  • Synonym: jest - a joke or witty remark

Etymology

From Old French *saillie* (a sudden rush or leap), from *saillir* (to leap), ultimately from Latin *salīre* (to jump).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsæli / (AmE), / ˈsæli / (BrE)
a sudden outburst, excursion, or venture away from a usual course, often for adventure or exploration
a sudden excursion or venture

Examples

Her adventurous sally into the mountains inspired her friends to explore as well.
They laughed about their spontaneous sally into the unfamiliar part of town.
The writer's sally into journalism lasted only a few years before he returned to fiction.

Synset Examples

a sally into the wide world beyond his home

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excursion - a short journey or trip, especially for leisure
  • Synonym: expedition - a journey undertaken for a specific purpose

Etymology

From Old French saillie (a rushing forth), from saillir (to leap), from Latin salīre (to jump).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsæli / (AmE), / ˈsæli / (BrE)
a sortie made by troops in a besieged position in order to attack the besiegers
military sortie from besieged position

Examples

The defenders made a bold sally against the besieging army at dawn.
A sudden sally surprised the attackers and pushed them back from the fortress walls.
During the siege, several sallies were attempted to break the enemy's lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sortie - an attack made by troops coming out from a position of defense

Etymology

From Old French 'saillie' (a rushing forth, a sally), from 'sailir' (to leap), from Latin 'salīre' (to leap).
