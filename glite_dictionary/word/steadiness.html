steadiness

1. NOUN

/ ˈstɛd.i.nəs / (AmE), / ˈstɛd.i.nəs / (BrE)
the quality of being firm, reliable, and unwavering in resolve or behavior
firmness and reliability of character

Examples

Her steadiness during the crisis inspired confidence in those around her.
You can always rely on his steadiness in difficult situations.
The team's success was due in part to the steadiness of their captain.

Synset Examples

He trusted her clear steadiness that she would do what she said

Related Terms

  • Synonym: constancy - the quality of being unchanging and faithful
  • Antonym: fickleness - the state of being easily changed or unreliable in loyalty

Etymology

From steady + -ness; 'steady' from Middle English 'stedi', related to Old English 'stede' (place, position).

2. NOUN

/ ˈstɛdinəs / (AmE), / ˈstɛdinɪs / (BrE)
The quality of being steady, stable, or securely fixed in place, without moving or shaking.
quality of being steady or stable

Examples

The steadiness of her hands was remarkable during the surgery.
Pilots are trained to maintain steadiness when flying through turbulence.
He admired the steadiness of the old bridge, even after so many years.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: instability - lack of stability or steadiness
  • Synonym: stability - the state of being stable

Etymology

From steady + -ness, formed in English.

3. NOUN

/ ˈstɛdi.nɪs / (AmE), / ˈstɛd.i.nɪs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being steady, regular, and unchanging; consistency and reliability
the quality of being steady or consistent

Examples

The steadiness of her hand made her an excellent surgeon.
Investors appreciate the steadiness of his decision-making during turbulent times.
You could hear the steadiness in his voice as he gave his speech.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consistency - the quality of always behaving or performing in a similar way
  • Antonym: instability - the state of being likely to change or not firmly fixed

Etymology

From English 'steady' + '-ness', forming a noun indicating a state or quality.
