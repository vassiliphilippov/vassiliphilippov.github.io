adiaphoron

1. NOUN

/ ˌædiˈæfərɑn / (AmE), / ˌædiˈæfərɒn / (BrE)
A religious observance or practice regarded as neither commanded nor forbidden by sacred scriptures, and therefore considered optional or a matter of individual conscience.
ritual not required or forbidden by scripture

Examples

The council ruled that wearing specific colors of vestments was an adiaphoron and left it to local customs.
Some controversies in the church arise from misunderstandings about what qualifies as an adiaphoron.
Many reformers argued that festival observance was an adiaphoron rather than a doctrinal requirement.

Synset Examples

For all of the wild-eyed (and completely sectarian) assertions floating around the LCMS that ordination is a mere adiaphoron, that ‘laying on of hands’ does not mean ritual ordination but rather a democratic election, that ordination confers nothing - I have yet to ever read about anyone actually graduating seminary and taking a call to a congregation and choosing to forgo the rite of ordination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indifferent - having no particular interest or sympathy; unconcerned

Etymology

From Greek ἀδιάφορον (adiaphoron), meaning 'indifferent', from ἀ- (not) + διαφέρω (to differ).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæd.iˈæf.əˌrɒn / (AmE), / ˌæd.iˈæf.ərɒn / (BrE)
(in Stoic philosophy) a matter that is morally neutral, having neither moral merit nor demerit.
morally neutral matter (Stoic philosophy)

Examples

In Stoic philosophy, wealth is often considered an adiaphoron because it does not determine virtue.
The debate revolved around whether fame should be treated as an adiaphoron by true philosophers.
Some students struggled with the concept of adiaphoron during their ethics seminar.

Synset Examples

Esteem (or reputation, fame, glory) was for Stoics an adiaphoron, and though it could be classed as prolegomenon with a certain ‘value’, it was only after Diogenes' time that they conceded that its ‘value’ was more than instrumental.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indifferent - lacking moral importance; neutral (philosophical sense)

Etymology

From Greek adiaphoron (indifferent thing), from a- (not) + diaphoros (differing).
