ply

1. VERB

/ plaɪ / (AmE), / plaɪ / (BrE)
to join together layers, strands, or threads by twisting, weaving, or molding
join by twisting or weaving

Examples

She learned to ply fibers together to make sturdy rope.
The craftsman plied several layers of wood to create a strong board.
He spent the afternoon plying strips of fabric into a colorful rug.

Synset Examples

ply fabric

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weave - to form fabric by interlacing threads
  • Synonym: twist - to form into a spiral by winding together

Etymology

From French *plier* (to fold), from Latin *plicare* (to fold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01294420-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ plaɪ / (AmE), / plaɪ / (BrE)
a layer of material, such as cloth, paper, or wood, especially when used in combination to form a thicker or stronger structure
a layer in combined materials

Examples

This plywood is made of several plies glued together for extra strength.
The thread is a two-ply yarn, making it thicker and more durable than single-ply.
The roof was repaired with three plies of waterproof material to prevent leaks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: layer - a sheet, quantity, or thickness of material, typically one of several, covering a surface or body

Etymology

From Old French ploi (fold), from Latin plicare (to fold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03977242-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ plaɪ / (AmE), / plaɪ / (BrE)
one of the layers or strands twisted together to form yarn, rope, or thread; often used with a number to indicate thickness
a strand or layer in yarn, rope, or thread

Examples

The sweater is made from fine two-ply wool, making it both warm and lightweight.
She bought some thick four-ply yarn for her knitting project.
This rope consists of several strong plies twisted together for extra durability.

(of a vehicle, ship, or boat) to travel or sail regular routes, especially for commercial purposes or between fixed points.
