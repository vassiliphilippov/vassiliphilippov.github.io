seaward

1. NOUN

/ ˈsiːwərd / (AmE), / ˈsiːwəd / (BrE)
the direction that is toward the sea, especially from land
direction toward the sea

Examples

The fishermen gazed seaward as they prepared to launch their boat.
The cliff slopes gently seaward, giving a clear view of the open water.
We set up camp facing seaward to catch the morning breeze coming off the ocean.

Etymology

From Old English 'sǣ' (sea) + 'weard' (toward).

2. ADJ

/ ˈsiːwərd / (AmE), / ˈsiːwəd / (BrE)
facing, directed toward, or situated in the direction of the sea or coast, away from inland areas.
toward or facing the sea or coast

Examples

The balcony offers a stunning seaward view that captures the horizon.
They built a seaward wall to protect the village from storms.
The path took us along the seaward edge of the cliffs.

Synset Examples

from the hill he took a seaward course
on the seaward side of the road

Related Terms

  • Antonym: landward - facing or directed toward the land or inland

Etymology

From Middle English seward, from sea +‎ ward (direction toward).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00465613-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADV

/ ˈsiːwərd / (AmE), / ˈsiːwəd / (BrE)
towards the sea; in the direction of the sea.
towards the sea

Examples

The ship began to drift seaward as the tide came in.
They built the lighthouse on the most seaward point of the cliff.
A strong wind was blowing seaward all afternoon.

Synset Examples

the sailor looked seaward

Related Terms

  • Antonym: landward - towards the land; in the direction away from the sea

Etymology

From 'sea' + '-ward' (directional suffix).
