to postdate

1. VERB

/ poʊsˈdeɪt / (AmE), / pəʊsˈdeɪt / (BrE)
to occur, happen, or exist later than something else in time
to happen later than something else

Examples

Technological advances often postdate important scientific discoveries.
The renovations postdate the original construction by several decades.
Her findings postdate the initial experiment, providing new insight into the results.

Synset Examples

Tuesday always follows Monday

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to antedate - to occur earlier in time than something else
  • Synonym: to follow - to come after something in time or sequence

Etymology

From Latin 'post-' (after) + English 'date'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02718647-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌpoʊstˈdeɪt / (AmE), / ˌpəʊstˈdeɪt / (BrE)
to establish something, especially a document or event, as occurring at a date later than another specified date
to date as later than something else

Examples

You should not postdate checks, as banks may cash them immediately.
The letter appeared to postdate the official announcement by several days.
Historians postdate the construction of the wall compared to the original settlement.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to predate - to date as earlier than something else

Etymology

From Latin 'post' (after) + English 'date'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00278024-v
Synset POS: v
