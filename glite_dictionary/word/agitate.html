to agitate

1. VERB

/ ˈædʒɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈædʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to make someone feel excited, troubled, or stirred up, often with strong emotions
make excited or stirred up

Examples

The controversial announcement served to agitate the students, who began protesting immediately.
Her passionate speech agitated the crowd and inspired a wave of action.
Do not agitate the public with exaggerated claims.

Synset Examples

The speaker charged up the crowd with his inflammatory remarks

Related Terms

  • Antonym: calm - make someone quiet or less excited

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to put in motion, drive, agitate), frequentative of agere (to do, drive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01766380-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈædʒɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈadʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to move or cause something to move briskly back and forth or with irregular, rapid motion
move back and forth rapidly

Examples

You should agitate the solution for several minutes to ensure proper mixing.
The washing machine agitates the clothes during the wash cycle.
She agitated the paint can before opening it to make sure the color was even.

Synset Examples

The chemist shook the flask vigorously
My hands were shaking

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shake - to move violently back and forth or up and down
  • Synonym: stir - to mix with a circular motion or move slightly

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to set in motion, drive, excite), from agere (to do, act).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01893603-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈædʒɪteɪt / (AmE), / ˈadʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to move or stir something, especially so as to change its arrangement or position
stir or move to change position

Examples

You must agitate the mixture thoroughly before adding the other ingredients.
The scientist agitated the solution by swirling the flask gently.
If you agitate the sediment, it will disperse into the liquid.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stir - to mix or move around with a circular motion
  • Synonym: shake - to move back and forth or up and down with quick movements

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to put in motion, shake), frequentative of agere (to do, drive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01422127-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈædʒɪteɪt / (AmE), / ˈadʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to campaign actively and publicly for political or social change, or to advocate strongly for a particular cause
campaign or advocate for change

Examples

She began to agitate for better working conditions at the factory.
Several groups continue to agitate against the new law.
He has long agitated for social justice and equal rights.

Synset Examples

The liberal party pushed for reforms
She is crusading for women's rights
The Dean is pushing for his favorite candidate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: campaign - to work in an organized and active way toward a particular goal, especially political or social change
  • Synonym: crusade - to make an energetic and organized effort to achieve a social or political change
  • Antonym: to oppose - to actively resist or refuse to comply with something

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to set in motion, to drive).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ ˈædʒɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈædʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to try to arouse public concern or stir up public opinion, often with the aim of achieving political or social change
stir up public opinion

Examples

Activists began to agitate for climate change policies after the report was released.
Some political groups agitate against new legislation they consider unjust.
He has spent years agitating for improved working conditions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: campaign - work in an organized and active way toward a particular goal, especially a political or social one
  • Antonym: calm - make less agitated or upset; soothe public opinion

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to set in motion, disturb), frequentative of agere (to do, drive).
