reproach

1. VERB

/ rɪˈproʊtʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈprəʊtʃ / (BrE)
to express criticism or disappointment towards someone, especially for their actions or behavior
express criticism or disappointment

Examples

The manager reproached the team for missing the deadline.
She reproached him gently for forgetting her birthday.
Instead of helping, he just reproached me for making mistakes.

Synset Examples

The president reproached the general for his irresponsible behavior

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to criticize - to express disapproval or point out faults
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From Old French 'reprocher', from Vulgar Latin *repropare (to reproach), from Latin 'reprehendere' (to hold back, blame).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈproʊtʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈprəʊtʃ / (BrE)
The state of being in disgrace or shame, or an expression of disapproval or disappointment.
disgrace; state of shame

Examples

The scandal was a source of reproach for the entire organization.
She feared that her actions would bring reproach upon her family name.
Living in poverty is no reproach if one behaves with dignity.

Synset Examples

he brought reproach upon his family

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - Loss of reputation or respect, often as a result of a dishonorable action
  • Antonym: honor - High respect or esteem; the opposite of disgrace

Etymology

From Old French 'reproche', from reprocher (to blame), originally from Latin 'reprobare' (to disapprove, reject).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14465180-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈproʊtʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈprəʊtʃ / (BrE)
a mild rebuke or criticism; an expression of disapproval or disappointment
mild criticism or disapproval

Examples

Her voice was filled with reproach as she addressed her careless teammate.
The manager’s mild reproach made the staff aware of their mistake.
He looked at his friend with a glance of reproach after she broke her promise.

Synset Examples

words of reproach

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuke - an expression of sharp disapproval or criticism
  • Antonym: praise - expression of approval or admiration

Etymology

From Old French 'reproche' (blame, shame), from reprocher (to reproach), from Latin 'reprobare' (to disapprove, reject).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06726451-n
Synset POS: n
