jealousy

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɛləsi / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛl.ə.si / (BrE)
fierce vigilance in guarding or preserving something one possesses or values strongly
fierce vigilance over what is valued

Examples

The nation guarded its independence with jealousy throughout the centuries.
She watched over her family's reputation with a jealousy that brooked no slights.
Academic freedom is often preserved with jealousy by universities.

Synset Examples

cherish their official political freedom with fierce jealousy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vigilance - the action or state of keeping careful watch for possible danger or difficulties

Etymology

From Old French *jalousie* (jealousy), from Latin *zelosus* (full of zeal).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɛləsi / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛləsi / (BrE)
a feeling of resentment or anger caused by the belief that someone else has something or someone you want, especially a rival in love or success
resentment or envy toward a rival

Examples

She felt overwhelming jealousy when she saw her ex-boyfriend with someone new.
Sibling jealousy can arise when children feel they are competing for their parents' attention.
His jealousy was obvious whenever he saw his colleague receive praise at work.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: envy - a feeling of discontent or covetousness regarding another's advantages, success, or possessions
  • Antonym: admiration - respect and warm approval, often contrasted with resentment or envy

Etymology

From Old French jalousie, from jalous (jealous), from Late Latin zelosus (full of zeal)
