Sapindaceae

1. NOUN

/ ˌsæpɪnˈdeɪsiˌi / (AmE), / ˌsapɪnˈdeɪsɪˌiː / (BrE)
A family of chiefly tropical and subtropical trees and shrubs with leathery fruits, often producing toxic saponins, including the lychee and maple trees.
family of trees and shrubs including lychee and maple

Examples

The Sapindaceae family includes well-known plants such as maples and lychees.
Many Sapindaceae species are valued for their ornamental or edible fruits.
Some members of the Sapindaceae produce saponins, which can be toxic if ingested.

Etymology

From Latin *Sapindus*, a type genus in the family, combined with the standard botanical suffix *-aceae*.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsæpɪnˈdeɪsi.i / (AmE), / ˌsapɪnˈdeɪsɪ.iː / (BrE)
A family of chiefly tropical and subtropical deciduous and evergreen trees and shrubs, often bearing leathery drupes and producing toxic saponins.
a family of tropical and subtropical trees and shrubs
medical

Examples

The Sapindaceae family includes economically important species such as the lychee and the longan.
Botanists recently reclassified several genera into the Sapindaceae based on genetic studies.
Many members of the Sapindaceae contain saponins, making their seeds potentially toxic if ingested.

Etymology

From Latin Sapindus (soapwort tree) + -aceae (a standard suffix for plant family names).
