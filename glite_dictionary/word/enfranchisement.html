enfranchisement

1. NOUN

/ ɛnˈfræn.tʃaɪz.mənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz.mənt / (BrE)
the act of granting someone the rights of citizenship, especially the right to vote
granting of citizenship or voting rights

Examples

The enfranchisement of women was a milestone in the struggle for equality.
After years of activism, the group finally achieved enfranchisement for all citizens.
Debates over enfranchisement continue to shape voting laws around the world.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: disenfranchisement - the act of depriving someone of the right to vote or other rights
  • Synonym: emancipation - the act of being set free from legal, social, or political restrictions; liberation

Etymology

From Medieval French enfranchissement, from enfranchir (to set free), from Old French franc (free).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz.mənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪzmənt / (BrE)
the granting of the rights of citizenship, especially the right to vote, to a person or group
granting of citizenship or voting rights

Examples

The enfranchisement of women was a major milestone in the fight for equality.
Universal enfranchisement changed the nature of the country's political system.
Recent laws have furthered the enfranchisement of minority groups.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: disenfranchisement - the removal of the right to vote
  • Synonym: suffrage - the right to vote in political elections

Etymology

From Old French 'enfranchiss-', stem of 'enfranchir' (set free), from Latin 'francus' (free).

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz.mənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz.mənt / (BrE)
the act of granting freedom from political subjection or servitude, especially the right to vote or full citizenship
granting political freedom or rights

Examples

The enfranchisement of women dramatically reshaped political life in the twentieth century.
After centuries of subjugation, the new constitution guaranteed enfranchisement to all citizens.
Debates over enfranchisement were central to the country's struggle for independence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emancipation - the process of being set free from legal, social, or political restrictions
  • Antonym: disenfranchisement - the state of being deprived of a right, especially the right to vote

Etymology

From French 'enfranchissement', from 'enfranchir' (to set free), from 'franc' (free).
