to moralise

1. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈmɒrəlaɪz / (BrE)
to improve the morals or moral character of (someone or a group) through teaching or admonishment
improve someone's morals

Examples

The teacher tried to moralise her students by discussing the value of honesty.
Some parents believe it is their duty to moralise their children from a young age.
The film aims to moralise audiences without being overly preachy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preach - to give moral advice, especially in a self-righteous or tedious way

Etymology

From French moraliser, from Latin moralis (moral).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00385718-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈmɔrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈmɒrəlaɪz / (BrE)
to interpret or explain the moral meaning of something, especially a story or event
interpret the moral meaning of something
british

Examples

It is easy to moralise a fable by stating its lesson at the end.
Some critics moralise traditional myths to teach ethical values to children.
Authors often moralise stories by highlighting the consequences of the characters' actions.

Synset Examples

moralize a story

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to moralize - interpret or explain the moral meaning (American spelling)

Etymology

From French 'moraliser' and Medieval Latin 'moralisare', from Latin 'moralis' (moral).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00940652-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈmɒrəˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to speak in a way that expresses moral judgments or gives moral advice, especially in a self-righteous or tedious manner
to express moral judgments or preach
british

Examples

It's annoying when people moralise about the choices of others without understanding their situation.
She couldn't help but moralise after hearing about his reckless behavior.
If you keep moralising every discussion, people will stop listening to you.

Synset Examples

This man always sermonizes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sermonize - to preach or give a sermon; to speak in a moralizing way
  • Synonym: to preach - to give moral advice, often annoyingly or intrusively

Etymology

From Old French moraliser, from Late Latin moralizare (to explain in a moral sense) from Latin moralis (moral).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00830035-v
Synset POS: v
