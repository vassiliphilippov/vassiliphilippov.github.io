to outstay

1. VERB

/ aʊtˈsteɪ / (AmE), / aʊtˈsteɪ / (BrE)
to remain in a place longer than is acceptable, necessary, or desired
stay longer than is appropriate

Examples

He managed to outstay his welcome at the party, leaving hours after everyone else.
Tourists who outstay their visas may face legal consequences.
You shouldn't outstay your visit, especially when your hosts look tired.

Synset Examples

overstay or outstay one's welcome

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to overstay - to stay longer than allowed or intended

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'out-' meaning 'beyond' or 'more than' and the verb 'stay'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02643550-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ aʊtˈsteɪ / (AmE), / aʊtˈsteɪ / (BrE)
To remain in a place, situation, or contest longer than someone else; to surpass in staying power.
remain longer than others

Examples

The marathon runner managed to outstay all her rivals and won the race.
He hoped to outstay his colleagues in the exhausting negotiation.
The old oak tree seemed to outstay every other plant in the garden.

Synset Examples

They outstayed their competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outlast - to last longer than someone or something

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'out-' (meaning 'beyond' or 'more than') and 'stay.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02625371-v
Synset POS: v
