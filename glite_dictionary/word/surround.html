to surround

1. VERB

/ səˈraʊnd / (AmE), / səˈraʊnd / (BrE)
to enclose or envelop completely on all sides
enclose or envelop completely

Examples

The warmth of the blanket surrounded her, making her feel safe and comfortable.
Tall trees surround the cabin, giving it a feeling of seclusion from the outside world.
As soon as he stepped onto the stage, cheering fans surrounded him from every direction.

Synset Examples

smother the meat in gravy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to envelop - to enclose completely and cover on all sides
  • Synonym: to encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Antonym: to expose - to leave unprotected or uncovered

Etymology

From Old French 'suronder', from sur- (over) + onde (wave)

2. VERB

/ səˈraʊnd / (AmE), / səˈraʊnd / (BrE)
to enclose (a place) with a wall or other barrier for protection or fortification
enclose with a wall for protection

Examples

Medieval cities often surrounded themselves with thick stone walls to prevent invasions.
The general ordered his troops to surround the fortress with barricades for added security.
In ancient times, people would surround their settlements with ditches or walls to keep out wild animals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fortify - to strengthen a place by building defensive works

Etymology

From French *surroundre*, based on Old French *soronder* (to overflow) and influenced by *round* (circle).

4. VERB

/ səˈraʊnd / (AmE), / səˈraʊnd / (BrE)
to encircle a place or person, especially with hostile intent so as to force them to give up or surrender
to encircle in order to force surrender

Examples

The army surrounded the city, cutting off all supply routes.
During the war, the rebels surrounded the fortress to force its surrender.
Police officers surrounded the building, leaving the suspects with no way to escape.

Synset Examples

The Turks besieged Vienna

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to besiege - to surround a place with armed forces in order to capture it or force surrender

Etymology

From Old French 'suronder' and Middle English 'surrounden', meaning to overflow or flood, later taking on the sense of encircle.
