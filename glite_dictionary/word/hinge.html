hinge

1. VERB

/ hɪndʒ / (AmE), / hɪndʒ / (BrE)
to attach or join with a hinge so as to allow movement
attach with a hinge

Examples

The cabinet doors were carefully hinged to ensure they swung open smoothly.
You need to hinge the gate so that it will open and close properly.
We watched the carpenter hinging each panel onto the folding screen.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unhinge - to remove from hinges or make unstable

Etymology

From Old English 'hyngran' (to hang), later associated with the noun 'hinge'.

2. NOUN

/ hɪndʒ / (AmE), / hɪndʒ / (BrE)
a crucial circumstance or factor upon which later events or decisions depend
crucial factor on which later events depend

Examples

Trust is the hinge on which their relationship turns.
The outcome of the election became the hinge for policy change.
Her decision was the hinge that determined everyone’s next step.

Synset Examples

his absence is the hinge of our plan

Related Terms

  • Synonym: linchpin - a person or thing vital to an enterprise or organization
  • Synonym: pivot - the central or most important point or person

Etymology

From Middle English 'hynge', of uncertain origin.

3. NOUN

/ hɪndʒ / (AmE), / hɪndʒ / (BrE)
A joint or mechanism that connects two solid objects, allowing one to move relative to the other, typically used on doors, lids, or gates.
joint allowing objects to swing open or closed

Examples

The door creaked loudly because its hinge needed oiling.
She repaired the old chest by replacing a broken hinge on the lid.
A well-made hinge allows the cabinet door to open smoothly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pivot - a central point on which something turns

Etymology

From Middle English 'hynge', of unknown origin.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
