obscurantism

1. NOUN

/ əbˈskjʊrənˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ɒbˈskjʊərəntɪzəm / (BrE)
The practice or policy of deliberately making information or knowledge obscure or difficult to understand.
deliberate making of something obscure

Examples

The government's obscurantism made it difficult for the public to get accurate information.
Academic obscurantism can alienate outsiders from important discussions.
Some critics accuse the organization of obscurantism to avoid scrutiny.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand

Etymology

From French 'obscurantisme', from Latin 'obscurare' (to darken) + '-ism'.

2. NOUN

/ əbˈskjʊrənˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌɒbskjʊəˈræntɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
the practice or policy of deliberately preventing the facts or full details of something from becoming known; opposition to the spread of knowledge or enlightenment
deliberate prevention of knowledge or enlightenment

Examples

The regime's obscurantism kept the public in ignorance of scientific advancements.
Some critics accused the organization of obscurantism for withholding important information.
Throughout history, obscurantism has been used to protect those in power from scrutiny.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: enlightenment - the act of giving someone greater knowledge and understanding about a subject or situation
  • Synonym: censorship - the suppression or prohibition of speech or writing deemed subversive or objectionable

Etymology

From French 'obscurantisme', from Latin 'obscurare' (to darken).
