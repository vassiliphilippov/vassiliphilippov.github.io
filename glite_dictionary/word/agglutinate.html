agglutinate

1. ADJ

/ əˈɡluːtɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / əˈɡluːtɪˌneɪt / (BrE)
united or joined together as if by glue; stuck together
joined or stuck together

Examples

The agglutinate cells formed visible clumps under the microscope.
Certain bacteria produce substances that allow them to become agglutinate in clusters.
The agglutinate particles were difficult to separate due to their sticky surfaces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cohesive - characterized by or causing cohesion; tending to stick together
  • Antonym: dispersed - separated or spread out; not joined together

Etymology

From Latin agglutinatus, past participle of agglutinare (to glue to).

2. VERB

/ əˈɡluː.tə.neɪt / (AmE), / əˈɡluː.tɪ.neɪt / (BrE)
(of a language or its structure) to join or string together morphemes in a sequence so that each adds a distinct meaning.
to string together morphemes

Examples

Some languages agglutinate morphemes instead of using word order to express grammatical relations.
In Turkish, nouns often agglutinate several suffixes.
Linguists study how these languages agglutinate elements to convey nuances of meaning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to concatenate - to link things together in a series

Etymology

From Latin agglutinatus, past participle of agglutinare (to glue to).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ əˈɡluː.tənˌeɪt / (AmE), / əˈɡluː.tɪ.neɪt / (BrE)
(of cells or particles, especially in biology or medicine) to stick together and form clumps.
to clump or stick together
medical

Examples

When certain antibodies are present, the red blood cells may agglutinate rapidly in the test tube.
The bacteria began to agglutinate when exposed to the immune serum.
Incompatibility between blood types can cause donated blood to agglutinate dangerously.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulate - to change to a solid or semi-solid state, often referring to blood

Etymology

From Latin 'agglutinatus', past participle of 'agglutinare' (to glue to), from 'ad-' (to) + 'gluten' (glue).

Synset POS: v
