wretchedness

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɛtʃɪdnɪs / (AmE), / ˈrɛtʃɪdnɪs / (BrE)
the state or quality of being extremely poor, unhappy, or inferior
state of being poor or miserable

Examples

The wretchedness of the slums was visible in every broken window and crumbling wall.
After years of conflict, the nation's wretchedness seemed to have no end.
She escaped a life of wretchedness through education and determination.

Synset Examples

he has compiled a record second to none in its wretchedness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: misery - a state of great suffering and unhappiness
  • Antonym: affluence - the state of having a great deal of money; wealth

Etymology

From 'wretched' + '-ness'; 'wretched' attested from Old English *wrecca* (exile, outcast).

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛtʃɪdnəs / (AmE), / ˈrɛtʃɪdnɪs / (BrE)
A state of great unhappiness or suffering, often caused by poverty, misfortune, or affliction.
state of great misery or suffering

Examples

He could not bear the wretchedness he saw in the aftermath of the disaster.
The novel explores the wretchedness of life in the poorest districts.
Her wretchedness was clear from the sadness in her eyes.

Synset Examples

the misery and wretchedness of those slums is intolerable

Related Terms

  • Synonym: misery - a state of great suffering or discomfort
  • Antonym: bliss - a state of perfect happiness

Etymology

From Old English *wrecced* (wretched) + *-ness*.

3. NOUN

/ ˈrɛtʃɪdnəs / (AmE), / ˈrɛtʃɪdnɪs / (BrE)
the state or quality of being extremely unpleasant, miserable, or uncomfortable
state of extreme misery or discomfort

Examples

After losing his home, he sank into a deep wretchedness that colored every day.
They could not escape the wretchedness of poverty during the harsh winter.
The novel vividly describes the wretchedness of life in the overcrowded city.

Synset Examples

the wretchedness for which these prisons became known
the grey wretchedness of the rain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: misery - great unhappiness or suffering
  • Antonym: comfort - state of physical or mental ease and well-being

Etymology

From Old English wreccednes (misery, exile); related to "wretched."
