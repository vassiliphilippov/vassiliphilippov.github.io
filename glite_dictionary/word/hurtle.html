to hurtle

1. VERB

/ ˈhɝːtəl / (AmE), / ˈhɜːtəl / (BrE)
to throw or send something with great force and speed
throw or send forcefully

Examples

He hurtled the rock across the river in a single, powerful motion.
The children hurtled snowballs at each other during the winter storm.
She hurtled her bag into the corner of the room, frustrated after a long day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurl - to throw with great force

Etymology

From Middle English hurten (to knock, dash), probably of imitative origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈhɝː.təl / (AmE), / ˈhɜː.təl / (BrE)
to move or travel with great speed, often accompanied by a loud rushing sound
move or travel with great speed

Examples

The train hurtled down the track, making everyone in the station take notice.
A meteor hurtled through the sky, leaving a glowing trail behind it.
He hurtled around the corner on his bicycle, narrowly missing the mailbox.

Synset Examples

The cars hurtled by

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - move with speed and urgency
  • Antonym: slow - move at a low speed

Etymology

Probably from Middle English hurten (to strike) and hurt (injure), possibly influenced by hurtle (to clash). Exact origin uncertain.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02096450-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈhɜːrtəl / (AmE), / ˈhɜːtəl / (BrE)
to move or cause to move rapidly and forcefully, often with a rushing or violent motion
move rapidly and forcefully

Examples

The train continued to hurtle down the tracks despite the heavy rain.
A meteor seemed to hurtle through the night sky, leaving a bright trail.
She watched the skier hurtle down the mountain at breakneck speed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - to move with urgent haste
  • Synonym: plunge - to move or fall suddenly forward or downward

Etymology

Probably from Middle English hurtlen, frequentative of hurt (to knock, strike).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02066140-v
Synset POS: v
