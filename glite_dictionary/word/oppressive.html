oppressive

1. ADJ

/ əˈprɛsɪv / (AmE), / əˈprɛsɪv / (BrE)
(of rules, systems, or people) using power unjustly and harshly; inflicting excessive restrictions or control.
unjustly severe or harsh

Examples

The new tax laws were considered oppressive by many citizens.
She grew up under an oppressive regime that limited basic freedoms.
Workers protested the oppressive conditions in the factory.

Synset Examples

the oppressive government
oppressive laws
a tyrannical parent
tyrannous disregard of human rights

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tyrannical - using power in a cruel or arbitrary way
  • Antonym: lenient - tolerant and merciful; not severe

Etymology

From Medieval Latin oppressivus, from Latin opprimere (to press against, to oppress).

2. ADJ

/ əˈprɛsɪv / (AmE), / əˈprɛsɪv / (BrE)
(of a situation or atmosphere) weighing heavily on the mind or spirit and causing discomfort or distress.
weighing heavily on mind or spirit

Examples

The humidity made the summer air feel particularly oppressive.
She struggled under the oppressive silence that filled the room after the argument.
His grief created an oppressive sadness that lingered for months.

Synset Examples

the atmosphere was oppressive
oppressive sorrows

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stifling - (of atmosphere) making one feel constrained or suffocated
  • Antonym: uplifting - making people feel happier or more hopeful

Etymology

From Latin 'oppressus', past participle of 'opprimere' (to press against, overwhelm).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01193541-s
Synset POS: s
