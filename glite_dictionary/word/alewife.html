alewife

1. NOUN

/ ˈeɪlˌwaɪf / (AmE), / ˈeɪlˌwaɪf / (BrE)
a North American fish of the herring family (Alosa pseudoharengus), which migrates up rivers to spawn and is often salted or smoked as food.
a migratory North American food fish

Examples

During the spring, large numbers of alewife migrate upriver to spawn.
Smoked alewife was once a staple food in many coastal communities.
Fishermen noticed that the population of alewife had declined sharply over the decade.

Etymology

Originally referred to a female keeper of an alehouse; adopted for the fish, possibly due to its plump belly resembling an alewife's apron.

2. NOUN

/ ˈeɪlˌwaɪf / (AmE), / ˈeɪlˌwaɪf / (BrE)
The flesh of a North American shad-like fish (Alosa pseudoharengus) found along the Atlantic coast or in coastal streams, used as food.
flesh of North American shad-like fish

Examples

Fishermen along the coast prize the alewife for its rich flavor when smoked.
The restaurant served a seasonal dish made from fresh-caught alewife.
In early spring, schools of alewife migrate up the river, supporting a traditional fishery.

Etymology

From early colonial American English, possibly so named because it was common in streams near alehouses or due to the fish’s stout body resembling an alehouse keeper (‘wife’ meaning woman), though the exact origin is uncertain.
