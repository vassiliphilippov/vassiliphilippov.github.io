X-radiation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛks ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛks ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
Radiation in the form of X-rays, used for imaging structures inside the body or materials.
radiation in the form of X-rays
medical

Examples

The doctor used X-radiation to examine the patient's broken bone.
Researchers measured the intensity of X-radiation emitted from the source for safety.
Prolonged exposure to X-radiation should be avoided without proper protection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: X-ray - a form of electromagnetic radiation used in imaging

Etymology

Formed from 'X-ray' (from the symbol X, indicating unknown) and 'radiation'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɛks ˌɹeɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛks ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
Electromagnetic radiation with very short wavelengths, produced when high-speed electrons strike a solid target, commonly used in medical imaging and analysis.
short-wavelength electromagnetic radiation (X-rays)

Examples

The doctor explained how X-radiation helps to create images of bones and internal organs.
Modern airport scanners use X-radiation to inspect luggage for dangerous items.
Exposure to excessive X-radiation can be harmful, so protective measures are essential.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: X-ray - a form of electromagnetic radiation used in imaging and analysis
  • Degree Higher: gamma radiation - electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths shorter than X-rays

Etymology

Named after the 'X' denoting the unknown nature when discovered (1895) combined with 'radiation'.
