lysogenic

1. ADJ

/ ˌlaɪsəˈdʒɛnɪk / (AmE), / ˌlaɪsəˈdʒɛnɪk / (BrE)
(of a bacterium or virus) capable of producing or undergoing lysis, especially by incorporating genetic material that may later trigger cell destruction.
capable of producing or undergoing lysis
medical

Examples

The lysogenic cycle allows a virus to integrate its genome into the DNA of its host cell.
Certain bacteria can become lysogenic after infection by specialized bacteriophages.
The switch between the lytic and lysogenic phases depends on environmental signals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lytic - causing lysis or cell destruction

Etymology

From Greek 'lysis' (loosening) + '-genic' (producing).

2. ADJ

/ ˌlaɪsəˈdʒɛnɪk / (AmE), / ˌlɪsəˈdʒɛnɪk / (BrE)
Relating to or involving lysogeny, the process by which a virus integrates its genetic material into the host cell's genome.
relating to lysogeny (virus integration)
medical

Examples

Certain bacteriophages follow a lysogenic cycle, integrating their DNA into the host cell.
The lysogenic pathway allows the virus to remain dormant for extended periods.
Research on lysogenic viruses has advanced our understanding of gene regulation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lytic - relating to lysis (breaking down of a cell by viral infection)

Etymology

From Greek 'lysis' (loosening, dissolution) and '-genic' (producing or caused by).
