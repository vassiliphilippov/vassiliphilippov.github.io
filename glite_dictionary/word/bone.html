bone

1. VERB

/ boʊn / (AmE), / bəʊn / (BrE)
to remove the bones from (meat, fish, or poultry) before cooking or serving
remove bones from meat or fish

Examples

The chef carefully boned the fish before baking it.
It takes skill to bone a chicken without tearing the meat.
She asked the butcher to bone the leg of lamb for the meal.

Synset Examples

bone the turkey before roasting it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to debone - to remove the bones from meat, fish, or poultry

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ boʊn / (AmE), / bəʊn / (BrE)
the hard, porous, calcified tissue forming the skeleton of vertebrates
hard tissue forming skeleton

Examples

The archaeologist discovered a fragment made of bone dating back thousands of years.
Whale bone was once used to make corsets and other items.
The doctor explained how new bone grows to repair a fracture.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cartilage - firm, flexible connective tissue found in various forms in the body

Etymology

From Old English 'bān' (bone).

/ boʊn / (AmE), / bəʊn / (BrE)
a shade of white resembling the color of bleached bones, often used in describing paint, fabric, or other materials
a shade of white (resembles bleached bones)

Examples

She painted the kitchen walls a subtle bone to make the space feel brighter.
The suit is available in classic black, navy, and a crisp bone.
Their dining table was set with elegant bone linen napkins.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ivory - a shade of white with a slight yellowish tinge, named after ivory tusks

Etymology

4. NOUN

/ boʊn / (AmE), / bəʊn / (BrE)
a hard, rigid connective tissue that forms the skeleton of vertebrate animals
hard tissue of the skeleton

Examples

The X-ray showed a fracture in the bone of his arm.
Calcium is important for keeping your bones strong and healthy.
Archaeologists discovered ancient bones buried beneath the ground.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: cartilage - flexible connective tissue found in joints and some body parts

Etymology

From Old English 'bān' (bone).
