safeguard

1. VERB

/ ˈseɪfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈseɪfˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
to protect something or someone from harm or danger; to make safe
protect from harm or danger

Examples

The new regulations are designed to safeguard public health.
Parents do their best to safeguard their children from dangers online.
We must safeguard our environment for future generations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to protect - to keep someone or something safe from harm or injury

Etymology

Formed from 'safe' and 'guard', influenced by Middle English and Old French origins.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01140528-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈseɪfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈseɪfˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
to escort or accompany someone in order to protect them and ensure their safety
escort to protect

Examples

The police were assigned to safeguard the witnesses as they traveled to court.
A security team will safeguard the ambassador during the event.
Soldiers safeguarded the convoy through the dangerous region.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to protect - to keep someone or something safe from harm or injury

Etymology

From safe + guard, influenced by Middle English *saufgard*, from Old French *sauvegarde*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02030173-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈseɪfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈseɪfɡɑːd / (BrE)
a measure taken to protect someone or something from possible danger, harm, or loss
precautionary measure for protection

Examples

Installing smoke detectors is an important safeguard against fire in the home.
The company implemented new safeguards to protect users' private information.
Wearing a helmet should be a basic safeguard for anyone riding a bicycle.

Synset Examples

he put an ice pack on the injury as a precaution
an insurance policy is a good safeguard
we let our guard down

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precaution - an action taken to prevent harm or danger
  • Synonym: protection - the act of keeping someone or something safe
  • Antonym: hazard - a danger or risk

Etymology

From safe + guard, influenced by Old French sauve-garde (protection).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00824631-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈseɪfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈseɪfˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
a document or escort granting safe passage, especially through a region during a time of war
safe-conduct document or protection in war

Examples

The general issued a safeguard so the envoys could cross enemy lines unharmed.
Travelers needed a safeguard to ensure their safe passage through the war-torn territory.
The king's army provided a safeguard for the diplomats as they traveled to negotiations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: safe-conduct - an official document or assurance guaranteeing safe passage

Etymology

From safe + guard; 'safe' from Old French sauf, from Latin salvus (unharmed), and 'guard' from Old French garder (to guard).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06704012-n
Synset POS: n
