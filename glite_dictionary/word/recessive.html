recessive

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈsɛsɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛsɪv / (BrE)
(of a gene or allele) producing its effect in an individual only when two copies of the gene are present, i.e., not masked by a dominant allele.
(of gene) expressed only when two identical alleles are present
medical

Examples

Blue eyes are often caused by the recessive gene for eye color, which must be inherited from both parents.
Cystic fibrosis is a recessive genetic disorder, requiring two copies of the faulty gene to develop the condition.
The recessive trait was not visible in the first generation but reappeared in the next.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dominant - (of a gene) producing its effect even when only one copy is present

Etymology

From Latin 'recessus' (a going back, retreat), reflecting the idea of being held back or not expressed unless unopposed.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈsɛsɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈsɛsɪv / (BrE)
An allele that only affects the organism's traits if it is paired with an identical allele, not with a dominant allele.
genetics: allele expressed only when paired identically
medical

Examples

Cystic fibrosis is caused by inheriting two recessives, one from each parent.
A person with one dominant and one recessive for a trait will express the dominant.
Some genetic disorders appear only when the recessive is inherited from both parents.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dominant - an allele that expresses its trait regardless of the other allele

Etymology

From Latin 'recessus' (a going back) via French 'récessif', used in genetics since the 19th century.
