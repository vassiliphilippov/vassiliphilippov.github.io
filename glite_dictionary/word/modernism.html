modernism

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɑːdərˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmɒdənɪzəm / (BrE)
A style or movement in art, literature, and architecture that makes a deliberate break with traditional forms and conventions, especially those prevailing in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
art/literature movement breaking from tradition

Examples

Many artists in the early 20th century embraced modernism to challenge traditional artistic values.
The gallery features works from the key figures of modernism in painting and sculpture.
Virginia Woolf and James Joyce are often cited as leaders of literary modernism.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: postmodernism - artistic movement emerging as a reaction to and development from modernism

Etymology

From Latin 'modernus' (modern) + '-ism'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɑdərˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmɒdənɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
the ideas, styles, and practices typical of modern society or thought, especially as distinct from traditional or classical approaches
practices typical of modern times

Examples

Some critics believe that modernism disrupted centuries-old artistic traditions.
The rapid changes in city life were seen as symbols of modernism.
Her writing reflects the spirit of modernism that swept through Europe in the early 20th century.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: traditionalism - adherence to traditional ideas or practices

Etymology

From modern + -ism; modelled on French modernisme.
