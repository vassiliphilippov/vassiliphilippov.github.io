tammy

1. NOUN

/ ˈtæmi / (AmE), / ˈtæmi / (BrE)
A plain-woven fabric, often glazed, made of wool or a blend of wool and cotton, formerly used especially for linings, garments, and curtains.
type of plain-woven woolen or wool-cotton fabric
archaic

Examples

The dress was lined with a soft tammy fabric to provide extra warmth.
They used green tammy for the curtains in the parlor, giving the room a gentle sheen.
In historical reenactments, tammy is often used to replicate period-authentic clothing.

Etymology

From French 'tammy', from obsolete French 'estamet' (type of cloth), from Medieval Latin 'stamettum'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtæmi / (AmE), / ˈtæmi / (BrE)
A round, flat woolen cap of Scottish origin, typically with a pompom in the center, also known as a tam o'shanter.
Scottish woolen cap

Examples

My grandmother always knits me a tammy every winter to keep my head warm.
He wore a traditional tartan tammy when visiting the Scottish Highlands.
The little girl pulled her tammy down over her ears to guard against the cold wind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tam o'shanter - a round, flat Scottish woolen cap, often with a pompom; alternate full form of tammy

Etymology

Shortened from 'tam o'shanter,' after the character in Robert Burns' 1790 poem.
