cudweed

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌdˌwid / (AmE), / ˈkʌdwiːd / (BrE)
any plant of the genus Filago, characterized by clusters of small, woolly flower heads often found in dry, sandy soils.
a plant of the genus Filago with woolly flowers

Examples

We noticed patches of cudweed growing along the roadside.
Some gardeners deliberately plant cudweed for its unique woolly appearance.
Cudweed thrives in poor, sandy soil and requires minimal maintenance.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkʌdˌwid / (AmE), / ˈkʌdˌwiːd / (BrE)
A plant of the genus Gnaphalium or related genera, with cottony-white foliage and flowers, commonly found in dry habitats.
a cottony-white plant of the genus Gnaphalium

Examples

The dry hillsides were covered in cudweed after the spring rains.
Gardeners sometimes plant cudweed as a ground cover for its unique texture.
We found a patch of cudweed growing along the dusty roadside.

Etymology

From the practice of feeding it to cattle as a substitute when cud was lacking.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkʌdˌwid / (AmE), / ˈkʌd.wiːd / (BrE)
any of various plants of the genus Gnaphalium, known for their flowers which can be dried without losing their form or color
a plant of the genus Gnaphalium

Examples

The dry, papery flowers of cudweed make it popular for floral arrangements.
We found several patches of cudweed growing along the country path.
Some gardeners plant cudweed as a decorative ground cover in dry areas.

Etymology

From 'cud' (food chewed by cattle) + 'weed', possibly referring to the plant's soft, woolly texture resembling chewed cud.
