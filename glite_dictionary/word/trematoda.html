Trematoda

1. NOUN

/ trɛməˈtoʊdə / (AmE), / trɛməˈtəʊdə / (BrE)
A class of parasitic flatworms, commonly known as flukes, that infect various vertebrates.
class of parasitic flatworms (flukes)

Examples

The life cycle of Trematoda often involves multiple hosts, including snails and mammals.
Researchers discovered several new species within the Trematoda class in freshwater environments.
Veterinarians must be aware of Trematoda infections in livestock to ensure proper treatment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fluke - A parasitic flatworm of the class Trematoda

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek *trēmatōdēs* (full of holes), from *trēma* (hole).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtrɛməˈtoʊdə / (AmE), / ˌtrɛm.əˈtəʊ.də / (BrE)
a class of parasitic flatworms known as flukes, which often infest the internal organs of vertebrates
a class of parasitic flatworms (flukes)
medical

Examples

Liver flukes are a common type of Trematoda that infect cattle and sheep.
Researchers study the life cycle of Trematoda to better understand parasitic diseases.
The Trematoda have complex reproductive systems and often require multiple hosts.

Etymology

From New Latin *Trematoda*, from Greek *trēmatōdēs* (full of holes), referring to the suckers of the flukes.
