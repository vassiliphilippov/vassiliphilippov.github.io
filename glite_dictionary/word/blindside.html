to blindside

1. VERB

/ ˈblaɪndˌsaɪd / (AmE), / ˈblaɪndˌsaɪd / (BrE)
to attack, hit, or surprise someone from the side where their view is obstructed or they are unprepared
attack or surprise from an unprotected side

Examples

The defender managed to blindside the quarterback, causing a fumble.
She was completely blindsided by the sudden announcement of layoffs.
During the match, one player tried to blindside his opponent, but the referee spotted the foul.

Etymology

From 'blind' + 'side', originally used in American football to describe an unprotected side.

2. VERB

/ ˈblaɪnˌsaɪd / (AmE), / ˈblaɪn(d)sʌɪd / (BrE)
to surprise someone unpleasantly or harmfully by doing something unexpected
to surprise and harm unexpectedly

Examples

The financial crisis blindsided many small business owners.
She was completely blindsided by the news of her colleague's resignation.
The new policy changes blindsided most employees, leaving them unprepared.

Synset Examples

The economic downturn blindsided many investors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambush - attack by surprise
  • Antonym: to warn - safeguard from surprise or danger by informing

3. VERB

/ ˈblaɪnd.saɪd / (AmE), / ˈblaɪnd.saɪd / (BrE)
to surprise or shock someone, especially in a way that they do not expect
to surprise or shock unexpectedly

Examples

The announcement of the merger completely blindsided the employees.
She felt blindsided by the criticism during the meeting.
The company was blindsided by the sudden drop in sales.

Synset Examples

I didn’t expect him to blindside me with that question.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surprise - to cause someone to feel sudden wonder or amazement
  • Synonym: shock - to cause someone to feel upset or disturbed unexpectedly

Etymology

Formed from 'blind' + 'side', originally from American football meaning to tackle an opponent from their blind side.
