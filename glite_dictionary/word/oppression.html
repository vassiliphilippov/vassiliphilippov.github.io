oppression

1. NOUN

/ əˈprɛʃən / (AmE), / əˈprɛʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
A feeling of being weighed down or burdened, especially with anxiety or discomfort.
feeling of being weighed down

Examples

She felt a sudden oppression in her chest as the anxiety grew.
The oppressive heat left everyone with a sense of oppression and fatigue.
During the storm, a strong oppression seemed to fill the air, making it hard to breathe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distress - extreme anxiety, sorrow, or pain
  • Antonym: relief - a feeling of reassurance and relaxation following release from anxiety or distress

Etymology

From Old French 'oppression', from Latin 'oppressio' (a pressing down).

2. NOUN

/ əˈprɛʃ.ən / (AmE), / əˈprɛʃ.ən / (BrE)
The exercise of authority or power in a burdensome, cruel, or unjust manner; the state of being subject to such treatment.
cruel or unjust use of authority

Examples

Throughout history, many societies have suffered under oppression imposed by authoritarian leaders.
The novel explores the enduring effects of oppression on the human spirit.
International organizations often speak out against political oppression wherever it occurs.

Synset Examples

the tyrant's oppression of the people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persecution - persistent cruel or unfair treatment of a person or group
  • Antonym: liberation - the act of setting someone free from imprisonment or oppression

Etymology

From Latin oppressio(n-), from opprimere 'to press against, crush'.

3. NOUN

/ əˈprɛʃən / (AmE), / əˈprɛʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the prolonged and unjust exercise of authority or power over a person or group, resulting in hardship or suffering
unjust exercise of authority causing suffering

Examples

Centuries of oppression have left deep scars on the community.
The novel explores the theme of oppression under a totalitarian regime.
Many fought courageously to end the oppression of their people.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: persecution - persistent harassment or mistreatment, especially for beliefs
  • Antonym: liberation - the act of freeing from oppression or imprisonment

Etymology

From Latin 'opprimere' (to press against, overwhelm), from 'ob-' (against) + 'premere' (to press).
