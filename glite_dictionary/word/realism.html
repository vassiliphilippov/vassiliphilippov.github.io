realism

1. NOUN

/ ˈriəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈrɪəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
(Philosophy) The doctrine that physical objects continue to exist independently of being perceived or thought about.
belief that objects exist independently of perception

Examples

Philosophers who support realism argue that objects exist even when no one is observing them.
The debate between realism and idealism has shaped much of modern philosophy.
Many scientists adopt realism as the foundation for their understanding of the physical world.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealism - the philosophical theory that reality is fundamentally mental or immaterial

Etymology

From French réalisme and Latin realis (actual), referring to the nature of what is real.

2. NOUN

/ ˈriːəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈrɪəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
the tendency to accept facts as they are and to prefer practical, literal approaches over idealism or abstraction
accepting facts and preferring practicality

Examples

Her realism helped the team make practical decisions during the crisis.
A healthy dose of realism is needed when planning such an ambitious project.
His realism prevented him from chasing unrealistic dreams.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealism - the practice of forming or pursuing ideals, often unrealistically
  • Synonym: pragmatism - a pragmatic, practical approach to problems and affairs

Etymology

From French réalisme, from réel (real), based on Latin realis (real).

3. NOUN

/ ˈriəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈrɪəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
(Philosophy) The doctrine that abstract concepts or universals exist independently of the mind and of their names.
philosophical doctrine that universals exist objectively

Examples

In medieval philosophy, realism argues that universals have a real existence beyond language and thought.
Debates between realism and nominalism shaped the development of Western metaphysics.
The professor traced the roots of realism back to ancient Greek thinkers like Plato.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominalism - the doctrine that universals exist only as names or mental concepts

Etymology

From French réalisme, from réel (real), from Latin realis (actual, real).

4. NOUN

/ ˈriːəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈrɪə.lɪ.zəm / (BrE)
an artistic movement that emerged in 19th-century France, characterized by the depiction of subjects as they appear in everyday life, without embellishment or idealization.
19th-century French artistic movement depicting reality

Examples

Gustave Courbet is often regarded as a leading figure in realism in French painting.
Many writers adopted realism to portray the struggles of ordinary people.
The shift to realism marked a significant break from the romantic idealism of previous art.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: naturalism - a related artistic movement emphasizing detailed, lifelike depiction often influenced by science

Etymology

From French réalisme, from réal (real) + -isme (-ism).
