cohesion

1. NOUN

/ koʊˈhiːʒən / (AmE), / kəʊˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
(Physics) The intermolecular force that holds together the molecules in a solid or liquid.
force holding molecules together

Examples

Water has strong cohesion due to hydrogen bonding between its molecules.
The cohesion of the material makes it difficult to break apart.
Surface tension is a result of molecular cohesion at the surface of a liquid.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: adhesion - the tendency of dissimilar particles or surfaces to cling to one another

Etymology

From Latin 'cohaesio(n-)' (a sticking together), from 'cohaerere' (to stick together).

3. NOUN

/ koʊˈhiʒən / (AmE), / kəʊˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
(Linguistics) the way in which words and structures are linked together within a text or sentence to hold it together as a meaningful whole.
grammatical and lexical linking in a text (linguistics)

Examples

The report lacked cohesion, making it difficult for readers to follow the argument.
Using pronouns and conjunctions can improve the cohesion of your writing.
Teachers emphasize the importance of cohesion when instructing students on essay structure.

Synset Examples

While lexical cohesion is obviously achieved by the selection of vocabulary, the other types of cohesion are considered as grammatical cohesion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coherence - the logical and orderly presentation of ideas in a text

Etymology

From Latin 'cohaesio' (a sticking together), from 'cohaerere' (to stick together).

4. NOUN

/ koʊˈhiʒən / (AmE), / kəʊˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
(Botany) The process by which normally separate parts of a plant, such as petals, grow together.
botanical process of plant parts growing together
medical

Examples

Some flowers display cohesion when their petals merge during development.
Botanists studied the cohesion of stamens in that species of lily.
Petal cohesion results in a tubular flower structure in certain plants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: adhesion - the process by which different plant organs stick together

Etymology

From Latin cohaesio (a sticking together), from cohaerēre (to cling together).
