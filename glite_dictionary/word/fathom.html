fathom

1. NOUN

/ ˈfæð.əm / (AmE), / ˈfað.əm / (BrE)
a unit of length equal to six feet (approximately 1.83 meters), chiefly used in measuring the depth of water
unit of water depth (six feet)

Examples

The ship was sailing in waters sixty fathoms deep.
They measured the depth and found it to be ten fathoms.
Divers descended to a depth of twenty fathoms in search of the wreck.

Etymology

From Old English 'fæðm' (embrace, span of the outstretched arms), referring to the length measured by outstretched arms.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfæð.əm / (AmE), / ˈfað.əm / (BrE)
a unit of volume, equal to 6 cubic feet, formerly used in mining to measure bodies of ore
unit of volume in mining (6 cubic feet)

Examples

The mine's reserves were calculated in fathoms to estimate the total volume of ore available.
Old records often mention deposits measured in fathoms, reflecting past mining practices.
Surveyors noted a section containing twenty fathoms of copper ore ready for extraction.

3. VERB

/ ˈfæðəm / (AmE), / ˈfaðəm / (BrE)
to understand or comprehend something after much thought, especially something difficult or complex
understand (after thought or effort)

Examples

It's hard to fathom why she made that decision given the circumstances.
He tried to fathom the meaning behind the mysterious message.
I can't fathom how anyone could live without music.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grasp - to understand something thoroughly
  • Synonym: to comprehend - to understand fully
  • Antonym: to misunderstand - to fail to understand

Etymology

Originally from Old English 'fæðm' meaning 'embrace, grasp', later figuratively 'to measure, understand'.

4. VERB

/ ˈfæðəm / (AmE), / ˈfaðəm / (BrE)
to measure the depth of (water) using a sounding line
measure the depth of water

Examples

Early sailors would fathom the sea to ensure safe passage for their ships.
The crew fathomed the river before setting anchor near the shore.
Using a weighted line, he fathomed the channel to check its depth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sound - to measure the depth of water, especially with a sounding line

Etymology

From Old English fæthm (outstretched arms; a measure of length).

a unit of length equal to six feet (about 1.83 meters), chiefly used in measuring the depth of water
unit for water depth (6 feet)

Examples

The diver measured the wreck at fifty fathoms below the surface.
The old map showed a depth of ten fathoms near the shoreline.
He signaled to lower anchor at twenty fathoms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meter - SI unit of length (about 3.28 feet)
  • Degree Lower: yard - unit of length equal to 3 feet
  • Degree Higher: mile - unit of length equal to 5,280 feet

Etymology

From Old English *fæðm* (embrace, spanning the arms), patterned on the idea of the width of outstretched arms.
