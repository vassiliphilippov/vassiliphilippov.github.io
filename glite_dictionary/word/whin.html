whin

1. NOUN

/ wɪn / (AmE), / wɪn / (BrE)
A hard, dark-colored rock, especially chert or basalt, often found in the British Isles.
hard dark rock (esp. chert or basalt)

Examples

The cliffs are composed primarily of whin, giving them a dark and rugged appearance.
Builders often avoid using whin due to its extreme hardness.
After the storm, fragments of whin were scattered along the shoreline.

Etymology

Probably of North English origin; related to Old Norse *hvīna* (to whine), perhaps referencing the sound of wind over exposed rocks.

2. NOUN

/ wɪn / (AmE), / wɪn / (BrE)
A very spiny, dense evergreen shrub with fragrant golden-yellow flowers, common throughout western Europe; also known as gorse.
spiny evergreen shrub of western Europe

Examples

The fields were thick with whin in full bloom, coloring the landscape brilliant yellow.
She pricked her finger on a whin while walking along the country lane.
Local farmers often clear patches of whin to create space for grazing sheep.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gorse - another name for whin, yellow-flowered shrub

Etymology

Of uncertain origin; possibly from Old Norse 'hvīn' meaning 'gorse' or 'furze'.

3. NOUN

/ wɪn / (AmE), / wɪn / (BrE)
A small Eurasian shrub with spiny branches and clusters of yellow flowers, also known as gorse, sometimes grown as an ornamental and considered a weed in some regions.
a shrub with yellow flowers (gorse)

Examples

The hillsides were covered in bright yellow whin that bloomed each spring.
Farmers often find whin to be a troublesome weed in their pastures.
She picked some whin flowers to brighten the kitchen windowsill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gorse - a spiny shrub with yellow flowers, similar or identical concept

Etymology

Possibly of Old Norse origin, related to Icelandic *hvönn* (angelica) or Old English *hwenn* (a kind of plant).
