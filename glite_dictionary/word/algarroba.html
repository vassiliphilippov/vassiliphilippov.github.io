algarroba

1. NOUN

/ ˌælɡəˈroʊbə / (AmE), / ˌælɡəˈrəʊbə / (BrE)
An evergreen Mediterranean tree (Ceratonia siliqua) with edible pods, also known as the carob tree.
evergreen Mediterranean tree, carob

Examples

The algarroba is cultivated for its sweet, edible pods.
In ancient times, the seeds of the algarroba were used as a standard of weight.
Many Mediterranean desserts include ground algarroba as a chocolate substitute.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carob - the evergreen tree or its edible pods

Etymology

From Spanish 'algarroba', from Arabic 'al-ḵarrūb' (the carob tree).

2. NOUN

/ ˌælɡəˈroʊbə / (AmE), / ˌalɡəˈrəʊbə / (BrE)
The pod of certain mesquite trees, used particularly in tanning and dyeing.
mesquite pod used for tanning and dyeing

Examples

The algarroba is prized for its high content of tannins used in the leather tanning industry.
The dye extracted from algarroba pods produces a rich, dark color on fabrics.
Local communities gather algarroba pods every season for traditional uses.

Etymology

From Spanish 'algarroba', from Arabic 'al-ẖarrūba' (the carob tree), originally referring to carob pods but also used for similar pods in the Americas.

3. NOUN

/ ˌælɡəˈroʊbə / (AmE), / ˌalɡəˈrəʊbə / (BrE)
A long pod containing small beans and sweet, edible pulp, commonly used as animal feed and as a source of a chocolate substitute; also known as carob.
carob pod with edible pulp

Examples

The farmer mixed crushed algarroba pods into the cattle feed for added nutrition.
Many health food stores sell algarroba powder as an alternative to cocoa.
The sweet pulp of the algarroba pod can be eaten fresh or dried.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carob - the pod of the carob tree, used as a chocolate substitute

Etymology

From Spanish 'algarroba', from Arabic 'al- ḵarrūba' (the carob pod).

4. NOUN

/ ˌælɡəˈroʊbə / (AmE), / ˌalɡəˈrəʊbə / (BrE)
A mesquite tree native to the Gulf Coast and Caribbean Islands from Mexico to Venezuela, known for its long seed pods.
tropical mesquite tree of the Americas

Examples

The algarroba is valued both for its shade and its nutritious seed pods.
Farmers in Venezuela plant algarroba to help enrich the soil in dry areas.
The sweet pulp of the algarroba pod is often used in traditional Caribbean cuisine.

Etymology

From Spanish 'algarroba', originally from Arabic 'al-ḵarrūba' (the carob pod).
