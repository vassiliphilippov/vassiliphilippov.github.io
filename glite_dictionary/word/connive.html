to connive

1. VERB

/ kəˈnaɪv / (AmE), / kəˈnaɪv / (BrE)
secretly allow or encourage (something immoral, illegal, or wrong) to occur; to give tacit approval to wrongdoing.
secretly allow wrongdoing

Examples

The officials were accused of having connived in the fraud that cost taxpayers millions.
She refused to connive at her colleague's illegal activities.
By staying silent, they connived with the criminal enterprise.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to collude - to cooperate secretly for an illegal or deceitful purpose
  • Synonym: to condone - to accept or allow morally wrong or offensive behavior
  • Antonym: to oppose - to act against or resist

Etymology

From Latin 'connivere' (to close the eyes, overlook).

2. VERB

/ kəˈnaɪv / (AmE), / kəˈnaɪv / (BrE)
to secretly cooperate with others or form plots, especially for dishonest or deceitful purposes
secretly cooperate in wrongdoing

Examples

Several officials were discovered to connive with the smugglers in exchange for bribes.
She accused her coworkers of trying to connive against her for the management position.
He would never connive in such a dishonest scheme, even if it benefited him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to collude - to cooperate secretly for a fraudulent or deceitful purpose
  • Synonym: plot - to make secret plans to carry out something illegal or harmful
  • Antonym: to betray - to be disloyal or unfaithful; act against someone's trust

Etymology

From Latin 'connivere', meaning 'to close the eyes', suggesting pretending not to see wrongdoing.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00709785-v
Synset POS: v
