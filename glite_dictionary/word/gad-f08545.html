gad

1. VERB

/ ɡæd / (AmE), / ɡæd / (BrE)
to go from place to place, especially in a carefree or restless way, for enjoyment or entertainment
wander or travel for fun without purpose

Examples

On weekends, she loves to gad about town, discovering new cafes and boutiques.
Instead of working, he spent the afternoon gadding around the city with his friends.
Travel writers often gad from country to country in search of stories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to roam - to move about aimlessly or without any destination
  • Synonym: meander - to wander aimlessly, often with leisure

Etymology

Origin uncertain, but possibly from obsolete 'gadder', related to Middle English 'gadden' (to hurry).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ɡæd / (AmE), / ɡæd / (BrE)
a sharp metal spike attached to a rider's heel, used to urge a horse forward; a spur
a sharp spike on a rider's heel for urging a horse

Examples

The cowboy pressed his gad against the horse to make it move faster.
He decorated his boots with an ornate silver gad.
A sharp gad can make a stubborn horse obey its rider.

Synset Examples

cowboys know not to squat with their spurs on

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spur - a device worn on a rider's heel to urge a horse forward

Etymology

From Old Norse 'gaddr' (spike, pointed stick).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04297397-n
Synset POS: n
