gabardine

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡæbərˌdiːn / (AmE), / ˌɡæbəˈdiːn / (BrE)
a firmly woven, durable fabric with a twill weave, often used for suits, trousers, and coats
durable twilled fabric

Examples

His jacket was made of gabardine that still looked new after many years of use.
The tailor recommended gabardine for its resistance to wrinkles and wear.
She preferred suits in navy blue gabardine for their classic appearance.

Etymology

From French 'gabardine', originally referring to a cloak, later to the fabric; see Spanish 'gabardina'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌɡæbərˈdin / (AmE), / ˌɡæbəˈdiːn / (BrE)
a long, loose outer garment, such as a coat or frock, reaching down to the ankles, typically worn in the past
long, loose outer garment
archaic

Examples

The wanderer wrapped himself in a gabardine to shield against the evening chill.
In Shakespeare's time, a gabardine was a common sight among travelers and shepherds.
He was easily recognized by his tattered gabardine and wide-brimmed hat.

Etymology

From Old French 'gaban', meaning a cloak, possibly from Spanish 'gabán'.
