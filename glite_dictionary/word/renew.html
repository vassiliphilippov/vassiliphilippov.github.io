to renew

1. VERB

/ rɪˈnu / (AmE), / rɪˈnjuː / (BrE)
to make something new, fresh, or strong again; to begin something again after a pause or interruption
make new again; resume after pause

Examples

After years apart, they decided to renew their friendship and start meeting regularly again.
It's time to renew your driver's license before it expires next month.
The company is working hard to renew its commitment to customer service.

Synset Examples

We renewed our friendship after a hiatus of twenty years
They renewed their membership

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to restore - to bring back to a former condition
  • Synonym: resume - to begin again after interruption
  • Antonym: cancel - to end or terminate something so that it does not continue

Etymology

From Old French *renouveler*, from Latin *renovare* (to make new again).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈnu / (AmE), / rɪˈnjuː / (BrE)
to make something appear in a new, updated, or different form
make appear in a new form

Examples

The company decided to renew its brand image to attract a broader audience.
With a few modern touches, the architect managed to renew the look of the old building.
The publisher aims to renew interest in the classic by releasing a special edition.

Synset Examples

the old product was reincarnated to appeal to a younger market

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to revamp - to give new and improved form, structure, or appearance to something
  • Synonym: to refresh - to give new strength or energy to; to update or make more modern

Etymology

From Old French 'renover', from Latin 'renovare' (to renew).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00167460-v
Synset POS: v
