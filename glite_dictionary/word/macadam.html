macadam

1. NOUN

/ məˈkædəm / (AmE), / məˈkadəm / (BrE)
Broken stone of even size, used to form the surface of a road, especially one constructed by compacting layers of such stone.
broken stone used for road surfaces

Examples

The rural road was covered in macadam to create a smoother driving surface.
Workers spread new macadam over the old, worn-out road to repair it.
Early highways were often constructed using layers of macadam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a mixture of bitumen with gravel, used for road surfacing

Etymology

From the name of John Loudon McAdam (1756–1836), Scottish engineer who developed the road-building method.

2. NOUN

/ məˈkædəm / (AmE), / məˈkædəm / (BrE)
A type of road surface made from layers of compacted broken stone, often bound together with tar or bitumen.
paved road of crushed stone and tar

Examples

The country road was surfaced with macadam to allow for smoother travel.
After decades of use, the old macadam began to crumble and needed repair.
They replaced the dirt paths with macadam to improve access to the village.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - road surface material made of bitumen and aggregates

Etymology

Named after John Loudon McAdam, the Scottish engineer who developed the method in the early 19th century.

3. VERB

/ məˈkædəm / (AmE), / məˈkadəm / (BrE)
to surface (a road) with macadam, a type of broken stone compacted with a binder
to surface a road with broken stone

Examples

The old road was completely macadamed last year to improve its durability.
Workers spent the week macadaming the approach to the bridge before winter set in.
After the plan was approved, the city council decided to macadam the main highway.

Etymology
