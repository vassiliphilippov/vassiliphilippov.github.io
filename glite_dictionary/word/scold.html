scold

1. VERB

/ skoʊld / (AmE), / skəʊld / (BrE)
to speak to someone angrily or severely because they have done something wrong
speak angrily to someone for wrongdoing

Examples

If you don't finish your homework, your parents might scold you.
The teacher scolded the class for talking during the lesson.
She scolds her dog gently when he misbehaves.

Synset Examples

The mother scolded the child for entering a stranger's car
The deputy ragged the Prime Minister
The customer dressed down the waiter for bringing cold soup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuke - to express sharp disapproval or criticism
  • Synonym: reprimand - to officially tell someone that their behavior is wrong
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'escoldier' (to scold), of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ skoʊld / (AmE), / skəʊld / (BrE)
an old-fashioned term for a person, especially a woman, who criticizes or complains frequently and annoyingly
a person who nags or complains constantly
archaic

Examples

In the old novel, the village scold was notorious for her constant bickering.
No one wanted to cross paths with the town scold, who never missed an opportunity to complain.
He tired quickly of living with a persistent scold always pointing out his faults.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nag - someone who persistently complains or criticizes

Etymology

From Middle English 'scolden' (to quarrel), probably from Old Norse 'skald' (scold, insult).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ skoʊld / (AmE), / skəʊld / (BrE)
to speak angrily or critically to someone because they have done something wrong; to complain angrily about something
to speak angrily or complain

Examples

The teacher began to scold the students for talking during the test.
Whenever the children forgot to feed the dog, their father would scold them.
She scolded her brother for tracking mud into the house.

Synset Examples

He scolded about anything that he thought was wrong
We grumbled about the increased work load

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuke - to express sharp disapproval or criticism
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From late Middle English, from Anglo-Norman French *escoldēr* (to rebuke, scold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00911526-v
Synset POS: v
