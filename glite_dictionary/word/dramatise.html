to dramatise

1. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to make something seem more exciting or important than it really is by adding details, exaggerating, or presenting it in a theatrical way
exaggerate or make something seem theatrical
british

Examples

He tends to dramatise every small problem as if it's a major crisis.
The journalist was accused of trying to dramatise the story to attract more readers.
You don't need to dramatise the event; just tell us what really happened.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - to represent something as greater than it is
  • Antonym: to understate - to represent something as less important or smaller than it actually is

Etymology

From Greek 'drama' (action, play); via French 'dramatiser'.

2. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to adapt a story, event, or situation so that it can be presented as a play, film, or television drama
adapt for drama or performance
british

Examples

The director wanted to dramatise the novel for a television series.
She is planning to dramatise the events of the expedition as a stage play.
Many authors struggle to dramatise historical facts without distorting the truth.

Synset Examples

adopt a book for a screenplay

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dramatize - adapt for drama or performance (American spelling)

Etymology

From Greek 'drāmat-', meaning 'drama' + '-ize'.

3. VERB

/ ˈdræm.ə.taɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræm.ə.taɪz / (BrE)
to present or represent something in a dramatic or exaggerated way
represent in a dramatic way
british

Examples

The documentary dramatises the experiences of refugees to emphasize their struggles.
He tends to dramatise every minor setback as a major disaster.
The novel was later dramatized for the stage by a renowned playwright.

Synset Examples

These events dramatize the lack of social responsibility among today's youth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - to represent something as greater or more important than it really is
  • Antonym: to understate - to represent something as less important than it really is

Etymology

From Greek drama (action, play) via late Latin dramatizare.
