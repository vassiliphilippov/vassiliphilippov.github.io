laterality

1. NOUN

/ ˌleɪtəˈrælɪti / (AmE), / ˌleɪtəˈrælɪti / (BrE)
the preference for using one side of the body, especially one hand, more than the other
preference for one side of the body

Examples

Doctors tested the patient's laterality to determine if he was right- or left-handed.
Research shows that strong laterality may be associated with improved motor skills.
Athletes sometimes train to reduce laterality and improve coordination between both hands.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: handedness - the tendency to use one hand more naturally than the other

Etymology

From Latin 'lateralis' (of or belonging to the side) + English suffix '-ity'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌleɪtəˈræləti / (AmE), / ˌleɪtəˈraləti / (BrE)
the localization of a function, such as speech or handedness, to either the right or left side of the brain or body
right or left side preference in body or brain function
medical

Examples

Research has shown that language laterality may differ between individuals.
Studies in brain laterality help scientists understand the origins of handedness.
The doctor explained how laterality could affect cognitive abilities.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bilaterality - having functions on both sides of the body or brain
  • Synonym: hemisphere - one half of the brain (left or right)

Etymology

From Latin 'lateralis' (of the side) + English '-ity'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌleɪtəˈræləti / (AmE), / ˌleɪtəˈrælɪti / (BrE)
the tendency or superior development of one side of the body or brain over the other
preference for one side of the body
medical

Examples

Right-handedness is a common form of laterality observed in humans.
Some neurological disorders can affect laterality, leading to unusual patterns of dominance.
Research into brain laterality has advanced our understanding of language and motor skills.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: handedness - the preference for using one hand over the other
  • Antonym: ambidexterity - ability to use both hands with equal skill

Etymology

From Latin 'lateralis' (of the side) + English '-ity'.
