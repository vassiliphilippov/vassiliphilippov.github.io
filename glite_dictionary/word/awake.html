awake

1. VERB

/ əˈweɪk / (AmE), / əˈweɪk / (BrE)
to stop sleeping; to become conscious after sleeping
to stop sleeping

Examples

Every morning, I awake to the chirping of birds outside my window.
She often awakes before sunrise to meditate and exercise.
He awoke suddenly in the night, unsure of what had disturbed him.

Synset Examples

She woke up to the sound of the alarm clock

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wake up - to stop sleeping; to become awake
  • Antonym: sleep - to be in a state of rest with eyes closed and mind inactive

Etymology

From Old English 'awæcnan' (to arise, awaken), from 'a-' (up) + 'wacan' (to wake).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00018519-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ əˈweɪk / (AmE), / əˈweɪk / (BrE)
not sleeping; fully conscious and alert
not sleeping; fully conscious

Examples

I was still awake at three in the morning, unable to fall asleep.
The sound of thunder kept everyone awake during the storm.
Please try to stay awake until midnight to welcome the new year.

Synset Examples

lay awake thinking about his new job
still not fully awake

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asleep - in a state of sleep
  • Synonym: alert - fully attentive and aware

Etymology

Old English awæcnan, onwæcnan (verb), from a- (on) + wācian (to wake).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00188104-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ əˈweɪk / (AmE), / əˈweɪk / (BrE)
aware of and responsive to something; mentally perceptive and alert to specific issues or situations.
mentally alert and responsive

Examples

She is fully awake to the risks involved in the new venture.
It took him some time to become awake to what was happening around him.
As citizens, we must remain awake to the challenges facing our community.

Synset Examples

an alert mind
alert to the problems
alive to what is going on
awake to the dangers of her situation
was now awake to the reality of his predicament

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alert - quick to notice and respond to things
  • Antonym: unaware - not aware or conscious of something

Etymology

From Old English 'awæcnan', meaning to arise, originate, or come into being.
