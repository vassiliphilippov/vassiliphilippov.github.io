mock

1. VERB

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
to imitate someone or something in a way that makes them appear foolish or ridiculous, often to ridicule or show contempt
imitate to ridicule

Examples

She was upset when her classmates mocked her accent during the presentation.
The comedian mocked politicians in his stand-up routine.
He mocked his brother by copying his walk in an exaggerated way.

Synset Examples

The children mocked their handicapped classmate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imitate - to copy the actions or appearance of someone or something
  • Synonym: ridicule - to make fun of someone in a cruel or harsh way
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Middle French *moquer* (to mock, deride).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00851012-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
to treat someone or something with scorn or contempt, often by ridicule or derision
treat with contempt or ridicule

Examples

She would mock his ideas in front of the entire team.
The comedian mocked politicians throughout his performance.
People often mock what they do not understand.

Synset Examples

The new constitution mocks all democratic principles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridicule - to make fun of someone or something in a cruel or dismissive way
  • Antonym: respect - to admire someone or something deeply, as a result of their abilities, qualities, or achievements

Etymology

From Old French 'moquer' (to mock, deride), origin uncertain.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00850760-v
Synset POS: v

3. ADJ

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
Imitating or pretending to be something, especially to appear real or genuine though not authentic.
imitation or pretend (not real)

Examples

The children engaged in a mock trial to learn about the legal system.
He gave her a mock salute as a joke.
The army held a mock battle to prepare for real combat.

Synset Examples

boys in mock battle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imitation - something that is made to look like something else
  • Antonym: genuine - actually being what it is claimed to be; authentic

Etymology

From Middle French 'moquer' (to mock, deride), via Old French, probably from Vulgar Latin *muccare (to blow the nose at).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01121586-s
Synset POS: s

4. NOUN

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
an act of mocking or ridiculing someone or something, especially in a contemptuous manner
an act of mocking or ridicule
archaic

Examples

His cruel mock wounded her deeply, leaving her feeling humiliated.
They turned the ceremony into a mock by making jokes throughout.
The actor's clumsy accent became the mock of the entire cast.

Synset Examples

they made a mock of him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridicule - the act of making fun of or laughing at someone or something

Etymology

From Middle French *moquer* (to mock), from Old French.
