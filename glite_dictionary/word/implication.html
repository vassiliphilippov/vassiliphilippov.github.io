implication

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪmpləˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the fact or state of being involved with or connected to something, especially in a way that is incriminating or suggestive of guilt
involvement, especially suggesting guilt

Examples

There was significant implication of the senator in the corruption scandal.
The implication of the manager in the fraud led to a lengthy investigation.
Despite his denial, the evidence suggested his implication in the scheme.

Synset Examples

he was suspected of implication in several robberies

Etymology

From Latin implicatio, from implicare (to entangle, involve).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
a conclusion or result that can be drawn from something, though it is not explicitly stated
something implied or suggested

Examples

The new law has serious implications for the local community.
Her words carried an implication that he was not welcome.
We need to consider the possible implications of our actions before proceeding.

Synset Examples

his resignation had political implications

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inference - a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning
  • Synonym: suggestion - an idea put forward for consideration
  • Antonym: explicit statement - something stated clearly and directly

Etymology

From Latin implicatio, from implicare (to entwine, entangle).

3. NOUN

/ ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Logic) A relationship between two propositions in which if the first (p) is true, the second (q) must also be true; expressed as 'if p, then q.'
logical relationship: if p then q

Examples

The truth table helps illustrate how logical implication works in mathematics.
In formal proofs, implication is often denoted by an arrow symbol.
Mathematicians carefully distinguish between implication and other logical relations, such as equivalence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: entailment - logical relationship where one statement necessarily follows from another
  • Antonym: contradiction - a logical relationship where statements cannot both be true

Etymology

From Latin implicātiō (an entangling), from implicāre (to entangle, involve).

4. NOUN

/ ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪmplɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
A meaning that is suggested or can be inferred though not directly stated.
suggested or inferred meaning

Examples

Although he didn't say it outright, the implication of his words was clear.
Her tone carried an implication that she doubted his honesty.
The policy change has important implications for the future of the company.

Synset Examples

the significance of his remark became clear only later
the expectation was spread both by word and by implication

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hint - a slight or indirect suggestion
  • Synonym: insinuation - an indirect or covert suggestion, often of something negative
  • Antonym: explicit meaning - a meaning stated clearly and in detail

Etymology

From Latin implicatio (entanglement, involvement), from implicare (to entangle, involve).

5. NOUN

/ ˌɪm.pləˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪm.plɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the act of suggesting or indicating that someone is involved in something wrong or illegal, often without direct proof
suggestion of involvement in wrongdoing

Examples

The report's implication of several senior officials shocked the entire department.
He denied any implication in the financial scandal and insisted on his innocence.
Journalists discussed the possible implication of the governor in the corruption case.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accusation - a charge or claim that someone has done something illegal or wrong
  • Antonym: exoneration - the act of officially stating that someone is not guilty

Etymology

From Latin implicatio (entangling, involvement), from implicare (to entwine, involve).
