genitive

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɛnətɪv / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (BrE)
the grammatical case used to show ownership or a relationship similar to ownership
grammatical case of possession

Examples

In some languages, nouns change form in the genitive case to indicate possession.
The English possessive 's' is a remnant of the genitive case from Old English.
You can recognize the genitive by the use of 'of' or the apostrophe-s in English.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possessive - the case or form that shows possession

Etymology

From Latin genitivus (of or belonging to birth/kind).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛnɪtɪv / (BrE)
(of a grammatical case) expressing possession or close association, typically marked in English by the apostrophe-s ('s) or by a possessive pronoun.
expressing possession (grammar)

Examples

In Latin, the genitive case is used to show ownership or relationship.
Most English nouns form the genitive by adding an apostrophe and s, as in 'the girl's book'.
The phrase 'the president's office' uses the genitive construction to show possession.

Synset Examples

possessive pronouns
the genitive endings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possessive - indicating possession, especially a grammatical form

Etymology

From Latin 'genitivus' (of or belonging to birth, origin), from 'gignere' (to beget).
