domestication

1. NOUN

/ dəˌmɛstəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / dəˌmɛstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of adapting someone or something to domestic life or home routines
adaptation to domestic life

Examples

After years of travel, he found domestication challenging but rewarding.
Her domestication of the wild artist surprised their friends.
The domestication of former adventurers was a common theme in literature.

Synset Examples

her explorer husband resisted all her attempts at domestication

Related Terms

  • Synonym: taming - the process of making something less wild or easier to live with

Etymology

From Latin 'domesticus' (of the home), from 'domus' (house).

2. NOUN

/ dəˌmɛstəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / dəˌmɛstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or state of taming an animal or cultivating a plant for use by humans, resulting in adaptation to living with people
process of taming animals or plants

Examples

The domestication of dogs is believed to have occurred over 15,000 years ago.
Through domestication, many wild plants have been transformed into crops.
Selective breeding is a key aspect of animal domestication.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: taming - the act of making an animal less wild and more tractable
  • Antonym: wildness - the state of being wild or undomesticated

Etymology

From Latin 'domesticare' (to make domestic) + '-ion'.

3. NOUN

/ dəˌmɛstəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / dəˌmɛstɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which animals or plants are adapted over time to live in close association with humans, often to serve human needs
adaptation of animals/plants to humans

Examples

The domestication of dogs began thousands of years ago and changed the nature of human societies.
Scientists study the process of domestication to understand how crops like wheat were first cultivated.
The domestication of animals has had a profound impact on agriculture and transportation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: taming - the act of making an animal less wild, especially through training
  • Antonym: wildness - the state of animals living independently of humans; not domesticated

Etymology

From Latin 'domesticus' (of the home) + '-ation', indicating action or process.
