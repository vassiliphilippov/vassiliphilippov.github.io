vindication

1. NOUN

/ ˌvɪndəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvɪndɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
proof that something is justified or true, especially after doubt or criticism.
proof of justification or correctness

Examples

The DNA evidence provided the vindication that the wrongly accused man needed.
Her vindication came when the investigation proved she was telling the truth all along.
Many campaigners see the court's ruling as a vindication of their years of work.

Synset Examples

he offered a persuasive defense of the theory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - showing something to be right or reasonable
  • Antonym: condemnation - the expression of strong disapproval; censure

Etymology

From Latin vindicatio (a laying claim, defense, justification), from vindicare (to lay claim, declare, avenge).

2. NOUN

/ ˌvɪndɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvɪndɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of clearing someone from blame, suspicion, or criticism; justification of someone's actions or beliefs, especially when previously doubted or criticized
the act of clearing from blame or criticism

Examples

After months of accusations, the court's decision brought vindication to her reputation.
His research found vindication when new evidence supported his theory.
The public apology was seen as a form of vindication for those who had been wrongly accused.

Synset Examples

friends provided a vindication of his position

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - the action of showing something to be right or reasonable
  • Antonym: condemnation - the expression of very strong disapproval; censure

Etymology

From Latin vindicatio (a claiming, defending), from vindicare (to claim, avenge).
