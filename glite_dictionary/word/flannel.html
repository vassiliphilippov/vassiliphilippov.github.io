flannel

1. NOUN

/ ˈflænəl / (AmE), / ˈflænəl / (BrE)
A small piece of soft cloth used for washing the face or body, especially in the bath.
washcloth for face or body
british

Examples

Could you pass me a flannel? I want to wash my face.
She prefers using a flannel to soap up in the shower.
The hotel provides each guest with a clean flannel every day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: washcloth - A small cloth used for washing one's face or body

Etymology

From Old French 'flaine' (a type of wool), probably from Welsh 'gwlanen' (woolen article), from 'gwlan' (wool).

2. NOUN

/ ˈflæn(ə)l / (AmE), / ˈflæn(ə)l / (BrE)
A soft, light woolen or cotton fabric, often used for clothing or bed linens.
soft woolen or cotton fabric

Examples

She wore a comfortable shirt made of flannel on the chilly morning.
The baby was wrapped snugly in a soft flannel blanket.
Many people prefer flannel sheets during the winter for extra warmth.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: tweed - a rough-surfaced woolen cloth, typically of mixed flecked colors

Etymology

From Old French flanelle, probably from Welsh gwlanen (woolen article).

3. NOUN

/ ˈflænəl / (AmE), / ˈflænəl / (BrE)
(British, usually plural) Trousers made of flannel or similar fabric, such as gabardine or tweed; often refers to light-colored formal or sports trousers.
trousers made of flannel fabric (British)
british

Examples

He wore a crisp white shirt with grey flannels to the garden party.
College students once considered a blazer and flannels appropriate campus attire.
The golf club required all members to wear flannels on the course.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trousers - an outer garment covering the body from the waist to the ankles, with a separate part for each leg

Etymology

From Old French *flaine* (a kind of wool), of uncertain origin, possibly from Welsh *gwlanen* (flannel wool).
