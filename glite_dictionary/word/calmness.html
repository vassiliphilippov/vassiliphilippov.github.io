calmness

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmnəs / (AmE), / ˈkɑːmnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being free from agitation or strong emotion, especially during difficult situations
state of being calm, especially under stress

Examples

She was admired for her calmness even during the chaotic meeting.
His calmness in emergencies helps reassure everyone around him.
Meditation can help you develop greater calmness in your daily life.

Synset Examples

he accepted their problems with composure and she with equanimity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: composure - mental calmness under stress
  • Antonym: agitation - state of being excited or disturbed

Etymology

From the adjective 'calm' + noun-forming suffix '-ness', ultimately from Latin 'cauma' (rest, heat).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmnəs / (AmE), / ˈkɑːmnəs / (BrE)
A state in which the weather is free from strong winds or rain.
absence of strong winds or rain

Examples

After the storm, the calmness of the sea was almost surreal.
The calmness in the air made it a perfect day for sailing.
There was a rare calmness outside, with not a single gust of wind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: calm - still air or sea; absence of wind or disturbance (noun, as in 'the calm')

Etymology

From Latin 'cauma' (heat, calm), via Old French 'calme'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmnəs / (AmE), / ˈkɑːmnəs / (BrE)
A state or quality of being free from agitation, excitement, or disturbance; serenity.
state of being calm or serene

Examples

He handled the stressful situation with remarkable calmness and patience.
The calmness of the early morning made it perfect for meditation.
Despite the chaos outside, there was a sense of calmness inside the building.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: serenity - the state of being calm, peaceful, and untroubled
  • Antonym: agitation - a state of anxiety or nervous excitement

Etymology

From 'calm' + '-ness', with 'calm' originating from Old French 'calme', from Latin 'cauma' (heat of the day, rest in the heat), from Greek 'kauma'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
