to arrogate

1. VERB

/ ˈær.əˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈær.əˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to claim or seize (a right or privilege) without justification or authority
claim without right or authority

Examples

The new committee tried to arrogate more power than was allocated to them.
It is unjust to arrogate the achievements of others as your own.
No government should arrogate the people's fundamental rights.

Synset Examples

He assumed to himself the right to fill all positions in the town
he usurped my rights
She seized control of the throne after her husband died

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to usurp - to take a position of power or importance illegally or by force

Etymology

From Latin arrogare (to claim for oneself).

2. VERB

/ ˈærəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈærəɡeɪt / (BrE)
to claim or take something, especially a right or privilege, without justification or authority
claim or take without right

Examples

They attempted to arrogate powers that did not belong to them.
It is not for you to arrogate the authority of the committee.
Some leaders arrogate privileges to themselves that rightfully belong to the people.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to usurp - to take (a position or power) illegally or by force
  • Antonym: to relinquish - to voluntarily cease to keep or claim

Etymology

From Latin arrogare (to claim for oneself).

3. VERB

/ ˈærəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈærəˌɡeɪt / (BrE)
to claim or seize a right or privilege for oneself without justification
to unjustly claim a right or privilege

Examples

The committee tried to arrogate powers that did not lawfully belong to it.
He tends to arrogate authority in meetings, often ignoring the views of others.
Some leaders arrogate the right to speak for everyone, even without broad support.

Synset Examples

He claimed his suitcases at the airline counter
Mr. Smith claims special tax exemptions because he is a foreign resident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: claim - to demand or assert something as one's right
  • Synonym: to usurp - to take power or property by force or without right

Etymology

From Latin arrogare (to claim for oneself, to appropriate), from ad- (to) + rogare (to ask).
