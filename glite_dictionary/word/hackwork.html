hackwork

1. NOUN

/ ˈhækˌwɜrk / (AmE), / ˈhækˌwɜːk / (BrE)
writing or other professional work produced mechanically or to a formula, rather than from creative effort or genuine interest.
uninspired, formulaic professional work

Examples

Many critics dismissed the novel as mere hackwork produced to meet publisher demands.
He refused to spend his career turning out hackwork just to pay the bills.
The film's script felt like hackwork, lacking any originality or depth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hack - a writer or journalist who produces dull, routine, or formulaic work

Etymology

From 'hack' (as in hack writer, a person who produces dull or routine work) + 'work'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhækˌwɝk / (AmE), / ˈhækˌwɜːk / (BrE)
Writing, art, or work produced quickly and to order, usually considered to be of poor quality and lacking originality.
inferior work done to order

Examples

The critic dismissed the novel as mere hackwork, lacking any creative spark.
She refused to write hackwork for the magazine, insisting on higher standards.
Many poets struggle to earn a living and sometimes resort to hackwork.

Synset Examples

James Scully has observed that much of what is called political poetry, or poetry that deals with politics, is hackwork.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: potboiler - a literary or artistic work produced quickly to make money, usually regarded as of poor quality

Etymology

Formed from 'hack' (as in hack writer) and 'work', referring to work done for hire without concern for quality.
