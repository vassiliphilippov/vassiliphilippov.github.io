to sustain

1. VERB

/ səˈsteɪn / (AmE), / səˈsteɪn / (BrE)
(of a court, judge, etc.) to accept or uphold as valid, especially a motion or objection in a legal proceeding.
uphold as valid (legal)
legal

Examples

The judge sustained the lawyer's objection during the trial.
After a lengthy debate, the court sustained the plaintiff's motion.
If the evidence is inadmissible, the judge may sustain the objection.

Synset Examples

The court sustained the motion

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to overrule - to reject or disallow (especially a legal motion or objection)

Etymology

From Latin sustinere (to hold up, support).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00820083-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ səˈsteɪn / (AmE), / səˈsteɪn / (BrE)
to physically support or bear the weight of something; to keep something from falling or collapsing
hold up physically; bear weight

Examples

The pillars sustain the immense weight of the ancient temple's roof.
That cable is not strong enough to sustain the bridge's load during heavy traffic.
The old bookshelf could no longer sustain the growing pile of books and started to sag.

Synset Examples

The beam holds up the roof
He supported me with one hand while I balanced on the beam
What's holding that mirror?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to bear all or part of the weight of; hold up
  • Synonym: to uphold - to support or keep upright

Etymology

From Latin sustinere, from sub- (up from below) + tenere (to hold).
