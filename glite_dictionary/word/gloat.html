to gloat

1. VERB

/ ɡloʊt / (AmE), / ɡləʊt / (BrE)
to dwell on or display one's satisfaction about something, often in a way that is considered smug or malicious, especially regarding another's misfortune
to show smug satisfaction

Examples

He couldn't resist the urge to gloat about his team's victory.
It's not polite to gloat when others make mistakes.
She stood there gloating over her competitor's defeat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relish - to enjoy or take pleasure in something
  • Synonym: to exult - to show or feel elation, often after a success
  • Antonym: to sympathize - to share the feelings of another, typically feeling pity or compassion

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *glouten* (to look at), of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ ɡloʊt / (AmE), / ɡləʊt / (BrE)
to dwell on one's own success or another's misfortune with smugness or malignant pleasure
to relish success or misfortune smugly

Examples

After winning the game, he couldn't help but gloat over his rival's defeat.
The press was accused of gloating about the scandal.
She tried not to gloat, but her satisfaction was obvious.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to rejoice - to feel or show great joy or delight
  • Antonym: lament - to express sorrow or regret, often for the misfortune of others

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English 'glouten' (to scowl); possible link to German 'glotzen' (to stare).
