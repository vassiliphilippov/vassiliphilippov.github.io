creosote

1. NOUN

/ ˈkriːəˌsoʊt / (AmE), / ˈkriːəsəʊt / (BrE)
a colorless to yellowish oily liquid obtained by distilling wood tar, used as an antiseptic and preservative
oily liquid from wood tar, antiseptic

Examples

The fence posts were treated with creosote to prevent rot and decay.
Exposure to creosote fumes can be hazardous to your health.
Historically, creosote was used as an antiseptic for wounds and infections.

Etymology

From Greek *kreas* (flesh) + *sōtēr* (preserver), referring to its use as a preservative.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkriːəˌsoʊt / (AmE), / ˈkriːəsəʊt / (BrE)
a dark, oily liquid obtained from the distillation of coal tar, used especially to preserve wood
chemical used to preserve wood

Examples

Railroad ties are often treated with creosote to prevent decay and insect damage.
The smell of creosote is strong and distinctive, lingering on freshly preserved wood.
Exposure to creosote requires safety precautions due to its toxic properties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tar - a dark, thick, oily substance obtained from wood or coal distillation

Etymology

From Greek 'kreas' (flesh) + 'sōtēr' (preserver), coined in 1832 to describe its ability to preserve flesh.

3. VERB

/ ˈkriːəˌsoʊt / (AmE), / ˈkriːəˌsəʊt / (BrE)
to treat (wood, timber, etc.) with creosote to protect it from decay and pests
treat with creosote (esp. wood)

Examples

Many railroad ties are creosoted to increase their durability against rot.
The company creosotes wooden posts before installing them along the fence line.
They were creosoting the docks to protect the wood from marine borers.

Synset Examples

creosoted wood

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preserve - to treat to prevent decay (especially of wood)

Etymology

From Greek *kreo* (flesh) + *sōtēr* (preserver), originally referring to the disinfectant qualities of creosote.
