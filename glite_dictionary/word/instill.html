to instill

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈstɪl / (AmE), / ɪnˈstɪl / (BrE)
to gradually put a feeling, idea, or principle into someone's mind or character over time
impart gradually

Examples

Parents try to instill good values in their children from an early age.
The coach worked hard to instill confidence in his team.
Teachers can instill a love of reading through engaging stories and discussions.

Synset Examples

Her presence instilled faith into the children
transfuse love of music into the students

Related Terms

  • Synonym: implant - to fix an idea, attitude, etc. firmly in someone's mind
  • Antonym: to eradicate - to remove or destroy something completely

Etymology

From Latin instillare (to drip in, inspire), from in- (in) + stillare (to drip).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈstɪl / (AmE), / ɪnˈstɪl / (BrE)
to gradually but firmly establish an idea, feeling, or attitude in a person's mind
gradually establish an idea or feeling

Examples

Her teacher hoped to instill a love of reading in all her students.
Parents often try to instill good manners in their children from an early age.
The coach works hard to instill confidence and discipline in the team.

Synset Examples

Mother tried to ingrain respect for our elders in us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: implant - to fix an idea firmly and deeply
  • Antonym: to eradicate - to remove completely, especially an idea or feeling

Etymology

From Latin 'instillare', meaning to drip in or impart drop by drop.

/ ɪnˈstɪl / (AmE), / ɪnˈstɪl / (BrE)

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin 'instillare' (to drop in, to put in gradually), from 'in-' (in) + 'stillare' (to drip).
