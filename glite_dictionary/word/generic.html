generic

1. ADJ

/ dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (AmE), / dʒɪˈnɛrɪk / (BrE)
(of a drug) not protected by a trademark and typically sold under its chemical name rather than a brand name.
not protected by trademark (pharmaceuticals)
medical

Examples

Doctors often prescribe generic drugs because they are more affordable than branded medications.
The pharmacy offers both brand-name and generic versions of the antibiotic.
After the patent expired, several companies started manufacturing generic painkillers.

Synset Examples

‘Acetaminophen’ is the generic form of the proprietary drug ‘Tylenol’

Related Terms

  • Antonym: branded - (of a drug) sold under a brand name and protected by trademark
  • Antonym: proprietary - (of a drug) protected by trademark and sold under a brand name

Etymology

From Latin 'genus' (kind, sort) via French 'générique'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01113310-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (AmE), / dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of a whole group or class; not specific or individual.
relating to a group or class, not specific

Examples

The scientist made a generic statement about mammals rather than discussing individual species.
Many people prefer generic solutions that can be widely applied instead of tailored approaches.
The store sells both brand-name and generic products that are essentially the same.

Synset Examples

is there a generic Asian mind?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: specific - clearly defined or identified; opposite of generic

Etymology

From Latin 'genus' (kind, type) via French 'générique'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01105857-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (AmE), / dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of a whole group, class, or genus rather than a specific member; general or not specific.
general or relating to a group or genus

Examples

The generic name of the plant refers to its entire genus, not just one species.
In scientific writing, a generic term can encompass a wide range of items.
The medication's generic label lists the main ingredients common to all brands.

Synset Examples

the generic name

Related Terms

  • Antonym: specific - clearly identified or relating to an individual member
  • Antonym: particular - relating to a single, specific member or instance

Etymology

From Latin 'genus' (birth, race, kind), via French 'générique'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02744752-a

4. NOUN

/ dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (AmE), / dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (BrE)
a product, especially a drug or other consumer good, sold without a brand name, often as a cheaper alternative to branded products
unbranded product, especially a drug

Examples

Many people opt for the generic because it offers the same quality at a lower price.
The pharmacy stocks both generic and brand-name medications for each prescription.
Consumers are often surprised by how effective a generic can be compared to its branded counterpart.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: brand-name - a product sold under a well-known brand name
  • Antonym: proprietary - an item manufactured and sold under the name of a particular company

Etymology

From Latin 'genus' (kind, race), via Medieval Latin 'genericus' (of a kind or genus).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03439338-n
Synset POS: n

5. NOUN

/ dʒəˈnɛrɪk / (AmE), / dʒɪˈnɛrɪk / (BrE)
A wine that does not qualify for designation by appellation of origin or varietal content and is labeled only by a general name, color, or class.
wine not identified by variety or origin

Examples

The restaurant served only generic with its lunch specials.
Some European producers market surplus grapes as generic rather than designated wine.
He preferred a labeled varietal over the cheaper generic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: table wine - wine of ordinary quality not labeled by variety or region

Etymology

From Latin 'genus' (kind, class) via Late Latin 'genericus'.
