communisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmjʊnɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒmjʊnaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The process of transferring private ownership or control to state or collective ownership, particularly under a communist system.
transfer to collective or state ownership

Examples

The communisation of land was a central feature of the new government's policy.
Opponents argued that communisation would reduce economic efficiency and innovation.
Debates over communisation were prominent during the country's transition to socialism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nationalization - the process of bringing private assets under state ownership

Etymology

From French 'communisation,' from 'commune' (common) + '-isation' (-ization).

2. NOUN

/ kəˌmjunəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / kəˌmjuːnaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of organizing a nation or society along the principles of communism
process of making something communist

Examples

The communisation of the country's industries brought profound economic changes.
Revolutionary theorists often debate the pathways to communisation in modern societies.
Following the revolution, the government began a process of communisation that affected land ownership and agriculture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: communism - a political and economic system based on common ownership

Etymology

From French 'communisation', based on 'commune' and the suffix '-isation'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmjənaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒmjʊnaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of changing private property into property owned by the community or the public
conversion of private property to community ownership

Examples

The government's program of communisation aimed to transfer all farmland to the villages.
Debates about communisation grew more intense during the early years of the revolution.
In some countries, communisation led to widespread social and economic changes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nationalization - the transfer of private assets into public ownership by a national government
  • Antonym: privatization - the transfer of public or community property into private ownership

Etymology

From French 'communisation', from 'communiser' (to make common), ultimately from Latin 'communis' (common).
