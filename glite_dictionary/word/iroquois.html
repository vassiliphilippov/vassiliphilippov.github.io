Iroquois

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪr.ə.kwɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈɪr.ə.kwɔɪ / (BrE)
A family of North American Indigenous languages traditionally spoken by the Iroquois peoples.
family of North American Indigenous languages

Examples

The Iroquois languages include Mohawk, Oneida, and Seneca.
Anthropologists study the structure of Iroquois to understand its influence on neighboring languages.
The decline in the number of Iroquois speakers has raised concerns among linguists.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Iroquoian - relating to or denoting the language family of the Iroquois

Etymology

From French 'Iroquois', of unknown origin, possibly from Haudenosaunee name.

2. PROPN

/ ˈɪrəkˌwɔɪ / (AmE), / ˈɪərəkwɔɪ / (BrE)
A member of any of a group of Native American peoples formerly living in what is now New York State, especially those belonging to the Iroquois League, which played a major role in early American history.
member of a North American Native people

Examples

The Iroquois were known for their powerful confederacy and influence in early American history.
Several Iroquois leaders played crucial roles in negotiations with European settlers.
Today, descendants of the Iroquois continue to preserve their cultural traditions and language.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Haudenosaunee - Self-designated name for the Iroquois; meaning 'People of the Longhouse'

Etymology

From French 'Iroquois', adopted from Algonquian, possibly meaning 'real adders' or 'rattlesnakes'.
