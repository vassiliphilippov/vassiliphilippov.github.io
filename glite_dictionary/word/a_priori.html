a priori

1. ADV

/ ˌeɪ praɪˈɔri / (AmE), / ˌeɪ praɪˈɔːri / (BrE)
Relating to reasoning or knowledge that is derived from theoretical deduction rather than from observation or experience.
from theoretical deduction, not observation

Examples

Many philosophers argue that moral principles can be known a priori, without relying on empirical evidence.
The theory was developed a priori, based solely on logical assumptions.
He rejected their claims, stating that they were not supported a priori by deductive reasoning.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: a posteriori - derived from observed facts rather than from theoretical deduction

Etymology

From Latin, meaning 'from the earlier'; used in philosophy since the 17th century.

2. ADJ

/ ˌeɪ praɪˈɔri / (AmE), / ˌeɪ praɪˈɔːraɪ / (BrE)
based on reasoning or knowledge that comes from theoretical deduction rather than from observation or experience; known independently of specific facts or evidence
based on theoretical deduction, not observation

Examples

Many philosophers distinguish between a priori and a posteriori knowledge.
The theorem was proven a priori, without reference to specific examples.
He dismissed the conclusion as purely a priori reasoning, lacking empirical support.

Synset Examples

an a priori judgment

Related Terms

  • Antonym: a posteriori - based on observation or empirical evidence rather than theory

Etymology

From Latin 'a priori' meaning 'from the former'; used in philosophy to mean knowledge based on theoretical deduction.

Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ˌeɪ praɪˈɔri, ˌɑː priˈɔri / (AmE), / ˌɑː priˈɔːri, ˌeɪ praɪˈɔːri / (BrE)
based on theoretical deduction rather than empirical observation or experiment
known or assumed without experience

Examples

The argument was criticized for relying solely on a priori assumptions.
His theory was developed a priori without direct experimental data.
Philosophers often debate the validity of a priori knowledge.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: a posteriori - deriving knowledge from experience or empirical evidence

Etymology

From Latin, meaning 'from the former', used in philosophical contexts since the 17th century.
