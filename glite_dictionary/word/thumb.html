thumb

1. VERB

/ θʌm / (AmE), / θʌm / (BrE)
to move, touch, or handle with the thumb or with the fingers, often in an exploratory or repeated way
handle or feel with the thumb or fingers

Examples

She thumbed the pages of the magazine while waiting for her appointment.
He absentmindedly thumbed the edge of the envelope as he thought.
The detective thumbed through the old photos looking for clues.

Synset Examples

finger the binding of the book

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finger - to touch or handle with the fingers

Etymology

From Old English 'þūma', of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01212517-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ θʌm / (AmE), / θʌm / (BrE)
to travel by getting free rides from passing vehicles, especially by signaling with an outstretched thumb
to travel by hitchhiking

Examples

They decided to thumb their way across Europe during their summer break.
He thumbed a ride to the nearest town after his car broke down.
She spent the afternoon thumbing along the highway, hoping someone would pick her up.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to hitchhike - to travel by getting free rides in passing vehicles

3. VERB

/ θʌm / (AmE), / θʌm / (BrE)
to turn the pages of a book or a document quickly, typically only glancing at the contents
turn pages quickly (e.g., in a book)

Examples

He thumbed through the magazine while waiting for his appointment.
She quickly thumbed through her notes before the exam began.
I often thumb through cookbooks for dinner ideas.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leaf - to turn pages quickly without careful reading

4. NOUN

/ θʌm / (AmE), / θʌm / (BrE)
the short, thick, innermost finger on the hand, set lower and apart from the other four fingers
short, thick finger on the hand

Examples

He accidentally cut his thumb while slicing vegetables in the kitchen.
The baby wrapped her tiny hand around her mother's thumb.
A sprained thumb made it hard for him to write notes during the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finger - any of the five digits at the end of the human hand

Etymology

Old English 'þūma', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'duim' and German 'Daumen'.

Source: WordNet
