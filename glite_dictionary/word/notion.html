notion

1. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈnəʊʃən / (BrE)
(usually plural) small articles used in sewing, such as buttons, pins, and thread, or other small personal items.
small sewing articles and personal items
american

Examples

My grandmother kept all her sewing notions in a wooden box by her armchair.
The shop sells fabrics, threads, and a wide variety of notions.
She bought some colorful notions, including buttons and ribbons, to decorate her dress.

Synset Examples

buttons and needles are notions

2. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈnəʊʃən / (BrE)
an idea, belief, or opinion, especially one that is fanciful, odd, or capricious
odd or fanciful idea

Examples

He suddenly had a notion to paint his room bright purple, despite his usual conservative taste.
The notion of talking animals amused the children throughout the story.
She followed a curious notion and ended up exploring the city without a map.

Synset Examples

the theatrical notion of disguise is associated with disaster in his stories
he had a whimsy about flying to the moon
whimsy can be humorous to someone with time to enjoy it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whim - a sudden or fanciful idea; impulse
  • Synonym: caprice - a sudden change of mood or behavior; whim
  • Synonym: fantasy - an idea with no basis in reality; imagination

Etymology

From Latin notio (concept, idea), from noscere (to know).

3. NOUN

/ ˈnoʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈnəʊʃən / (BrE)
an idea, belief, or opinion, especially one that is vague or not very certain
vague idea or belief

Examples

He had a notion that he could finish the project in one day, but he was mistaken.
She dismissed the notion that success comes without hard work.
The notion of moving abroad excited and terrified him at the same time.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: belief - an acceptance that something exists or is true, especially without proof
  • Antonym: certainty - something that is definitely true or known

Etymology

From Latin 'notio' (idea, conception), from 'noscere' (to know).

Examples
