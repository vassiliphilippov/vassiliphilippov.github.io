bipolar

1. ADJ

/ baɪˈpoʊ.lɚ / (AmE), / baɪˈpəʊ.lə / (BrE)
Relating to, or occurring in, both the Arctic and Antarctic polar regions.
occurring in both polar regions

Examples

Some seabirds have a bipolar distribution, breeding in both the Arctic and Antarctic.
The scientist studied the bipolar occurrence of certain plankton species.
Changes in climate can affect animals with a bipolar habitat range.

Synset Examples

the bipolar distribution of certain species

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two) + 'polar' (having poles).

2. ADJ

/ baɪˈpoʊlɚ / (AmE), / baɪˈpəʊlə / (BrE)
Relating to or affected by bipolar disorder, a mental illness characterized by episodes of mania and depression.
relating to bipolar disorder
medical

Examples

Her psychiatrist diagnosed her with bipolar disorder after several months of mood swings.
The bipolar patient experienced periods of both intense excitement and deep depression.
Advances in treatment have greatly improved the outlook for people with bipolar illness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manic-depressive - relating to manic-depressive illness (bipolar disorder)
  • Antonym: unipolar - relating to depression without manic episodes

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two) + 'polar' (having poles).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02849259-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ baɪˈpoʊlər / (AmE), / baɪˈpəʊlə / (BrE)
Having or involving two poles or extremes.
having two poles or extremes

Examples

A bipolar molecule has a positive and a negative end due to its charge distribution.
The Earth's magnetic field is bipolar, with distinct north and south poles.
This experiment demonstrates the characteristics of a bipolar electrical circuit.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unipolar - having only one pole
  • Degree Lower: polar - having a pole or relating to a pole

Etymology

From Latin 'bi-' (two) + 'polaris' (of/relating to a pole).
