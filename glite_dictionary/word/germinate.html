to germinate

1. VERB

/ ˈdʒɜːrmɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈdʒɜːmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to cause (a seed or spore) to begin to grow or sprout
cause to sprout or begin to grow

Examples

Consistent warmth and moisture will germinate the seeds quickly.
The gardener used a special light to germinate the tomato seeds indoors.
Sometimes old seeds require extra care to germinate successfully.

Synset Examples

the plentiful rain germinated my plants

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sprout - to begin to grow; shoot forth
  • Antonym: to wither - to become dry and shriveled (opposite of sprouting/germinating)

Etymology

From Latin germinatus, past participle of germinare, from germen (bud, sprout).

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒɜːrmɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈdʒɜːmɪneɪt / (BrE)
(of a seed or spore) to begin to grow and put out shoots after a period of dormancy.
begin to grow and develop (of seeds/spores)

Examples

The seeds need moist soil to germinate successfully.
After a week, the beans began to germinate in the window box.
Given the right conditions, some species of cacti germinate very quickly.

Synset Examples

the potatoes sprouted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sprout - to begin to grow; shoot forth, as a plant from a seed
  • Antonym: to wither - to become dry and shriveled

Etymology

From Latin germinatus, past participle of germinare (to sprout, bud), from germen (sprout, bud).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00357986-v
Synset POS: v
