popular

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɑpjələr / (AmE), / ˈpɒpjʊlə / (BrE)
liked or admired by many people or by the general public.
liked or admired by many people

Examples

The new smartphone quickly became popular among young people.
She was a popular teacher, admired by her students and colleagues.
Skiing is especially popular in the mountains during the winter months.

Synset Examples

a popular tourist attraction
a popular girl
cabbage patch dolls are no longer popular

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unpopular - not liked or approved by many people

Etymology

From Latin 'popularis' (belonging to the people), from 'populus' (people).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01821123-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈpɑːpjələr / (AmE), / ˈpɒpjʊlə / (BrE)
(of music or art) relating to styles that are new and widely enjoyed or sought after by the general public, especially young people.
new and widely enjoyed music or art

Examples

The band's popular sound resonated with teenagers across the country.
She prefers popular music over classical or jazz.
That song became popular almost overnight among young audiences.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mainstream - widely accepted music or art

Etymology

From Latin popularis (belonging to the people), from populus (people).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00416037-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈpɑː.pjələr / (AmE), / ˈpɒp.jʊ.lə / (BrE)
(of actions, institutions, etc.) carried on by or representing the people as a whole rather than a privileged group; of or relating to the populace at large.
of or by the people at large

Examples

The presidential election was decided by the popular vote rather than the electoral college.
Many reforms were achieved through popular movements and widespread public participation.
Constitutions of popular government aim to give power to ordinary citizens.

Synset Examples

the popular vote
popular representation
institutions of popular government

Related Terms

  • Synonym: democratic - relating to, or supporting democracy or its principles

Etymology

From Latin 'populus' (the people), via French 'populaire.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00719814-s
Synset POS: s

4. ADJ

/ ˈpɑːpjələr / (AmE), / ˈpɒpjʊlə / (BrE)
intended for or accessible to the general public; representing or appealing to ordinary people rather than specialists.
intended for ordinary people; accessible to the public

Examples

The magazine aims to make complicated science accessible through popular articles and explanations.
Her novel belongs to the genre of popular fiction that attracts a wide audience.
There is growing interest in the popular movement for accessible education.

Synset Examples

democratic art forms
a democratic or popular movement
popular thought
popular science
popular fiction

Related Terms

  • Synonym: democratic - representing or appealing to the people at large; for ordinary people

Etymology

From Latin popularis (of the people), from populus (people).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00488527-s
Synset POS: s
