cartoon

1. NOUN

/ kɑrˈtun / (AmE), / kɑːˈtuːn / (BrE)
a film made by photographing a sequence of drawings or images to create the illusion of movement; an animated film
animated film made from drawings

Examples

As a child, she loved watching Saturday morning cartoons on television.
The studio released a new cartoon featuring talking animals and colorful backgrounds.
Classic Disney cartoons like 'Snow White' are enjoyed by people of all ages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: animation - the technique of photographing successive drawings to create motion

Etymology

From Italian cartone (large sheet of paper), later applied to humorous drawings and animated films.

2. NOUN

/ kɑrˈtun / (AmE), / kɑːˈtuːn / (BrE)
a humorous or satirical drawing, especially one published in a newspaper or magazine, often conveying a message or commentary
humorous or satirical drawing in print

Examples

The political cartoon in today's newspaper made a clever point about the election.
He laughed out loud at the cartoon depicting the mayor as a cat.
Many magazines feature a weekly cartoon on their editorial page.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comic strip - a sequence of drawings in boxes that tell an amusing story, published in newspapers or magazines
  • Synonym: caricature - a drawing that exaggerates certain features for comic or satirical effect

Etymology

From Italian 'cartone' (large paper), originally referring to a preparatory drawing; later evolved to comic drawings.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06793517-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ kɑrˈtun / (AmE), / kɑːˈtuːn / (BrE)
to draw a humorous or satirical picture or series of pictures (a cartoon), often for publication in a newspaper or magazine
draw cartoons

Examples

The artist was asked to cartoon the leading politicians for the Sunday edition.
She loves to cartoon everyday situations with a witty twist.
He spent hours cartooning his classmates in the back of his notebook.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caricature - draw a representation exaggerating features for satirical effect

Etymology

From Italian 'cartone' (strong, heavy paper), via French 'carton', originally referring to the medium used; noun use developed to refer to comic drawings before the verb sense.
