obiter dictum

1. NOUN

/ ˈoʊ.bɪ.tər ˈdɪk.təm / (AmE), / ˈɒ.bɪ.tər ˈdɪk.təm / (BrE)
A remark or observation made by a judge in a legal opinion that is not essential to the decision and therefore not legally binding as precedent.
non-binding judicial remark
legal

Examples

The judge made an obiter dictum regarding issues not formally before the court.
Legal scholars often discuss the influence of obiter dictum in shaping future judicial decisions.
Although interesting, her comment was considered an obiter dictum and was not legally binding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dictum - an authoritative statement; a formal pronouncement

Etymology

From Latin 'obiter dictum', meaning 'something said in passing'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈoʊbɪtər ˈdɪktəm / (AmE), / ˈəʊbɪtə ˈdɪktəm / (BrE)
A judge's incidental remark or observation that is not essential to the decision and does not have binding authority as precedent.
incidental judicial remark (not binding)
legal

Examples

The judge's comments about social policy were merely obiter dictum and had no binding effect.
Legal scholars often distinguish between the court's decision and obiter dictum.
In this case, the reference to previous cases was held to be obiter dictum rather than part of the judgment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dictum - an authoritative or formal statement from a judge
  • Antonym: holding - the court's actual decision or ruling which is binding precedent

Etymology

From Latin obiter dictum, meaning 'something said in passing'.
