to unsex

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈsɛks / (AmE), / ʌnˈsɛks / (BrE)
to deprive someone or something of sexual characteristics or sexual powers
deprive of sexual characteristics

Examples

The hormone treatment may unsex the animals, leaving them infertile.
In the play, Lady Macbeth's famous line asks the spirits to unsex her, removing her feminine qualities.
Some procedures intentionally unsex wildlife as a form of population control.

Etymology

From prefix 'un-' (not) + 'sex' (biological distinction); earliest use in early 17th century.

2. VERB

/ ʌnˈsɛks / (AmE), / ʌnˈsɛks / (BrE)
to make infertile or to deprive of sexual characteristics
make infertile or deprive of sexual traits

Examples

Controversial policies in the past sought to unsex certain groups for eugenic reasons.
The procedure was designed to unsex animals to prevent uncontrolled breeding.
Some chemicals can inadvertently unsex fish populations by disrupting their endocrine systems.

Synset Examples

in some countries, people with genetically transmissible disabilities are sterilized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sterilize - to make unable to produce offspring

Etymology

From un- (not) + sex (from Latin sexus, sex, gender).

3. VERB

/ ʌnˈsɛks / (AmE), / ʌnˈsɛks / (BrE)
to deprive (someone or something) of the qualities or characteristics traditionally associated with their sex or gender.
remove sex-specific qualities

Examples

Lady Macbeth asked the spirits to unsex her so she could commit cruel acts.
Some critics argued that the film attempts to unsex traditional gender roles.
The character felt she had to unsex herself to be accepted in the male-dominated world.

Synset Examples

She unsexed herself

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'un-' (not, remove) and 'sex' (biological distinction), first recorded use in English circa 1600.
