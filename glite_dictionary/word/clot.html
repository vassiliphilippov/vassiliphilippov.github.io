clot

1. NOUN

/ klɑt / (AmE), / klɒt / (BrE)
a thick, lump-like mass formed when a liquid, especially blood, coagulates or solidifies
a lump formed from a liquid

Examples

Doctors discovered a clot in his artery during the examination.
Fat droplets can form a clot in soups if not stirred continuously.
The injury led to the formation of a blood clot that needed treatment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulate - to change from a fluid into a thickened mass
  • Antonym: fluid - a substance that flows, such as a liquid or gas

Etymology

From Old English 'clott' (lump, mass).

2. VERB

/ klɑt / (AmE), / klɒt / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to thicken or solidify, often by the formation of clumps; especially of blood or other fluids.
to thicken or solidify (of a liquid)

Examples

If the blood does not clot properly, it can lead to excessive bleeding.
Milk will clot when lemon juice is added to it.
The wound was slow to clot, prolonging the bleeding.

5. VERB

/ klɑt / (AmE), / klɒt / (BrE)
to cause to change from a liquid to a solid or thickened mass, especially by coagulation
to change from liquid to thickened mass

Examples

As the blood began to clot, the wound finally stopped bleeding.
If milk is left out too long, it will clot and turn sour.
Some chemicals can clot oil during a spill, making cleanup easier.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulate - to change from a liquid to a solid or semi-solid state
  • Antonym: to liquefy - to make or become liquid

Etymology
