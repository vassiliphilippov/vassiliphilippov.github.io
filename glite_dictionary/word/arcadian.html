Arcadian

1. NOUN

/ ɑːrˈkeɪdiən / (AmE), / ɑːˈkeɪdiən / (BrE)
A native or inhabitant of Arcadia, a region in ancient Greece, often idealized as a place of rural peace and simplicity.
inhabitant of Arcadia (ancient Greece)

Examples

The Arcadian shepherd gazed across the green valleys of his homeland.
Several poets described an idyllic life among the Arcadians in their writings.
Ancient historians documented the customs of the Arcadian people in detail.

Etymology

From Latin Arcadianus, from Greek Arkadios, meaning 'of Arcadia'.

2. ADJ

/ ɑːrˈkeɪdiən / (AmE), / ɑːˈkeɪdiən / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of an idealized, peaceful, rural life; idyllically simple and unspoiled, especially with reference to country life.
idyllically rustic; idealized country life

Examples

The novel evokes an Arcadian vision of rural life untouched by modern woes.
They spent their vacation in an Arcadian village, surrounded by rolling green hills and quiet streams.
Painters of the Romantic period often depicted Arcadian landscapes filled with shepherds and peaceful meadows.

Synset Examples

a country life of arcadian contentment
a pleasant bucolic scene
charming in its pastoral setting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bucolic - relating to the pleasant aspects of the countryside and country life
  • Synonym: pastoral - associated with rural life or shepherds; idyllically rustic

Etymology

From Latin 'Arcadius', from Greek 'Arkadia' (a region in ancient Greece noted for its rural simplicity).
