humanism

1. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (BrE)
the doctrine that people's chief duty is to promote human welfare and human values, emphasizing the importance of human beings individually and collectively
doctrine emphasizing human welfare and values

Examples

Her support for education reform is inspired by a strong belief in humanism.
Many Enlightenment thinkers promoted humanism as a fundamental principle of modern society.
The charity is guided by the ideals of humanism, putting people's needs at the center of its work.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: philanthropy - the desire to promote the welfare of others, especially by generous donation
  • Antonym: egoism - the doctrine that self-interest is the foundation of morality

Etymology

From Latin humanus (human, humane) + -ism.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (BrE)
The doctrine or belief that emphasizes the value and agency of human beings, stressing reason, individual self-realization, and often rejecting religious or supernatural explanations.
doctrine emphasizing reason and human agency

Examples

Modern humanism promotes the idea that people can lead fulfilling lives without belief in the supernatural.
Her commitment to humanism guided her work in education and social reform.
Many philosophers argue that humanism encourages critical thinking and ethical responsibility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secularism - the principle of separation of the state from religious institutions
  • Antonym: spiritualism - belief that the spirit exists as distinct from matter, or the belief in communication with spirits

Etymology

From Latin humanismus, from humanus (human).

3. NOUN

/ ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmənɪzəm / (BrE)
The cultural and intellectual movement of the Renaissance that focused on the study of classical antiquity and emphasized human values, achievements, and capacities.
Renaissance cultural movement based on classical studies

Examples

The rise of humanism transformed education and philosophy in Europe during the Renaissance.
Many Renaissance artists were influenced by the ideals of humanism and sought inspiration from classical art.
Studying Latin and Greek texts was central to the spread of humanism in the fifteenth century.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Renaissance - The period of European history at the close of the Middle Ages, marked by a revival of classical learning and art

Etymology

From Italian umanismo, from umano (human), ultimately from Latin humanus (human).
