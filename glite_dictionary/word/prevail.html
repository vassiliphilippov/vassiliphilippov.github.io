to prevail

1. VERB

/ prɪˈveɪl / (AmE), / prɪˈveɪl / (BrE)
to persuade someone to do something, often after repeated effort (usually used as 'prevail upon/on someone')
persuade someone after effort

Examples

After much discussion, he finally prevailed upon his friends to change their travel plans.
It took a lot of effort to prevail on her to accept the job offer.
She prevailed upon her parents to allow her to study abroad.

Synset Examples

He prevailed upon her to visit his parents

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to convince - to cause someone to believe or do something by argument or persuasion

Etymology

From Latin 'praevalere' (to be stronger).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00779838-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ prəˈveɪl / (AmE), / prɪˈveɪl / (BrE)
to be greater in number, power, influence, or importance than others; to be dominant or widespread
be dominant or widespread

Examples

Although new technologies are emerging, traditional methods still prevail in many rural areas.
In the heated debate, reason and civility did not prevail over anger.
A sense of optimism prevailed among the team despite earlier setbacks.

Synset Examples

Money reigns supreme here
Hispanics predominate in this neighborhood

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dominate - to have control or power over
  • Synonym: to predominate - to be the largest in number or have more influence
  • Antonym: yield - to give way or be overcome by others

Etymology

From Latin praevalere (to be more powerful), from prae- (before) + valere (to be strong).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02650318-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ prɪˈveɪl / (AmE), / prɪˈveɪl / (BrE)
to prove more powerful or superior; to win in the end, especially after a struggle or contest
prove superior or win

Examples

Despite many obstacles, the team managed to prevail and secure the championship.
In the courtroom, justice must prevail above all else.
After hours of debate, reason finally prevailed among the group.

Synset Examples

The champion prevailed, though it was a hard fight

Related Terms

  • Synonym: triumph - to achieve a victory or success
  • Antonym: to lose - to be defeated in a contest or struggle

Etymology

From Latin praevalere (to be stronger), from prae- (before) + valere (to be strong).

/ prɪˈveɪl / (AmE), / prɪˈveɪl / (BrE)

continue to be valid or applicable

The theory prevails even after decades of scientific debate.

5. VERB

/ prɪˈveɪl / (AmE), / prɪˈveɪl / (BrE)
(of an idea, belief, custom, or condition) to continue to exist and be accepted, especially for a long time, despite difficulties or opposition.
to continue to exist or be accepted

Examples

Ancient traditions still prevail in some rural communities.
Despite many challenges, the old beliefs prevail among the villagers.
Superstitions sometimes prevail long after science has provided explanations.

Synset Examples

These stories die hard
The legend of Elvis endures

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endure - to continue to exist; to last over time
  • Synonym: to persist - to continue to exist or happen, especially for longer than expected
  • Antonym: fade - to gradually disappear or lose importance

Etymology

From Latin praevalēre, from prae- (before) + valēre (to be strong).
