minstrel

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪnstrəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnstrəl / (BrE)
a performer in a traditional minstrel show, typically involving music, comedy, and variety acts, historically associated with blackface performance in 19th- and early 20th-century America.
performer in a minstrel show

Examples

Many minstrels in the 19th century performed in blackface, a legacy now widely condemned.
The traveling minstrel entertained crowds with comedy and music during the show.
Historians study the costumes and music used by minstrels on stage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: performer - someone who entertains an audience

Etymology

From Old French 'menestrel' (servant, entertainer), from Late Latin 'ministralis' (servant), related to 'minister.'

2. VERB

/ ˈmɪnstrəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnstrəl / (BrE)
(archaic) To celebrate or entertain by singing, in the manner of minstrels.
celebrate by singing as minstrels did
archaic

Examples

The bard minstrelled in the great hall, reviving tales of heroism and love.
On festival nights, villagers minstreled together beneath the stars.
She minstreling her joys, filled the air with sweet melodies.

Etymology

From Old French menestrel (entertainer, servant), from Late Latin ministerialis.

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɪnstrəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnstrəl / (BrE)
a singer or musician, especially one of medieval times, who sang or recited poetry and played music, often traveling from place to place
medieval singer or musician

Examples

The minstrel entertained the crowded hall with soulful ballads.
In the castle courtyard, a wandering minstrel performed stories of heroes and legends.
The minstrels of old played a vital role in medieval storytelling and music.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bard - a poet and singer, especially one reciting epics and associated with a particular oral tradition

Etymology

From Old French menestrel (entertainer, servant), from Late Latin ministerialis (servant), ultimately from Latin minister (servant, attendant).
