boarder

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdər / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
a student who lives and sleeps at school during term time rather than traveling home each day
student living at school

Examples

She was a boarder at a prestigious private school for six years.
The school provides separate dormitories for boarders and day pupils.
As a boarder, he often stayed on campus over the weekends.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: day student - a student who attends school but does not live there
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09881751-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdɚ / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
a person who pays to live and have meals in someone else's house
person lodging and eating in someone's home

Examples

After Mary lost her job, she became a boarder in Mrs. White's house to save money.
The family decided to take in a boarder to help cover their bills.
Each boarder had their own room but ate meals together with the host family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lodger - a person who rents accommodation in another person's house
  • Synonym: tenant - a person who rents a property from a landlord

Etymology

From 'board' (in the sense of 'table for meals'), from Old English 'bord' (table).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdər / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
a person who forcibly enters a ship, especially during an attack or hostile action
person who forcibly boards a ship

Examples

The crew prepared themselves to repel the boarders as enemy ships approached.
Pirates acted as boarders, climbing onto merchant vessels to plunder their cargo.
Historical naval battles often involved hand-to-hand combat between boarders and defenders.

Synset Examples

stand by to repel boarders

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attacker - a person who attacks someone or something
  • Antonym: defender - a person who protects from attack

Etymology

From 'board' (to get onto a ship) + -er (agent noun suffix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09881885-n
Synset POS: n
