tributary

1. ADJ

/ ˈtrɪb.jəˌtɛɹ.i / (AmE), / ˈtrɪb.jʊ.tər.i / (BrE)
(of a stream or river) flowing into a larger stream or river rather than directly into the sea.
(of a stream) flowing into a larger stream

Examples

The tributary stream joined the main river just north of the village.
Several tributary creeks feed into the larger river during the rainy season.
A tributary channel carries water from the hills to the main waterway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confluent - (of streams or rivers) flowing together; merging

Etymology

From Latin tributarius, from tributum (tribute).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrɪbjəˌtɛri / (AmE), / ˈtrɪbjʊtəri / (BrE)
a river or stream that flows into a larger river or lake
a stream flowing into a larger one

Examples

The Amazon River has many tributaries feeding into it from the rainforest.
The town is situated at the junction of the main river and a small tributary.
Flooding occurred when the tributary overflowed its banks after heavy rain.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: distributary - a branch of a river that flows away from the main stream

Etymology

From Latin tributarius (paying tribute), from tributum (tribute). Refers to lesser streams 'paying tribute' to a larger river.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09301903-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈtrɪbjəˌtɛri / (AmE), / ˈtrɪbjʊtəri / (BrE)
(of a country, state, or people) paying tribute to another, especially as a sign of dependence or submission.
paying tribute to another power

Examples

The smaller kingdom became a tributary state after its defeat in battle.
Several ancient empires maintained control over tributary colonies across vast regions.
The city remained tributary to its powerful neighbor for centuries.

Synset Examples

a tributary colony

Related Terms

  • Synonym: subject - under the control of another as a subject nation
  • Antonym: independent - not subject to another's rule or not paying tribute

Etymology

From Latin tributarius (pertaining to tribute), from tributus (paid, granted).
