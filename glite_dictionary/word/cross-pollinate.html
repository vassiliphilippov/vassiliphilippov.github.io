to cross-pollinate

1. VERB

/ ˌkrɑs ˈpɑləˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˌkrɒs ˈpɒlɪneɪt / (BrE)
to fertilize a plant by transferring pollen from the flower of one plant to the flower of another, typically of a different variety or species
to fertilize by transferring pollen

Examples

The gardener decided to cross-pollinate the two rose varieties to create a new color.
Bees often cross-pollinate flowers as they move from one plant to another.
Scientists cross-pollinated the crops to improve their resistance to disease.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-pollinate - to fertilize a plant with its own pollen

Etymology

Formed from 'cross-' (indicating between different plants) and 'pollinate' (from pollen).

2. VERB

/ krɔs ˈpɑləˌneɪt / (AmE), / krɒs ˈpɒləˌneɪt / (BrE)
to combine or exchange ideas, methods, or influences from different fields or groups, especially to create something new or innovative
to mix ideas from different fields

Examples

The conference was designed to cross-pollinate ideas between engineers and artists.
Successful companies often cross-pollinate different teams to inspire innovation.
She encouraged her students to cross-pollinate their knowledge from various disciplines.

Synset Examples

Their ideas began to cross-pollinate during the workshop.
The project thrived due to the cross-pollination of diverse perspectives.
Artists and scientists often benefit from cross-pollination of their disciplines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to collaborate - to work together with others to achieve something
  • Antonym: to isolate - to keep something separate from others, avoid influence

Etymology

Formed from 'cross-' + 'pollinate', originally from horticultural terminology.

3. VERB

/ ˌkrɔs ˈpɑl.əˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˌkrɒs ˈpɒl.ɪ.neɪt / (BrE)
to exchange and combine different ideas, methods, or styles to produce something new or improved
combine and exchange ideas

Examples

If we cross-pollinate our thoughts, we might develop more creative solutions.
The conference allowed experts from different fields to cross-pollinate new ideas.
Teachers often cross-pollinate their teaching methods by sharing strategies with colleagues.

Synset Examples

We should cross-pollinate our thoughts for better solutions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to brainstorm - to generate ideas as a group
  • Synonym: to collaborate - to work together to produce something

Etymology

From botany: 'cross-' (across) + 'pollinate' (to transfer pollen), originally referring to plants; first used figuratively in the mid-20th century.
