indirect

1. ADJ

/ ˌɪn.dəˈrɛkt, ˌɪn.daɪˈrɛkt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.dɪˈrɛkt, ˌɪn.daɪˈrɛkt / (BrE)
not resulting as a direct effect or consequence; happening through an intermediate step, person, or thing
not as a direct effect or result

Examples

The new policy provides indirect benefits to rural communities.
Many people experienced indirect effects from the economic downturn.
Her influence on the project was mostly indirect, through her colleagues.

Synset Examples

indirect benefits
an indirect advantage

Related Terms

  • Antonym: direct - without any intervening agency or step; straight; immediate

Etymology

From Latin indirectus, from in- ‘not’ + directus ‘straight, direct’.

3. ADJ

Examples

Soft indirect lighting made the room feel more comfortable.

Related Terms

5. ADJ

/ ˌɪn.dəˈrɛkt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.dɪˈrɛkt / (BrE)
(of descent or relationship) not in a direct ancestral line but related through a collateral line or different branch of the family.
not in direct ancestral line

Examples

He is an indirect descendant of the original founder of the estate.
The family traced their indirect relationship back several generations.
As indirect heirs, they were not first in line to inherit the property.

Synset Examples

cousins are collateral relatives
an indirect descendant of the Stuarts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: collateral - relating to descent from a common ancestor but through a different line

Etymology

From Latin indirectus, from in- (not) + directus (straight, direct).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01420079-a
Synset POS: a
