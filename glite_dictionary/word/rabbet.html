rabbet

1. NOUN

/ ˈræbɪt / (AmE), / ˈræbɪt / (BrE)
a rectangular groove cut along the edge or end of a piece of wood or other material to receive another piece and form a joint
rectangular groove for joining pieces

Examples

The carpenter cut a rabbet along the edge of the board to fit the back panel.
To create a strong corner joint, a rabbet is often used in cabinet making.
She measured the width of the rabbet before attaching the frame.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groove - a long narrow cut or depression in a surface

Etymology

From Old French 'rabet', possibly of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈræbɪt / (AmE), / ˈræbɪt / (BrE)
to join two pieces of wood or other material using a rabbet joint (a recess or groove cut into the edge of a piece to receive another piece)
join using a rabbet joint

Examples

He decided to rabbet the edges of the boards to create a stronger joint.
When you rabbet properly, the shelves fit snugly into the cabinet sides.
The carpenter rabbeted the frame pieces before attaching the panel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: notch - to cut a notch as in woodworking

Etymology

From Old French rabat (a recess), influenced by rabattu (beaten down); related to the noun 'rabbet'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01298496-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈræb.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈræb.ɪt / (BrE)
to cut a rectangular groove along the edge or face of a piece of wood, usually to form a joint
cut a rectangular groove (in wood)

Examples

The carpenter will rabbet the edge of the board to fit the window frame.
You need a special tool to rabbet wood cleanly and accurately.
He rabbeted the panels so they would join flush with the frame.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groove - a long, narrow cut or depression

Etymology

From Old French *rabbat*, from *rabattre* (to beat down).
