solmization

1. NOUN

/ ˌsoʊlməˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɒlmɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the system of designating the notes of a musical scale by syllables (such as do, re, mi) instead of letters
naming musical notes with syllables

Examples

The choir practiced solmization to help members learn their parts quickly.
Medieval theorists used solmization to teach singers the basics of music.
Modern music education often introduces children to solmization with the do-re-mi system.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solfège - the teaching of pitch and sight singing using syllables

Etymology

From French 'solmisation', ultimately from the syllables 'sol' and 'mi' used in early scale naming.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsoʊlmɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɒlmɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or system of assigning syllables (such as do, re, mi, etc.) to the notes of a musical scale, especially for singing and teaching music
singing notes with syllables (do, re, mi, etc.)

Examples

Young students often learn to sing using solmization to help them recognize musical notes.
The teacher introduced solmization as a way to improve our sight-reading skills.
Through solmization, singers relate each note to a specific syllable, such as do, re, and mi.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solfège - system of using syllables to teach pitch and sight-singing

Etymology

From Medieval Latin solmizatio, based on the syllables 'sol' and 'mi' used in singing exercises.
