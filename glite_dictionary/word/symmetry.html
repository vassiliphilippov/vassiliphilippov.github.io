symmetry

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪm.ə.tri / (AmE), / ˈsɪm.ɪ.tri / (BrE)
(Physics) The property of having identical values or characteristics when measured in any direction; isotropy.
property of being the same in every direction (physics)

Examples

In physics, symmetry often refers to invariance under a set of transformations or directions.
The laws of nature exhibit a remarkable degree of symmetry across space and time.
Rotational symmetry in a sphere means its properties remain constant in every direction.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asymmetry - lack of symmetry; not identical in all directions

Etymology

From Greek *symmetria* (agreement in dimensions, proportion), from *syn-* (together) + *metron* (measure).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪmətri / (AmE), / ˈsɪmɪtri / (BrE)
the quality of having identical or similar parts facing each other or around an axis, especially in shapes or figures; exact correspondence on opposite sides of a dividing line or plane
exact correspondence of parts on opposite sides

Examples

The butterfly's wings display remarkable symmetry on each side of its body.
Architects often use symmetry to create aesthetically pleasing building designs.
In mathematics, a circle has complete symmetry about its center.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asymmetry - lack of symmetry; absence of equal arrangement on opposite sides
  • Synonym: balance - an even distribution of weight or elements

Etymology

From Greek *summetria* (commensurateness, symmetry), from *syn-* (together) + *metron* (measure).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɪmətri / (AmE), / ˈsɪmətri / (BrE)
the quality of having parts that match each other, especially in shape, size, or arrangement
balance and correspondence among parts

Examples

The symmetry of the building made it especially pleasing to the eye.
Butterfly wings are known for their almost perfect symmetry.
Artists often strive for symmetry when designing patterns or sculptures.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: asymmetry - lack of symmetry or balance among parts
  • Synonym: proportion - the relationship of sizes or amounts between parts

Etymology

From Greek 'summetria' (commensurability, proportion), from 'sym-' (together) + 'metron' (measure).
