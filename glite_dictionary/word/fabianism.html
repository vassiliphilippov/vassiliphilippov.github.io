Fabianism

1. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪ.bi.əˌnɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈfeɪ.bɪ.ə.nɪz.əm / (BrE)
The belief in bringing about socialism gradually through reform rather than revolution, in accordance with the law.
gradualist socialism through legal reform

Examples

The British Labour Party was influenced by Fabianism in its early years.
Advocates of Fabianism prefer progressive social change through legislation rather than abrupt revolution.
He wrote a thesis on the impact of Fabianism on modern welfare states.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: socialism - a political and economic theory advocating collective or governmental ownership and administration of the means of production
  • Degree Higher: revolutionary socialism - the doctrine that socialism should be achieved through revolution

Etymology

From the Fabian Society, named after the Roman general Fabius Maximus, noted for his gradual tactics.
