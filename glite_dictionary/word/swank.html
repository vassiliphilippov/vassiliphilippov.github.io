swank

1. VERB

/ swæŋk / (AmE), / swæŋk / (BrE)
to display oneself or one's possessions in an ostentatious or pretentious way; to show off
show off ostentatiously
informal

Examples

He loves to swank around town in his luxury car.
She swanked her designer handbag at every opportunity.
They couldn't resist swanking about their recent holiday to Bali.

Synset Examples

he showed off his new sports car

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to show off - to boastfully display something; act ostentatiously
  • Synonym: to flaunt - to display ostentatiously in order to provoke envy or admiration

Etymology

Probably from dialectal 'swank' (to strut), perhaps related to German 'schwanken' (to sway).

2. NOUN

/ swæŋk / (AmE), / swæŋk / (BrE)
Stylish or showy elegance, especially in clothing, appearance, or manners.
stylish elegance; fashionable showiness

Examples

The new restaurant is all swank and glamour, attracting celebrities and trendsetters.
She entered the ballroom with an air of effortless swank that turned heads.
His apartment had a certain swank, decorated with modern art and designer furniture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: style - elegant or fashionable manner or appearance
  • Synonym: elegance - refined grace or tasteful richness in appearance or style
  • Antonym: plainness - lack of adornment or flamboyance; simplicity

Etymology

Probably from English dialect swank 'to swagger or show off'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04820943-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ swæŋk / (AmE), / swæŋk / (BrE)
Fashionable and elegant in an impressive or showy way.
imposingly fashionable and elegant

Examples

They hosted a party at their swank downtown loft.
He drove up in a swank new car that caught everyone's attention.
The hotel lobby was decorated in a swank and modern style.

Synset Examples

a swank apartment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stylish - fashionable and attractive in appearance
  • Synonym: luxurious - extremely comfortable or elegant
  • Antonym: modest - not showy or elaborate

Etymology

Probably from British dialect, meaning 'to strut' or 'swagger', origin uncertain.
