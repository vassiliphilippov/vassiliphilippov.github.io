ordination

1. NOUN

/ ˌɔːrdəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the logical or methodical arrangement of separate elements or parts
methodical arrangement of elements

Examples

The ordination of the data made the analysis much clearer for the research team.
A good essay relies on the careful ordination of supporting arguments.
She admired the ordination of colors in the artist's composition.

Synset Examples

we shall consider these questions in the inverse order of their presentation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: organization - the act of arranging elements in a structured way
  • Antonym: disorder - lack of arrangement or order

Etymology

From Latin 'ordinatio' (arrangement, order), from 'ordinare' (to put in order).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɔrdnˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or ceremony of appointing someone to a religious office, especially as a priest, minister, or rabbi
religious appointment ceremony

Examples

After his ordination, he began serving as a parish priest in the countryside.
The cathedral was filled with friends and family for her ordination as a minister.
He spent years studying theology before finally receiving ordination into the clergy.

Synset Examples

the rabbi's family was present for his ordination

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consecration - the act of making something sacred, often in a religious rite
  • Synonym: installation - the act of formally placing someone in an office or position

Etymology

From Latin 'ordinatio' (arrangement, appointment).

3. NOUN

/ ˌɔːr.dəˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɔː.dɪˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the status or act of being formally invested with priestly or ministerial authority in a religious ceremony
formal investiture into religious office

Examples

After his ordination, he began serving the local parish as a priest.
Her family attended her ordination ceremony at the cathedral.
He spent years in theological study before finally receiving ordination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consecration - the act of making something sacred, especially a religious office
  • Antonym: deposition - removal from a religious office

Etymology

From Latin ordinatio (arrangement, appointment), from ordinare (to arrange, appoint).
