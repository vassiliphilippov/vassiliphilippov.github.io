fluidity

1. NOUN

/ fluˈɪdəti / (AmE), / fluˈɪdəti / (BrE)
the quality or state of being able to flow easily, like a liquid; the ability of a substance to flow smoothly
ability of a substance to flow easily

Examples

The fluidity of the new paint allows it to spread smoothly across the canvas.
Engineers measured the fluidity of the molten metal before casting the parts.
Rising temperatures increase the fluidity of ice, causing it to melt more quickly.

Synset Examples

adding lead makes the alloy easier to cast because the melting point is reduced and the fluidity is increased
they believe that fluidity increases as the water gets warmer

Related Terms

  • Antonym: viscosity - the thickness or resistance to flow of a liquid

Etymology

From French 'fluidité', from Latin 'fluidus' (flowing) + '-ity'.

2. NOUN

/ fluˈɪdəti / (AmE), / fluˈɪdəti / (BrE)
the quality of being able to change or adapt easily; changeability or adaptability
changeable or adaptable quality

Examples

The fluidity of her thinking made her adaptable to changing circumstances.
There is a certain fluidity in modern job roles that wasn't present decades ago.
Cultural fluidity has increased with globalization and frequent travel.

Synset Examples

a charming Oriental fluidity of manner
a certain fluidness in his perception of time made him an unpredictable colleague
demographers try to predict social fluidity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rigidity - the quality of being inflexible or resistant to change

Etymology

From Latin 'fluiditas', from 'fluidus' (flowing, fluid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04741512-n
Synset POS: n
