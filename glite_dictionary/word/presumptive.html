presumptive

1. ADJ

/ prɪˈzʌmptɪv / (AmE), / prɪˈzʌmptɪv / (BrE)
Affording reasonable grounds for belief or acceptance, though not conclusive; based on a presumption.
affording reasonable grounds for belief

Examples

There was presumptive evidence of fraud in the company’s financial records.
The defendant's guilt was supported by presumptive facts but not proved beyond doubt.
She is the presumptive nominee according to the latest polls.

Synset Examples

presumptive evidence
a strong presumptive case is made out

Related Terms

  • Antonym: conclusive - Serving to prove a case; decisive or convincing
  • Synonym: probable - Likely to be true or to happen

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *praesumptivus* (conjectural), from Latin *praesumptus* (taken beforehand).

2. ADJ

/ prɪˈzʌmptɪv / (AmE), / prɪˈzʌmptɪv / (BrE)
having a reasonable basis for belief, assumption, or acceptance; likely to be considered true or real based on available evidence
likely based on reasonable evidence

Examples

The committee named her the presumptive nominee for the upcoming election.
He is the presumptive winner based on the current vote count.
The will identified Daniel as the presumptive heir to the estate.

Synset Examples

the presumptive heir (or heir apparent)

Related Terms

  • Synonym: probable - likely to happen or be true
  • Antonym: unlikely - not likely to happen or be true

Etymology

From Middle French *présumptif*, from Late Latin *praesumptivus* (assumed), from Latin *praesumptio* (assumption).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01416413-s
Synset POS: s
