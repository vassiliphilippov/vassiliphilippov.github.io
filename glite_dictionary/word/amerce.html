to amerce

1. VERB

/ əˈmɜrs / (AmE), / əˈmɜːs / (BrE)
(of a court) to punish someone with a fine imposed at its discretion, rather than according to a fixed statute.
impose a discretionary fine (legal)
legal

Examples

The court decided to amerce the company for violating safety regulations.
According to the old laws, the sheriff could amerce an offender without a jury.
The judge threatened to amerce anyone disrupting the trial with a heavy fine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fine - to punish by making someone pay money as a penalty

Etymology

From Old French *amesurer* (to fine), influenced by Medieval Latin *amiciare*, from Latin *ad-* (to) + *merces* (reward, fee).

2. VERB

/ əˈmɜrs / (AmE), / əˈmɜːs / (BrE)
to punish or penalize someone by imposing a discretionary or arbitrary fine, especially used in historical legal contexts.
impose an arbitrary fine as punishment
archaic legal

Examples

The court decided to amerce the defendant rather than sentence him to prison.
In medieval times, village leaders could amerce wrongdoers without a fixed scale of penalties.
The landowner was amerced for failing to uphold the common law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fine - to charge someone a monetary penalty as punishment for an offence

Etymology

From Old French 'amercier', from 'à merci' (at mercy), meaning at the mercy (of a court).
