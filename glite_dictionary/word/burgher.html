burgher

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɜrɡər / (AmE), / ˈbɜːɡə / (BrE)
A citizen of a town or borough, especially in medieval England or in historical contexts.
citizen of a town or borough

Examples

In medieval times, only a burgher could vote in the town council elections.
The rights of a burgher were distinct from those of peasants or nobles.
Several prominent burghers contributed to the construction of the city hall.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: citizen - an inhabitant of a particular place; a member of a community

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *burgher* (citizen), related to *burg* (town, fortress); influenced by Old English *burg* (fortified place).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɝːɡər / (AmE), / ˈbɜːɡə / (BrE)
A citizen of a town or city, typically a member of the wealthy middle class, especially in medieval and early modern European towns.
A middle-class citizen of a town or city

Examples

During the Renaissance, a wealthy burgher could wield significant influence in local politics.
The burghers of the city gathered in the market square to discuss new trade regulations.
He was proud to be a burgher and took part in civic events and festivals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: townsman - a resident or citizen of a town
  • Antonym: peasant - a poor farmer of low social status

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'burgher' (citizen), from 'burg' (fortified town).
