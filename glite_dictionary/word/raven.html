raven

1. NOUN

/ ˈreɪ.vən / (AmE), / ˈreɪ.vən / (BrE)
a large, mainly black bird with a straight bill and long, wedge-shaped tail, known for its intelligence and loud, harsh call
large black bird with wedge-shaped tail

Examples

A solitary raven circled above the cliffs, its croak echoing through the canyon.
The raven is known in many cultures as a symbol of mystery and intelligence.
We watched a pair of ravens nesting high in the pine tree near our cabin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crow - a smaller black bird related to the raven

Etymology

Old English *hræfn*, related to Dutch *raaf* and German *Rabe*.

2. VERB

/ ˈreɪvən / (AmE), / ˈreɪvən / (BrE)
(archaic) To seize or carry off something by violence; to plunder or prey upon.
to seize or plunder violently (archaic)
archaic

Examples

The marauders would raven the countryside, leaving destruction in their wake.
Armies used to raven villages, taking whatever they could by force.
The pirates ravened the coastal towns, looting and burning as they went.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunder - to steal goods, typically using force and in a time of disorder
  • Synonym: pillage - to rob using violence, especially in wartime
  • Antonym: to protect - to keep safe from harm or injury

Etymology

From Old French 'raviner' (to ravage), from Latin 'rapina' (robbery).
