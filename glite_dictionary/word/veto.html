veto

1. NOUN

/ ˈvitoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
the power or right to reject a decision or proposal made by a law-making body; a vote or decision that prevents something from happening
the power to block decisions or laws

Examples

The president used his veto to block the proposed legislation.
Any member of the Security Council can exercise a veto on major issues.
A single veto can overturn a majority decision in some organizations.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: approval - the act of accepting or consenting to something
  • Synonym: block - to prevent something from happening or continuing

Etymology

From Latin *veto* ('I forbid'), from *vetare* ('to forbid').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00210395-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈviːtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
to formally reject or refuse to approve a decision, proposal, or law, especially by exercising official authority
formally reject a proposal or law

Examples

The President threatened to veto the proposed tax legislation.
If the committee members disagree, they can veto the plan.
The mayor vetoed the new city ordinance yesterday.

Synset Examples

The President vetoed the bill

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to approve - to officially accept or agree to something
  • Synonym: reject - to refuse to accept, allow, or consider something

Etymology

From Latin 'veto' (I forbid), first person singular of 'vetare' (to forbid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02478854-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈviːtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
the right or power of a person in authority, especially a chief executive, to reject or prohibit a proposed law or decision
the right to reject proposals or laws

Examples

The president used his veto to block the proposed healthcare legislation.
A two-thirds majority is required in the assembly to override a veto.
The governor's veto prevented the bill from becoming law.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to approve - to accept or agree to a proposal or decision

Etymology

From Latin veto (I forbid), from vetare (to forbid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05206196-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈviːtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
to refuse to allow something to happen or to officially reject a decision or proposal
officially reject or forbid

Examples

The president threatened to veto the new law if it passed both houses of Congress.
She decided to veto the idea of getting a dog after thinking it over.
The committee can veto any proposal that doesn't meet their criteria.

Synset Examples

I forbid you to call me late at night
Mother vetoed the trip to the chocolate store
Dad nixed our plans

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to approve - to accept or officially agree to
  • Synonym: to forbid - to command someone not to do something

Etymology

From Latin veto, meaning 'I forbid.'

Source: WordNet
