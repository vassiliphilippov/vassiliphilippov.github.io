morphology

1. NOUN

/ mɔrˈfɑlədʒi / (AmE), / mɔːˈfɒlədʒi / (BrE)
the branch of geology that studies the characteristics, configuration, and evolution of rocks and landforms
study of form of rocks and landforms in geology

Examples

The morphology of the region reveals evidence of ancient volcanic activity.
Geologists use morphology to interpret how natural forces have shaped the landscape over time.
Understanding the morphology of coastal areas is crucial for studying erosion and sediment deposition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: geomorphology - the scientific study of landforms and the processes that shape them

Etymology

From Greek morphē (form) + -logia (study of).

3. NOUN

/ mɔːrˈfɑːlədʒi / (AmE), / mɔːˈfɒlədʒi / (BrE)
the branch of linguistics that studies the structure, formation, and classification of words.
study of word formation in linguistics

Examples

The morphology of English allows for the creation of new words by adding prefixes or suffixes.
Students studied the morphology of verbs to understand irregular word forms.
In linguistics, morphology is essential for analyzing how words change meaning or function.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: syntax - the arrangement of words and phrases to create sentences
  • Synonym: phonology - the study of the sound system of languages

Etymology

From Greek morphe (form) + -logy (study of).

4. NOUN

/ mɔːrˈfɑlədʒi / (AmE), / mɔːˈfɒlədʒi / (BrE)
The study of the structure and form of words in language, including the ways in which sounds are arranged to create words.
study of word structure and formation

Examples

Linguists often compare the morphology of different languages to understand how words are formed.
English morphology allows for the creation of new words through prefixes and suffixes.
The morphology of a word includes the rules for arranging its sounds and meaningful parts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: phonology - study of the sound system of a language

Etymology

From Greek morphē (form) + -logia (study).
