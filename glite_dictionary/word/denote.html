to denote

1. VERB

/ dɪˈnoʊt / (AmE), / dɪˈnəʊt / (BrE)
to be a sign or indication of something; to represent or mean something
be a sign or indication of

Examples

A red flag often denotes danger in many countries.
The sign on the door denotes that smoking is not allowed.
In mathematics, 'π' denotes the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter.

Synset Examples

Her smile denoted that she agreed

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to connote - to imply or suggest an additional meaning
  • Synonym: to indicate - to point out or show

Etymology

From Latin 'denotare', from 'de-' (completely) + 'notare' (to mark).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈnoʊt / (AmE), / dɪˈnəʊt / (BrE)
to indicate, make known, or give a sign of; to announce or show clearly
to make known or indicate

Examples

She denoted her feelings clearly during the meeting.
The gesture denotes his willingness to help.
By wearing white, she denoted her support for peace.

Synset Examples

She denoted her feelings clearly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to announce - to make a public declaration about something

Etymology

From Latin denotare (to mark out, indicate), from de- (completely) + notare (to mark, note).

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈnoʊt / (AmE), / dɪˈnəʊt / (BrE)
to be a sign of; to represent or mean something specific
to indicate or mean

Examples

A red octagon usually denotes a stop sign in traffic signals.
In chemistry, 'H2O' denotes water.
The word 'dog' denotes a domesticated canine animal.

Synset Examples

‘multi-’ denotes ‘many’

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to connote - to imply or suggest in addition to the literal meaning

Etymology

From Latin 'denotare' (to mark out, specify), from 'de-' (completely) + 'notare' (to mark).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00933429-v
Synset POS: v
