scotch

1. NOUN

/ skɑtʃ / (AmE), / skɒtʃ / (BrE)
a slight cut or notch on a surface, especially one made to keep a tally or record
a slight cut or notch (esp. for a tally)

Examples

Each time a task was finished, the worker made a scotch in the wooden board.
The old ledger was marked by a single scotch for every payment received.
He counted the scotches on the stick to track the number of deliveries.

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English 'scocchen' (to cut or score), of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ skɑtʃ / (AmE), / skɒtʃ / (BrE)
to make a small cut or score into something, especially superficially
make a small cut or score

Examples

Be careful not to scotch the wood when you are trimming the edges.
The car's tire was scotched after running over a sharp stone.
Use a sharp knife to scotch the surface lightly before painting.

Etymology

Origin unknown; unrelated to 'Scotch' (from Scotland).

3. VERB

/ skɑtʃ / (AmE), / skɒtʃ / (BrE)
to stop something from happening; to hinder or prevent the realization of a plan, effort, or desire
hinder or prevent plans or efforts

Examples

The new regulations will scotch any attempts to cheat the system.
Rumors of a merger were quickly scotched by company officials.
Her determined opposition scotched his plans for major reform.

informal
