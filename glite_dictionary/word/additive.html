additive

1. ADJ

/ ˈæd.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈæd.ɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
(of a process or property) characterized by, involving, or produced by addition rather than multiplication or other operation.
involving or produced by addition

Examples

The additive effect of the medication increased the overall potency of the treatment.
In art, color mixing is often an additive process, where each new color is formed by combining others.
This system uses an additive approach to layer new features onto existing structures.

Synset Examples

an additive process

Related Terms

  • Antonym: multiplicative - involving or relating to multiplication rather than addition

Etymology

From Latin additivus (that may be added), from addere (to add).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00048349-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈæd.ə.t̬ɪv / (AmE), / ˈæd.ɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
(Mathematics) Designating or involving an equation in which all terms are of the first degree and combine by addition.
of equations: involving only addition of first-degree terms

Examples

The equation is additive because each variable appears only to the first power.
In an additive model, the effects of the variables are summed rather than multiplied.
Linear regression is a form of additive modeling often used in statistics.

Etymology

From Latin additivus, from addere (to add).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01420302-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ ˈædətɪv / (AmE), / ˈadɪtɪv / (BrE)
a substance added to something in small quantities to improve or preserve it, especially in food, fuel, or other products.
substance enhancing another product

Examples

This bread contains a chemical additive to keep it fresh for longer.
Many gasoline brands use an additive to improve engine performance.
Some people avoid foods with artificial additives due to health concerns.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preservative - a substance used to preserve food or other perishable items

Etymology

From Latin additivus, from addere (to add).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02682642-n
Synset POS: n
