Psychodidae

1. NOUN

/ saɪˈkɒdəˌdi / (AmE), / saɪˈkɒdɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of very small, two-winged flies with hairy wings, typically developing in moss and damp vegetable matter, known as sand flies
family of small, hairy-winged flies (sand flies)
medical

Examples

The Psychodidae include several species commonly referred to as sand flies.
Larvae of the Psychodidae are often found in damp, decaying organic matter.
In some tropical regions, diseases can be transmitted by female Psychodidae.

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'psyche' (soul, breath, also 'moth') + '-idae' (zoological family suffix).

2. NOUN

/ saɪˈkɑːdəˌdiː / (AmE), / saɪˈkɒdɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of very small, two-winged flies with hairy wings, often developing in moss or damp organic matter, including the sand flies
family of small, hairy flies (sand flies)

Examples

The Psychodidae are known for their hairy wings and distinctive flight patterns near water.
Sand flies, members of the Psychodidae family, can be vectors for certain diseases.
Entomologists often study Psychodidae in environments rich with decaying plant matter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sand fly - a small fly of the Psychodidae family, known for biting and transmitting diseases

Etymology

From New Latin Psychodidae, from the type genus Psychoda, from Greek psukhē (soul, breath) + -id (formative suffix) + -ae (pl.)
