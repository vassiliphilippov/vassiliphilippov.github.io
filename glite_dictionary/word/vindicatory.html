vindicatory

1. ADJ

/ ˈvɪndɪkəˌtɔri / (AmE), / ˈvɪndɪkətri / (BrE)
serving to justify or clear from blame or suspicion
serving to justify or clear blame

Examples

The evidence had a vindicatory effect, absolving her of all charges.
His actions were seen as vindicatory in the eyes of the public.
The report's vindicatory conclusions restored trust in the committee.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justificatory - serving to justify; making just or lawful

Etymology

From Latin vindicatorius (of or concerning vindication), from vindicare (to claim, avenge, or justify).

2. ADJ

/ ˈvɪndəˌkeɪtɔri / (AmE), / ˈvɪndɪkətəri / (BrE)
relating to or intended as retribution or punishment according to one's merits or deserts; serving to vindicate by requital
serving as punishment or retribution

Examples

The court imposed a vindicatory sentence to reflect the seriousness of the crime.
Her actions, though harsh, had a distinctly vindicatory character meant to restore justice.
Some philosophers debate whether punishment should be vindicatory or rehabilitative in nature.

Synset Examples

retributive justice

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retributive - relating to or involving retribution; deserved punishment

Etymology

From Latin vindicatorius (serving to punish or avenge), from vindicare (to claim, avenge).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01373232-s

3. ADJ

/ ˈvɪndɪkəˌtɔri / (AmE), / ˈvɪndɪkətəri / (BrE)
Relating to or having the nature of retribution; intended to punish or seek retribution.
related to retribution or punishment

Examples

The judge imposed a harsh sentence in keeping with the vindicatory nature of the law.
Some critics argue that a vindicatory approach to punishment fails to promote rehabilitation.
The government's actions were widely perceived as vindicatory rather than just.

Synset Examples

retributive justice demands an eye for an eye

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retributive - relating to or involving retribution

Etymology

From Latin vindicatus (to avenge, claim) + -ory (forming adjectives).
