Hittite

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɪˌtaɪt / (AmE), / ˈhɪ.taɪt / (BrE)
a member of an ancient people who inhabited Anatolia and northern Syria from about 2000 to 1200 BC
member of an ancient Anatolian people

Examples

Archaeologists discovered artifacts left by the Hittite civilization in central Turkey.
The Hittite empire was known for its advanced use of iron.
Many ancient records were written in the Hittite language found on clay tablets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Assyrian - a member of an ancient Semitic people from Mesopotamia
  • Synonym: Egyptian - a member of the ancient civilization of Egypt

Etymology

From Latin 'Hethaeus', from Hebrew 'Ḥittī', relating to the descendants of Heth in the Bible.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɪtaɪt / (AmE), / ˈhɪtaɪt / (BrE)
an extinct Indo-European language once spoken by the Hittite people of ancient Anatolia, known primarily from cuneiform inscriptions
ancient Anatolian language of the Hittites

Examples

The discovery of Hittite tablets helped scholars understand the origins of Indo-European languages.
She specializes in ancient languages, including Hittite and Luwian.
Many Hittite inscriptions have been found in modern-day Turkey.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Anatolian - relating to the oldest branch of Indo-European languages found in Anatolia

Etymology

From Hittite *nešili* (the language of Nesa), referring to the Hittites; English from 'Hittite'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06988562-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈhɪˌtaɪt / (AmE), / ˈhɪtaɪt / (BrE)
of or relating to the Hittite people, their language, or their culture, which flourished in ancient Anatolia.
relating to the Hittite people, language, or culture

Examples

The Hittite empire was a significant power in Anatolia during the second millennium BC.
Several Hittite inscriptions have been discovered by archaeologists in modern-day Turkey.
Scholars have studied Hittite laws to learn about ancient legal systems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Hittite - the language of the Hittite people

Etymology

From Hittite, the name of an ancient Anatolian people, possibly from their own language.
