nickel

1. NOUN

/ ˈnɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈnɪkəl / (BrE)
a United States coin worth five cents, or one twentieth of a dollar
US coin worth five cents
american

Examples

Tom paid for his coffee with a nickel and some pennies.
Do you have a nickel for the parking meter?
The cashier handed me back a nickel as change.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: dime - A United States coin worth ten cents
  • Degree Lower: penny - A United States coin worth one cent

Etymology

Originally from the metal name, because the coin is largely composed of nickel.

2. NOUN

/ ˈnɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈnɪkəl / (BrE)
a hard, silvery-white, malleable metallic element, resistant to corrosion and used mainly in alloys and as a coating for other metals
a silvery corrosion-resistant metal element

Examples

Pure nickel is rarely used; it's commonly combined with other metals to make stainless steel.
The scientist demonstrated how nickel can resist rust in various environments.
Cans sometimes have a thin coating of nickel to prevent corrosion.

Etymology

From German *Nickel*, short for *Kupfernickel* (devil's copper), due to miners' frustration when smelting nickel ores.

/ ˈnɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈnɪkəl / (BrE)
to cover or plate (a metal object) with a thin layer of nickel, especially by electrochemical means
to coat with a layer of nickel

Examples

Many old coins have been nickeled to restore their appearance.

4. NOUN

/ ˈnɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈnɪkəl / (BrE)
(slang) Five dollars worth of an illicit drug, especially when referring to a small bag or quantity sold for that amount.
five dollars worth of a drug (slang)
slang american

Examples

He bought a nickel of weed from his friend behind the school.
The dealer only had nickel bags available for sale last night.
Back in the day, you could get a nickel of heroin for just five bucks.

Synset Examples

a nickel bag of drugs
a nickel deck of heroin

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: dime - ten dollars worth of a drug (slang)
  • Synonym: nickel bag - a small bag containing five dollars worth of a drug

Etymology

From the US slang use of 'nickel' for the five-cent coin, extended to mean five dollars.
