octave

1. NOUN

/ ˈɑk.teɪv / (AmE), / ˈɒk.tɪv / (BrE)
(in Christian liturgy) A feast day and the period of seven days following it, observed with special celebrations.
feast and seven days after it (Christian calendar)

Examples

The church marked the octave of Easter with daily masses and processions.
During the octave, special prayers are recited each day to commemorate the feast.
Pilgrims visited the city throughout the octave following the holy day.

Etymology

From Latin 'octava' (eighth, octave), referring to the eighth day after a feast, inclusive.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɑk.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈɒk.tɪv / (BrE)
a musical interval spanning eight notes, in which the first and eighth note have the same name and the upper note has twice the frequency of the lower
musical interval of eight notes

Examples

The pianist played the melody an octave higher for a brighter sound.
When the choir reaches the chorus, the sopranos often sing in octave with the tenors.
Moving up one octave doubles the frequency of the note.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: unison - interval between two identical pitches
  • Degree Lower: fifth - musical interval spanning five notes

Etymology

From Latin 'octava' (eighth), via Old French 'octave', meaning 'eight-day period'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈɑk.teɪv / (AmE), / ˈɒk.tɪv / (BrE)
A group of eight lines of verse, especially forming a stanza or section in a poem.
a group of eight lines in a poem

Examples

The sonnet's first octave sets up the central conflict.
Many poets use an octave to introduce a theme or question.
In the poem, the octave is followed by a six-line sestet.

Etymology

From Italian 'ottava', from Latin 'octavus' (eighth).
