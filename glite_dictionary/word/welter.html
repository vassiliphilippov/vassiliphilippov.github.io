welter

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɛltər / (AmE), / ˈwɛltə / (BrE)
a large and confusing mass of things, ideas, or emotions
a confused mass or jumble

Examples

The country was caught in a welter of bureaucratic rules and regulations.
Her desk was piled high with a welter of books, papers, and unfinished work.
After the announcement, a welter of emotions swept through the crowd.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jumble - an untidy or confused mixture or mass
  • Synonym: chaos - a state of complete disorder and confusion

Etymology

Probably from Middle Dutch *welteren* (to roll, wallow) or Middle Low German *weltern*, related to the verb sense.

WordNet ID: oewn-14524151-n

2. VERB

/ ˈwɛltɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɛltə / (BrE)
to move in a turbulent or uncontrolled way; to be tossed, rolled, or heaved chaotically, especially by water or waves
to move chaotically; tossed or rolled

Examples

The small boat weltered helplessly as the storm intensified.
Emotions weltered within him after hearing the unexpected news.
Debris from the wreck weltered in the rough waters throughout the night.

Synset Examples

The shipwrecked survivors weltered in the sea for hours

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toss - to move or cause to move up and down or back and forth
  • Synonym: roll - to move by turning over and over

Etymology

From Middle English *welteren* (to roll about), probably from Middle Dutch *welteren*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02090509-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈwɛltɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɛltə / (BrE)
to be deeply involved or immersed in a particular situation, activity, or emotion, typically in a way that is overwhelming or disordered
be immersed or deeply involved in

Examples

She would often welter in anxiety before exams, unable to find calm.
The company continued to welter in debt after several poor investments.
He seemed to welter in work during the busy season, barely finding time to rest.

Synset Examples

welter in work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wallow - to indulge or immerse oneself in a feeling or activity

Etymology

From Middle English 'welteren' (to roll, rise and fall), of uncertain origin, possibly related to Dutch 'welteren'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00602976-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈwɛltər / (AmE), / ˈwɛltə / (BrE)
(literary) To move in a turbulent, rolling, or chaotic manner, often used to describe rolling or tossing about in something soft or yielding.
to move or roll about chaotically

Examples

The horses began to welter in the deep snow, struggling to find solid ground.
The wounded soldiers were left to welter in pain on the battlefield.
During the storm, the ship seemed to welter helplessly on the wild sea.

Synset Examples

pigs were wallowing in the mud

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wallow - to roll about in mud or water, especially for pleasure

Etymology

From Middle English 'welteren', perhaps of Germanic origin, related to Dutch 'welteren' (to roll, wallow).
