courser

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrsər / (AmE), / ˈkɔːsə / (BrE)
a swift-running bird of southern Asia and Africa, resembling plovers and related to pratincoles
a swift-running bird of Asia and Africa

Examples

The courser is well adapted to running swiftly across open ground.
Several species of courser can be found in the grasslands of Africa.
Researchers tracked the movements of the Indian courser during the breeding season.

Etymology

From French 'coursier' (runner), from Old French 'courre' (to run), from Latin 'currere'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɔr.sɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɔː.sə / (BrE)
a strong, swift horse, especially one used in battle or hunting in former times
swift horse for battle or hunting (historical)
archaic

Examples

The knight mounted his courser and rode fearlessly into the fray.
In medieval times, a courser was prized for its speed and endurance on the battlefield.
He admired the painting of a nobleman astride a magnificent courser.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warhorse - a horse trained for and used in battle
  • Synonym: charger - a horse ridden by a knight or soldier in battle

Etymology

From Old French 'coursier', from Medieval Latin 'cursarius', from Latin 'currere' (to run).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrsər / (AmE), / ˈkɔːsə / (BrE)
a dog trained or bred for hunting game animals by sight, especially in the sport of coursing
dog trained for hunting by sight

Examples

The hunter released his courser into the field to chase after the hare.
Greyhounds are often considered one of the fastest breeds of courser for competitive events.
Each courser in the kennel was trained to respond to the signal to start running.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: greyhound - a breed of dog commonly used in coursing

Etymology

From Old French 'corsor', meaning 'runner', from 'courre' (to run).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɔrsər / (AmE), / ˈkɔːsə / (BrE)
a huntsman who pursues small animals using fast dogs that hunt by sight rather than scent
huntsman using sight-hunting dogs
archaic

Examples

The skilled courser relied on his greyhounds to chase down hares across the open fields.
In medieval times, a courser was often employed by nobles during hunting expeditions.
Unlike hunters with scent hounds, the courser depended on the dogs' keen eyesight to track prey.

Etymology

From Old French 'coursier' (runner, charger), from Vulgar Latin 'cursarius', based on 'currere' (to run).
