to foreclose

1. VERB

/ fɔrˈkloʊz / (AmE), / fɔːˈkləʊz / (BrE)
(formal) To prevent something from happening or arising; to make impossible.
prevent or make impossible

Examples

Strict safety regulations foreclose the possibility of such accidents occurring again.
His refusal to compromise foreclosed any hope of a peaceful resolution.
The new evidence does not foreclose the case, but it makes a guilty verdict unlikely.

Synset Examples

My sense of tact forbids an honest answer
Your role in the projects precludes your involvement in the competitive project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preclude - to prevent something from happening or someone from doing something
  • Synonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening
  • Antonym: permit - to allow something to happen

Etymology

From Old French *forsclos*, past participle of *forsclore* (to exclude), from Latin *foris* (out) + *claudere* (to close).

2. VERB

/ fɔrˈkloʊz / (AmE), / fɔːˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to take legal action to take possession of a mortgaged property when the borrower fails to keep up their mortgage payments, thereby ending the right of redemption
take possession of mortgaged property for nonpayment
legal

Examples

The bank decided to foreclose on the property after several missed payments.
If you fail to make your mortgage payments, the lender may foreclose on your home.
Due to rising unemployment, more lenders have been forced to foreclose this year than last.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to repossess - to take back property due to nonpayment

Etymology

From Old French *forclos*, from Latin *foris* (outside) + *claudere* (to close).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
