to conflagrate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːn.fləˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.fləˌɡreɪt / (BrE)
to cause to start burning; to set fire to something
to set on fire

Examples

The careless camper's match threatened to conflagrate the entire forest.
A single spark was enough to conflagrate the old, dry barn.
If not properly monitored, faulty wiring could easily conflagrate the building.

Synset Examples

The setting sun kindled the sky with oranges and reds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ignite - to set on fire or start burning

Etymology

From Latin conflagratus, past participle of conflagrare (to burn up), from con- (together) + flagrare (to burn).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑn.fləˌɡreɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.fləˌɡreɪt / (BrE)
to catch fire or burst suddenly into flames
to burst suddenly into flames

Examples

Dried leaves can easily conflagrate in extreme heat.
After the short circuit, the wires began to conflagrate within seconds.
Experts warned that the chemicals could conflagrate if not stored properly.

Synset Examples

Marsh gases ignited suddenly
The oily rags combusted spontaneously

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ignite - to catch fire or cause to catch fire
  • Synonym: to combust - to burn or cause to burn

Etymology

From Latin 'conflagrare' (to burn up), from 'con-' (together) + 'flagrare' (to burn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02766580-v
Synset POS: v
