apposition

1. NOUN

/ ˌæpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌapəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
the grammatical relationship between two noun phrases or elements, where the second explains or identifies the first, typically appearing side by side.
the grammatical relationship of two adjacent nouns

Examples

In the phrase 'my friend John', 'John' is in apposition to 'my friend'.
The book, a bestseller, illustrates the use of apposition in a sentence.
Her mother, a talented pianist, is an example of apposition in English grammar.

Synset Examples

‘Rudolph the red-nosed reindeer’ is an example of apposition

Related Terms

  • Synonym: appositive - a noun or noun phrase placed next to another to explain it

Etymology

From Latin 'appositio' (a setting beside), from 'apponere' (to put near).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌapəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
(Biology) The increase in the thickness of a cell wall by the successive deposition of layers of material.
biological process of wall thickening
medical

Examples

Certain plant cell walls grow by apposition, increasing thickness layer by layer.
The process of apposition allows for the strengthening of woody tissue in trees.
Some bacteria rely on apposition to reinforce their protective barriers.

Etymology

/ ˌæpəˈzɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌapəˈzɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of placing things close together or side by side, especially for comparison or contrast
the act of positioning side by side

Examples

In art, the apposition of light and dark colors can produce dramatic effects.
Their arguments gained strength through the apposition of opposing viewpoints.
