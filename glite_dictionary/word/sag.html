sag

1. NOUN

/ sæɡ / (AmE), / sæɡ / (BrE)
a dip or downward curve in something, especially as a result of weight or weakness
a dip or downward curve, especially from weight

Examples

There was a noticeable sag in the middle of the old sofa.
The sag in the power line was more pronounced after the storm.
Over time, the mattress developed a slight sag that made it uncomfortable.

Synset Examples

there was a sag in the chair seat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dip - a slight depression or curve downward in a surface

Etymology

Originates from the verb 'sag', likely of Scandinavian origin (related to Norwegian 'sagga' meaning to sink or droop).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ sæɡ / (AmE), / sæɡ / (BrE)
to cause something to sink, bend, or droop downward, typically due to weight or lack of support
make something bend or sink down

Examples

She tried to sag the mattress to make it more comfortable.
The tailor sagged the waistband to fit the new style.
The children sagged their bottoms down even more comfortably.

Synset Examples

The children sagged their bottoms down even more comfortably

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to droop - to bend or hang downward limply

Etymology

From Middle English saggen (to sink or droop), probably of Scandinavian origin; akin to Norwegian 'sagga' (to sag).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01989771-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ sæɡ / (AmE), / sæɡ / (BrE)
to droop, sink, or bend downward, especially from pressure, weight, or loss of firmness
droop or sink downward

Examples

The old sofa cushions sag in the middle from years of use.
After hours on her feet, her shoulders began to sag with fatigue.
The power lines sag slightly between the poles under the weight of ice.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to droop - to bend or hang downward limply
  • Antonym: to perk up - to become more upright or lively

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse 'saga' (to droop).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01989538-v
Synset POS: v
