to erode

1. VERB

/ ɪˈroʊd / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊd / (BrE)
(of something abstract) to gradually diminish or be destroyed over time; to weaken or deteriorate slowly.
to gradually weaken or deteriorate

Examples

Years of criticism gradually eroded his enthusiasm for the project.
The constant pressure can erode even the strongest self-confidence.
Her trust in the company has slowly eroded over time after repeated disappointments.

Synset Examples

Her confidence eroded

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deteriorate - to become worse in quality or value
  • Synonym: to corrode - to be gradually destroyed, especially by chemical action
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to become stronger

Etymology

From Latin *erodere*, from *e-* (out) + *rodere* (to gnaw).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈroʊd / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊd / (BrE)
(of natural forces) to gradually wear away or remove soil, rock, or land.
remove soil or rock gradually

Examples

Over time, the constant flow of water eroded the riverbank.
Strong winds can quickly erode exposed hillsides in the desert.
If left unchecked, acid rain will erode the limestone statues in the park.

Synset Examples

Rain eroded the terraces

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wear away - to gradually remove material by friction or erosion
  • Antonym: deposit - to lay down material, especially by natural forces—a process opposite to erosion

Etymology

From Latin 'erodere' (to gnaw away), from 'e-' (away) + 'rodere' (to gnaw).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00275523-v
Synset POS: v
