Neandertal

1. NOUN

/ niˈæn.dərˌtɑl / (AmE), / niˈændətɑːl / (BrE)
an extinct species of robust humans (Homo neanderthalensis) who lived in Europe and western Asia during the Middle Paleolithic period
extinct human species of the Middle Paleolithic

Examples

The remains of a Neandertal were discovered in a cave in Germany.
Archaeologists study Neandertal tools to learn about their daily lives.
DNA analysis shows that modern humans share some genes with Neandertal populations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Neanderthal - variant spelling of Neandertal (extinct robust human species)

Etymology

From the Neander Valley (German: Neandertal) near Düsseldorf, Germany, where remains were first discovered in 1856.

2. ADJ

/ niˈæn.dər.tɑl / (AmE), / niˈan.də.tɑːl / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of Neanderthals, an extinct species or subspecies of archaic humans who lived in Eurasia until about 40,000 years ago.
relating to Neanderthals (archaic humans)

Examples

The Neandertal skull displayed prominent brow ridges and a broad nose.
Researchers compared modern and Neandertal DNA to understand their relationship.
A Neandertal tool was discovered at the archaeological site.
