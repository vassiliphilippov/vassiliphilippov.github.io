cuneiform

1. ADJ

/ kjuˈniəˌfɔrm / (AmE), / kjuːˈniːɪfɔːm / (BrE)
relating to wedge-shaped bones, especially the tarsal bones of the foot
of wedge-shaped bones in anatomy
medical

Examples

The surgeon examined the cuneiform bones during the operation on the patient's foot.
X-rays revealed a fracture in the medial cuneiform after the accident.
Arthritis in the cuneiform region of the foot can make walking painful.

Etymology

From Latin *cuneus* (wedge) + *-form* (shape).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkjuːniəˌfɔrm / (AmE), / kjuːˈniːɪfɔːm / (BrE)
shaped like a wedge; having the form of a wedge.
wedge-shaped

Examples

The fossil displayed distinctly cuneiform marks left by ancient toolmakers.
In anatomy, certain wrist bones are described as cuneiform due to their wedge-like shape.
The tablets featured cuneiform impressions characteristic of early writing systems.

Etymology

From Latin *cuneus* (wedge) + *-formis* (shaped).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03156623-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ ˈkjuːni.əˌfɔrm / (AmE), / ˈkjuːnɪ.ɪfɔːm / (BrE)
an ancient writing system with wedge-shaped characters, used in Mesopotamia and Persia
ancient wedge-shaped writing system

Examples

The ancient Sumerians used cuneiform to record their laws and transactions.
Archaeologists discovered tablets covered in cuneiform script at the excavation site.
Learning to decipher cuneiform opened a window into early Mesopotamian civilization.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hieroglyph - a writing system using picture symbols, especially in ancient Egypt

Etymology

From Latin cuneiformis ‘wedge-shaped’, from cuneus ‘wedge’ and forma ‘form’.

Synset POS: n
