to divert

1. VERB

/ daɪˈvɝt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to take money away from its intended purpose or account and move it elsewhere, often secretly or with dishonest intent
move money for dishonest purposes

Examples

The accountant was accused of trying to divert company funds into his personal account.
Investigators found that the mayor had diverted millions meant for public projects.
She was caught diverting donations from the charity for her own use.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embezzle - steal or misappropriate money placed in one's trust or belonging to one's employer

Etymology

From Latin divertĕre (to turn aside) via Old French 'diverter'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02316994-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈvɜrt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to change direction; to turn aside or away from a course or path
turn aside; change direction

Examples

The river was diverted to prevent flooding in the town.
She tried to divert his attention away from the broken vase.
Construction work will divert traffic onto a different route for several weeks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to redirect - to send something to a different place or in a different direction
  • Synonym: to deflect - to cause something to change direction

Etymology

From Latin divertere (to turn in different directions, turn aside), from dis- (aside) + vertere (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02068815-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈvɜrt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to entertain or amuse someone so that they are pleasantly occupied
entertain or amuse

Examples

The storyteller tried to divert the children during the long train journey.
She brought music and games to divert her guests at the party.
A good movie can divert you from your worries for a while.

Synset Examples

The play amused the ladies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to amuse - to entertain or make someone laugh or smile
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel weary and uninterested

Etymology

From Latin 'divertere' (to turn aside, to amuse).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02498011-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ daɪˈvɝt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to change direction or go along a different route from the original one
cause to change direction or route

Examples

Due to roadworks, police had to divert traffic onto a side street.
The river was diverted to prevent flooding in the nearby village.
They quickly diverted the plane to an alternative airport because of the storm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to redirect - to send something to a different place or along a different route
  • Synonym: to deflect - to cause something to change direction by interposing something; turn aside from a straight course
  • Antonym: to continue - to keep going in the same direction or on the same course

Etymology

From Latin 'divertere', from 'di-' (aside) + 'vertere' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01956222-v
Synset POS: v
