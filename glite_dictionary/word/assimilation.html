assimilation

1. NOUN

/ əˌsɪməˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
in linguistics, the process by which a sound becomes more like a neighboring sound in pronunciation
sound change to match nearby sound (linguistics)

Examples

In rapid speech, assimilation often causes 'handbag' to be pronounced as 'hambag'.
The assimilation of the 'n' sound in 'input' makes it sound like 'imput'.
Many languages exhibit assimilation when certain consonants are placed together.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dissimilation - the process by which two similar sounds in a word become less similar

Etymology

From Latin assimilatio (likening to), from assimilare (to make similar).

2. NOUN

/ əˌsɪməˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which people of different backgrounds come to be integrated into a larger group, often adopting its customs and values
integration into a larger group or culture

Examples

The assimilation of immigrants into the local community can take several generations.
Language classes play an important role in the assimilation of newcomers.
Some groups resist assimilation in order to preserve their cultural heritage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: integration - the process of combining into a whole, especially with respect to people in society
  • Antonym: segregation - the act of separating people of different races, religions, etc., and treating them differently

Etymology

From Latin 'assimilatio' (making similar), from 'assimilare' (to make like, assimilate).

absorption of nutrients into the body
medical

Proper assimilation of nutrients is essential for overall health and well-being.
The assimilation of vitamins occurs mainly in the small intestine.
Medical tests showed an issue with the child's assimilation of dietary minerals.

Related Terms

4. NOUN

/ əˌsɪməˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which new ideas or information are incorporated into an existing cognitive framework or structure
incorporating new ideas into existing knowledge

Examples

Children use assimilation when they fit new experiences into their existing ideas about the world.
Jean Piaget described assimilation as a key process of cognitive development.
Good teachers encourage assimilation by connecting new material to what students already know.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: accommodation - the cognitive process of modifying existing mental patterns to incorporate new information; often contrasted with assimilation

Etymology

From Latin assimilatio, from assimilare (to make similar).

The school programs were designed to assist with the assimilation of refugee children.

  • Antonym: segregation - the act or policy of separating groups, especially those of different races or religions

6. NOUN

/ əˌsɪməˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
In Piagetian psychology, the process by which an individual applies an existing mental schema to new experiences or information.
use of existing schema in new situations (Piagetian)

Examples

According to Piaget, assimilation involves fitting new experiences into pre-existing mental frameworks.
In childhood learning, assimilation helps children interpret unfamiliar situations using what they already know.
The theory suggests that assimilation is complemented by accommodation, leading to cognitive development.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: accommodation - In Piagetian theory, modifying existing schemas in response to new information

Etymology

From Latin assimilatio (likeness, comparison), from assimilo (to liken, make similar).
