infrared

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.frəˈrɛd / (AmE), / ˌɪn.frəˈrɛd / (BrE)
the part of the electromagnetic spectrum with wavelengths longer than visible light but shorter than microwaves, just beyond the red end of the visible spectrum
region of electromagnetic spectrum beyond visible red

Examples

The sensors are able to detect light in the infrared as well as the visible spectrum.
Some animals can perceive the infrared, which is invisible to the human eye.
Astronomers observe certain celestial objects using infrared to study heat emissions.

Synset Examples

they could sense radiation in the infrared

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ultraviolet - the region of the spectrum with wavelengths shorter than visible violet

Etymology

From Latin 'infra' (below) and 'red', meaning 'below red' (in wavelength).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪnfrəˈrɛd / (AmE), / ˌɪnfrəˈrɛd / (BrE)
Electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths longer than visible light but shorter than radio waves, often used in technology and astronomy.
type of electromagnetic radiation

Examples

The remote control uses infrared signals to communicate with the television.
Astronomers observe infrared light to study objects obscured by dust in space.
Certain cameras can detect infrared radiation invisible to the human eye.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ultraviolet - electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths shorter than visible light

Etymology

From infra- (below) + red, indicating wavelengths below visible red light.

3. ADJ

/ ˌɪn.frəˈrɛd / (AmE), / ˌɪn.frəˈrɛd / (BrE)
having or using electromagnetic waves with wavelengths longer than visible red light but shorter than microwaves, beyond the visible spectrum's red end
beyond red in the electromagnetic spectrum

Examples

The camera uses infrared light to capture images in the dark.
Some animals can detect infrared radiation to find warm-blooded prey.
Astronomers study infrared wavelengths to observe distant stars obscured by dust.

Synset Examples

infrared radiation
infrared photography

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ultraviolet - having wavelengths shorter than visible violet light, beyond the visible spectrum's violet end

Etymology

From Latin 'infra' (below) + 'red', indicating position below red in the spectrum.
