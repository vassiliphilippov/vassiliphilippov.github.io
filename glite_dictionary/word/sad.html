sad

1. ADJ

/ sæd / (AmE), / sæd / (BrE)
(of a situation or condition) bad, deplorable, or unfortunate; inspiring regret or pity.
bad or unfortunate (situation)

Examples

After years of neglect, the house was in a sad state.
It was a sad situation when the company had to close down.
The old playground equipment is in such sad shape that it's unsafe for children.

Synset Examples

my finances were in a deplorable state
a lamentable decision
her clothes were in sad shape
a sorry state of affairs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deplorable - deserving strong condemnation; shockingly bad in quality
  • Synonym: lamentable - bad or unsatisfactory; deserving to be lamented
  • Antonym: fortunate - lucky; having good fortune

2. ADJ

/ sæd / (AmE), / sæd / (BrE)
(of things) causing feelings of unhappiness or sorrow.
causing unhappiness or sorrow

Examples

The movie had a sad ending that made everyone cry.
They received the sad news of her passing yesterday.
No one wanted to listen to his sad story again.

Synset Examples

sad news
she doesn't like sad movies
it was a very sad story
When I am dead, my dearest, / Sing no sad songs for me

Related Terms

  • Antonym: happy - feeling or showing pleasure or contentment
  • Synonym: unhappy - not happy; feeling or showing sorrow

Etymology

From Old English sæd (sated, weary), later influenced by Old English sæd (sorrowful).

3. ADJ

/ sæd / (AmE), / sæd / (BrE)
feeling or showing sorrow; unhappy or causing unhappiness
feeling or showing unhappiness

Examples

She felt sad when her best friend moved away to another city.
His sad eyes told me everything I needed to know about how he was feeling.
It was a sad day for the community when the old library closed.

Synset Examples

feeling sad because his dog had died
Better by far that you should forget and smile / Than that you should remember and be sad

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unhappy - feeling sad or not cheerful
  • Antonym: joyful - feeling or expressing great happiness
  • Degree Higher: miserable - extremely unhappy or uncomfortable

Etymology

From Old English sæd (sated, weary, sorrowful), related to Old High German sat (sated).
