masculine

1. ADJ

/ ˈmæskjəlɪn / (AmE), / ˈmæskjʊlɪn / (BrE)
having qualities or appearance traditionally associated with men, especially strength and aggressiveness; relating to or characteristic of men or boys.
having qualities typical of men or boys

Examples

His deep voice gave him a distinctly masculine presence.
The decor of the room had a strong, masculine feel, with dark woods and leather furniture.
She preferred clothing with a more masculine style.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: feminine - having qualities or appearance traditionally associated with women

Etymology

From Latin masculinus (male, pertaining to a male), from masculus (male).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmæskjəlɪn / (AmE), / ˈmæskjʊlɪn / (BrE)
(in grammar) the gender of nouns, pronouns, or adjectives that typically refer to male beings or are classified as male.
grammatical gender for male referents

Examples

In French, the word 'chien' is of the masculine gender.
Spanish nouns often end with 'o' to indicate they are masculine.
Teachers ask students to learn which articles match the masculine nouns.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: feminine - the grammatical gender referring to female beings or objects classified as female
  • Antonym: neuter - the grammatical gender referring to things neither masculine nor feminine

Etymology

From Latin masculinus (male, manly), from masculus (male, masculine).

4. ADJ

/ ˈmæskjəlɪn / (AmE), / ˈmæskjʊlɪn / (BrE)
(of a grammatical gender) denoting a class of nouns, pronouns, or adjectives conventionally associated with male beings or characteristics, as in many gendered languages.
male grammatical gender

Examples

In French, the noun 'livre' is masculine and thus takes masculine articles and adjectives.
Spanish adjectives change their endings to agree with masculine or feminine nouns.
The word 'amigo' is masculine in Spanish, while 'amiga' is its feminine counterpart.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: feminine - female grammatical gender
  • Antonym: neuter - neither masculine nor feminine in grammatical gender

Etymology

From Latin masculinus, from masculus meaning 'male'.
