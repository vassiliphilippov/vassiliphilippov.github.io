bushel

1. NOUN

/ ˈbʊʃəl / (AmE), / ˈbʊʃl̩ / (BrE)
a unit of dry measure in the US, equal to 4 pecks or 2,152.42 cubic inches, used chiefly for grain and produce
US dry measure (4 pecks)

Examples

The farmer harvested over fifty bushels of apples this season.
Wheat is often sold by the bushel in the United States.
A bushel of corn weighed heavily in the basket.

Etymology

From Old French 'boissel' (bushel), likely from Late Latin 'buxidellum' (small box).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbʊʃəl / (AmE), / ˈbʊʃəl / (BrE)
a British imperial and US customary capacity measure (for liquid or dry goods) equal to 4 pecks, or approximately 36.4 liters in the UK and 35.2 liters in the US
a measure of capacity equal to 4 pecks

Examples

He harvested over twenty bushels of apples from his orchard this season.
A bushel of wheat will weigh about 60 pounds in the US.
Prices for a bushel of corn have risen sharply this year.

Etymology

From Old French boissel, from Medieval Latin bussellus (small box).
