motive

1. ADJ

/ ˈmoʊtɪv / (AmE), / ˈməʊtɪv / (BrE)
(attrib.) Causing or tending to cause motion or action; having the power to impel or move.
causing or impelling to action

Examples

The prosecutor argued that the defendant's statements had a clear motive purpose.
Steam engines convert heat into motive power to drive machinery.
The lawyer presented motive arguments to persuade the judge.

Synset Examples

it may well be that ethical language has primarily a motivative function
motive pleas
motivating arguments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motivating - causing someone to act or feel motivated
  • Antonym: inert - lacking the ability or power to move or act

Etymology

From Latin motivus (moving), from movere (to move).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊtɪv / (AmE), / ˈməʊtɪv / (BrE)
a short musical idea or theme that is repeated or developed throughout a piece of music
repeated musical idea or theme

Examples

The composer introduced a simple motive that recurred throughout the symphony.
Each variation of the piece expanded on the original motive.
The motive in the first movement is echoed in the finale.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: theme - a recurring melody or pattern in music
  • Synonym: leitmotif - a recurring musical phrase associated with a particular idea or character

Etymology

From French *motif* (dominant idea, theme), from Latin *motivus* (serving to move).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07043169-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊ.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈməʊ.tɪv / (BrE)
a decorative design or figure that consists of repeating shapes or colors, often used in architecture or the applied arts
recurring decorative design

Examples

The wallpaper features a floral motive repeated across the entire surface.
Ancient Greek temples were often decorated with a geometric motive along the frieze.
The designer incorporated a wave-like motive into the tile pattern for the bathroom.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motif - a recurring element or theme, especially in art or literature

Etymology

From French *motif*, meaning 'motive, pattern'; from Medieval Latin *motivum*.

4. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊtɪv / (AmE), / ˈməʊtɪv / (BrE)
a reason for doing something, especially one that is hidden or not obvious; something that makes a person act in a particular way
reason for an action or behavior

Examples

Detectives searched for a motive that could explain the crime.
Her true motive for helping us was not immediately clear.
Jealousy was suspected as the motive behind his actions.

Synset Examples

we did not understand his motivation
he acted with the best of motives

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motivation - the reason or reasons behind someone’s actions or behavior
  • Synonym: reason - a cause or explanation for an action or event
  • Degree Lower: excuse - a reason given to justify a fault or offense
  • Synonym: purpose - the reason for which something is done or created

Etymology

From Old French *motif* and Latin *motivus* meaning 'moving, causing motion'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00023953-n

5. ADJ

/ ˈmoʊtɪv / (AmE), / ˈməʊtɪv / (BrE)
(of a force, power, etc.) causing or able to cause motion or movement.
causing or able to cause motion

Examples

The engine produces the motive force needed to propel the train forward.
Steam is used as a motive power in many industrial processes.
Water wheels harnessed the motive energy of rivers to run the mills.

Synset Examples

a motive force
motive power
motor energy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motor - producing or causing motion (adjective)

Etymology

From Latin 'motivus' (moving, that moves), from 'motus', the past participle of 'movēre' (to move).
