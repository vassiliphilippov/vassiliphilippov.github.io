chant

1. NOUN

/ tʃænt / (AmE), / tʃɑːnt / (BrE)
A simple, repetitive song or melody in which many syllables are sung on a single tone, typically used in religious or ceremonial contexts.
repetitive song on a single tone

Examples

The monks began their evening chant as the candles flickered in the chapel.
A solemn chant echoed through the cathedral during the ceremony.
Her morning meditation included listening to a soothing Sanskrit chant.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hymn - a religious song of praise, often sung in churches
  • Synonym: song - a short musical composition with words

Etymology

From Old French chanter, from Latin cantare 'to sing'.

2. VERB

/ tʃænt / (AmE), / tʃɑːnt / (BrE)
to sing or recite words with a repeated, rhythmic, and musical intonation, often in a religious or ritual context
recite or sing with rhythmic intonation

Examples

Every morning, the monks chant prayers in the main hall.
The children chanted their school song before the ceremony began.
The choir is chanting ancient hymns as part of the service.

Synset Examples

The rabbi chanted a prayer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intone - to say or recite with little rise and fall of the pitch of the voice
  • Synonym: to sing - to produce musical tones with the voice

Etymology

From Old French chanter, from Latin cantare (to sing).

3. VERB

/ tʃænt / (AmE), / tʃɑːnt / (BrE)
to say or sing a phrase, word, or slogan repeatedly and rhythmically, often in a monotone
say or sing repetitively and rhythmically

Examples

The crowd began to chant slogans in support of their team.
He would chant the same mantra every morning as part of his routine.
Protesters chanted loudly outside the building for hours.

Synset Examples

The students chanted the same slogan over and over again

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intone - to say or recite in a singing voice
  • Degree Lower: yell - to shout loudly, usually in anger or excitement

Etymology

From Old French 'chanter', from Latin 'cantare' (to sing).
