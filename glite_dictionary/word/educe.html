to educe

1. VERB

/ ɪˈdus / (AmE), / ɪˈdjuːs / (BrE)
to bring out or develop something latent or potential, especially to infer or deduce a principle or meaning
bring out or deduce; infer meaning

Examples

The professor tried to educe a general theory from the specific data.
Through careful questioning, she was able to educe the hidden meaning behind his words.
Researchers hope to educe useful insights from the complex experimental results.

Synset Examples

We drew out some interesting linguistic data from the native informant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deduce - to reach a conclusion by reasoning; to infer

Etymology

From Latin educere, meaning 'to lead out'; related to 'educe' and 'education'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01634074-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪˈdus / (AmE), / ɪˈdjuːs / (BrE)
To bring out or develop something that is latent or potential; to draw forth.
bring out or develop from a latent state

Examples

The philosopher sought to educe truth from careful questioning and debate.
Her careful observations helped educe a new theory from the experimental data.
Through discussion, the teacher aimed to educe the students' natural curiosity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to elicit - to draw out a response or reaction

Etymology

From Latin educere, from e- (out) + ducere (to lead).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00252501-v
Synset POS: v
