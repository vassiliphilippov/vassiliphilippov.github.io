to adjourn

1. VERB

/ əˈdʒɝn / (AmE), / əˈdʒɜːn / (BrE)
to suspend or pause a meeting, session, or gathering, typically with the intention to resume later
suspend or pause a meeting

Examples

The chairman decided to adjourn the meeting until after lunch.
Let's adjourn for a fifteen-minute coffee break.
The court will adjourn at noon and reconvene in the afternoon.

Synset Examples

We adjourned for lunch
The men retired to the library

Related Terms

  • Antonym: resume - to begin again after a pause or interruption

Etymology

From Old French 'ajourner' (to appoint a day), from 'a-' (to) + 'jour' (day).

2. VERB

/ əˈdʒɝn / (AmE), / əˈdʒɜːn / (BrE)
to suspend proceedings to another time or indefinitely, especially at the end of a meeting, session, or court case
suspend a meeting or session

Examples

The judge decided to adjourn the hearing until next week.
The meeting will adjourn at 5 pm sharp.
After a long debate, the committee finally adjourned for lunch.

Synset Examples

The court adjourned

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recess - to take a break from a formal meeting or session
  • Antonym: to convene - to begin or bring together for a meeting or session

Etymology

From Old French adjourner (to postpone), from Latin ad (to) + diurnum (day).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00364950-v
Synset POS: v
