2. VERB

/ ˈælɪˌɡeɪtər / (AmE), / ˈælɪˌɡeɪtə / (BrE)
(of paint or varnish) to crack and develop a rough, broken pattern resembling the skin of an alligator, usually as a result of aging or improper application.
crack and form alligator-skin patterns (paint, varnish)

Examples

If you apply the paint too thickly, it may alligator as it dries.
Old varnish on the windowsills began to alligator after years of sun exposure.
Improper surface preparation can cause the finish to alligator within just a few months.

Etymology

Metaphor from the appearance of alligator skin; used in technical painting and finishing contexts.

3. NOUN

/ ˈæ.ləˌɡeɪ.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈæ.lɪˌɡeɪ.tə / (BrE)
A large, semi-aquatic reptile similar to a crocodile, with a shorter, broader snout, found chiefly in the southeastern United States and China.
large semi-aquatic reptile with broad snout

Examples

The alligator basked on the riverbank, its jaws slightly open in the sun.
She spotted an alligator swimming slowly through the murky water.
Zookeepers warned visitors not to feed the alligators during their tour.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crocodile - large predatory aquatic reptile with a long, narrow snout

Etymology

From Spanish 'el lagarto' (the lizard).
