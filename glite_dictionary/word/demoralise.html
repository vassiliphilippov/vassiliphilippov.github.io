to demoralise

1. VERB

/ dɪˈmɔːr.əˌlaɪz / (AmE), / dɪˈmɒr.ə.laɪz / (BrE)
to make someone lose confidence, hope, or spirit; to discourage or lower someone's morale
to lower someone's confidence or morale

Examples

The constant criticism began to demoralise the entire team.
The soldiers were demoralised by the series of defeats.
Hearing about the project's cancellation can really demoralise those who worked hard.

Synset Examples

These news depressed her
The bad state of her child's health demoralizes her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to discourage - to make someone less confident or hopeful
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give someone confidence or hope

Etymology

From French 'démoraliser', from 'démoraliser' (to corrupt morals), based on 'moral'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈmɔːrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / dɪˈmɒrəlaɪz / (BrE)
to corrupt someone morally, especially through indulgence in sensuality or intemperance
corrupt morally or by intemperance

Examples

It is dangerous to demoralise young people by encouraging reckless drinking and partying.
Some believed the decadent lifestyle would demoralise the entire community.
He warned that excessive wealth could demoralise even the most principled leaders.

Synset Examples

debauch the young people with wine and women
Socrates was accused of corrupting young men
Do school counselors subvert young children?
corrupt the morals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrupt - to cause someone or something to become dishonest or immoral
  • Antonym: uplift - to improve morally or spiritually

Etymology

From French 'démoraliser', from 'dé-' (dis-) + 'moraliser' (to moralize).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02585532-v
Synset POS: v
