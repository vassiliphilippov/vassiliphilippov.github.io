twig

1. VERB

/ twɪɡ / (AmE), / twɪɡ / (BrE)
to suddenly understand or realize something, often after some initial confusion or difficulty
suddenly realize or understand
informal british

Examples

It took me a while, but I finally twigged what he was trying to say.
She didn't twig his sarcasm until much later.
You could see the moment he twigged that he had made a mistake.

Synset Examples

She didn't know what her classmates were plotting but finally caught on

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to catch on - to understand or realize something, especially after some delay
  • Synonym: grasp - to understand something fully

Etymology

Probably originally British slang, possibly rhyming or cant, emerging in 19th-century English.

2. NOUN

/ twɪɡ / (AmE), / twɪɡ / (BrE)
a small, thin branch growing from a tree or shrub, typically one of the current or previous year
small, thin tree branch

Examples

She found a twig on the ground and used it to draw in the dirt.
The bird built its nest from small twigs and leaves.
He snapped a twig when he walked through the thick underbrush.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: branch - a part of a tree that grows out from the trunk or from a bough
  • Synonym: stick - a thin piece of wood, often fallen from a tree

Etymology

From Old English 'twigge' (branch, twig).

3. VERB

/ twɪɡ / (AmE), / twɪɡ / (BrE)
(informal) to suddenly realize or understand something, especially after not noticing it before.
suddenly realize or understand
informal british

Examples

It took me a while to twig that they were joking.
She suddenly twigged what he really meant during the conversation.
It can be embarrassing when you don't twig the joke until everyone else is laughing.

Synset Examples

It took me a while to twig that they were joking.
Once I twigged the plan, everything made sense.
I finally twigged to what he was implying.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to catch on - to understand or realize something, often after some delay
  • Synonym: to get it - to understand or realize something, especially a joke or explanation
  • Antonym: miss - to fail to notice or understand something

Etymology

19th-century British slang; probably from an earlier sense of 'twig' meaning 'to observe' (possibly from Irish tuig, meaning 'understand').
