degeneration

1. NOUN

/ dɪˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
The process by which something declines to a lower state in quality, power, or vitality.
decline in quality or vitality

Examples

The degeneration of the city's infrastructure was evident after years of neglect.
Medical research focuses on preventing the degeneration of nerve cells in the elderly.
He feared the degeneration of moral standards in society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deterioration - the process of becoming worse
  • Antonym: improvement - the process of becoming better

Etymology

From Latin degenerātiō (decline to a lower state), from degenerare (to degenerate).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or state of declining to a lower or less moral or mental condition
decline in moral or mental qualities

Examples

Many critics saw the novel as a sign of moral degeneration in society.
The degeneration of values was lamented by the older generation.
Some believed that urbanization would lead to cultural degeneration and loss of tradition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deterioration - the process of becoming progressively worse
  • Synonym: corruption - dishonest or immoral behavior or decay
  • Antonym: improvement - the process of becoming better

Etymology

From Latin degeneratio (decline to a lower state), from degenerare (to degenerate).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˌdʒɛnɚˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which something, especially a biological structure, becomes less complex or returns to a simpler form
reversion to a simpler biological form
medical

Examples

The scientist studied the degeneration of certain organs in cave-dwelling creatures.
Over time, some species show degeneration in features that are no longer necessary for survival.
The theory suggests degeneration is a natural consequence in isolated populations with limited resources.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: regression - return to an earlier or less advanced state
  • Antonym: evolution - process of gradual development from a simpler to a more complex form

Etymology

From Latin 'dēgenerātiō', from 'dēgenerāre' (to deteriorate, to become unlike one's kind).
