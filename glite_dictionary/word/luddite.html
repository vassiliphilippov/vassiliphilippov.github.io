Luddite

1. NOUN

/ ˈlʌɪˌdaɪt / (AmE), / ˈlʌd.aɪt / (BrE)
a person who opposes or resists the introduction of new technology or technological change
opponent of technological progress

Examples

Some people call him a Luddite because he refuses to use smartphones.
The company's Luddite attitude slowed their adoption of modern software.
Despite being labeled a Luddite, she simply prefers reading printed books over using tablets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: technophobe - a person who fears, dislikes, or avoids new technology
  • Antonym: innovator - a person who introduces new methods or technologies

Etymology

From the name of Ned Ludd, a possibly mythical leader of textile workers who destroyed machinery in England, early 19th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlʌdaɪt / (AmE), / ˈlʌdaɪt / (BrE)
a member of a group of 19th-century English workers who destroyed machinery, especially in cotton and woolen mills, that they believed was threatening their jobs
19th-century machine-destroying worker

Examples

Many historians believe the Luddites were protesting poor working conditions as much as they were new technology.
The government responded harshly to Luddite uprisings in 1812.
During the Industrial Revolution, Luddites became famous for smashing weaving machines.

Etymology

From Ned Ludd, a legendary leader of the machine-breakers, combined with the suffix -ite.
