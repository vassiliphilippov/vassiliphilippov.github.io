prescriptivism

1. NOUN

/ prɪˈskrɪptɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / prɪˈskrɪptɪvɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The doctrine, especially in ethics, that holds moral statements express prescriptions for action or attitudes rather than factual claims.
ethical doctrine prescribing actions/attitudes

Examples

Many ethicists debate the merits of prescriptivism versus descriptivism in moral philosophy.
According to prescriptivism, saying 'You ought to be honest' is prescribing a standard of behavior.
He wrote his doctoral thesis on the impact of prescriptivism in twentieth-century ethical theory.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: descriptivism - the view that moral statements describe facts rather than prescribe actions

Etymology

From prescribe + -ism, indicating a belief system focused on prescribing actions.

2. NOUN

/ prɪˈskrɪptɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / prɪˈskrɪptɪvɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The doctrine or belief that certain rules or norms should be prescribed and followed, especially in language use.
doctrine prescribing language rules

Examples

Linguists often debate the merits of prescriptivism versus descriptivism in language teaching.
Some teachers favor prescriptivism, insisting on strict grammar rules in the classroom.
The rise of prescriptivism influenced how dictionaries listed standard word usage.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: descriptivism - The doctrine that describes how language is actually used, rather than prescribing rules.

Etymology

From 'prescriptive' + '-ism'; 'prescriptive' from Latin praescribere (to write before, to order).
