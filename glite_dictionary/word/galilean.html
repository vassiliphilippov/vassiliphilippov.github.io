Galilean

1. NOUN

/ ˌɡælɪˈliən / (AmE), / ˌɡalɪˈliːən / (BrE)
any of the four large moons of Jupiter discovered by Galileo—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.
one of the four main moons of Jupiter discovered by Galileo

Examples

Io is a Galilean moon known for its intense volcanic activity.
Scientists have studied each Galilean for signs of subsurface oceans.
The four Galilean satellites are visible with a small telescope.

Etymology

From Galileo Galilei, Italian astronomer who discovered the moons in 1610.

2. ADJ

/ ˌɡæləˈliən / (AmE), / ˌɡalɪˈliːən / (BrE)
Relating to Galilee, a region in northern Israel, or its people.
of Galilee or its inhabitants

Examples

The Galilean countryside is known for its beautiful hills and historical sites.
Her family traced their roots to a Galilean village near Nazareth.
Many traditions from the Galilean region have shaped local culture.

Etymology

From Latin 'Galilaeus', from 'Galilaea' (Galilee).

3. ADJ

/ ˌɡælɪˈleɪən / (AmE), / ˌɡælɪˈleɪən / (BrE)
of or relating to Galileo Galilei or his works, ideas, or discoveries
relating to Galileo Galilei

Examples

The Galilean moons are the four largest satellites of Jupiter discovered by Galileo.
His Galilean telescope revolutionized astronomy in the early 17th century.
The principle of relativity was first expressed in the context of Galilean transformations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Copernican - relating to Copernicus or his theories

Etymology

From Galileo Galilei (1564–1642), Italian astronomer and physicist, with adjective-forming suffix -an.

4. NOUN

/ ˌɡæləˈliən / (AmE), / ˌɡalɪˈliːən / (BrE)
An inhabitant of Galilee, especially used as an epithet for Jesus Christ.
a person from Galilee, esp. Jesus Christ

Examples

Many early followers of Jesus were known as Galileans in first-century Judea.
The Roman authorities regarded the Galileans as a rebellious group.
Jesus of Nazareth is sometimes called the Galilean in historical texts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Nazarene - a native or inhabitant of Nazareth, often used as a reference to Jesus Christ

Etymology

From Latin 'Galilaeus', from Greek 'Galilaios', meaning 'of Galilee'.
