dictate

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪkteɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪkteɪt / (BrE)
a principle or command that guides actions or beliefs, especially as a rule or standard to be followed
guiding principle or command

Examples

She always tried to follow the dictates of her conscience.
The company's policies are governed by the dictates of fairness and integrity.
He acted according to the dictates of reason rather than emotion.

Synset Examples

the dictates of reason

Related Terms

  • Synonym: command - an authoritative order
  • Synonym: principle - a fundamental truth or proposition that guides action

Etymology

From Latin 'dictatum' (something dictated), from 'dictare' (to say repeatedly, dictate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05879867-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪk.teɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪk.teɪt / (BrE)
to say words aloud so that they may be written down or recorded by someone else
say aloud for someone to write down

Examples

The manager asked his assistant to dictate the meeting minutes.
She prefers to dictate her letters instead of typing them herself.
The professor would dictate notes while the students wrote them down.

Synset Examples

He dictated a report to his secretary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to recite - to repeat aloud from memory
  • Synonym: to transcribe - to put spoken words into written form

Etymology

From Latin dictāre (to say repeatedly, dictate), from dicere (to say).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00628527-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdɪkˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪkteɪt / (BrE)
to rule or command with absolute authority, typically in an oppressive or autocratic manner
to rule as a dictator

Examples

The general attempted to dictate the country's laws without consulting anyone.
For decades, the king dictated every aspect of daily life in his nation.
She fears her new boss will try to dictate the team's every move.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: command - to give an authoritative order
  • Synonym: to govern - to conduct the policy, actions, and affairs of a state or people
  • Antonym: to submit - to yield or surrender to authority

Etymology

From Latin dictātus, past participle of dictāre (to dictate, to order repeatedly).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02593176-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ ˈdɪkˌteɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪkteɪt / (BrE)
An authoritative command or rule that must be obeyed.
authoritative command or rule

Examples

The company followed the dictates of the new policy without question.
She refused to ignore the dictates of her conscience.
Despite external dictates, the community maintained its traditions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: order - a command or directive that must be followed
  • Synonym: command - an instruction given with authority
  • Antonym: suggestion - an idea or proposal put forward for consideration without authority

Etymology

From Latin 'dictatum' (something dictated), from 'dictare' (to say often, dictate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06676685-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈdɪkˌteɪt / (AmE), / dɪkˈteɪt / (BrE)
to give orders or commands with authority; to state something authoritatively to be followed or carried out
to give commands or orders

Examples

The manager tried to dictate every aspect of the project to his team.
She resented having her actions dictated by someone else.
The new policy dictates that all employees must wear identification badges.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: command - to give an order with authority
  • Synonym: order - to direct or instruct authoritatively
  • Antonym: request - to ask for something politely or formally

Etymology

From Latin 'dictatus', past participle of 'dictare' (to say repeatedly, declare, prescribe).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00749139-v
Synset POS: v
