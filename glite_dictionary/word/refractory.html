refractory

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈfræktəri / (AmE), / rɪˈfræktəri / (BrE)
a lining made of material with a very high melting point, used to line the inside walls of furnaces and similar high-temperature industrial equipment
heat-resistant furnace lining

Examples

The steel plant replaced the refractory to ensure the furnace could withstand higher temperatures.
A damaged refractory can lead to costly shutdowns in industrial operations.
Choosing the correct refractory is essential for the efficiency and longevity of the kiln.

Etymology

From Latin 'refractarius' (stubborn), with technical sense from the capacity to resist heat and chemical attack.

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈfræk.tə.ri / (AmE), / rɪˈfræk.tər.i / (BrE)
not responding to treatment, especially when a disease or condition resists medical intervention
not responding to treatment
medical

Examples

The patient had a refractory infection that did not improve with antibiotics.
Doctors described her epilepsy as refractory after several treatments failed to control it.
Some cancers are refractory to conventional chemotherapy.

Synset Examples

a stubborn infection
a refractory case of acne
stubborn rust stains

Related Terms

  • Synonym: resistant - able to withstand something, not affected by treatment
  • Antonym: responsive - reacting positively to treatment

Etymology

From Latin 'refractarius', from 'refractus' (broken up, resisted).

3. ADJ

/ rɪˈfræktəri / (AmE), / rɪˈfræktəri / (BrE)
(of a body, tissue, or organism) temporarily unresponsive or not fully responsive to a stimulus or process, especially after being activated.
temporarily unresponsive to stimulus

Examples

After firing, a neuron enters a refractory state in which it cannot immediately fire again.
Some cardiac cells are refractory and do not respond to another stimulus until recovery.
The refractory period prevents muscles from being restimulated too soon.

Synset Examples

the refractory period of a muscle fiber

Related Terms

  • Antonym: responsive - reacting quickly and positively

Etymology

From Latin 'refractarius', from 'refragari' (to oppose or resist).

/ rɪˈfræk.tɚ.i / (AmE), / rɪˈfræk.tər.i / (BrE)
resisting authority or control; stubbornly uncooperative or difficult to manage
stubbornly resistant to authority or control

Examples

The refractory student refused to follow classroom rules despite repeated warnings.
Doctors had trouble treating the patient's refractory infection, which did not respond to standard medications.
His refractory attitude made teamwork difficult for the rest of the group.

Synset Examples

Related Terms
