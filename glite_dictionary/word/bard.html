bard

1. NOUN

/ bɑrd / (AmE), / bɑːd / (BrE)
an ornamental covering for a horse, especially one used in ceremonial or parade contexts
ornamental horse covering

Examples

The knight's horse wore a richly decorated bard during the royal procession.
At the tournament, every steed had a unique bard displaying its rider's heraldic colors.
The museum displayed a medieval bard made of velvet and embroidered with gold thread.

Etymology

From Old French *barde*, from Italian *barda*, likely of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ bɑrd / (AmE), / bɑːd / (BrE)
a poet, traditionally one reciting epic or lyrical poetry and associated with oral traditions, especially in ancient times
a poet, especially an ancient or traditional one

Examples

The wandering bard captivated the villagers with tales of ancient heroes.
Many refer to Shakespeare as the greatest bard of English literature.
In Celtic society, a bard held a respected place as both musician and historian.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poet - a writer of poems

Etymology

From Old Celtic *bardos, adopted into English via Scottish Gaelic and French.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09858410-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ bɑrd / (AmE), / bɑːd / (BrE)
to cover (a horse) with ornamental cloth or armor, especially for ceremonial or military purposes
put ornamental covering on a horse

Examples

The knights prepared for battle and barded their steeds with decorative armor.
For the parade, each horse was carefully barded in vibrant, embroidered cloth.
Before the tournament, the squires spent hours barding the horses with elaborate caparisons.

Synset Examples

caparison the horses for the festive occasion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caparison - to put ornamental coverings on a horse

Etymology

From Old French *barde* (protective covering for a horse), from Late Latin *barda*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01674210-v
Synset POS: v
