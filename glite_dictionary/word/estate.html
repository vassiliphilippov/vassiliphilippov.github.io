estate

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈsteɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈsteɪt / (BrE)
all the money, property, and possessions owned by a person, especially at death, including any debts owed
all of a person's property and debts

Examples

After her grandfather passed away, Jane inherited his entire estate.
The lawyer explained how the estate would be divided among the heirs.
Managing an estate can be complicated if there are outstanding debts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assets - things of value owned by a person or company
  • Antonym: liabilities - debts or financial obligations

Etymology

From Old French *estat* (state, position), from Latin *status* (state, condition).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈsteɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈsteɪt / (BrE)
a major social class or order in a country, historically regarded as part of the body politic and often with distinct political rights
major social class or order (historical)

Examples

In medieval France, the nobility formed the second estate of the realm.
The clergy, nobility, and commoners were the three recognized estates.
During the revolution, representatives of each estate were called to the assembly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: class - a group of people with similar social or economic status

Etymology

From Old French *estat*, from Latin *status* (state, condition, position).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈsteɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈsteɪt / (BrE)
A large area of land, usually in the countryside, owned by one person, family, or organization, often with a large house.
large country property owned by a person or family

Examples

The family lived on a beautiful estate in the countryside for generations.
He inherited a vast estate including several farms and forests.
The gardens surrounding the main house on the estate are open to the public in summer.

Synset Examples

the family owned a large estate on Long Island

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manor - a large house with lands; the main house of an estate
  • Synonym: property - a piece of land or real estate; ownership of land

Etymology

From Old French 'estat' (condition, situation, property), from Latin 'status' (state, condition).
