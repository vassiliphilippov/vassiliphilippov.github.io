devilfish

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛvəlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈdɛvəlˌfɪʃ / (BrE)
a very large tropical ray, especially the manta ray, known for its broad, wing-like fins and plankton-feeding habits; generally harmless but can be dangerous due to its size when threatened
large plankton-eating tropical ray

Examples

The diver was thrilled to spot a massive devilfish gliding gracefully through the water.
Fishermen once feared the devilfish because of its impressive size.
The devilfish feeds on plankton and small fish as it swims near the ocean surface.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manta ray - a large ray with triangular pectoral fins, commonly called devilfish

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɛvəlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈdɛv.əl.fɪʃ / (BrE)
A cephalopod with a soft, oval body and eight long tentacles, typically living on the sea bottom; used especially for certain octopuses or rays.
type of marine cephalopod (octopus or ray)

Examples

The devilfish glided silently along the ocean floor, blending into its surroundings.
Fishermen were surprised to catch a large devilfish near the rocky shore.
The documentary showed how the devilfish uses its tentacles to capture prey.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: octopus - a soft-bodied, eight-armed marine cephalopod mollusk

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɛvəlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈdɛvəlˌfɪʃ / (BrE)
A medium-sized greyish-black whale of the northern Pacific, also known as the killer whale or orca.
medium-sized whale of the northern Pacific

Examples

The devilfish is often spotted hunting in pods along the Alaskan coast.
Early sailors called the orca a devilfish because of its fearsome reputation.
The documentary featured rare footage of a devilfish breaching the water's surface.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: orca - large predatory whale, also called killer whale
