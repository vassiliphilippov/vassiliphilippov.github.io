casuistic

1. ADJ

/ ˌkæʒuˈɪstɪk / (AmE), / ˌkæzjuˈɪstɪk / (BrE)
relating to or characteristic of casuistry, especially in solving moral problems by applying general principles to particular cases
relating to casuistry or subtle moral reasoning

Examples

His arguments were criticized for their casuistic logic and evasiveness.
The philosopher adopted a casuistic approach to resolve ethical dilemmas.
Some legal scholars warn against casuistic analysis that ignores the broader context.

Synset Examples

overly subtle casuistic reasoning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: casuistry - the use of clever but unsound reasoning, especially in moral questions

Etymology

From Latin 'casuistica', from 'casus' meaning 'case'.

2. ADJ

/ ˌkæʒuˈɪstɪk / (AmE), / ˌkazjʊˈɪstɪk / (BrE)
Relating to the application of general ethical principles to resolve specific moral problems.
related to resolving moral problems by ethical principles

Examples

The philosopher presented a casuistic argument to address the moral dilemma.
Some critics find casuistic reasoning to be overly complex and impractical.
Her approach to the issue was thoroughly casuistic, focusing on applying ethical rules to each unique situation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: casuistry - the use of clever but unsound reasoning, especially in relation to moral questions

Etymology

From Latin 'casuisticus' (relating to cases of conscience), from 'casus' (case).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03064865-a
Synset POS: a
