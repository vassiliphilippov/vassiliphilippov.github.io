synoptic

1. ADJ

/ sɪˈnɑptɪk / (AmE), / sɪˈnɒptɪk / (BrE)
presenting or taking a similar point of view; especially used to refer to the first three Gospels of the New Testament, which share common content, order, and outlook.
presenting a common point of view; biblical term

Examples

The synoptic Gospels—Matthew, Mark, and Luke—contain many of the same stories in similar sequence.
His research provides a synoptic overview of early Christian teachings.
Scholars often compare synoptic sayings to identify shared sources in biblical texts.

Synset Examples

synoptic sayings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: synopsis - a brief summary or general survey

Etymology

From Greek synoptikos (giving a synopsis), from synopsi (seeing all together).

2. ADJ

/ sɪˈnɑːptɪk / (AmE), / sɪˈnɒptɪk / (BrE)
giving a general summary or comprehensive overview of a subject or situation.
giving a general summary or overview

Examples

The professor provided a synoptic overview of modern philosophy in the first lecture.
This chart offers a synoptic summary of the project milestones and deadlines.
The report is highly synoptic, helping readers grasp the main themes at a glance.

Synset Examples

a synoptic presentation of a physical theory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: summary - a brief statement or account of the main points

Etymology

From Greek 'synoptikos' (giving a general view), from 'syn-' (together) + 'opsis' (view).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02819203-a
Synset POS: a
