exculpation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛkˌskʌlˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛkskʌlˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of freeing someone from blame or proving their innocence
the act of clearing from blame

Examples

The new evidence led to his exculpation after years of wrongful imprisonment.
Her lawyer argued passionately for her exculpation in court.
After months of investigation, the official report resulted in his complete exculpation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vindication - the act of clearing someone of blame or suspicion
  • Antonym: inculpation - the act of accusing or blaming

Etymology

From Latin exculpāre (to clear from blame), from ex- (out of) + culpa (blame).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɛkˌskʌlˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛkskʌlˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
The act of excusing or justifying someone's offensive behavior, failure, or wrongdoing to clear them of blame.
act of excusing or clearing from blame

Examples

His lawyer's skillful exculpation convinced the jury of his innocence.
The manager's repeated exculpation of her team's mistakes was not well received by the board.
Public exculpation is sometimes necessary when a celebrity is accused of misconduct.

Synset Examples

he kept finding excuses to stay
every day he had a new alibi for not getting a job
his transparent self-justification was unacceptable

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - the act of showing something to be right or reasonable
  • Synonym: excuse - a reason or explanation put forward to defend or justify a fault or offense
  • Antonym: condemnation - the act of expressing strong disapproval or sentencing someone to punishment

Etymology

From Latin exculpatus, past participle of exculpare (to clear from blame), from ex- (out) + culpa (blame, fault).
