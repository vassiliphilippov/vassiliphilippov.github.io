allegory

1. NOUN

/ ˈæl.əˌɡɔːr.i / (AmE), / ˈæl.ɪɡəri / (BrE)
A story, poem, or picture that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden meaning, typically a moral or political one; an extended metaphor in which characters and events represent abstract ideas or principles.
a story with a hidden moral or political meaning

Examples

George Orwell's 'Animal Farm' is often read as an allegory for totalitarianism.
The painting uses visual elements as an allegory for the passage of time.
Teachers often use fables as an allegory to explain complex moral lessons.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metaphor - a figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to something it does not literally denote

Etymology

From Latin 'allegoria', from Greek 'allēgoria' meaning 'speaking otherwise'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈæləˌɡɔri / (AmE), / ˈalɪɡəri / (BrE)
A short moral story, often with animal characters, in which the events and characters symbolize deeper meanings and teach a lesson.
moral story with symbolic meaning

Examples

The story of the tortoise and the hare is an allegory about perseverance and overconfidence.
In English class, we studied an allegory where each animal represented a human virtue or flaw.
Many ancient allegories were designed to teach children important life lessons.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fable - a short story, typically with animals as characters, conveying a moral

Etymology

From Latin allegoria, from Greek allēgoria (‘veiled language, figurative’).

3. NOUN

/ ˈæl.əˌɡɔː.ri / (AmE), / ˈæl.ɪ.ɡər.i / (BrE)
A symbol, often visible or concrete, that represents an abstract idea, principle, or moral, typically used in art or literature.
symbol representing an abstract idea

Examples

The statue of Justice, blindfolded and holding scales, is an allegory of impartiality.
Artists often use colors and objects as an allegory to convey deeper meanings in their work.
In the mural, the rising sun is an allegory for hope and new beginnings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: symbol - something that stands for or suggests something else by reason of relationship, association, or convention

Etymology

From Latin allegoria, from Greek allēgoria (veiled language, figurative language).
