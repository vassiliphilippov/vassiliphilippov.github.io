pander

1. NOUN

/ ˈpændər / (AmE), / ˈpændə / (BrE)
a person who arranges clients for prostitutes; a pimp
person who procures clients for prostitutes
archaic

Examples

The police arrested the pander during an undercover operation downtown.
He was known as a notorious pander in the city's criminal circles.
In seventeenth-century literature, a pander often appeared as a villainous character.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pimp - a person who controls prostitutes and arranges clients for them

Etymology

From Middle English pander, from the character Pandare in Chaucer's 'Troilus and Criseyde,' derived from Italian Pandaro.

2. VERB

/ ˈpændər / (AmE), / ˈpandə / (BrE)
to gratify or indulge (an immoral or distasteful desire, need, or habit, or a person with such a desire)
to indulge or gratify an improper desire

Examples

The tabloids often pander to people’s basest instincts for sensational news.
Some politicians are accused of pandering to voters just to win support.
You shouldn’t pander to every unreasonable demand your child makes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to indulge - to allow oneself or another to enjoy the pleasure of something, especially something considered wrong or unwise
  • Synonym: to satisfy - to fulfill a desire, need, or appetite
  • Antonym: to resist - to withstand the action or effect of something; to refuse to yield

Etymology

From Middle English 'pandare', ultimately from Pandarus, a character in Chaucer who procured lovers.

3. VERB

/ ˈpændɚ / (AmE), / ˈpændə / (BrE)
to arrange sexual liaisons or procure sexual partners for others, especially for immoral purposes
arrange sexual partners for others
archaic

Examples

In past centuries, an unscrupulous servant might pander to the desires of wealthy clients.
The law strictly punishes anyone who attempts to pander for profit.
He was notorious for having pandered to powerful men, arranging secret meetings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to procure - to obtain something, especially with effort or difficulty
  • Synonym: pimp - to arrange clients for prostitutes

Etymology

From Middle English pandare, from the name Pandarus, a character in Chaucer's 'Troilus and Criseyde' who procured lovers for others.
