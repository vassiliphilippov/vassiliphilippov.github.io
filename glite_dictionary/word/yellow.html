yellow

1. ADJ

/ ˈjɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈjɛləʊ / (BrE)
(of a person or their skin) affected by jaundice, causing a yellowish discoloration.
having skin yellowed by jaundice
medical

Examples

The doctor noticed that the patient's skin had turned yellow due to liver problems.
He became alarmed when his normally fair complexion suddenly looked yellow.
Newborns sometimes appear yellow in the first week if they develop mild jaundice.

2. ADJ

/ ˈjɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈjɛləʊ / (BrE)
having the color that is intermediate between green and orange in the spectrum, like that of egg yolk, lemons, or sunflowers
of the color between green and orange

Examples

The bright sunflowers were a vibrant yellow in the afternoon light.
She wore a yellow dress that stood out at the party.
The school bus is always painted a distinctive shade of yellow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: golden - having the bright yellow color of gold
  • Degree Lower: yellowish - somewhat yellow in color
  • Degree Higher: orange - having the color between red and yellow

Etymology

Old English *geolu*, from Proto-Germanic *gelwaz* (yellow), related to Latin *galbinus*.

Synset POS: s

7. ADJ

/ ˈjɛloʊ / (AmE), / ˈjɛləʊ / (BrE)
(of journalism) sensational, biased, or unprofessional in style, typically focused on scandal or lurid stories rather than serious news.
sensational or unprofessional journalism

Examples

Some critics accused the newspaper of adopting a yellow approach to reporting the celebrity scandal.
The rise of yellow journalism in the 19th century changed the media landscape dramatically.
Many people distrust tabloids because of their reputation for yellow stories and misleading headlines.

Synset Examples

sensational journalistic reportage of the scandal
yellow press

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensationalist - presenting stories in a way that is intended to provoke public interest or excitement
  • Antonym: responsible - showing careful and balanced approach, especially in news reporting

Etymology

From the phrase 'yellow journalism,' coined in the late 19th century, originally referencing newspaper practices marked by sensationalism; influenced by the Yellow Kid comic strip associated with such papers.
