mast

1. NOUN

/ mæst / (AmE), / mɑːst / (BrE)
fallen nuts of forest trees, such as acorns and beechnuts, that accumulate on the ground and often serve as food for animals
nuts fallen on ground for animal food

Examples

The forest floor was covered in mast after a bumper crop of acorns and beech nuts.
Pigs were traditionally turned out into the woods to forage for mast in the autumn.
Wild boars rely on the abundance of mast to fatten up before winter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acorn - the nut of the oak tree
  • Synonym: beechnut - the nut of the beech tree

Etymology

From Old English mæst (food for swine), related to German Mast (fattening) and Latin madius (moist).

2. NOUN

/ mæst / (AmE), / mɑːst / (BrE)
A tall, upright pole on a ship or boat that supports the sails and rigging.
tall vertical pole on a ship for sails

Examples

The sailors climbed the mast to unfurl the sails.
A lookout stood at the top of the ship’s mast, scanning the horizon.
During the storm, the mast snapped in half, leaving the boat adrift.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boom - horizontal spar at the bottom of a sail
  • Synonym: spar - a strong pole used on ships to support sails and rigging

Etymology

From Old English mæst, of Germanic origin, related to Dutch mast and German Mast.

3. NOUN

/ mæst / (AmE), / mɑːst / (BrE)
a tall, sturdy upright pole, especially one that supports the sails of a ship or is used for other purposes such as holding antennas or flags
tall upright pole (ship, antenna, etc.)

Examples

The ship's mast snapped during the fierce storm.
Workers climbed the radio mast to perform routine maintenance.
A tall mast stood in the middle of the field, supporting a large flag.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antenna - a structure that transmits or receives radio waves; commonly called a 'mast' in some contexts

Etymology

Old English mæst, related to Old Norse mastr and Dutch mast.

4. NOUN

/ mæst / (AmE), / mɑːst / (BrE)
the fruit of forest trees, such as acorns and beechnuts, that falls to the ground and is eaten by animals, especially pigs
nuts of forest trees used as animal feed

Examples

In autumn, the forest floor is covered with mast that provides natural food for wild boar.
Farmers historically released their pigs into woodlands to forage for mast.
Heavy rainfall led to a particularly abundant crop of mast this year, benefiting local wildlife.

Etymology

Old English mæst (food for swine, acorns), related to Old High German mast (fodder).
