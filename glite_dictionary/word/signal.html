signal

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪɡnəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡnəl / (BrE)
a nonverbal action, gesture, or movement intended to convey information or instructions to others
nonverbal action conveying a message

Examples

The lifeguard raised his arm as a signal for everyone to leave the pool.
She gave him a quick nod, which he took as a signal to start talking.
The runner waited for the starter’s signal before sprinting down the track.

Synset Examples

signals from the boat suddenly stopped

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gesture - a movement of part of the body, especially a hand or the head, to express an idea or meaning

Etymology

From Latin 'signum' (mark, token, sign).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06804229-n
Synset POS: n

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (BrE)
notably out of the ordinary; striking or distinguished
striking; out of the ordinary

Examples

She received a signal honor at the awards ceremony for her contributions.
His resignation was seen as a signal event that changed the company's direction.

Synset Examples

the year saw one signal triumph for the Labour party

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remarkable - worthy of attention; striking
  • Antonym: ordinary - with no special or distinctive features; normal

Etymology

From Latin 'signalis' (of a sign), from 'signum' (sign).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (BrE)
an action, event, or sound that serves to incite or prompt someone to begin or proceed with something; an indication that something should happen
incitement or indication to act

Examples

The referee blew the whistle as a signal for the players to start the match.
She gave a subtle signal to her friend to leave the party early.
The loud cheers were a signal that the concert was about to begin.

Synset Examples

he awaited the signal to start
the victory was a signal for wild celebration

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cue - a signal to an actor or performer to begin a specific action
  • Synonym: prompt - a signal or act that encourages someone to take action

Etymology

From Latin signālis (of a sign), from signum (sign).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09208177-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nəl / (BrE)
to communicate information or intentions silently, using gestures, signs, or other non-verbal cues
communicate non-verbally with gestures or signs

Examples

He signals his agreement with a nod rather than speaking out loud.
The teacher signaled the class to be quiet with a finger to her lips.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

Etymology

5. NOUN

/ ˈsɪɡnəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡnəl / (BrE)
an electrical quantity, such as voltage or current, that conveys information when modulated, especially in communication or electronic systems
electrical quantity carrying information

Examples

The radio receiver amplifies the signal to make it easier to process.
A weak signal can result in poor sound quality on your phone call.
Electronic engineers analyzed the signal for interference from other devices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transmission - the act or process of sending information, especially electronically
  • Antonym: noise - unwanted disturbances that interfere with a signal

Etymology

From Latin signālis (of a sign), from signum (mark, sign).

6. VERB

/ ˈsɪɡnəl / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡnəl / (BrE)
to be a sign that something exists or is likely to happen; to indicate or suggest something
to indicate or be a sign of

Examples

His sudden silence signaled his discomfort with the topic.
Rising sea levels signal the urgent need for action on climate change.

Synset Examples

The economic indicators signal that the euro is undervalued

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to imply - to suggest that something is true without saying so directly

Etymology

From Latin 'signum' (mark, sign).
