Dicotyledones

1. NOUN

/ daɪˌkɑtɪlɪˈdoʊniːz / (AmE), / daɪˌkɒtɪlɪˈdəʊniːz / (BrE)
A large group of flowering plants (seed plants) that have embryos with two cotyledons (seed leaves) and typically have net-veined leaves, formerly classified as a major division in plant taxonomy.
class of flowering plants with two seed leaves
medical

Examples

Most trees and shrubs belong to the Dicotyledones, sharing characteristics like net-veined leaves.
The rose plant is classified among the Dicotyledones due to its two seed leaves.
Advances in plant genetics have led to new insights into the classification of Dicotyledones.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Monocotyledones - Group of flowering plants with only one cotyledon

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek 'di-' (two) + 'kotyledon' (cup-shaped cavity).

2. NOUN

/ daɪˌkɑtɪlɪˈdoʊniːz / (AmE), / daɪˌkɒtɪlɪˈdəʊniːz / (BrE)
A class of flowering plants whose seeds typically have two embryonic leaves or cotyledons, usually characterized by net-veined leaves.
class of plants with two cotyledons

Examples

Many familiar trees such as oaks and maples belong to the Dicotyledones.
The Dicotyledones are distinguished from monocots by the presence of two seed leaves.
According to traditional classification, roses are members of the Dicotyledones.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monocotyledon - plants with a single cotyledon in the seed

Etymology

From Greek di- (two) + kotyledon (cup-shaped cavity), referring to the two seed leaves.
