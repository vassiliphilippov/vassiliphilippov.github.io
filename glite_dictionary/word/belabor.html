to belabor

1. VERB

/ bɪˈleɪbɚ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to hit or attack someone or something repeatedly and forcefully; to beat soundly.
to beat or attack repeatedly

Examples

The attacker began to belabor his victim with a heavy stick.
In the old tale, the giant would belabor anyone who trespassed in his territory.
They belabored the door with battering rams until it finally gave way.

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + labor (to work), Middle English and earlier.

2. VERB

/ bəˈleɪbɚ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to discuss or treat a subject at excessive or unnecessary length
discuss at excessive length

Examples

There's no need to belabor the point; everyone understands your argument.
The article tends to belabor the obvious, making it tedious to read.
Please don't belabor every minor detail during the meeting.

Synset Examples

belabor the obvious

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to overdo - to do something to an excessive degree

Etymology

From be- (intensive prefix) + labor (to work), from Latin 'laborare'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02420395-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bəˈleɪbɚ / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to criticize or attack someone or something harshly and repeatedly, especially in speech or writing
criticize or attack harshly, repeatedly

Examples

The senator continued to belabor his opponent's record during the debate, repeating the same criticisms several times.
It's unnecessary to belabor the mistake; we've already acknowledged it and learned from it.
She felt unfairly belabored by the harsh comments in the review.

Synset Examples

She was belabored by her fellow students

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to criticize - to express disapproval of someone or something by pointing out faults
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Middle English 'belaboren', from be- (intensive prefix) + 'laboren' (to work).

Source: WordNet
