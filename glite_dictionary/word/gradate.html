to gradate

1. VERB

/ ˈɡreɪ.deɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡreɪ.deɪt / (BrE)
to pass gradually from one degree, shade, or tone into another
pass gradually from one degree or shade to another

Examples

The colors in this tapestry gradate from deep blue to pale pink.
When mixing watercolors, the hues gradate softly across the paper.
Sunset skies often gradate from gold to violet in a beautiful display.

Synset Examples

The paint on these walls gradates but you don't see it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grade - to arrange or classify in grades; to level off

Etymology

From Latin gradatus, past participle of gradari 'to walk' (from gradus ‘step’).

2. VERB

/ ˈɡreɪ.deɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡreɪ.deɪt / (BrE)
to arrange or sort into grades, levels, or stages; to form with a gradual transition from one degree to another
arrange or form in grades or stages

Examples

The artist chose to gradate the colors, creating a smooth transition from blue to green across the canvas.
The stones were carefully gradated in size to create a visually pleasing effect along the pathway.
In geography class, students learned how to gradate the slopes of different hills for their mapping project.

Synset Examples

These lines are gradated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grade - to arrange in order according to quality, rank, or degree

Etymology

From Latin gradus (step, degree), via Italian gradato (graded) and Late Latin gradātus.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01616983-v
Synset POS: v
