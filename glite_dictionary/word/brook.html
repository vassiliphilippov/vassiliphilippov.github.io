brook

1. VERB

/ brʊk / (AmE), / brʊk / (BrE)
to tolerate or allow something, especially something unpleasant or unwelcome; usually used in the negative or formal contexts.
tolerate or allow (formal/literary use)

Examples

She would not brook any criticism of her work.
The manager will brook no interference in the decision-making process.
He refuses to brook any delays to the project.

Synset Examples

I cannot bear his constant criticism
The new secretary had to endure a lot of unprofessional remarks
he learned to tolerate the heat
She stuck out two years in a miserable marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to tolerate - to allow the existence or occurrence of something one dislikes
  • Synonym: to endure - to suffer patiently, to tolerate over time
  • Antonym: to forbid - to order not to do something; refuse to allow

Etymology

From Old English 'brūcan' (to use, enjoy, digest), related to German 'brauchen' (to need).

2. NOUN

/ brʊk / (AmE), / brʊk / (BrE)
a small natural stream of water, smaller than a river and often a tributary
small natural stream of water

Examples

We crossed the brook on stepping stones during our hike.
The brook behind her house flows gently all year long.
In autumn, leaves drifted quietly down the brook near the orchard.

Synset Examples

the creek dried up every summer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creek - a small stream, especially used in North America
  • Degree Higher: river - a large natural stream of water flowing towards an ocean, lake, or another river
  • Synonym: stream - a small, narrow river

Etymology

From Old English 'broc' (brook, stream).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09252316-n
Synset POS: n
