wrongness

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɔŋ.nəs / (AmE), / ˈrɒŋ.nəs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being contrary to what is morally right or acceptable; the condition of being wrong, especially in a moral sense
the state of being morally wrong

Examples

The wrongness of his actions became clear to everyone in the courtroom.
She felt a deep sense of wrongness about his dishonest plan.
Debating the wrongness of lying is common in ethics classes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rightness - the state of being morally or ethically correct
  • Synonym: wickedness - the quality of being evil or morally wrong

Etymology

Formed from 'wrong' + '-ness', a suffix denoting a quality or state.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɔŋ.nəs / (AmE), / ˈrɒŋ.nəs / (BrE)
The quality or state of being incorrect or not in accordance with fact or truth.
state of being incorrect or untrue

Examples

She acknowledged the wrongness of her previous assumptions after seeing the evidence.
There was an obvious wrongness in the report that made everyone suspicious.
Philosophers often debate the wrongness of certain actions in moral discussions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incorrectness - the quality of being inaccurate or not correct
  • Antonym: rightness - the quality of being correct or true

Etymology

From 'wrong' + '-ness', forming a noun indicating the state of being wrong.

3. NOUN

/ ˈrɔŋ.nəs / (AmE), / ˈrɒŋ.nəs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being morally or socially incorrect or inappropriate
quality of being wrong or inappropriate

Examples

The judge discussed the wrongness of the defendant's actions during the trial.
She felt a deep sense of wrongness when she witnessed the unfair treatment.
In his speech, he emphasized the wrongness of discriminating against others.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rightness - the quality or state of being correct or appropriate

Etymology

From 'wrong' + '-ness', Old English 'wrang' (wrong) + '-ness' (state or quality).
