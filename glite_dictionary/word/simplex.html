simplex

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɪm.plɛks / (AmE), / ˈsɪm.plɛks / (BrE)
having only one part or element; not complex or compounded
having one part or element

Examples

The linguist explained that a simplex word, like 'dog', contains no affixes or compounds.
In certain technical fields, a simplex system is one that operates in only one direction.
A simplex molecule consists of a single, unbranched structure.

Synset Examples

a simplex word has no affixes and is not part of a compound — like ‘boy’ compared with ‘boyish’ or ‘house’ compared with ‘houseboat’

Related Terms

  • Antonym: complex - having many parts or elements
  • Antonym: compound - consisting of two or more parts or elements

Etymology

From Latin simplex (single, simple), from sem- (one) + plicare (to fold).

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɪm.plɛks / (AmE), / ˈsɪm.plɛks / (BrE)
(of a communications system) allowing transmission or communication in only one direction at a time.
one-way communication system

Examples

The walkie-talkies used a simplex system, so only one person could speak at a time.
In early telegraph networks, communication was simplex, limiting efficiency.
Many radio broadcasts operate in simplex mode, sending signals to listeners without receiving feedback.

Synset Examples

simplex system

Related Terms

  • Antonym: duplex - system allowing communication in both directions, but not simultaneously
  • Degree Higher: half-duplex - system allowing two-way communication, but not at the same time
  • Degree Higher: full-duplex - system allowing simultaneous two-way communication

Etymology

From Latin simplex (simple, single).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00236114-s
Synset POS: s
