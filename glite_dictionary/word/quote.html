quote

1. NOUN

/ kwoʊt / (AmE), / kwəʊt / (BrE)
a punctuation mark, usually a pair of inverted commas or quotation marks (“ ” or ‘ ’), used to show that the enclosed text is attributed to someone else or is a direct speech
punctuation mark for direct speech or attribution

Examples

Be sure to put the dialogue inside quotes when you write your story.
He forgot to close the quote at the end of the sentence.
In American English, the comma usually goes inside the quote.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quotation mark - punctuation marks indicating direct speech or quotation

Etymology

Shortened form of 'quotation mark', itself from 'quotation' (from Latin 'quotare', to number or cite).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06857249-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ kwoʊt / (AmE), / kwəʊt / (BrE)
to repeat or copy out a passage, statement, or phrase from a book, speech, or author, usually indicating its source
repeat or copy a passage from someone else

Examples

She likes to quote famous philosophers during her lectures.
He quoted a line from Shakespeare in his speech.
Writers often quote statistics to support their arguments.

Synset Examples

He quoted the Bible to her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cite - to mention or refer to as evidence for justification of an argument or statement

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'quotare' (to mark the number of), from Latin 'quot' (how many).

Source: WordNet
