dismay

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈmeɪ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmeɪ / (BrE)
a sudden feeling of fear or worry, often caused by the realization of danger or something unexpected
sudden fear or anxiety

Examples

The news of the approaching storm filled the villagers with dismay.
She looked at the broken vase in dismay, unsure of what to do next.
To my dismay, the plan had completely fallen apart at the last minute.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alarm - a sudden fear or anxiety about danger
  • Antonym: calm - a state of being free from fear or anxiety

Etymology

From Old French desmai (distress), from des- (dis-, expressing reversal) + esmai (trouble, fear).

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈmeɪ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmeɪ / (BrE)
to make someone feel shocked, worried, or disappointed, often in a way that causes alarm or distress
cause alarm or distress

Examples

Many parents were dismayed by the school's sudden decision to close.
The news of rising prices dismayed the entire community.
Her careless comments dismayed her colleagues during the meeting.

Synset Examples

I was horrified at the thought of being late for my interview
The news of the executions horrified us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alarm - to make someone suddenly fearful or anxious
  • Antonym: to reassure - to restore confidence or remove anxiety from someone

Etymology

From Old French *esmaier* (to frighten), from Vulgar Latin *exmagare* (to remove power or strength).

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈmeɪ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmeɪ / (BrE)
to make someone feel unhappy, disappointed, or worried, often suddenly or unexpectedly
to cause unhappiness or worry

Examples

The news of the company's bankruptcy dismayed many employees.
It would dismay her to see the state of the garden now.
Their delays and excuses dismayed the project manager.

Synset Examples

These news depressed her
The bad state of her child's health demoralizes her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dishearten - to make someone lose hope or confidence
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give someone confidence or hope

Etymology

From Old French *esmaier* (to frighten) and ultimately from Vulgar Latin *exmagare*.

4. NOUN

/ dɪsˈmeɪ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmeɪ / (BrE)
a strong feeling of shock, disappointment, or distress caused by something unexpected or difficult
a feeling of shock or distress

Examples

Sarah looked at the broken vase in dismay, unsure of how to explain it.
The teacher's decision to cancel the trip was met with dismay by the students.
He felt a wave of dismay when he realized he had forgotten his keys.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: despair - complete loss of hope
  • Antonym: delight - a feeling of great pleasure or happiness

Etymology

From Old French 'esmai' (distress), from 'esmaier' (to frighten), from Vulgar Latin *exmagare.
