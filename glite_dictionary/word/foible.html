foible

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈfɔɪbəl / (BrE)
a minor weakness or eccentricity in someone's character; a distinctive or peculiar habit
minor character weakness or eccentricity

Examples

Everyone has a personal foible that friends gently tease them about.
Her habit of humming while she worked was just a harmless foible.
Despite his many foibles, he was admired for his generosity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weakness - a personal weakness; flaw in character
  • Antonym: strength - a personal strong point; an asset in character

Etymology

From French *foible* (weak), from Old French, from Latin *feblis* (weak).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɔɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈfɔɪbəl / (BrE)
A minor flaw, weakness, or eccentricity in someone's character.
minor character flaw or weakness

Examples

Everyone has a foible or two, but that doesn't make them any less admirable.
Her most endearing foible was her tendency to laugh at her own jokes.
We try to overlook his foibles because he is otherwise a talented colleague.

Synset Examples

The students admired their teacher despite his foibles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weakness - a lack of strength or a weak point in character
  • Antonym: strength - a strong trait or quality in character

Etymology

From French 'faible' meaning 'weak', itself from Latin 'flebilis' (lamentable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-92435289-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɔɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈfɔɪbəl / (BrE)
the weaker section of a sword blade, located between the middle and the point, opposite to the forte
weaker part of a sword blade

Examples

The fencer deftly struck with the foible of his blade to disarm his opponent.
Wear and damage are more likely to occur at the foible of a sword during combat.
Some swords are designed with a flexible foible for better maneuverability in fencing.

Etymology

From French 'faible' (weak), related to Latin 'flebilis' (lamentable).
