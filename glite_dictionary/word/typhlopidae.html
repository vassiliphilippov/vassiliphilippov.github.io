Typhlopidae

1. NOUN

/ taɪˈflɑːpɪˌdi / (AmE), / tɪˈflɒpɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of small, burrowing snakes, commonly known as blind snakes, characterized by their vestigial eyes and worm-like appearance.
family of blind, burrowing snakes

Examples

Scientists discovered a new species within the Typhlopidae family in the Amazon rainforest.
Members of the Typhlopidae are often mistaken for earthworms due to their size and appearance.
The Typhlopidae are known for their burrowing habits and reduced eyesight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blind snake - a common name for snakes of the Typhlopidae family

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek typhlos (blind) + -idae (zoological family suffix).

2. NOUN

/ taɪˈflɑːpɪdiː / (AmE), / tɪˈflɒpɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of small, nonvenomous, worm-like blind snakes found chiefly in tropical and subtropical regions.
family of blind, worm-like snakes

Examples

The Typhlopidae are often mistaken for earthworms due to their slender, cylindrical bodies.
Members of the Typhlopidae family have reduced eyesight and burrow underground.
Researchers discovered a new species within the Typhlopidae in Southeast Asia.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blind snake - small, burrowing, nonvenomous snake with reduced eyes

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek typhlōp-, from 'typhlos' (blind) and 'ōps' (eye), referring to the snakes' reduced eyesight.
