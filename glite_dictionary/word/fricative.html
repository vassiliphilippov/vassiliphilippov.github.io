fricative

1. ADJ

/ ˈfrɪk.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈfrɪk.ə.tɪv / (BrE)
(of a speech sound) produced by forcing air through a narrow passage, causing friction; characteristic of sounds such as 'f', 's', 'z', or 'th' in 'thin' and 'then'.
sound made by forcing air through a narrow passage

Examples

The sound at the beginning of ‘fish’ is a fricative consonant.
In English, the ‘s’ in ‘see’ is pronounced as a voiceless fricative.
She studied the different ways languages use fricative sounds in their phonetic systems.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: plosive - a speech sound produced by stopping airflow and then releasing it (e.g. 'p', 't', 'k')

Etymology

From Latin fricativus (produced by rubbing), from fricare (to rub).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfrɪkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈfrɪkətɪv / (BrE)
a consonant sound produced by forcing air through a narrow channel in the vocal tract, causing friction
a consonant made with friction sound

Examples

The English 'f' sound in 'fine' is a classic example of a fricative.
Linguists study how different languages use fricative sounds in speech.
Children often find it challenging to pronounce certain fricative consonants when learning to speak.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: plosive - a consonant produced by stopping airflow then releasing it suddenly
  • Degree Lower: approximant - a consonant produced with a narrower constriction than a vowel but without friction

Etymology

From Latin fricativus, from fricare (to rub).
