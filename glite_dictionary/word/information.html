information

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fərˈmeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fəˈmeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
facts or details about something; knowledge communicated or received concerning a particular subject or event.
facts or knowledge communicated

Examples

The report provided useful information about the local economy.
If you need more information, please contact our help desk.
She gathered all the information she could before making a decision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: data - facts and statistics collected for reference or analysis
  • Antonym: misinformation - false or inaccurate information

Etymology

From Latin 'informare' (to shape, form), via Old French 'informacion'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fɚˈmeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fəˈmeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(communication theory) A quantitative measure of the uncertainty or surprise associated with a particular outcome, usually expressed in bits.
quantitative measure of uncertainty (theory)

Examples

In digital communication, information is measured in bits that represent the reduction of uncertainty.
The information content of a message depends on how unpredictable the outcome is.
Scientists use entropy to quantify the amount of information in a random variable.

Synset Examples

the signal contained thousands of bits of information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: entropy - (in information theory) measure of information content or uncertainty

Etymology

From Latin 'informatio' (a shaping, a conception), from 'informare' (to inform, shape).

4. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.fɚˈmeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.fəˈmeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A formal accusation of a crime made by a public official, without the need for a grand jury indictment.
formal legal accusation of a crime
legal

Examples

The prosecutor filed an information charging the suspect with theft.
Under some jurisdictions, prosecution may begin with an information rather than a grand jury indictment.
The judge explained the distinction between an indictment and an information to the defendant.

Etymology

From Latin informatio (outline, concept, explanation); legal sense attested from the 15th century.
