to jettison

1. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛtɪsən / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛtɪsən / (BrE)
to throw or drop something from an aircraft or ship, especially to lighten the load in an emergency
throw or drop from an aircraft/ship

Examples

The crew had to jettison fuel to reduce the aircraft's weight during the emergency landing.
In rough seas, sailors sometimes jettison cargo to keep the ship afloat.
The pilot decided to jettison the equipment before reaching the storm zone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discard - to get rid of something as no longer useful or desirable

Etymology

From Middle English via Old French, from Latin 'jectāre' (to throw), frequentative of 'jacere' (to throw).

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒɛt.ə.sən / (AmE), / ˈdʒɛt.ɪ.sən / (BrE)
to throw or drop something from a ship, aircraft, or vehicle, especially to lighten the load or discard something unwanted
throw or discard something to lighten a load

Examples

The crew had to jettison some cargo to keep the boat afloat.
During the emergency, the pilot jettisoned excess fuel before landing.
It's often wise to jettison outdated ideas when starting a new project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discard - to get rid of something that is no longer useful or wanted
  • Antonym: to retain - to keep possession of something

Etymology

From Middle English via Old French *getaison* from Latin *jectātiō* (a throwing), from *jactāre* (to throw).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
