Scots

1. NOUN

/ skɑts / (AmE), / skɒts / (BrE)
the variety of English spoken in Scotland, distinct from Standard English and Scottish Gaelic
the English dialect spoken in Scotland

Examples

Many classic poems were written in Scots rather than in Standard English.
He grew up speaking Scots at home and English at school.
The linguist specialized in the study of Scots and its history.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Scottish Gaelic - the Celtic language of the Scottish Highlanders

Etymology

From Old English ‘Scottas’ (the Scots), referring to the people of Scotland.

2. ADJ

/ skɑts / (AmE), / skɒts / (BrE)
of or relating to Scotland, its people, culture, or either its English dialect or Gaelic language
relating to Scotland or its people/language

Examples

The Scots language has a rich literary tradition dating back centuries.
Many Scots customs are celebrated around the world, especially on Burns Night.
She is proud of her Scots heritage and regularly visits her grandparents in Edinburgh.

Synset Examples

Scots Gaelic
the Scots community in New York
‘Scottish’ tends to be the more formal term as in ‘The Scottish Symphony’ or ‘Scottish authors’ or ‘Scottish mountains’
‘Scotch’ is in disfavor with Scottish people and is used primarily outside Scotland except in such frozen phrases as ‘Scotch broth’ or ‘Scotch whiskey’ or ‘Scotch plaid’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Scottish - of or relating to Scotland or its people; more formal adjective
  • Synonym: Scotch - old-fashioned or disfavored adjective for things of Scotland, except in certain fixed phrases

Etymology

From Middle English 'Scot(s)', from Old English 'Scottas', ultimately from Late Latin 'Scotti' (Gaelic-speaking people of Ireland/Scotland).
