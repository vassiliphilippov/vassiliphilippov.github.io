demoralisation

1. NOUN

/ dɪˌmɔrələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌmɒrələˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
A state in which people or a group lose confidence and hope, often resulting in disorder and confusion.
loss of morale and disorder

Examples

The team's repeated losses led to complete demoralisation within the locker room.
Widespread layoffs created a sense of demoralisation across the company.
The sudden policy changes caused confusion and demoralisation among the staff.

Synset Examples

his inconsistency resulted in the demoralization of his staff

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demoralization - loss of confidence or morale; same meaning
  • Antonym: order - an organised, controlled state; opposite of disorder

Etymology

From French 'démoralisation', from 'démoraliser' (to demoralize), ultimately from Latin 'moralis' (moral).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˌmɔrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌmɒrəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
The act of undermining or destroying the moral justification for a doctrine, belief, or policy.
destroying moral justification

Examples

The investigative report contributed to the demoralisation of the party's core beliefs.
Widespread demoralisation of the doctrine occurred after the ethics scandal.
The movement's main strategy was the demoralisation of old political dogmas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demoralization - the act of undermining moral standards (US spelling)

Etymology

From French 'démoralisation', from 'démoraliser' (to demoralize) + '-ation'.

3. NOUN

/ dɪˌmɔrələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / dɪˌmɒrələˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
A state of depression or discouragement resulting from the loss or undermining of morale.
loss of morale; discouragement
british

Examples

The constant criticism led to complete demoralisation within the team.
Widespread layoffs caused significant demoralisation among the staff.
After weeks of setbacks, a sense of demoralisation spread through the community.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demoralization - loss of confidence or morale (American spelling)

Etymology

From French 'démoralisation', from 'démoraliser' (to demoralize) + '-ation'.
