sourdough

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊrˌdoʊ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊədəʊ / (BrE)
a seasoned settler or prospector, especially in the western United States, northwest Canada, or Alaska, often one who participated in gold rushes
seasoned settler or prospector (western North America)

Examples

The old sourdough shared tales of the Yukon Gold Rush with the newcomers.
Many seasoned sourdoughs settled in Alaska after years of prospecting.
As a true sourdough, he knew every trail and shortcut through the mountains.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cheechako - a newcomer; an inexperienced person in the north

Etymology

From the practice of gold rush settlers carrying sourdough starter for breadmaking; by extension, applied to the settlers themselves.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsaʊrˌdoʊ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊəˌdəʊ / (BrE)
A mixture of flour and water in which wild yeasts and bacteria are allowed to ferment, used as a natural leaven for making bread.
fermented bread starter

Examples

She made her own bread using a homemade sourdough starter.
The bakery is famous for its crusty sourdough loaves.
After days of careful fermentation, the sourdough was ready to bake.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leaven - a substance used to make dough rise, especially yeast

Etymology

From 'sour' + 'dough', referring to the characteristic taste and the dough used as a starter.
