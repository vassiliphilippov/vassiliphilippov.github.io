affect

1. VERB

/ əˈfɛkt / (AmE), / əˈfɛkt / (BrE)
to produce a change in someone or something; to influence
to influence or change

Examples

How will the new law affect small businesses?
The weather can greatly affect your mood.
Her decision will affect everyone in the department.

Synset Examples

Will the new rules affect me?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: influence - to have an effect on the character or behavior of someone or something
  • Synonym: impact - to have a strong effect on something or someone
  • Synonym: effect - to bring about; to cause to happen

Etymology

From Latin 'afficere' meaning 'to act upon, influence'.

2. VERB

/ əˈfɛkt / (AmE), / əˈfɛkt / (BrE)
to act upon something so as to produce a change or influence it
to influence or have an effect on

Examples

The new law will affect millions of people across the country.
High humidity can affect the way bread dough rises during baking.
Certain chemicals in the water affect the growth of aquatic plants.

Synset Examples

the medicine affects my heart rate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: influence - to have an effect on the character, development, or behavior of someone or something
  • Synonym: impact - to have a strong effect on someone or something
  • Synonym: effect - to bring about a change

Etymology

From Latin affectare (to strive after), frequentative of afficere (to do to, influence).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00019441-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ əˈfɛkt / (AmE), / əˈfɛkt / (BrE)
to pretend to have or feel something, especially to deceive others
pretend to feel (to deceive)

Examples

She tried to affect indifference when she saw her ex at the party.
Despite his nervousness, he managed to affect a calm demeanor during the interview.
He would often affect an interest in sports just to fit in with his friends.

Synset Examples

He feigned that he was ill
He shammed a headache

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to feign - to pretend to be affected by a feeling or injury
  • Synonym: pretend - to behave as if something is true when it is not

Etymology

From Latin affectāre (to strive after, pretend to), frequentative of afficere (to do to, act upon).

4. NOUN

/ əˈfɛkt / (AmE), / əˈfɛkt / (BrE)
the conscious, subjective experience or display of feeling or emotion, especially as observed objectively in psychology or psychiatry
subjective experience of feeling or emotion
medical

Examples

The patient displayed a flat affect during the interview, showing little emotional expression.
Clinicians observe changes in a person's affect to help diagnose certain mental health conditions.
Her bright affect contrasted sharply with the somber mood of the room.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emotion - a strong feeling such as love, anger, joy, hate, or fear
  • Degree Lower: mood - a temporary state of mind or feeling

Etymology

From Latin affectus (a disposition, feeling, passion).

6. VERB

/ əˈfɛkt / (AmE), / əˈfɛkt / (BrE)
to produce a change in or influence someone or something
to influence or change

Examples

Changes in temperature can affect the growth of crops significantly.
Her words deeply affected everyone at the meeting.
The new law will affect millions of people across the country.

Synset Examples

The virus spreads quickly; if it kills one, it affects all.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: influence - to have an effect on the character, development, or behavior of someone or something
  • Synonym: impact - to have a strong effect on someone or something
  • Antonym: effect - to bring about a result (as a verb), often confused with 'affect'

Etymology

From Latin 'afficere' (to influence, to act upon), from 'ad-' (to) + 'facere' (to do, to make).
