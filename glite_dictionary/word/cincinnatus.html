Cincinnatus

1. PROPN

/ ˌsɪnsɪˈnætəs / (AmE), / ˌsɪnsɪˈnætəs / (BrE)
(c. 519–430 BC) A Roman statesman celebrated for his integrity and simplicity, who twice assumed the dictatorship of Rome and then returned to his farm.
Roman statesman known for his virtue

Examples

The story of Cincinnatus inspired later generations to value humility and public service.
Many leaders have been compared to Cincinnatus for their willingness to relinquish power.
George Washington was sometimes called the American Cincinnatus because he also returned to private life after leading his country.

Etymology

From Latin Cincinnātus, the cognomen of Lucius Quinctius Cincinnatus.
