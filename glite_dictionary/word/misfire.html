misfire

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɪsˌfaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɪsˌfaɪə / (BrE)
an instance of an explosion—especially in a gun or engine—that fails to occur as intended
failure of an explosion as expected

Examples

The old rifle had a misfire during the demonstration, alarming the onlookers.
After the misfire, the technician carefully checked the rocket's ignition system.
Fortunately, the grenade was a misfire and did not explode.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dud - an explosive device that fails to detonate

Etymology

From mis- (wrongly) + fire (set off a gun or explosive), originally in firearms terminology.

2. VERB

/ ˌmɪsˈfaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˌmɪsˈfaɪə / (BrE)
(of a gun or explosive) to fail to discharge or detonate as expected.
(of a gun) to fail to fire

Examples

The old pistol misfired during the demonstration, startling the crowd.
If your engine misfires frequently, you should take your car to a mechanic.
She pulled the trigger, but the rifle misfired and nothing happened.

Synset Examples

The guns misfired

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fire - to discharge a firearm or explosive

Etymology

From mis- (wrongly) + fire (to discharge a firearm), first used in the 17th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01137079-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɪsˌfaɪɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɪsˌfaɪə / (BrE)
an instance of failing to achieve the intended result, especially a failure to hit a target or succeed in an attempt
failed attempt to hit or succeed

Examples

The launch of the new product turned out to be a misfire, with disappointing sales.
His attempt at humor was a complete misfire and nobody laughed.
Despite careful planning, their strategy was ultimately a misfire.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: failure - lack of success in doing or achieving something
  • Antonym: success - the achievement of a goal or target

Etymology

From mis- (wrongly) + fire (shoot a gun); originally referring to firearms failing to discharge.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07334374-n
Synset POS: n
