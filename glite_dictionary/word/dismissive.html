dismissive

1. ADJ

/ dɪsˈmɪsɪv / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪsɪv / (BrE)
showing that you do not believe someone or something is important or worth considering; showing indifference or disregard.
showing indifference or disregard

Examples

Her dismissive attitude hurt the feelings of her colleagues.
He gave a dismissive wave of his hand and walked away.
The report was met with a dismissive response from the board.

Synset Examples

a dismissive shrug
the firm is dismissive of the competitor's product
‘chronic fatigue syndrome’ was known by the dismissive term ‘housewife syndrome’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contemptuous - showing scorn or a strong lack of respect
  • Antonym: respectful - showing respect or consideration; opposite in attitude

Etymology

From dismiss + -ive, first recorded in English in the mid-19th century.

2. ADJ

/ dɪsˈmɪsɪv / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪsɪv / (BrE)
showing that you do not think something or someone is worth considering; feeling or showing a lack of interest or respect and a willingness to stop associating with.
showing disregard or rejection

Examples

He gave a dismissive wave of his hand and walked away from the meeting.
Her dismissive attitude made it clear she did not value our feedback.
The manager's response to concerns was surprisingly dismissive, making everyone feel unappreciated.

Synset Examples

they took dismissive action after the third violation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contemptuous - showing contempt or disdain; scornful
  • Antonym: respectful - showing respect or consideration; polite

Etymology

From dismiss + -ive, formed after the verb 'dismiss' (to send away).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00077188-s
Synset POS: s
