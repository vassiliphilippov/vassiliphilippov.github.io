retentivity

1. NOUN

/ rɪˌtɛnˈtɪvəti / (AmE), / rɪˌtɛnˈtɪvɪti / (BrE)
the ability of a material or substance to retain or hold liquid within it
ability to retain liquid

Examples

The moisture retentivity of clay is greater than that of sand.
Certain fabrics are designed to have high retentivity for water and sweat.
Gardeners consider soil retentivity important for growing healthy plants.

Synset Examples

moisture retentivity of soil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorbency - the ability to soak up or absorb liquid
  • Antonym: permeability - the quality of allowing liquids to pass through

Etymology

From Latin retentivus (tending to retain), from retinere (to hold back).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˌtɛnˈtɪv.ə.ti / (AmE), / rɪˌtɛnˈtɪv.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the ability to retain or keep what has been acquired, such as possessions, information, or skills
ability to retain or keep things

Examples

His remarkable retentivity enabled him to keep even the smallest souvenirs from his childhood.
Collectors are often noted for their retentivity, rarely parting with any item they acquire.
The museum's retentivity ensured that many historical artefacts were preserved for future generations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retention - the act or power of retaining
  • Antonym: forgetfulness - the inability or failure to remember or retain

Etymology

From Latin retentivus (tending to retain), from retinere (to hold back, retain).
