to outsmart

1. VERB

/ aʊtˈsmɑrt / (AmE), / aʊtˈsmɑːt / (BrE)
to defeat or gain an advantage over someone by being more clever or intelligent
defeat by being more clever

Examples

She managed to outsmart her opponents in the debate with a clever argument.
No matter how tricky the puzzle was, he always found a way to outsmart it.
The thief tried to escape, but the police outsmarted him by anticipating his moves.

Synset Examples

I beat the traffic
She outfoxed her competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outwit - to defeat by being cleverer
  • Synonym: to outfox - to deceive or defeat by cunning

Etymology

Formed from 'out-' (meaning 'surpassing') and 'smart' (clever, intelligent).

2. VERB

/ aʊtˈsmɑrt / (AmE), / aʊtˈsmɑːt / (BrE)
to defeat or gain an advantage over someone by being more clever or skillful
defeat by being more clever

Examples

She managed to outsmart her competitors in the final round of the contest.
No one could outsmart the clever detective during the investigation.
The fox tried to outsmart the hunters by doubling back on its tracks.

Synset Examples

The English troops outmaneuvered the Germans
My new supervisor knows how to outmaneuver the boss in most situations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outwit - to gain an advantage over someone by being more clever

Etymology

From 'out-' (surpassing) + 'smart' (clever), first attested late 19th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01112658-v
Synset POS: v
