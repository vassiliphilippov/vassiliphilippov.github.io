agglutinative

1. ADJ

/ əˈɡluː.t̬ənˌeɪ.t̬ɪv / (AmE), / əˌɡluː.tɪˈneɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
characterized by being united or joined together as if by glue; tending to stick together.
sticking or joined together, as if by glue

Examples

The agglutinative quality of the paste made it perfect for joining the broken pottery pieces.
Some bacteria form agglutinative clusters when exposed to certain chemicals.
The scientists noted an agglutinative mass under the microscope, indicating strong cellular adhesion.

Etymology

From Latin 'agglutinare' (to glue to), from 'ad-' (to) + 'gluten' (glue).

2. ADJ

/ əˌɡluːtəˈneɪtɪv / (AmE), / əˌɡluːtɪˈneɪtɪv / (BrE)
(of a language or linguistic structure) forming words by combining separate elements, each of which conveys a distinct meaning.
characterized by word formation with separable elements

Examples

Turkish is often cited as an agglutinative language because it forms words by stringing together distinct morphemes.
In an agglutinative language, each affix adds a specific meaning or function to the word.
These linguists studied how agglutinative structures affect the complexity of grammar.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: isolating - (of a language) characterized by the use of single-morpheme words without inflection
  • Antonym: fusional - (of a language) expressing grammatical relationships by changing the internal structure of words

Etymology

From Latin 'agglutinare' (to glue to), from 'ad-' (to) + 'gluten' (glue), reflecting the idea of sticking elements together.
