mil

1. NOUN

/ mil / (AmE), / mɪl / (BrE)
a Swedish unit of length equal to 10 kilometres (about 6.2 miles)
Swedish unit of length (10 km)

Examples

In Sweden, a mil is commonly used to describe distances between towns.
The marathon covered just over four mils according to Swedish road signs.
He drove 8 mils to reach his family's summer cottage.

Etymology

From Swedish 'mil', ultimately from Latin 'milia' (thousands).

2. NOUN

/ mɪl / (AmE), / mɪl / (BrE)
a former Cypriot monetary unit equal to one thousandth of a pound, used before the adoption of the euro
former Cypriot monetary unit

Examples

The old Cypriot one-pound note was subdivided into 1,000 mils.
Many coins from Cyprus before 1983 had their value expressed in mils.
Few people remember using the mil since Cyprus switched to cents and then to the euro.

3. NOUN

/ mɪl / (AmE), / mɪl / (BrE)
a metric unit of volume equal to one thousandth of a liter, also known as a milliliter
one thousandth of a liter; milliliter

Examples

The syringe can measure up to 5 mil of liquid for accurate dosing.
He added two mil of the reagent to the test tube.
The instructions said to mix 10 mil of water with the powder.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: milliliter - a metric unit of volume equal to one thousandth of a liter

Etymology

Shortened form of 'milliliter', from French 'milli-' (thousandth) and 'litre'.

4. NOUN

/ mɪl / (AmE), / mɪl / (BrE)
a unit of length equal to one thousandth of an inch, used especially to measure thickness of materials such as sheets and wire
unit, one thousandth of an inch

Examples

The plastic sheeting is available in 6 mil and 10 mil thicknesses.
Wire insulation thickness is often measured in mil rather than millimeters.
A 20 mil coating will offer greater protection compared to a 10 mil coating.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: millimeter - a metric unit of length equal to one thousandth of a meter

Etymology

Abbreviation of 'millimeter' and influenced by Latin 'mille' (thousand).
