gregarious

1. ADJ

/ ɡrɪˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ɡrɪˈɡɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
(of plants) growing in groups or clusters that are close together.
(plants) growing in closely grouped clusters

Examples

These gregarious reeds thrive along the banks of the river in dense stands.
The orchid is a gregarious species, often forming large groups in open meadows.
Unlike solitary cacti, some gregarious varieties flourish only when growing close together.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clustered - growing closely together in clusters (plants)

Etymology

From Latin 'gregarius', from 'grex' (herd, flock).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02261145-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ɡrəˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ɡrɪˈɡɛːrɪəs / (BrE)
(of animals) tending to form groups with others of the same species; living or moving in flocks or herds.
tending to form groups (of animals)

Examples

Flamingos are gregarious birds that gather in large colonies on salt flats.
Some primates are gregarious by nature, spending most of their lives in tight-knit groups.
Unlike tigers, wolves are highly gregarious and depend on pack cooperation to hunt.

Synset Examples

gregarious bird species

Related Terms

  • Synonym: social - seeking or enjoying the company of others; sociable
  • Antonym: solitary - living or spending a lot of time alone; not gregarious

Etymology

From Latin 'gregarius' (belonging to a flock or herd), from 'grex, gregis' (flock, herd).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02260390-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɡrəˈɡɛriəs / (AmE), / ɡrɪˈɡeəriəs / (BrE)
(of a person) fond of company; sociable.
sociable; fond of company

Examples

She was so gregarious that her parties were always lively and well-attended.
Unlike his gregarious friends, Mark preferred to read quietly at home.
Many salespeople are gregarious and thrive in social environments.

Synset Examples

he is a gregarious person who avoids solitude

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sociable - enjoying being with other people
  • Antonym: introverted - preferring to be alone or with a few close friends

Etymology

From Latin 'gregarius' (belonging to a flock), from 'grex' (flock).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s
