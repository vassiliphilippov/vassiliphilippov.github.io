dauphin

1. NOUN

/ doʊˈfæ̃, dɔˈfæ̃ / (AmE), / doʊˈfæ̃, dɔːˈfæ̃ / (BrE)
the eldest son of a king of France, who was the direct heir to the French throne before the French Revolution
eldest son and heir to the French throne

Examples

In 1789, the dauphin of France was a child destined never to wear the crown.
The young dauphin was tutored extensively in statecraft and languages.
The title of dauphin was abolished during the French Revolution.

Etymology

From Old French 'dauphin', literally 'dolphin'; the title was derived from the Lords of Dauphiné, whose arms bore a dolphin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɔː.fɪn / (AmE), / ˈdɔː.fæ̃ / (BrE)
the heir apparent in a family or organization; a person who is expected to inherit or take over leadership, often used figuratively
heir apparent; expected successor

Examples

After his father's retirement, John became the dauphin of the family business.
She was seen as the dauphin within her political party, destined to lead one day.
Rumors spread quickly that the youngest son had become the new dauphin of the wealthy dynasty.

Synset Examples

He was once considered the dauphin of the family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heir apparent - the person who is expected to inherit a title, position, or fortune

Etymology

From French 'dauphin', originally the title given to the heir apparent to the French throne.
