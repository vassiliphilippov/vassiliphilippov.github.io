interdict

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪntərˌdɪkt / (AmE), / ˈɪntədɪkt / (BrE)
A legal order issued by a court that prohibits a person or group from performing a specific act.
court order forbidding an action
legal

Examples

The company sought an interdict to prevent the publication of confidential documents.
A temporary interdict was granted, halting construction until the hearing.
Without an interdict, the protestors continued their demonstration outside the factory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: injunction - a court order requiring a party to do or refrain from doing something

Etymology

From Latin interdictum (prohibition, interdict), neuter of interdictus, past participle of interdicere (to forbid).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to formally prohibit or forbid something, especially by an authoritative order
formally forbid or prohibit

Examples

The court decided to interdict the sale of the disputed property until the case was resolved.
Religious leaders attempted to interdict certain books they considered harmful to society.
The school board may interdict students from using their phones during class hours.

Synset Examples

I forbid you to call me late at night
Mother vetoed the trip to the chocolate store
Dad nixed our plans

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to forbid - to order someone not to do something
  • Synonym: to prohibit - to formally forbid something by law or authority

Etymology

From Latin *interdictus*, past participle of *interdicere* (to forbid, prohibit).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪntərˌdɪkt / (AmE), / ˈɪntədɪkt / (BrE)
An ecclesiastical censure by the Roman Catholic Church that withdraws certain sacraments and Christian burial from a person or from all persons in a specific area.
church censure withdrawing sacraments

Examples

The pope placed the entire kingdom under an interdict after the conflict with the king.
During the Middle Ages, kings would sometimes use political pressure to have the interdict lifted.
The villagers feared the consequences of the interdict imposed on their parish.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excommunication - official exclusion from participation in the sacraments and services of the Christian Church

Etymology

From Latin *interdictum* (prohibition), from *interdicere* (to forbid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06723269-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
(of military forces) to destroy, block, or impede (an enemy's movements or communications) by use of firepower or bombardment.
to impede or destroy by firepower (military)

Examples

The air force was ordered to interdict enemy supply routes throughout the valley.
They successfully interdicted hostile convoys, cutting off reinforcements to the front lines.
Allied bombers interdicted the railway bridges, hindering enemy troop movements.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to prevent movement or progress
  • Antonym: permit - to allow to happen

Etymology

From Latin 'interdictus', past participle of 'interdicere' (to forbid), later extended to the military sense.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
