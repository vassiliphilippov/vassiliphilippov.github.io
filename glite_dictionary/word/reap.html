to reap

1. VERB

/ riːp / (AmE), / riːp / (BrE)
to obtain or receive something, especially as a result of one's own actions or efforts
obtain or receive as a result

Examples

After years of hard work, she finally reaped the rewards of her dedication.
You only reap what you sow, so make wise choices.
The company hopes to reap substantial profits from the new product line.

Synset Examples

He drew great benefits from his membership in the association

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to derive - to get something from a specified source
  • Synonym: to earn - to receive something in return for one's work or effort
  • Antonym: to lose - to cease to have or no longer get something

Etymology

From Old English *rīpan* (to reap, gather a harvest).

2. VERB

/ riːp / (AmE), / riːp / (BrE)
to cut and gather a crop, especially grain, from a field; to obtain a reward or result as a consequence of one's actions
gather a crop or obtain a result

Examples

Farmers reap wheat in late summer, when the grain is fully ripe.
If you sow kindness, you will eventually reap rewards in return.
After months of hard work, the company began to reap the benefits of its new strategy.

Synset Examples

harvest the grapes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harvest - to gather crops from the fields
  • Antonym: sow - to plant seeds in the ground

Etymology

Old English *rīpan* (to reap, gather a harvest).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01322669-v
Synset POS: v
