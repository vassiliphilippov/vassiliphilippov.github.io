scupper

1. NOUN

/ ˈskʌpər / (AmE), / ˈskʌpə / (BrE)
A drain in the side of a ship's deck that allows water to flow overboard.
drain on a ship's deck

Examples

Heavy rain poured across the deck, but the scupper quickly drained the water away.
The sailors checked to make sure each scupper was clear of debris before the storm hit.
Barnacles can sometimes clog a scupper, leading to dangerous pools of water on deck.

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *scopar* or Old French *escopir* (to spit out).

2. VERB

/ ˈskʌpər / (AmE), / ˈskʌpə / (BrE)
to prevent something from happening or succeeding; to put in a dangerous, disadvantageous, or difficult position
to ruin or put at a disadvantage

Examples

The project was scuppered by a sudden lack of funding.
A late goal scuppered their chances of winning the championship.
Her careless comment nearly scuppered the negotiations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thwart - to prevent someone from accomplishing something
  • Synonym: sabotage - to deliberately destroy or damage something to prevent it from succeeding
  • Antonym: assist - to help or support someone

Etymology

Originally nautical, from 'scupper' (a drainage opening in a ship), later used figuratively in British English.
