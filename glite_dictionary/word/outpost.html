outpost

1. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌpoʊst / (AmE), / ˈaʊtpəʊst / (BrE)
A small settlement or camp established in a remote or frontier area, usually far from the main centers of population or civilization.
remote settlement or camp on the frontier

Examples

The soldiers built an outpost at the edge of the desert to monitor activity in the region.
The research team established an outpost in Antarctica to study the climate.
Life at a remote outpost can be both challenging and rewarding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settlement - a place where people establish a community

Etymology

Formed from 'out-' (outside) + 'post' (position or station).

2. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌpoʊst / (AmE), / ˈaʊtpəʊst / (BrE)
a small station or settlement situated in a remote or sparsely populated area, often serving as a base for exploration, military, or trading activities
station in a remote location

Examples

The research team set up an outpost in Antarctica to study the melting ice.
During the gold rush, many miners established an outpost along the riverbanks.
The remote outpost was supplied by helicopter once a month.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: base - a main place where activities are planned or organized
  • Synonym: settlement - a small community in a new or sparsely populated area

Etymology

From out + post, originally military; post meaning 'station'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌpoʊst / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.pəʊst / (BrE)
a military post stationed away from the main body of troops, often used to guard against surprise attacks or provide early warning
remote military post

Examples

The soldiers were sent to man the remote outpost near the border.
After months at the outpost, the unit finally received new supplies.
The enemy tried to capture the outpost in a surprise attack before dawn.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fort - a fortified building or military station
  • Synonym: garrison - a body of troops stationed in a fort or town

Etymology

From out + post, modelled after military terminology; post meaning 'station' or 'position'.
