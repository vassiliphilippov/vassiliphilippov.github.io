synthetical

1. ADJ

/ sɪnˈθɛtɪkəl / (AmE), / sɪnˈθɛtɪkəl / (BrE)
(of a proposition) having a truth value that can be determined only by observation or empirical evidence, rather than by logic or definition.
determined by observation (philosophy)

Examples

In philosophy, a synthetical judgement is one that requires external evidence to determine its truth.
The statement 'water boils at 100°C' is considered synthetical, as it relies on empirical observation.
Kant distinguished between analytical and synthetical propositions in his works on epistemology.

Synset Examples

‘all men are arrogant’ is a synthetic proposition

Related Terms

  • Antonym: analytic - of a proposition whose truth depends only on logic or definition

Etymology

From Greek *synthetikos* (skilled in putting together), via Late Latin *syntheticus*.

2. ADJ

/ sɪnˈθɛtɪkəl / (AmE), / sɪnˈθɛtɪkəl / (BrE)
involving or relating to synthesis; characterized by combining separate parts or elements to form a whole, especially as opposed to analysis
relating to synthesis; combining elements

Examples

The synthetical approach integrates ideas from various disciplines to solve complex problems.
His research is known for its synthetical treatment of diverse theories in the field.
She prefers an synthetical method that brings together multiple perspectives rather than analyzing details separately.

Synset Examples

limnology is essentially a synthetic science composed of elements … that extend well beyond the limits of biology

Related Terms

  • Antonym: analytic - relating to analysis; breaking something into parts to examine it
  • Synonym: synthetic - made by synthesis; also relating to synthesis as a process

Etymology

From synthesis + -ical, modelled after synthetic.
