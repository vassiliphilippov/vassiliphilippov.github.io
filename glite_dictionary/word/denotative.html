denotative

1. ADJ

/ ˌdiːˈnoʊtətɪv / (AmE), / ˌdiːˈnəʊtətɪv / (BrE)
having the power of explicitly naming or designating; relating to the primary or literal meaning of a word, not its connotations
relating to explicit or literal meaning

Examples

The denotative meaning of the word 'snake' is simply a type of reptile.
Her explanation was strictly denotative, avoiding any emotional implications.
Writers often balance the denotative and connotative meanings of words for deeper effect.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: connotative - relating to suggested or implied meanings

Etymology

From Medieval Latin denotativus, from Latin denotare (to denote).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdiː.noʊˌteɪ.tɪv / (AmE), / ˌdiː.nəˈteɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
having the primary, literal, or explicit meaning of a word or expression, as opposed to its implied or associated meanings.
relating to literal or primary meaning

Examples

The denotative meaning of 'snake' is simply a type of reptile.
Writers often play with both the denotative and connotative meanings of words to create deeper effects.
His explanation focused on the denotative definition of the term, avoiding any emotional interpretation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: connotative - relating to implied or associated meanings

Etymology

From Medieval Latin denotativus, from Latin denotare (to specify, indicate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01421862-s
Synset POS: s
