fragile

1. ADJ

/ ˈfrædʒəl / (AmE), / ˈfrædʒaɪl / (BrE)
easily broken or damaged; not strong or sturdy
easily broken or damaged

Examples

Please handle the fragile vase with great care.
Because the bones are fragile, extra precautions must be taken during surgery.
The parcel was labeled 'fragile' to ensure it would not be damaged in transit.

Synset Examples

a kite too delicate to fly safely
fragile porcelain plates
fragile old bones
a frail craft

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delicate - easily damaged or requiring careful handling
  • Antonym: sturdy - strong and not easily broken or damaged

Etymology

From Latin fragilis, from frangere (to break).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfrædʒ.əl / (AmE), / ˈfrædʒ.aɪl / (BrE)
delicate and easily damaged, both physically or emotionally; vulnerable to harm or injury.
delicate; easily damaged or harmed

Examples

After her illness, she seemed fragile and in need of rest.
The negotiations are in a fragile state and could collapse at any moment.
Their fragile peace was broken by a single misunderstanding.

Synset Examples

she has the fragile beauty of youth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delicate - easily damaged or requiring careful handling
  • Antonym: robust - strong and not easily damaged or affected

Etymology

From Latin fragilis (easily broken), from frangere (to break).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00710132-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈfrædʒ.əl / (AmE), / ˈfrædʒ.aɪl / (BrE)
Lacking substance, strength, or significance; easily destroyed or dismissed.
lacking substance or significance

Examples

His fragile claim to leadership quickly fell apart when challenged.
The report was based on fragile evidence and did not convince the committee.
Their alliance was always fragile, held together by only a thin thread of mutual interest.

Synset Examples

slight evidence
a tenuous argument
a thin plot
a fragile claim to fame

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tenuous - lacking a sound basis; weak or insubstantial
  • Antonym: strong - having great substance or significance

Etymology

From Latin fragilem, from frangere 'to break'.
