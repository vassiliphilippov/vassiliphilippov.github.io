to neutralize

1. VERB

/ ˈnuːtrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnjuːtrəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make a substance chemically neutral by adding an acid to a base or vice versa
make chemically neutral

Examples

The scientist carefully neutralized the acid with a base during the experiment.
Adding enough sodium hydroxide will neutralize the acidic solution.
In the lab, they neutralized the sample before safe disposal.

Synset Examples

She neutralized the solution

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to acidify - to make or become acidic
  • Antonym: to alkalize - to make or become alkaline

Etymology

From French 'neutraliser', from 'neutre' (neutral).

WordNet ID: oewn-00470835-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈnuːtʃrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnjuːtrəˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to make (a country or organization) politically neutral, so that it does not take sides in a conflict or dispute
make politically neutral

Examples

The government chose to neutralize the region to prevent further conflict between neighboring states.
Following years of disputes, the island was neutralized by international agreement.
The treaty aimed to neutralize the small republic, ensuring it remained uninvolved in military alliances.

Synset Examples

The treaty neutralized the small republic

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to politicize - to make something political or give it a political character

Etymology

From French 'neutraliser', from Latin 'neutralis' (of neither side).

3. VERB

/ ˈnuːtrəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈnjuːtrəlaɪz / (BrE)
to counteract or reduce the effect of something by taking opposite or mitigating action
counteract or offset the effect of

Examples

The government introduced new measures to neutralize the impact of the economic crisis.
Adding a base will neutralize the acidity of the solution.
She tried to neutralize his arguments by presenting strong evidence.

to make (a military target, force, or weapon) incapable of action or effective response, especially by military means
