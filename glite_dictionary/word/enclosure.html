enclosure

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkloʊʒɚ / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊʒə / (BrE)
a space that is surrounded by natural boundaries, such as hills, trees, or rocks
naturally enclosed space

Examples

The horses wandered freely within the sheltered enclosure formed by the surrounding cliffs.
A natural enclosure of trees provided a safe retreat for the deer.
They set up camp in a quiet enclosure at the base of the mountains.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cavity - a hollow space in something, especially in a body

Etymology

From Old French enclos, from Latin includere (to shut in, enclose).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkloʊʒər / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊʒə / (BrE)
an area that is surrounded by a fence, wall, or other barrier, often for a particular purpose
an area that is fenced or contained

Examples

The zoo built a new enclosure for the polar bears to give them more space.
Children are not allowed inside the construction enclosure without supervision.
The fragile plants were kept safe in a glass enclosure during the cold months.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pen - a small enclosure for animals
  • Synonym: cage - an enclosure made of bars or wire for confining animals
  • Antonym: open area - an area that is not enclosed or surrounded by barriers

Etymology

From Middle English 'enclos', from Old French 'enclos', past participle of 'enclore' (to enclose), from Latin 'includere' (to shut in).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03290823-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkloʊʒər / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊʒə / (BrE)
the act of surrounding or closing off something within something else
the act of enclosing something

Examples

The enclosure of the animals in the pen kept them safe from predators.
Strict enclosure of confidential documents is required by company policy.
He completed the enclosure of the garden with a high wooden fence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confinement - the act of keeping something within limits or boundaries
  • Antonym: release - the act of setting something free or letting it go

Etymology

From Middle English 'enclosure', from Old French 'enclos', past participle of 'enclore' (to enclose), from Latin 'includere' (to shut in, enclose).

4. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkloʊʒɚ / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊʒə / (BrE)
a document or item placed inside an envelope along with a covering letter, usually as additional information or support
item included with a letter or package

Examples

Please find the enclosure with the relevant documents attached to this letter.
The hiring manager reviewed each enclosure submitted by the candidates.
Remember to mention any enclosure at the bottom of your cover letter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attachment - a file or document sent with an email or letter

Etymology

From Old French *enclos*, from Latin *inclusus*, past participle of *includere* (to shut in).
