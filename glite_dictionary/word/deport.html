to deport

1. VERB

/ dɪˈpɔrt / (AmE), / dɪˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to officially force someone to leave a country, especially because they have broken the law or do not have legal right to stay
to expel someone from a country

Examples

The government decided to deport the illegal immigrant after his visa expired.
She was deported to her home country due to immigration violations.
The authorities threatened to deport anyone found in the country without proper documentation.

Synset Examples

They extradited the fugitive to his native country so he could be tried there

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to extradite - to officially send someone back to their own country for trial or punishment

Etymology

From Latin deportare, from de- ‘away’ + portare ‘carry’.

2. VERB

/ dɪˈpɔrt / (AmE), / dɪˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to conduct or behave oneself in a particular manner, especially in accordance with rules or expectations
conduct or behave oneself

Examples

She always deports herself with remarkable dignity at public events.
One should deport oneself courteously at all times, especially when meeting new people.
The students were expected to deport themselves with maturity during the ceremony.

Synset Examples

You should act like an adult
Don't behave like a fool
What makes her do this way?
The dog acts ferocious, but he is really afraid of people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to behave - to act or conduct oneself in a specified way
  • Synonym: to comport - to behave in a particular manner

Etymology

From Latin deportare (to carry away, to behave), via French déporter.

3. VERB

/ dɪˈpɔrt / (AmE), / dɪˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to officially force someone to leave a country, usually for legal or political reasons
officially remove from a country

Examples

The government decided to deport the foreign journalist after his visa expired.
Several people were deported for entering the country illegally.
Immigration officials are currently deporting those found without valid documents.

Synset Examples

The poet was exiled because he signed a letter protesting the government's actions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to expel - to force someone to leave a place or organization
  • Synonym: exile - to force someone to live in another country
  • Antonym: to admit - to allow someone to enter a country or place

Etymology

From Latin 'deportare' (to carry away), from 'de-' (away) + 'portare' (to carry).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02504961-v
Synset POS: v
