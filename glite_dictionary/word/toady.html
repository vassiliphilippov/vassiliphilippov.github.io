toady

1. VERB

/ ˈtoʊdi / (AmE), / ˈtəʊdi / (BrE)
to behave in an excessively flattering or servile way in order to gain favor with someone in authority
act in a servile, flattering way for favor

Examples

She refused to toady to those in power, choosing honesty instead.
He spent the entire meeting toadying to the new manager.
The intern toadied shamelessly, hoping to get a promotion.

Synset Examples

He is always kowtowing to his boss

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to flatter - to praise someone excessively, often insincerely
  • Synonym: kowtow - to act in an excessively subservient manner
  • Antonym: snub - to ignore or behave coldly toward someone

Etymology

Originally from 'toad-eater', referring to a charlatan's assistant who ate toads; the term evolved into 'toady'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtoʊdi / (AmE), / ˈtəʊdi / (BrE)
A person who behaves obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain favor or advantage.
sycophantic person; fawner

Examples

He acted like a toady around his boss, agreeing with everything she said.
The king surrounded himself with toadies who told him only what he wanted to hear.
No one respected him because he was seen as a mere toady to those in power.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sycophant - a person who acts obsequiously toward someone important
  • Synonym: flatterer - a person who excessively praises someone
  • Antonym: critic - someone who expresses an unfavorable opinion

Etymology

Probably from 'toad-eater,' a fawning flatterer in 17th-century English fairs.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10704310-n
Synset POS: n
