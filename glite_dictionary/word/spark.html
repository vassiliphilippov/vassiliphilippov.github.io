spark

1. VERB

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
to emit or produce sparks, especially of electricity or fire
emit or produce sparks

Examples

Do not touch the cable, as it may still spark unexpectedly.
The faulty outlet began to spark during the rainstorm.
Old machinery can sometimes spark when it is powered on.

Synset Examples

A high tension wire, brought down by a storm, can continue to spark

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flash - to produce a sudden burst of light
  • Synonym: to ignite - to catch fire or cause to catch fire

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)

4. NOUN

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
a small fragment of a burning substance thrown off by burning material or created by friction
small burning fragment

Examples

The campfire sent a spark flying into the night air.
A single spark from the machinery started the blaze.
She watched a spark land on the rug and quickly stamped it out.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ember - a small piece of burning or glowing coal or wood in a dying fire

Etymology

From Old English *spearca* (spark), related to Middle Low German *sperke* (spark).

Examples

Related Terms

7. NOUN

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
a flash of light caused by electricity passing through a gas or air, often producing a sudden burst of energy or sound
flash of light from electrical discharge

Examples

The broken wire produced a visible spark when it came into contact with metal.
A strong spark jumped between the plug's two terminals as the switch was flipped.
The scientist observed the formation of a spark in the laboratory apparatus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: arc - a continuous luminous electrical discharge between two electrodes

Etymology

From Middle English 'sparke', from Old English 'spearca' (spark, flame).
