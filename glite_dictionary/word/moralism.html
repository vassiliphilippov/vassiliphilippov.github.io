moralism

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɔr.əˌlɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈmɒr.əˌlɪz.əm / (BrE)
the practice of making judgments about the morality of others, often with an air of superiority or criticism
making judgments about others' morality

Examples

He grew tired of her constant moralism and judgmental remarks.
Critics accused the author of slipping into moralism in his later works.
Many people resent unsolicited moralism from strangers about their lifestyle choices.

Synset Examples

he could not stand her hectoring moralism

Related Terms

  • Synonym: self-righteousness - an unfounded confidence in one's own moral superiority

Etymology

From French 'moralisme', from Latin 'moralis' (moral).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɔrəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈmɒrəlɪzəm / (BrE)
a moral maxim; a statement expressing a general principle about right and wrong behavior
a statement of moral principle

Examples

She quoted an old moralism to emphasize the value of honesty.
The teacher's lectures were filled with moralisms about proper behavior.
He dismissed their advice as mere moralism, preferring practical solutions instead.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: maxim - a short statement expressing a general truth or rule of conduct

Etymology

From French moralisme and Latin moralis (moral), with '-ism' denoting a practice or doctrine.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07168349-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɔr.əˌlɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈmɒr.əˌlɪz.əm / (BrE)
the tendency to express or impose moral judgments, especially in literature, art, or social discussion
tendency to make moral judgments

Examples

The novel explores the moralism of its main character through her judgments of others.
Critics accused the speech of excessive moralism, rather than offering practical solutions.
He disliked the preachy moralism sometimes found in children's stories.

Synset Examples

The film critiques the moralism of its characters.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moralizing - expressing judgments about right and wrong in a disapproving way

Etymology

From French moralisme, from Latin moralis (moral).
