tinsel

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɪn.səl / (AmE), / ˈtɪn.səl / (BrE)
Showy, superficial decoration, especially something that is attractive but has little or no real value.
showy, superficial decoration

Examples

Politicians are often surrounded by unnecessary tinsel and fanfare to grab attention.
Beneath the tinsel of the celebration, few noticed the real issues at hand.
She dismissed his compliments as mere tinsel, preferring genuine praise.

Synset Examples

all the tinsel of self-promotion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bauble - a small, showy trinket or decoration
  • Synonym: glitter - a shiny or shimmering decoration, often used to imply superficial attraction

Etymology

From Old French estincelle (sparkle), from Latin scintilla (spark).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɪn.səl / (AmE), / ˈtɪn.səl / (BrE)
A thin thread or strip of glittering material, often metallic, used for decoration.
glittering decorative thread or strip

Examples

Each branch of the Christmas tree was draped with tinsel and colorful lights.
The costume was trimmed with gold tinsel to catch the stage lights.
After the party, strands of tinsel were scattered across the floor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: garland - a decorative wreath or cord, often of flowers or leaves, used for ornamentation
  • Synonym: bauble - a small, shiny decorative object, especially for hanging on a Christmas tree

Etymology

From Old French estincelle (spark), from Latin scintilla (spark).

5. VERB

/ ˈtɪn.səl / (AmE), / ˈtɪn.səl / (BrE)
to give something a superficial or cheaply showy brightness or glamour; to make something appear more attractive than it truly is, especially in a way that lacks genuine value
make superficially showy or glamorous

Examples

Critics argued that the director tinselled the story with unnecessary glitz.
The politician's speech was tinseled with empty promises and flashy rhetoric.
Holiday commercials often tinsel everyday products to make them seem exciting.

Synset Examples

his tinseled image of Hollywood

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embellish - to make something more attractive by adding decorative details
  • Antonym: tarnish - to make or become less bright or attractive

Etymology

From Middle English 'tinsil', from Old French 'estincelle' (spark), influenced by 'tin'.
