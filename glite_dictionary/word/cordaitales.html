Cordaitales

1. NOUN

/ kɔːr.daɪˈteɪliːz / (AmE), / kɔː.daɪˈteɪliːz / (BrE)
an extinct order of tall, woody plants from the late Paleozoic era, similar to or more advanced than cycads, and known mainly from fossils from the Pennsylvanian period
extinct order of Paleozoic woody plants

Examples

Fossils of Cordaitales provide crucial evidence about the evolution of early seed plants.
During the Pennsylvanian period, dense forests of Cordaitales dominated swampy landscapes.
Scientists believe that Cordaitales became extinct by the end of the Mesozoic era.

Etymology

From the genus name Cordaites, named after French botanist Auguste Cordait, plus the taxonomic suffix -ales indicating an order.

2. NOUN

/ kɔrˌdaɪˈteɪliːz / (AmE), / kɔːˌdaɪˈteɪliːz / (BrE)
an extinct order of plants with tall, tree-like trunks, prominent during the Pennsylvanian period and believed extinct since the Mesozoic era
extinct order of ancient tree-like plants

Examples

Fossils of Cordaitales provide important clues about the evolution of early gymnosperms.
Paleobotanists believe that Cordaitales thrived in wetland forests during the Pennsylvanian period.
The extinction of Cordaitales marked a significant change in prehistoric plant ecosystems.

Etymology

From genus name Cordaite, named after Czech botanist August Carl Joseph Corda, plus scientific Latin -ales (denoting an order).
