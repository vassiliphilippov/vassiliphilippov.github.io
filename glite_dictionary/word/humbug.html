humbug

1. NOUN

/ ˈhʌmˌbʌɡ / (AmE), / ˈhʌmbʌɡ / (BrE)
deceptive or false talk or behavior intended to trick people or gain an advantage; fraud or sham.
deceitful or fraudulent behavior

Examples

Many people felt the politician's promises were just humbug to win votes.
He called the idea that ghosts exist pure humbug.
Her apology sounded like humbug after everything she had done.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception intended to result in financial or personal gain
  • Antonym: truth - the quality or state of being true

Etymology

Probably from Old English 'humbugg' (to deceive), popularized in the 18th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhʌmˌbʌɡ / (AmE), / ˈhʌmbʌɡ / (BrE)
dishonest, pretentious, or insincere talk or behaviour intended to deceive or impress others
dishonest or pretentious talk or behaviour

Examples

The politician dismissed the criticism as mere humbug and empty rhetoric.
All this talk about easy success is just humbug meant to mislead the public.
Don't believe his promises—they are nothing but humbug.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nonsense - words or ideas that have no meaning or make no sense
  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception intended to result in financial or personal gain
  • Antonym: truth - the quality or state of being true

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from the English dialect 'hum' (to hoax, trick) + 'bug' (something frightening).

3. VERB

/ ˈhʌmˌbʌɡ / (AmE), / ˈhʌm.bʌɡ / (BrE)
to trick or deceive someone, especially by pretending to be something that one is not
to trick or deceive

Examples

He tried to humbug his way into the exclusive event by pretending to be a reporter.
Don't let them humbug you with empty promises and false claims.
The salesman humbugged several customers with his too-good-to-be-true offers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: to mislead - to cause someone to have a wrong idea or impression

Etymology

Perhaps from English dialect 'humbug', of unknown origin, first recorded in the mid-18th century.
