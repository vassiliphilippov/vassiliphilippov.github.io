to dampen

1. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
to make something less distinct, less intense, or more obscure; to reduce visibility or clarity
make less distinct or clear

Examples

The fog began to dampen the outlines of the buildings, making them hard to see.
Too many technical details can dampen the message of a speech and make it difficult to understand.
Noise-cancelling headphones are designed to dampen unwanted background sounds.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: muffle - make a sound or message less clear or distinct
  • Synonym: obscure - make less visible or unclear

2. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
to make something less strong, active, or intense; to moderate or suppress
make less intense; moderate

Examples

The news did little to dampen their excitement about the trip.
Strict rules are intended to dampen students' enthusiasm for risky behavior.
Her disappointment did not dampen her determination to try again.

  • Antonym: to intensify - to make or become more intense

to reduce the amplitude of oscillations, vibrations, or waves, especially gradually

Examples

Engineers designed a new system to dampen the oscillations caused by high winds.
Special materials in the device help dampen unwanted vibrations during operation.

7. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
to make something less strong or intense; to restrain or check, especially a fire or enthusiasm
to restrain or make less intense

Examples

The firefighters worked quickly to dampen the blaze before it could spread to nearby buildings.
Heavy rain helped to dampen the wildfire, slowing its progress significantly.
Efforts to dampen the flames continued throughout the night until the fire was under control.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to restrain - to hold something back or limit it
  • Antonym: to intensify - to make something stronger or more intense
  • Synonym: to quench - to extinguish or put out, especially a fire

Etymology

From damp + -en, modeled after the pattern of 'soften,' 'harden,' etc.
