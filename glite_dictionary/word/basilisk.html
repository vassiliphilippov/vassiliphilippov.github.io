basilisk

1. NOUN

/ ˈbæzəlɪsk / (AmE), / ˈbæzɪlɪsk / (BrE)
(In classical mythology) a legendary serpent or reptile said to have the power to cause death with its breath or gaze.
legendary deadly serpent or lizard

Examples

In ancient legends, the basilisk could slay anyone who dared to look into its eyes.
The hero ventured into the cave, knowing a basilisk was rumored to dwell within.
Stories of the deadly basilisk have terrified people for centuries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cockatrice - a mythical serpent able to kill with its glance, similar to a basilisk

Etymology

From Latin basiliscus, from Greek basilikos (little king), referring to its regal status among serpents.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbæzəlɪsk / (AmE), / ˈbæzɪlɪsk / (BrE)
A type of large, ancient brass cannon used in the 16th and 17th centuries.
ancient large brass cannon

Examples

The fortress was guarded by a massive basilisk overlooking the harbour.
During the siege, the army used a basilisk to batter the castle walls.
Collectors of rare weaponry sometimes display a restored basilisk among their historical artifacts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cannon - a large, heavy gun typically mounted on wheels

Etymology

From Old French 'basilisque', from Latin 'basiliscus', originally referring to a legendary serpent; the cannon was named for its size and supposed destructive power.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbæzəlɪsk / (AmE), / ˈbæsɪlɪsk / (BrE)
a small, crested, tree-dwelling lizard of tropical America, known for its ability to run on its hind legs, especially across water
crested arboreal lizard of tropical America

Examples

The basilisk is famous for its ability to run across the surface of water to escape predators.
We spotted a green basilisk perched on a low branch near the riverbank.
Basilisks are often called 'Jesus lizards' because of their remarkable running ability.

Etymology

From Latin 'basiliscus', from Greek 'basiliskos' meaning 'little king'. Originally referred to a legendary serpent; later applied to the lizard due to its appearance.
