fertilisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɝːtɪləˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɜːtɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a male gamete (sperm or pollen) unites with a female gamete (egg or ovule) to form a new organism
union of male and female gametes

Examples

After fertilisation, the embryo begins to develop rapidly.
The fertilisation of the ovule by pollen is crucial in plant reproduction.
Scientists study fertilisation to better understand genetic inheritance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conception - the process of becoming pregnant involving fertilisation
  • Synonym: pollination - transfer of pollen to fertilise plants
  • Antonym: sterilisation - process that makes an organism unable to reproduce

Etymology

From Latin 'fertilis' (fruitful) via French 'fertilisation'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɝːtəlɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɜːtəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of making soil or land more fertile, especially by applying fertilizer or manure
enriching soil with fertilizer or manure

Examples

Modern farms rely on fertilisation to boost crop yields and improve soil quality.
The regular fertilisation of fields helps prevent nutrient depletion and supports healthy plant growth.
Organic fertilisation methods are becoming increasingly popular among eco-conscious gardeners.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fertilization - the process of making soil or land more fertile, especially by applying fertilizer or manure (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin 'fertilis' (fertile) via French 'fertilisation'.
