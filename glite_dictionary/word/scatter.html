scatter

1. VERB

/ ˈskædɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
to throw or distribute things so that they cover a wide area, often in a random or irregular way
to spread things over an area

Examples

The children scattered their toys all over the living room.
Autumn leaves were scattered by the wind across the park.
She likes to scatter birdseed in the garden every morning.

Synset Examples

He spread fertilizer over the lawn
scatter cards across the table

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spread - to distribute something over an area or surface

Etymology

From Middle English 'scateren', likely of Scandinavian origin; compare Danish 'skatre', Norwegian 'skatra'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02086937-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈskæt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
an act of spreading things or people over a wide area in a disorderly way
the act of spreading things about

Examples

There was a sudden scatter of birds when the dog ran into the park.
The scatter of papers on his desk made it hard to find anything.
After the fireworks, there was a scatter of people heading home through the park.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dispersal - the act of distributing or spreading things over a wide area
  • Antonym: gathering - the act of bringing things together
Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈskæt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
a small number of things spread randomly over an area; a haphazard distribution in all directions
random distribution of items

Examples

A scatter of coins lay across the floor after the box tipped over.
There was only a scatter of applause at the end of the speech.
She observed a scatter of wildflowers blooming in the meadow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spread - the process or result of distributing things over a wide area

Etymology

From Old English 'sceater', related to the verb 'scatter', ultimately of Germanic origin.
