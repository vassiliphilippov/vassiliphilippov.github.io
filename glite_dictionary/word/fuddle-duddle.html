fuddle-duddle

1. NOUN

/ ˌfʌd.əlˈdʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˌfʌd.əlˈdʌd.əl / (BrE)
An expression used to convey dismissive annoyance or as a nonsensical remark, sometimes as a euphemism for offensive language.
expression of dismissive annoyance or nonsense
informal british

Examples

He just said fuddle-duddle and walked away, refusing to answer any more questions.
The politician's reply was a classic fuddle-duddle, evading the issue entirely.
Whenever she's annoyed, she mutters fuddle-duddle under her breath instead of swearing.

Synset Examples

He just said fuddle-duddle and walked away.
His response was simply a fuddle-duddle dismissal.
She waved her hand and said fuddle-duddle to the critics.

Etymology

Popularized in Canadian English in the 1970s, notably by Prime Minister Pierre Trudeau as a euphemism for strong language.
