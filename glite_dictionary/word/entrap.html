to entrap

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtræp / (AmE), / ɪnˈtræp / (BrE)
to catch or trick someone into a situation from which they cannot easily escape, often by deception or skillful maneuvering
to catch or trap using deceit

Examples

The detective managed to entrap the suspect with a carefully planned sting operation.
They tried to entrap him by asking leading questions during the interview.
Wild animals can become accidentally entrapped by discarded fishing nets along the coastline.

Synset Examples

I was set up!
The innocent man was framed by the police

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trap - to catch or capture someone or something in a trap

Etymology

From Old French *entraper*, from en- (in) + trappe (trap).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtræp / (AmE), / ɪnˈtræp / (BrE)
to catch (someone or something) in or as if in a trap, often by using trickery or deception
catch in or as if in a trap

Examples

The police managed to entrap the criminal by setting up a clever sting operation.
Wild animals can often be entrapped by nets or snares left in the forest.
It is illegal for investigators to deliberately entrap someone into committing a crime.

Synset Examples

The men trap foxes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trap - to catch (an animal or person) in a trap
  • Antonym: release - to set free from confinement or trap

Etymology

From Middle English 'entrapen', from Old French 'entraper', from 'en-' (in) + 'trappe' (trap).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01483400-v
Synset POS: v
