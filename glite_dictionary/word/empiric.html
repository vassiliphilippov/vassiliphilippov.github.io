empiric

1. ADJ

/ ɛmˈpɪrɪk / (AmE), / ɪmˈpɪrɪk / (BrE)
Based on experiment and observation rather than theory.
based on experiment and observation

Examples

The scientist insisted on using an empiric approach to test the new hypothesis.
Medical knowledge in ancient times was primarily empiric, relying on observation and experience.
Unlike theoretical philosophers, she favored empiric methods in her research.

Synset Examples

an empirical basis for an ethical theory
empirical laws
empirical data
an empirical treatment of a disease about which little is known

Related Terms

  • Synonym: empirical - based on experiment and observation rather than theory

Etymology

From Greek empeirikos (experienced), via Latin empiricus.

2. ADJ

/ ɛmˈpɪrɪk / (AmE), / ɛmˈpɪrɪk / (BrE)
(historical) Involving or characteristic of medical quackery or unscientific practice; relying on trial-and-error rather than scientific methods.
relating to medical quackery or unscientific practice
archaic medical

Examples

The doctor was accused of using empiric methods with no scientific basis.
In the eighteenth century, empiric medicine often relied on anecdotal remedies.
Many empiric treatments were later discredited as ineffective or dangerous.

Synset Examples

empiric treatment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quack - a person who dishonestly claims to have medical knowledge

Etymology

From Greek *empeirikos* (experienced), later used derogatorily for practitioners using unscientific methods.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03074710-a
Synset POS: a
