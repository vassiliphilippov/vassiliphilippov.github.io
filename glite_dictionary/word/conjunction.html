conjunction

1. NOUN

/ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
a thing or state that joins, connects, or is joined together with something else
thing or state that joins or connects

Examples

The successful conjunction of the two rivers created a powerful new waterway.
A solar eclipse occurs at the conjunction of the sun and the moon.
Good teamwork requires the conjunction of different talents and skills.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: connection - a relationship in which a person or thing is linked or associated with something else

Etymology

From Latin conjunctio (joining), from coniungere (to join together).

5. NOUN

/ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
In astronomy, the apparent meeting or passing of two or more celestial bodies in the same degree of the zodiac, as observed from Earth.
apparent meeting of celestial bodies

Examples

The conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn was a spectacular astronomical event.
Venus will be in conjunction with Mars next week, making them appear close together in the night sky.
Astronomers eagerly awaited the conjunction as a rare opportunity to observe both planets together.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: opposition - astronomical event where two celestial bodies appear opposite each other in the sky

Etymology

From Latin conjunctio (a joining together), from coniungere (to join).

6. NOUN

/ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
The grammatical relationship between words, phrases, or clauses that are joined together by a conjunction.
grammatical relation joining linguistic units

Examples

In the sentence 'She sings and he dances,' the conjunction connects two clauses.
The conjunction creates a relationship between the two phrases in the sentence.
A coordinating conjunction joins elements of equal grammatical rank.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coordination - the linking of words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance
  • Antonym: subordination - the linking of clauses where one is dependent on the other

Etymology

From Latin coniunctiō (joining together), from coniungere (to join together).
