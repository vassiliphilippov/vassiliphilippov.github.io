singe

1. VERB

/ sɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪndʒ / (BrE)
to burn something superficially or lightly, especially so that only the surface is affected and not completely destroyed
burn lightly or superficially

Examples

Be careful not to singe your shirt when ironing close to the collar.
The flames singed his hair but left him otherwise unharmed.
She singed the edges of the paper to give it an antique appearance.

Synset Examples

I singed my eyebrows

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something with flame or heat

Etymology

Old English *sincgan* (to singe), related to Old High German *sengan* (to singe).

2. VERB

/ sɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪndʒ / (BrE)
to become superficially or lightly burned, especially at the edges or surface
become superficially burned

Examples

Be careful not to singe your hair when you light the barbecue.
The heat from the oven door began to singe the edges of her apron.
I could feel my eyebrows singe as I leaned over the open flame.

Synset Examples

my eyebrows singed when I bent over the flames

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something slightly
  • Degree Higher: burn - to be damaged or destroyed by fire or heat

Etymology

From Old English *sengian* (to burn lightly, scorch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00583820-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ sɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪndʒ / (BrE)
a slight burn or scorch on the surface of something, especially caused by brief exposure to flame or heat
a slight surface burn or scorch

Examples

The wool blanket had a small singe near the edge from the candle flame.
She noticed a singe mark on her shirt after ironing it too quickly.
There was only a minor singe on the tablecloth after the spark.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something slightly

Etymology

From Old French *sengier* (to burn lightly), from Frankish *sengōn* (to singe), related to Germanic roots.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14313444-n
Synset POS: n
