intensive

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛn.sɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛn.sɪv / (BrE)
involving or requiring a great amount of effort, attention, or resources within a short period or small area; marked by a high degree or intensity
involving a high degree of effort or intensity

Examples

The language course was extremely intensive, with daily lessons and assignments.
The hospital has an intensive care unit for critical patients.
That factory uses a labor-intensive production method requiring many workers.

Synset Examples

the questioning was intensive
intensive care
research-intensive
a labor-intensive industry

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intense - having strong feelings or a high degree of force
  • Antonym: extensive - covering a large area or scope; wide-ranging

Etymology

From Latin intensus (stretched, strained) + -ive, meaning having the quality of.

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛnsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛnsɪv / (BrE)
(of agriculture or industrial methods) using a high level of input such as labor or capital to achieve greater productivity from a given area or system.
requiring high effort or input for increased productivity

Examples

The company is shifting to intensive farming to maximize crop yields on limited land.
Modern greenhouses use intensive methods to grow vegetables year-round in small spaces.
Because of its intensive production system, the dairy farm requires substantial labor and technology.

Synset Examples

intensive agriculture
intensive conditions

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extensive - (of agriculture) using large areas with lower input per area; opposite of intensive

Etymology

From Medieval Latin intensīvus, from Latin intendere (to stretch toward, direct attention).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01518457-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛnsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛnsɪv / (BrE)
giving force or emphasis to something; strengthening or intensifying meaning, especially in grammatical contexts
giving force or emphasis

Examples

Some linguists classify 'really' as an intensive adverb when used before adjectives.
The exclamation mark in the sentence serves as an intensive element.
In this phrase, 'so' acts as an intensive word to add emphasis.

Synset Examples

an intensive adverb

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emphatic - showing or giving emphasis
  • Antonym: weak - lacking force or emphasis

Etymology

From Medieval Latin intensivus (forcibly, strongly), from Latin intensus (stretched, intense).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a
