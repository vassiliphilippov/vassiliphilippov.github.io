to superpose

1. VERB

/ ˌsuːpərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to place one object, figure, or image over another so that their corresponding parts align precisely, especially in geometry.
to place or lay over another, exactly aligning

Examples

In geometry class, we were asked to superpose the two triangles to check if they were congruent.
The technician will superpose the blueprints to identify any discrepancies between the drafts.
By carefully superposing these transparent images, subtle differences become immediately visible.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overlay - to place or lay something over another, usually partially covering it

Etymology

From Latin superponere: super- (over) + ponere (to place).

2. VERB

/ ˌsuːpərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to place or lay one thing on top of another, especially so that both are still evident
place one thing on top of another

Examples

The architect decided to superpose a modern extension over the historical building.
When you superpose two transparent maps, you can see how the cities align.
In physics, wave patterns can superpose to produce interference.

Synset Examples

can you superimpose the two images?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to superimpose - place or lay something over another, typically so that both are evident

Etymology

From Latin superponere, from super (above) + ponere (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01471892-v
Synset POS: v
