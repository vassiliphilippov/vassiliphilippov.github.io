squeal

1. NOUN

/ skwil / (AmE), / skwiːl / (BrE)
a long, high-pitched cry or sound, especially one expressing pain, fear, excitement, or protest
high-pitched cry or sound

Examples

The pig let out a loud squeal as it was lifted into the pen.
The children's squeal of excitement could be heard from across the playground.
The tires emitted a sharp squeal as the car rounded the corner too quickly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shriek - a loud, high-pitched cry or yell
  • Synonym: howl - a long, loud, mournful cry
  • Antonym: whisper - a quiet, soft sound

Etymology

Imitative origin, from the sound itself.

2. VERB

/ skwil / (AmE), / skwiːl / (BrE)
to confess to a punishable or immoral act, typically under pressure or to avoid punishment oneself, especially to the authorities
confess to wrongdoing under pressure
informal

Examples

The suspect refused to squeal on his accomplices, despite the intense questioning.
He got caught stealing, but he won't squeal unless they threaten him with jail.
Under pressure from the police, she finally squealed and revealed everything.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snitch - to secretly inform on someone, especially to the police
  • Antonym: to keep quiet - to refrain from giving information, especially to authorities
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00821169-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ skwil / (AmE), / skwiːl / (BrE)
to make a long, high-pitched cry or noise, especially like that of a pig
make a high-pitched cry or noise

Examples

The piglets began to squeal loudly when the farmer entered the pen.
She couldn't help but squeal with excitement when she saw her birthday present.
The brakes on the old car squeal whenever you press them too hard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scream - to emit a loud, sharp, piercing cry
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak very softly using one's breath

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound itself.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
