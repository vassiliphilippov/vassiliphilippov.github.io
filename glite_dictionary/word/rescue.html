rescue

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛskju / (AmE), / ˈrɛskjuː / (BrE)
to take someone forcibly from legal custody, especially by illegal means
take forcibly from legal custody

Examples

The rebels plotted to rescue their leader from police custody.
A group of armed men tried to rescue the prisoners while they were being transferred.
It was illegal to rescue someone who had been duly arrested by the authorities.

Synset Examples

rescue prisoners

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to liberate - to set someone free from a situation, especially imprisonment or oppression

Etymology

From Latin 'rescuere', meaning to deliver or save.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛs.kju / (AmE), / ˈrɛs.kjuː / (BrE)
The act of saving someone or something from a dangerous or distressing situation.
the act of saving from danger

Examples

The rescue of the stranded hikers was broadcast live on television.
Firefighters performed a daring rescue during the night-time blaze.
Thanks to the quick rescue efforts, everyone made it out safely.

Synset Examples

work is the deliverance of mankind
a surgeon's job is the saving of lives

Related Terms

  • Synonym: save - to keep someone or something safe from harm or danger
  • Synonym: deliverance - the act of being rescued or set free
  • Antonym: endangerment - the act of putting someone or something in danger

Etymology

From Old French *rescoure* (to rescue), from Late Latin *rescūtāre* (to free).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛs.kju / (AmE), / ˈres.kjuː / (BrE)
to save someone or something from a dangerous, harmful, or unpleasant situation
to save from danger or harm

Examples

The firefighters managed to rescue the family from the burning building.
He risked his life to rescue the stranded hikers during the storm.
Volunteers worked tirelessly to rescue animals affected by the flood.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: save - to keep someone or something safe from danger or harm
  • Antonym: abandon - to leave someone or something in a dangerous or difficult situation

Etymology

From Latin 'rescutere' (to free, deliver), via Old French 'rescourre'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
