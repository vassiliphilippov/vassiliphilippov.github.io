to nitrify

1. VERB

/ ˈnaɪtrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈnaɪtrɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to treat soil with nitrates or to convert into nitrates, especially to increase fertility
treat soil with nitrates
medical

Examples

Farmers often nitrify their fields to improve crop yields.
The company developed a new method to nitrify acidic soils more efficiently.
If you nitrify the land, it can significantly boost plant growth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fertilize - to add nutrients or substances to soil to increase fertility

Etymology

From French 'nitrifier', from 'nitre' (niter, potassium nitrate) + '-fier' (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00503559-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈnaɪtrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈnɪtrɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to convert a substance into nitrites or nitrates, especially through chemical or biological processes by bacteria
convert into nitrites or nitrates
medical

Examples

Soil bacteria nitrify ammonia, transforming it into nitrites and nitrates that plants can absorb.
Certain wastewater treatment processes are designed to nitrify organic nitrogen into safer compounds.
If you don't nitrify the soil properly, the nitrogen cycle may be disrupted.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to denitrify - to remove nitrogen or its compounds, especially by bacteria

Etymology

From French nitrifier, from nitre (niter) + -fier (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00505982-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈnaɪtrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈnɪtrɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to treat or combine with nitrogen or a nitrogen compound, especially in order to promote certain chemical processes or soil fertility
treat with nitrogen or nitrogen compounds

Examples

Farmers often nitrify the soil to enhance crop growth.
Researchers discovered a new way to nitrify wastewater efficiently.
The compost pile was treated to nitrify the organic materials more rapidly.

Etymology

From French 'nitrifier', from 'nitre' (niter) + '-fier' (to make).
