sediment

1. VERB

/ ˈsɛdəˌmɛnt / (AmE), / ˈsɛdɪmənt / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to allow solid particles to settle at the bottom, or to cause something to be deposited as sediment.
to deposit as sediment

Examples

After standing overnight, tiny particles begin to sediment at the bottom of the container.
The solution was spun in a centrifuge to sediment any remaining solids.
If left undisturbed, clay will eventually sediment out of the river water.

Etymology

From Latin 'sedimentum' (a settling, sinking down), from 'sedere' (to sit).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɛdəmənt / (AmE), / ˈsɛdɪmənt / (BrE)
solid material that settles at the bottom of a liquid or is deposited by water, wind, or ice over time.
solid matter deposited by natural processes

Examples

After the floodwaters receded, a thick layer of sediment covered the streets.
Wine bottles often contain sediment at the bottom that should not be poured into the glass.
River sediment can build up over time and change the shape of the riverbed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deposit - a layer of accumulated matter left by a natural process
  • Antonym: erosion - gradual destruction or removal of soil or rock by natural forces

Etymology

From Latin 'sedimentum' (a settling), from 'sedere' (to sit).

3. VERB

/ ˈsɛd.əˌmɛnt / (AmE), / ˈsɛd.ɪ.mənt / (BrE)
(of particles or substances in a liquid) to settle or be deposited as a layer at the bottom.
to settle as sediment

Examples

After the water was allowed to sit undisturbed, the heavier particles began to sediment at the bottom of the jar.
If the solution is cooled quickly, the solids will not have enough time to sediment properly.
Scientists observed the suspended clay slowly sediment during the experiment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precipitate - to cause (a substance) to be deposited from a solution

Etymology

From Latin 'sedimentum' (a settling, sediment), from 'sedere' (to sit).
