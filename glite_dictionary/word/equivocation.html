equivocation

1. NOUN

/ ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
The use of ambiguous or vague language to deceive or mislead; an evasion or misleading statement.
use of ambiguous language to deceive

Examples

The politician's equivocation left the audience unsure of his true intentions.
During the investigation, her repeated equivocation raised suspicions among the detectives.
Clear answers are essential in court, not the equivocation that the witness provided.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambiguity - the quality of being open to more than one interpretation
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and straightforward

Etymology

From Late Latin equivocationem, from equivocus (ambiguous).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the use of ambiguous or unclear language to avoid telling the truth or committing to a position, especially deliberately
deliberately ambiguous statement

Examples

The politician’s equivocation frustrated the journalists seeking a clear answer.
Her constant equivocation made it difficult to know what she really believed.
Detectives quickly recognized his equivocation as a tactic to hide the truth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambiguity - the quality of being open to more than one interpretation
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and straightforward

Etymology

From Late Latin equivocatio (ambiguity), from Latin aequivocus (of equal voice, ambiguous).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪˌkwɪvəˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
The use of ambiguous or vague language, especially to avoid telling the truth or committing oneself.
use of ambiguous or vague language

Examples

The politician's equivocation frustrated reporters seeking a direct answer to the question.
His constant equivocation made it hard to know where he really stood.
Shakespeare's Macbeth explores the dangerous consequences of equivocation and deceit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambiguity - the quality of being open to more than one interpretation
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful

Etymology

From Late Latin aequivocationem, from Latin aequivocus (equivocal).
