alkali

1. NOUN

/ ˈæl.kəˌlaɪ / (AmE), / ˈæl.kə.laɪ / (BrE)
a compound, typically a hydroxide or carbonate of an alkali metal, that dissolves in water to form a strongly basic solution and turns litmus blue
water-soluble basic compound

Examples

Potassium hydroxide is a strong alkali that is often used in industry.
When dissolved in water, an alkali can neutralize acids to produce salt and water.
The laboratory experiment demonstrated how an alkali turns red litmus paper blue.

Synset Examples

bases include oxides and hydroxides of metals and ammonia

Related Terms

  • Synonym: base - a substance that reacts with acids to form salts and water
  • Antonym: acid - a substance that reacts with a base to form a salt and water

Etymology

From Arabic 'al-qaly' (ashes of saltwort), via Medieval Latin 'alkali'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈæl.kəˌlaɪ / (AmE), / ˈæl.kə.laɪ / (BrE)
a mixture of soluble salts, often found in arid soils and some bodies of water, that can be damaging to agriculture
mixture of soluble salts harmful to land

Examples

The presence of alkali in the soil makes it difficult for crops to grow.
High levels of alkali are often found in the water of desert regions.
Farmers must treat their land to reduce harmful alkali buildup.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acid - a substance with a pH less than 7; the chemical opposite of an alkali

Etymology

From Arabic al-qaly (ashes of saltwort), through Medieval Latin alkali.
