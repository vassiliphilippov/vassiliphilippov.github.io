intercept

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.tɚˌsɛpt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.təˌsɛpt / (BrE)
the point at which a line or curve crosses a coordinate axis, especially the y-axis or x-axis in a graph
point where a line crosses an axis

Examples

The y-intercept of the line is 5, showing where it crosses the vertical axis.
To find the intercept, set x to zero and solve for y in the equation.
The graph’s x-intercept is at the origin, meaning the line passes through (0,0).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intersection - the point where two lines or curves meet

Etymology

From Latin 'interceptus', past participle of 'intercipere' (to seize, interrupt).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06021142-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈsɛpt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈsɛpt / (BrE)
to secretly listen to phone conversations or telegraph messages in order to gather information, especially by tapping a line
tap a phone or wire for information

Examples

The agents tried to intercept the suspect's phone calls to gather evidence.
Authorities managed to intercept a message by tapping into the secure communication line.
The police intercepted several conversations related to the investigation.

Synset Examples

The FBI was tapping the phone line of the suspected spy
Is this hotel room bugged?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tap - to listen secretly to telephone calls by connecting to the line
  • Synonym: to eavesdrop - to secretly listen to someone else's conversation

Etymology

From Latin 'intercipere' (to seize, cut off), from 'inter-' (between) + 'capere' (to take).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02193294-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈsɛpt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈsɛpt / (BrE)
to stop or seize something or someone that is on its way from one place to another
stop or seize en route

Examples

The police were able to intercept the package before it reached its destination.
A missile defense system is designed to intercept incoming threats.
She tried to intercept her friend's message before it was delivered.

Synset Examples

The fighter plane was ordered to intercept an aircraft that had entered the country's airspace

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to stop the movement, progress, or action of something
  • Antonym: to allow - to permit something to proceed without stopping it

Etymology

From Latin 'intercipere', from 'inter-' (between) + 'capere' (to take).
