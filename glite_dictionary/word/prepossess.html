to prepossess

1. VERB

/ ˌpriːpəˈzɛs / (AmE), / ˌpriːpəˈzɛs / (BrE)
to influence (someone's) opinion or feelings beforehand, often unfairly or favorably
influence opinion in advance

Examples

The article aimed to prepossess readers against the candidate before the debate began.
Her charming manners prepossessed the jury in her favor early on.
Careful advertising was used to prepossess consumers with positive expectations about the new product.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prejudice - to cause someone to have an unjustified opinion

Etymology

From Latin praepossessus, past participle of prae- (before) + possidēre (to possess).

2. VERB

/ ˌpriːpəˈzɛs / (AmE), / ˌpriːpəˈzɛs / (BrE)
to cause someone to be preoccupied or influenced by a particular idea or feeling, often before experiencing something directly
cause to be preoccupied or influenced

Examples

The prospect of failure seemed to prepossess his mind before the interview even began.
She was so prepossessed by worry that she could not concentrate on her work.
Anxiety about the results can prepossess students long before the exams start.

Synset Examples

The idea of his failure prepossesses him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preoccupy - to dominate or engross the mind of someone to the exclusion of other thoughts

Etymology

From Latin 'praepossessus', past participle of 'praepossidere', meaning 'to possess beforehand'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01787565-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌpriː.pəˈzɛs / (AmE), / ˌpriː.pəˈzɛs / (BrE)
to influence or impress someone beforehand in a positive way, often creating a favorable opinion before actual experience
to impress or influence positively beforehand

Examples

Her kindness and intelligence prepossessed everyone she met at the interview.
The charming decor immediately prepossessed guests in favor of the restaurant.
His confident manner seemed to prepossess the judges before the competition even began.

Synset Examples

A prepossessing appearance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bias - cause to feel or show inclination or prejudice for or against someone or something

Etymology

From Latin praepossessus, past participle of praepossidere (to possess beforehand), from prae- (before) + possidere (to possess).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01774017-v
Synset POS: v
