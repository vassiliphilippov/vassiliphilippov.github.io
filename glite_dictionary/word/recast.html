to recast

1. VERB

/ ˌriˈkæst / (AmE), / ˌriːˈkɑːst / (BrE)
to assign (an actor) a different role, or to select a new actor for a role in a play, film, or television production
to assign a new role to an actor

Examples

After the lead actor quit, the director decided to recast the part entirely.
The producer chose to recast her as the film's antagonist for the sequel.
Due to creative differences, the studio had to recast several supporting roles.

Synset Examples

He was recast as Iago

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cast - to select actors for roles in a play or film

Etymology

From re- (again) + cast (assign a role in acting).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01714793-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ riˈkæst / (AmE), / ˌriːˈkɑːst / (BrE)
to present or arrange something in a different form or style; to model or shape anew
present or arrange differently; model anew

Examples

After the criticism, she decided to recast her proposal in a more positive light.
The director chose to recast the lead role for the new production.
He had to recast his argument to appeal to a broader audience.

Synset Examples

She had to recast her image to please the electorate in her home state

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reshape - to shape something again or differently
  • Synonym: to remodel - to change the structure or form of something

Etymology

From re- (again) + cast (Middle English, from Old Norse kasta).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01662480-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌriˈkæst / (AmE), / ˌriːˈkɑːst / (BrE)
to cast something again by melting it down and forming it into a new shape
to cast again by melting and forming

Examples

After it broke, the ancient bell was recast to preserve its sound.
The statue was melted down and recast with a new design.
When the church needed a new bell, they decided to recast the old one using the same bronze.

Synset Examples

The bell cracked and had to be recast

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to remold - to mold again or into a new form
  • Synonym: reform - to make changes in order to improve or restore to a former condition

Etymology

From re- (again) + cast (to form by pouring into a mold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01667100-v
Synset POS: v
