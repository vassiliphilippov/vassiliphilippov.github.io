hydroplane

1. NOUN

/ ˈhaɪdroʊˌpleɪn / (AmE), / ˈhaɪdrəˌpleɪn / (BrE)
an aircraft designed to take off from and land on water
aircraft that operates on water

Examples

The old hydroplane gently landed on the surface of the lake.
Pilots need special training to safely operate a hydroplane in windy conditions.
The museum displayed a restored wooden hydroplane from the 1920s.

Synset Examples

the designer of marine aircraft demonstrated his newest hydroplane

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seaplane - an aircraft that can take off from and land on water

Etymology

From Greek 'hydro' (water) + 'plane' (airplane).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04167647-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈhaɪdrəˌpleɪn / (AmE), / ˈhaɪdrəʊˌpleɪn / (BrE)
(of a boat or similar vehicle) to skim or glide over the surface of water at high speed.
skim over water in a hydroplane

Examples

The racing boat began to hydroplane as it reached full speed across the lake.
As soon as the hull lifted, we could feel the boat hydroplaning over the surface of the water.
With a sudden burst of power, the vessel hydroplaned smoothly toward the finish line.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skid - to slide sideways, especially on a slippery surface

Etymology

From 'hydro-' (relating to water) + 'plane' (as in aeroplane).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01947012-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈhaɪ.droʊˌpleɪn / (AmE), / ˈhaɪ.drəʊˌpleɪn / (BrE)
a speedboat fitted with winglike structures (hydrofoils) that lift the hull above the water at high speeds, allowing it to skim across the surface
speedboat that skims on water using hydrofoils

Examples

The new hydroplane set a speed record on the lake during the annual boat race.
A famous hydroplane crashed during a test run but was later fully restored.
He devoted his weekends to restoring an old hydroplane he found in a warehouse.

Synset Examples

the museum houses a replica of the jet hydroplane that broke the record

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hydrofoil - boat supported by underwater foils for high-speed travel

Etymology

From Greek 'hydro-' (water) and 'plane' (from Latin 'planum', flat surface).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03557929-n
Synset POS: n
