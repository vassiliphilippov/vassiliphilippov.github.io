to retaliate

1. VERB

/ rɪˈtælieɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈtælieɪt / (BrE)
to make an attack or take harmful action in return for a similar attack or action against oneself
respond to harm with harm

Examples

After the attack, the army decided to retaliate immediately.
If you insult him, he might retaliate with even harsher words.
The company warned it would retaliate against unfair trade practices.

Synset Examples

The Empire strikes back
The Giants struck back and won the opener
The Israeli army retaliated for the Hamas bombing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to avenge - to inflict harm in return for an injury or wrong
  • Antonym: to forgive - to stop feeling angry or resentful toward someone for an offense or mistake

Etymology

From Latin retaliare, meaning 'to repay in kind.'

2. VERB

/ rɪˈtæliˌeɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈtælieɪt / (BrE)
to hurt someone or do something harmful in return for something bad they have done to you
take revenge or respond to harm with harm

Examples

After the insult, he decided to retaliate by ignoring her at the party.
The country threatened to retaliate if its citizens were harmed.
She refused to retaliate against her critics, choosing instead to remain silent.

Synset Examples

He wants to avenge the murder of his brother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to avenge - take action to punish or get even for a wrong
  • Antonym: to forgive - to stop feeling anger or resentment towards someone

Etymology

From Latin retaliare (to pay back in kind, repay).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
