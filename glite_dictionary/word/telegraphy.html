telegraphy

1. NOUN

/ təˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / tɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
the process or technique of communicating over a distance by transmitting messages using wires and electrical signals, typically in Morse code
long-distance communication by wire (Morse code)

Examples

The invention of telegraphy revolutionized communication in the 19th century.
Samuel Morse contributed greatly to the development of telegraphy and Morse code.
Before telephones, urgent messages were sent via telegraphy across continents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegraph - system or device for long-distance communication using electrical signals

Etymology

From French télégraphie, from Greek tēle (far) + graphein (to write).

2. NOUN

/ təˈlɛɡrəfi / (AmE), / tɪˈlɛɡrəfi / (BrE)
The process or practice of transmitting messages over distances by electric signals through wires, especially as used historically before the telephone.
long-distance communication by electric signals

Examples

Before the invention of the telephone, telegraphy was the fastest method of long-distance communication.
The development of telegraphy revolutionized how news and information spread across continents.
Modern electronics have replaced telegraphy, but its impact on society can still be felt today.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telecommunication - the transmission of signals over a distance for communication
  • Degree Higher: radio - communication using electromagnetic waves instead of wires

Etymology

From Greek 'tēle' (far) and 'graphein' (to write), originally via French 'télégraphie'.
