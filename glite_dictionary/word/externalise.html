to externalise

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈstɝː.nə.laɪz / (AmE), / ɪkˈstɜː.nə.laɪz / (BrE)
to make something external or objective; to express, manifest, or give reality to (an idea, feeling, or thought)
make external or manifest
british

Examples

Children often externalise their feelings by drawing or acting them out.
Therapists encourage patients not to externalise blame for their problems.
His ability to externalise complex ideas makes his lectures very engaging.

Synset Examples

language externalizes our thoughts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to externalize - make external or objective (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin externus (outside) + -al + -ise (verb-forming suffix).

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈstɝrnəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ɪkˈstɜːnəlaɪz / (BrE)
to regard (something, especially an idea or feeling) as existing outside oneself; to treat as objective rather than subjective
regard as existing outside oneself

Examples

Therapists help patients to externalise their fears so they can address them more effectively.
He tends to externalise blame rather than reflect on his own actions.
By writing about her anxiety, she could externalise her emotions and better understand them.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to internalise - to make something part of one's own inner being or mind

Etymology

From external + -ise, modeled after French 'externaliser'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00731627-v
Synset POS: v
