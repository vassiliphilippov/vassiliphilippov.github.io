Ruminantia

1. NOUN

/ ruːˌmɪniˈænʃiə / (AmE), / ruːˌmɪniˈantɪə / (BrE)
A suborder of mammals that includes cattle, sheep, deer, goats, antelopes, giraffes, and similar animals, characterized by stomachs that allow them to chew cud.
suborder of cud-chewing mammals

Examples

The Ruminantia are distinguished by their complex, multi-chambered stomachs.
Giraffes, included in the Ruminantia, have evolved long necks for feeding on tall trees.
The digestive system of the Ruminantia allows them to extract nutrients from tough plant material.

Etymology

From modern Latin, based on Latin 'rumen' (throat, gullet, first stomach of ruminants).

2. NOUN

/ ˌruːmɪˈnænʃiə / (AmE), / ˌruːmɪˈnantɪə / (BrE)
A suborder of mammals that includes animals such as cattle, sheep, goats, antelopes, deer, giraffes, and camels, characterized by their ability to ruminate (chew cud).
suborder of cud-chewing mammals

Examples

Cows, sheep, and deer all belong to the Ruminantia suborder.
Scientists studied the digestive processes unique to members of the Ruminantia.
Giraffes are a surprising member of the Ruminantia, alongside cattle and antelopes.

Etymology

From Neo-Latin 'Ruminantia', from Latin 'ruminare' (to chew the cud).
