Phocidae

1. NOUN

/ ˈfoʊsɪˌdiː / (AmE), / ˈfəʊsɪˌdiː / (BrE)
A family of aquatic, earless seals comprising the so-called true seals, characterized by the lack of external ear flaps and well-adapted for swimming.
family of earless true seals

Examples

The family Phocidae includes some of the best-known seal species, such as the harbor seal and the grey seal.
Unlike other pinnipeds, members of the Phocidae lack external ear flaps.
Researchers are studying Phocidae to better understand their adaptation to aquatic environments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seal - a large marine mammal with flippers

Etymology

From modern Latin Phocidae, from Greek phōkē (seal).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfoʊsɪdiː / (AmE), / ˈfəʊsɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of aquatic mammals known as earless seals, characterized by the absence of external ear flaps and their adaptation to marine life
family of earless seals

Examples

The Phocidae family includes species like the harbor seal and the elephant seal.
Unlike sea lions, members of Phocidae lack external ear flaps and move awkwardly on land.
Researchers compared the skull structures of Phocidae with those of other marine mammals.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Otariidae - family of eared seals, such as sea lions and fur seals

Etymology

From scientific Latin Phocidae, based on Greek phōkē (seal).
