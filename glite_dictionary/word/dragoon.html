dragoon

1. NOUN

/ drəˈɡun / (AmE), / drəˈɡuːn / (BrE)
a member of a European military unit, especially in the 17th and 18th centuries, formerly composed of heavily armed cavalrymen who were trained to fight on foot as well as on horseback
European cavalry soldier (historical)

Examples

The museum showcased the uniform worn by a British dragoon during the Napoleonic Wars.
A squadron of dragoons charged across the field, sabers drawn.
The dragoon dismounted quickly, ready to fight alongside the infantry.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cavalryman - a soldier who fights on horseback

Etymology

From French 'dragon', originally referring to a type of firearm carried by these soldiers, then to the soldiers themselves.

2. VERB

/ drəˈɡun / (AmE), / drəˈɡuːn / (BrE)
to subjugate or coerce someone, especially by using force or military presence
to force or subjugate by force

Examples

The government attempted to dragoon the population into supporting its policies by deploying the army.
He felt his colleagues were being dragooned into accepting the new regulations against their will.
The protesters refused to be dragooned into submission by the show of force.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to coerce - to force someone to do something by using threats or force
  • Antonym: to persuade - to convince someone by reasoning or pleading

Etymology

From French 'dragon' (a type of soldier), originally referring to mounted infantry.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02429380-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ drəˈɡun / (AmE), / drəˈɡuːn / (BrE)
to force someone to do something by using threats, pressure, or bullying
force by threats or pressure

Examples

The company tried to dragoon its employees into working overtime.
She felt she had been dragooned into organizing the event against her will.
He refused to be dragooned by his peers into making a risky investment.

Synset Examples

They sandbagged him to make dinner for everyone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to coerce - to force someone to do something by use of threats or pressure
  • Antonym: to persuade - to convince someone to do something without force or threats

Etymology

From French 'dragon' (dragoon soldier), referring to the practice of quartering dragoons on civilians to force compliance.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
