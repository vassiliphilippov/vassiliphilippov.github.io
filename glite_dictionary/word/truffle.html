truffle

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrʌfəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌf(ə)l / (BrE)
a highly prized edible subterranean fungus of the genus Tuber, found especially in Europe, valued as a delicacy
edible subterranean fungus, culinary delicacy

Examples

Chefs prize the intense aroma of truffle in gourmet dishes.
We found several black truffles beneath the roots of oak trees.
The restaurant's pasta was topped with fresh truffle shavings for added flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mushroom - an edible fungus with a stem, cap, and gills, growing above ground

Etymology

From French truffe, from Medieval Latin *tuber* (swelling, growth, truffle).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrʌfəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌf(ə)l / (BrE)
An edible subterranean fungus of the genus Tuber, prized as a delicacy in cooking for its distinctive aroma and flavor.
edible subterranean fungus delicacy

Examples

The chef shaved black truffle over the pasta to add a rich, earthy flavor.
White truffles are considered a rare delicacy and can be very expensive.
Pigs and dogs are often trained to sniff out truffles in the wild.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mushroom - a fungus that grows above ground, often edible

Etymology

From French 'truffe', from Latin 'tuber' meaning 'swelling, lump, truffle'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈtrʌfəl / (AmE), / ˈtrʌf.əl / (BrE)
A rich, creamy chocolate confection, typically made with a ganache center coated in chocolate, cocoa powder, or nuts.
creamy chocolate confection

Examples

She offered me a homemade truffle filled with dark chocolate ganache.
The dessert tray included a selection of truffles dusted with cocoa powder.
He bought a box of assorted truffle candies as a gift.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chocolate - a sweet food made from roasted and ground cacao seeds

Etymology

Originally from French 'truffe', referring to the fungus, later used for the chocolate sweet due to its resemblance in shape.
