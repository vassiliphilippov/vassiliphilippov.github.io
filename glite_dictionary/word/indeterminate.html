indeterminate

1. ADJ

/ ˌɪndəˈtɝːmənət / (AmE), / ˌɪndɪˈtɜːmɪnət / (BrE)
(of a plant or plant part) having the capacity to continue growing at the apex or tip.
having growth that continues at the apex

Examples

Tomatoes with indeterminate growth continue producing fruit throughout the season.
The botanist explained that the indeterminate stems would never stop extending unless pruned.
Some beans have indeterminate vines that keep climbing and flowering for months.

Synset Examples

an indeterminate stem

Related Terms

  • Antonym: determinate - having growth that stops at the apex

Etymology

From Latin indeterminatus, from in- (not) + determinatus (limited).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00745042-a

2. ADJ

/ ˌɪndɪˈtɝːmɪnət / (AmE), / ˌɪndɪˈtɜːmɪnət / (BrE)
not exactly known, defined, or decided; having an uncertain or ambiguous nature
uncertain or ambiguous in nature

Examples

The outcome of the trial remained indeterminate due to a lack of evidence.
Surrealist painters often depicted indeterminate forms that defied easy interpretation.
His answer was so indeterminate that no one knew what he really meant.

Synset Examples

the equivocal (or indeterminate) objects painted by surrealists

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uncertain - not able to be relied on; not known or definite
  • Antonym: definite - clearly defined or determined; having exact limits

Etymology

From Latin 'indeterminatus', from 'in-' (not) + 'determinatus' (determined).

3. ADJ

/ ˌɪndəˈtɝːmənət / (AmE), / ˌɪndɪˈtɜːmɪnət / (BrE)
(of a quantity, value, or form in mathematics) having no definite or unique value, especially when an equation or expression does not yield a single solution.
having no definite value (math)

Examples

When x equals zero, the expression becomes an indeterminate form.
The limits resulted in an indeterminate value, so further analysis was needed.
Dividing zero by zero is considered an indeterminate expression in calculus.

Synset Examples

0/0 is an indeterminate form

Related Terms

  • Synonym: undetermined - not decided or settled; not having a definite value or result

Etymology

From Latin indeterminatus, from in- (not) + determinatus (determined).
