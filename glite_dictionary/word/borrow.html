to borrow

1. VERB

/ ˈbɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɒrəʊ / (BrE)
to take and use something belonging to someone else with the intention of returning it
take something temporarily with intent to return

Examples

Can I borrow your notes for the lecture this afternoon?
She often borrows books from the local library.
He borrowed my umbrella and promised to return it tomorrow.

Synset Examples

May I borrow your lawn mower?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to lend - to give something to someone for a period of time, expecting it to be returned

Etymology

From Old English borgian (to lend, borrow), related to Old High German borgan (to guarantee), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02329022-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈbɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɒrəʊ / (BrE)
to take and use (an idea, method, or custom) from another source as if it were one's own
adopt and use from another source

Examples

The artist decided to borrow elements of cubism for her new work.
Many modern technologies borrow concepts from nature to solve engineering problems.
His speech borrowed heavily from the language of past leaders.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to adopt - to take and use an idea or method as one's own

Etymology

From Old English *borgian* (to borrow, lend), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02351720-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɒrəʊ / (BrE)
to take and use something that belongs to someone else with the intention of returning it
take and use temporarily

Examples

Can I borrow your phone for a moment to make a quick call?
She forgot to return the book she had borrowed from the library.
He is always borrowing tools from his neighbors.

Synset Examples

I’ll let you borrow my car for this once.
My roommate borrowed my book without asking.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to lend - to give something to someone for a period of time, expecting it to be returned

Etymology

From Old English 'borgian' (to borrow, lend), related to 'borg' (pledge, security).
