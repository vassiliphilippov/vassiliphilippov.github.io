realist

1. NOUN

/ ˈriːəlɪst / (AmE), / ˈrɪəlɪst / (BrE)
A philosopher who believes that universals exist independently of the mind.
philosopher believing in universal reality

Examples

Plato is often considered a realist because he believed in the existence of universals.
Medieval debates between the realist and nominalist schools shaped much of Western philosophy.
A realist maintains that concepts like 'redness' have a reality independent of the mind.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominalist - A philosopher who denies the real existence of universals, asserting they exist only as names

Etymology

From French réaliste, from réel (real) + -iste (-ist).

2. NOUN

/ ˈriːəlɪst / (AmE), / ˈrɪə.lɪst / (BrE)
a person who accepts the situation as it is and deals with it in a practical way, without imagination or idealism
practical person; not idealistic

Examples

She's a true realist who always prepares for the worst-case scenario.
He calls himself a realist because he doesn't believe in wishful thinking.
When planning the project, a realist will focus on practical obstacles and solutions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealist - a person guided more by ideals than by practical considerations
  • Synonym: pragmatist - a person who is guided more by practical considerations than by ideals or theories

Etymology

From French réaliste, based on Latin realis (actual).

3. NOUN

/ ˈriːəlɪst / (AmE), / ˈrɪəlɪst / (BrE)
An artist, especially a painter, who depicts subjects in a realistic manner, avoiding idealization or romanticism.
artist depicting reality as it is

Examples

As a committed realist, she painted only what she saw in everyday life.
The gallery features the works of a young realist who refuses to romanticize his subjects.
Many 19th-century realists opposed the exaggerations of the Romantic movement.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: romantic - an artist who focuses on emotion and imagination rather than reality
  • Antonym: idealist - an artist who depicts subjects in an idealized or perfected form rather than realistically

Etymology

From French 'réaliste', from 'réalité' (reality), ultimately from Latin 'realis' (actual).
