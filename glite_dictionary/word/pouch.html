pouch

1. VERB

/ paʊtʃ / (AmE), / paʊtʃ / (BrE)
to put or enclose into a small bag or pouch
put into a small bag

Examples

She carefully pouched the seeds to keep them safe during her hike.
The pharmacist pouched the pills before handing them to the customer.
After collecting the gold coins, he pouched them for the journey.

2. NOUN

/ paʊtʃ / (AmE), / paʊtʃ / (BrE)
a small saclike anatomical structure in certain animals, such as a marsupial's abdominal fold for carrying young or a pelican's gular sac
anatomical sac in animals

Examples

The kangaroo carries its joey in a special pouch on its abdomen.
A pelican stores fish in the large pouch beneath its beak.
Some rodents have cheek pouches for carrying food back to their burrows.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sac - a pouch-like anatomical cavity or structure

Etymology

From Old French pouche (bag), from a Germanic source related to poke.

(of a part of the body or object) to swell or protrude outwards like a pouch.
to swell or bulge outwards

4. VERB

/ paʊtʃ / (AmE), / paʊtʃ / (BrE)
to send something by a special mail service, especially one used for official or diplomatic correspondence
send by official or diplomatic mail

Examples

The embassy will pouch your documents to the home office for secure delivery.
Sensitive information is sometimes pouched to avoid interception by ordinary mail services.
They are pouching critical letters to the capital instead of sending them electronically.

5. NOUN

/ paʊtʃ / (AmE), / paʊtʃ / (BrE)
a small or medium-sized bag or container used for holding or carrying things, typically made of soft material and often closed with a drawstring, flap, or zipper.
small soft bag or container

Examples

She kept her coins in a small leather pouch in her purse.
The kangaroo carries its baby in its abdominal pouch.
He handed me a pouch containing dried herbs and spices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bag - a flexible container used for carrying things
  • Synonym: sack - a large bag made of rough material used for storage or carrying

Etymology

From Old French pouche, possibly of Germanic origin.
