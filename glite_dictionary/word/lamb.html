lamb

1. NOUN

/ læm / (AmE), / læm / (BrE)
the flesh of a young domestic sheep used as food
meat from a young sheep

Examples

She roasted a leg of lamb with garlic and rosemary for dinner.
The restaurant is famous for its grilled lamb chops served with mint sauce.
Doctors recommend avoiding undercooked lamb to prevent foodborne illness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mutton - the flesh of a mature sheep used as food
  • Synonym: veal - meat from a young calf

Etymology

From Old English lamb (young sheep).

2. NOUN

/ læm / (AmE), / læm / (BrE)
A young sheep, especially one that is less than one year old.
young sheep

Examples

The shepherd carried the lost lamb back to the flock.
We visited a farm where several lambs had just been born.
A mother sheep is very protective of her newborn lamb.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: sheep - domesticated ruminant mammal raised for wool and meat

Etymology

Old English *lamb* (young sheep); related to Old Norse *lamb* and German *Lamm*.

Examples

She walked into the negotiation as a lamb, unaware of the tricks they would use.

4. NOUN

/ læm / (AmE), / læm / (BrE)
a gentle, innocent, and mild-mannered person, especially a child
gentle or innocent person

Examples

Everyone loved her because she was such a lamb and never caused any trouble.
He's a real lamb, always trying to help his classmates and never raising his voice.
The teacher described the new student as a lamb due to her gentle nature.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innocent - a person not guilty or free from moral wrong
  • Antonym: wolf - a predatory or aggressive person (in contrast to 'lamb')

Etymology

From Old English 'lamb', referring first to the young sheep, metaphorically extended to mean innocence or gentleness.
