decrescendo

1. NOUN

/ ˌdiː.krəˈʃɛn.doʊ / (AmE), / ˌdeɪ.krəˈʃen.dəʊ / (BrE)
(Music) a gradual decrease in loudness.
gradual decrease in musical loudness

Examples

The orchestra followed the conductor's signal and entered a gentle decrescendo.
The passage is marked with a decrescendo to create a soft, fading effect.
A sudden decrescendo can dramatically alter the mood of the music.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: crescendo - a gradual increase in loudness (music)

Etymology

From Italian 'decrescendo,' present participle of 'decrescere' meaning 'to decrease'.

2. ADJ

/ ˌdi.krəˈʃɛn.doʊ / (AmE), / ˌdiː.krɛˈʃɛn.dəʊ / (BrE)
(in music) gradually decreasing in loudness or intensity; also, a passage performed with diminishing volume.
gradually decreasing in loudness (music)

Examples

The orchestra played the final bars in a beautiful decrescendo.
As the storm faded, the sound of thunder became a gentle decrescendo.
The conductor signaled for a decrescendo as the piece came to a close.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: crescendo - gradually increasing in loudness (music)

Etymology

From Italian 'decrescendo', present participle of 'decrescere' (to decrease).

3. VERB

/ ˌdi.krəˈʃɛn.doʊ / (AmE), / ˌdiː.krɪˈʃɛn.dəʊ / (BrE)
(of sound, especially in music) to gradually become quieter.
grow gradually quieter (music, verb)

Examples

As the storm passed, the thunder decrescendoed into gentle rumbles.
The choir decrescendoed beautifully at the end of the hymn.
After the dramatic climax, the music slowly decrescendoed to a whisper.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: crescendo - to gradually become louder (music, verb)

From Italian 'decrescendo', present participle of 'decrescere' (to decrease).
