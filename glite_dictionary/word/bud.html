bud

1. VERB

/ bʌd / (AmE), / bʌd / (BrE)
(of a plant) to develop or produce buds as part of growth or reproduction.
(of a plant) develop buds

Examples

Every spring, the rose bush begins to bud with new life.
The trees have started to bud after the long winter.
When conditions are right, certain cacti will bud rapidly.

Synset Examples

The hibiscus is budding!

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: blossom - (of a flower or tree) to produce flowers or masses of flowers

Etymology

From Middle English budde, of uncertain origin.

2. NOUN

/ bʌd / (AmE), / bʌd / (BrE)
a small swelling on a plant that develops into a leaf, flower, or shoot
plant growth swelling; develops leaf or flower

Examples

The rose bud opened slowly in the morning sunlight.
In early spring, tiny buds appeared on the bare branches of the tree.
She carefully examined the bud to see if it would bloom soon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shoot - a young branch or growth from a plant
  • Degree Higher: flower - the reproductive structure in flowering plants

Etymology

From Middle English budde (young growth).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13185984-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ bʌd / (AmE), / bʌd / (BrE)
to begin to grow or develop, especially referring to new shoots on a plant or the emergence of something new
begin to grow or develop

Examples

After the long winter, the trees finally began to bud in early spring.
A new idea started to bud in her mind as she listened to the lecture.
Their friendship continued to bud throughout the school year.

Synset Examples

a budding friendship

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sprout - to begin to grow; shoot forth as a plant from a seed
  • Synonym: blossom - to produce flowers; to develop in a promising way

Etymology

From Middle English budde, of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00346306-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ bʌd / (AmE), / bʌd / (BrE)
A small swelling on a plant that develops into a flower, leaf, or shoot, especially when partially opened.
partially opened flower or new growth

Examples

The rose bud opened fully after a few warm days.
She pinned a small bud behind her ear for a fresh, floral scent.
In spring, green buds appear on the trees before the leaves unfurl.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: blossom - a flower, especially one that has fully opened

Etymology

From Middle English budde; origin uncertain, possibly from Old French bouton (button, bud).
