mile

1. NOUN

/ maɪl / (AmE), / maɪl / (BrE)
a footrace covering a distance of one mile (1,609.34 metres), especially in track and field competitions
a one-mile footrace

Examples

She won the gold medal in the mile at the national championships.
He trained for months to improve his time in the mile.
The school track team specializes in both the mile and the 800-meter events.

Synset Examples

he holds the record in the mile

Related Terms

  • Synonym: race - a competition of speed in running, riding, driving, sailing, or rowing

Etymology

From Latin 'milia passuum', meaning 'a thousand paces'.

3. NOUN

/ maɪl / (AmE), / maɪl / (BrE)
a unit of linear measurement equal to 1,760 yards, 5,280 feet, or exactly 1,609.344 meters, chiefly used in the United States and the United Kingdom
unit of length (1,609.344 meters)

Examples

The city is ten miles from here by road.
He can run a mile in just under five minutes.
The nearest town is about three miles away.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: kilometer - a metric unit of length equal to 1,000 meters; approximately 0.621 miles
  • Degree Lower: yard - a unit of length equal to 3 feet or 0.9144 meters

Etymology

From Latin 'milia passuum' (thousand paces).

4. NOUN

/ maɪl / (AmE), / maɪl / (BrE)
A unit of length used in navigation, equal to exactly 1,852 meters; also called a nautical mile.
unit of length (navigation), 1,852 meters

Examples

The ship was reported to be five miles offshore, according to the navigator.
Modern aircraft calculate their speed in nautical miles per hour, rather than kilometers.
One mile on the sea equals exactly 1,852 meters.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nautical mile - a unit of length equal to 1,852 meters used in maritime and air navigation

Etymology

From Latin 'mille' ('thousand'), originally referring to a thousand paces; nautical sense based on distance spanned by one minute of arc in latitude.
