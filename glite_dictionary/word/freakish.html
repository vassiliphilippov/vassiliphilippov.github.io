freakish

1. ADJ

/ ˈfriː.kɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfriː.kɪʃ / (BrE)
Very strange, abnormal, or unusual, often in a way that seems unnatural or unexpected.
very strange or abnormal

Examples

The storm took on a freakish intensity, causing unexpected damage throughout the city.
She was born with a freakish talent for mathematics that astounded her teachers.
The sculpture had a freakish appearance that drew mixed reactions from visitors.

Synset Examples

a freakish extra toe

Related Terms

  • Synonym: odd - unusual or strange
  • Antonym: normal - conforming to a standard; usual, typical, or expected

Etymology

From 'freak' + '-ish', denoting characteristic of or resembling a freak.

2. ADJ

/ ˈfriːkɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfriːkɪʃ / (BrE)
Unusual or abnormal; unpredictably changeable in a way that is strange or extreme.
strangely unpredictable or abnormal

Examples

The freakish storm caused unexpected damage across the city.
Sudden and freakish changes in temperature made the climate unpredictable.
They were caught off guard by the freakish turn in the weather.

Synset Examples

a capricious summer breeze
freakish weather

Related Terms

  • Synonym: capricious - given to sudden and unaccountable changes of mood or behavior
  • Synonym: unpredictable - not able to be predicted; changeable
  • Antonym: normal - conforming to the usual or typical standard

Etymology

From "freak" (an unusual event or person) + "-ish" (forming adjectives).

Source: WordNet

3. ADJ

/ ˈfriːkɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈfriːkɪʃ / (BrE)
very unusual, strange, or abnormal, especially in a way that attracts attention.
very unusual or abnormal

Examples

The tornado that hit the town was a freakish event for this region.
Her style has a freakish charm that draws a lot of attention.
A freakish combination of weather patterns led to record snowfall.

Synset Examples

outre and affected stage antics

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bizarre - very strange or unusual
  • Synonym: outlandish - looking or sounding bizarre or unfamiliar
  • Antonym: normal - conforming to a standard; usual, typical, or expected

Etymology

From "freak" + "-ish"; "freak" possibly from Middle English "freke" (brave man, warrior).
