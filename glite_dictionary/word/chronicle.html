chronicle

1. VERB

/ ˈkrɑː.nɪ.kəl / (AmE), / ˈkrɒn.ɪ.kəl / (BrE)
to record events in the order in which they happened; to make a historical record
record events in order; make a historical record

Examples

The historian set out to chronicle the events leading to the revolution in meticulous detail.
Journalists chronicle major disasters so future generations can learn from them.
Her diary chronicles her journey across four continents over ten years.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: record - to set down in writing or some other permanent form for later reference

Etymology

From Greek 'khronika' (annals), from 'khronos' (time), via Latin 'chronica'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkrɑːnɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈkrɒnɪkəl / (BrE)
A factual written account of important or historical events in the order they occurred; a record or narrative of past events.
record or narrative of past events

Examples

The ancient chronicle detailed the rise and fall of the forgotten kingdom.
Her memoir acts as a personal chronicle of her experiences during the war.
The museum preserves a chronicle of local traditions and festivals.

Synset Examples

a history of France
he gave an inaccurate account of the plot to kill the president
the story of exposure to lead

Related Terms

  • Synonym: history - a continuous, typically chronological, record of important or public events
  • Synonym: annals - a historical record of events, arranged year by year
  • Degree Lower: biography - an account of someone's life written by someone else

Etymology

From Old French *cronicle*, based on Latin *chronica* (annals), from Greek *khronika* (annals), from *khronos* (time).
