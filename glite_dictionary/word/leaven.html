leaven

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvən / (AmE), / ˈlɛvən / (BrE)
a substance, such as yeast or baking powder, that causes dough or liquid to ferment and rise
substance that makes dough rise

Examples

Yeast is a common leaven used in bread making.
Too much leaven can make the dough rise too quickly and collapse.
She added the leaven and waited patiently for the dough to rise.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yeast - a common leavening agent used in baking

Etymology

From Latin 'levamen' (alleviation), related to 'levare' (to raise).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɛvən / (AmE), / ˈlɛvən / (BrE)
a quality or influence that lightens, modifies, or enlivens something, especially in a subtle or indirect way
a lightening or modifying influence

Examples

Her witty remarks acted as a leaven in the otherwise serious meeting.
The new manager brought a leaven of innovation to the team.
A leaven of hope spread throughout the community during difficult times.

Synset Examples

his sermons benefited from a leavening of humor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: influence - the effect one thing has on another

Etymology

From Old French 'levain', from Latin 'levamen' (alleviation), from 'levare' (to raise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05878029-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈlɛvən / (AmE), / ˈliːv(ə)n / (BrE)
to cause (dough or batter) to rise and become light by adding a leavening agent such as yeast or baking powder
cause dough to rise with leaven

Examples

Add yeast to leaven the dough before baking.
Bakers often leaven bread to achieve a soft, airy texture.
If you forget to leaven the batter, the cake will turn out dense.

Synset Examples

unleavened bread

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unleavened - not leavened; made without a raising agent

Etymology

From Old French 'levain' (leaven), from Latin 'levamen' (means of lifting up), from 'levare' (to lift).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
