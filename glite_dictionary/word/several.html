several

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɛvɚəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛv(ə)rəl / (BrE)
Considered as distinct and individual; separate; respective.
separate; individual; respective
archaic

Examples

Each defendant had a several responsibility for the debt.
The contract listed the several parties to the agreement.
The property was divided into several parcels, each owned by a different person.

Synset Examples

three several times

Etymology

From Latin 'separalis' (separate), from 'separare' (to separate).

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɛvrəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛv(ə)rəl / (BrE)
More than two or three but not many; an indefinite small number, used with countable nouns.
more than two or three, but not many

Examples

Several books were stacked on the table in the corner of the room.
Several students stayed after class to ask questions about the assignment.
The museum displayed several paintings from the 19th century.

Synset Examples

several letters came in the mail
several people were injured in the accident

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: few - a small number of; not many
  • Degree Higher: many - a large number of

Etymology

From Late Latin 'separalis' (separate), from Latin 'separare' (to separate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02276025-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈsɛvɚəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛvərəl / (BrE)
Considered individually; respective to each person or thing mentioned.
individually; respective

Examples

Each specialist contributed knowledge from their several fields to the discussion.
The two brothers inherited their several shares of the estate.
The committee members prepared reports in their several areas of expertise.

Synset Examples

the respective club members
specialists in their several fields
the various reports all agreed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: respective - belonging separately to each of two or more people or things
  • Synonym: various - of different kinds; several and different

Etymology

From Latin separalis (separate, different), via Old French several.
