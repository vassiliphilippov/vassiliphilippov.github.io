ordeal

1. NOUN

/ ɔrˈdil / (AmE), / ɔːˈdiːl / (BrE)
(historical) A method of determining guilt or innocence by subjecting the accused to dangerous or painful tests, with judgment believed to be decided by divine intervention.
historical test of guilt by painful trial

Examples

During the Middle Ages, an ordeal by water was sometimes used to determine a person's guilt.
The accused faced an ordeal of fire, believing divine forces would protect the innocent.
Passing the ordeal often meant surviving a dangerous test unharmed.

Etymology

From Old English *ordāl* (judgment, trial), related to German *Urteil* (judgment).

2. NOUN

/ ɔɹˈdil / (AmE), / ɔːˈdiːl / (BrE)
a very difficult or unpleasant experience, especially one that lasts for a long time
severe or trying experience

Examples

After recovering from her illness, she described the hospital stay as a terrifying ordeal.
The long climb in the heat proved to be a real ordeal for the hikers.
He went through the ordeal of being questioned for hours by the police.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trial - a test of endurance or patience
  • Synonym: test - a situation that reveals a person's abilities or character
  • Antonym: pleasure - a feeling of happy satisfaction and enjoyment

Etymology

From Old English ordāl (trial, judgment), from Proto-Germanic *uzdailijan (to share out, allot).
