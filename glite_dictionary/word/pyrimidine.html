pyrimidine

1. NOUN

/ pɪˈrɪməˌdin / (AmE), / pɪˈrɪmɪˌdiːn / (BrE)
a heterocyclic organic compound with a single six-membered ring containing two nitrogen atoms at positions 1 and 3, notable as a structural component of nucleic acids
heterocyclic organic compound with two nitrogen atoms
medical

Examples

Pyrimidine is one of the building blocks of DNA and RNA.
Chemists often study pyrimidine derivatives because of their pharmaceutical applications.
The structure of pyrimidine features two nitrogen atoms in a six-membered ring.

Etymology

From pyridine, with -mide inserted, based on the nitrogen atom positions; coined in late 19th century scientific Latin.

2. NOUN

/ pɪˈrɪmɪˌdin / (AmE), / pɪˈrɪmɪdiːn / (BrE)
a heterocyclic organic compound with a six-membered ring containing two nitrogen atoms at positions 1 and 3, and any of its derivatives
heterocyclic compound in chemistry/biology
medical

Examples

Uracil is a pyrimidine base found in RNA.
The structure of a pyrimidine ring is similar to that of benzene, except for the nitrogen atoms.
Thymine and cytosine are two common pyrimidines found in DNA.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: purine - another nitrogen-containing heterocyclic compound, major base in nucleic acids

Etymology

From German Pyrimidin, influenced by pyridine and amidine.
