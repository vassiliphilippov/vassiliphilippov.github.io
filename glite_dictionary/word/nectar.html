nectar

1. NOUN

/ ˈnɛktɚ / (AmE), / ˈnɛktə / (BrE)
(in classical mythology) the drink or food of the gods, said to bring immortality to mortals who consumed it.
drink/food of the gods (mythology)

Examples

According to Greek myths, only the gods were allowed to consume nectar on Mount Olympus.
The hero tempted fate by tasting nectar at the gods’ banquet.
Legends say that drinking nectar could grant a mortal eternal life.

Etymology

From Greek nektar, the drink of the gods.

2. NOUN

/ ˈnɛktɚ / (AmE), / ˈnɛktə / (BrE)
a sweet liquid extracted from fruit, especially when it is undiluted; fruit juice that is thick and concentrated
concentrated fruit juice

Examples

She served fresh peach nectar with breakfast.
The company only uses pure mango nectar in their smoothies.
Grape nectar is often thicker and sweeter than regular grape juice.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: juice - the natural liquid extracted from fruits or vegetables

Etymology

From Greek nektar (the drink of the gods in mythology).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07940516-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈnɛktər / (AmE), / ˈnɛktə / (BrE)
a sweet liquid produced by flowers to attract pollinators such as bees and birds
sweet liquid from flowers for pollinators

Examples

Bees collect nectar from flowers to make honey.
Some flowers produce more nectar to attract butterflies and hummingbirds.
The scent of nectar draws many insects to the garden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: honey - a sweet, viscous food substance made by bees using nectar

Etymology

From Greek nektar (the drink of the gods).
