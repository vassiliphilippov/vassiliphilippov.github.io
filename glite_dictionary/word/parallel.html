parallel

1. ADJ

/ ˈpærəˌlɛl / (AmE), / ˈpærəlɛl / (BrE)
(of computing or processes) relating to the simultaneous performance of multiple operations.
simultaneous processing/computing

Examples

Many modern computers use parallel processing to speed up computations.
Researchers are developing parallel algorithms to solve complex problems more efficiently.
A parallel system can handle multiple tasks at the same time.

Synset Examples

parallel processing

Related Terms

  • Antonym: serial - performed one after another, not simultaneously (opposite in computing context)

Etymology

From Greek 'parallēlos', meaning 'beside one another'.

WordNet ID: oewn-02387725-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ ˈpærəˌlɛl / (AmE), / ˈparəlɛl / (BrE)
An imaginary line around the Earth that is parallel to the equator, used to indicate latitude.
imaginary line around Earth (latitude)

Examples

The 49th parallel marks part of the border between the United States and Canada.
Explorers used the 30th parallel as a reference point for their journey.
Many cities are located along the same parallel, experiencing similar climates.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: latitude - the angular distance north or south of the equator, measured in degrees
  • Antonym: meridian - an imaginary line on Earth's surface from pole to pole, used to measure longitude

Etymology

From Latin 'parallelus', from Greek 'parallēlos' (alongside one another).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈpærəˌlɛl / (AmE), / ˈparəlɛl / (BrE)
(Mathematics) A line or plane that is equally distant from another at all points and never meets it, even if extended indefinitely.

Examples

In geometry, parallels never intersect, no matter how far they extend.
The architect drew several parallels to illustrate the design of the floor plan.
On the globe, the lines of latitude are often called parallels.
