to isolate

1. VERB

/ ˈaɪ.səˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈaɪ.sə.leɪt / (BrE)
to obtain a substance or organism in a pure form, separate from others, especially for scientific purposes.
obtain in pure form (science)

Examples

The researchers were able to isolate the virus from the sample.
To conduct accurate tests, it is necessary to isolate the bacteria first.
She worked for months to isolate the active compound found in the plant.

Synset Examples

The chemist managed to isolate the compound

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to purify - to make free from impurities or contaminants

Etymology

From Latin isolatus, from insula (island).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00497851-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈaɪ.səˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈaɪ.sə.leɪt / (BrE)
to separate someone or something from others so that they are alone or kept apart
separate from others; set apart

Examples

The doctor will isolate the infected patient to prevent the disease from spreading.
Researchers often isolate bacteria in a lab to study them more closely.
During the experiment, she isolated one variable to observe its specific effect.

Synset Examples

The dentist sequesters the tooth he is working on

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separate - to set or keep apart
  • Antonym: to integrate - to combine or bring together into a whole

Etymology

From French 'isoler', from Italian 'isolare', from 'isola' (island), from Latin 'insula'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00496744-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈaɪ.səˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈaɪ.sə.leɪt / (BrE)
to place or set apart from others; to separate someone or something from the rest
to set apart; separate

Examples

The scientists decided to isolate the virus to study its properties.
Prison officials may isolate inmates who pose a threat to others.
She felt the need to isolate herself to concentrate on her writing.

Synset Examples

They isolated the political prisoners from the other inmates

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separate - to set or keep apart from others
  • Antonym: to integrate - to combine into a whole; bring together

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'isolatus', past participle of 'insulare' (to make into an island), from Latin 'insula' (island).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00495205-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈaɪsəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈaɪsəleɪt / (BrE)
to separate experiences from the emotions associated with them, often as a psychological defense mechanism
separate experiences from their emotions (psychology)

Examples

Some people isolate traumatic memories from how they feel about them.
Therapists may help patients who isolate their emotions from past incidents.
He learned to isolate unpleasant experiences, keeping the feelings locked away.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dissociate - to disconnect or separate (used in psychology, e.g. dissociate feelings from events)

Etymology

From Latin isolatus, past participle of insulare (to make into an island).

Source: WordNet
