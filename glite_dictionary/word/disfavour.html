disfavour

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈfeɪvər / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
An attitude of disapproval or a lack of support for someone or something.
disapproval; lack of support

Examples

The candidate's controversial remarks placed him in public disfavour.
She noticed the disfavour in her colleagues' attitudes after the meeting.
Falling sales led to the company's disfavour among shareholders.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: favor - approval, support, or liking for someone or something

Etymology

From the prefix dis- (expressing reversal) + favour, from Old French faveur.

2. NOUN

/ dɪsˈfeɪvər / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
The state of being regarded with disapproval or lack of approval; being out of favor.
lack of favor or approval
british

Examples

After the scandal, the minister quickly fell into disfavour with party leaders.
Wearing casual clothes was frowned upon and could put you in disfavour at the exclusive club.
His radical ideas brought him into disfavour with the academic community.

Synset Examples

he is in disfavor with the king

Related Terms

  • Antonym: favor - approval, support, or liking for someone or something
  • Synonym: disfavor - lack of approval or support (American spelling)

Etymology

From dis- (expressing reversal) + favour; modelled after 'disfavor'.

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈfeɪvər / (AmE), / dɪsˈfeɪvə / (BrE)
to put someone or something at a disadvantage; to hinder or harm
put at a disadvantage; hinder

Examples

The new policy may disfavour smaller companies in the market.
Strict visa requirements can disfavour international students seeking admission.
She argued that the proposal would disfavour rural communities.

Synset Examples

This rule clearly disadvantages me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disadvantage - to put in an unfavorable position; to harm
  • Antonym: favour - to support or give advantage to

Etymology

From Old French *desfaveur*, from Latin *dis-* (apart, away) + *favorem* (favour).
