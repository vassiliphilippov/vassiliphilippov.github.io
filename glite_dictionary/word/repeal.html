repeal

1. VERB

/ rɪˈpiːl / (AmE), / rɪˈpiːl / (BrE)
to officially cancel a law or rule, so that it is no longer in effect
formally cancel a law or rule

Examples

The government decided to repeal the outdated law after much debate.
Several activists are campaigning to repeal the new tax regulations.
Congress overwhelmingly voted to repeal the controversial measure.

Synset Examples

He revoked the ban on smoking
lift an embargo
vacate a death sentence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: revoke - to officially cancel a decision, order, or law
  • Antonym: to enact - to make a bill into law
  • Synonym: to abolish - to formally put an end to a system or practice

Etymology

From Old French *rapeler* (to recall), from Latin *repellere* (to drive back).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈpil / (AmE), / rɪˈpiːl / (BrE)
the act of officially cancelling a law, rule, or agreement
the official cancellation of a law or rule
legal

Examples

The repeal of the old law was welcomed by many civil rights groups.
After much debate, Parliament finally voted for the repeal of the controversial tax.
The new government promised the immediate repeal of outdated regulations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abrogation - the act of abolishing by official means
  • Antonym: enactment - the process of making into law

Etymology

From Old French 'rapel' (calling back), from 'rapeler', from Latin 're-' (back) + 'appellare' (to call).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00232754-n
Synset POS: n
