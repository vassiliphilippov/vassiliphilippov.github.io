exempt

1. ADJ

/ ɪgˈzɛmpt / (AmE), / ɪgˈzɛmpt / (BrE)
(Of goods, funds, etc.) not required to pay tax or not liable to taxation.
not subject to taxation

Examples

Nonprofit organizations may have exempt status for certain types of income.
Certain charitable donations are exempt from local taxes.
Interest earned on municipal bonds is often exempt from federal taxation.

Synset Examples

the funds of nonprofit organizations are nontaxable
income exempt from taxation

Related Terms

  • Antonym: taxable - subject to taxation

Etymology

From Latin 'exemptus', past participle of 'eximere' (to take out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02409653-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ɪgˈzɛmpt / (AmE), / ɪgˈzɛmpt / (BrE)
(of a person or thing) free from an obligation or liability to which others are subject.
free from an obligation or duty

Examples

Children under the age of five are exempt from ticket charges.
Certain medical conditions may make you exempt from military service.
She was exempt from the final exam because of her outstanding grades.

Synset Examples

a beauty somehow exempt from the aging process
exempt from jury duty
only the very poorest citizens should be exempt from income taxes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: liable - subject to an obligation or duty

Etymology

From Latin 'exemptus', past participle of 'eximere' (to take out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02373965-a
Synset POS: a

3. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzɛmpt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɛmpt / (BrE)
to officially free someone from an obligation or duty that others have to fulfill
officially free from a duty or obligation

Examples

The new law will exempt small businesses from paying the new tax.
Students with medical conditions can be exempted from physical education classes.
His long service exempts him from the usual training requirements.

Synset Examples

Please excuse me from this class

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excuse - to release from a duty or requirement
  • Antonym: to require - to demand as necessary

Etymology

From Latin 'exemptus' (taken out, freed), past participle of 'eximere' (to take out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00895399-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzɛmpt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɛmpt / (BrE)
to officially free someone from an obligation or rule that others are required to follow
to free from a rule or obligation

Examples

Many students were exempted from the final project due to their excellent attendance.
The new policy exempts small businesses from certain taxes.
She argued that her condition should exempt her from jury duty.

Synset Examples

She exempted me from the exam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excuse - to release from a duty or requirement
  • Antonym: to oblige - to require someone to do something by law or rule

Etymology

From Latin exemptus, past participle of eximere ('to take out'), from ex- ('out') + emere ('take').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02570061-v
Synset POS: v
