omnibus

1. NOUN

/ ˈɑːmnɪˌbʌs / (AmE), / ˈɒmnɪbəs / (BrE)
a volume containing several works, articles, or stories, often by a single author or on a single subject
collection of works in one volume

Examples

The publisher released an omnibus edition of all the author's short stories.
She bought an omnibus of mystery novels for her summer reading.
The omnibus contains articles that originally appeared in several different magazines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anthology - a published collection of poems or other pieces of writing

Etymology

From Latin 'omnibus', dative plural of 'omnis' (all), originally meaning 'for all'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06605048-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈɑːmnɪˌbʌs / (AmE), / ˈɒmnɪbəs / (BrE)
a large vehicle designed to carry many passengers, especially one used for public transport; now mainly historical or literary usage for a bus
public transport vehicle (historical)

Examples

In Victorian London, the omnibus was a common sight along the busy streets.
She boarded an early morning omnibus to begin her daily commute.
Historical novels often mention the omnibus as a primary means of transport.

Synset Examples

he always rode the bus to work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bus - a large road vehicle for carrying passengers

Etymology

From Latin 'omnibus' (for all), originally used in French as 'voiture omnibus'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02927500-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈɑːm.nɪ.bəs / (AmE), / ˈɒm.nɪ.bəs / (BrE)
(of a law, bill, or document) dealing with or providing for many different items or matters at once, often as a single package.
dealing with many things at once

Examples

The government introduced an omnibus bill that addressed healthcare, education, and infrastructure reform all at once.
An omnibus law can be efficient but often makes legislation harder to review.
They opposed the omnibus proposal, arguing it was too broad and covered unrelated issues.

Synset Examples

an omnibus law

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comprehensive - including all or nearly all elements or aspects

Etymology

From Latin 'omnibus', dative plural of 'omnis' (all).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00530246-s
Synset POS: s
