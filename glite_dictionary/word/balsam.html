balsam

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɑːl.səm / (AmE), / ˈbɔːl.səm / (BrE)
Any of several seed plants that yield a resinous, aromatic substance used in medicine and perfumery.
plant yielding aromatic resin

Examples

The balsam produces a fragrant resin valued in traditional medicine.
Some species of balsam are cultivated for their ornamental flowers as well as their aromatic gum.
Collectors carefully tap the balsam trees to extract the precious sap.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: balm - an aromatic ointment or resin (balm)

Etymology

From Latin 'balsamum', from Greek 'balsamon', referring to the balsam tree.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɑːl.səm / (AmE), / ˈbɔːl.səm / (BrE)
An aromatic ointment or resin, often used for healing or soothing purposes.
aromatic ointment or resin

Examples

She applied a soothing balsam to the burn on her arm.
The fragrance of the balsam filled the small apothecary shop.
Ancient healers often used balsam to treat wounds and skin irritations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ointment - a smooth substance applied to the skin for medicinal purposes
  • Synonym: balm - a fragrant ointment or preparation used to heal or soothe the skin

Etymology

From Latin 'balsamum', from Greek 'balsamon', referring to the balsam tree or its resin.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɑːlsəm / (AmE), / ˈbɔːlsəm / (BrE)
a fragrant resinous substance obtained from certain trees and plants, used in medicines and perfumes
fragrant resin from trees used in medicine or perfume

Examples

The balsam extracted from this tree is highly valued in traditional medicine.
She added a few drops of balsam to the perfume to enhance its fragrance.
Some ointments contain natural balsam for their soothing properties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: resin - a sticky organic substance exuded by some trees
  • Synonym: gum - a plant exudate used similarly to resins and balsams

Etymology

From Latin 'balsamum', from Greek 'balsamon', probably of Semitic origin.
