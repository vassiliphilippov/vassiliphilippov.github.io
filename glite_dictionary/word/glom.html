to glom

1. VERB

/ ɡlɑm / (AmE), / ɡlɒm / (BrE)
to take hold of or seize something, often eagerly or with determination
seize or latch onto (something)
informal american

Examples

She managed to glom the last piece of cake before anyone else.
The company quickly glommed onto the new technology to stay ahead of competitors.
He tends to glom onto every trend that comes along.

Synset Examples

The Republicans glommed onto Whitewater

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grab - to take hold of suddenly or quickly
  • Synonym: snatch - to seize quickly and often roughly

Etymology

From Scots *glam* (to snatch, grab), influenced by Yiddish *glomern* (to steal).

2. VERB

/ ɡlɑm / (AmE), / ɡlɒm / (BrE)
(informal) To steal or take something, especially in a sneaky way.
to steal; to take (informal)
informal american

Examples

Someone tried to glom my bag while I was distracted on the subway.
He glommed the pen off my desk when nobody was looking.
If you leave your snacks out, someone will probably glom them.

Synset Examples

Someone snitched my wallet!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steal - to take (another person's property) without permission or legal right
  • Synonym: snatch - quickly seize something in a forceful or abrupt way

Etymology

Probably from Scots 'glam' (to snatch), influenced by Yiddish 'glomen' (to steal).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02327226-v
Synset POS: v
