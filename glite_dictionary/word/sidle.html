to sidle

1. VERB

/ ˈsaɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈsaɪdəl / (BrE)
to walk or move in a shy or stealthy way, often sideways or with caution, so as not to attract attention
move unobtrusively or stealthily

Examples

The cat began to sidle along the edge of the fence, hoping not to be seen.
She tried to sidle out of the crowded room without drawing attention to herself.
He would often sidle up to his boss with suggestions during coffee breaks.

Synset Examples

The young man began to sidle near the pretty girl sitting on the log

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creep - to move slowly and quietly, especially to avoid being noticed
  • Degree Lower: amble - to walk at a slow, relaxed pace

Etymology

Probably from Middle English, from a derivative of 'side' (noun), referring to moving sideways.

2. VERB

/ ˈsaɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈsaɪdəl / (BrE)
to move, especially in a furtive or unobtrusive way, sideways or with one side leading
move sideways or furtively

Examples

The cat began to sidle along the wall, careful not to be seen.
She tried to sidle up to her friend to whisper a secret.
Without a word, he sidled into the room and sat down quietly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slide - to move smoothly along a surface
  • Antonym: stride - to walk with long decisive steps

Etymology

Probably from 'side' plus diminutive or frequentative suffix '-le'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01873189-v
Synset POS: v
