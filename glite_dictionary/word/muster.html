muster

1. VERB

/ ˈmʌstər / (AmE), / ˈmʌstə / (BrE)
to gather or bring together people, resources, or qualities, especially for a particular purpose; to summon up (a feeling, quality, or response)
gather or summon up

Examples

She tried to muster the confidence to speak in front of the audience.
The general had to muster his troops before the sunrise attack.
He couldn't muster enough support for his proposal.

Synset Examples

muster the courage to do something
she rallied her intellect
Summon all your courage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assemble - to gather together in one place
  • Antonym: to disperse - to scatter or break up a group

Etymology

From Old French *mostrer* (to show), from Latin *monstrare* (to show, indicate).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌstɚ / (AmE), / ˈmʌstə / (BrE)
a gathering of troops or military personnel for inspection, roll-call, or duty
military gathering for duty

Examples

All soldiers are expected to report for muster at 0600 hours sharp.
Failure to attend muster can result in disciplinary action within the military.
The commander called a muster to brief the troops on their next mission.

Synset Examples

he was thrown in the brig for missing muster

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assembly - a group of people gathered together, typically for a particular purpose
  • Antonym: disbandment - the act of breaking up or dissolving a group, especially military personnel

Etymology

From Middle English 'mustren', from Old French 'moustrer', from Latin 'monstrare' (to show).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08496626-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈmʌstɚ / (AmE), / ˈmʌstə / (BrE)
to assemble or call up people for duty, especially for military service or an official obligation
assemble for service or duty

Examples

The sergeant will muster the soldiers at dawn for inspection.
Citizens were mustered for jury duty by official summons.
At a moment's notice, the team can be mustered in case of emergency.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to summon - to call people to appear or be present, especially officially
  • Antonym: to dismiss - to send away or release from duty or service

Etymology

From Old French 'mostrer' (to assemble), from Latin 'monstrare' (to show).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00794933-v
Synset POS: v
