boil

1. NOUN

/ bɔɪl / (AmE), / bɔɪl / (BrE)
a painful, swollen area on the skin that is caused by a bacterial infection and filled with pus
painful, pus-filled sore on the skin

Examples

She had a large boil on her arm that needed medical treatment.
The doctor lanced the boil to drain the pus and reduce the pain.
Poor hygiene can sometimes cause a boil to develop on the skin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abscess - a swollen area within body tissue containing pus

Etymology

From Old French *boillir* (to boil), from Latin *bullīre* (to bubble, boil), referring to the swelling's resemblance to something bubbling.

2. NOUN

/ bɔɪl / (AmE), / bɔɪl / (BrE)
the temperature at which a liquid changes to a vapor, especially at sea level; the point at which a liquid boils
the boiling temperature of a liquid

Examples

Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat and simmer for ten minutes.
The soup started to bubble as it neared a boil.
Milk will rise quickly once it reaches a boil, so watch carefully.

Synset Examples

they brought the water to a boil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boiling point - the temperature at which a liquid boils and turns to vapor

3. VERB

/ bɔɪl / (AmE), / bɔɪl / (BrE)
(of a liquid or a surface) to be agitated by bubbling or swirling violently, especially due to heat or turbulence.
be agitated or turbulent (liquid/surface)

Examples

The river began to boil after the heavy rains, making crossing dangerous.
Emotions seemed to boil beneath the surface of the heated debate.
The water in the pot will boil if you leave it on high heat for too long.

Synset Examples

the sea was churning in the storm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: churn - (of a liquid) move or shake violently
  • Antonym: calm - (of a surface or liquid) not agitated or disturbed

Etymology

From Old French *boillir*, from Latin *bullīre* (to bubble, to boil).

7. VERB
