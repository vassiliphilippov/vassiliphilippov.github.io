to interrelate

1. VERB

/ ˌɪntərɪˈleɪt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəriˈleɪt / (BrE)
to place or show things in a mutual or reciprocal relationship with each other
place in mutual relationship

Examples

It is important to interrelate different concepts when studying complex subjects.
The report failed to interrelate the financial data with the company's strategic objectives.
Scientists often interrelate their findings to develop a broader understanding of the phenomenon.

Synset Examples

I cannot interrelate these two events

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relate - to show or make a connection between things

Etymology

From 'inter-' (between) + 'relate' (to bring into relation), first attested in the early 20th century.

2. VERB

/ ˌɪn.tər.rɪˈleɪt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.tə.rɪˈleɪt / (BrE)
to have or show a mutual relationship or connection with one another
have a mutual relationship

Examples

The two theories interrelate in several fundamental ways.
It is important to understand how culture and language interrelate within a society.
Economic and social factors often interrelate, influencing policy decisions.

Synset Examples

How are these two observations related?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relate - show or have a connection with
  • Antonym: disconnect - to separate or have no relationship

Etymology

From prefix 'inter-' (between) + 'relate'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02730292-v
Synset POS: v
