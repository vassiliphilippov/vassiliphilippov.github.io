rhetoric

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɛt̬ərɪk / (AmE), / ˈrɛtərɪk / (BrE)
The art of using language effectively to influence or persuade people, especially through speaking or writing.
effective or persuasive use of language

Examples

The politician's rhetoric was designed to inspire hope among his supporters.
She studied ancient rhetoric to improve her own persuasive speaking skills.
Critics accused the company of empty rhetoric instead of real action on climate change.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oratory - skill or eloquence in public speaking
  • Synonym: eloquence - fluent or persuasive speaking or writing
  • Antonym: plain speaking - speaking in a direct, clear, and straightforward manner

Etymology

From Greek *rhētorikē* (art of oratory), via Latin *rhetorica*.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛtəˌrɪk / (AmE), / ˈrɛtərɪk / (BrE)
language that is loud, confused, or empty, and lacks meaningful content or substance, especially used to impress or persuade.
loud or empty language without real meaning

Examples

The politician's promises were dismissed as mere rhetoric by the public.
He accused his opponent of relying on empty rhetoric rather than facts.
All the heated rhetoric during the debate failed to offer real solutions.

Synset Examples

mere rhetoric

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bluster - empty, pretentious talk or boasting
  • Synonym: grandiloquence - pompous or extravagant language
  • Antonym: substance - meaningful or important content in speech or writing

Etymology

From Greek 'rhetorike' (art of oratory), via Latin 'rhetorica'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈrɛt̬ərɪk / (AmE), / ˈrɛtərɪk / (BrE)
the art or study of using language effectively and persuasively, especially in public speaking
art of effective or persuasive language

Examples

She studied rhetoric at university to improve her public speaking skills.
The politician’s mastery of rhetoric helped him persuade the audience.
Ancient philosophers wrote treatises on the rules and principles of rhetoric.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oratory - the art of public speaking
  • Synonym: eloquence - fluent or persuasive speaking or writing

Etymology

From Greek rhētorikē (art of oratory), from rhētōr (orator, speaker).

4. NOUN

/ ˈrɛtərɪk / (AmE), / ˈrɛtərɪk / (BrE)
language that is elaborate, pretentious, or over-ornate, often with little real meaning or sincerity.
excessively ornate or grand language

Examples

The politician's speech was full of rhetoric but lacked real substance.
Despite the rhetoric of the advertisement, the product was rather ordinary.
Her writing relies heavily on rhetoric rather than clear argument.

Synset Examples

an excessive ornateness of language

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grandiloquence - pompous or extravagant language
  • Antonym: plainness - the quality of being simple and straightforward in style

Etymology

From Latin 'rhetorica', from Greek 'rhētorikē' (art of oratory).
