diction

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪkʃən / (AmE), / ˈdɪkʃən / (BrE)
the clarity and distinctness with which words are pronounced in speech, affecting how easily the audience can understand them
clarity and pronunciation of speech

Examples

The actor's excellent diction made every word easy to understand.
Teachers often emphasize the importance of diction in public speaking.
He worked with a coach to improve his diction before the debate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enunciation - clear and precise pronunciation of words
  • Synonym: articulation - the act of pronouncing words clearly

Etymology

From Latin 'dictio' (saying, speech), from 'dicere' (to say).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɪkʃən / (AmE), / ˈdɪkʃən / (BrE)
the choice and use of words in speech or writing; the manner in which something is expressed verbally or in text
choice or style of words in expression

Examples

The poet's diction is both simple and powerful, making the emotions easy to feel.
She was praised for her clear diction during the debate, which helped her arguments stand out.
A formal diction is often used in academic essays to convey seriousness and precision.

Synset Examples

use concise military verbiage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wording - the choice of words used to express something
  • Synonym: phrasing - the way in which something is phrased or worded

Etymology

From Latin 'dictio' (a saying, expression), from 'dicere' (to say).
