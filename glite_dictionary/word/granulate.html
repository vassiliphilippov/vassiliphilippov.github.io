to granulate

1. VERB

/ ˈɡræn.jə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡran.jʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
to form into small grains or particles; to make or become grainy or granular.
form into grains or granules

Examples

The machine is used to granulate the raw plastic before it is molded.
Some medications granulate during manufacturing to aid in controlled release.
As the sugar cooled, it began to granulate and turn into crystals.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: powder - reduce to fine particles

Etymology

From Latin granulum (small grain), from granum (grain).

2. VERB

/ ˈɡræn.jəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡran.jʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
(of a wound or ulcer) to form new tissue made up of tiny granules during healing.
form granulating tissue (medical)
medical

Examples

The patient's ulcer began to granulate within a week of proper treatment.
Proper wound care ensures that tissues granulate and heal more effectively.
If the edges of the wound granulate well, recovery is usually faster.

Synset Examples

wounds and ulcers can granulate

Etymology

From Latin granulum (small grain), referring to the granule-like tissue formed in healing wounds.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00099128-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɡræn.jə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡran.jʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
to form or become grainy or granular in texture or appearance
to become grainy or granular

Examples

As the syrup cools, it will granulate if left undisturbed.
Certain salts granulate when moisture is removed from them.
The soap mixture began to granulate as it hardened in the mold.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: powder - to reduce to fine, dry particles
  • Degree Higher: coagulate - to change from a fluid into a thickened mass

Etymology

From Latin granulum (small grain), diminutive of granum (grain).
