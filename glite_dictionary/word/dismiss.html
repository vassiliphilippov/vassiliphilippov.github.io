to dismiss

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
(of a court or judge) to decide that a legal case or charge should not be continued or considered further.
end or reject a legal case
legal

Examples

The judge decided to dismiss the case due to insufficient evidence.
The motion to dismiss was granted in the courtroom this morning.
If no new facts are presented, the court may dismiss the complaint.

Synset Examples

This case is dismissed!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reject - refuse to accept or consider
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00803288-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
to remove someone from their job; to terminate the employment of someone
remove from employment

Examples

After repeated warnings, the company decided to dismiss the employee for poor performance.
The manager had to dismiss several staff members due to budget cuts.
If you violate company policy, they may dismiss you immediately.

Synset Examples

The boss fired his secretary today
The company terminated 25% of its workers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fire - to dismiss someone from their job, especially suddenly
  • Synonym: to lay off - to end the employment of workers for economic reasons
  • Antonym: hire - to employ someone for a job

Etymology

From Latin 'dimittere' (to send away), via Old French 'desmis'.

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
to send someone away or allow them to leave, especially from a position or an activity
send someone away; allow to leave

Examples

The teacher dismissed her students early due to the snowstorm.
After the meeting, the manager dismissed everyone from the room.
Once his testimony was finished, the judge dismissed the witness.

Synset Examples

I was dismissed after I gave my report

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to expel - to force someone to leave a place or organization
  • Antonym: to retain - to keep someone in a place or position

Etymology

From Latin 'dismissus', past participle of 'dimittere' (to send away).

4. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
to stop associating with or end a relationship with someone; to remove someone from one's company or group
end association with someone

Examples

After the scandal, the group chose to dismiss her from their meetings.
When he learned about the betrayal, he decided to dismiss his friend from his life.
Many clubs will dismiss members who consistently break the rules.

Synset Examples

They dropped her after she had a child out of wedlock

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drop - stop associating with someone
  • Synonym: reject - refuse to accept or associate with someone
  • Antonym: to admit - allow someone to join a group or company

Etymology

From Latin 'dimittere' (to send away, release), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'mittere' (send).

5. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
To officially remove or end something, especially a legal case or proceeding, making it no longer valid.
formally end or throw out (esp. legal case)

Examples

The judge decided to dismiss the charges due to lack of evidence.
The court may dismiss the lawsuit if the claim is found to be invalid.
After reviewing the case, the council voted unanimously to dismiss the petition.

Synset Examples

The President dissolved the parliament and called for new elections

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discharge - to officially end or release someone from a duty, legal case, etc.
  • Antonym: to uphold - to confirm or support a decision, often in a legal context

Etymology

From Latin dis- (apart) + mittere (send).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00339369-v
Synset POS: v

6. VERB

/ dɪsˈmɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈmɪs / (BrE)
to decide that something or someone is not worth thinking about or considering; to refuse to give attention or credence to
refuse to consider or pay attention to

Examples

The committee chose to dismiss the allegations due to a lack of evidence.
She quickly dismissed his comments as irrelevant to the discussion.
Many people dismiss myths without considering their historical significance.

Synset Examples

She dismissed his advances

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ignore - to pay no attention to

Etymology

From Latin 'dimittere' (to send away), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'mittere' (to send).
