squeak

1. NOUN

/ skwiːk / (AmE), / skwiːk / (BrE)
an achievement or escape that happens by a very narrow margin; a close call
narrow escape or close achievement

Examples

It was a squeak, but they managed to catch their train at the last minute.
Winning the match turned out to be a real squeak for the underdog team.
They had a narrow squeak when the fire alarm went off just in time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: close call - a situation where disaster is narrowly avoided
  • Synonym: narrow escape - an escape from danger by a slim margin

2. NOUN

/ skwik / (AmE), / skwiːk / (BrE)
a short, high-pitched noise, often made by something being pressed, rubbed, or moved suddenly
a short high-pitched noise

Examples

The only sound in the hallway was the squeak of her sneakers against the floor.
I heard a squeak coming from the wheel of my old bicycle.
The squeak of the mouse startled the cat.

Synset Examples

the squeak of shoes on powdery snow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creak - a harsh, high-pitched sound, especially from wood or hinges

Etymology

Imitative origin, representing the sound itself.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07410223-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ skwik / (AmE), / skwiːk / (BrE)
to make a short, high-pitched, screeching or squealing sound
make a high-pitched sound

Examples

The mouse began to squeak when it was startled by the cat.
Her new shoes squeak every time she walks across the hallway.
Don't squeak the chair, or you'll wake the baby.

Synset Examples

The door creaked when I opened it slowly
My car engine makes a whining noise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creak - to make a harsh, high-pitched, grating sound
  • Synonym: whine - to make a long, high-pitched, complaining sound
  • Synonym: squawk - to emit a loud, harsh, abrupt sound

Etymology

Imitative; from Middle English 'sqweke', likely echoing the sound itself.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02176108-v
Synset POS: v
