kaleidoscope

1. NOUN

/ kəˈlaɪdəˌskoʊp / (AmE), / kəˈlʌɪdəskəʊp / (BrE)
a toy consisting of a tube containing mirrors and pieces of colored glass, whose reflections produce changing symmetrical patterns when the tube is rotated
optical toy with changing patterns

Examples

As she turned the kaleidoscope, vibrant patterns shifted and changed before her eyes.
Children are often fascinated by the colorful images a kaleidoscope creates.
He remembered his childhood days spent gazing into a kaleidoscope for hours.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek 'kalos' (beautiful) + 'eidos' (form) + 'skopein' (to look at), via French or modern Latin.

2. NOUN

/ kəˈlaɪdəˌskoʊp / (AmE), / kəˈlaɪdəˌskəʊp / (BrE)
a complex pattern or scene of constantly changing colors and shapes
a pattern of shifting colors and shapes

Examples

The city presented a kaleidoscope of vibrant lights at night.
Her feelings were a kaleidoscope of emotions after hearing the news.
Spring brings a kaleidoscope of colors to the garden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: medley - a varied mixture of people or things

Etymology

From Ancient Greek 'kalos' (beautiful) + 'eidos' (form) + 'skopein' (to look at); coined by Scottish scientist Sir David Brewster in the early 19th century.
