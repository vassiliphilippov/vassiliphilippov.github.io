zigzag

1. ADJ

/ ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (AmE), / ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (BrE)
having a series of short, sharp turns or angles, moving or formed in a zigzag pattern
having sharp turns or angles

Examples

The hikers followed a zigzag path up the steep mountain slope.
I drew a zigzag line with my pencil across the paper.
The river makes a zigzag through the flat countryside before reaching the sea.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: serpentine - having a winding or curving form, like a snake

Etymology

From French 'zigzag', probably of imitative origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02322131-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADV

/ ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (AmE), / ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (BrE)
in a manner moving from side to side in sharp turns rather than in a straight line
with sharp turns from side to side

Examples

The rabbit darted zigzag through the tall grass to evade the fox.
Lightning streaked zigzag across the stormy night sky.
He ran zigzag down the hill to avoid slipping on the wet ground.

Synset Examples

birds flew zigzag across the blue sky

Related Terms

  • Antonym: straight - in a direct course without deviation

Etymology

From French 'zigzag', likely of imitative origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00500266-r
Synset POS: r

3. ADJ

/ ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (AmE), / ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (BrE)
having a series of short, sharp turns or angles, moving alternately left and right or in a similar pattern
having sharp turns or angles

Examples

The zigzag road up the mountain made driving slow and challenging.
Lightning often follows a zigzag path through the sky as it strikes.
He drew a zigzag line across the page to show the erratic route.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: straight - not having bends or curves; in a single direct line

Etymology

From French zigzag, of imitative origin, possibly influenced by German Zickzack.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02322131-s
Synset POS: s

4. VERB

/ ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (AmE), / ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (BrE)
to move or travel forward by turning sharply first in one direction and then in another alternately.
move or travel in sharp alternately changing turns

Examples

The path zigzags up the steep hillside, making the climb easier.
She watched the plane zigzag through the clouds to avoid turbulence.
The deer zigzagged across the field to escape the predator.

Synset Examples

The river zigzags through the countryside

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swerve - to turn aside abruptly from a straight course
  • Synonym: meander - to follow a winding and turning course

Etymology

From French 'zigzag', perhaps imitative or from German 'zickzack'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01995776-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (AmE), / ˈzɪɡˌzæɡ / (BrE)
a line or course that moves repeatedly back and forth or from side to side, making sharp turns in alternating directions
angular line with alternating sharp turns

Examples

The road followed a zigzag up the steep hill, making for a slow but steady climb.
She drew a zigzag across her notebook, mimicking the shape of lightning.
The mountain path is marked by a series of sharp zigzags to reduce the slope.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: serpentine - having a winding, snake-like shape or movement
  • Antonym: straight line - a line without curves or turns

Etymology

From French 'zigzag', probably of imitative origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13910012-n
Synset POS: n
