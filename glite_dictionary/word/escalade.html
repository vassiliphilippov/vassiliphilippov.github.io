escalade

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛskəˈleɪd / (AmE), / ˈɛskəleɪd / (BrE)
the act of scaling a wall or fortification with the help of ladders, especially during a military attack
scaling walls with ladders (military)

Examples

The enemy attempted an escalade but were repelled by the defenders on the walls.
During medieval times, escalade played a crucial role in the siege of fortified cities.
The soldiers prepared their ladders in anticipation of the daring escalade at dawn.

Etymology

From French 'escalade', from Italian 'scalata', from 'scalare' (to climb).

2. VERB

/ ˌɛskəˈleɪd / (AmE), / ˌɛskəˈlɑːd / (BrE)
to climb up and over, especially by means of ladders in a military attack; to scale (a wall or other obstacle) by climbing.
climb up and over (esp. a wall)

Examples

The troops attempted to escalade the fortress walls at dawn.
They needed ropes and skill to escalade the steep ravine.
Adventurers sometimes escalade sheer cliffs to reach hidden plateaus.

Synset Examples

They had to escalade canyons to reach their destination

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scale - to climb up or over something high, such as a wall or mountain

Etymology

From French 'escalade' (an act of scaling walls), from Italian 'scalare' (to climb), from 'scala' (ladder).
