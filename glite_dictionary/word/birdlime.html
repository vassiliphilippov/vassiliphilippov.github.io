birdlime

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɝːdˌlaɪm / (AmE), / ˈbɜːdˌlaɪm / (BrE)
a sticky substance spread on branches or twigs to trap small birds by making them adhere to the surface
sticky substance for trapping birds

Examples

Hunters once used birdlime to capture songbirds perched on branches.
Making birdlime from holly bark was a common practice in rural areas.
The use of birdlime is now banned in many countries to protect wildlife.

Etymology

From Middle English birdeleme, from bird + lime (the sticky substance).

2. VERB

/ ˈbɝːdˌlaɪm / (AmE), / ˈbɜːdˌlaɪm / (BrE)
to coat branches or surfaces with birdlime to catch birds
to spread sticky substance to catch birds

Examples

Farmers sometimes birdlime the lower branches to protect their crops from flocks of sparrows.
In the past, hunters would birdlime trees to trap small songbirds for food.
He deftly birdlimed the twigs, hoping to catch nightingales at dawn.

Etymology

From 'bird' + 'lime' (Old English līm, sticky substance).
