Constitution

1. PROPN

/ ˌkɑnstəˈtuːʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃən / (BrE)
the United States Navy's historic 44-gun frigate, launched in 1797 and famous for its victories against British ships during the War of 1812, now preserved as a museum ship in Boston.
famous historic U.S. Navy frigate

Examples

The USS Constitution is often referred to as "Old Ironsides" because of its strong wooden hull.
Visitors to Boston can tour the historic Constitution in the Charlestown Navy Yard.
The Constitution played a key role in American naval victories during the War of 1812.

2. PROPN

/ ˌkɑn.stəˈtu.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒn.stɪˈtjuː.ʃən / (BrE)
the fundamental document, ratified in 1788, that establishes the framework of government for the United States of America
the foundational document of U.S. government

Examples

The Constitution lays out the structure of the federal government and the rights of citizens.
Many legal scholars spend years studying the Constitution and its amendments.
There are ongoing debates about how the Constitution should be interpreted in modern times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Bill of Rights - the first ten amendments to the U.S. Constitution

Etymology

From Latin 'constitutio' (arrangement, decree), from 'constituere' (to set up).
