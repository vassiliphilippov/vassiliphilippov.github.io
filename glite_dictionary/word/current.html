current

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɝː.ənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌr.ənt / (BrE)
A continuous and prevailing flow or movement of events, ideas, or opinions, often compared to the movement of water.
prevailing movement of ideas or events

Examples

Many people followed the current of popular opinion during the election.
Academic theories often change with the current of new discoveries and perspectives.
He tried to resist the current of revolutionary thought sweeping through his country.

Synset Examples

two streams of development run through American history
stream of consciousness
the flow of thought
the current of history

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trend - a general direction in which something is developing or changing

Etymology

From Latin 'currens', present participle of 'currere' (to run).

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɝːrənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌrənt / (BrE)
A flow of electricity through a conductor.
flow of electricity

Examples

The engineer checked the current flowing through the circuit before turning the device on.
Too much electrical current can damage sensitive equipment.
The current in this wire is calculated to be two amperes.

Synset Examples

the current was measured in amperes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: voltage - electric potential difference
  • Synonym: amperage - strength of an electric current measured in amperes

Etymology

From Latin 'currens', present participle of 'currere' meaning 'to run'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11463628-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈkɜːrənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌrənt / (BrE)
happening or existing now; belonging to the present time.
of the present time

Examples

The newspaper covers all the current events in the city.
Our current project will be completed by the end of this month.
Keep your address current to receive important mail.

Synset Examples

current events
the current topic

current psychoanalytic theories
the ship's current position

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contemporary - existing or happening in the same period; modern
  • Antonym: past - belonging to an earlier time
  • Degree Lower: recent - having happened not long ago
  • Degree Higher: up-to-date - incorporating the latest developments

Etymology

From Old French 'corant' (running), present participle of 'courre', from Latin 'currere' (to run).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɝː.ənt / (AmE), / ˈkʌr.ənt / (BrE)
a steady flow of water, air, or another fluid, especially in a definite direction, typically as part of a larger body or system
steady flow of water or air

Examples

The swimmer fought against the strong current to reach the shore.
Warm air currents swept across the plain, bringing a sudden change in temperature.
A current of cold water ran beneath the surface of the lake.

Synset Examples

the raft floated downstream on the current
he felt a stream of air
the hose ejected a stream of water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stream - a small, narrow river; also, a continuous flow of something
  • Degree Lower: eddy - a circular movement of water, counter to a main current

Etymology

From Middle English *curraunt* (flowing), from Old French *courant*, from Latin *currere* (to run).
