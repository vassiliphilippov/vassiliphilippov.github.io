latency

1. NOUN

/ ˈleɪ.tən.si / (AmE), / ˈleɪ.tən.si / (BrE)
(computing) The delay before a transfer of data begins following an instruction for its transfer, especially the time it takes for a particular block of data to become accessible to the read/write head.
delay before data becomes accessible (computing)
medical

Examples

The latency of hard drives affects how quickly files can be accessed.
Engineers work to minimize network latency to improve user experience.
High latency can cause noticeable delays in video streaming and online gaming.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lag - a delay in response or progress

Etymology

From Latin latēre (to lie hidden); modern sense in technology developed in the 20th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈleɪtənsi / (AmE), / ˈleɪtənsi / (BrE)
the interval of time that passes between a stimulus and the resulting response, especially in scientific or technical contexts
interval between stimulus and response
medical

Examples

The latency between pressing the button and hearing the sound was only a few milliseconds.
Scientists measured the latency of the subject's response to the visual cue.
Reducing signal latency is important for real-time computer applications.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reaction time - the length of time between a stimulus and a response

Etymology

From Latin latēre (to lie hidden), via Late Latin latentia (being latent).

3. NOUN

/ ˈleɪ.tən.si / (AmE), / ˈleɪ.tən.si / (BrE)
The state or period in which something exists but is not yet visible, active, or evident.
state of being inactive or hidden

Examples

The virus can remain in latency for years before symptoms appear.
There was a long period of latency before the volcano erupted.
Innovation often undergoes a phase of latency before becoming widespread.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dormancy - the state of being temporarily inactive
  • Antonym: manifestation - the process of becoming evident or active

Etymology

From Latin 'latens' (present participle of latere, to lie hidden) + English '-cy'.
