to popularise

1. VERB

/ ˈpɑːpjəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpɒpjʊləraɪz / (BrE)
to make an idea, subject, or concept easily understood and appreciated by the general public
make widely accessible to the public
british

Examples

The professor worked hard to popularise complex scientific theories for a wider audience.
Many television programs aim to popularise history to engage younger viewers.
She has popularised environmental issues through her books and public talks.

Synset Examples

Carl Sagan popularized cosmology in his books

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to popularize - make widely known or accessible to the public; American spelling

Etymology

From popular + -ise, after French populariser (to make popular).

2. VERB

/ ˈpɑːpjəˌlɚˌaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpɒpjʊləˌaɪz / (BrE)
to make (an idea, activity, or product) well known, liked, or accepted by many people; to bring into widespread use or popularity
to make something widely accepted or well known
british

Examples

Her efforts helped popularise organic food across the country.
The television show did much to popularise classical music among young people.
He popularised the use of the personal computer in everyday life.

Synset Examples

They popularized coffee in Washington State
Relativity Theory was vulgarized by these authors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to popularize - to make popular and widely known (American spelling)
  • Synonym: to vulgarize - to make less refined for general acceptance

Etymology

From 'popular' + '-ise', patterned after French 'populariser'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00972247-v
Synset POS: v
