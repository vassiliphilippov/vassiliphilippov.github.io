fermentation

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɜr.mənˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɜː.mənˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A chemical process in which an agent, usually a microorganism, causes an organic substance to break down into simpler substances, especially the anaerobic conversion of sugars into alcohol.
chemical breakdown by microorganisms

Examples

The production of wine depends on the fermentation of grape juice by yeast.
Sauerkraut is made through the natural fermentation of cabbage and salt.
During fermentation, sugars are converted into alcohol and carbon dioxide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decay - the process of rotting or decomposition
  • Antonym: respiration - the process in living organisms of producing energy, typically with oxygen

Etymology

From Latin 'fermentatio' (leavening, causing to rise), from 'fermentare' (to leaven).

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɜr.mənˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɜː.mənˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A state of intense agitation, unrest, or rapid change, often referring to social or political situations.
state of agitation or change

Examples

The city is in a period of political fermentation as groups demand reform.
The decade was marked by cultural fermentation and new artistic movements.
There was a sense of fermentation in the air before the revolution started.

Synset Examples

the political ferment produced new leadership
social unrest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unrest - a state of dissatisfaction, disturbance, and agitation in a group of people
  • Synonym: turmoil - a state of great disturbance, confusion, or uncertainty
  • Antonym: calm - a period or state of freedom from storms, wind, or agitation

Etymology

From Latin fermentatio, from fermentare (to leaven, cause to rise).
