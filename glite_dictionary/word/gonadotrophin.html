gonadotrophin

1. NOUN

/ ɡoʊˌnædəˈtroʊfɪn / (AmE), / ˌɡəʊnədəˈtrəʊfɪn / (BrE)
a hormone produced by the anterior pituitary gland or placenta that stimulates the gonads and regulates reproductive activity
hormone regulating reproductive activity
medical

Examples

Levels of gonadotrophin rise during pregnancy to support fetal development.
Treatment for infertility may involve injections of gonadotrophin to stimulate the ovaries.
A deficiency in gonadotrophin can result in reproductive disorders.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hormone - chemical messenger produced in the body to regulate physiology

Etymology

From Greek 'gonos' (seed, offspring) + 'trophē' (nourishment).
