expedience

1. NOUN

/ ɪkˈspiːdiəns / (AmE), / ɪkˈspiːdiəns / (BrE)
the quality of being convenient and practical despite possibly being improper or immoral; taking advantage of opportunities for personal gain without concern for others.
practical advantage, often regardless of ethics

Examples

His decision was guided more by expedience than by moral principle.
In politics, expedience sometimes triumphs over honesty and integrity.
Their cooperation was based purely on expedience, not on any real trust.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: self-interest - one's own advantage or welfare
  • Antonym: principle - moral rule or belief that guides action

Etymology

From Latin 'expedientia' (expediency, usefulness), from 'expedientem' (convenient, advantageous).

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈspiːdiəns / (AmE), / ɪkˈspiːdiəns / (BrE)
the quality of being convenient and practical despite possibly being improper or immoral; suitability for achieving a particular end
quality of being practical or suitable

Examples

He questioned the expedience of the new policy, given the ethical concerns.
Sometimes, political decisions are made based on expedience rather than principle.
The committee chose expedience over tradition in order to meet the deadline.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expediency - the quality of being convenient and practical
  • Antonym: inconvenience - the state of causing trouble or difficulty

Etymology

From Latin 'expedientia' (advantage, suitability), from 'expedientem' (beneficial).
