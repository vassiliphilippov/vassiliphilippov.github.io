cheating

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (BrE)
An act of dishonestly deceiving someone in order to gain an unfair advantage or personal benefit.
dishonest deception for gain

Examples

The coach was fired after his cheating was discovered by the league officials.
Academic cheating is taken very seriously at the university.
There was evidence of cheating during the final round of the competition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception intended to result in financial or personal gain
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being honest

Etymology

From the verb 'cheat', which is from Middle English 'cheten', from Old French 'eschier', meaning to escheat or forfeit.

2. ADJ

/ ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (BrE)
(of a spouse or lover) not faithful; having a romantic or sexual relationship outside a committed partnership.
unfaithful in a relationship

Examples

She discovered her cheating husband had been lying for months.
Rumors spread quickly about the cheating actress and her secret affairs.
Being in a relationship with a cheating partner can be emotionally devastating.

Synset Examples

adulterous husbands and wives
a two-timing boyfriend

Related Terms

  • Antonym: faithful - loyal to a spouse or lover
  • Synonym: adulterous - having a sexual relationship outside marriage

3. ADJ

/ ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈtʃiːtɪŋ / (BrE)
violating accepted standards or rules, especially in a dishonest or unfair way
dishonest; violating rules

Examples

The referee penalized him for his cheating tactics during the match.
Her cheating behavior at the quiz was noticed by several students.
The organization was accused of using cheating methods to win the contract.

Synset Examples

a dirty fighter
used foul means to gain power
a nasty unsporting serve
fined for unsportsmanlike behavior

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unsporting - not behaving in a fair or honorable way in sport
  • Antonym: fair - in accordance with the rules or standards; legitimate

Etymology

Present participle of 'cheat,' from Old French 'eschier' (to escape).
