Aphyllophorales

1. NOUN

/ əˌfɪləˌfɔːˈreɪliːz / (AmE), / əˌfɪl.ə.fɒˈrɑː.liːz / (BrE)
a former order of chiefly saprophytic fungi, typically with shelf-like fruiting bodies, sometimes classified in Hymenomycetes or Agaricales.
former order of shelf-like fungi
medical

Examples

Many wood-decaying fungi once classified in the Aphyllophorales can be found in temperate forests.
The taxonomic position of Aphyllophorales has changed as fungal classification has advanced.
Some shelf fungi, previously grouped under Aphyllophorales, are now placed in different families.

Etymology

From Neo-Latin, combining Greek 'a-' (without), 'phyllon' (leaf), and 'phoros' (bearing), referring to the order's lack of gills.

2. NOUN

/ əˌfɪləˌfɔːˈreɪliːz / (AmE), / əˌfɪləˌfɒˈreɪliːz / (BrE)
a former order of chiefly saprophytic fungi, typically with shelflike bodies; sometimes classified within Hymenomycetes or Agaricales.
a former order of fungi with shelflike bodies

Examples

Many wood-rotting fungi in forests belong to the Aphyllophorales.
The Aphyllophorales include species commonly found growing on decaying logs.
Researchers used to classify various bracket fungi under the Aphyllophorales.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Agaricales - an order of fungi with gilled mushrooms
  • Synonym: Hymenomycetes - class of fungi including mushrooms and bracket fungi

Etymology

From Greek 'a-' (without) + 'phyllon' (leaf) + 'phora' (bearing), referring to the absence of gills.
