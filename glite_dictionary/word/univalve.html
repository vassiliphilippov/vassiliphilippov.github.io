univalve

1. ADJ

/ ˈjuːnɪˌvælv / (AmE), / ˈjuːnɪˌvælv / (BrE)
(of a mollusk) having a single shell, especially referring to gastropods such as snails.
having a single shell (zoology)

Examples

Many snails are classified as univalve mollusks due to their single, spiraled shell.
The univalve structure provides some gastropods with protection and mobility.
Unlike clams, which are bivalves, periwinkles are examples of univalve organisms.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bivalve - (of a mollusk) having two shells, as in clams

Etymology

From Latin 'uni-' (one) and 'valva' (leaf of a door or shell).

2. NOUN

/ ˈjuːnɪˌvælv / (AmE), / ˈjuːnɪˌvɑːlv / (BrE)
A mollusk, especially a gastropod, with a shell consisting of a single piece.
mollusk with a single-piece shell

Examples

The shell collector was most interested in the rare univalve specimens found on the reef.
A garden snail is a common example of a univalve found in temperate climates.
Unlike bivalves, univalves have a spiral shell and a distinct head structure.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bivalve - mollusk with a shell consisting of two hinged parts

Etymology

From Latin 'uni-' (one) + 'valva' (leaf of a door, folding door).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
