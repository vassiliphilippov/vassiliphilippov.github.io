illusion

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈluʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
a false or misleading perception or belief; something that appears different from what is real or true
false or misleading perception

Examples

The magician created the illusion of making the rabbit disappear.
For years, he lived under the illusion that he would become famous overnight.
The mirrored walls gave an illusion of more space in the small room.

Synset Examples

Artists use perspective in art to create an illusion of depth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delusion - a false belief or opinion, especially as a symptom of mental disorder
  • Antonym: reality - the state of things as they actually exist

Etymology

From Latin 'illusio' (mockery, deception), from 'illudere' (to mock, to deceive).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈluʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
the act of deceiving or misleading someone by producing a false impression or belief
the act of deception or creating a false idea

Examples

The magician's trick was an illusion that fooled the entire audience.
He was living under the illusion that he could not be replaced.
The company's advertising created the illusion of greater value.

Synset Examples

She has a delusion that she is being spied on.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delusion - a false belief that is firmly maintained despite evidence
  • Antonym: reality - the state of things as they actually exist

Etymology

From Latin 'illusio' (mockery, irony), from 'illudere' (to mock at).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈluːʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
an act or display that deceives the senses, especially a magic trick or feat that appears impossible or supernatural to onlookers
a deceptive act, esp. in magic

Examples

The magician amazed the audience with a breathtaking illusion involving disappearing coins.
Her favorite illusion was the classic saw-the-woman-in-half trick.
The show featured an illusion so convincing that many thought it was real magic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trick - an action intended to deceive or surprise
  • Synonym: magic - the art of producing illusions as entertainment

Etymology

From Latin illusio (mockery, deception), from illudere (to mock at), from in- (at, against) + ludere (to play).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ɪˈluʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈluːʒən / (BrE)
a false idea or belief that many people accept as true, especially one that misleads them from reality
false idea or belief

Examples

Many people live under the illusion that money can buy true happiness.
The company created the illusion of success through clever advertising.
It is an illusion to think that all problems can be solved quickly.

Synset Examples

they have the illusion that I am very wealthy
He fancies himself to be a local celebrity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delusion - a false belief held with strong conviction
  • Antonym: reality - the state of things as they actually exist

Etymology

From Latin illusio (mockery, deception), from illudere (to mock, to deceive).
