glimmer

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡlɪmɚ / (AmE), / ˈɡlɪmə / (BrE)
a slight suggestion or vague understanding of something; a faint perception
slight suggestion; vague understanding

Examples

She felt a glimmer of hope as the news unfolded.
There wasn't even a glimmer of understanding in his expression.
He had a glimmer of what the painting represented, but couldn't explain it.

Synset Examples

he had no inkling what was about to happen

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inkling - a slight suspicion or hint
  • Synonym: notion - an idea or belief, especially a vague or fanciful one
  • Antonym: certainty - a state of being sure or certain

Etymology

From Middle English glimeren, related to 'gleam'.

2. VERB

/ ˈɡlɪmɚ / (AmE), / ˈɡlɪmə / (BrE)
to shine with a faint or unsteady light
shine faintly or unsteadily

Examples

A single candle glimmered in the dark room, barely illuminating the walls.
Stars glimmered faintly through the clouds as night fell over the city.
Her hope glimmered despite the overwhelming odds against her.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gleam - to shine with a steady but not very bright light
  • Synonym: flicker - to shine or burn with a wavering or unsteady light

Etymology

From Middle English 'glimeren', of imitative origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02165209-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈɡlɪmər / (AmE), / ˈɡlɪmə / (BrE)
a faint or brief flash or appearance of light, especially as reflected or seen intermittently
a faint flash or appearance of light

Examples

A glimmer of light shone through the heavy curtains, announcing the morning.
There was a glimmer on the surface of the lake as the sun set.
He saw a glimmer in the darkness and moved toward it cautiously.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gleam - a brief or faint appearance of light
  • Synonym: shine - to emit light; be bright by reflecting light
  • Degree Higher: glow - give out a steady light

Etymology

From Middle English 'glymeren', probably of Scandinavian origin; compare with Norwegian 'glimra' (to glimmer).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
