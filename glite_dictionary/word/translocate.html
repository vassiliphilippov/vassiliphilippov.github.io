to translocate

1. VERB

/ trænsˈloʊkeɪt / (AmE), / trænzˈləʊkeɪt / (BrE)
to transfer a chromosomal segment to a new position, especially on a different chromosome
move a chromosomal segment to a new position
medical

Examples

Certain genetic disorders occur when chromosomes translocate segments to nonhomologous partners.
Scientists were able to translocate a portion of the chromosome during the experiment.
A cell may become cancerous if it translocates a gene to an inappropriate location.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: translocation - the process of moving a chromosomal segment to a new location

Etymology

From Latin trans- (across) + locate (place), formed on analogy with 'translocation'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02367322-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ trænzˈloʊkeɪt / (AmE), / trænsˈləʊkeɪt / (BrE)
to move something, especially a living organism, from one place to another, often for conservation purposes
move an organism to a new place

Examples

Conservationists decided to translocate the rare birds to a protected island.
Wildlife agencies sometimes translocate animals to prevent conflicts with humans.
Several hundred frogs were carefully translocated to restore their population in the wetland.

Synset Examples

The endangered turtles were translocated to a safe environment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relocate - to move something or someone to a new place
  • Synonym: transfer - to move from one place to another

Etymology

From Latin translocare (to move to a new place), from trans- (across) + locare (to place).

Source: WordNet
