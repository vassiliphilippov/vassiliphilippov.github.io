jumpstart

1. VERB

/ ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑrt / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑːt / (BrE)
to start a car with a dead battery by connecting it to another vehicle's battery using cables
start a car by connecting to another battery

Examples

My car wouldn't start this morning, so my neighbor helped me jumpstart it.
You should read the manual before trying to jumpstart your vehicle for safety reasons.
After leaving the headlights on all night, I needed to jumpstart the battery to get to work.
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01863036-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑrt / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑːt / (BrE)
An act of starting a vehicle with a weak or dead battery by connecting it to another vehicle's battery with jumper cables.
starting a vehicle's battery with jumper cables

Examples

When my car wouldn't start this morning, I needed a jumpstart from a passing motorist.
She learned how to give a jumpstart in her driver's education class.
After leaving the lights on overnight, his truck required a jumpstart to get going.

Synset Examples

my battery was dead so I had to get a jumpstart from my neighbor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jump-start - to start a vehicle by connecting it to another vehicle's battery

Etymology

From 'jump' and 'start', originally as a verb meaning to start suddenly or with a boost, later adapted as a noun.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01268414-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑrt / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌmpˌstɑːt / (BrE)
to start or restart something energetically or forcefully, especially to quickly make it active or effective again after a period of inactivity or stagnation
start or revive something energetically

Examples

The government hopes the tax cuts will jumpstart the sluggish economy.
We need a new strategy to jumpstart sales this quarter.
She worked all weekend to jumpstart her stalled project.

Synset Examples

The Secretary of State intends to jumpstart the Middle East Peace Process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to restart - to start something again
  • Synonym: to revive - to make active or alive again

Etymology

From U.S. automotive slang, originally referring to starting a car with a depleted battery using jumper cables (1940s).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00349877-v
Synset POS: v
