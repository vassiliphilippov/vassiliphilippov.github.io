paraffin

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɛrəˌfɪn / (AmE), / ˈpærəfɪn / (BrE)
A white or colorless, waxy solid derived from petroleum, used in making candles, as a preservative, and for waterproofing.
waxy solid from petroleum used for candles, coating

Examples

The old lantern was fueled with paraffin to light the room.
She coated the cheese in paraffin to preserve it for several months.
Paraffin is commonly used in making candles because it burns cleanly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wax - a general term for various substances, including paraffin, used for candles and coatings

Etymology

From Latin 'parum' (little) + 'affinis' (lacking affinity); coined in 19th-century German as 'Paraffin' for its chemical unreactiveness.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɛrəfɪn / (AmE), / ˈpærəfɪn / (BrE)
(British) A thin, clear oil used as a fuel or solvent, known as kerosine in American English.
(British) kerosine; clear fuel oil
british

Examples

Many old stoves in Britain still use paraffin as a fuel.
We bought a bottle of paraffin to fill the heater.
In the UK, paraffin lamps are common during power cuts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kerosine - a thin, clear oil used as fuel (American equivalent of British 'paraffin')

Etymology

From Latin 'parum' (barely) + 'affinis' (affinity), referring to its chemical inertness.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpæɹ.ə.fɪn / (AmE), / ˈpær.ə.fɪn / (BrE)
A series of non-aromatic saturated hydrocarbons with the general formula CnH(2n+2), commonly used as fuel, in candles, or for sealing.
a type of saturated hydrocarbon

Examples

The laboratory used paraffin as a solvent in several experiments.
Candles are often made from paraffin wax due to its stability and affordability.
He spilled some paraffin on the bench while fueling the lamp.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alkane - a saturated hydrocarbon with single bonds only

Etymology

From Latin 'parum' (little) + 'affinis' (affinity), meaning low reactivity.
