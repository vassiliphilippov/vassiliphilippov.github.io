sabre

1. VERB

/ ˈseɪbər / (AmE), / ˈseɪbə / (BrE)
to attack, cut, or injure with a saber (a type of sword)
attack or cut with a saber

Examples

The soldier sabred his way through the enemy’s defense.
Revolutionary cavalry would often sabre their opponents in battle.
During the charge, they sabred anyone who tried to stand against them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slash - to cut with a sweeping or striking movement

Etymology

From French 'sabre', originally from Hungarian 'szablya' (sword).

2. NOUN

/ ˈseɪbɚ / (AmE), / ˈseɪbə / (BrE)
a stout sword with a curved, single-edged blade and a thick back, historically used by cavalry
curved cavalry sword

Examples

The cavalry officer drew his sabre and charged into battle.
In the museum, you can see an ornate sabre from the Napoleonic Wars.
He practiced fencing with a sabre, mastering its curved blade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sword - a weapon with a long metal blade and a hilt
  • Synonym: cutlass - a short, heavy, curved sword

Etymology

From French sabre, likely from Hungarian szablya (cutting sword).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02990947-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈseɪbɚ / (AmE), / ˈseɪbə / (BrE)
a type of light fencing sword with a v-shaped blade and a slightly curved handle, used in the sport of fencing and formerly as a cavalry weapon
a fencing sword with curved handle and v-shaped blade

Examples

During the fencing match, he skillfully parried his opponent's attack with his sabre.
The museum displayed an antique cavalry sabre from the early 19th century.
In modern fencing, the sabre is used in events that emphasize speed and agility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foil - a light fencing sword with a flexible, rectangular blade
  • Synonym: épée - a fencing sword with a stiff, triangular blade

Etymology

From French sabre, from Hungarian szablya (cutting sword).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04128605-n
Synset POS: n
