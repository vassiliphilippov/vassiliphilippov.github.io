to strangle

1. VERB

/ ˈstræŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈstræŋɡl / (BrE)
To severely restrict or prevent the progress, growth, or free movement of something.
prevent progress or free movement

Examples

Strict regulations can strangle economic growth in developing countries.
The new policy threatens to strangle independent media outlets.
Bureaucracy often strangles creativity and innovation in large organizations.

Synset Examples

He was hampered in his efforts by the bad weather
the imperialist nation wanted to strangle the free trade between the two small countries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to something forcibly
  • Synonym: hamper - to hinder or impede the progress of
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support, confidence, or hope to (opposite of suppressing progress)

Etymology

From Latin strangulare, from Greek strangalan (to strangle).

2. VERB

/ ˈstræŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈstræŋɡl / (BrE)
to kill or severely injure someone by squeezing or constricting the throat so as to prevent breathing
kill by squeezing the throat

Examples

The villain tried to strangle the hero with a rope.
She almost strangled on a piece of food, but was quickly helped by a friend.
He was arrested after attempting to strangle his business partner.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: choke - prevent someone from breathing by blocking the throat

Etymology

From Old French 'estrangler', from Latin 'strangulare' (to strangle, choke).

(of a person or animal) to die as a result of having pressure applied to the neck, cutting off the air supply.

Examples
