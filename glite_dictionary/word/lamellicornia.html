Lamellicornia

1. NOUN

/ ləˌmɛlɪˈkɔrniə / (AmE), / ləˌmɛlɪˈkɔːnɪə / (BrE)
A former taxonomic group of beetles that includes scarab beetles and stag beetles, characterized by their clubbed, leaf-like antennae.
group of beetles including scarabs and stag beetles

Examples

The ancient insect order Lamellicornia is known for species with elaborate antennae.
Many dung beetles belong to the group formerly recognized as Lamellicornia.
Scientists once classified stag beetles within Lamellicornia due to shared anatomical features.

Etymology

From Latin 'lamella' (a thin plate) + 'cornu' (horn), referring to the beetles' antennae.

2. NOUN

/ læˌmɛlɪˈkɔrniə / (AmE), / læˌmɛlɪˈkɔːnɪə / (BrE)
A former superfamily of beetles that includes scarabaeid beetles and stag beetles.
superfamily of scarab and stag beetles

Examples

The Lamellicornia were once considered a single superfamily but are now classified into several families.
Stag beetles, members of the Lamellicornia, are noted for their large mandibles.
Researchers debated the boundaries of Lamellicornia in historical entomological texts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Scarabaeidae - a family of beetles known as scarab beetles

Etymology

From Latin 'lamella' (small plate) + 'cornu' (horn), referring to the structure of the antennae.
