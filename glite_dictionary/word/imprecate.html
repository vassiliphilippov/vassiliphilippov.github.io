to imprecate

1. VERB

/ ˈɪm.prəˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪm.prɪ.keɪt / (BrE)
to wish harm or invoke evil upon someone or something, especially through a spoken curse
wish harm or curse (formally)

Examples

The villain threatened to imprecate a curse on the entire family.
She would never imprecate harm upon her enemies, no matter how badly they treated her.
Ancient rituals often called for priests to imprecate against those who broke sacred laws.

Synset Examples

The bad witch cursed the child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to call down harm or misfortune as if by magic
  • Antonym: to bless - to ask for divine favor or protection for someone

Etymology

From Latin imprecari (to invoke, to pray for), from in- (upon) + precari (to pray).

2. VERB

/ ˈɪmprɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪmprɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to utter curses or invoke harm or evil upon someone or something, often using profane or strong language
to utter curses or invoke harm

Examples

Standing before the ruins, he began to imprecate those he blamed for his misfortune.
The angry fans imprecated at the referee after the controversial decision.
She was known to imprecate whenever things didn't go her way.

Synset Examples

The drunken men were cursing loudly in the street

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to call down harm or evil upon

Etymology

From Latin 'imprecari' (to invoke by prayer, to curse), from 'in-' (upon) + 'precari' (to pray).
