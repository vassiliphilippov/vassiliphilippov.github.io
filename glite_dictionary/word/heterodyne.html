heterodyne

1. ADJ

/ ˈhɛtəˌdaɪn / (AmE), / ˈhɛt.ə.daɪn / (BrE)
relating to the production of new frequencies by mixing two oscillations, typically used in electronics to refer to the generation of sum and difference frequencies by combining signals.
relating to mixing signals to create new frequencies

Examples

The heterodyne principle is essential in most modern radio receivers.
A heterodyne system combines two frequencies to generate beat frequencies for signal processing.
Engineers use heterodyne detection methods in optical and radio communication systems.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: homodyne - involving the mixing of a signal with a signal of the same frequency

Etymology

From Greek 'heteros' (other) + 'dynamis' (power).

2. VERB

/ ˈhɛt̬.əˌdaɪn / (AmE), / ˈhɛt.ə.daɪn / (BrE)
to combine a radio frequency wave with another wave of a different frequency, producing new frequencies equal to their sum or difference
combine radio waves to create new frequencies

Examples

Modern radios often heterodyne incoming signals to convert them to an easier frequency for processing.
Technicians used a mixer circuit to heterodyne the high-frequency signal with a local oscillator.
She explained how to heterodyne two frequencies to achieve the desired output in the receiver.

Etymology

From Greek 'heteros' (other, different) + 'dyne' (power), coined in early 20th century for electronics.
