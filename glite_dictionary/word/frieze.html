frieze

1. NOUN

/ friːz / (AmE), / friːz / (BrE)
a heavy woolen fabric with a long, raised nap, formerly used for coats and outer garments
heavy woolen fabric with a long nap

Examples

The coat was made from a thick frieze to keep out the cold winds.
Irish workers traditionally wore jackets of coarse frieze during winter.
He ran his hand over the rough frieze and admired its warmth.

Etymology

From Middle English *frise*, from Old French *frise*, possibly from Medieval Latin *frisia* (Frisian cloth).

2. NOUN

/ friz / (AmE), / friːz / (BrE)
an architectural ornament forming a horizontal sculptured or painted band, typically found between the architrave and the cornice of a building
decorative horizontal architectural band

Examples

The classical temple featured a beautifully detailed frieze depicting scenes from mythology.
Visitors admired the ornate frieze that ran along the walls of the grand hall.
The sculptor spent months restoring the faded frieze above the building's entrance.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: cornice - a projecting molding on the top of a building or wall
  • Degree Lower: architrave - a main beam resting across the tops of columns

Etymology

From Old French frise, from Medieval Latin frisium or phrygium (embroidered border), possibly related to Phrygian (an ancient region in Anatolia) noted for embroidery.
