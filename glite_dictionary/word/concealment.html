concealment

1. NOUN

/ kənˈsiːlmənt / (AmE), / kənˈsiːlmənt / (BrE)
The state of being hidden or kept out of sight.
the state of being hidden

Examples

The burglar relied on the concealment of darkness to avoid detection.
She revealed her secret after years of strict concealment.
Effective concealment of the evidence made the investigation difficult.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: exposure - the state of being exposed or revealed

Etymology

From Old French 'concellement', from Latin 'concelare' (to hide).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈsiːlmənt / (AmE), / kənˈsiːlmənt / (BrE)
something that covers or shelters and thus hides or protects another thing from view; a means of hiding.
means of hiding or sheltering

Examples

The army unit relied on thick bushes for concealment during the operation.
Hunters waited quietly behind natural concealment provided by fallen logs.
In nature, many animals rely on color patterns as a form of concealment from predators.

Synset Examples

a screen of trees afforded privacy
under cover of darkness
the brush provided a covert for game
the simplest concealment is to match perfectly the color of the background

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cover - something that covers and protects another thing
  • Synonym: shelter - something that provides protection or refuge

Etymology

From Old French *concelement*, from *conceeler* (to hide), from Latin *concelāre* (to hide, cover).

3. NOUN

/ kənˈsiːlmənt / (AmE), / kənˈsiːlmənt / (BrE)
the act of preventing something from being known; the state of being kept secret
the act of keeping something secret

Examples

The company's concealment of important safety data led to a major scandal.
Her concealment of the truth made it hard to trust her again.
The spy was skilled in the art of concealment and evaded capture for years.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: disclosure - the act of making something known or public
  • Synonym: secrecy - the condition of being kept hidden or secret

Etymology

From Old French *concelement*, from *concealer* (to hide).
