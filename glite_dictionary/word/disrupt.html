to disrupt

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈrʌpt / (AmE), / dɪsˈrʌpt / (BrE)
to interrupt or throw into disorder, causing confusion or disturbance
interrupt or throw into disorder

Examples

The strike will disrupt train services across the city.
Sudden loud noises can easily disrupt my concentration.
The new law could disrupt the entire industry if implemented.

Synset Examples

This event disrupted the orderly process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disturb - to interrupt the normal arrangement or functioning of something
  • Antonym: to restore - to bring something back to its original or normal state

Etymology

From Latin disruptus, past participle of dirumpere (to break apart).

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈrʌpt / (AmE), / dɪsˈrʌpt / (BrE)
to interrupt the normal progress, activity, or flow of something
interrupt normal activity or flow

Examples

Construction noise can disrupt your concentration during exams.
The power outage disrupted the entire neighborhood for hours.
Unscheduled meetings often disrupt our regular workflow.

Synset Examples

We interrupt the program for the following messages

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interrupt - to stop the continuous progress of something for a time
  • Antonym: to continue - to go on or persist without interruption

Etymology

From Latin disruptus, past participle of disrumpere (to break apart).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00780320-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈrʌpt / (AmE), / dɪsˈrʌpt / (BrE)
to interrupt or prevent the normal progress of an activity or process, usually causing disorder or difficulty
interrupt or cause disorder to an activity

Examples

The construction work will disrupt traffic downtown for several weeks.
She didn't mean to disrupt the meeting, but her question changed the subject entirely.
Heavy rain could disrupt the outdoor festival this Saturday.

Synset Examples

Please don't interrupt me while I'm on the phone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interrupt - to stop someone from continuing what they are doing by saying or doing something

Etymology

From Latin disruptus, past participle of disrumpere, from dis- 'apart' + rumpere 'to break'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00521437-v
Synset POS: v
