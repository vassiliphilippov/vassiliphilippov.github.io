canter

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæn.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈkæn.tə / (BrE)
a smooth, moderate three-beat gait of a horse that is faster than a trot but slower than a gallop
moderate horse gait between trot and gallop

Examples

The horse broke into a gentle canter as it entered the open field.
With a relaxed canter, the rider circled the arena several times.
She preferred to approach the jumps at a steady canter rather than a fast gallop.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: gallop - the fastest gait of a horse
  • Degree Lower: trot - a two-beat gait of a horse, slower than a canter

Etymology

Shortened from Canterbury gallop, referring to a moderate pace used by pilgrims to Canterbury.

2. VERB

/ ˈkæn.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈkæn.tə / (BrE)
(of a horse or rider) to move at a canter, which is a pace between a trot and a gallop.
to ride or move at a canter

Examples

She cantered her horse gently across the field at dawn.
After warming up, the instructor told everyone to canter down the long side of the arena.
The horses cantered past the spectators, moving gracefully and steadily.

Synset Examples

The men cantered away

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: gallop - (of a horse or rider) to move at the fastest gait, faster than a canter
  • Degree Lower: trot - (of a horse or rider) to move at a pace slower than a canter

Etymology

From noun 'canter', short for 'Canterbury gallop', referring to the pace pilgrims rode to Canterbury.

4. VERB

/ ˈkænər / (AmE), / ˈkæntə / (BrE)
(of a horse) to move at a canter, which is a moderate, easy pace faster than a trot but slower than a gallop.
move at moderate horse pace

Examples

The horse began to canter gracefully around the arena as the rider loosened the reins.
We watched as the wild ponies cantered along the shore at sunrise.
She gently urged her pony to canter over the soft, grassy field.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: gallop - move at the fastest pace of a horse
  • Degree Lower: trot - move at a pace slower than a canter, but faster than a walk

Etymology

Shortened from 'Canterbury gallop', an easy pace said to be favored by pilgrims to Canterbury.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01963528-v
Synset POS: v
