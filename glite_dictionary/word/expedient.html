expedient

1. ADJ

/ ɪkˈspiːdiənt / (AmE), / ɪkˈspiːdiənt / (BrE)
(of an action) convenient and practical, although possibly improper or immoral, especially as a means of attaining an end.
convenient but possibly improper or self-serving

Examples

The manager made an expedient decision that saved the company money, but it angered many employees.
While it was expedient to ignore the problem for now, it could cause trouble in the future.
She found it expedient to tell a white lie rather than admit her mistake.

Synset Examples

was merciful only when mercy was expedient

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advantageous - beneficial; resulting in advantage
  • Antonym: disadvantageous - causing disadvantage; not beneficial

Etymology

From Latin 'expediens', present participle of 'expedire' (to free, extricate, prepare).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00934824-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈspidiənt / (AmE), / ɪkˈspiːdiənt / (BrE)
a means of achieving a particular end, especially one that is convenient but possibly improper or immoral
means to an end (often unethical)

Examples

As a temporary expedient, they arranged a meeting online rather than in person.
The government saw the curfew as a necessary expedient to restore order.
He took the easiest expedient rather than the most ethical solution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: device - something devised or contrived, especially for a specific purpose
  • Synonym: stratagem - a plan or scheme, especially one used to outwit an opponent

Etymology

From Latin ex- (out) + expedire (to free, extricate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00178297-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ɪkˈspiːdiənt / (AmE), / ɪkˈspiːdiənt / (BrE)
(of an action) suitable or practical for achieving a particular end, especially if not necessarily moral or ideal.
suitable or practical for a purpose

Examples

It was deemed expedient to delay the meeting until more information was available.
While not ideal, taking a taxi was the most expedient way to reach the airport on time.
The manager made an expedient decision that benefited the company but upset some employees.

Synset Examples

in the circumstances it was expedient to express loyalty

Related Terms

  • Synonym: practical - concerned with actual use or practice rather than theory
  • Antonym: inexpedient - not suitable or advisable for achieving a purpose

Etymology

From Latin 'expediens', present participle of 'expedire' (to free, make ready).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01819018-s
Synset POS: s
