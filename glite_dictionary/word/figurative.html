figurative

1. ADJ

/ ˈfɪɡjɚətɪv / (AmE), / ˈfɪɡjʊrətɪv / (BrE)
(of language) not literal; using words or expressions with a meaning that is different from the usual interpretation.
not literal; using figures of speech

Examples

Writers often use figurative language to create vivid images in the reader’s mind.
Her poem is full of figurative expressions describing the changing seasons.
In a figurative sense, he was chained to his desk.

Synset Examples

figurative language

Related Terms

  • Antonym: literal - (of words or meanings) taking words in their usual or most basic sense without metaphor or exaggeration

Etymology

From Late Latin figurativus (of the nature of a figure of speech), from Latin figura (figure).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfɪɡjərətɪv / (AmE), / ˈfɪɡjərətɪv / (BrE)
(of art or design) representing forms of people, animals, or other recognizable physical objects, especially as distinct from abstract art.
depicting human or animal figures

Examples

The museum's new collection focuses on figurative paintings from the twentieth century.
Her sculpture style shifted from abstract to highly figurative forms.
Many artists in the Renaissance era preferred figurative art depicting the human body.

Synset Examples

a figural design
the figurative art of the humanistic tradition

Related Terms

  • Antonym: abstract - not representing recognizable figures or objects

Etymology

From Late Latin figurativus (of a figure, figurative).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01986669-s
Synset POS: s
