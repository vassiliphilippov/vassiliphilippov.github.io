disquiet

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈkwaɪət / (AmE), / dɪsˈkwaɪət / (BrE)
A feeling of worry or unease about possible developments.
a feeling of worry or unease

Examples

She couldn't shake off her disquiet about the looming changes at work.
A sense of disquiet spread across the community after the news broke.
Despite his smile, there was an underlying disquiet in his eyes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anxiety - a feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease
  • Antonym: calm - a state of being peaceful and quiet

Etymology

From Latin 'disquietus' (restless), from 'dis-' (not) + 'quietus' (quiet).

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈkwaɪ.ət / (AmE), / dɪsˈkwaɪ.ət / (BrE)
to make someone worried, uneasy, or disturbed
to make uneasy or anxious

Examples

The troubling headlines served only to disquiet the public further.
Her silence began to disquiet those around her.
Nothing seemed to disquiet him, even in difficult times.

Synset Examples

She was rather perturbed by the news that her father was seriously ill

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disturb - to interrupt the peace or calm; to bother
  • Antonym: to reassure - to comfort or restore confidence to someone

Etymology

From Latin 'disquietare' from 'dis-' (apart) + 'quietus' (quiet, calm).

3. NOUN

/ dɪsˈkwaɪət / (AmE), / dɪsˈkwaɪət / (BrE)
A feeling of worry, unease, or anxiety; the state of being disturbed or lacking peace.
a feeling of unease or worry

Examples

A sense of disquiet spread through the audience as the news was announced.
There was a growing disquiet among staff about the company's future.
Her sudden silence filled him with disquiet about what might have happened.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unease - a feeling of discomfort or anxiety
  • Antonym: calm - the absence of disturbance or anxiety

Etymology

From Latin 'dis-' (apart, away) + 'quietus' (quiet, calm).
