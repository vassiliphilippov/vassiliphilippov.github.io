vocative

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɑːkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈvɒkətɪv / (BrE)
(in some languages) the grammatical case used when addressing someone or something directly.
grammatical case for addressing someone

Examples

In Latin, the vocative is used when calling someone by name.
The vocative case does not exist in modern English, but is present in many other languages.
Greek learners must learn to recognize and use the vocative correctly.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominative - grammatical case for subjects of verbs

Etymology

From Latin vocātīvus, from vocāre (to call).

2. ADJ

/ ˈvɑkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈvɒkətɪv / (BrE)
(of a word or case) relating to the grammatical case used to indicate the person or thing being addressed.
relating to the case for addressing someone

Examples

The Latin noun has a special vocative form when directly addressing someone.
In Ancient Greek, the vocative case is used when calling a person's name.
Some languages, unlike English, have distinct vocative endings in their grammar.

Synset Examples

vocative verb endings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accusative - relating to the grammatical case of the direct object
  • Synonym: nominative - relating to the grammatical case for the subject of a verb

Etymology

From Latin 'vocativus' (of calling), from 'vocare' (to call).
