jeer

1. VERB

/ dʒɪr / (AmE), / dʒɪə / (BrE)
to laugh at or shout rude remarks at someone to show contempt or mockery
to mock or ridicule with rude remarks

Examples

The fans began to jeer as the referee made another controversial decision.
She refused to let her classmates jeer at her presentation.
Despite being jeered by the audience, he finished his speech bravely.

Synset Examples

The crowd jeered at the speaker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to make fun of someone, often in a cruel way
  • Antonym: cheer - to shout in encouragement and support (opposite of jeer)

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'jeerren', possibly imitative of laughter or scoffing sounds.

2. NOUN

/ dʒɪr / (AmE), / dʒɪə / (BrE)
a mocking or derisive remark or shout; an expression of scorn or contempt
a mocking or scornful remark

Examples

The crowd responded with a loud jeer when the referee made an unpopular call.
His speech was interrupted by a jeer from the back of the auditorium.
She ignored the jeer and continued her performance with confidence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: taunt - a remark made to provoke or mock someone
  • Antonym: cheer - a shout of encouragement or praise

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *jeren* (to deride), of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06729173-n
Synset POS: n
