to signify

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪɡ.nɪ.faɪ / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nɪ.faɪ / (BrE)
to make something known, especially by words, actions, or signals
express or indicate by words or signals

Examples

A nod from the waiter signified that our table was ready.
He raised his hand to signify his agreement with the proposal.
The red flag signifies danger ahead on the course.

Synset Examples

He signified his wish to pay the bill for our meal

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to indicate - to point out or show
  • Synonym: express - to convey a thought or feeling in words or actions

Etymology

From Old French signifier, from Latin significare (to mean, indicate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01041726-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪɡ.nəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nɪ.faɪ / (BrE)
to be a sign of; to mean or represent something, especially in a symbolic or explicit way
to mean; to be a sign of

Examples

The red traffic light signifies that vehicles must stop.
A handshake can signify agreement or friendship between two people.
In some cultures, a white dove signifies peace and harmony.

Synset Examples

‘maison’ means ‘house’ in French
An example sentence would show what this word means

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to denote - to be a sign or indication of; to mean exactly
  • Synonym: to connote - to imply or suggest in addition to the literal meaning

Etymology

From Latin significare (to indicate, make known), from signum (sign) + facere (to make).

3. VERB

/ ˈsɪɡ.nəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈsɪɡ.nɪ.faɪ / (BrE)
to be a sign of; to mean or represent something
to mean or represent

Examples

The red light signifies that you must stop your car.
Her smile didn't necessarily signify agreement with the plan.
What does this symbol signify in the context of the story?

Synset Examples

What does his strange behavior signify?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mean - to have as a meaning or definition
  • Synonym: to indicate - to show, point out, or be a sign of

Etymology

From Old French signifier, from Latin significare (to indicate, mean).
