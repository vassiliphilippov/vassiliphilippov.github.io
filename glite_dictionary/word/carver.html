carver

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrvər / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvə / (BrE)
a person whose job or role is to carve cooked meat, especially at a meal or banquet
person who carves cooked meat

Examples

At the wedding, the carver skillfully sliced portions of roast beef for each guest.
She worked as the designated carver at family gatherings, always ensuring everyone got their favorite cut.
The restaurant employed a professional carver to serve meats at the buffet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: server - person who serves food or drink

Etymology

From carve (verb) + -er (agent suffix); 'carve' from Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut, carve).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑrvɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvə / (BrE)
a person who uses tools to shape and decorate wood, especially to create decorative wooden panels and sculptures
person who decorates wood by carving

Examples

The famous cathedral hired a skilled carver to create intricate woodwork for the altar.
Each chair in the collection was made by a master carver known for his detailed designs.
The museum featured an exhibit on the history of carver guilds in medieval Europe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sculptor - a person who creates sculptures, especially in stone or metal

Etymology

From carve + -er, first recorded in late Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10809603-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːrvər / (AmE), / ˈkɑːvə / (BrE)
an artist who creates sculptures by cutting or shaping materials such as wood, stone, or ivory
an artist who sculpts by cutting or shaping

Examples

The talented carver spent months perfecting the statue’s delicate features.
A skilled carver can transform a simple block of wood into a work of art.
Many museums feature the work of a master carver from the early 20th century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sculptor - an artist who creates three-dimensional works of art

Etymology

From Middle English 'carven' (to cut), from Old English 'ceorfan' (to cut, carve).

Source: WordNet
