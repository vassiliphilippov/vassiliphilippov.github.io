mouse

1. NOUN

/ maʊs / (AmE), / maʊs / (BrE)
A small hand-operated electronic device used to control a pointer on a computer screen by moving it along a surface.
computer pointing device

Examples

Please move the mouse to select an icon on your desktop.
Many laptop users prefer a wireless mouse for convenience.
The old mouse with a rolling ball works differently from modern optical ones.

Synset Examples

a mouse takes much more room than a trackball

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trackball - a computer input device consisting of a ball housed in a socket

Etymology

Originally from Old English 'mūs' (the rodent); applied to the device from its size and shape resemblance to a mouse.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03799022-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ maʊs / (AmE), / maʊs / (BrE)
to move or operate a computer mouse in order to control the cursor or select items on a screen
operate a computer mouse

Examples

She expertly moused over the links to navigate the website.
If you mouse to the corner of the screen, a menu will appear.
He spent hours mousing through complex graphic designs for his presentation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: click - to press a button on a computer mouse

Etymology

Derived from the noun 'mouse', referring to the computer pointing device, which was named for its resemblance to the animal.

6. NOUN

/ maʊs / (AmE), / maʊs / (BrE)
a small rodent with a pointed snout, small ears, a slender hairless tail, and an elongated body, often found in a variety of environments
small rodent with pointed snout and tail

Examples

The mouse darted under the cupboard as soon as the lights were turned on.
She bought a cage for her pet mouse and filled it with bedding.
A mouse can squeeze through surprisingly small holes in the wall.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: rat - a larger rodent similar in appearance to a mouse
  • Synonym: vole - a small rodent similar to a mouse but with a stouter body and shorter tail
  • Synonym: shrew - a small, mouse-like mammal with a long pointed nose

Etymology

From Old English 'mūs,' from Proto-Germanic '*mūs', from Proto-Indo-European '*mūs' (mouse).
