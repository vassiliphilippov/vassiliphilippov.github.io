wicket

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɪkɪt / (BrE)
a small arch made of wire that forms part of the course in croquet and through which the ball must pass
croquet arch; croquet equipment

Examples

Each player aimed to guide the ball through the wicket with precision.
The wicket was slightly bent after yesterday's match.
You must hit your ball through every wicket to complete the course.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hoop - another word for the croquet arch or wicket

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɪkɪt / (BrE)
a small gate or door, especially one that is part of a larger door or gate
small gate or door in a larger entrance

Examples

The old castle had a wicket set within its massive wooden door.
The guard let me through the wicket while the main gate remained closed.
A small wicket in the fence allowed easy access to the garden.

Etymology

From Old Northern French 'wiket' meaning 'small door or gate'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɪkɪt / (BrE)
in cricket, a set of three wooden stumps topped with two crosspieces (bails), placed at each end of the pitch and used as a target for the bowler
set of stumps and bails in cricket

Examples

The batsman was bowled out when the ball hit the wicket.
Before starting the match, the umpire inspected the wicket closely.
Damage to the wicket during play can lead to a delay in the game.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stumps - the three upright wooden posts forming part of the wicket in cricket

Etymology

From Middle English 'wiket', meaning a small gate or opening.

4. NOUN

/ ˈwɪkɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɪkɪt / (BrE)
a small window or opening, especially one in a door or wall, through which business is transacted or communication takes place
small transaction window or opening

Examples

The clerk handed the documents through the wicket to the customer.
She slipped her payment under the glass wicket at the train station.
The guard peered out from the wicket before unlocking the door.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: window - an opening in a wall to admit light and air

Etymology

From Old Northern French 'wiket' (small door or gate), diminutive of Old Norse 'vika' (corner, bend).
