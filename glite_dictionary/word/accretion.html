accretion

1. NOUN

/ əˈkriːʃən / (AmE), / əˈkriːʃən / (BrE)
(law) an increase in a beneficiary's share of an estate, as when another beneficiary dies, fails to meet a condition, or rejects the inheritance.
increase in inheritance share (law)
legal

Examples

When John refused his inheritance, his share of the estate passed by accretion to his siblings.
Under the doctrine of accretion, any unclaimed portion of the estate was distributed among the remaining heirs.
The will included a clause addressing accretion in case a beneficiary failed to survive the testator.

Etymology

From Latin accretio, from accrescere (to grow).

2. NOUN

/ əˈkriʃən / (AmE), / əˈkriːʃən / (BrE)
(astronomy) The process by which a celestial object forms as gravity pulls together surrounding gas and matter.
formation of celestial object by gravity

Examples

The planet formed through the accretion of dust and gas in its early years.
Researchers study accretion disks around young stars to understand how solar systems are born.
Massive black holes grow larger by the accretion of surrounding matter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aggregation - the process of collecting or gathering together
  • Antonym: erosion - the gradual wearing away of something by natural forces

Etymology

From Latin accretio (growth), from accrescere (to grow).

3. NOUN

/ əˈkriʃən / (AmE), / əˈkriːʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(Geology) An increase in land area caused by the natural deposition of sediment, especially by waterborne materials such as alluvium.
increase in land by sediment deposit (geology)
medical

Examples

Coastal accretion has added several acres to the shoreline over the past century.
The river’s steady flow promotes accretion of fertile soil on its banks.
Legal disputes sometimes arise over land created by accretion in floodplains.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: erosion - the gradual destruction or diminution of land, especially by water or wind

Etymology

From Latin accretio (a growing to), from accrescere (to increase).
