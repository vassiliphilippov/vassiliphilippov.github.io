to scavenge

1. VERB

/ ˈskævɪndʒ / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒ / (BrE)
to search for and collect anything usable from discarded waste or refuse
search and collect discarded items

Examples

After the storm, people began to scavenge for anything useful among the debris.
The stray dogs scavenge through rubbish bins looking for scraps of food.
She used to scavenge old furniture from alleys and restore it at home.

Synset Examples

She scavenged the garbage cans for food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forage - to search widely for food or provisions

Etymology

From Old French *escharver* (to clean out), later influenced by 'scavenger.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01384022-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈskævəndʒ / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒ / (BrE)
(of an animal or person) to feed on dead or decaying matter, or on discarded refuse.
feed on dead or discarded matter

Examples

Vultures scavenge on carcasses left behind by predators in the savanna.
Urban foxes often scavenge through rubbish bins for food scraps.
The hyenas began to scavenge once the lions abandoned their kill.

Synset Examples

hyenas scavenge

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forage - searching widely for food or provisions
  • Antonym: graze - to eat grass in a field (for herbivores)

Etymology

From Middle English 'scavanger', an official in charge of street cleaning, from Old North French 'escauenger'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01180270-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈskævɪndʒ / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒ / (BrE)
to remove refuse or unwanted material from a place, especially in order to keep it clean
remove refuse or unwanted material

Examples

Teams were hired to scavenge the park after the festival.
Each morning, workers scavenge the streets to collect debris left by pedestrians.
Volunteers scavenged the riverbank, removing plastic bottles and other trash.

Synset Examples

Scavenge a street

Etymology

From Old French *eschever* (to avoid) and later influenced by 'scavenger'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01536831-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈskævɪndʒ / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒ / (BrE)
to remove unwanted or harmful substances from a place, material, or system, especially as part of a cleaning or purifying process
remove unwanted substances

Examples

The filter is designed to scavenge impurities from the water supply.
Certain chemicals can scavenge free radicals in the body, reducing cell damage.
Engineers developed a system to scavenge sulfur dioxide from industrial emissions.

Etymology

From scavenger (derived from Middle English 'scawager', an official who collected taxes or cleaned streets).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00180982-v
Synset POS: v
