to engulf

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡʌlf / (BrE)
to absorb or involve someone completely; to devote fully to a task or activity
to absorb or involve fully

Examples

She allowed grief to engulf her after the loss of her friend.
Passion for chess engulfed him during his teenage years.
Sometimes, work can engulf us so completely that we forget to take breaks.

Synset Examples

He immersed himself into his studies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to immerse - to involve deeply; absorb fully
  • Synonym: to engage - to occupy or involve fully

Etymology

From Old French *engloutir*, from Latin *ingluttire* (to swallow up).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00602438-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡʌlf / (BrE)
to completely cover or surround something or someone, often suddenly and forcefully
to cover or surround completely

Examples

Flames quickly engulfed the entire building, leaving little behind.
She felt a wave of sadness engulf her as she read the letter.
Fog engulfed the city, making it hard to see more than a few feet ahead.

Synset Examples

The bright light engulfed him completely

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to submerge - to go under water or another liquid; to soak completely
  • Synonym: to immerse - to completely cover in liquid; to involve deeply
  • Antonym: to uncover - to remove a covering from; to reveal

Etymology

From Old French *engolfer*, from en- (in) + golfe (gulf, whirlpool), originally from Late Latin *gulfus*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01584066-v
Synset POS: v
