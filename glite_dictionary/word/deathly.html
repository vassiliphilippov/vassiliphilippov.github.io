deathly

1. ADV

/ ˈdɛθli / (AmE), / ˈdɛθli / (BrE)
To a degree resembling death; extremely or intensely (used to qualify an adjective, especially with negative connotations).
to a degree resembling death

Examples

After hearing the shocking news, she turned deathly pale and slumped into her chair.
The hospital room was deathly quiet after the doctors left.
He was deathly afraid of heights and refused to look down.

Synset Examples

he was deathly pale

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deadly - causing or able to cause death; fatal

Etymology

From 'death' + '-ly', dating from the Middle English period.

2. ADJ

/ ˈdɛθli / (AmE), / ˈdɛθli / (BrE)
having the appearance or characteristics of death; resembling death in pallor, stillness, or silence
resembling death in appearance

Examples

She had a deathly pallor after being ill for so long.
There was a deathly silence in the room when the news was announced.
His face looked deathly white under the harsh lights.

Synset Examples

a deathly pallor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lifeless - showing no signs of life or movement
  • Synonym: ghastly - causing great horror or fear; resembling a ghost, very pale
  • Antonym: lively - full of life and energy

Etymology

Formed from 'death' + '-ly,' meaning 'like death.'

4. ADV

/ ˈdɛθli / (AmE), / ˈdɛθli / (BrE)
Extremely; to a degree resembling death or suggestive of death, often used to intensify adjectives, especially those describing fear, silence, or cold.
to an extreme or intense degree

Examples

The room was deathly silent as everyone waited for the results.
She became deathly pale when she heard the shocking news.
He was deathly afraid of heights, refusing to go near the edge of the cliff.

Synset Examples

extremely unpleasant
she is super smart

as a child, I was deathly afraid of snakes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deadly - extremely or causing death
  • Antonym: slightly - to a small degree

Etymology

From 'death' + '-ly', formed on the analogy of 'deadly', first recorded in Middle English.
