intemperate

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (BrE)
Prone to excessive indulgence of bodily appetites, especially alcohol.
given to excess, especially with alcohol

Examples

His intemperate habits led to frequent absences from work.
The novel depicts an intemperate man unable to resist the lure of drink.
Critics accused the politician of intemperate behavior during the campaign.

Synset Examples

a hard drinker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excessive - exceeding normal or proper limits
  • Antonym: temperate - showing moderation or self-restraint

Etymology

From Latin 'intemperatus' (immoderate), from 'in-' (not) + 'temperatus' (tempered, moderate).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (BrE)
(of weather or climate) severe or extreme; not mild.
(of weather) severe; not mild

Examples

The explorers struggled to adapt to the intemperate climate in the region.
Certain plants cannot survive in an intemperate environment.
The area is known for its intemperate weather, with harsh winters and scorching summers.

Synset Examples

an intemperate climate
intemperate zones

Related Terms

  • Antonym: temperate - (of climate) mild; moderate in temperature or weather

Etymology

From Latin 'intemperatus' (immoderate, excessive), from 'in-' (not) + 'temperatus' (moderate, tempered).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02411754-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɛmpərət / (BrE)
(of a person's actions or feelings) showing a lack of self-control; excessive or extreme.
excessive or lacking self-control

Examples

The coach was criticized for his intemperate remarks after the match.
Her intemperate spending habits soon led to financial trouble.
He displayed intemperate anger during the heated discussion.

Synset Examples

intemperate rage

Related Terms

  • Antonym: temperate - showing moderation or self-restraint

Etymology

From Latin intemperatus, meaning 'unrestrained' or 'immoderate'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a
