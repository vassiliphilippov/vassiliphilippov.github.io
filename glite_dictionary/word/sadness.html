sadness

1. NOUN

/ ˈsædnəs / (AmE), / ˈsædnəs / (BrE)
A feeling or state of being unhappy or sorrowful.
the state of being sad

Examples

A wave of sadness washed over her as she read the letter.
His sadness was apparent even though he tried to smile.
After the movie ended, there was a lingering sadness in the room.

Synset Examples

she tired of his perpetual sadness

Related Terms

  • Antonym: happiness - the state of being happy
  • Synonym: sorrow - deep distress or grief
  • Degree Higher: misery - a state of great suffering or unhappiness
  • Degree Lower: disappointment - sadness caused by unfulfilled expectations

Etymology

From Old English sædnes (weariness, sadness).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsædnəs / (AmE), / ˈsædnəs / (BrE)
A feeling or state of unhappiness or sorrow; the quality of being sad or mournful.
feeling or state of being sad

Examples

She tried to hide her sadness when she heard the news.
A wave of sadness washed over him as he remembered his childhood.
His eyes reflected the sadness he felt after losing his closest friend.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: happiness - the state of being happy; joy
  • Synonym: melancholy - a deep, persistent sadness or gloom

Etymology

From Old English sædnes (fullness, satiety), later influenced by 'sad.'

3. NOUN

/ ˈsædnəs / (AmE), / ˈsædnəs / (BrE)
The feeling of being unhappy or sorrowful, especially for a significant period or with a recognizable cause.
feeling of being unhappy or sorrowful

Examples

There was a deep sadness in his eyes after he heard the news.
She felt overwhelming sadness when her best friend moved away.
Writing in her journal helped her cope with the sadness she was experiencing.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: happiness - feeling of being happy
  • Degree Higher: grief - intense sorrow, especially caused by someone's death
  • Synonym: melancholy - a deep, lasting feeling of sadness; often without clear cause

Etymology

From sad + -ness, tracing to Old English sæd (sated, weary, later sorrowful) and the Old English suffix -ness.
