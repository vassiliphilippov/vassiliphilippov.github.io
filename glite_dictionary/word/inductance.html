inductance

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌktəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌktəns / (BrE)
The property of an electrical circuit or component that causes an electromotive force (EMF) to be generated by a change in the current flowing through it.
property of generating EMF by current change
medical

Examples

The inductance of the coil increases as more turns are added.
High inductance in a circuit can delay changes in current flow.
Engineers measured the inductance to design an efficient transformer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: capacitance - the ability of a system to store electric charge
  • Synonym: resistance - the opposition a material offers to the flow of electric current

Etymology

From 'induct' + '-ance', after the pattern of 'resistance' and 'capacitance'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌktəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌktəns / (BrE)
an electrical component, typically a coil, that introduces inductance into a circuit by opposing changes in current
electrical component introducing inductance

Examples

The inductance in the circuit helps filter out unwanted electrical noise.
You can increase the inductance by adding more turns to the coil.
Measuring the inductance accurately is crucial for designing efficient transformers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inductor - a component that provides inductance in a circuit
  • Antonym: capacitor - an electrical component that stores energy in an electric field
  • Antonym: resistor - an electrical component that resists the flow of current

Etymology

From Latin 'inducere' (to lead in), influenced by scientific use in 19th-century physics.
