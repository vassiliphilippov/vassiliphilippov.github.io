to sympathise

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪmpəˌθaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪmpəθaɪz / (BrE)
to share or understand the feelings of someone, especially by showing compassion or support in times of trouble
share or understand someone’s feelings
british

Examples

It's often hard to sympathise with someone until you've experienced a similar situation yourself.
She sympathised deeply with the victims of the disaster.
Many readers will sympathise with the character’s struggles in the novel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to empathise - to understand and share the feelings of another
  • Synonym: to commiserate - to express sympathy or sorrow for another
  • Antonym: disregard - to ignore or pay no attention to someone's feelings

Etymology

From Greek 'sympatheia' (fellow feeling), via Late Latin and Old French.

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪmpəˌθaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪmpəθaɪz / (BrE)
to feel or express compassion or understanding for someone else's situation or feelings
feel or show compassion
british

Examples

I sympathise with you over the loss of your pet.
Many people sympathise with the victims of natural disasters.
She always tries to sympathise with her friends when they are upset.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to empathize - to understand and share the feelings of another

Etymology

From Greek sympatheia (fellow feeling), via Late Latin sympathizare and French sympathiser.

3. VERB

/ ˈsɪm.pə.ˌθaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪm.pə.θaɪz / (BrE)
to show understanding for someone’s feelings or situation; to feel or express sympathy
feel or show sympathy

Examples

I completely sympathise with your frustration after the delay.
She sympathised with him when she heard about his loss.
Many customers sympathise with the workers who are on strike.

Synset Examples

You don't need to explain — I understand!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to empathise - to understand and share another person's feelings
  • Synonym: pity - to feel sorrow or compassion for someone
  • Antonym: to antagonize - to cause someone to become hostile

Etymology

From Greek sympatheia via Latin sympathia, meaning 'fellow feeling'.
