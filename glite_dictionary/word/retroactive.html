retroactive

1. ADJ

/ ˌrɛtroʊˈæktɪv / (AmE), / ˌrɛtrəʊˈæktɪv / (BrE)
(of a law, decision, or action) applying or referring to a period before its enactment or occurrence.
applying to a past period

Examples

The government passed a retroactive tax law affecting income earned last year.
Her promotion was made retroactive to January, so she received additional pay.
The court ruled that the new regulation would not be retroactive.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prospective - likely to happen or expected to happen in the future
  • Synonym: retrospective - looking back on or dealing with past events or situations

Etymology

From Latin 'retroactus', past participle of 'retroagere' (to drive back).

2. ADJ

/ ˌrɛtroʊˈæktɪv / (AmE), / ˌrɛtrəʊˈæktɪv / (BrE)
(of a law, decision, or action) taking effect from a date in the past, so as to affect earlier situations or actions.
affecting the past; applying to earlier events

Examples

The new law is retroactive and will affect contracts signed years ago.
Employees will receive retroactive pay for the period before the wage increase was officially approved.
The court ruled that the tax increase could not be retroactive.

Synset Examples

retroactive tax increase
an ex-post-facto law
retro pay

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prospective - (of a law or action) affecting future events only

Etymology

From Latin 'retro' (backward) + 'activus' (active).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01890843-s
Synset POS: s
