universal

1. NOUN

/ ˌjuːnɪˈvɜrsəl / (AmE), / ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəl / (BrE)
(Linguistics) A grammatical rule or linguistic feature that appears in all human languages.
a feature present in all languages

Examples

The search for a true universal has been a central question in linguistic theory for decades.
Some scholars debate whether recursion is an actual universal or merely common among languages.
Word order is not consistent enough across languages to be considered a universal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: linguistic universal - a feature or property common to all human languages

Etymology

From Latin universalis (of or belonging to all).

7. NOUN

/ ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːrsəl / (AmE), / ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəl / (BrE)
In logic or philosophy, a proposition or concept that is asserted of all members of a class.
(logic) property or proposition true of all members of a class

Examples

In medieval philosophy, the debate over universals was central to understanding the nature of reality.
A universal asserts that every entity in a certain class shares a property.
The statement 'All humans are mortal' is an example of a universal in logic.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: particular - (logic) a proposition or property that is true of some but not all members of a class

Etymology

From Latin universalis (relating to all), from universus (whole, entire).
