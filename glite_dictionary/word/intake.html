intake

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌteɪk / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌteɪk / (BrE)
An opening through which fluid enters a tube, pipe, or container.
opening for admitting fluid

Examples

The water flows into the reservoir through the intake at the dam.
Engineers inspected the intake to ensure it was free from debris.
A blockage in the air intake can reduce an engine’s efficiency.

Etymology

From 'in' + 'take', originally meaning 'what is taken in'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌteɪk / (AmE), / ˈɪn.teɪk / (BrE)
the act of breathing in; the drawing in of air or other gases, especially into the lungs
the act of breathing in

Examples

His rapid intake of breath signaled his surprise.
Regular exercise can increase your lung intake during physical activity.
The doctor measured the patient's oxygen intake to assess lung function.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: exhalation - the act of breathing out
  • Synonym: inhalation - the action of inhaling or breathing in

Etymology

From 'in' + 'take'; attested since the late 18th century.

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌteɪk / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌteɪk / (BrE)
the process of taking food or drink into the body through the mouth
act of consuming food or drink

Examples

Doctors often recommend reducing your daily intake of sugar for better health.
She increased her water intake during the hot summer months.
Tracking calorie intake can help with weight management.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consumption - the act of eating or drinking something

Etymology

From 'in' + 'take', first used in the 19th century.
