refractive

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈfræktɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈfraktɪv / (BrE)
relating to or capable of causing refraction, especially of light or other waves.
relating to refraction of light or waves

Examples

The lens has a high refractive index, making it ideal for precise optics.
Changes in the eye's refractive power can cause vision problems like nearsightedness.
Scientists studied the refractive properties of crystals to develop better lasers.

Synset Examples

the refractive characteristics of the eye

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reflective - relating to the reflection of light or waves

Etymology

From Medieval Latin refractivus, from refractus (past participle of refringere, to break up).

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈfræktɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈfræktɪv / (BrE)
(of a material or surface) able to change the direction of a light or sound wave as it passes through.
bending light or sound waves

Examples

Glass is highly refractive, which is why it bends light passing through it.
The lens's refractive properties are essential for focusing images onto the retina.
Scientists measured the refractive index of water to better understand how light travels in the ocean.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reflective - tending to reflect light or sound waves rather than refract them
  • Synonym: refractile - capable of refracting, alternative form; used in optics

Etymology

From Medieval Latin refractivus, from Latin refractus (broken up, bent back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02320598-s
Synset POS: s
