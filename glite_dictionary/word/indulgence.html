indulgence

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (BrE)
foolish or senseless behavior; an act of allowing oneself to do something unwise or imprudent.
foolish or senseless behavior

Examples

His weekend gambling spree was another indulgence that left him regretting his choices.
She apologized for her momentary indulgence in gossip at the office party.
Sometimes a small indulgence is harmless, but repeated carelessness can cause real problems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: folly - lack of good sense or judgment; foolish action
  • Antonym: prudence - wise or cautious behavior; exercising sound judgment

Etymology

From Latin indulgentia (leniency, concession), from indulgere (to be kind or yielding).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (BrE)
(in the Roman Catholic Church) a remission of the temporal punishment still due for sins after absolution, granted by the pope or the Church.
remission of punishment for sins in Catholicism

Examples

The church granted an indulgence to all who made the pilgrimage to Rome.
Martin Luther criticized the sale of indulgences in his famous Ninety-five Theses.
Many people in the Middle Ages purchased indulgences in hopes of reducing their time in purgatory.

Synset Examples

in the Middle Ages the unrestricted sale of indulgences by pardoners became a widespread abuse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absolution - formal release from guilt, obligation, or punishment, especially in a religious context

Etymology

From Latin indulgentia (fondness, leniency), via Old French indulgence.

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌl.dʒəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌl.dʒəns / (BrE)
the act of allowing oneself or someone else to have or enjoy something, especially something considered to be a special pleasure or not strictly necessary
the act of gratifying a desire

Examples

Sometimes a little indulgence in chocolate is all you need to lift your mood.
Her weekend spa visit was a rare indulgence she allowed herself after a stressful week.
He regretted his indulgence in late-night snacks when he stepped on the scale.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-restraint - the act of holding back or controlling one's desires

Etymology

From Latin indulgentia (kindness, leniency), from indulgere (to be kind or yielding).

Source: WordNet

4. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (BrE)
the act or habit of allowing someone to have or do what they want, especially too much or too often
allowing someone's wishes, often too much

Examples

His grandmother's indulgence made him a very spoiled child.
Teachers should balance indulgence with clear expectations.
Too much indulgence can lead to a lack of discipline.

Synset Examples

too much indulgence spoils a child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leniency - the quality of being more merciful or tolerant than expected
  • Antonym: strictness - the quality of being rigorous or not allowing leniency

Etymology

From Latin indulgentia (kindness, favor, leniency).

5. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒəns / (BrE)
the act or habit of allowing oneself to satisfy one's whims or desires, often with little self-restraint
the act of yielding to desires

Examples

A little indulgence now and then is good for the soul.
His indulgence in desserts made it difficult to lose weight.
She allowed herself the indulgence of sleeping in on Sundays.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-control - the ability to restrain one's impulses or desires
  • Antonym: restraint - the act of holding back or limiting oneself
  • Degree Higher: self-indulgence - excessive or unrestrained gratification of one's desires

Etymology

From Latin indulgentia (leniency, kindness, favor), from indulgere (to be kind or yielding).
