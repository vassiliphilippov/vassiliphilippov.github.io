corral

1. VERB

/ kəˈræl / (AmE), / kəˈrɑːl / (BrE)
to gather together and enclose (animals or people) in a corral or confined area
enclose in a corral or confine

Examples

The ranchers worked together to corral the cattle before the storm arrived.
It took nearly an hour to corral all the sheep into the pen.
Volunteers tried to corral the children for a group photo.

Synset Examples

corral the horses

Related Terms

  • Synonym: herd - to gather and move a group of animals
  • Antonym: release - to set free from confinement

Etymology

From Spanish 'corral', meaning an enclosure for animals.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01603399-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ kəˈræl / (AmE), / kəˈrɑːl / (BrE)
an enclosure or pen for keeping livestock, especially cattle or horses
enclosure for cattle or horses

Examples

The ranchers led the cattle into the corral before dusk.
After the roundup, the mustangs were kept in the corral overnight.
The sturdy wooden corral can hold up to fifty animals at a time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pen - an enclosure for confining livestock

Etymology

From Spanish 'corral' meaning 'yard, enclosure', from Vulgar Latin 'currāle' (enclosure for vehicles, yard).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03129241-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ kəˈræl / (AmE), / kəˈræl / (BrE)
to arrange wagons, vehicles, or animals in a circle or enclosed area for protection or organization, especially as was done on the American frontier
to arrange wagons or animals in a circle

Examples

The settlers quickly corralled their wagons each night to guard against possible attacks.
They corralled the horses and supplies close together to create a safe perimeter.
During the reenactment, participants corralled the old wagons just as pioneers once did.

Etymology

From Spanish 'corral' (pen, enclosure), from Vulgar Latin 'currale' (enclosure for carts).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01468827-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ kəˈɹæl / (AmE), / kəˈral / (BrE)
to gather together and confine (people, animals, or things) in one place, especially to control or organize them
gather and confine

Examples

The organizer managed to corral everyone into the main hall for the meeting.
Security was able to corral the crowd and prevent them from going backstage.
She spent most of the afternoon corralling stray paperwork for the audit.

Synset Examples

corralling votes for an election

Related Terms

  • Synonym: collect - to bring or gather together
  • Antonym: to disperse - to scatter or spread apart

Etymology

From Spanish 'corral' (pen for animals), from Vulgar Latin 'curralis'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01384450-v
Synset POS: v
