to confound

1. VERB

/ kənˈfaʊnd / (AmE), / kənˈfaʊnd / (BrE)
to mistake one thing for another; to confuse or mix up (things or people)
mistake one thing for another

Examples

It is easy to confound these two species of birds because they look so similar.
The witness confounded the suspect with another man in her testimony.
Historians sometimes confound the facts when records are incomplete or unclear.

Synset Examples

you are confusing me with the other candidate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to confuse - to mistake one thing for another; to fail to distinguish between things

Etymology

From Old French 'confondre' and Latin 'confundere' meaning 'to pour together, mix up'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00621282-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kənˈfaʊnd / (AmE), / kənˈfaʊnd / (BrE)
to confuse or perplex someone, making them unable to think clearly
to confuse or perplex

Examples

The complex instructions confounded even the most experienced technicians.
Her unexpected reaction confounded everyone in the room.
These contradictory findings continue to confound researchers.

Synset Examples

These questions confuse even the experts
This question completely threw me
This question befuddled even the teacher

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to confuse - to make someone unable to think clearly or understand something
  • Synonym: to perplex - to make someone feel completely baffled
  • Antonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge or understanding

Etymology

From Latin 'confundere' meaning 'to pour together, mix up, confuse'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00623406-v
Synset POS: v
