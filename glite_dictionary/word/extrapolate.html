to extrapolate

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
To infer or estimate something unknown by extending or projecting known information.
infer by extending known data

Examples

Scientists often extrapolate future climate trends from current data.
You can't always extrapolate results from small studies to the general population.
From the limited evidence, she tried to extrapolate what might happen next.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infer - draw a conclusion based on evidence and reasoning

Etymology

Formed from Latin 'extra' (outside) and 'polare' (to make smooth, polish), modelled after 'interpolate'.

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
to infer or estimate information about an unknown area or situation by extending or projecting from known data or experience
infer unknown from known data

Examples

Scientists often extrapolate trends from limited data to predict future outcomes.
It is risky to extrapolate from one small example to an entire population.
She extrapolated the possible effects of the new policy based on past experience.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infer - to deduce or conclude information from evidence and reasoning

Etymology

From Latin extra (outside) + English 'interpolate' (to insert), modelled as 'to project outwards.'

3. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
To estimate or infer an unknown value or result by extending or projecting from known information or trends.
estimate by projecting from known data

Examples

By analyzing last year's sales data, we can extrapolate future market trends.
Scientists sometimes extrapolate the effects of climate change based on current temperature increases.
You shouldn't extrapolate too much from a small sample size, as it may not represent the whole population.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to interpolate - insert something (often values) between fixed points; also to estimate between known values

Etymology

From Latin 'extra' (outside) + 'polar' (to polish, smooth, but here by analogy to interpolate), coined in the late 19th century based on 'interpolate'.
