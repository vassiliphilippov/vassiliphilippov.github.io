citrange

1. NOUN

/ sɪˈtreɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪˈtrɑːndʒ / (BrE)
A hybrid citrus fruit, produced by crossing a sweet orange with a trifoliate orange, often more aromatic and acidic than typical oranges.
hybrid citrus fruit (orange × trifoliate orange)

Examples

The orchard included several rows of citrange trees thriving in the mild climate.
She found the citrange too sour to eat raw but excellent for making marmalade.
The unique flavor of citrange makes it popular among chefs seeking something different from standard oranges.

Etymology

From 'cit(rus)' + 'ange' (from 'orange'), indicating its hybrid origin between citrus and orange varieties.

2. NOUN

/ sɪˈtreɪndʒ / (AmE), / sɪˈtrɑːndʒ / (BrE)
A hybrid citrus fruit resulting from a cross between a sweet orange and the trifoliate orange, known for its aromatic, acidic flavor and use in beverages and marmalade.
a hybrid citrus fruit

Examples

The citrange is often used to make tangy marmalades due to its high acidity.
Farmers developed the citrange to combine the hardiness of trifoliate orange with the sweetness of regular oranges.
She added slices of citrange to her beverage to give it a unique, zesty flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: orange - a sweet, round citrus fruit with a tough bright reddish-yellow rind

Etymology

Formed from 'citrus' and 'orange'; originally coined in early 20th-century horticulture.
