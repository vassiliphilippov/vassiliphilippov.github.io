plantigrade

1. ADJ

/ ˈplæn.təˌɡreɪd / (AmE), / ˈplæn.tɪ.ɡreɪd / (BrE)
(of mammals) walking so that the entire sole of the foot touches the ground, as seen in animals like humans and bears.
walking on the sole of the foot

Examples

Bears are plantigrade animals, walking on the full length of their feet.
Humans, unlike cats and dogs, have a plantigrade stance when moving.
The evolutionary advantages of a plantigrade gait include greater stability and endurance.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: digitigrade - walking on the toes with the heel elevated off the ground
  • Antonym: unguligrade - walking on the tips of the toes, usually hooved animals

Etymology

From Latin plantigradus, from planta (sole of the foot) + gradus (step).

2. NOUN

/ ˈplæn.tɪˌɡreɪd / (AmE), / ˈplæn.tɪ.ɡreɪd / (BrE)
an animal that walks so that the entire sole of the foot touches the ground, such as a bear or a human
animal walking with entire sole on ground

Examples

Bears are classic examples of plantigrade mammals, walking with their entire sole touching the ground.
Unlike cats, which are digitigrade, humans are plantigrade.
Many ancient mammals were plantigrade, but some evolved different walking styles.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: digitigrade - an animal that walks on its toes, not touching the ground with the heels

Etymology

From Latin 'planta' (sole of the foot) + 'gradus' (step).
