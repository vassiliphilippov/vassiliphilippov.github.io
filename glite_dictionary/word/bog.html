bog

1. VERB

/ bɑɡ / (AmE), / bɒɡ / (BrE)
(of a process or activity) to become stuck or slow down, especially due to difficulty or complexity.
to become stuck or slowed down

Examples

The project began to bog down once new regulations were introduced.
She tends to bog down in details instead of focusing on the bigger picture.
Discussions may bog if everyone talks at once.

Synset Examples

She bogged down many times while she wrote her dissertation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stall - to stop making progress, especially unexpectedly

Etymology

From the noun 'bog', originally from Irish and Scottish Gaelic 'bogach' (soft, marshy ground).

2. VERB

/ bɑg / (AmE), / bɒɡ / (BrE)
to cause to slow down or become stuck, especially in a difficult situation or process
cause to slow down or get stuck

Examples

We shouldn't let the project bog in unnecessary bureaucracy.
The discussion started to bog when they argued over details.
Without clear leadership, the reforms may bog and lose momentum.

Synset Examples

The vote would bog down the house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bog down - to become or cause to become stuck or slowed

3. NOUN

/ bɑɡ / (AmE), / bɒɡ / (BrE)
an area of wet, spongy ground made up of decaying vegetation, often with poor drainage and unsuitable for farming, sometimes used as a source of fuel when dried
wet, spongy ground; marshy land

Examples

The hikers got stuck in a bog after heavy rains flooded the trail.
Peat is often harvested from a bog and used as fuel in some countries.
The scientist studied rare plants that only grow in this remote bog.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swamp - an area of low-lying, uncultivated ground where water collects; larger and with more open water than a bog
  • Synonym: marsh - an area of low-lying wetland, often covered with grasses and reeds
  • Synonym: moor - an open area of uncultivated, often peaty land, sometimes waterlogged

Etymology

From Irish and Scottish Gaelic 'bogach' (soft place).
