Lepidosauria

1. NOUN

/ ˌlɛpɪdəˈsɔriə / (AmE), / ˌlɛpɪdəˈsɔːrɪə / (BrE)
a major order of diapsid reptiles that includes lizards, snakes, and tuataras
order of reptiles: lizards, snakes, tuataras

Examples

The Lepidosauria first appeared in the fossil record during the Mesozoic era.
Both lizards and snakes belong to the order Lepidosauria.
Scientists often study Lepidosauria to understand reptilian evolution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lizard - a type of reptile with scaly skin and typically four legs
  • Synonym: snake - a legless reptile with an elongated body
  • Synonym: tuatara - a rare reptile endemic to New Zealand

Etymology

From Greek 'lepis' (scale) + 'sauros' (lizard), referring to their scaled skin.

2. NOUN

/ ˌliːpɪdoʊˈsɔriə / (AmE), / ˌlɛpɪdəˈsɔːrɪə / (BrE)
a large subclass of diapsid reptiles that includes lizards, snakes, and tuataras
subclass of reptiles: lizards, snakes, tuataras

Examples

The Lepidosauria are distinguished by their unique arrangement of skull bones.
Scientists have discovered many extinct members of the Lepidosauria from the fossil record.
Modern representatives of the Lepidosauria include snakes, lizards, and the tuatara.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Squamata - order of reptiles including lizards and snakes
  • Antonym: Archosauria - reptilian clade including crocodiles, dinosaurs, and birds (contrasted group)

Etymology

From Greek 'lepidos' meaning 'scale' and 'sauros' meaning 'lizard'.
