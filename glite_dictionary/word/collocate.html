to collocate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑː.ləˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ə.keɪt / (BrE)
(of words) to tend to occur together more often than would be expected by chance.
(of words) to occur together frequently

Examples

The words ‘fast’ and ‘food’ often collocate in English.
In linguistics, we study which adjectives collocate with particular nouns.
Phrasal verbs frequently collocate with certain prepositions.

Synset Examples

The words ‘new’ and ‘world’ collocate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to co-occur - to occur together with something else

Etymology

From Latin collocare (to place together).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːləˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒləkeɪt / (BrE)
to arrange or place things together or side by side, especially in a specific order
arrange things side by side

Examples

The librarian carefully collocated the new books by genre on the shelf.
In linguistics, certain words tend to collocate with one another regularly.
He collocated all the spices and herbs in alphabetical order in the kitchen drawer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to arrange - to put things in a neat, attractive, or required order
  • Antonym: to disperse - to distribute things over a wide area

Etymology

From Latin collocatus, past participle of collocare (to place together), from com- (together) + locare (to place).
