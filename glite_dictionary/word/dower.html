dower

1. NOUN

/ ˈdaʊ.ər / (AmE), / ˈdaʊ.ə / (BrE)
A widow's legal right to a portion of her deceased husband's property, typically for her lifetime.
widow's right to husband's property
legal

Examples

After her husband died, she claimed her dower rights in the family estate.
The law ensured that a widow could not be deprived of her dower.
The couple arranged the property to include a dower for the wife.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dowry - property or money brought by a bride to her husband upon marriage

Etymology

From Old French *douaire*, from Medieval Latin *dotarium* (dowry), from Latin *dotare* (to endow).

3. NOUN

/ ˈdaʊ.ɚ / (AmE), / ˈdaʊ.ə / (BrE)
money or property that a woman brings to her husband at the time of marriage, especially as part of an arrangement or contract
property a woman brings to her marriage
archaic legal

Examples

She was entitled to a dower after her husband's death, as specified in the marriage contract.
Historical novels often mention the importance of a woman's dower in securing a good match.
The lawyer explained how the dower rights affected the inheritance of the estate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dowry - money or property brought by a bride to her husband upon marriage

Etymology

From Old French 'doaire', based on Latin 'dotare' (to endow), from 'dos' (dowry).
