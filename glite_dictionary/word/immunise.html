to immunise

1. VERB

/ ˈɪmjəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɪmjʊnaɪz / (BrE)
to make a person or animal resistant to an infectious disease, typically by inoculation or vaccination
make immune by vaccination or inoculation
british medical

Examples

Doctors recommend that children be immunised against measles at an early age.
The government launched a campaign to immunise the entire population against influenza.
If you travel to certain countries, you may need to be immunised for yellow fever.

Synset Examples

We vaccinate against scarlet fever
The nurse vaccinated the children in the school

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vaccinate - to treat with a vaccine to produce immunity

Etymology

From Latin 'immunis' (exempt, immune) via French 'immuniser'.

WordNet ID: oewn-00086587-v

2. VERB

/ ˈɪmjəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈɪmjʊnaɪz / (BrE)
(legal) To grant someone legal immunity from prosecution, typically in exchange for testimony or cooperation.
grant legal immunity from prosecution
legal

Examples

The witness was immunised in return for testifying against the main suspect.
Authorities agreed to immunise her so she could provide crucial information in court.
He refused to immunise the defendant, fearing it might undermine the investigation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to prosecute - institute legal proceedings against a person

Etymology

From Latin 'immunis' (exempt, free from).

Synset POS: v
