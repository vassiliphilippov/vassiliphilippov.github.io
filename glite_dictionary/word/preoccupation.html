preoccupation

1. NOUN

/ ˌpriːˌɑːkjəˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpriːˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of taking possession or occupancy of something before someone else does
prior occupancy; taking possession first

Examples

The preoccupation of the land by settlers prevented others from claiming it.
The court ruled that his preoccupation of the property was legitimate and binding.
Historical disputes often began with the preoccupation of valuable territory.

Etymology

From Latin praeoccupatio (the act of taking possession beforehand), from prae- (before) + occupatio (occupation).

2. NOUN

/ ˌpriːˌɑːkjuˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpriːˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
A state of being engrossed or absorbed in something; a persistent concern or thought.
the state of being absorbed in thought

Examples

His preoccupation with work made it hard for him to relax during vacations.
The teacher noticed her student's preoccupation and asked if something was wrong.
Despite the noise, her preoccupation with the problem kept her focused.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorption - the state of being deeply engaged or engrossed
  • Antonym: distraction - something that diverts attention

Etymology

From Latin 'praeoccupatio' (a previous seizure, anticipation).

3. NOUN

/ priˌɑːkjuˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / priˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
an idea or subject that dominates the mind and holds one's attention, often to the exclusion of other thoughts or activities
idea dominating the mind

Examples

Her preoccupation with work made it difficult for her to relax on weekends.
The dangers of climate change have become a major preoccupation for scientists worldwide.
His constant preoccupation with details sometimes slows down the project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsession - an idea or thought that continually preoccupies or intrudes on a person's mind
  • Antonym: distraction - a thing that prevents someone from concentrating on something else

Etymology

From French 'préoccupation', from Latin 'praeoccupatio' (preconceived notion).
