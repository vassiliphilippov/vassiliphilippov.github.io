pulsation

1. NOUN

/ pʌlˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / pʌlˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a regularly recurring increase and decrease in the strength or quantity of something, such as sound, light, or pressure
rhythmic increase and decrease

Examples

The pulsation of the music could be felt through the floor.
Astronomers detected a regular pulsation in the radio signals from the star.
The pulsation of blood in her veins was steady and strong.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - a single beat or throb, especially of the heart or arteries

Etymology

From Latin pulsatio (beating, striking), from pulsare (to beat).

2. NOUN

/ pʌlˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / pʌlˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a rhythmic throbbing or vibrating, especially the regular contraction and expansion of arteries with each beat of the heart
rhythmic throbbing, esp. of arteries
medical

Examples

Doctors can detect an irregular pulsation in the neck during a physical exam.
The strong pulsation of his arteries was visible beneath the skin.
Athletes often monitor their heart's pulsation after vigorous exercise.

Synset Examples

he could feel the beat of her heart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - the regular beating of blood through arteries, especially as felt at the wrist or neck

Etymology

From Latin pulsatio (a beating or striking).

3. NOUN

/ pʌlˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / pʌlˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a sudden, brief change in an electrical signal, or a series of such changes, especially in electronic systems
a sharp, brief change in an electrical signal

Examples

The technician noticed several pulsations on the oscilloscope during the experiment.
Faulty wiring can cause unexpected pulsations in an electrical circuit.
Astronomers monitored the regular pulsations coming from the distant pulsar.

Synset Examples

the pulsations seemed to be coming from a star

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - a rapid, brief increase in electrical, electromagnetic, or acoustical energy

Etymology

From Latin pulsare (to beat, strike) + -ation (forming nouns indicating action or process).
