withholding

1. NOUN

/ wɪðˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
income tax deducted from employees' wages by an employer and paid directly to the government on their behalf
income tax deducted from wages by employer

Examples

The company is responsible for calculating the correct amount of withholding for each worker.
My pay stub shows how much federal withholding was taken out this pay period.
If your withholding is too low, you might owe taxes at the end of the year.

2. NOUN

/ wɪðˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of deducting a portion of an employee's salary by an employer, often to cover taxes or other mandatory payments before the remainder is paid
salary deduction (esp. for taxes)

Examples

Your employer is responsible for withholding income taxes from your paycheck.
The government updated the withholding tables to reflect new tax rates.
She checked her pay stub to see how much withholding had reduced her net pay.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deduction - the process of subtracting an amount from a total, especially from pay

Etymology

From the verb 'withhold', from Old English 'wiðhealdan' (to keep back).

3. NOUN

/ wɪðˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of holding back or keeping something under one's control, especially by refusing or failing to give it when expected or required
the act of holding back something

Examples

The company's withholding of key financial information drew criticism from regulators.
Repeated withholding of medical records can hinder patient care.
She accused her colleague of withholding important details about the project.

Synset Examples

I resented his withholding permission
there were allegations of the withholding of evidence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retention - the act of keeping something rather than giving it up
  • Antonym: release - the act of setting something free or making it available

Etymology

From the verb 'withhold' + -ing, derived from Middle English, from Old English 'wiþhāldan' (to keep back).
