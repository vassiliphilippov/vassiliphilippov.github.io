to imprison

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈprɪzən / (AmE), / ɪmˈprɪz(ə)n / (BrE)
to confine someone or keep them in a place, especially as a punishment or by force
confine or put in prison

Examples

The rebels were imprisoned for more than ten years after the failed coup.
She felt as though she was imprisoned by the strict rules of her parents.
Governments sometimes imprison people for expressing their political views.

Synset Examples

His daughters are virtually imprisoned in their own house: he does not let them go out without a chaperone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to incarcerate - to put someone in prison; to confine
  • Antonym: release - to set someone free from confinement

Etymology

From Old French 'emprisoner', from 'en-' (in) + 'prison' (prison).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈprɪzən / (AmE), / ɪmˈprɪzən / (BrE)
to put someone in prison or keep them confined, especially as a legal punishment
confine or put in prison

Examples

The government decided to imprison the rebel leader after his capture.
She felt as though her strict parents tried to imprison her at home.
The activists were unfairly imprisoned for speaking out against the regime.

Synset Examples

The suspects were imprisoned without trial
the murderer was incarcerated for the rest of his life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to incarcerate - to imprison or confine
  • Antonym: release - to set free from confinement or prison

Etymology

From Old French emprisoner, from en- (in) + prison (prison).
