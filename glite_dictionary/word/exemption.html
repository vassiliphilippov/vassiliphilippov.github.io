exemption

1. NOUN

/ ɪɡˈzɛm(p)ʃən / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɛmpʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the action of officially freeing someone from an obligation, duty, or rule that others are subject to
official permission to not follow a rule or obligation

Examples

The company received an exemption from the new tax regulations for its first year.
She applied for a medical exemption so she would not have to participate in the tests.
Certain small businesses are eligible for an exemption from paying this fee.

Synset Examples

he was granted immunity from prosecution

Related Terms

  • Synonym: immunity - official protection from legal or other obligations

Etymology

From Latin 'exemptio' (a freeing, exemption), from 'eximere' (to take out, remove).

2. NOUN

/ ɪɡˈzɛm(p)ʃən / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɛmpʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
Official permission to not do something that others are required to do; freedom from an obligation, rule, or duty.
freedom from an obligation or duty

Examples

She received an exemption from paying property taxes due to her disability.
Military service exemption is granted to students in certain circumstances.
The charity applied for a tax exemption under federal law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: immunity - protection or exemption from something, especially an obligation or penalty
  • Antonym: liability - the state of being responsible for something, especially by law

Etymology

From Latin 'exemptio' (a removal), from 'eximere' (to take out).

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪɡˈzɛmpʃən / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɛmpʃən / (BrE)
a fixed amount or deduction allowed by law to reduce taxable income, based on a taxpayer's status or dependents
tax deduction for status or dependents
legal american

Examples

Because she has two children, her exemptions reduce the amount of income tax she must pay.
Taxpayers can claim an additional exemption if they are over the age of 65.
Each dependent you support qualifies you for a separate exemption on your federal tax return.

Synset Examples

additional exemptions are allowed for each dependent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deduction - an amount subtracted from taxable income

Etymology

From Latin exemptio (a freeing from), from eximere (to remove).
