collimator

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑː.ləˌmeɪ.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ɪˌmeɪ.tə / (BrE)
a small telescope or optical device attached to a larger instrument to help align its line of sight accurately
device for aligning a telescope

Examples

The astronomer adjusted the collimator to ensure the telescope was perfectly aligned with the North Star.
Before each observation, the technician checked the collimator for accuracy.
A faulty collimator can cause significant errors in astronomical measurements.

Etymology

From Latin 'collimare' (to direct in a straight line), a variant influenced by 'colligare' (to bind together).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑː.lɪˌmeɪ.tər / (AmE), / ˈkɒl.ɪ.meɪ.tər / (BrE)
an optical device consisting of a tube with a slit at one end and a convex lens at the other, designed to produce a parallel beam of light
device producing parallel light beams

Examples

The laboratory uses a collimator to produce a parallel beam of light for precise measurements.
A collimator is essential in telescopes to align optical components correctly.
Radiation therapy devices often employ a collimator to direct X-rays at a target area.

Etymology

From Latin *collimare* (to direct in a straight line), probably a misreading of 'collineare', meaning to align.
