toll

1. NOUN

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
the sound produced by a bell, especially when struck slowly and repeatedly
sound of a struck bell

Examples

The solemn toll of the funeral bell echoed through the valley.
At midnight, the distant toll of the church bell carried on the wind.
She paused to listen to the toll that marked the end of the service.

Synset Examples

saved by the bell
she heard the distant toll of church bells

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peal - the loud ringing sound of bells

Etymology

From Old English *toll* (ringing of a bell).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07391844-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
to charge a fee for the use of a road, bridge, or similar infrastructure
charge a fee for use (road, bridge, etc.)

Examples

The government plans to toll the new expressway to cover maintenance costs.
If they toll the bridge, fewer people might use it during rush hour.
Several cities have started to toll busy roads to reduce traffic congestion.

Synset Examples

Toll the bridges into New York City

Related Terms

  • Synonym: charge - to ask for payment as a price; demand payment
  • Antonym: to waive - to refrain from claiming or enforcing a fee or charge

Etymology

From Old English *toll* (a tax, charge), ultimately from Late Latin *toloneum*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02311708-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
a charge paid to use a particular road, bridge, or other public facility
fee for using a road or bridge

Examples

The highway charges a toll for each vehicle that passes through.
We stopped to pay the toll at the bridge before continuing our drive.
Many drivers avoid the route because the toll is considered too expensive.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fee - a sum paid for a service
  • Synonym: fare - the money paid for a journey on public transport

Etymology

From Old English 'tol' (tax, fee), from Old Norse 'tollr'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13344771-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
(of a bell) to ring slowly, especially as a signal or sign, often for a death or at a solemn event.
ring a bell slowly (often for a death)

Examples

The church bells tolled at midnight, signaling the passing of the old year.
A single bell tolled solemnly during the funeral procession.
Every evening, the cathedral bell tolls to signal the end of the day.

Synset Examples

For whom the bell tolls

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ring - to make a bell produce sound by striking it

Etymology

From Old English *tollian* (to ring a bell), influenced by Middle Dutch *tollen* (to pull, ring).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02186419-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
the amount of suffering, loss, or damage resulting from something; the cost or consequence to people or things
cost or loss resulting from something

Examples

The war took a heavy toll on the country's population.
Years of overwork had begun to exact their toll on his health.
The disaster's emotional toll on survivors was severe and long-lasting.

Synset Examples

the cost in human life was enormous
the price of success is hard work
what price glory?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cost - the price paid for something or the effort required to achieve it
  • Synonym: price - what must be given up or suffered to achieve something

Etymology

From Old English toll (custom, tax), ultimately from Late Latin toloneum (customs house).

Source: WordNet
