to relinquish

1. VERB

/ rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (BrE)
to voluntarily give up or surrender a possession, right, or claim
voluntarily give up; surrender

Examples

After years of leading the company, she decided to relinquish control to her successor.
He agreed to relinquish his claim to the property in exchange for compensation.
Sometimes parents must relinquish their own desires for the benefit of their children.

Synset Examples

I am relinquishing my bedroom to the long-term house guest
resign a claim to the throne

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surrender - to give up possession or control, often under pressure
  • Antonym: keep - to retain possession of something

Etymology

From Latin relinquere (to leave behind).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (BrE)
to voluntarily cease to keep or claim; give up
voluntarily give up or let go

Examples

After much consideration, she decided to relinquish her position as team leader.
He had to relinquish control of the project to a more experienced colleague.
They agreed to relinquish all claims to the property.

Synset Examples

I am foreswearing women forever

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surrender - to give up possession or control, often formally
  • Antonym: to retain - to keep possession of something

Etymology

From Latin 'relinquere' (to leave behind), from 're-' (back) + 'linquere' (to leave).

3. VERB

/ rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈlɪŋkwɪʃ / (BrE)
to let go of, release, or give up something, such as a hold, claim, or possession
to release or give up something

Examples

He refused to relinquish his hold on the steering wheel despite his exhaustion.
After much debate, the leader finally decided to relinquish control of the project.
Please relinquish your grip on the railing so others can pass.

Synset Examples

to give up or abandon

Examples

She refused to relinquish hope, even when the situation seemed desperate.
The country agreed to relinquish control of the disputed territory as part of the peace treaty.
