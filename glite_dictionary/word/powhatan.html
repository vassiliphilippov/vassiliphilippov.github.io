Powhatan

1. NOUN

/ ˈpaʊ.hə.tæn / (AmE), / ˈpaʊ.hə.tæn / (BrE)
a member of an Algonquian-speaking indigenous people who formerly lived in eastern Virginia
member of an Algonquian people of Virginia

Examples

Many Powhatan lived along the rivers of eastern Virginia.
The Powhatan played a significant role in early American history.
Artifacts of the Powhatan people can be found in local museums.

Etymology

From the name of a village and chief in what is now Virginia, possibly from an Algonquian language.

2. PROPN

/ ˈpaʊ.hə.tæn / (AmE), / ˈpaʊ.hə.tæn / (BrE)
an Indian chief (c. 1550–1618) who founded the Powhatan Confederacy of tribes in eastern Virginia and was the father of Pocahontas
Indian chief and founder of the Powhatan Confederacy

Examples

Chief Powhatan played a key role in the early years of English settlement at Jamestown.
The story of Pocahontas frequently references her father, Powhatan.
Historians still debate the full impact that Powhatan had on Native and colonial relations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Pocahontas - daughter of Powhatan, notable Native American figure

Etymology

From the Powhatan name Wahunsenacawh; 'Powhatan' also refers to his tribal nation and confederacy.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ paʊˈhætən / (AmE), / paʊˈhætən / (BrE)
the extinct Algonquian language formerly spoken by the Powhatan people of eastern Virginia
Algonquian language of the Powhatan

Examples

Linguists have reconstructed parts of the Powhatan language from colonial records and word lists.
Few written records of Powhatan remain, but some words were adopted into English.
The museum exhibit included a display about the history of Powhatan and its speakers.

Etymology

From the Native American tribal name Powhatan, from an Algonquian term meaning 'falls in a current of water.'
