to resign

1. VERB

/ rɪˈzaɪn / (AmE), / rɪˈzaɪn / (BrE)
To voluntarily leave a job, position, or office; to give up an official role or responsibility, typically by formal notice.
voluntarily leave a position or job

Examples

After the allegations came to light, the minister decided to resign from his post.
If you are unhappy in your job, you always have the option to resign.
The CEO resigned following the company's disappointing annual results.

Synset Examples

The Secretary of the Navy will leave office next month
The chairman resigned over the financial scandal

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quit - to leave a job or position
  • Synonym: to retire - to leave one's job, especially when reaching an age or after long service

Etymology

From Latin resignare (to unseal, cancel), via Old French resigner.

2. VERB

/ rɪˈzaɪn / (AmE), / rɪˈzaɪn / (BrE)
to officially leave a job, position, or responsibility, especially by informing your employer or organization
leave a job or position voluntarily

Examples

After much consideration, she decided to resign from her position at the company.
The manager will resign if the board does not accept his proposal.
He officially resigned last week and sent a letter to the entire department.

Synset Examples

She vacated the position when she got pregnant
The chairman resigned when he was found to have misappropriated funds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quit - to leave a job, position, or place, especially permanently or without intention to return
  • Degree Lower: to retire - to leave your job or stop working because of old age or ill health

Etymology

From Latin 'resignare' (to unseal, cancel), from 're-' (again) + 'signare' (to sign).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02372031-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈzaɪn / (AmE), / rɪˈzaɪn / (BrE)
to accept something unpleasant or inevitable; to submit oneself to a situation without resistance, often used reflexively as 'resign oneself to'
accept something as inevitable

Examples

Unable to change the outcome, she resigned herself to living in the city.
After months of searching for a new job without success, he finally resigned himself to his current position.
The team had to resign themselves to defeat after the opposing side scored the winning goal.

Synset Examples

He resigned himself to his fate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to submit - to accept or yield to a superior force or to the authority or will of another person
  • Antonym: to resist - to withstand the action or effect of something; to oppose

Etymology

From Latin resignare (to unseal, annul, relinquish), later evolving to mean 'give up, submit to'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00734417-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ rɪˈzaɪn / (AmE), / rɪˈzaɪn / (BrE)
to formally give up a right, possession, or claim; to relinquish or surrender something
formally give up a right or claim

Examples

After years of dispute, he finally decided to resign his claim to the property.
She chose to resign her rights to the inheritance in favor of her brother.
The prince was forced to resign his title after the scandal.

Synset Examples

I am relinquishing my bedroom to the long-term house guest
resign a claim to the throne

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relinquish - to voluntarily cease to keep or claim; give up
  • Synonym: to abdicate - to formally renounce a throne, right, or responsibility
  • Antonym: to retain - to keep possession of something

Etymology

From Latin resignare (to unseal, annul), from re- (again) + signare (to sign).
