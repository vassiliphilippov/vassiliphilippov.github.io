to underbid

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to offer to do work or supply goods for a lower price than a competitor, especially in an auction or contract situation
to bid lower than another

Examples

Several companies tried to underbid each other to win the government contract.
She decided not to underbid this time, as the profit margin was already low.
The contractor underbid his rivals and secured the renovation project.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: overbid - to offer more than others in a bidding process

Etymology

From 'under-' (meaning 'below') + 'bid' (offer a price), patterned after similar terms like 'underpay'.

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndɚˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to offer a lower price than a competitor, especially in bidding for a contract or item
offer a lower bid than another

Examples

We decided to underbid our competitors to win the contract.
If you underbid on a house, the seller may reject your offer.
She underbid everyone at the auction and walked away with a great deal.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to outbid - to offer more than someone else in a bidding process

Etymology

Formed from prefix 'under-' (below) + 'bid' (to offer a price).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02304885-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌʌndɚˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈbɪd / (BrE)
To bid less than the true value or strength of one's hand, especially in card games.
bid less than one's hand merits (cards)

Examples

She accidentally underbid her hand, missing the chance for a higher score.
Players are cautioned not to underbid when their cards are strong.
He has a tendency to underbid because he often doubts his luck.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: overbid - to bid more than the strength of the hand warrants (cards)

Etymology

From under- (less than) + bid (make an offer).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02305827-v
Synset POS: v
