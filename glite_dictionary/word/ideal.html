ideal

1. ADJ

/ aɪˈdiəl / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəl / (BrE)
existing only in the mind or as a concept, not in reality; forming a mental image rather than a real example
existing only as a concept or idea

Examples

Some philosophers argue that justice, as discussed in theory, is often only ideal and rarely achieved in practice.
The artist created an ideal landscape that could never exist in the real world.
Her essay contrasted the ideal city described in literature with the flaws of actual urban life.

Synset Examples

a poem or essay may be typical of its period in idea or ideal content

Etymology

From Latin idealis (relating to an idea), from idea.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00012708-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ aɪˈdiəl / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəl / (BrE)
A person or thing regarded as perfect or the best possible; a standard of perfection or excellence that one hopes to achieve.
a perfect standard or goal

Examples

For many athletes, winning an Olympic gold medal is the ideal they strive for.
The company’s new product is far from the ideal envisioned by its founders.
In his essay, he discussed the gap between reality and the ideal in modern society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: perfection - the condition, state, or quality of being free from all flaws or defects
  • Antonym: reality - the state of things as they actually exist, as opposed to an ideal

Etymology

From Late Latin idealis, from Latin idea (form, pattern) and Greek idea (form, pattern).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05932328-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ aɪˈdiəl / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəl / (BrE)
representing or embodying the highest standard of perfection or excellence; perfect or most suitable in every way
perfect; embodying the highest standard

Examples

She is the ideal candidate for the position because of her experience and skills.
The climate in this region is ideal for growing grapes.
He described the city as an ideal place to raise a family.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: perfect - having all the required or desirable elements; as good as possible
  • Antonym: flawed - imperfect; not meeting the highest standard

Etymology

From Late Latin idealis, from Latin idea (form, pattern).

4. ADJ

/ aɪˈdiːəl / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəl / (BrE)
Relating to the philosophical doctrine that ideas or the mind are the ultimate reality, as opposed to material objects; associated with idealism.
pertaining to the doctrine of ideas as reality

Examples

The philosopher explained an ideal system where only ideas truly exist.
Her arguments were grounded in an ideal conception of reality, not in physical phenomena.
Some schools of thought believe in an ideal world that is fundamentally mental rather than material.

Etymology

From Late Latin idealis (existing in idea), from Latin idea.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02756056-a
Synset POS: a

5. NOUN

/ aɪˈdiəl / (AmE), / aɪˈdɪəl / (BrE)
a person or thing regarded as a perfect example or model of a particular quality or standard
perfect example or model

Examples

For centuries, the Renaissance artist was considered the ideal of creative genius.
He has always been my ideal of what a true leader should be.
The company strives to be the ideal in customer service and innovation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: paragon - a person or thing viewed as a model of excellence
  • Synonym: exemplar - a typical or perfect example
  • Antonym: imperfect - lacking completeness or perfection

Etymology

From Late Latin idealis, from Latin idea (form, pattern).
