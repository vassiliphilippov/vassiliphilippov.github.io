takeaway

1. NOUN

/ ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (BrE)
a concession or a benefit given up by employees in labor negotiations, typically as part of an agreement to help reduce a company's costs
a concession by employees in labor talks

Examples

The union's agreement to fewer vacation days was a significant takeaway in the contract negotiations.
Management insisted on a takeaway regarding overtime pay to control rising costs.
Although some pay raises were granted, the healthcare changes were seen as a takeaway by many workers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: concession - something given up or conceded, especially in negotiations

2. NOUN

/ ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (BrE)
an act of gaining possession of the ball or puck from the opposing team, especially by intercepting a pass, in sports such as football or hockey
an act of gaining possession from the opponent (sports)

Examples

The defender's quick takeaway shifted the momentum of the game.
She led the league in takeaways with her aggressive style of play.
A crucial takeaway in the final minute secured their team's victory.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interception - an act of catching a ball that was thrown by an opponent (sports)

3. NOUN

/ ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈteɪkəweɪ / (BrE)
(British) Prepared food that is purchased to be eaten elsewhere, not on the premises.
sold food eaten off premises (British)
british

Examples

We picked up a takeaway for dinner because neither of us wanted to cook.
In the UK, there are many Indian and Chinese takeaways on every high street.
She ordered her favorite pizza as a takeaway after a long day at work.

Synset Examples

in England they call takeout food ‘takeaway’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: takeout - prepared food intended to be eaten off the premises (American)

Etymology

From the phrase 'take away,' referring to food taken away from the place of sale.
