to accost

1. VERB

/ əˈkɑst / (AmE), / əˈkɒst / (BrE)
to approach and speak to someone, often in a bold or aggressive way
approach and speak to someone boldly

Examples

As she walked through the park, a stranger suddenly accosted her and asked for directions.
The journalist was accosted by several angry protesters outside the building.
He felt uncomfortable when a group of salespeople accosted him in the shopping mall.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to confront - to face someone, often in a challenging way
  • Antonym: to ignore - to deliberately not pay attention to someone

Etymology

From Old French 'acoster' (to come alongside), from Latin 'ad-' (to) + 'costa' (side, rib).

2. VERB

/ əˈkɑst / (AmE), / əˈkɒst / (BrE)
to approach and address someone boldly or aggressively, often with a proposition or offer, especially of a sexual nature
approach and address someone boldly

Examples

The police arrested the man after he tried to accost several passersby late at night.
She felt uncomfortable when a stranger attempted to accost her with unwanted comments.
Prostitutes would often accost people walking alone on that street.

Synset Examples

he was solicited by a prostitute
The young man was caught soliciting in the park

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to solicit - to approach someone with a request or offer, especially of a sexual nature
  • Antonym: to ignore - to deliberately pay no attention to someone

Etymology

From French accoster, from Latin accostare (to go alongside).
