to commove

1. VERB

/ kəˈmoʊv / (AmE), / kəˈməʊv / (BrE)
to disturb or change the arrangement or position of; to agitate or stir up
to disturb or rearrange
archaic

Examples

The earthquake did not merely shake the ground, but commoved the entire city.
The sudden arrival of the messenger commoved the tranquil assembly.
He feared that any rash action might commove the delicate balance of power in the region.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disturb - to interrupt the normal arrangement or position
  • Synonym: to disarrange - to upset the order or arrangement

Etymology

From Latin commovēre (to move together, to disturb), from com- (together) + movēre (to move).

2. VERB

/ kəˈmoʊv / (AmE), / kəˈməʊv / (BrE)
to cause to be agitated, excited, or roused; to stir up or disturb
stir up, agitate, or excite
archaic

Examples

The leader's passionate speech commoved the crowd into action.
News of the disaster commoved the entire nation.
Her sudden arrival commoved the quiet atmosphere of the meeting.

Synset Examples

The speaker charged up the crowd with his inflammatory remarks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to agitate - to stir up or excite (emotions, feelings, or actions)
  • Antonym: calm - to make quiet or peaceful; to soothe

Etymology

From Latin commovēre, from com- (altogether) + movēre (to move).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
