guillotine

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɪl.ə.tin / (AmE), / ˈɡɪl.ə.tiːn / (BrE)
a device with a heavy, angled blade held between upright posts, used for beheading people, especially as a form of execution
execution device with falling blade

Examples

During the French Revolution, many prisoners were executed by the guillotine.
The invention of the guillotine was intended to make executions more humane.
A replica of the guillotine is displayed in the museum's section on capital punishment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beheading - the act of cutting off someone's head

Etymology

From French 'guillotine', named after Dr. Joseph-Ignace Guillotin, who proposed its use.

2. VERB

/ ˈɡɪləˌtin / (AmE), / ˈɡɪlətˌiːn / (BrE)
to execute someone by cutting off their head with a guillotine
execute by beheading with a guillotine

Examples

During the French Revolution, many nobles were guillotined for opposing the new government.
The captured spy was guillotined at dawn after a brief trial.
The revolutionaries threatened to guillotine anyone who betrayed their cause.

Synset Examples

The French guillotined many Vietnamese while they occupied the country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to behead - to cut off someone's head

3. NOUN

/ ˈɡɪləˌtin / (AmE), / ˈɡɪlətiːn / (BrE)
A procedure in a legislative body for limiting or ending debate on particular sections of a bill, so that a vote must be taken.
closure of debate in legislative procedure

Examples

The Speaker imposed a guillotine on debate to ensure the bill would pass before midnight.
Critics argued that use of the guillotine stifled important discussion in Parliament.
After hours of heated arguments, the government resorted to the guillotine to push the legislation through.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: closure - a legislative procedure for ending debate and taking a vote

Etymology

From French 'guillotine', originally referring to the execution device; the parliamentary sense derives metaphorically from the abruptness of the action.

Synset POS: n
