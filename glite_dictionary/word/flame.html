to flame

1. VERB

/ fleɪm / (AmE), / fleɪm / (BrE)
to shine or glow with a bright, intense, or fiery light; to be ablaze.
to glow or burn with bright fire

Examples

The sky seemed to flame in brilliant shades of orange and red at sunset.
During the festival, lanterns flame brightly along the riverbank.
The mountain peak flamed with the first light of dawn, casting a golden glow.

Synset Examples

The sky seemed to flame in the Hawaiian sunset

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blaze - to burn with a bright flame
  • Antonym: smolder - to burn slowly with smoke but no flame

Etymology

From Latin flamma (flame).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ fleɪm / (AmE), / fleɪm / (BrE)
to criticize or attack someone harshly on the internet or via electronic communication, often using angry or insulting language
criticize harshly online

Examples

After she posted her opinion on the forum, several users began to flame her relentlessly.
He was flamed in the comments section for expressing an unpopular view.
It's best not to flame others during online debates, even if you disagree strongly.

Synset Examples

the person who posted an inflammatory message got flamed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insult - to speak to or treat with disrespect or scornful abuse
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration for someone or something

Etymology

Metaphor from 'flame' as fire, adopted in the 1980s computer subculture for heated exchanges.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00826282-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ fleɪm / (AmE), / fleɪm / (BrE)
to shine or burn with a sudden, bright light
shine or burn with sudden light

Examples

Candles flamed suddenly when the wind swept through the room.
The horizon flamed with brilliant red as the sun set.
His eyes flamed with excitement when he heard the news.

Synset Examples

The night sky flared with the massive bombardment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flare - to blaze up with a sudden burst of flame or light

Etymology

From Old French *flambe*, from Latin *flamma* (flame).
