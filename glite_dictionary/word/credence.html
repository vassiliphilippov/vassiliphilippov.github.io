credence

1. NOUN

/ ˈkriːdəns / (AmE), / ˈkriːdəns / (BrE)
belief or acceptance that something is true or valid; the state of being regarded as credible
belief in the truth of something

Examples

The theory gained credence after several experiments confirmed its predictions.
She gave little credence to the rumors about her colleague.
Within the community, his words held great credence due to his reputation for honesty.

Synset Examples

he gave credence to the gossip
acceptance of Newtonian mechanics was unquestioned for 200 years

Related Terms

  • Synonym: belief - acceptance that something exists or is true
  • Antonym: disbelief - inability or refusal to accept that something is true

Etymology

From Latin credentia (belief, trust), from credere (to believe).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkriːdəns / (AmE), / ˈkriːdəns / (BrE)
a small sideboard or buffet, especially one in a church for holding the elements used in the Eucharist or items for a meal
small sideboard or buffet (often in church)

Examples

A 16th-century credence stood in the corner of the dining hall, displaying antique silver.
The priest placed the chalice and paten on the credence before the service began.
She inherited a carved oak credence from her grandmother's estate.

Etymology

From Latin *credentia* (belief, trust); the furniture sense comes from its use in medieval ceremonies where food was tested for poison.
