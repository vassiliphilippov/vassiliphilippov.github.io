espousal

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈspaʊzəl / (AmE), / ɪˈspaʊzəl / (BrE)
(Archaic) A wedding ceremony or a wedding feast.
(archaic) wedding or wedding feast
archaic

Examples

In medieval villages, the espousal was a grand affair lasting several days.
The poet wrote about the joy and unity felt during the espousal of the young couple.
Historians found records of a lavish espousal attended by the entire town.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wedding - a marriage ceremony, especially considered as including the associated celebrations

Etymology

From Old French 'espousaille' (wedding), from Latin 'sponsalia' (betrothal).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈspaʊzəl / (AmE), / ɪˈspaʊzəl / (BrE)
the act of becoming engaged to be married or forming a formal agreement to marry
act of engagement to marry

Examples

Their families gathered to celebrate the espousal of the young couple.
In some cultures, the espousal is accompanied by elaborate ceremonies and traditions.
The public announcement of their espousal delighted friends and relatives alike.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: betrothal - formal engagement to be married
  • Degree Higher: wedding - marriage ceremony

Etymology

From Old French espousaille (betrothal), from Latin sponsalia (betrothal), from sponsus (betrothed).

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈspaʊzəl / (AmE), / ɪˈspaʊzəl / (BrE)
the act of adopting, supporting, or accepting a cause, idea, or proposal with approval
the act of adopting or supporting something

Examples

His espousal of environmental causes won him widespread respect.
The company's espousal of new technologies set it apart from its competitors.
Her public espousal of the reform agenda surprised many of her colleagues.

Synset Examples

its adoption by society
the proposal found wide acceptance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adoption - the act of taking up or accepting something
  • Antonym: rejection - the act of refusing to accept, use, or believe something

Etymology

From Old French 'espousaille' (betrothal), from Latin 'sponsalia' (betrothal), originally relating to marriage, later broadened.
