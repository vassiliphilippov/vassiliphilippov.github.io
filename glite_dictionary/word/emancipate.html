to emancipate

1. VERB

/ ɪˈmæn.sɪˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈmæn.sɪ.peɪt / (BrE)
to give someone, especially a group such as women or minorities, equal rights or freedom from legal, social, or political restrictions
grant equal rights or set free

Examples

The law was designed to emancipate women and grant them equal rights in the workplace.
Several organizations fought to emancipate minorities from discriminatory laws.
It took many years of activism to finally emancipate those who had been oppressed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to liberate - to set someone free from a situation, especially imprisonment or oppression
  • Antonym: to enslave - to make someone a slave or deprive of freedom

Etymology

From Latin 'emancipare' (declare free, set free), from 'e-' (out) + 'mancipium' (ownership).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈmænsɪˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈmansɪpeɪt / (BrE)
to set someone free, especially from legal, social, or political restrictions; to free from slavery or servitude.
set free from slavery or restrictions

Examples

The proclamation would emancipate all slaves in the rebel states.
She fought tirelessly to emancipate women from oppressive laws.
Efforts to emancipate the oppressed continued throughout the century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to liberate - to set someone free from a situation, especially imprisonment or oppression
  • Antonym: to enslave - to make someone a slave or to bring under control

Etymology

From Latin emancipare (to declare someone free), from e- (out) + mancipare (to transfer ownership).
