affixation

1. NOUN

/ ˌæfɪkˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæfɪkˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or result of adding an affix to a root word, forming a new word or word form
process of adding an affix to a word

Examples

The affixation of 'un-' to 'happy' creates the word 'unhappy'.
Understanding affixation is important for studying the structure of words in English.
In some languages, affixation is the primary method of forming new words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affix - a morpheme added to a word to change its meaning

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'affixatio', from Latin 'affixus' (fastened to).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæfɪkˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæfɪkˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of forming a word by adding a prefix, suffix, or other affix to a base word
word formation using affixes

Examples

Affixation is a common way to create new words in English.
The word 'unhappy' is formed by affixation of the prefix 'un-' to 'happy'.
Linguists study affixation to understand how languages form words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affix - a morpheme attached to a word stem to form a new word or word form

Etymology

From Latin affixus (fastened to) + -ation (forming nouns).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00929509-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˌæfɪkˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌafɪkˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of attaching or adding something, especially the addition of prefixes, suffixes, or infixes to a word
the act of attaching or adding something

Examples

The affixation of stamps to the envelopes was done carefully.
Linguists study affixation to understand how new words are formed.
The affixation of warning labels is required by law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attachment - the process of attaching something

Etymology

From Latin affixus (attached) + -ation.
