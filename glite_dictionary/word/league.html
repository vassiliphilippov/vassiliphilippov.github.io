league

1. NOUN

/ ˈliɡ / (AmE), / ˈliːɡ / (BrE)
an association of states, organizations, or individuals formed for a common purpose
association for common action

Examples

Several European countries joined the league to promote peace and cooperation.
He was president of the student league at his university.
The cities formed a league to increase their bargaining power.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alliance - an association formed for mutual benefit, especially between countries or organizations
  • Synonym: confederation - an organization consisting of groups united in an alliance

Etymology

From Old French 'ligue', from Italian 'lega', from legare (to bind), from Latin 'ligare'.

2. NOUN

/ liːɡ / (AmE), / liːɡ / (BrE)
an association of sports teams or clubs that organizes competitions and fixtures among its members
sports association organizing competitions

Examples

The Premier League is one of the most popular football competitions in the world.
After winning every game, their team topped the league standings this season.
She has been playing in an amateur basketball league for three years.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conference - an association of teams, especially in sports, that compete with each other
  • Synonym: division - a separate group of teams within a league or system

Etymology

From Old French 'ligue', from Italian 'liga', from legare (to bind), ultimately from Latin 'ligare'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08248105-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈliɡ / (AmE), / ˈliːɡ / (BrE)
to unite or come together to form a league; to join in an alliance or group for a common purpose
to unite or join in an alliance

Examples

Several small countries decided to league together to protect their interests.
Rival companies leagued against the larger competitor in the market.
Activists are leaguing themselves with environmental organizations for the campaign.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ally - to join with another for a common purpose
  • Antonym: to disband - to break up an organization or group

Etymology

From Old French 'liguer', from Italian 'ligare', from Latin 'ligare' (to bind).

4. NOUN

/ liɡ / (AmE), / liːɡ / (BrE)
an old unit of distance, once used in English-speaking countries, typically equal to about 3 miles (4.8 km) but varying by time and place
obsolete distance unit (about 3 miles)
archaic

Examples

In old maps, the distance between towns was often measured in leagues.
The novel described a journey of twenty leagues across barren desert.
He set a target to travel five leagues before sunset.

Etymology

From Old French *lieue*, from Latin *leuga*, a Celtic word for a measure of distance.
