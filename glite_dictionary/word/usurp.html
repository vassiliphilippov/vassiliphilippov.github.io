to usurp

1. VERB

/ juˈsɝːp / (AmE), / juːˈzɜːp / (BrE)
to take a position of power or importance illegally or by force
take power or control illegally

Examples

The general attempted to usurp the throne after the king's sudden death.
If you try to usurp authority, you might face serious consequences.
Several rebels plotted to usurp control of the region from the ruling party.

Synset Examples

He assumed to himself the right to fill all positions in the town
he usurped my rights
She seized control of the throne after her husband died

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take hold of suddenly and forcibly
  • Synonym: to assume - to take on a role or responsibility, sometimes wrongfully
  • Antonym: to relinquish - to voluntarily cease to keep or claim; give up

Etymology

From Latin 'usurpare' (to take into use, seize for use).

2. VERB

/ juːˈsɜrp / (AmE), / juːˈzɜːp / (BrE)
to take the place of someone or something, especially by force or without the right to do so
take the place of (usually by force or illegitimately)

Examples

After the accident, anxiety usurped her usual sense of security.
With technology evolving rapidly, new devices often usurp outdated ones in the market.
Doubt usurped his confidence as the deadline drew nearer.

Synset Examples

gloom had usurped mirth at the party after the news of the terrorist act broke

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to supplant - to take the place of, especially by force or strategy
  • Synonym: to replace - to take the place of (someone or something)

Etymology

From Latin usurpare (to seize for use), from usus (use) + rapere (to seize).
