idiom

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪdiəm / (AmE), / ˈɪdiəm / (BrE)
A group of words established by usage as having a meaning not deducible from those of the individual words.
expression with non-literal meaning

Examples

The phrase "kick the bucket" is an idiom meaning to die.
Learning new idioms can be challenging for students of English.
She used a common idiom to express her feelings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expression - a word or phrase with a particular meaning

Etymology

From French *idiome*, from Latin *idioma*, from Greek *idiōma* (peculiarity, peculiarity of language).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪdiəm / (AmE), / ˈɪdɪəm / (BrE)
the language, expressions, or vocabulary characteristic of a group, community, or region
distinctive vocabulary or usage of a group

Examples

Each region in Spain has its own idiom that sets it apart from the rest of the country.
The idiom of legal professionals can be difficult for outsiders to understand.
The novel is written entirely in the local idiom, giving it a unique and authentic voice.

Synset Examples

the immigrants spoke an odd dialect of English
he has a strong German accent
it has been said that a language is a dialect with an army and navy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dialect - a particular form of a language peculiar to a specific region or group

Etymology

From French *idiome*, from Late Latin *idioma* (peculiarity of language), from Greek *idiōma* (peculiarity).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪdiəm / (AmE), / ˈɪdiəm / (BrE)
a form of expression or a way of using language that is characteristic of a particular language, person, or group, and often cannot be understood from the individual meanings of its words
distinctive manner of expression

Examples

Every language has its own set of idiom that can be difficult for learners to master.
Her writing style is rich in the native idiom of her region.
He struggled to understand the idiom used by British speakers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expression - a word or phrase that expresses an idea
  • Synonym: phrase - a small group of words standing together as a conceptual unit

Etymology

From Late Latin 'idioma' (special property, peculiar phraseology), from Greek 'idiōma' (peculiarity, personal style).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈɪdiəm / (AmE), / ˈɪdiəm / (BrE)
the distinctive style or way of expression characteristic of a particular artist, school, or movement, especially in language, music, or the arts
distinctive style of an artist or movement

Examples

The composer developed a unique idiom that set his work apart from others.
Modernist painters share a visual idiom that often challenges traditional forms.
Her novels are written in a highly original idiom that reflects her unconventional background.

Synset Examples

an imaginative orchestral idiom

Etymology

From French *idiome*, from Latin *idioma* (peculiarity, property), from Greek *idiōma* (peculiarity, special feature).
