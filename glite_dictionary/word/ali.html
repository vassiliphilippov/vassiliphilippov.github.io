Ali

1. PROPN

/ ɑːˈliː / (AmE), / ˈɑːli / (BrE)
(c. 600–661) The fourth caliph of Islam and the first caliph recognized by Shiites; cousin and son-in-law of Muhammad, whose assassination led to the division of Islam into Shiite and Sunni sects.
the fourth caliph of Islam; cousin and son-in-law of Muhammad

Examples

Shiites regard Ali as the rightful successor to Muhammad.
Many historical accounts discuss Ali's role during the early years of Islam.
After the assassination of Ali, the Muslim community split into Sunni and Shiite branches.

Etymology

Arabic ʿAlī, meaning 'exalted' or 'noble'.

2. PROPN

/ ɑːˈliː / (AmE), / ɑːˈliː / (BrE)
(1942–2016) United States boxer, widely regarded as one of the greatest heavyweight champions in history, known for winning the world heavyweight title three times.
famous American heavyweight boxing champion

Examples

Ali defeated George Foreman in the legendary 1974 'Rumble in the Jungle' fight.
Many consider Ali a symbol of athletic excellence and social change.
The life of Ali has inspired books, films, and countless aspiring athletes.

Etymology

Born Cassius Clay; changed name to Muhammad Ali after converting to Islam.
