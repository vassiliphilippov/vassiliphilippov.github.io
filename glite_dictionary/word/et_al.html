et al.

1. ADV

/ ɛt ˈæl / (AmE), / ɛt ˈæl / (BrE)
and others; used to indicate the presence of additional people or items not individually named, especially in references and bibliographies.
and others (esp. in references)

Examples

The study by Smith et al. has been widely cited in the field.
Several authors, such as Johnson et al., have discussed this issue.
The article referenced Williams et al. for further reading.

Etymology

From Latin 'et alii' (and others).

WordNet ID: oewn-00192665-r
Synset POS: r

2. ADV

/ ɛt ˈæl / (AmE), / ɛt ˈæl / (BrE)
used after a name or list of names to indicate that more people are involved, meaning 'and others'
and others (used after names)

Examples

The book was written by Johnson et al. and published last year.
This method was first described by Chan et al. in 2010.
Research by Martinez et al. supports these findings.

Synset Examples

the data reported by Smith et al.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: etc. - and so forth (used to indicate continuation of a list of things)

Etymology

From Latin 'et alii' meaning 'and others'.

3. ADV

/ ɛt ˈæl / (AmE), / ɛt ˈæl / (BrE)
used after a name to indicate that there are other people involved, especially in citations to refer to multiple authors; means 'and others.'
and others (in citations)

Examples

The study by Smith et al. demonstrated a significant correlation between diet and heart health.
This phenomenon is discussed in Johnson et al. (2018) in greater depth.
For more information, see the work by Wang et al. on environmental sustainability.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: et cetera - and the rest; and so forth

Etymology

From Latin et alii, meaning 'and others.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00192665-r
Synset POS: r

4. ADV

/ ɛt æl / (AmE), / ɛt æl / (BrE)
used after a name to mean 'and other people', especially in academic or formal writing when citing authors of a work.
and others (in citations)

Examples

The paper was co-authored by Dr. Jones et al. and published in a major journal.
The court cited Johnson et al. as the appellants in the case.
This method was first introduced by Wang et al. in 2008.

Synset Examples

the data reported by Smith et al.

Etymology

From Latin 'et alii' (and others), commonly used in academic writing.
