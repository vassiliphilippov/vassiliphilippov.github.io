to civilise

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪv.əˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪv.ɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to teach someone to acquire more refined tastes, manners, or judgment; to make more cultured or sophisticated
make more refined or cultured

Examples

The art class aims to civilise the students' sense of aesthetics.
She worked hard to civilise his uncouth manners.
Travel and literature can civilise the mind and broaden one's horizons.

Synset Examples

Cultivate your musical taste
Train your tastebuds
She is well schooled in poetry

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cultivate - to improve or refine the mind, taste, or manners
  • Antonym: to barbarize - to make less civilized or more barbaric

Etymology

From Latin 'civilis' (relating to a citizen), through French 'civiliser'.

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪv.ə.laɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪv.ɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to bring (a person or society) out of a primitive or barbaric condition and into a more advanced or refined state of society, culture, or behavior
bring to a more advanced state

Examples

Efforts were made to civilise the indigenous population by introducing new laws and customs.
Missionaries sought to civilise the region, believing it would benefit the local people.
The teacher hoped to civilise the unruly class through education and discipline.

Synset Examples

The wild child found wandering in the forest was gradually civilized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge and understanding
  • Antonym: to barbarise - to make barbarous or to become barbaric

Etymology

From Latin 'civilis' (relating to a citizen), via French 'civiliser'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00412237-v
Synset POS: v
