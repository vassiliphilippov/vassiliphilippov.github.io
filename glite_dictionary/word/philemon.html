Philemon

1. PROPN

/ fəˈlimən / (AmE), / fɪˈliːmɒn / (BrE)
(in Greek mythology) a simple countryman who, along with his wife Baucis, showed hospitality to Zeus and Hermes when they visited earth in disguise.
hospitable countryman in Greek mythology

Examples

In the myth, Philemon and Baucis were rewarded by the gods for their generosity.
The tale of Philemon highlights the virtue of hospitality in Greek culture.
A statue of Philemon and Baucis stands in the town square to commemorate their kindness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Baucis - Philemon's wife in Greek mythology, known for hospitality to gods

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Φιλήμων (Philḗmōn), the mythological character's name.

2. PROPN

/ fəˈliːmən / (AmE), / fɪˈliːmən / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) a Christian inhabitant of Colossae, known for being the addressee of an epistle from Saint Paul regarding his runaway slave Onesimus.
Christian figure addressed by Paul in the New Testament

Examples

The apostle Paul wrote a letter to Philemon concerning his escaped slave Onesimus.
Philemon is considered a significant figure in early Christian history.
The Epistle to Philemon is one of the shortest books in the New Testament.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Φιλήμων (Philēmōn), a personal name meaning 'affectionate' or 'loving.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11252040-n
Synset POS: n

/ fɪˈliːmən / (AmE), / fɪˈliːmən / (BrE)
a book of the New Testament, consisting of a letter from Saint Paul to Philemon asking him to forgive his runaway slave
New Testament epistle from Paul to Philemon

Examples

The Book of Philemon is one of the shortest letters in the New Testament.
In his sermon, the pastor referenced Philemon to discuss themes of forgiveness.
Many biblical scholars have analyzed Paul's message in Philemon regarding the treatment of slaves.

Related Terms
