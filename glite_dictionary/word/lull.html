lull

1. NOUN

/ lʌl / (AmE), / lʌl / (BrE)
a temporary period of calm or quiet, especially in an otherwise active or turbulent situation
temporary calm or quiet period

Examples

There was a brief lull in the conversation before the debate resumed.
The sailors took advantage of the lull in the storm to make quick repairs.
After a lull in business, the shop became busy with customers again.

Synset Examples

there was a lull in the storm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pause - a temporary stop in activity or speech
  • Antonym: flurry - a brief period of activity or excitement

Etymology

From Middle English *lullen* (to calm by soothing), probably imitative of the sound used to quieten.

2. NOUN

/ lʌl / (AmE), / lʌl / (BrE)
A temporary period of quiet or reduced activity; a pause when things are calm.
temporary period of calm or pause

Examples

After a hectic morning, there was a brief lull in customer traffic at the café.
The conversation fell into a lull as everyone waited for someone to speak.
There was a lull in the fighting during which aid workers could tend to the wounded.

Synset Examples

there was never a letup in the noise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pause - a short period in which something is stopped before it starts again
  • Synonym: break - a short interruption or interval in an activity
  • Antonym: flurry - a brief period of intense activity or excitement, opposite of lull

Etymology

From Middle English lullyn (to quieten), probably imitative of soft sounds used to soothe.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-15299427-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ lʌl / (AmE), / lʌl / (BrE)
to become quiet or less intense; to diminish in activity or intensity, usually for a short time
become quiet or less intense

Examples

After hours of arguing, the conversation finally lulled and the room grew quiet.
The storm lulled briefly before returning with greater force.
Noise in the city rarely lulls, even late at night.

Synset Examples

the fighting lulled for a moment

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to intensify - to become stronger or more intense

Etymology

From Middle English *lullen* (to calm or quiet), likely of imitative origin.

4. VERB

/ lʌl / (AmE), / lʌl / (BrE)
to make someone or something calm, quiet, or less active, especially so that they are less aware or alert
make calm or quiet

Examples

The gentle music helped lull the baby to sleep.
Her calm voice managed to lull his fears before the interview.
They tried to lull the audience into a false sense of security.

Synset Examples

quiet the dragons of worry and fear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to soothe - to gently calm someone
  • Antonym: to agitate - to make someone nervous or upset

Etymology

From Middle English *lullen* (to lull to sleep), of imitative origin.

5. VERB

/ lʌl / (AmE), / lʌl / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel calm, safe, or less alert by deception or false assurance, often leading them to overlook danger or threat
to calm or soothe by deception

Examples

The steady voice of the leader lulled the crowd into believing everything was under control.
They were lulled into complacency by the repeated assurances of safety.
Don't let yourself be lulled into a false sense of security by the apparent calm.
