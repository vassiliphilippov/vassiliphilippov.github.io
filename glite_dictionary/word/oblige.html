to oblige

1. VERB

/ əˈblaɪdʒ / (AmE), / əˈblaɪdʒ / (BrE)
to do something as a favor or service for someone, often because it is expected or polite
do a favor or service for someone

Examples

Could you oblige me by opening the window?
She was happy to oblige when he asked for help.
He rarely refuses when customers ask him to oblige with special requests.

Synset Examples

We had to oblige him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accommodate - to do a favor or kindness; oblige
  • Antonym: refuse - to decline to do something requested

Etymology

From Old French 'obliger', from Latin 'obligare' meaning 'to bind' or 'to oblige'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00887446-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ əˈblaɪdʒ / (AmE), / əˈblaɪdʒ / (BrE)
to make someone do something because it is necessary by law, duty, or circumstances; to force or compel.
to force or compel someone

Examples

The new regulations oblige companies to provide safety information to employees.
Parents are legally obliged to send their children to school in most countries.
The court obliged him to pay damages to the victims.

Synset Examples

We compel all students to fill out this form

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compel - to force someone to do something
  • Synonym: to coerce - to force someone to do something by using threats or pressure
  • Antonym: to allow - to let someone do something (opposite of force)

Etymology

From Old French obliger, from Latin obligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02512195-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ əˈblaɪdʒ / (AmE), / əˈblaɪdʒ / (BrE)
to make someone legally or morally bound to do something; to require or compel by law, promise, or sense of duty
to bind or require someone to do something

Examples

The contract will oblige her to complete the work within a month.
Parents are legally obliged to provide for their children.
If you join the committee, you are obliged to attend the meetings.

Synset Examples

He's held by a contract
I'll hold you by your promise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compel - to force or drive someone to do something
  • Antonym: release - to free someone from an obligation

Etymology

From Old French obliger, from Latin obligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00886738-v
Synset POS: v
