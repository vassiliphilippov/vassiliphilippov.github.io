compromising

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːmprəˌmaɪzɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkɒmprəˌmaɪzɪŋ / (BrE)
making someone vulnerable to danger, especially of discredit or suspicion
vulnerable to discredit or suspicion

Examples

The journalist obtained some compromising photos of the politician.
He was caught in a compromising position that damaged his reputation.
There was nothing compromising in her emails, despite the accusations.

Synset Examples

she found herself in a compromising situation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embarrassing - causing embarrassment
  • Synonym: incriminating - showing involvement in a crime or wrongdoing

Etymology

From the verb 'compromise,' ultimately from Latin 'compromissum' (a mutual promise).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːm.prəˌmaɪ.zɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prəˌmaɪ.zɪŋ / (BrE)
willing to make concessions or accept less than originally desired in order to reach an agreement.
willing to make concessions

Examples

The manager was compromising in her approach to settling workplace disputes.
He admired her compromising attitude, which made negotiations smoother for everyone involved.
Unlike his predecessor, the new leader proved more compromising on major policy debates.

Synset Examples

loneliness tore through him … whenever he thought of … even the compromising Louis du Tillet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accommodating - ready to help or willing to adjust to others
  • Antonym: inflexible - unwilling to change or adapt

Etymology

From 'compromise' + '-ing'. 'Compromise' from Old French 'compromis', from Latin 'compromissum' (a mutual promise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01029877-a
Synset POS: a
