to forbid

1. VERB

/ fərˈbɪd / (AmE), / fəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to order someone not to do something; to command against an action or behaviour
order not to do something

Examples

The teacher forbid the students from entering the laboratory without supervision.
My doctor has forbidden me to eat sugary snacks.
Parents often forbid their children from watching scary movies.

Synset Examples

I forbid you to call me late at night
Mother vetoed the trip to the chocolate store
Dad nixed our plans

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ban - to officially or legally prohibit
  • Antonym: permit - to allow something to happen

Etymology

From Old English *forbēodan* (to prohibit), from for- (against) + bēodan (to command).

2. VERB

/ fərˈbɪd / (AmE), / fəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to prevent something from happening or make it impossible
prevent or make impossible

Examples

A lack of funding can forbid meaningful progress on the project.
His illness forbids any strenuous activity for now.
Heavy rain may forbid the outdoor concert from taking place.

Synset Examples

My sense of tact forbids an honest answer
Your role in the projects precludes your involvement in the competitive project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening or existing
  • Antonym: to allow - to permit something to happen

Etymology

From Old English 'forbēodan' (to prohibit, command against), from 'for-' (away, against) + 'bēodan' (to bid, command).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02458316-v
Synset POS: v
