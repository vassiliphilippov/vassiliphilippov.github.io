to dandle

1. VERB

/ ˈdændl̩ / (AmE), / ˈdændl̩ / (BrE)
to move a baby or small child up and down in one's arms or on one's knees, usually to amuse or soothe them
move a baby up and down to amuse or soothe

Examples

She gently dandled the baby on her knee until he started to giggle.
The grandmother loved to dandle her grandson every time she visited.
He dandled his daughter in his arms to calm her when she cried.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rock - to move gently back and forth or from side to side, especially to calm a baby

Etymology

Probably from Old French dondeler (to dandle, swing), of imitative origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈdændl̩ / (AmE), / ˈdændl̩ / (BrE)
to move (a baby or young child) gently up and down on one's knee or in the arms, often to amuse or soothe
move a child playfully on one's knee or arms

Examples

The grandfather dandled the laughing toddler on his knee.
She gently dandled the baby to calm him before bedtime.
He used to dandle his niece in his arms every time he visited.

Synset Examples

the grandfather dandled the small child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bounce - to move something up and down quickly, especially a child

Etymology

Possibly from Old English *dand* (to move up and down) or from a nursery word.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01214145-v
Synset POS: v
