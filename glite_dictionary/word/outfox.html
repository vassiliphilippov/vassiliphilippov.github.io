to outfox

1. VERB

/ aʊtˈfɑks / (AmE), / aʊtˈfɒks / (BrE)
to defeat or outsmart someone by being more clever or cunning than they are
defeat by cleverness or cunning

Examples

He managed to outfox his opponents during the debate with quick thinking.
The thieves tried to outfox the security system but were eventually caught.
She always finds a way to outfox her rivals in business negotiations.

Synset Examples

I beat the traffic
She outfoxed her competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outsmart - to gain an advantage over someone by being more intelligent or clever
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive or cheat someone
  • Synonym: to outwit - to defeat someone by being more clever

Etymology

Formed from 'out-' (meaning 'beyond') and 'fox' (a symbol of cunning); modeled after the animal's reputation for cleverness.

2. VERB

/ aʊtˈfɑks / (AmE), / aʊtˈfɒks / (BrE)
to gain an advantage over someone by being more clever or cunning; to outwit
outwit or outsmart someone

Examples

The young detective managed to outfox the seasoned criminal with her clever plan.
Every time we set up a trap for the raccoon, it seems to outfox us easily.
He hoped to outfox his competitors by launching his product early.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outwit - to defeat or trick someone by being more intelligent or clever
  • Synonym: to outsmart - to defeat someone by being more intelligent or clever

Etymology

Formed from 'out-' (surpass) + 'fox' (as a symbol of cunning), meaning to be cleverer than like a fox.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01110217-v
Synset POS: v
