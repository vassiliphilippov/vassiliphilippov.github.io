glut

1. VERB

/ ɡlʌt / (AmE), / ɡlʌt / (BrE)
to supply or fill something with an excessive amount, especially so that it becomes undesirable or worthless
supply with an excess of

Examples

Supermarkets often glut their shelves with seasonal products after the holidays.
A sudden drop in demand can glut the market with unsold inventory.
They warned that more production could glut the city with low-quality apartments.

Synset Examples

flood the market with tennis shoes
Glut the country with cheap imports from the Orient

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flood - to overwhelm or fill with an excessive amount (as in flood the market)

Etymology

From Middle English *gloten*, from Latin *gluttire* (to swallow, devour).

2. VERB

/ ɡlʌt / (AmE), / ɡlʌt / (BrE)
to eat or consume food or drink in excess; to overindulge oneself
eat or drink to excess

Examples

He would often glut himself on sweets during the holidays.
After the long hike, they glutted on the feast laid out before them.
It is unwise to glut on rich foods if you want to remain healthy.

Synset Examples

She stuffed herself at the dinner
The kids binged on ice cream

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gorge - to eat a large amount greedily
  • Synonym: stuff - to eat until one is full or beyond
  • Antonym: to starve - to suffer or die from lack of food

Etymology

From Latin 'gluttire' (to swallow), related to 'gluttus' (greedy).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01195632-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ɡlʌt / (AmE), / ɡlʌt / (BrE)
An excessive supply of something, especially leading to a decrease in its value or price.
an excessive supply; oversupply

Examples

There was a glut of strawberries on the market, causing prices to plummet.
The housing bubble burst after a glut of new apartments became available.
A glut of oil in global markets has pushed fuel prices to record lows.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surplus - an amount that is more than what is needed
  • Antonym: shortage - a situation in which there is not enough of something
  • Antonym: dearth - a lack or scarcity of something

Etymology

From Latin 'gluttire' (to swallow, gulp down).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05127137-n
Synset POS: n
