Ciliophora

1. NOUN

/ sɪl.iˈɑf.ə.rə / (AmE), / sɪl.iˈɒf.ə.rə / (BrE)
A class of protozoa characterized by the presence of cilia or hairlike appendages on part or all of the surface during some stage of their life cycle.
class of protozoa with cilia

Examples

Many species within the Ciliophora use their cilia for movement and feeding.
The Ciliophora are distinguished from other protozoa by their unique locomotory organelles.
Researchers studied the diversity of Ciliophora in freshwater ecosystems.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining Latin 'cilium' (eyelash, cilium) and Greek 'phora' (bearing).

2. NOUN

/ sɪliˈɑfərə / (AmE), / sɪliˈɒfərə / (BrE)
a class of protozoa characterized by the presence of cilia or hairlike appendages on part or all of their surface during some stage of their life cycle
class of protozoa with cilia

Examples

Researchers observed the Ciliophora under the microscope to study their unique movement patterns.
The Ciliophora play an important role in aquatic ecosystems by consuming bacteria and small particles.
In biology class, we learned that Ciliophora move using tiny hairlike structures called cilia.

Etymology

From Latin 'cilium' (eyelash, cilium) + Greek 'phoros' (bearing), referring to their cilia.
