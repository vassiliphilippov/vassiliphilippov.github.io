gaudy

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɔdi / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːdi / (BrE)
(Britain) A celebratory reunion feast or social event, especially one held at a college.
college reunion feast or event (Britain)
british

Examples

She attended her college's annual gaudy last weekend and met many old friends.
The gaudy is a cherished tradition at several Oxford colleges.
Invitations to the gaudy were sent to all alumni of the graduating class.

Etymology

From Latin *gaudium* (joy), via medieval traditions of celebration.

2. ADJ

/ ˈɡɔdi / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːdi / (BrE)
(especially of clothes or decoration) excessively bright or showy, typically so as to be tasteless.
excessively bright or showy, tasteless

Examples

She wore a gaudy dress covered in sequins that turned every head at the party.
The decorations were so gaudy that they overwhelmed the small living room.
He regretted buying such a gaudy tie when he saw how it clashed with his suit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flashy - bright and intended to attract attention
  • Antonym: subtle - not obvious or showy; understated

Etymology

From Latin 'gaudium' (joy, delight), with 16th-century usage shifting to mean showy or tasteless.

3. ADJ

/ ˈɡɔdi / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːdi / (BrE)
Unpleasantly bright or showy in a way that lacks good taste.
tastelessly showy; overly bright

Examples

She wore a gaudy necklace that clashed with her elegant dress.
The hotel's lobby was decorated with gaudy chandeliers and brightly colored carpets.
I prefer simple designs to the gaudy patterns on those shirts.

Synset Examples

a flash car
a flashy ring
garish colors
a gaudy costume
loud sport shirts
a meretricious yet stylish book
tawdry ornaments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: garish - unpleasantly bright or flashy
  • Synonym: tawdry - cheap and gaudy in appearance or quality
  • Antonym: understated - presented or expressed in a subtle and effective way

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English 'gaud' (a jest, trick, or ornament), from Latin 'gaudium' (joy).
