coloured

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌlɚd / (AmE), / ˈkʌləd / (BrE)
(offensive, dated, US) A term formerly used in the United States to refer to a Black person.
offensive US term for a Black person (dated)
offensive

Examples

The word coloured was once used in official documents and signage in the United States.
Many consider the term coloured to be outdated and offensive today.
The organization was originally founded to support coloured people in the early 20th century.

2. ADJ

/ ˈkʌlɚd / (AmE), / ˈkʌləd / (BrE)
(of a color) artificially produced; not natural; dyed or altered from its original state.
artificially produced color

Examples

The wood was coloured to look like mahogany.
She wore coloured contact lenses for the party.
The fabric had been coloured rather than left in its natural state.

Synset Examples

a bleached blonde

Related Terms

  • Antonym: natural - existing in nature; not man-made or artificial (of color)
  • Synonym: dyed - having had color added by the process of dyeing

/ ˈkʌlərd / (AmE), / ˈkʌləd / (BrE)
having color or a particular color; not colorless or transparent, sometimes used in combination (e.g., amber-coloured, brightly coloured).
having color or a particular color
british

Examples

4. ADJ

/ ˈkʌlɚd / (AmE), / ˈkʌləd / (BrE)
(of people) having skin that is not white, especially as historically used to classify people of mixed race in South Africa or, formerly, to refer to Black people; now considered offensive or dated in many contexts.
having non-white skin (dated, offensive)
offensive archaic british

Examples

He grew up in a community where most of the residents were identified as coloured.
The term coloured is now considered offensive and is largely avoided in modern usage.
Historical documents often referred to people of mixed ancestry as coloured in South Africa.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: black - having dark skin pigmentation, especially of African descent
  • Antonym: white - having light skin pigmentation, especially of European descent

Etymology

Derived from 'colour' + '-ed'. Used as a racial classification from the 18th century, especially in British and South African English.
