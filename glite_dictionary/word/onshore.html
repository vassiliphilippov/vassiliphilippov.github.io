onshore

1. ADJ

/ ˈɑnˌʃɔr / (AmE), / ˈɒnʃɔː / (BrE)
(of winds) blowing from the sea toward the land.
(of wind) from sea to land

Examples

The onshore breeze brought cool, salty air to the coastal village.
Beachgoers were relieved when an onshore wind pushed the smoke from wildfires away from shore.
When the onshore gale intensified, boats hurried back to the harbor.

Synset Examples

an inshore breeze
an onshore gale

Related Terms

  • Antonym: offshore - (of winds) blowing from the land toward the sea
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00466228-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈɑnˌʃɔr / (AmE), / ˈɒnˌʃɔː / (BrE)
Situated on or moving toward the land, especially along the coast; located on the land near the sea.
located on or near the land by the sea

Examples

The onshore wind brought the scent of salt and seaweed across the dunes.
Lifeguards keep a close watch on onshore swimming areas during the busy season.
The onshore factories depend on goods arriving by ship from overseas.

Synset Examples

an onshore lighthouse

Related Terms

  • Antonym: offshore - situated at sea some distance from the shore

Etymology

From on + shore, indicating location by the shore or land.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00125940-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADV

/ ˈɑnˌʃɔr / (AmE), / ˈɒnˌʃɔː / (BrE)
on or toward the land, especially as opposed to the sea; in the direction of land from the sea or water
toward or onto the land from the sea

Examples

Most of the fishermen returned onshore before the storm hit.
The wind began to blow onshore in the early afternoon.
After months at sea, the crew was eager to spend time onshore.

Synset Examples

they were living onshore

Related Terms

  • Antonym: offshore - away from or at a distance from the shore; toward the sea from the land
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00141202-r
Synset POS: r
