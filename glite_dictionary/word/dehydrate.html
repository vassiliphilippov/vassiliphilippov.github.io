to dehydrate

1. VERB

/ diˈhaɪdreɪt / (AmE), / diˈhaɪdreɪt / (BrE)
to lose water or moisture; to become dry
lose water or become dry

Examples

Hikers can easily dehydrate if they don't drink enough water in hot weather.
Food left out in the sun will quickly dehydrate and become inedible.
Athletes may dehydrate during intense exercise unless they stay properly hydrated.

Synset Examples

In the desert, you get dehydrated very quickly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desiccate - to remove all moisture from; to dry out completely
  • Antonym: rehydrate - to absorb or restore moisture

Etymology

From Greek de- (removal) + hydor (water).

2. VERB

/ diˈhaɪdreɪt / (AmE), / diˈhaɪdreɪt / (BrE)
to preserve (especially food) by removing all water or liquid content from it
remove water to preserve

Examples

It's important to dehydrate the fruit completely to prevent mold.
Many hikers dehydrate their own meals before long trips.
Commercial machines can quickly dehydrate vegetables for storage.

Synset Examples

carry dehydrated food on your camping trip

Related Terms

  • Synonym: desiccate - to dry out thoroughly, often for preservation

Etymology

From Greek 'de-' (removal) + 'hydor' (water).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00213216-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ diˈhaɪdreɪt / (AmE), / diˈhaɪdreɪt / (BrE)
to remove water from something, especially to preserve it or as a result of heat or physical activity
remove water from

Examples

Athletes can easily dehydrate if they don't drink enough fluids during intense training.
During the summer, the sun can dehydrate the soil quickly, making it hard for plants to grow.
The company uses special machines to dehydrate fruits for long-term storage.

Synset Examples

All this exercise and sweating has dehydrated me

Related Terms

  • Antonym: rehydrate - to restore water to something

Etymology

From Greek 'de-' (off) + 'hydor' (water) via French 'déshydrater'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00211822-v
Synset POS: v
