telescopic

1. ADJ

/ ˌtɛləˈskɑpɪk / (AmE), / ˌtɛlɪˈskɒpɪk / (BrE)
(of an object) visible only when observed through a telescope.
visible only with a telescope

Examples

The faint telescopic satellite was not discovered until the 20th century.
Many asteroids remain telescopic objects, invisible to the naked eye.
That galaxy is a telescopic marvel, lying millions of light-years away from Earth.

Synset Examples

a bright star with a telescopic companion

Etymology

From Greek tēleskopos (far-seeing), via French télescopique.

WordNet ID: oewn-02527059-s

2. ADJ

/ ˌteləˈskɑpɪk / (AmE), / ˌtelɪˈskɒpɪk / (BrE)
(of an object) having parts that slide or fit inside each other, making it length adjustable or allowing it to be compacted.
having sliding, nested parts

Examples

The telescopic pole can be extended to reach the ceiling easily.
She packed a telescopic cup for her hiking trip, saving space in her backpack.
The camera's telescopic lens allows photographers to adjust the focal length smoothly.

Synset Examples

a telescopic antenna
a telescopic drinking cup

Etymology

From telescope + -ic, modeled after the structure of a telescope.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00468068-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ tɛləˈskɑpɪk / (AmE), / tɛlɪˈskɒpɪk / (BrE)
able to discern or see distant objects, as if using a telescope.
able to see distant objects

Examples

With her telescopic vision, she could see details on the mountaintop miles away.
The scientist marveled at the telescopic capabilities of the owl's eyes.
His telescopic gaze scanned the horizon, picking out ships barely visible to the naked eye.

Synset Examples

a telescopic eye
telescopic vision

Related Terms

  • Antonym: microscopic - so small as to be visible only with a microscope

Etymology

From Italian 'telescopio', from Greek 'tēle' (far) + 'skopein' (to look).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02165351-s
Synset POS: s
