sepia

1. NOUN

/ ˈsiːpiə / (AmE), / ˈsiːpɪə / (BrE)
a rich brown pigment originally prepared from the ink of cuttlefish, often used in drawing and formerly in photography
rich brown pigment from cuttlefish ink

Examples

The artist preferred to use sepia for his landscape sketches, giving them a timeless quality.
Many old photographs have a distinct sepia tone that evokes a sense of nostalgia.
She chose sepia ink to write her letters, inspired by vintage correspondence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: umber - a natural brown or reddish-brown earth pigment
  • Synonym: sienna - a reddish-brown pigment used in painting

Etymology

From Latin 'sepia', from Greek 'sēpia' meaning 'cuttlefish'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsiː.pi.ə / (AmE), / ˈsiː.pi.ə / (BrE)
a reddish-brown color, especially used to describe a shade resembling that of ink formerly made from the cuttlefish
a reddish-brown color

Examples

The photograph was printed in a beautiful sepia tone, giving it a vintage look.
Artists often use sepia ink to create warm, monochromatic drawings.
Her living room was decorated in soft sepia and cream colors.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: brown - a dark color made by mixing red, yellow, and blue, the main color to which sepia belongs
  • Degree Higher: mahogany - a deep reddish-brown color, somewhat darker and richer than sepia

Etymology

From Latin sepia (cuttlefish), from Greek sēpía.
