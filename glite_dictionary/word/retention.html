retention

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (BrE)
the act of keeping or holding onto something; the continued possession, use, or control of something
the act of keeping something

Examples

The company improved employee retention by offering better benefits.
Rainwater retention systems help prevent flooding in urban areas.
His retention of facts is remarkable, making him an excellent student.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: loss - the state or process of losing or no longer possessing something

Etymology

From Latin 'retentio', from 'retinere' meaning 'to hold back'.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (BrE)
the ability to remember information or past experiences
ability to remember

Examples

Her excellent retention of names and faces made her a great host.
The training aims to improve the retention of safety procedures among workers.
As people age, retention of detailed information can become more challenging.

Synset Examples

he had a good memory when he was younger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: memory - the faculty by which the mind stores and remembers information
  • Antonym: forgetfulness - the quality of being unable to remember things

Etymology

From Latin retentio, retentionis (a holding back), from retinere (to hold back).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛn.ʃən / (BrE)
the ability or power of a material or substance to hold or retain liquid
the ability to hold liquid

Examples

The clay in the field improves the soil's retention of water during dry weather.
Modern diapers are engineered for maximum liquid retention.
Sandy soils have poor retention of moisture compared to loamy soils.

Synset Examples

moisture retentivity of soil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorption - the process by which a substance takes in another substance, such as a liquid
  • Antonym: permeability - the quality of a material that allows liquids to pass through it

Etymology

From Latin 'retentio', from 'retinere' (to hold back).
