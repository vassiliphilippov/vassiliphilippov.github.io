haste

1. NOUN

/ heɪst / (AmE), / heɪst / (BrE)
great speed or urgency of movement or action, often with the implication of carelessness or lack of deliberation
urgency or speed in action or movement

Examples

She finished the report in such haste that she forgot to check for errors.
In their haste to leave, they left the lights on.
Don't act in haste, or you may regret it later.

Synset Examples

in a hurry to lock the door

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurry - rush or move quickly, usually because of urgency
  • Antonym: delay - the act of making something late or slower

Etymology

From Old French *hast* (haste, urgency), from Frankish *haifst* (violence, fury).

2. NOUN

/ heɪst / (AmE), / heɪst / (BrE)
speed or urgency of movement or action, often careless or rushed
hurried or rushed action

Examples

In her haste to finish the project, she made several mistakes.
He packed his bag in great haste and left without saying goodbye.
Acting with haste rarely leads to the best decisions.

Synset Examples

in his haste to leave he forgot his book

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurry - rush or swift action, especially to complete something quickly
  • Antonym: leisure - freedom from haste; unhurried ease

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French *haste*, of Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ heɪst / (AmE), / heɪst / (BrE)
excessive speed or urgency of movement or action, often with careless results
excessive or careless speed

Examples

In his haste to leave, he forgot his keys on the kitchen table.
She finished the report in great haste, which led to several mistakes.
Many accidents happen through haste rather than genuine emergencies.

Synset Examples

he soon regretted his haste

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurry - rush or great haste
  • Antonym: leisure - free time, relaxed pace
  • Antonym: slowness - the quality of being slow

Etymology

From Middle English *hast*, from Old French *haste* (urgency), of Germanic origin.
