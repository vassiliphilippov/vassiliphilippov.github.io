gradual

1. ADJ

/ ˈɡrædʒuəl / (AmE), / ˈɡrædʒuəl / (BrE)
happening or changing slowly and steadily over a period of time, in small stages or increments
happening slowly over time

Examples

The recovery was gradual, with improvements seen over several months.
There has been a gradual shift in public opinion on the matter.
A gradual incline made the hike manageable for beginners.

Synset Examples

a gradual increase in prices

Related Terms

  • Antonym: sudden - happening quickly and unexpectedly

Etymology

From Latin 'gradualis', from 'gradus' (step).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01146289-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈɡrædʒuəl / (AmE), / ˈɡrædʒuəl / (BrE)
(of a slope or incline) not steep or abrupt; rising or descending slowly and evenly.
not steep or abrupt (for slopes)

Examples

The hill had a gradual incline, making the climb much easier.
A gradual slope leads down to the riverbank, perfect for picnics.
Their driveway is gradual enough that it can be cleared easily after a snowfall.

Synset Examples

a gradual slope

Related Terms

  • Antonym: steep - rising or falling rapidly; not gradual

Etymology

From Latin 'gradualis', from 'gradus' (step).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01147844-a
Synset POS: a

3. NOUN

/ ˈɡrædʒuəl / (AmE), / ˈɡrædʒuəl / (BrE)
A chant or hymn, usually from the Book of Psalms, sung or recited after the epistle at Mass in the Roman Catholic Church.
chant after the epistle at Mass

Examples

The choir sang the gradual beautifully after the epistle.
During Mass, the gradual is an important part of the liturgy.
She selected a moving gradual for the Sunday service.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'graduale', from Latin 'gradus' (step), referring to the steps of the altar from which it was sung.
