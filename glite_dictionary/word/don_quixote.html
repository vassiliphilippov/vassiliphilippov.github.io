Don Quixote

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɑn kiˈhoʊti / (AmE), / ˌdɒn kɪˈhəʊti / (BrE)
A person who is exceedingly idealistic, unrealistic, and impractical, especially in the pursuit of ideals; an impractical idealist.
an impractical idealist

Examples

People called him a Don Quixote for pursuing such an impossible dream.
Her Don Quixote attitude led her to fight battles others thought were pointless.
In his quest to reform the corrupt system, he sometimes seemed like a modern Don Quixote.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: visionary - a person with original ideas about what the future will or could be like
  • Antonym: realist - a person who accepts a situation as it is and is prepared to deal with it accordingly

Etymology

From the title character of Miguel de Cervantes' Spanish novel *Don Quixote* (1605–1615).

2. NOUN

/ dɑn kiˈhoʊti / (AmE), / dɒn kiˈhəʊtɪ / (BrE)
the impractically idealistic hero of Miguel de Cervantes' novel, noted for pursuing chivalrous but unrealistic goals; also, a person resembling this character
impractical, idealistic literary hero

Examples

He was called a Don Quixote for trying to end poverty single-handedly.
Many view her environmental crusade as a modern Don Quixote fight against industry.
It takes a Don Quixote to believe one person can change the system.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quixotic - impractically idealistic or romantic

Etymology

From Spanish, the name of the character in Cervantes' novel (1605-1615).
