haze

1. NOUN

/ heɪz / (AmE), / heɪz / (BrE)
A state of mental confusion or vagueness, in which things are not clear or easy to understand.
state of mental confusion or vagueness

Examples

After surgery, Marie spent the next few days in a haze of medication and pain.
Grief left him in such a haze that he could hardly focus on his work.
The details of that night passed by in a haze of exhaustion and worry.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand

2. VERB

/ heɪz / (AmE), / heɪz / (BrE)
(of a substance, view, or surface) to become hazy, dull, or cloudy.
to become hazy or cloudy

Examples

Early morning fog can cause the city skyline to haze until the sun rises.
The glass will haze if not cleaned regularly, making it hard to see through.
Humidity and dust can haze the view from the mountain top.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cloud - to become covered or filled with clouds; to become less clear

to harass or punish someone, especially a new member of a group, by making them do humiliating or painful tasks, as part of an initiation ritual
harass with humiliating or painful tasks (initiation)

Several universities have banned fraternities that haze their members as part of the initiation process.
It is illegal in some states to haze new students during orientation week.

4. NOUN

/ heɪz / (AmE), / heɪz / (BrE)
a slight obscuration of the lower atmosphere, typically caused by fine suspended particles such as dust, smoke, or moisture, that makes it difficult to see clearly
reduced visibility due to suspended particles

Examples

A thin haze hung over the city, blurring the distant skyline.
The mountaintops were barely visible through the morning haze.
As the sun set, a haze of dust settled over the fields following the day's harvest.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: fog - thick cloud of tiny water droplets suspended near the earth's surface
  • Degree Lower: mist - cloud of tiny water droplets in the air, less dense than fog

Etymology

Perhaps from obsolete Dutch *hasen* (to drive off, frighten), with influence from Latin *hesus* (mist, fog), or related to Old English *hasu* (gray).
