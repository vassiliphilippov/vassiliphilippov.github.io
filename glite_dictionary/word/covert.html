covert

1. ADJ

/ ˈkoʊ.vɚt / (AmE), / ˈkʌ.vət / (BrE)
not openly acknowledged or displayed; secret or hidden
secret; not openly shown

Examples

The agency was accused of conducting covert surveillance on its citizens.
They held a covert meeting late at night to avoid detection.
Through a covert operation, the documents were recovered without anyone noticing.

Synset Examples

covert actions by the CIA
covert funding for the rebels

Related Terms

  • Antonym: overt - open and not secret; done or shown openly
  • Synonym: secret - not known or seen by others; hidden

Etymology

From Old French *covrir* (to cover), from Latin *cooperire* (to cover completely).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01710108-a
Synset POS: a

2. NOUN

/ ˈkoʊ.vɚt / (AmE), / ˈkʌv.ət / (BrE)
a place where someone or something can hide, especially a thicket in which game can hide; a covering that conceals or shelters
a shelter or hiding place

Examples

The deer darted into the covert to escape the hunters.
The soldiers hid in a covert of thick bushes, waiting for nightfall.
Birds often nest in a covert for protection from predators.

Synset Examples

a screen of trees afforded privacy
under cover of darkness
the brush provided a covert for game
the simplest concealment is to match perfectly the color of the background

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cover - something that covers or conceals
  • Synonym: shelter - a place giving protection from bad weather or danger
  • Antonym: exposure - the state of being unprotected or uncovered

Etymology

From Old French *covrir* (to cover), from Latin *cooperire* (to cover completely).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04159023-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈkoʊ.vɚt / (AmE), / ˈkʌv.ət / (BrE)
(Archaic, legal) Of a wife: under the protection or authority of her husband in law.
wife under husband's legal protection (archaic)
archaic legal

Examples

In medieval England, a woman covert could not own property separately from her husband.
According to old common law, a wife was considered covert and lacked independent legal status.
Historical legal texts often refer to married women as covert in contrast to unmarried women's status.

Synset Examples

a woman covert

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *covert* (covered, protected), from Latin *cooperīre* (to cover).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01891713-s
Synset POS: s
