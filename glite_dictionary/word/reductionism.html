reductionism

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌkʃəˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌkʃənɪzəm / (BrE)
The practice or theory of explaining complex systems or phenomena by reducing them to the interactions of their simpler, more fundamental parts.
explaining complex things by simpler components

Examples

Some critics argue that reductionism fails to capture the true complexity of biological systems.
In physics, reductionism has led to significant breakthroughs by analyzing matter at the atomic level.
The debate between reductionism and holism remains central in the philosophy of science.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: holism - the theory that parts of any whole cannot exist independently of the whole

Etymology

From 'reduction' + '-ism'; 'reduction' from Latin reductio (a leading back).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌk.ʃəˌnɪz.əm / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌk.ʃən.ɪz.əm / (BrE)
the theory that complex systems can be fully understood by analyzing their simpler, constituent parts
theory explaining systems by their components

Examples

Many critics argue that reductionism oversimplifies the complexity of biological organisms.
In philosophy of mind, reductionism has been debated for decades.
His approach to psychology favors reductionism over more holistic perspectives.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: holism - the theory that systems and their properties should be analyzed as wholes, not just as a collection of parts

Etymology

From reduction + -ism, modeled after words like 'materialism' or 'dualism'.
