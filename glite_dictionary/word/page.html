page

1. VERB

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
to number the pages of (a book or manuscript) in sequence
number the pages of a book or document

Examples

After editing the manuscript, she began to page it carefully to avoid mistakes.
The printer forgot to page the document before binding it.
Each chapter must be paged consecutively for the book to meet the publisher’s requirements.
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
one side of a sheet of paper in a book, magazine, newspaper, letter, or similar item, or the content printed or written on it
one side of a sheet in a book or document

Examples

She turned the page of her novel to continue reading.
The article appeared on the front page of the newspaper.
He scribbled a note in the margin of the page.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leaf - a single sheet of paper in a book or collection

Etymology

From Latin 'pagina' (a written page or sheet).

3. VERB

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
to contact someone, especially by using a pager or by announcing their name over a public address system
contact by pager or announcement

Examples

The hospital staff will page Dr. Smith if there's an emergency.
Please page me as soon as the package arrives at reception.
Security was paging a lost child over the loudspeaker.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to summon - to call someone to appear or be present

4. NOUN

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
a young person who attends and assists at official ceremonies or functions, especially in legislative assemblies and weddings
youthful ceremonial attendant

Examples

The young page carried the senator's messages during the session.
At the royal wedding, the pages wore traditional uniforms and helped manage the bride's train.
During the ceremony, a page stood ready to assist the officials if needed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attendant - someone who waits on or assists another, especially at official functions

Etymology

From Old French *page*, from Italian *paggio*, of uncertain origin, possibly from Greek *paidion* (little boy).

Source: WordNet

5. NOUN

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
a boy in the Middle Ages who served as a knight's attendant as the first stage of training for knighthood
boy serving a knight as first stage to knighthood

Examples

The young page eagerly polished the knight's armor each morning.
At the age of seven, he became a page at the lord's castle.
Every page hoped one day to earn the honor of becoming a knight.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: squire - a young nobleman acting as an attendant to a knight before becoming a knight himself

Etymology

From Old French *page*, from Italian *paggio*, probably ultimately from Greek *paidion* (little boy).

7. NOUN

/ peɪdʒ / (AmE), / peɪdʒ / (BrE)
a young boy employed to run errands or attend to a person of rank, especially in a noble household or at ceremonial events
boy employed to run errands

Examples

The young page hurried through the grand hall, carrying a message for the queen.
As a page, he learned the customs of the royal court and delivered important notes.
The knight was often attended by his faithful page during tournaments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: servant - a person who performs duties for others, especially in a household

Etymology

From Middle English page, from Old French, from Italian paggio, from Greek paidion (little boy).
