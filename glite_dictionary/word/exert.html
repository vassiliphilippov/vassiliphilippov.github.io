to exert

1. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzɜrt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to make a strenuous physical or mental effort
make a strong effort

Examples

After running the marathon, she was exhausted from having exerted herself so much.
It is important to exert yourself if you want to achieve your goals.
He exerted all his strength to lift the heavy box.

Synset Examples

exert oneself

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to strive - to make great efforts to achieve something
  • Antonym: to relax - to make less effort or to rest

Etymology

From Latin 'exserere' (to thrust out), via French 'exercer' (to exercise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02376902-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪgˈzɜrt / (AmE), / ɪgˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to use power, influence, or another quality to affect something or make a change
use power or influence

Examples

The president exerted his authority to resolve the crisis quickly.
She decided to exert her influence to help the project succeed.
Countries often exert power in international negotiations to achieve their objectives.

Synset Examples

wield power and authority

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wield - to hold and use (power, influence, etc.)

Etymology

From Latin exertus, past participle of 'exserere' (to thrust out, exert).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02209346-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪɡˈzɝːt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to use power, influence, or effort to achieve a result
to use power, influence, or effort

Examples

She decided to exert her authority to solve the conflict.
You must exert more effort if you want to succeed in this role.
The lobbyists tried to exert their influence over the new legislation.

Synset Examples

exert one's power or influence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to apply - to put something into operation
  • Synonym: to wield - to have and use power, influence, or authority

Etymology

From Latin 'exserere' (to thrust out), via Middle French 'exercer' (to exercise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01168409-v
Synset POS: v
