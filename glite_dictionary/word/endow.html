to endow

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈdaʊ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdaʊ / (BrE)
to give a large sum of money or property to a person, institution, or cause, usually to provide an ongoing income
give a permanent fund or gift

Examples

The billionaire decided to endow the university with a new science building.
Many museums are endowed by private foundations or philanthropists.
She hopes to endow a scholarship for students from her hometown.

Synset Examples

When she got married, she got dowered

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to donate - to give something, especially to a charity or institution
  • Antonym: to disinherit - to deprive of an inheritance or endowment

Etymology

From Old French *endouer* (provide with a dowry), from Latin *dotare* (to endow with a dowry), from *dos* (dowry).

2. VERB

/ ɛnˈdaʊ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdaʊ / (BrE)
to provide someone or something with a particular quality, ability, or asset
to give a quality or ability to

Examples

Her intelligence seemed to endow her with the ability to solve complex problems quickly.
The artist was endowed with extraordinary creativity from a young age.
Nature endows some places with breathtaking beauty.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bestow - to give something as a gift or honor
  • Antonym: to deprive - to take something away from someone

Etymology

From Old French 'endouer', from Latin 'dotare' (to provide with a dowry).
