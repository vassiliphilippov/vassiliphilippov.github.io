to propel

1. VERB

/ prəˈpɛl / (AmE), / prəˈpɛl / (BrE)
to cause something to move forward or onward, often with force
cause to move forward with force

Examples

The strong current propelled the boat down the river quickly.
A burst of air propels the ping pong ball high into the air.
She used her legs to propel herself forward on the swing.

Synset Examples

Steam propels this ship

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drive - to force something to move in a particular direction
  • Synonym: push - to exert force on something to move it away from oneself
  • Antonym: to restrain - to prevent something from moving forward or acting

Etymology

From Latin 'propellere' (to drive forward), from 'pro-' (forward) + 'pellere' (to drive).

2. VERB

/ prəˈpɛl / (AmE), / prəˈpɛl / (BrE)
to cause someone to do something or to move into action, especially by giving an incentive or strong motivation
to drive or motivate into action

Examples

The desire to help others propelled her into a career in medicine.
A passion for justice propelled him to run for public office.
Fear of failure can sometimes propel people to work harder than ever.

Synset Examples

This moved me to sacrifice my career

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to motivate - to provide with a reason to act; to stimulate interest or enthusiasm
  • Antonym: to discourage - to make someone less willing to do something; to deter

Etymology

From Latin 'propellere', from 'pro-' (forward) + 'pellere' (to drive).

Synset POS: v
