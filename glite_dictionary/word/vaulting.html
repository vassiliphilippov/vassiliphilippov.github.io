vaulting

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɔltɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈvɔːltɪŋ / (BrE)
A light leap performed by a horse in which both hind legs leave the ground before the forelegs come down.
a horse's light leap

Examples

The horse's vaulting over the low hurdle impressed the judges.
She trained her mare to perfect its vaulting for dressage competitions.
During the event, the stallion's graceful vaulting drew a round of applause.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leap - a long or high jump

Etymology

From verb 'vault' (to leap), from Middle French 'volter', from Italian 'voltare' (to turn).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɔltɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈvɔːltɪŋ / (BrE)
An arched structure forming a roof or ceiling, especially in architecture.
arched roof or ceiling in architecture

Examples

The cathedral's Gothic vaulting created an impressive and spacious effect above the nave.
Restoring the damaged vaulting required skilled stonemasons and architects.
The ornate vaulting in the ancient basilica fascinated the students of architecture.

Synset Examples

arches and vaulting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vault - an arched structure forming a roof or ceiling

Etymology

From 'vault' + -ing, with 'vault' from Old French 'voute', from Latin 'voluta' (a roll, spiral).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04531288-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈvɔltɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈvɔːltɪŋ / (BrE)
(especially of ambition or pride) excessive or overreaching; showing excessive self-confidence or striving to reach very high goals.
excessive or overreaching (esp. ambition)

Examples

The leader's vaulting ambition ultimately led to his downfall.
Her vaulting pride made her unpopular with her colleagues.
He was criticized for his vaulting desire for success at any cost.

Synset Examples

vaulting ambition

Related Terms

  • Antonym: modest - having or showing a moderate estimation of oneself or one's abilities
  • Antonym: humble - having or showing a low estimate of one's importance

Etymology

From the verb 'vault' (to leap), figuratively extended to striving or reaching ambitiously.
