to tweedle

1. VERB

/ ˈtwiːdəl / (AmE), / ˈtwiːdəl / (BrE)
(of a bird, person, or instrument) to sing, play, or make a series of modulated, high-pitched sounds or notes, often with trills or variations in tone.
make modulated, trilling sounds

Examples

The canary began to tweedle happily every morning at sunrise.
She loves to tweedle melodies on her flute as the sun sets.
A small group of songbirds tweedled in the branches above our heads.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warble - sing or play trilling, melodic notes, especially like a bird

Etymology

Imitative origin, based on the sound's quality.

2. VERB

/ ˈtwiːdəl / (AmE), / ˈtwiːdəl / (BrE)
to play a musical instrument lightly, especially in a repetitive or careless manner
play lightly or negligently on an instrument

Examples

He tweedled absentmindedly on his guitar while waiting for the band to arrive.
The street musician sat on the corner and tweedled a carefree melody on his flute.
She often tweedles at the piano without really practicing her scales.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twiddle - to fiddle or play lightly with something, typically with the fingers

Etymology

Imitative origin, resembling the sound made by certain musical instruments.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01731107-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈtwiːdəl / (AmE), / ˈtwiːd(ə)l / (BrE)
to entice or attract by playing music, especially with a repetitive or simple tune
entice or attract with music

Examples

The flute player tried to tweedle the birds down from the trees.
Legend says the piper could tweedle rats out of the city.
She tweedled a simple tune to calm the restless crowd.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: charm - to attract someone in a delightful way, often used for magical or musical effects
  • Synonym: allure - to attract or tempt by something flattering or desirable

Etymology

Imitative, from the sound of simple repeating notes; related to 'twiddle' and early 18th-century usage.

Source: WordNet
