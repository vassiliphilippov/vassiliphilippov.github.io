homophonic

1. ADJ

/ ˌhoʊməˈfɑnɪk / (AmE), / ˌhɒməˈfɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of music) having a single melodic line with accompanying harmony or chords, as opposed to multiple independent melodies.
music with one melody and accompaniment

Examples

The choir performed a homophonic arrangement of the hymn, with all voices moving together in harmony.
Much of pop music features a homophonic texture, where a single melody is supported by chords.
Baroque composers often contrasted homophonic sections with more complex polyphonic passages.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: polyphonic - (of music) consisting of two or more independent melodic lines; opposite of homophonic.

Etymology

From Greek 'homophōnos' (having the same sound), from 'homo-' (same) + 'phōnē' (sound).

2. ADJ

/ ˌhoʊməˈfɑnɪk, ˌhɑməˈfɑnɪk / (AmE), / ˌhɒməˈfɒnɪk / (BrE)
having the same or similar sound; pronounced alike but differing in meaning or spelling
having the same or similar sound

Examples

The words "knight" and "night" are homophonic in English.
Some puns rely on the use of homophonic words to create humor.
In linguistics, homophonic phrases can sometimes lead to misunderstandings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: homophone - a word pronounced the same as another but differing in meaning, spelling, or both

Etymology

From Greek homophōnos ('having the same sound'), from homos ('same') + phōnē ('sound').
