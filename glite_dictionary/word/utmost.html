utmost

1. NOUN

/ ˈʌtˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈʌtməʊst / (BrE)
the greatest or highest degree or amount possible
the greatest degree or amount

Examples

She worked to the utmost of her ability to meet the deadline.
We must do our utmost to solve this problem.
He pushed himself to the utmost during the competition.

Synset Examples

he tried his utmost

Related Terms

  • Synonym: maximum - the largest amount possible
  • Antonym: minimum - the smallest amount possible

Etymology

From Old English ūtmest, superlative of ūt (out).

2. ADJ

/ ˈʌtˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈʌtməʊst / (BrE)
furthest or most remote in space, time, or order; at the farthest limit
most remote or furthest point

Examples

He pushed on to the utmost reaches of the mountain range, far beyond the nearest village.
The explorers journeyed to the utmost limits of the Arctic Circle.
At the utmost edge of the forest stood a single, ancient oak tree.

Synset Examples

the utmost tip of the peninsula

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uttermost - furthest or most extreme; alternative form of utmost
  • Antonym: innermost - situated at the very center or most inward part

Etymology

From Old English *ūt(e)mest*, superlative of *ūt* (out).

3. ADJ

/ ˈʌtˌmoʊst / (AmE), / ˈʌtməʊst / (BrE)
of the greatest or highest degree, amount, or intensity possible; greatest; maximum
greatest possible degree or extent

Examples

He treated his guests with the utmost respect and courtesy.
We must exercise utmost caution when handling dangerous chemicals.
The safety of our employees is of utmost importance to us.

Synset Examples

extreme cold
extreme caution
extreme pleasure
utmost contempt
to the utmost degree
in the uttermost distress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: maximum - greatest amount possible
  • Antonym: minimum - least amount possible
  • Synonym: extreme - reaching the highest or furthest degree

Etymology

From Middle English 'utmost(e)', variant of 'utermost', from Old English 'ūtmest' (outermost).
