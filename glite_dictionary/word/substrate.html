substrate

1. NOUN

/ ˈsʌbˌstreɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌbstreɪt / (BrE)
a surface or material on which an organism lives, grows, or is attached
surface an organism grows on

Examples

Moss will only thrive if grown on a suitable substrate that retains moisture.
The substrate in the terrarium must be cleaned regularly to prevent mold.
Bacteria adhere to the substrate and form biofilms in aquatic environments.

Synset Examples

the gardener talked about the proper substrate for acid-loving plants

Etymology

From Latin substratum, from sub- (under) + stratum (layer).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌbˌstreɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.streɪt / (BrE)
a layer or stratum that lies beneath another layer, especially in scientific or technical contexts
underlying layer or stratum

Examples

The substrate must be clean and dry before applying the paint.
Roots grow through the soil to reach the rock substrate below.
Researchers studied the effects of algae on the coral reef substrate.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: surface - the outer or upper layer of something

Etymology

From Latin substratum (something spread underneath), from sub- (under) + stratum (layer).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsʌb.streɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.streɪt / (BrE)
a substance on which an enzyme or ferment acts in a chemical reaction
a substance acted upon by an enzyme

Examples

The enzyme binds to its substrate to catalyze the reaction efficiently.
Glucose can act as a substrate for several important metabolic enzymes.
The specificity of an enzyme depends on the shape of its substrate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enzyme - a protein that catalyzes a chemical reaction
  • Antonym: product - the substance formed as a result of a chemical reaction

Etymology

From Latin substratum, from sub- (under) + stratum (layer).

4. NOUN

/ ˈsʌbstreɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌbstreɪt / (BrE)
(Linguistics) An indigenous language that contributes features to the language of an invading or dominant people, especially when the indigenous population adopts the conquerors' language.
linguistics: native language influencing a dominant language

Examples

The Celtic languages of Britain are considered a substrate for English.
Many local words in South American Spanish come from indigenous languages acting as a substrate.
In Morocco, Berber languages serve as a substrate for Moroccan Arabic.

Synset Examples

the Celtic languages of Britain are a substrate for English

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superstrate - a dominant language that influences the language of a conquered or subordinate group

Etymology

From Latin substratum (something spread under), from sub- (under) + stratum (layer).
