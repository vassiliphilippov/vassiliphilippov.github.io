fossilization

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɑsələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɒsɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The process by which something becomes inflexible, rigid, or out of date, often used figuratively to describe attitudes, systems, or institutions.
becoming rigid or out of date

Examples

Some argue that strict adherence to tradition leads to fossilization of cultural practices.
The fossilization of outdated policies can hinder a company’s ability to innovate.
Educational systems risk fossilization if they refuse to adapt to technological advances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: petrification - the process of becoming rigid or hard like stone; inflexibility (figurative sense)
  • Antonym: modernization - the process of making something more modern; renewal or updating

Etymology

From 'fossil', ultimately from Latin 'fossilis' (dug up), with '-ization' denoting the process.

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɑːsəlɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɒsɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a plant or animal becomes a fossil, often through being gradually replaced by minerals and turned to stone
process of becoming a fossil

Examples

The fossilization of dinosaur bones can take millions of years under the right conditions.
Geologists study fossilization to learn about extinct species and past environments.
Rapid burial is one factor that increases the likelihood of fossilization occurring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fossil - the remains or imprint of a prehistoric organism preserved in rock

Etymology

From fossil + -ization, following the pattern of English noun formation for processes.
