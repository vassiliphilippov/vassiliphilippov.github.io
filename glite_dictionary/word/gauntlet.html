gauntlet

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɔnt.lət / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːnt.lɪt / (BrE)
A strong glove, often made of armored leather or metal, worn to protect the hand, especially as part of a suit of armor.
a glove of armored leather or metal

Examples

The knight put on his gauntlet before riding into battle.
She admired the intricate design of the medieval gauntlet on display at the museum.
His hand was safely protected inside a leather gauntlet during the duel.

Etymology

From Old French 'gantelet', diminutive of 'gant' meaning 'glove'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑːnt.lət / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːnt.lɪt / (BrE)
a challenge, especially one offered or accepted as a test of courage, resolve, or ability (usually used in the phrases 'throw down the gauntlet' or 'take up the gauntlet')
a challenge; test of courage or ability

Examples

When the CEO threw down the gauntlet, the sales team knew they had to exceed last year's results.
The new policy is sure to throw down the gauntlet to traditional competitors.
She was quick to take up the gauntlet and defend her research findings against criticism.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: challenge - something that tests one's abilities or resources

Etymology

From Old French *gantelet*, a diminutive of *gant* (glove). Used metaphorically from the custom of throwing down a glove as a challenge.

5. NOUN

/ ˈɡɔntlɪt / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːntlɪt / (BrE)
A form of punishment in which a person is forced to run between two lines of people who strike them as they pass.
punishment involving running between two lines of attackers

Examples

In the old navy, offenders might be forced to run the gauntlet as a severe punishment.
He felt as if he had run the gauntlet of public opinion during the scandal.
Prisoners were sometimes required to walk the gauntlet for breaking the rules.

Etymology

From Swedish 'gatlopp' (lane-course), adopted in the 17th century.
