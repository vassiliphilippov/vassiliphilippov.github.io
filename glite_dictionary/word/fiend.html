fiend

1. NOUN

/ find / (AmE), / fiːnd / (BrE)
An evil spirit or demon; a wicked supernatural being often associated with the devil.
evil supernatural being; demon

Examples

In the old folktale, a terrible fiend haunted the dark forest.
The villagers believed the disease was caused by a fiend from the underworld.
With a cry of terror, she saw the shadowy fiend lurking in the hallway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demon - an evil spirit; supernatural being that causes harm
  • Antonym: angel - a benevolent celestial being in various religions

Etymology

From Old English 'fēond' (enemy), related to Old High German 'fiant', from a Germanic base meaning 'to hate'.

2. NOUN

/ find / (AmE), / fiːnd / (BrE)
a person who is extremely enthusiastic or obsessed with something, often to an irrational degree
person with extreme enthusiasm or obsession

Examples

He's a real fiend when it comes to solving difficult chess puzzles.
My brother is a video game fiend and can play for hours without stopping.
She became a fiend for spicy food after trying her first hot pepper.

Synset Examples

A fanatic is one who can't change his mind and won't change the subject

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fanatic - a person with excessive enthusiasm, especially for a cause
  • Synonym: enthusiast - a person who is very interested in a particular activity or subject

Etymology

Old English *fēond* (enemy, devil), related to *fēon* (to hate).

3. NOUN

/ find / (AmE), / fiːnd / (BrE)
a person who is extremely cruel, wicked, or inhuman
cruel or wicked person

Examples

The prisoner was described as a fiend for his merciless crimes.
Only a fiend could have committed such a brutal act.
The detective worked tirelessly to catch the fiend responsible for the terror in the town.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: villain - a wicked or evil person in stories or real life
  • Synonym: monster - a cruel or inhuman person
  • Antonym: saint - an exceptionally virtuous or good person

Etymology

From Old English 'fēond' (enemy, devil), from Proto-Germanic *fijandz.
