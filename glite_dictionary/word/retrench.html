to retrench

1. VERB

/ rɪˈtrɛntʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈtrɛntʃ / (BrE)
to reduce costs or spending in response to economic difficulty, often by cutting down on staff or other expenditures
reduce costs or cut expenses, esp. staff

Examples

Facing declining profits, the company was forced to retrench and lay off several employees.
During economic downturns, many firms retrench to minimize their financial losses.
The management decided to retrench by cutting unnecessary expenses and freezing new hires.

Synset Examples

The company had to retrench

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to downsize - reduce the number of employees to save money
  • Antonym: to expand - to increase in size, number, or amount

Etymology

From French 'retrancher' (to cut off), from re- (again) + trancher (to cut).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈtrɛntʃ / (AmE), / rɪˈtrɛntʃ / (BrE)
to reduce spending or use resources more carefully, often because of financial difficulty
reduce spending or resources

Examples

After the company lost several major clients, it had to retrench and implement strict budget controls.
In tough economic times, families often retrench by cutting down on unnecessary expenses.
The government announced plans to retrench its workforce to save money.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cut back - to reduce something, especially spending
  • Antonym: to expand - to increase in size or scope (opposite meaning)

Etymology

From French 'retrancher', from re- (again) + 'trancher' (to cut).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02274612-v
Synset POS: v
