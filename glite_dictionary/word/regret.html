regret

1. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
to express sorrow or disappointment about something, often formally or politely
express sorrow or disappointment

Examples

I regret to inform you that your application was unsuccessful.
She regretted having to cancel the meeting at the last minute.
We deeply regret any inconvenience this may have caused.

Synset Examples

I regret to say that you did not gain admission to Harvard

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to apologize - to express regret for something wrong or inappropriate

Etymology

From Old French *regreter*, from Vulgar Latin *recristare*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00913515-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rəˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
to feel sadness or disappointment about the loss or absence of something or someone
feel sad about a loss or absence

Examples

She will always regret not spending more time with her late grandmother.
I regret the absence of your laughter at our gatherings.
He deeply regretted the loss of his childhood home after it was sold.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: miss - to feel the absence of someone or something with longing
  • Antonym: to cherish - to hold something dear; antonym in the sense of losing vs. appreciating

Etymology

From Old French 'regreter', from Medieval Latin 'regretare'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01800935-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ rəˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
a feeling of sadness or disappointment about something wrong, missed, or lost
sadness about a mistake or loss

Examples

He spoke with deep regret about his decision to leave the company.
She felt a sense of regret after missing the concert.
Their parting was filled with regret, knowing things would never be the same.

Synset Examples

he drank to drown his sorrows
he wrote a note expressing his regret
to his rue, the error cost him the game

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remorse - a deep sense of guilt or sorrow for a wrongdoing
  • Synonym: sorrow - a feeling of deep distress caused by loss, disappointment, or misfortune
  • Antonym: contentment - a state of happiness and satisfaction

Etymology

From Old French 'regretter' (to bewail the dead), from re- (expressing intensive force) + 'greter' (to weep, lament).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07551058-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
to feel sadness, remorse, or sorrow about a past action or event
feel remorse or sorrow

Examples

I deeply regret the decision I made last year.
She regretted not telling the truth when she had the chance.
You may regret it later if you act too hastily.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to repent - to feel or express sincere regret or remorse about one's wrongdoing or sin
  • Synonym: feel sorry - to experience sympathy or regret for someone or something
  • Antonym: to rejoice - to feel or show great joy or delight

Etymology

From Old French 'regreter', of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01800593-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
to politely or formally decline an invitation or request, often expressing sorrow for being unable to accept
formally decline or express inability to accept

Examples

I must regret that I am unable to attend the meeting next week.
She wrote to regret that she could not accept the invitation to the gala.
They regretted that they would not be able to join us for dinner.

Synset Examples

I regret I can't come to the party
