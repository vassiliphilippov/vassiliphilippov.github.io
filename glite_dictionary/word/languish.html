to languish

1. VERB

/ ˈlæŋɡwɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈlaŋɡwɪʃ / (BrE)
to lose strength, health, or vitality, especially because of grief, illness, or neglect
lose strength or health, often from grief

Examples

After the sudden loss of her friend, she began to languish in sorrow.
Many plants will languish without enough sunlight and water.
He continued to languish in prison for years, his spirit slowly fading.

Synset Examples

After her husband died, she just pined away

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wither - to become dry and weak
  • Antonym: to thrive - to grow or develop vigorously

Etymology

From Old French *languiss-*, stem of *languir*, from Latin *languēre* (to be weak or faint).

2. VERB

/ ˈlæŋɡwɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈlaŋɡwɪʃ / (BrE)
to have a strong desire or longing for something or someone who is not present
long for something or someone absent

Examples

Whenever her husband traveled, she would languish for his return every day.
He began to languish for the comforts of his hometown after months abroad.
Children often languish for their parents when separated for long periods.

Synset Examples

She ached for a cigarette
I am pining for my lover

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pine - to feel a lingering, often nostalgic desire
  • Synonym: to yearn - to have an intense feeling of longing for something

Etymology

From Old French *languiss-, stem of *languir*, from Latin *languēre* (to be faint, to pine).

3. VERB

/ ˈlæŋɡwɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈlæŋɡwɪʃ / (BrE)
to become weak or feeble, especially as a result of neglect, hardship, or prolonged inactivity
to become weak or feeble

Examples

After years without funding, the arts program began to languish and eventually ceased to exist.
Plants left in the dark for too long will languish without sunlight.
He started to languish in his job, feeling unchallenged and uninspired.

Synset Examples

The prisoner has been languishing for years in the dungeon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wither - to dry up or weaken gradually
  • Antonym: to thrive - to grow or develop vigorously

Etymology

From Old French 'languiss-', stem of 'languir', from Latin 'languere' (to be faint or weak).

Synset POS: v
