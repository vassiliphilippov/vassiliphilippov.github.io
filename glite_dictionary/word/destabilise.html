to destabilise

1. VERB

/ diːˈsteɪbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diːˈsteɪbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make something unstable or less secure, especially a system, government, or situation
make unstable or insecure
british

Examples

The sudden increase in oil prices could destabilise the economy.
Opposition groups sought to destabilise the administration through strikes and protests.
External interference may destabilise existing political alliances.

Synset Examples

Terrorism destabilized the government

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to destabilize - make unstable (American spelling)
  • Antonym: to stabilise - make stable or steady

Etymology

From Latin 'de-' (reversal) + 'stabilis' (stable), via French and English forms.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00272510-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈsteɪbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / diːˈsteɪ.bɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to become unstable or less secure; to lose stability
become unstable; lose stability
british

Examples

After the reforms, the market began to destabilise and investors grew wary.
If environmental conditions suddenly change, the system may destabilise.
Their interference only served to destabilise the already fragile political situation.

Synset Examples

The economy destabilized rapidly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to destabilize - American spelling of 'destabilise'
  • Antonym: to stabilise - to make stable or become stable

Etymology

From de- (expressing reversal) + stabilise, from stable.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00272976-v
Synset POS: v
