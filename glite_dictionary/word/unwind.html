to unwind

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈwaɪnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈwʌɪnd / (BrE)
to reverse the winding or twisting of something, so that it becomes straight or loose
reverse winding or twisting

Examples

She began to unwind the long scarf from around her neck.
Carefully, he unwound the cords tangled around the chair legs.
Could you help me unwind this spool of thread so it doesn't get knotted?

Synset Examples

unwind a ball of yarn

Related Terms

  • Antonym: wind - to twist or coil something (such as thread or yarn) around a spool or object

Etymology

From un- (reversal) + wind (to turn or twist), dating from the early 17th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01526442-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ʌnˈwaɪnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈwaɪnd / (BrE)
to become less tense or anxious; to relax and take one's ease, especially after stress or work
to relax or become less tense

Examples

After a stressful week, she likes to unwind with a good book.
He took a walk along the beach to unwind after the long meeting.
Music helps me unwind and forget about my worries.

Synset Examples

He relaxed in the hot tub
Let's all relax after a hard day's work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relax - to become less tense or anxious

Etymology

From un- (reverse) + wind (verb, to coil or tighten), originally meaning 'to undo winding' and figuratively to relax.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00026378-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ʌnˈwaɪnd / (AmE), / ʌnˈwaɪnd / (BrE)
to separate the tangles or twists of something, such as string, rope, or thread
separate tangles or twists

Examples

She tried to unwind the knot in the necklace with gentle fingers.
After hours of effort, he managed to unwind the tangled hose in the garden.
It's not easy to unwind a ball of yarn once it's gotten knotted.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to untangle - to make something that is tangled become straight or untwisted

Etymology

From Old English 'un-' (reversal) + 'windan' (to wind, turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01526611-v
Synset POS: v
