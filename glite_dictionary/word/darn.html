darn

1. NOUN

/ dɑrn / (AmE), / dɑːn / (BrE)
A repair made by sewing over a worn or torn hole, especially in a garment.
a sewn repair in fabric

Examples

I made a careful darn in the elbow of my favorite sweater.
The old scarf was full of darn after years of use and repair.
Her skillful darn was almost invisible, blending in with the sock's original pattern.

Synset Examples

her stockings had several mends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mend - a repair of a hole or tear in fabric

Etymology

From Middle English derne (to hide), later influenced by the verb 'darn' (to mend).

2. VERB

/ dɑrn / (AmE), / dɑːn / (BrE)
to mend a hole in fabric, especially knitted material, by weaving yarn or thread across the gap
repair fabric by sewing

Examples

She patiently darned the hole in his favorite sweater.
My grandmother taught me how to darn socks using a special needle.
I need to darn these gloves before winter arrives.

Synset Examples

darn socks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mend - to repair something that is broken or damaged
  • Synonym: patch - to repair with a piece of material sewn over a hole
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01333358-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ dɑrn / (AmE), / dɑːn / (BrE)
a thing of little or no value; used in negative constructions to emphasize worthlessness
something of little value
informal

Examples

He doesn't care a darn what people think of him.
My old phone isn't worth a darn anymore.
She said the excuse wasn't worth a darn.

Synset Examples

his promise is not worth a damn
not worth one red cent
not worth shucks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: damn - a thing of little or no value; equivalent term, more vulgar
  • Synonym: red cent - a very small amount of money; used in negative expressions
  • Synonym: shuck - something worthless; used in negative phrases

Etymology

Alteration of 'damn' to avoid profanity, first used in the 18th century.

Source: WordNet
