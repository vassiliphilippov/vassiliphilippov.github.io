enactment

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈnæktmənt / (AmE), / ɪˈnæktmənt / (BrE)
the act of performing or portraying a character on stage through speech, action, and gesture
dramatic performance of a character

Examples

The enactment of the play's central character was met with thunderous applause.
Her enactment of Lady Macbeth left a lasting impression on the audience.
During the drama class, students practiced the enactment of different emotions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: performance - the act of performing a play, concert, or other form of entertainment

Etymology

From the verb 'enact', from Middle English, from en- + act.

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈnæktmənt / (AmE), / ɪˈnæktmənt / (BrE)
the process of making a law or putting a law into effect through formal approval by a legislative body
the passing of a law by a legislature

Examples

The enactment of the new policy was welcomed by environmental groups.
After its enactment, the law came into force immediately across the country.
Debate continued for hours before the enactment of the healthcare bill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: legislation - laws considered collectively or the process of making or enacting laws
  • Antonym: repeal - the action of revoking or annulling a law

Etymology

From 'enact' + '-ment'; 'enact' from Late Latin 'enactare', from Latin 'actum' (a thing done).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈnækt.mənt / (AmE), / ɪˈnakt.mənt / (BrE)
a law, statute, or regulation that has been formally approved and put into effect by a legislative or governing body
formal legal law or regulation
legal

Examples

The new environmental enactment aims to reduce carbon emissions across the city.
After months of debate, the committee finally issued an enactment regulating public health standards.
Each enactment passed by the council is published in the official government records.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: law - a system of rules created and enforced by governmental institutions
  • Synonym: statute - a written law enacted by a legislative body

Etymology

From the verb 'enact' (to make into law) + '-ment', ultimately from Old French and Latin roots related to 'act'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
