to infiltrate

1. VERB

/ ˈɪnfɪlˌtreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪnfɪltreɪt / (BrE)
to pass into or through a substance by filtering or permeating, especially gradually and often imperceptibly
pass through or permeate a substance

Examples

Heavy rain allowed water to infiltrate the basement walls over time.
Chemicals can infiltrate the soil and reach the groundwater.
Light easily infiltrates the thin curtains in the morning.

Synset Examples

the substance infiltrated the material

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to permeate - to spread or diffuse throughout
  • Synonym: to penetrate - to enter or pass into or through

Etymology

From Latin infiltratus, past participle of infiltrare, from in- 'in' + filtrare 'to filter'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01917525-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈɪn.fɪlˌtreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.fɪl.treɪt / (BrE)
to secretly enter or become part of a group, organization, or place in order to get information or do harm
enter secretly to spy or cause harm

Examples

The police feared that criminal gangs might infiltrate the protest groups.
Posing as a volunteer, she managed to infiltrate the organization undetected.
They worked overtime to ensure no one could infiltrate their security system.

Synset Examples

The student organization was infiltrated by a traitor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spy - to secretly collect information about an enemy or competitor
  • Antonym: to exfiltrate - to withdraw covertly from an organization or area

Etymology

From Latin *infiltratus*, past participle of *infiltrare* (to filter in).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02440481-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɪn.fɪlˌtreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.fɪl.treɪt / (BrE)
(of soldiers) to pass into or through an enemy area, especially in a secret or gradual manner, usually to obtain information or carry out a specific action.
enter or pass through enemy lines (military)

Examples

The special forces unit managed to infiltrate the enemy's defensive perimeter without being detected.
During the night, several soldiers attempted to infiltrate behind enemy lines to gather intelligence.
It was their mission to infiltrate the camp and disrupt communications before the main assault.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to penetrate - to go into or through something, especially with force or effort

Etymology

From Latin infiltratus, past participle of infiltrare, from in- 'into' + filtrare 'filter'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01917356-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈɪnˌfɪl.treɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.fɪl.treɪt / (BrE)
to cause (a liquid) to enter or pass through something by penetrating its small spaces or pores
cause a liquid to pass through pores or small spaces

Examples

Water can infiltrate the soil and reach the plant roots.
Engineers designed the dam so that no chemicals would infiltrate the groundwater.
Rainwater sometimes infiltrates through the cracks in old concrete walls.

Etymology

From Latin 'infiltratus', past participle of 'infiltrare' (to filter in).
