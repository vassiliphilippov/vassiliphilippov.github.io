catapult

1. VERB

/ ˈkætəˌpʌlt / (AmE), / ˈkætəpʌlt / (BrE)
to hurl or launch something with great force, as if by a catapult or sling
hurl or launch with force

Examples

The explosion catapulted debris across the street, breaking several windows.
She was catapulted to fame after her first novel became a bestseller.
The strong wind catapulted the sailboat forward, surprising everyone on board.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: launch - to send something out or away with force
  • Synonym: throw - to send something through the air by force of the arm

Etymology

From Old French 'catapulte' and Latin 'catapulta', from Greek 'katapeltēs'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈkætəˌpʌlt / (AmE), / ˈkætəpʌlt / (BrE)
a device on a warship, especially an aircraft carrier, that launches aircraft into flight
warship device launching aircraft

Examples

The fighter jet was launched from the deck by the ship’s catapult.
After repairs, the catapult was ready to send more planes into the air.
Crews performed regular checks on the catapult to ensure smooth aircraft launches.

Etymology

From Latin catapulta, from Greek katapeltēs (shield piercer, device for hurling).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02985687-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈkæt̬əˌpʌlt / (AmE), / ˈkætəpʌlt / (BrE)
a device used as a weapon, especially in ancient and medieval times, for hurling large stones or other missiles during sieges
medieval war engine for hurling missiles

Examples

The army used a massive catapult to breach the castle walls.
During the siege, boulders were launched over the ramparts by a catapult.
Engineers reconstructed a working catapult for the historical demonstration.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trebuchet - a type of catapult with a counterweight used in sieges
  • Synonym: ballista - an ancient missile weapon that launched projectiles at a distant target

Etymology

From Latin *catapulta*, from Greek *katapeltēs* (hurling machine).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02985366-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈkæt̬əˌpʌlt / (AmE), / ˈkætəpʌlt / (BrE)
A toy consisting of a Y-shaped stick with elastic attached between the arms, used to launch small objects such as stones.
Y-shaped toy for shooting small objects

Examples

He made a catapult out of a forked branch and some old rubber bands.
The children took turns firing pebbles with their homemade catapults.
Using his catapult, he launched a stone across the garden.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slingshot - a small hand-powered projectile launcher, as commonly known in American English

Etymology

From Latin catapulta, from Greek katapeltēs, a type of ancient siege engine.

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ ˈkædəˌpʌlt / (AmE), / ˈkatəpʌlt / (BrE)
to hurl or launch something forcefully or suddenly, as if by a catapult
to hurl or launch forcefully

Examples

The company hopes this new technology will catapult it to the forefront of the industry.
After tripping, he was practically catapulted across the room by the force.
The roller coaster catapults riders forward at incredible speed in just seconds.

Synset Examples

the enemy catapulted rocks towards the fort

Related Terms

  • Synonym: launch - to send into the air or into motion, especially suddenly
  • Synonym: to propel - to drive or push something forwards

Etymology

From Latin 'catapulta', via French and late Latin, originally from Greek 'katapeltēs'.
