palisade

1. NOUN

/ ˌpælɪˈseɪd / (AmE), / ˌpælɪˈseɪd / (BrE)
a fence of strong wooden or metal stakes fixed deeply in the ground, used as a defensive barrier or enclosure
a defensive fence of stakes

Examples

The soldiers built a palisade around the camp to ward off intruders.
Ancient villages often relied on a wooden palisade for protection.
A sturdy palisade surrounded the fortress, making it difficult to attack.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stockade - a defensive barrier formed by upright posts or timbers
  • Degree Lower: fence - a barrier, usually of wood or wire, marking a boundary or enclosure

Etymology

From French *palissade*, from Italian *palizzata*, from *palizzare* (to fence with pales), from Latin *palus* (stake).

2. VERB

/ ˌpælɪˈseɪd / (AmE), / ˌpælɪˈseɪd / (BrE)
to surround or fortify (an area, especially a settlement) with a strong fence or defensive wall, typically made of stakes or timbers
to enclose with a defensive wall or fence

Examples

The colonists decided to palisade their village after several attacks from hostile forces.
In anticipation of the invasion, the army quickly palisaded the fortress.
They worked through the night, palisading the encampment to ensure everyone's safety.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fortify - to strengthen a place with defensive works

Etymology

From French 'palissade', from Provençal 'palissada', from Latin 'palus' (stake).
