antenna

1. NOUN

/ ænˈtɛnə / (AmE), / ænˈtɛnə / (BrE)
a natural ability to sense or detect subtle signals, similar to a receptor organ; an intuitive awareness
intuitive ability to detect signals or subtle cues

Examples

She has an antenna for people's moods that helps her navigate conversations effortlessly.
Journalists often rely on their antennae for newsworthy stories developing around them.
He seems to have a special antenna for detecting opportunities in the market.

Synset Examples

he had a special antenna for public relations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intuition - the ability to understand or know something without conscious reasoning
  • Synonym: instinct - a natural or intuitive way of acting or thinking

2. NOUN

/ ænˈtɛnə / (AmE), / ænˈtɛnə / (BrE)
A device used to transmit or receive radio or television signals, typically consisting of a metallic rod or wire.
device for sending or receiving radio signals

Examples

The car's antenna was damaged during the storm, so the radio reception was poor.
The technician climbed the tower to repair the broken television antenna.
They installed a new antenna on the roof to improve the signal quality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aerial - another term for antenna, chiefly British usage

Etymology

From Latin 'antenna' (sail yard), later used for insect feelers and technical senses.

3. NOUN

/ ænˈtɛnə / (AmE), / ænˈtɛnə / (BrE)
either of a pair of long, thin, movable sensory appendages on the head of insects, crustaceans, and some other arthropods, sensitive to touch and taste
sensory appendage on insects or crustaceans

Examples

The butterfly uses its antennae to sense changes in the air.
A lobster's long antennae swept the ocean floor in search of food.
After molting, the crab's new antennae appeared longer and more delicate than before.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feeler - a long, flexible organ for touch or sensing, as in insects; synonym for antenna

Etymology

From Latin 'antenna' meaning 'sail yard', later used for insect feelers.

Source: WordNet
