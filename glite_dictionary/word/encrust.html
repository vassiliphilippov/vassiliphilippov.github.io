to encrust

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
to form a hard outer layer or crust on the surface of something
form a hard outer layer or crust

Examples

Minerals slowly encrust the surface of the underwater rocks.
Barnacles encrust the hull of the old ship after years at sea.
Over time, grime and salt began to encrust the statue's base.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coat - to cover something with a layer of a substance

Etymology

From Latin 'crusta' (a rind, shell, or crust), via French 'encruster'.

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
to cover or coat (something) with a hard, outer layer or crust
cover with a hard outer layer

Examples

The ancient coins were encrusted with layers of dirt from centuries underground.
Salt crystals can encrust the surface of rocks near the shoreline.
After years at sea, the anchor became encrusted with barnacles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coating - a layer of a substance spread over a surface
  • Antonym: bare - not covered or coated with anything

Etymology

From Latin 'crusta' (hard surface, shell, crust), via Old French 'encruster'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01264192-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈkrʌst / (AmE), / ɪnˈkrʌst / (BrE)
to decorate or cover a surface thickly or lavishly, especially with small, shiny objects such as gems or shells
cover or decorate lavishly, especially with gems

Examples

The ancient crown was encrusted with precious jewels and gold filigree.
Over time, the ship's hull became encrusted with barnacles and seashells, creating a rugged surface.
She wore a dress encrusted with shimmering beads that sparkled under the lights.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embellish - to make something more attractive by adding decorative details
  • Antonym: strip - to remove decorations or coverings

Etymology

From Latin 'crusta' meaning a crust or shell, via French 'encruster'.

Source: WordNet
