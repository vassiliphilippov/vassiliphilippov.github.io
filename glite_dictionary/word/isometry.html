isometry

1. NOUN

/ aɪˈsɑmətri / (AmE), / aɪˈsɒmətri / (BrE)
equal growth rate in different parts of an organism so that proportions remain constant during development
equal growth of body parts in organisms
medical

Examples

The concept of isometry explains why a young animal may look like a smaller version of the adult, with similar proportions.
In some species, isometry is observed when their body parts grow at the same rate throughout development.
Comparing isometry and allometry can help scientists understand evolutionary adaptations in morphology.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: allometry - growth of different parts of an organism at different rates

Etymology

From Greek isos (equal) + metron (measure).

2. NOUN

/ aɪˈsɑːmətri / (AmE), / aɪˈsɒmətɹi / (BrE)
equality in measurement or dimension, such as equality of height, length, or other quantities, often used in mathematics and science.
equality of measure or dimension

Examples

The experiment tested the isometry of sound intensity between different rooms.
Geologists use the concept of isometry to compare elevations accurately.
Ensuring isometry is crucial when measuring the properties of materials.

3. NOUN

/ aɪˈsɑmətri / (AmE), / aɪˈsɒmətri / (BrE)
a mapping between metric spaces that preserves the distance between every pair of points
distance-preserving mapping (math)

Examples

The isometry ensured that the measurements were preserved during the mapping.
Every rotation of a regular polygon is an isometry of the plane.
In geometry, reflections and translations are classic examples of an isometry.

Synset Examples

the isometries of the cube

Related Terms

  • Synonym: congruence - equality in shape and size, especially in geometry
  • Degree Higher: transformation - a general mathematical operation altering a space, but not always isometric
