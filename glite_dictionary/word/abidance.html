abidance

1. NOUN

/ əˈbaɪdəns / (AmE), / əˈbaɪdəns / (BrE)
the act of residing or staying in a particular place; habitation or residence.
the act of dwelling or residing in a place

Examples

His abidance in the quiet village lasted for many peaceful years.
The law required a minimum period of abidance to qualify for citizenship.
During their abidance at the retreat, they found time to reflect and recharge.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: residence - the act of living in a place

Etymology

From 'abide' + '-ance', modelled after Latin-based nouns.

2. NOUN

/ əˈbaɪdəns / (AmE), / əˈbaɪdəns / (BrE)
the act of following or conforming to rules, laws, or accepted standards
the act of conforming to rules or standards

Examples

The school's strict abidance to safety regulations has kept students secure.
She appreciated his abidance by the rules during the competition.
Corporate abidance with environmental laws is now closely monitored.

Synset Examples

their financial statements are in conformity with generally accepted accounting practices

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compliance - the act of obeying a rule or law
  • Antonym: noncompliance - failure or refusal to conform to rules or standards

Etymology

From 'abide' + '-ance', formed in English in the 16th century.

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ əˈbaɪdəns / (AmE), / əˈbaɪdəns / (BrE)
the act of enduring or continuing without yielding
enduring or continuing without yielding

Examples

His abidance through years of hardship was truly remarkable.
To succeed in such a harsh environment requires great abidance.
She showed admirable abidance in the face of repeated disappointments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: endurance - the ability to endure pain or hardship
  • Synonym: resilience - the capacity to recover quickly from difficulties

Etymology

From the verb 'abide' + suffix '-ance', modeled after similar abstract nouns.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
