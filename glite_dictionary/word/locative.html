locative

1. NOUN

/ ˈloʊkətɪv / (AmE), / ˈlɒkətɪv / (BrE)
(In linguistics) the grammatical case, or semantic role, indicating the place where something occurs or the location associated with an action or state.
linguistic role indicating location

Examples

In the sentence 'She lives in London', the prepositional phrase acts as a locative.
Some languages have a specific locative case for expressing location.
The locative often answers the questions 'where?' or 'in what place?' in a sentence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: instrumental - grammatical case/role indicating the means by which an action is performed
  • Synonym: ablative - grammatical case/role indicating motion away from something

Etymology

From Latin locus (place) + -ative (forming adjectives/nouns).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɑːk.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / ˈlɒk.ə.tɪv / (BrE)
(in grammar) a grammatical case indicating location or place where something happens or exists.
case indicating location (grammar)

Examples

In Latin, the locative was used to specify where an action took place.
The Russian language still retains a distinct locative case for some nouns.
Linguists study how the locative varies across different families of languages.

Synset Examples

Languages that use the locative case include Armenian, Azeri, Belarusian, Catalan, Serbo-Croatian, Czech, Dyirbal, Latin, Latvian, Lithuanian, Polish, Quechua, Russian, Sanskrit, Slovak, Slovene, Swahili, Turkish and Ukrainian.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dative - grammatical case indicating the indirect object or recipient
  • Synonym: genitive - grammatical case indicating possession or close association
  • Synonym: ablative - grammatical case indicating movement away from something

Etymology

From Latin locativus (of or relating to place), from locus (place).
