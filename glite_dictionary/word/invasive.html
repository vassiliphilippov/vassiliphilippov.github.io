invasive

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (BrE)
(of actions, forces, or attacks) involving invasion or an aggressive entry into a place, area, or country.
involving invasion or aggressive attack

Examples

The general planned an invasive campaign to seize control of the enemy's territory.
Some leaders condemned the invasive nature of the foreign intervention.
Historians still debate the causes of the invasive war that devastated the region.

Synset Examples

invasive war

Related Terms

  • Synonym: offensive - actively attacking; projecting force outwards
  • Antonym: defensive - intended to protect or defend against attack

Etymology

From Latin invasivus, from invadere (to go into, attack).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (BrE)
(of a disease or organism) tending to spread rapidly and undesirably, especially into healthy tissue or environments.
tending to spread undesirably

Examples

The doctor explained that invasive tumors can quickly affect nearby organs if not treated promptly.
Some procedures are classified as minimally invasive to reduce risk and speed up recovery.
Scientists study how invasive cells bypass the body's natural defenses.

Synset Examples

invasive cancer cells

Related Terms

  • Antonym: noninvasive - not tending to spread into healthy tissue

Etymology

From Medieval Latin invasivus (tending to attack), from Latin invadere (to go into, attack).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01358814-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (BrE)
tending to intrude gradually or without permission, often in a way that is unwelcome or harmful
gradually intrusive or unwelcome

Examples

The invasive weeds quickly took over the garden, crowding out the native plants.
She found the reporter's questions to be uncomfortably invasive.
There was growing concern about the invasive presence of tourists in the quiet village.

Synset Examples

we moved back from the encroaching tide
invasive tourists
trespassing hunters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intrusive - tending to intrude; interrupting or disturbing
  • Synonym: encroaching - intruding or trespassing gradually beyond proper limits
  • Antonym: welcoming - friendly or inviting, the opposite of intrusive

Etymology

From Medieval Latin invasivus, from Latin invadere (to go into, attack).

4. ADJ

/ ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪsɪv / (BrE)
(of medical procedures) involving entering the body, typically by cutting or puncturing the skin or by inserting instruments.
involving entry into the body (medicine)
medical

Examples

The patient opted for a less invasive surgical technique to reduce recovery time.
Many modern tests are now invasive, requiring minimal incisions or use of catheters.
Doctors explained that an invasive procedure might be necessary to diagnose the problem.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: noninvasive - not involving entry into the body

Etymology

From Latin 'invadere' meaning 'to go into or attack'.
