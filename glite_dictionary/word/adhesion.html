adhesion

1. NOUN

/ ədˈhiʒən / (AmE), / ədˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
the tendency or property of surfaces or materials to stick together or join, especially due to attractive forces between different materials
property of sticking together; joining of surfaces

Examples

The adhesion of the paint to the wall was remarkably strong.
Proper surface preparation ensures optimal adhesion when gluing metal parts together.
The adhesion between the tires and the road is crucial for safe driving.

Synset Examples

the mutual adhesiveness of cells
a heated hydraulic press was required for adhesion

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: cohesion - the property that causes like molecules or substances to stick together

Etymology

From Latin ‘adhaesio’ (a sticking to), from ‘adhaerere’ (to stick to).

2. NOUN

/ ədˈhiʒən / (AmE), / ədˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
An abnormal union of bodily tissues, often occurring in the abdomen, typically as a result of inflammation or injury.
abnormal joining of bodily tissues
medical

Examples

After the surgery, adhesion in her abdomen caused persistent pain and digestive issues.
The doctor explained that scar tissue could form adhesion between organs after an infection.
Laparoscopic procedures are often used to remove adhesion resulting from previous abdominal operations.

Etymology

From Latin 'adhaesio', from 'adhaerere' meaning 'to stick to'.

3. NOUN

/ ædˈhiʒən / (AmE), / ədˈhiːʒən / (BrE)
a band of fibrous scar tissue that forms between normally separate tissues and organs, often as a result of injury or surgery
fibrous tissue binding organs or tissues
medical

Examples

After her surgery, the doctor warned her about the possible formation of adhesions in her abdomen.
The patient's chronic pain was eventually linked to adhesions that developed after the injury.
Surgeons try to minimize the risk of adhesion formation during abdominal procedures.

Etymology

From Latin 'adhaesionem' (a sticking to), from 'adhaerere' (to stick to).

faithful support or attachment
