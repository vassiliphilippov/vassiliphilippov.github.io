crisscross

1. ADJ

/ ˈkrɪsˌkrɔs / (AmE), / ˈkrɪsˌkrɒs / (BrE)
marked with a pattern of crossing lines or intersecting paths
marked with crossing lines

Examples

The old map was crisscross with faded pencil lines showing possible routes.
She admired the crisscross pattern of shadows on the floor created by the blinds.
His shirt had a distinctive crisscross design woven into the fabric.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crosshatched - marked with a pattern of intersecting lines, especially in drawing

Etymology

Reduplication of 'cross' to mimic repeated crossing action.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02013796-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ ˈkrɪsˌkrɔs / (AmE), / ˈkrɪsˌkrɒs / (BrE)
a pattern or arrangement of lines that cross each other, forming a network or lattice
intersecting lines pattern

Examples

The wooden table was covered with a crisscross of knife marks and scratches.
Sunlight shone through the crisscross of wires on the fence, casting patterns on the ground.
The map was filled with a crisscross of roads and railway lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crosshatch - a pattern made of intersecting sets of parallel lines

Etymology

From Middle English 'cristcross' (Christ-cross), referring to the symbol of a cross.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04688744-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADV

/ ˈkrɪsˌkrɔs / (AmE), / ˈkrɪs.krɒs / (BrE)
in a way that crosses or moves in different directions over one another, especially forming a pattern of intersecting lines or paths
crossing in opposite directions

Examples

The narrow paths crisscross through the park, making it easy to explore every corner.
Their journeys crisscross across Europe, never staying in one place for long.
Wires crisscross overhead, creating a tangled web above the street.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intersect - to cross or pass through each other

Etymology

From the reduplication of Christ and cross, originally referring to the sign of the cross, later to intersecting lines.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00294485-r
Synset POS: r
