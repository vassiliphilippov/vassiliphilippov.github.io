mutable

1. ADJ

/ ˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (BrE)
(biology) capable of undergoing genetic mutation; liable to change genetically.
liable to genetic mutation

Examples

Some viruses are especially mutable, allowing them to evade immune responses rapidly.
Research focuses on the mutable genes of bacteria that adapt to antibiotics.
The mutable nature of certain DNA sequences increases the rate of genetic variation.

Synset Examples

It is likely, too, that the chromosomes of all eubacteria are as mutable as that of E. coli

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stable - not likely to undergo genetic mutation

Etymology

From Latin mutabilis (liable to change), from mutare (to change).

WordNet ID: oewn-00347163-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ˈmjuː.t̬ə.bəl / (AmE), / ˈmjuː.tjʊ.bəl / (BrE)
Liable or likely to change; prone to frequent alteration or inconstancy.
liable to change; inconstant

Examples

The political climate in the country is highly mutable, shifting rapidly with each election.
Fashion trends are mutable and rarely stay the same for long.
His opinions proved to be mutable, as he often changed his mind on important issues.

Synset Examples

the fickle and mutable nature of truth
the mutable ways of fortune

Related Terms

  • Antonym: immutable - unchanging over time; unable to be changed

Etymology

From Latin *mutabilis* (changeable), from *mutare* (to change).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00587318-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (BrE)
liable or likely to change; capable of change or being changed in form, quality, or nature.
liable or likely to change

Examples

The rules of the game are mutable and may be updated annually.
She admired his mutable personality, which allowed him to adapt to any situation.
Because the weather is highly mutable in spring, you should always be prepared for changes.

Synset Examples

a mutable substance
mutable weather patterns
a mutable foreign policy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: changeable - able to change or be changed; likely to change
  • Antonym: immutable - unchanging over time or unable to be changed

Etymology

From Latin 'mutabilis', from 'mutare' (to change).
