fleet

1. VERB

/ fliːt / (AmE), / fliːt / (BrE)
to move quickly and lightly over a surface; to skim or dart
move rapidly and lightly

Examples

The dragonflies fleet over the still surface of the pond each morning.
Clouds fleeted across the sky as the wind began to rise.
Her shadow fleeting through the hallway reminded me of a wisp of smoke.

Synset Examples

The hummingbird flitted among the branches

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flit - to move swiftly and lightly
  • Synonym: dart - to move suddenly and quickly

Etymology

Old English *fleotan* (to float, swim, drift), related to Dutch *vlieten*, German *fließen* (to flow).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ fliːt / (AmE), / fliːt / (BrE)
a group of motor vehicles, such as cars or trucks, owned or operated by a single organization or company
group of vehicles under one ownership

Examples

The company added ten new vans to its fleet to increase delivery capacity.
Our airport offers discounts to fleet operators who use multiple taxis daily.
Maintaining a fleet of trucks requires careful scheduling and regular servicing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: convoy - group of vehicles or ships traveling together for protection

Etymology

From Old English *fleot* (ship, floating vessel), later extended to groups of vehicles.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08310411-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ flit / (AmE), / fliːt / (BrE)
a group of warships operating together as an organized unit
group of warships acting together

Examples

The royal navy sent its entire fleet to patrol the coast.
Several ships in the fleet participated in the annual naval exercises.
A new aircraft carrier was added to the country's main fleet.

Related Terms

6. VERB

/ fliːt / (AmE), / fliːt / (BrE)
(literary or archaic) To disappear or pass away gradually; to fade slowly.
disappear or fade gradually
archaic

Examples

As the years fleet, memories of childhood slowly fade away.
The pain in his chest fleeted overnight, much to his relief.
After the excitement of the party, the feeling of joy began to fleet from her heart.

Synset Examples

The pain eventually passed off

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fade - to gradually disappear or lose intensity
  • Antonym: to linger - to last for a long time, delaying disappearance

Etymology

Old English *flēotan* (to float), related to Old Norse *fljóta*; sense development to 'pass away' in Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00422644-v
Synset POS: v

7. ADJ

/ fliːt / (AmE), / fliːt / (BrE)
moving very fast; swift or rapid, especially in movement
moving very fast; swift

Examples

The deer proved remarkably fleet as it darted through the forest.
She was known as the most fleet runner on the team.
With fleet movements, the children escaped before anyone saw them.

Synset Examples

fleet of foot
the fleet scurrying of squirrels
a swift current
swift flight of an arrow
a swift runner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swift - moving or capable of moving at high speed
  • Antonym: slow - moving or operating only at a low speed; not quick or fast

Etymology

Old English flēotan (float, swim), later influenced by Old Norse fljótr (swift).
