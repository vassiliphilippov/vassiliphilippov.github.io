tartar

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɑrt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːtə / (BrE)
a hard, yellowish deposit that forms on the teeth and gums, often leading to dental problems if not removed
hard deposit on teeth and gums
medical

Examples

Brushing twice a day helps prevent tartar buildup on your teeth.
If tartar is not removed, it can lead to gum disease and cavities.
A dental hygienist can remove stubborn tartar during a routine cleaning.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: plaque - a soft, sticky film that accumulates on the teeth

Etymology

From Medieval Latin tartarum, used for various crusty deposits, including the dental deposit.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɑrtər / (AmE), / ˈtɑːtə / (BrE)
A white, crystalline salt, especially potassium bitartrate, used mainly in baking powder to promote rising in baked goods.
a salt used in baking powder

Examples

The recipe calls for half a teaspoon of tartar to help the cake rise.
She bought some tartar from the grocery store to use in her baking.
Baking powder contains both an acid, such as tartar, and a base.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cream of tartar - Potassium bitartrate, commonly used in cooking and baking

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'tartarum', probably from Arabic 'ṭarṭār' (sediment).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɑːr.tɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɑː.tə / (BrE)
a fiercely strict, vigilant, and unpleasant person, especially a woman; someone who is hard to deal with
a fiercely strict or unpleasant person

Examples

The new headmistress is a real tartar and everyone is afraid of her.
Most employees dreaded their boss, who was known as a tartar in the office.
When she took charge, her tartar reputation ensured that rules were followed to the letter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: martinet - a strict disciplinarian, especially in the armed forces
  • Synonym: tyrant - a cruel and oppressive ruler or person
  • Antonym: softie - a person who is easily influenced or does not enforce strictness

Etymology

Originally from Medieval Latin 'Tartarus', from Greek mythology, associated with the Tartars (Tatars) thought to be fierce warriors.
