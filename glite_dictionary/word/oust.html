to oust

1. VERB

/ aʊst / (AmE), / aʊst / (BrE)
to remove someone or something from a position of power, authority, or place, often to be replaced by another
remove and replace

Examples

The board voted to oust the company's long-time CEO after months of controversy.
A military coup ousted the elected government from power.
The new smartphone quickly ousted older models from the market.

Synset Examples

The word processor has ousted the typewriter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to displace - to take the place of or move someone from a position
  • Synonym: to evict - to force someone to leave a place
  • Antonym: to install - to put someone into a position of power

Etymology

From Anglo-French *ouster*, from Latin *augere* (to increase, enrich; to remove in legal sense).

2. VERB

/ aʊst / (AmE), / aʊst / (BrE)
to remove someone from a position of power or authority, especially suddenly or forcefully
remove from office or power

Examples

The board voted to oust the CEO after a series of scandals.
Several politicians were ousted from their positions during the government shake-up.
Activists are campaigning to oust corrupt officials from office.

Synset Examples

The chairman was ousted after he misappropriated funds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dismiss - to remove someone from their job or position
  • Synonym: to expel - to force someone to leave a place, position, or organization

Etymology

From Old French *oster* (to take away), from Latin *obstare* (to stand in the way of).

Synset POS: v
