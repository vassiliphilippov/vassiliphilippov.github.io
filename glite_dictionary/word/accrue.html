to accrue

1. VERB

/ əˈkru / (AmE), / əˈkruː / (BrE)
to increase or accumulate over time, especially as a result of regular additions
to accumulate or increase over time

Examples

Interest will accrue on your savings account monthly.
Benefits will accrue to employees who stay with the company longer.
Over the years, a considerable sum has accrued in her retirement fund.

Synset Examples

The interest accrues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accumulate - to gather or build up gradually
  • Antonym: to diminish - to become less, decrease

Etymology

From Old French 'acreue', feminine past participle of 'acreu' (to increase), from Latin 'accrēscere' (to grow, increase).

2. VERB

/ əˈkru / (AmE), / əˈkruː / (BrE)
(of a right, property, or sum of money) to come into the possession of someone, especially as a result of the passage of time or by being assigned or inherited.
to come into possession of (formally)

Examples

The rights to the estate will accrue to her after the legal process is complete.
Interest will accrue on your savings account every month.
Upon the founder's death, control of the company accrued to his eldest daughter.

Synset Examples

The house accrued to the oldest son

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to inherit - to receive property or money after someone's death
  • Antonym: to lose - to cease to have something, often as an antonym to 'acquire' or 'accrue'

Etymology

From Old French 'acreue' (increase, growth), from 'acreu' (past participle of 'acréistre', to increase), from Latin 'accrescere' (to grow, increase).
