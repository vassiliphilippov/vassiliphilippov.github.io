parabolic

1. ADJ

/ ˌpærəˈbɑlɪk / (AmE), / ˌparəˈbɒlɪk / (BrE)
having the form or characteristics of a parabola; shaped like a parabola.
having the shape of a parabola

Examples

The flight path of the ball followed a parabolic arc.
Engineers designed the satellite dish with a parabolic shape to focus signals better.
The parabolic curve is a common concept in algebra and physics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: paraboloid - a three-dimensional surface with a parabolic cross-section

Etymology

From Late Latin parabolicus, from parabola (parabola, a geometric curve).

2. ADJ

/ ˌpærəˈbɑlɪk / (AmE), / ˌparəˈbɒlɪk / (BrE)
(of language, writing, or style) resembling or expressed by parables; allegorical or emblematic in nature.
resembling or expressed by parables

Examples

His parabolic teaching style encouraged students to think deeply about each lesson.
The story had a parabolic quality, using allegory to convey its moral message.
In ancient texts, wisdom was often communicated in parabolic form rather than direct instruction.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allegorical - using symbolism to represent ideas or concepts
  • Antonym: literal - taking words in their usual or most basic sense

Etymology

From Late Latin parabolicus, from Greek parabolikos (relating to a parable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03008412-a
Synset POS: a
