slope

1. NOUN

/ sloʊp / (AmE), / sləʊp / (BrE)
the degree to which a line or surface inclines from the horizontal
measure of inclination from horizontal

Examples

The slope of the hill made cycling up it quite challenging.
Engineers calculated the slope of the road to ensure proper drainage.
The graph shows a line with a constant slope throughout its length.

Synset Examples

a five-degree gradient

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gradient - a measure of how steep a slope is
  • Synonym: incline - an upward or downward slant

Etymology

From Middle English 'slop' (a slope), likely of Scandinavian origin; related to Swedish 'slupa' (to fall off).

2. VERB

/ sloʊp / (AmE), / sləʊp / (BrE)
(of a surface or line) to be at an angle away from a horizontal or vertical position.
to be at an angle

Examples

The driveway slopes gently toward the street.
The roof sloped so that rainwater could run off easily.
Beyond the garden, the land slopes down to a river.

Synset Examples

The terrain sloped down

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tilt - to move or cause to move into a sloping position
  • Antonym: level - to make or become flat or even
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02040935-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ sloʊp / (AmE), / sləʊp / (BrE)
a piece of land that slants upward or downward, especially the side of a hill or mountain
slanting side of a hill or mountain

Examples

The cabin is nestled on the slope of a green hillside.
After the rain, the muddy slope was difficult to climb.
Children love sledding down the snowy slope in winter.

Synset Examples

he climbed the steep slope
the house was built on the side of a mountain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incline - a sloping surface or angle
  • Antonym: flat - having a level surface without slope

Etymology

From Old French 'esclot' (sudden descent); sense developed in English in the 17th century.
