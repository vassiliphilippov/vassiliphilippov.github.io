cabotage

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæb.əˌtɑʒ / (AmE), / ˈkab.ə.tɑːʒ / (BrE)
the right or practice of navigating or operating ships or aircraft within a country's coastal waters or airspace, especially between ports or airports of the same country
navigation or transport within a country's borders

Examples

The government imposed restrictions on foreign cabotage to protect its domestic shipping industry.
Airline cabotage rights are a contentious issue in international agreements.
New laws were introduced to encourage competition in coastal cabotage services.

Etymology

From French 'cabotage', from 'caboter' (to sail along the coast), likely from Portuguese 'cabo' (cape).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæbətɑʒ / (AmE), / ˈkabətɑːʒ / (BrE)
the right of a country to regulate or restrict domestic transport, such as air or shipping services, within its own borders, excluding foreign operators
restriction of domestic transport to national carriers
legal

Examples

International airlines are prohibited from flying domestic routes due to cabotage regulations.
The government enforces cabotage to protect its local shipping industry.
Debate about cabotage arose during the negotiations for new trade agreements.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: open skies - policy allowing unrestricted air travel between countries

Etymology

From French 'cabotage', originally referring to coastal navigation, from 'caboter' (to sail along the coast).
