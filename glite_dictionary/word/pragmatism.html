pragmatism

1. NOUN

/ ˈpræɡməˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈpræɡmətɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
(philosophy) The theory that the truth or meaning of an idea or proposition lies in its observable practical consequences.
philosophical doctrine about practical consequences

Examples

The philosophy of pragmatism evaluates ideas by their practical results rather than by abstract principles.
Many educators are influenced by pragmatism and focus on what students can actively apply.
American philosopher William James is regarded as a key figure in the development of pragmatism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: practicality - the quality of being practical or suited to actual use
  • Antonym: idealism - the practice of forming or pursuing ideals, especially unrealistically

Etymology

From Greek pragma (deed, action) + -ism.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpræɡ.məˌtɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈpræɡ.mə.tɪz.əm / (BrE)
A practical approach to problems and affairs, focusing on results and real-world application rather than theoretical considerations or ideals.
a practical approach; focus on results

Examples

Her pragmatism helped her solve difficult problems efficiently at work.
Many voters admired his pragmatism when facing the country's economic crisis.
The committee valued pragmatism over ideology in their decision-making process.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: idealism - the pursuit of ideals, especially unrealistically; opposite of pragmatism
  • Synonym: practicality - concern with actual doing or use rather than theory; near-synonym of pragmatism

Etymology

From Greek pragma (deed, act), via French pragmatisme and modern Latin pragmatismus.
