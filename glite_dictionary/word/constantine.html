Constantine

1. PROPN

/ ˌkɑːnstənˈtin / (AmE), / ˌkɒnstənˈtiːn / (BrE)
a walled city in northeastern Algeria, east of Algiers, known for its dramatic gorges and historical significance; rebuilt in the 4th century by Emperor Constantine I.
historical city in northeastern Algeria

Examples

The city of Constantine is famous for its spectacular bridges and cliffs.
During his travels in Algeria, he visited Constantine to explore its rich history.
Many people consider Constantine one of the most beautiful cities in North Africa.

Etymology

Named after Roman Emperor Constantine I.

2. PROPN

/ ˈkɑːnstənˌtaɪn / (AmE), / ˈkɒnstəntiːn / (BrE)
The Roman emperor (circa 280–337) who ended the persecution of Christians, adopted Christianity as the Empire's official religion, and established Constantinople as the new capital.
Roman emperor (circa 280–337); adopted Christianity and founded Constantinople

Examples

Constantine is often credited with making Christianity the dominant religion in the Roman Empire.
The city of Constantinople, named after Constantine, became a major center of culture and trade.
Many historians consider Constantine's reign a turning point in both Roman and Christian history.

Etymology

From Latin Constantinus, a given name derived from 'constans', meaning steadfast or constant.
