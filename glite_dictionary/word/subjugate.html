to subjugate

1. VERB

/ ˈsʌbdʒəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌbdʒʊɡeɪt / (BrE)
to bring under domination or control, especially by conquest; to make someone or something subordinate or subservient
bring under control; make subservient

Examples

The conquerors sought to subjugate the indigenous people to their rule.
Throughout history, empires have tried to subjugate neighboring nations for resources and power.
She refused to let fear subjugate her will and determination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dominate - to have control or power over; to rule
  • Antonym: to liberate - to set free from oppression or control

Etymology

From Latin subjugare, from sub- (under) + jugum (yoke).

2. VERB

/ ˈsʌbdʒəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsʌbdʒʊɡeɪt / (BrE)
to bring under control, especially by force; to make subordinate or subject to domination
bring under control by force

Examples

Throughout history, powerful nations have often tried to subjugate weaker ones for their own gain.
The dictator used fear and violence to subjugate his people.
Activists fought against the laws that sought to subjugate minority groups.

Synset Examples

The government quashes any attempt of an uprising
China keeps down her dissidents very efficiently
The rich landowners subjugated the peasants working the land

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dominate - to exert control or authority over
  • Synonym: to oppress - to keep someone in hardship, especially by the unjust exercise of authority
  • Antonym: to liberate - to set free, as from oppression, confinement, or foreign control

Etymology

From Latin subjugare, from sub- (under) + jugum (yoke).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
