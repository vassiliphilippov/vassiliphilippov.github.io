chantry

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʃæntri / (AmE), / ˈtʃæntri / (BrE)
A chapel or part of a church endowed for the chanting of Masses for the soul of the donor.
endowed chapel for Masses for the dead

Examples

The wealthy merchant donated funds for a chantry to be built in the cathedral.
Many medieval churches contain a chantry dedicated to their original benefactors.
The dissolution of the chantries affected charitable works throughout England.

Etymology

From Old French chanterie (singing, chant), from chanter (to sing), from Latin cantare.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtʃæn.tɹi / (AmE), / ˈtʃæn.tri / (BrE)
an endowment or fund established for the singing of Masses for the soul of the founder, especially common in medieval Christian churches
endowment for Masses (medieval context)

Examples

The wealthy merchant left funds in his will to establish a chantry for prayers after his death.
Many medieval churches contained a chantry chapel funded by local nobility.
The dissolution of the chantries was a major event in English Reformation history.

Etymology

From Old French 'chanterie' (singing, song), from 'chanter' (to sing), from Latin 'cantare'.
