liquidity

1. NOUN

/ lɪˈkwɪdəti / (AmE), / lɪˈkwɪdəti / (BrE)
the quality or state of a substance being liquid, characterized by readiness to flow, low tendency to disperse, and relative incompressibility
the state of being liquid (readiness to flow)

Examples

The liquidity of water allows it to flow freely through narrow channels.
Engineers studied the liquidity of molten metals during the manufacturing process.
Oil's liquidity makes it an effective lubricant in machinery.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fluidity - the ability of a substance to flow easily

Etymology

From Latin liquiditas (fluidity, clearness), from liquidus (liquid).

2. NOUN

/ lɪˈkwɪdəti / (AmE), / lɪˈkwɪdəti / (BrE)

Examples

Adding certain solvents can improve the liquidity of a mixture, making it easier to pour.

3. NOUN

/ lɪˈkwɪdəti / (AmE), / lɪˈkwɪdɪti / (BrE)
The quality of being able to be quickly and easily converted into cash; the ability to meet short-term financial obligations.
ability to convert assets to cash

Examples

The company improved its liquidity by selling some of its assets.
Banks monitor their liquidity to ensure they can handle withdrawals.
During the crisis, many firms struggled with liquidity issues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solvency - ability to meet all long-term financial obligations
  • Antonym: illiquidity - the state of not being easily converted into cash

Etymology

From Latin 'liquidus' (liquid, flowing) + English suffix '-ity'.
