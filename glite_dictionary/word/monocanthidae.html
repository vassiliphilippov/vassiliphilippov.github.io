Monocanthidae

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɑnoʊˈkænθɪˌdi / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəʊˈkænθɪˌdiː / (BrE)
A family of marine fishes known as filefishes, characterized by compressed bodies and rough skin.
family of marine fishes (filefishes)

Examples

The Monocanthidae family includes many species that inhabit coral reefs.
Marine biologists have studied Monocanthidae adaptability to changing ocean temperatures.
Some Monocanthidae exhibit remarkable camouflage abilities among sea grasses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: filefish - a common name for fish in the Monocanthidae family

Etymology

From Greek 'monos' (single) + 'akantha' (thorn), referring to the single spine on their dorsal fin.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɑːnoʊˈkænθɪdiː / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəʊˈkanθɪdiː / (BrE)
A family of tropical and subtropical marine fishes known as filefishes, characterized by rough, sandpapery skin and a laterally compressed body.
family of filefishes
medical

Examples

The Monocanthidae family includes a variety of filefishes found in coral reefs around the world.
Researchers studied the camouflage abilities of several members of the Monocanthidae family.
Aquariums often display species from the Monocanthidae due to their unique appearance and behavior.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: filefish - any member of the family Monocanthidae, known for their rough skin

Etymology

From Greek 'monos' (single) + 'akantha' (thorn or spine), referring to the prominent dorsal spine.
