Saccharomyces

1. NOUN

/ ˌsæk.əroʊˈmaɪ.siːz / (AmE), / ˌsæk.ə.rəˈmaɪ.siːz / (BrE)
A genus of single-celled yeasts that reproduce asexually by budding, widely used to ferment carbohydrates in baking and alcoholic beverage production.
genus of yeast used in fermentation

Examples

Saccharomyces is essential in bread making for its ability to ferment sugars and produce carbon dioxide.
Scientists often use Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a model organism in genetics research.
The fermentation process in beer relies heavily on strains of Saccharomyces.

Etymology

From Greek 'sakcharon' (sugar) + 'mykēs' (fungus).

2. NOUN

/ sæk.əˌroʊˈmaɪ.siːz / (AmE), / sak.ə.rəˈmaɪ.siːz / (BrE)
A genus of single-celled yeasts that reproduce asexually by budding and are used to ferment carbohydrates, especially in baking and brewing.
genus of yeasts used in fermentation
medical

Examples

Saccharomyces cerevisiae is essential in the production of bread and beer.
Researchers are studying various strains of Saccharomyces to improve fermentation efficiency.
The laboratory grew Saccharomyces cultures to analyze their metabolic processes.

Etymology

From Greek sakcharon (sugar) + mykēs (fungus).
