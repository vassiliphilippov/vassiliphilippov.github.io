to ventilate

1. VERB

/ ˈvɛn.t̬ɪ.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛn.tɪ.leɪt / (BrE)
to provide a room or building with an opening that allows fresh air to circulate or gases to escape
provide with means for air or gas circulation

Examples

It's important to ventilate bathrooms regularly to prevent moisture buildup and mold.
The shelter was poorly ventilated, making it stuffy and uncomfortable for the animals.
We need to ventilate the basement by installing a window or vent.

Synset Examples

The architect did not think about ventilating the storage space

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to ventilate or expose to the open air

Etymology

From Latin 'ventilare' (to fan, agitate), from 'ventus' (wind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00582968-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈvɛn.t̬əˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛn.tɪ.leɪt / (BrE)
to cause fresh air to circulate through (a room, building, etc.), especially to replace stale or impure air
to circulate air through; freshen

Examples

We need to ventilate the kitchen after frying fish last night.
Please ventilate the room by opening the windows wide for a few minutes.
Hospitals are designed to ventilate every ward frequently to ensure good air quality.

Synset Examples

The gust of air ventilated the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to let fresh air enter a room or building

Etymology

From Latin ventilare (to fan, agitate, toss in the wind), from ventus (wind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02046562-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈvɛntɪˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛntɪleɪt / (BrE)
to allow fresh air to circulate through something, especially to prevent spoilage or remove stale air
let fresh air circulate (to prevent spoilage)

Examples

Grain bins must be regularly ventilated to keep the harvest fresh.
The cellar was poorly ventilated, leading to faster spoilage of stored vegetables.
It is important to ventilate the pantry to prevent mold growth.

Synset Examples

Wheat should be well ventilated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to expose (something) to air or to fresh air

Etymology

From Latin ventilare (to fan, agitate, winnow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02116828-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈvɛn.t̬əl.eɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛn.tɪ.leɪt / (BrE)
to expose to fresh air in order to cool or freshen
expose to fresh air to cool or freshen

Examples

It is a good idea to ventilate the basement by opening windows when the weather permits.
After painting the kitchen, they made sure to ventilate the room to remove the odor.
She regularly ventilates her closet by hanging the doors open for a few hours.

Synset Examples

air out the smoke-filled rooms

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to expose something to the air, especially to freshen it

Etymology

From Latin ventilare, meaning 'to fan, agitate, or expose to air'.

5. VERB

/ ˈvɛn.t̬əˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈvɛn.tɪ.leɪt / (BrE)
to express or give voice to (an opinion, feeling, or complaint) openly or publicly
express openly or give voice to

Examples

She felt it was important to ventilate her grievances at the meeting.
During the radio show, callers are invited to ventilate their opinions on current events.
He finally ventilated his frustrations after months of silence.

Synset Examples

She vented her anger
The graduates gave vent to cheers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to make known publicly or express openly
  • Synonym: express - to voice or put into words (a feeling or idea)

Etymology

From Latin ventilatus, past participle of ventilare (to fan, winnow, or agitate).
