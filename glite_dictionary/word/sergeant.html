sergeant

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɝːdʒənt / (AmE), / ˈsɑːdʒənt / (BrE)
(historical) An English barrister of the highest rank, entitled to plead in the Court of Common Pleas.
senior English barrister (historical)
legal archaic british

Examples

In the 18th century, a sergeant wore a distinctive coif as a symbol of his status in court.
The title of sergeant was considered a prestigious honor among English barristers.
He was appointed sergeant before eventually rising to the position of judge.

Etymology

From Old French 'sergent' (servant, officer) and Latin 'serviens' (servant).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɑrdʒənt / (AmE), / ˈsɑːdʒənt / (BrE)
a noncommissioned officer in the armed forces, ranking above a corporal and below a lieutenant
military rank above corporal

Examples

He was promoted to sergeant after serving five years in the army.
The sergeant called the squad to attention during the morning drill.
As a sergeant, she was responsible for training new recruits.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: corporal - a noncommissioned officer rank below sergeant
  • Degree Higher: lieutenant - a commissioned officer rank above sergeant

Etymology

From Old French sergent (servant, officer), from Latin serviens (servant).

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɝdʒənt / (AmE), / ˈsɑːdʒənt / (BrE)
a police officer or military officer ranked above a corporal and below a lieutenant, often responsible for supervising others
police or military rank above corporal

Examples

The sergeant instructed his team to secure the perimeter.
After years on the force, she was promoted to sergeant.
The sergeant reviewed the evidence with the detectives before the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: corporal - a rank below sergeant in the police or military
  • Degree Higher: lieutenant - a rank above sergeant in the police or military

Etymology

From Old French 'sergent', from Latin 'serviens', present participle of 'servire' (to serve).
