to decoct

1. VERB

/ dɪˈkɑkt / (AmE), / dɪˈkɒkt / (BrE)
to boil a substance until most of its liquid has evaporated, often to extract its essence or make it more concentrated
boil to reduce liquid; concentrate by boiling

Examples

The chef will decoct the broth to intensify its flavor before serving.
Herbalists often decoct roots to extract beneficial compounds.
After simmering for hours, he finally decocted the mixture to the desired strength.

Synset Examples

The sauce should reduce to one cup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reduce - to decrease the amount of liquid in a sauce by boiling

Etymology

From Latin decoquere, from de- (down) + coquere (to cook).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈkɑkt / (AmE), / dɪˈkɒkt / (BrE)
to extract the essence or active ingredients of something by boiling it in water; to steep in hot water
extract essence by boiling

Examples

The herbalist would decoct the roots to make a medicinal tea.
To extract the flavor, you must decoct the leaves in hot water for an hour.
She carefully decocted the bark to produce a concentrated remedy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infuse - to steep in liquid to extract flavor or healing properties
  • Synonym: boil - to heat a liquid until it bubbles and turns to vapor

Etymology

From Latin decoquere, meaning 'to boil down,' from de- (down) + coquere (to cook).

4. VERB

/ dɪˈkɑkt / (AmE), / dɪˈkɒkt / (BrE)
to extract the essence or active ingredients from something, especially a plant, by boiling it
extract by boiling

Examples

To make herbal medicine, you need to decoct the roots for at least an hour.
Some traditional teas are made by decocting the leaves rather than simply steeping them.
After he decocted the bark, a rich, dark liquid remained.

Synset Examples

To make tea, decoct the leaves in boiling water.
She learned how to decoct the roots for medicinal purposes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infuse - to extract flavors by soaking without boiling

Etymology

From Latin 'decoquere', meaning 'to boil down'.
