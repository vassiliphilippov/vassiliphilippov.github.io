abrasion

1. NOUN

/ əˈbreɪʒən / (AmE), / əˈbreɪʒən / (BrE)
the process of wearing away or scraping by friction, especially on a surface
erosion or scraping by friction

Examples

The constant movement of sand caused significant abrasion to the rocks over time.
Frequent cleaning can lead to the abrasion of delicate surfaces.
The geologist explained how wind contributes to the abrasion of exposed cliffs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erosion - the gradual destruction of something by natural forces such as water, wind, or friction

Etymology

From Latin 'abradere', meaning 'to scrape off'.

2. NOUN

/ əˈbreɪʒən / (AmE), / əˈbreɪʒən / (BrE)
an area of skin that has been scraped or worn away, often by rubbing or friction
a scraped or worn area on the skin

Examples

She fell off her bike and got a small abrasion on her knee.
The doctor cleaned the abrasion before applying a bandage.
After hiking through the bushes, he noticed a painful abrasion on his arm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: laceration - a deep cut or tear in the skin
  • Antonym: bruise - an injury appearing as an area of discolored skin

Etymology

From Latin 'abradere', meaning 'to scrape off'.

3. NOUN

/ əˈbreɪʒən / (AmE), / əˈbreɪʒən / (BrE)
the process by which rock or other surfaces are gradually worn down by friction, especially from water, wind, or ice
wearing down by friction (geology)

Examples

The sharp edges of the rocks were smoothed by abrasion from centuries of river flow.
Glacial abrasion occurs when rocks embedded in ice scrape against the valley floor.
Wind-driven sand can cause significant abrasion to exposed rock surfaces in the desert.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erosion - the gradual destruction or removal of surface material by natural forces

Etymology

From Latin 'abrasio', from 'abradere' meaning 'to scrape off'.
