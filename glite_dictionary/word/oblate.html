oblate

1. ADJ

/ ˈoʊˌbleɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒb.leɪt / (BrE)
(of a spheroid or object) having the equatorial diameter greater than the polar diameter; flattened at the poles.
flattened at the poles (of spheroids)

Examples

The Earth is not a perfect sphere; it is actually oblate due to its rotation.
Some planets are more oblate than others because they spin faster.
The satellite's data confirmed that the planet had an oblate shape.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prolate - having the polar diameter greater than the equatorial diameter; elongated at the poles

Etymology

From Latin 'oblatus', past participle of 'offerre' (to offer), in reference to being pressed or flattened.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɑbleɪt / (AmE), / ˈɒbleɪt / (BrE)
A lay person who is formally dedicated to religious life or religious work, often affiliated with a monastery or religious community.
lay person dedicated to religious life

Examples

As an oblate, she participated in the daily prayers and work of the monastery.
The monastery welcomed several new oblates who wished to dedicate their lives to service.
He became an oblate to strengthen his spiritual practice without taking full monastic vows.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monk - a man who is a member of a religious community, especially one living under vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience

Etymology

From Medieval Latin oblatus (one offered), from Latin offerre (to offer).
