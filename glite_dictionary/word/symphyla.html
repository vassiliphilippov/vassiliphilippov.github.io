Symphyla

1. NOUN

/ sɪmˈfaɪlə / (AmE), / sɪmˈfɪlə / (BrE)
a small class of minute arthropods resembling centipedes, found in soil and leaf litter; includes the garden centipede
minute soil-dwelling arthropods (class Symphyla)
medical

Examples

Symphyla are often mistaken for centipedes but belong to a separate class of arthropods.
Gardeners sometimes encounter Symphyla when digging in moist soil.
Despite their small size, Symphyla can damage plant roots in greenhouses.

Etymology

From New Latin 'Symphyla', from Greek 'syn-' (together) + 'phylon' (tribe), referring to their fused leg segments.

2. NOUN

/ sɪmˈfaɪlə / (AmE), / sɪmˈfɪlə / (BrE)
a small class of minute, soil-dwelling arthropods, closely related to centipedes and sometimes called garden centipedes
a class of tiny, soil-dwelling arthropods

Examples

The soil in my greenhouse was infested with Symphyla, damaging the plant roots.
Researchers discovered that Symphyla populations thrive in moist, organic-rich soils.
The garden centipede, a member of the Symphyla, is often mistaken for true centipedes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: centipede - a long, slender arthropod with many legs

Etymology

From New Latin, combining Greek 'symphyēs' (grown together) and '-la' (used in taxonomic names).
