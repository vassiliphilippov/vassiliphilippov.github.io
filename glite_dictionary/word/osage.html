Osage

1. NOUN

/ ˈoʊseɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈəʊseɪdʒ / (BrE)
A member of a Native American people originally living in Missouri and adjacent regions, especially known for their historical presence near the Missouri and Osage rivers.
member of a Native American people

Examples

The Osage were relocated to what is now Oklahoma after signing treaties with the U.S. government.
She is proud to be a descendant of the Osage and preserves many of their traditions.
The discovery of oil on Osage land brought both wealth and hardship to the community.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Sioux - a group of Native American peoples of the central North American plains

Etymology

From French Osage, an adaptation of the people's self-designation 'Wazhazhe'.

2. PROPN

/ ˈoʊseɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈəʊseɪdʒ / (BrE)
A river in central Missouri, United States, that flows into the Missouri River.
river in Missouri, USA

Examples

The Osage River winds through central Missouri before joining the Missouri River.
Flooding along the Osage has affected several nearby towns in recent years.
Many anglers visit the Osage each year for its renowned fishing spots.

Etymology

Named after the Osage Nation, an Indigenous people of the region.

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊseɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈəʊseɪdʒ / (BrE)
the Siouan language of the Osage people, spoken primarily in Oklahoma.
language of the Osage people

Examples

She is studying the Osage language as part of her university course.
Efforts are being made to revitalize Osage among younger members of the tribe.
The linguist published a comprehensive dictionary of Osage.

Etymology

From Osage (the indigenous people), ultimately from the French adaptation of the Osage self-designation Wažáže.
