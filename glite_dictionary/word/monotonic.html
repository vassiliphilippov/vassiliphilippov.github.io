monotonic

1. ADJ

/ ˌmɑnəˈtɑnɪk / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəˈtɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of a sound or voice) having an unvarying pitch or tone; lacking in variation of tone or inflection.
having an unvarying pitch or tone

Examples

The professor's monotonic voice made it difficult for the students to stay focused during the lecture.
Her singing was criticized as monotonic, lacking any emotional highs or lows.
The automated announcement was delivered in a monotonic tone, making it hard to distinguish important details.

Synset Examples

the owl's faint monotonous hooting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monotonous - lacking in variety and interest; sounding the same throughout

Etymology

From Greek 'monotonos' meaning 'of one tone'.

2. ADJ

/ ˌmɑː.nəˈtɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / ˌmɒ.nəˈtɒ.nɪk / (BrE)
(of a sequence or function) Consistently increasing and never decreasing, or consistently decreasing and never increasing, in value.
consistently increasing or decreasing (math)

Examples

The function is monotonic over the interval, never decreasing at any point.
Sorting algorithms may rely on input data being monotonic for optimization.
The company's revenue showed a monotonic increase throughout the year.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monotone - having a single, unvarying tone or pitch
  • Antonym: non-monotonic - not consistently increasing or decreasing

Etymology

From Greek monotonos, from monos (single) + tonos (tone).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01551387-a
Synset POS: a
