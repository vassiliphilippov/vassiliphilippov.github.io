billfish

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɪlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbɪl.fɪʃ / (BrE)
a predatory fish with a long, slender jaw and hard scales, native to North America
predatory fish with long jaw and hard scales

Examples

Fishermen often compete to catch the largest billfish during summer tournaments.
The long jaws and needlelike teeth of the billfish make it a formidable predator.
Researchers studied the migration patterns of North American billfishes for conservation purposes.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɪl.fɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbɪl.fɪʃ / (BrE)
A long, slender predatory fish found in coastal waters, noted for its elongated jaws and surface-dwelling habits.
long-jawed predatory coastal fish

Examples

The fisherman caught a large billfish near the rocky shore this morning.
Billfish are known for leaping out of the water when hooked.
Marine biologists study billfish to learn about their migratory patterns.

Etymology

From 'bill' (referring to the bird-like beak) + 'fish'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɪlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbɪlˌfɪʃ / (BrE)
A large, slender fish with a long, pointed beak, found in temperate and tropical seas, including marlins, sailfish, and spearfish.
large fish with a long, beak-like snout

Examples

Fishing enthusiasts often travel to the Gulf to catch billfish such as marlin and sailfish.
The elongated snout of a billfish helps it to slash through schools of smaller fish.
Many restaurants serve grilled billfish as a delicacy during the summer months.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marlins - large billfishes with an elongated upper jaw
  • Synonym: sailfish - a type of billfish with a sail-like dorsal fin
  • Synonym: swordfish - a large billfish with a long, flat bill

Etymology

From 'bill' (referring to the beak-like jaw) + 'fish'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈbɪlˌfɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbɪl.fɪʃ / (BrE)
a large, warm-water fish with an elongated, rounded, and toothless upper jaw, often sought after as a game fish
large warm-water game fish with a long upper jaw

Examples

Anglers travel around the world to catch billfish like marlin and swordfish for sport.
The elongated upper jaw is a distinctive feature of every billfish species.
Many conservation groups have called for limits on billfish fishing to protect their populations.

Etymology

From 'bill' (the beak or beak-like jaw of a fish) + 'fish'.
