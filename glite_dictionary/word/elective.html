elective

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈlɛktɪv / (AmE), / ɪˈlɛktɪv / (BrE)
a course at a school or university that students can choose to take, rather than one that is required
optional course in education

Examples

She decided to take a photography elective this semester.
Many students choose science electives to broaden their knowledge.
The university offers a wide range of electives outside the core curriculum.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: required course - a course that students must take as part of a curriculum
  • Synonym: option - something that may be chosen; a choice

Etymology

From Latin 'electivus' (that may be chosen).

WordNet ID: oewn-00892488-n

2. ADJ

/ ɪˈlɛktɪv / (AmE), / ɪˈlɛktɪv / (BrE)
(of a course, subject, or procedure) chosen by the person rather than being required; optional.
optional; not required

Examples

She decided to enroll in an elective art class to explore her creativity.
The surgery was elective, so the patient could schedule it at her convenience.
Many universities require a certain number of elective credits for graduation.

Synset Examples

elective surgery
an elective course of study

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mandatory - required or compulsory

Etymology

From Latin elective, from eligere (to choose).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00851098-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ɪˈlɛktɪv / (AmE), / ɪˈlɛktɪv / (BrE)
(of a position or post) involving or filled by the process of election; subject to popular vote.
chosen or fillable by election

Examples

He ran for an elective office in the local government.
The mayor is the most important elective official in the city.
Some positions are elective, while others are appointed by higher authorities.

Synset Examples

elective official

Related Terms

  • Antonym: appointive - (of a post) filled by appointment rather than election

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *electivus*, from Latin *electus* (chosen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00849591-a
Synset POS: a
