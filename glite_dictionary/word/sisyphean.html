Sisyphean

1. ADJ

/ sɪˈsɪfiən / (AmE), / sɪˈsɪfiən / (BrE)
(of a task) requiring endless effort, often with no hope of completion or success.
endless and futile (like Sisyphus's task)

Examples

Completing the paperwork became a Sisyphean task for the overworked staff.
They viewed negotiations with the stubborn committee as a Sisyphean effort.
Maintaining order in the chaotic classroom felt like a Sisyphean challenge.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: futile - incapable of producing any useful result; pointless
  • Synonym: hopeless - having no expectation of success
  • Antonym: productive - yielding results, benefits, or profits

Etymology

From Sisyphus, a figure in Greek mythology condemned to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity.

2. ADJ

/ sɪˈsɪfi.ən / (AmE), / sɪˈsɪfi.ən / (BrE)
(of a task) involving endless effort with no chance of success; extremely laborious and ultimately futile.
endlessly laborious and futile

Examples

Trying to keep the old car running has become a Sisyphean task for him.
The researchers abandoned their Sisyphean attempts to reconcile the contradictory data.
Cleaning up after toddlers often feels like a Sisyphean chore.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: futile - incapable of producing any useful result; pointless
  • Synonym: fruitless - failing to achieve the desired results; unproductive

Etymology

From Sisyphus, a figure in Greek mythology who was condemned to roll a boulder uphill forever.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00841407-s
Synset POS: s
