to logroll

1. VERB

/ ˈlɑːɡˌroʊl / (AmE), / ˈlɒɡˌrəʊl / (BrE)
(of legislators) to support each other's proposals or attempts to gain advantage by exchanging political favors, especially by trading votes.
exchange political favors, trade votes

Examples

The two senators agreed to logroll in order to secure enough support for their respective bills.
Critics argue that politicians who logroll often put personal gain ahead of the public interest.
By logrolling, the committee members managed to push several controversial measures through the legislature.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vote-trading - the act of exchanging votes to achieve mutual political benefit
  • Synonym: pork-barrel - government spending or favors intended to please constituents

Etymology

From the U.S. frontier practice of neighbors joining together to roll logs for building; later applied metaphorically in 19th-century American politics.
