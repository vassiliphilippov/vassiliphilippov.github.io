to prolong

1. VERB

/ pɹəˈlɔŋ / (AmE), / pɹəˈlɒŋ / (BrE)
to make something last longer or extend its duration or length
to make longer in time or space

Examples

The medication can prolong the patient's life by several years.
Delays in the project may prolong the completion date.
He tried to prolong the conversation because he didn't want her to leave.

Synset Examples

We sustained the diplomatic negotiations as long as possible
prolong the treatment of the patient
keep up the good work

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to shorten - to make something shorter in duration or length
  • Synonym: to extend - to make something longer in duration or space

Etymology

From Latin 'prolongare' (to extend in time), from 'pro-' (forward) + 'longus' (long).

2. VERB

/ prəˈlɔŋ / (AmE), / prəˈlɒŋ / (BrE)
to make something last longer; to extend the duration of something
extend the duration of

Examples

The new medication can prolong the patient's life by several years.
We decided to prolong our vacation and stay for an extra week.
Tension between the parties only served to prolong the negotiations.

Synset Examples

We prolonged our stay
She extended her visit by another day
The meeting was drawn out until midnight

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to extend - to make something longer or last longer
  • Antonym: to shorten - to make or become shorter or briefer

Etymology

From Latin 'prolongare', from 'pro-' (forth) + 'longus' (long).
