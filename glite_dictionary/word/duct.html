duct

1. NOUN

/ dʌkt / (AmE), / dʌkt / (BrE)
a tube or enclosed channel that carries a liquid, gas, or other substance from one place to another
enclosed channel for fluid

Examples

The air conditioning system uses a large duct to distribute cool air throughout the building.
A blocked duct in the liver can cause serious health problems.
Technicians inspected the ventilation duct for any signs of leaks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: channel - a passage for conveying fluids
  • Synonym: pipe - a tube through which fluids flow

Etymology

From Latin ductus (leading, a conduit), from ducere (to lead).

2. NOUN

/ dʌkt / (AmE), / dʌkt / (BrE)
a bodily passage or tube lined with epithelial cells that carries secretions or other substances
bodily passage carrying secretions
medical

Examples

A blocked duct can cause swelling and pain in the affected area.
The salivary duct helps transport saliva from the gland to the mouth.
Surgeons repaired the damaged bile duct during the operation.

Synset Examples

the tear duct was obstructed
the alimentary canal
poison is released through a channel in the snake's fangs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canal - a tubular passage or channel in the body
  • Synonym: vessel - a tube in the body for conveying fluids

Etymology

From Latin ductus (leading, a conduit), from ducere (to lead).

3. NOUN

/ dʌkt / (AmE), / dʌkt / (BrE)
A tubular structure in plants or animals, formed by a series of elongated cells without intervening end walls, allowing the passage of fluids or air.
tubular structure in plants or animals

Examples

The resin moves through the duct to be secreted outside the pine tree.
Water travels along specialized ducts in certain plants.
In anatomy, a duct may serve to transport digestive enzymes between organs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canal - an artificial or natural tubular passage in the body
  • Synonym: vessel - a tube in which a body fluid circulates

Etymology

From Latin ductus (a leading, conduct, tube), from ducere (to lead).
