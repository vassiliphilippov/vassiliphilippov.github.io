to second-guess

1. VERB

/ ˌsɛkəndˈɡɛs / (AmE), / ˌsɛkəndˈɡɛs / (BrE)
to try to anticipate or predict what will happen or what someone else will do or think, often with the implication of questioning someone's decisions
try to anticipate or predict

Examples

It's impossible to second-guess how the market will react to this news.
She tried to second-guess her boss's preferences before the meeting.
Instead of acting confidently, he kept second-guessing every decision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to predict - to say that something will happen in the future
  • Synonym: to foresee - to be aware of beforehand; to anticipate

Etymology

From the phrase 'second guess', originally American English (early 20th century), meaning to judge with hindsight.

2. VERB

/ ˌsɛkəndˈɡɛs / (AmE), / ˌsɛkəndˈɡɛs / (BrE)
to criticize, question, or doubt a decision or action after it has been made, especially with the benefit of hindsight
to question or criticize a decision after the fact

Examples

She tends to second-guess every decision she makes at work.
After hearing new evidence, the jury began to second-guess their earlier verdict.
It's impossible to second-guess how the market will react to the news.

Synset Examples

Don't second-guess your decision; trust your instincts.
It's easy to second-guess someone else's actions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubt - to feel uncertain about something
  • Antonym: trust - to have confidence in someone or something

Etymology

From 'second' (to follow or come after) and 'guess,' originally meaning to predict after the fact.
