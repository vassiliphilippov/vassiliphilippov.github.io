bleak

1. ADJ

/ blik / (AmE), / bliːk / (BrE)
(of a place) exposed and empty, offering little or no shelter or sustenance; bare and inhospitable.
bare and inhospitable (of a place)

Examples

The wind swept across the bleak moor with nothing to break its force.
They set up camp in a bleak stretch of desert far from civilization.
From the summit, he looked out over a bleak and empty landscape.

Synset Examples

bare rocky hills
barren lands
the bleak treeless regions of the high Andes
the desolate surface of the moon
a stark landscape

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barren - (of land) too poor to produce much or any vegetation
  • Antonym: fertile - (of land) producing many plants or crops

Etymology

From Old Norse 'bleikr' meaning 'pale, shining'.

2. ADJ

/ blik / (AmE), / bliːk / (BrE)
not hopeful or encouraging; offering little or no prospect of positive outcome
offering little hope; not encouraging

Examples

The news about the company's future was particularly bleak after the recent downturn.
Her bleak outlook on life made it hard for her to stay motivated.
They faced a bleak winter with no electricity and little food.

Synset Examples

the future looked black
prospects were bleak
Life in the Aran Islands has always been bleak and difficult
took a dim view of things

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hopeless - having or offering no hope
  • Antonym: promising - optimistically giving hope of success or improvement

Etymology

From Old Norse 'bleikr' (pale, pallid).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ blik / (AmE), / bliːk / (BrE)
(of weather or a place) cold and damp in an unpleasant or depressing way.
unpleasantly cold and damp

Examples

The bleak wind whipped across the open moor, chilling everyone to the bone.
They trudged through the bleak landscape, shivering in the cold drizzle.
After days of bleak weather, the sun finally broke through the heavy clouds.

Synset Examples

bleak winds of the North Atlantic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chilly - uncomfortably or unpleasantly cold
  • Synonym: dreary - dull, bleak, and lifeless; depressing

Etymology

From Old Norse bleikr (pale, shining, bleak), related to Swedish blek (pale), Old English blāc (pale, wan).

Synset POS: s
