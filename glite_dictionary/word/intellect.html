intellect

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.təˌlɛkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.tə.lɛkt / (BrE)
a person who has a highly developed ability to think and reason, especially creatively or analytically
a highly intelligent or creative person

Examples

She is a leading intellect in the field of neuroscience.
The conference attracted many great intellects from around the world.
His reputation as a brilliant intellect was well earned.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intellectual - a person possessing a highly developed intellect

Etymology

From Latin intellectus (understanding, perception), past participle of intelligere (to understand).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.təˌlɛkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.tə.lekt / (BrE)
the ability to think, reason, and understand things, especially at a high level; mental capacity
mental capacity, reasoning ability

Examples

His sharp intellect made him a formidable opponent in debates.
She admired the professor's brilliant intellect and wide-ranging curiosity.
Reading challenging books can help develop your intellect over time.

Synset Examples

he reads to improve his mind
he has a keen intellect

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mind - the element of a person that enables them to think, feel, and reason

Etymology

From Latin 'intellectus' (understanding), from 'intellegere' (to understand).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪntəˌlɛkt / (AmE), / ˈɪntɪlɛkt / (BrE)
the faculty of reasoning and understanding objectively, especially with regard to abstract or academic matters
faculty of reasoning and understanding

Examples

Her keen intellect made her an excellent scientist.
Children develop their intellect through play and learning.
He admired his professor's remarkable intellect and depth of knowledge.

Synset Examples

we are told that man is endowed with reason and capable of distinguishing good from evil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intelligence - the ability to acquire and apply knowledge and skills
  • Synonym: wit - mental sharpness and inventiveness
  • Antonym: stupidity - lack of intelligence or common sense

Etymology

From Latin intellectus (understanding), from intelligere (to understand).
