contempt

1. NOUN

/ kənˈtɛmpt / (AmE), / kənˈtɛmpt / (BrE)
A willful disobedience to or lack of respect for the authority or orders of a court or legislative body, often punishable by law.
disobedience or disrespect towards court authority
legal

Examples

The judge warned that further interruptions could result in charges of contempt of court.
He was held in contempt after refusing to answer the lawyer's questions.
Acts of contempt during a trial can lead to serious consequences.

Etymology

From Latin 'contemptus' meaning 'scorn, disdain'.

2. NOUN

/ kənˈtɛmpt / (AmE), / kənˈtɛmpt / (BrE)
a feeling that someone or something is worthless or deserving of scorn and disrespect
strong lack of respect or scorn

Examples

The politician's actions were met with widespread contempt from the public.
She could not hide her contempt for those who cheated on the exam.
His sarcastic tone revealed a deep sense of contempt for authority.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disdain - the feeling that someone or something is unworthy of one's respect
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something

Etymology

3. NOUN

/ kənˈtɛmpt / (AmE), / kənˈtɛmpt / (BrE)
Open disrespect or strong feeling of disapproval for someone or something considered unworthy.
open disrespect or disapproval

Examples

She looked at the broken vase with contempt, blaming her clumsy roommate.
His voice was filled with contempt when he spoke about cheating.
Many critics expressed their contempt for the film's lack of originality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disdain - feeling that someone or something is unworthy of consideration or respect
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin 'contemptus' (scorn, disdain), from 'contemnere' (to despise).
