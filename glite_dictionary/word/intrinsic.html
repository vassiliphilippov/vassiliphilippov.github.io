intrinsic

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtrɪnsɪk / (AmE), / ɪnˈtrɪn.zɪk / (BrE)
belonging naturally; essential to the basic nature of a person or thing
essential or inherent quality

Examples

The painting's value is not just monetary; it has intrinsic worth to the family.
Curiosity is an intrinsic quality of successful scientists.
The risks are intrinsic to the profession and cannot be entirely eliminated.

Synset Examples

form was treated as something intrinsic, as the very essence of the thing

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extrinsic - not part of the essential nature; coming from outside
  • Synonym: inherent - existing in something as a permanent, essential, or characteristic attribute

Etymology

From Late Latin *intrinsecus* (interior), meaning 'inward'.

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtrɪnsɪk / (AmE), / ɪnˈtrɪnsɪk / (BrE)
(of a muscle, nerve, etc.) situated within or belonging exclusively to the organ or body part on which it acts.
situated within the organ or part concerned (esp. in anatomy)
medical

Examples

The intrinsic muscles of the hand are responsible for its fine movements.
Damage to the intrinsic nerves can lead to loss of function in that organ.
She learned about the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic muscles in her anatomy class.

Synset Examples

intrinsic muscles

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extrinsic - (of a muscle, nerve, etc.) originating outside the organ or part on which it acts

Etymology

From Latin intrinsecus (inward, internal).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00952582-s
Synset POS: s
