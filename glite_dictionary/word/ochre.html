ochre

1. ADJ

/ ˈoʊ.kɚ / (AmE), / ˈəʊ.kə / (BrE)
of a moderate orange-yellow color, resembling the natural earth pigment ochre.
moderate orange-yellow color

Examples

The artist chose an ochre paint to give the walls a warm, earthy tone.
She wore a scarf of deep ochre that complemented her autumn coat.
Golden sunlight filled the room, casting everything in a soft ochre glow.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: yellow - a color resembling that of an egg yolk or ripe lemon
  • Degree Higher: orange - a color between red and yellow in the spectrum

Etymology

From Greek 'ōkhra' (pale yellow), via Latin and French.

2. NOUN

/ ˈoʊkər / (AmE), / ˈəʊkə / (BrE)
a moderate yellow-orange to orange color, resembling the natural earth pigment of the same name
moderate yellow-orange color

Examples

The artist chose ochre for the walls to create a warm, earthy atmosphere.
During sunset, the sky turned a beautiful shade of ochre.
She wore a dress in ochre that perfectly matched the autumn leaves.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: yellow - the color between green and orange in the spectrum, evoked by light with a dominant wavelength of roughly 570–590 nm
  • Degree Higher: orange - a color between red and yellow in the spectrum, resembling the orange fruit

Etymology

From Greek 'ōkhra' (pale yellow), via Latin 'ochra'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊkər / (AmE), / ˈəʊkə / (BrE)
a natural earth pigment containing silica, alumina, and ferric oxide, used for coloring and painting
natural earth pigment used in art

Examples

The artist mixed ochre with oil to paint the landscape.
Ancient cave paintings often feature vivid ochre hues.
She used a blend of ochre and red tones to achieve the desired effect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pigment - a substance used for coloring

Etymology

From Greek *ōkhra* (pale yellow), via Latin *ochra*.
