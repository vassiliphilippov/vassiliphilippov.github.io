inducement

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdusmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːsmənt / (BrE)
the act of causing something to happen or bring about a particular result
the act of causing something to happen

Examples

The inducement of sleep in patients was achieved with a mild sedative.
Through music and breathing exercises, the inducement of relaxation became easier.
Successful inducement of change requires both planning and motivation.

Synset Examples

inducement of sleep

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cause - a person or thing that brings about an effect or result
  • Antonym: prevention - the act of stopping something from happening

Etymology

From Latin inducere (to lead in), via Old French enducement.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdusmənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈdjuːsmənt / (BrE)
something that persuades or encourages someone to do something; a motive or incentive.
something that motivates or encourages

Examples

The company offered a generous bonus as an inducement for employees to meet their targets.
Tax breaks often serve as an inducement to attract investors to a region.
Without some kind of inducement, people may not be willing to participate in the study.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incentive - something that encourages a person to do something
  • Antonym: deterrent - something that discourages someone from doing something

Etymology

From Latin inducere, inductum 'to lead in'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09202673-n
Synset POS: n
