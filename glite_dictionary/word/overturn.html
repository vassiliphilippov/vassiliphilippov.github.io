overturn

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈtɜrn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtɜːn / (BrE)
to turn something from an upright or normal position so that it falls or is upside down
to turn upside down or tip over

Examples

The strong wind caused the chair to overturn on the porch.
If you don't secure the box, it might overturn during transit.
The boat overturned when the waves became too rough.

Synset Examples

The big vase overturned
The canoe tumped over

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to tip over - to fall or cause to fall over by turning

Etymology

From Middle English overturnen, from over- + turnen (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01913390-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈtɜrn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtɜːn / (BrE)
to officially reverse or cancel a decision, law, or judgment, especially by a higher authority
officially reverse a decision or law

Examples

The Supreme Court overturned the lower court's decision after a lengthy appeal process.
A new administration might overturn the controversial policy implemented last year.
His legal team hopes to overturn the conviction based on new evidence.

Synset Examples

He revoked the ban on smoking
lift an embargo
vacate a death sentence

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to uphold - to support or confirm a previous decision or judgment

Etymology

From Middle English 'overturnen', from over- + turnen (to turn)

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌtɜrn / (AmE), / ˈəʊvətɜːn / (BrE)
an act of causing something to turn over or be upset; a state of being overturned
the act of turning something over

Examples

The truck's sudden overturn blocked traffic for several hours.
After the boat's overturn, everyone was accounted for and rescued safely.
Investigators examined the cause of the sled's overturn on the icy hill.

Synset Examples

he was badly bruised by the upset of his sled at a high speed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: upset - an act of overturning or upsetting

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'overturn', from Middle English, from over- + turn.

6. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈtɜrn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtɜːn / (BrE)
to officially reverse or reject a previous decision, especially by a higher authority or court
formally reverse a decision

Examples

The Supreme Court overturned the lower court's ruling after a lengthy legal battle.
The appeal was successful, and the decision was overturned by the appellate judge.
If new evidence emerges, a higher authority might overturn the previous verdict.

Synset Examples

The Republicans were overruled when the House voted on the bill

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to uphold - to confirm or support a decision, typically by a higher authority
  • Synonym: reverse - to change a decision to its opposite, especially in a legal context

Etymology

From Middle English 'ovyrturnen', partly calqued from Anglo-French 'overtorner'.

7. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈtɜrn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈtɜːn / (BrE)
to change or alter something completely so that it is very different from before
change radically or completely

Examples

The new evidence could overturn everything we thought we knew about the case.
Innovative technologies have the power to overturn traditional business models.
A sudden policy change may completely overturn established social norms.

Synset Examples

E-mail revolutionized communication in academe

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to revolutionize - to change something so much that it is almost completely different, especially in a positive way
  • Synonym: to transform - to change something completely, especially in a way that improves it

Etymology

From Old English oferturnian, from ofer (over) + turnian (to turn). Related to Old Norse 'ofr' (over) and Latin 'tornare' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
