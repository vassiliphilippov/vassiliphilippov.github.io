to impose

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ɪmˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to officially order that a rule, tax, fine, or punishment must be paid or obeyed, and to collect it
to order and collect (tax, fine etc.)

Examples

The government decided to impose a new tax on luxury goods starting next year.
The university can impose fines on students who damage library property.
Local authorities have the power to impose penalties on businesses that violate health regulations.

Synset Examples

levy a fine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: levy - to impose and collect (a tax, fee, fine, etc.)

Etymology

From Latin imponere, from in- (in, upon) + ponere (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02311442-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ɪmˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to force someone to accept or put up with something, especially by using authority or pressure; to compel a certain behavior or obligation
force or compel (behavior, obligation)

Examples

The new manager tried to impose stricter rules on the staff.
Parents sometimes impose their own values on their children.
The government cannot impose change without public support.

Synset Examples

Social relations impose courtesy
duty constrains one to act often contrary to one's desires or inclinations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compel - force or oblige someone to do something
  • Synonym: to enforce - make people obey something, such as a rule or law
  • Antonym: to allow - permit, not prevent or compel

Etymology

From Latin imponere (to place upon), from in- (upon) + ponere (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02566249-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪmˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ɪmˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to force something unwelcome or unpleasant on someone or something
force something unpleasant on others

Examples

The teacher decided to impose a strict new rule after the incident.
I don’t want to impose my beliefs on anyone else.
These taxes impose an unfair burden on small businesses.

Synset Examples

The principal visited his rage on the students

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to inflict - to cause something unpleasant to be suffered by someone or something

Etymology

From Latin 'imponere', from 'in-' (into) + 'ponere' (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00750288-v
Synset POS: v
