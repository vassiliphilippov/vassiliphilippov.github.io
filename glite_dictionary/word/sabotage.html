sabotage

1. NOUN

/ ˈsæbəˌtɑdʒ / (AmE), / ˈsæbətɑːʒ / (BrE)
the deliberate destruction, damage, or obstruction of something, especially for political or military advantage
deliberate destruction or obstruction

Examples

The police suspected that the fire was an act of sabotage against the factory.
They increased security after several incidents of sabotage had disrupted production.
Evidence of sabotage was found in the damaged communication cables.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vandalism - willful or malicious destruction of property

Etymology

From French sabotage, from saboter 'to botch, sabotage', originally 'to walk noisily' (from sabot 'wooden shoe').

2. VERB

/ ˈsæbəˌtɑdʒ / (AmE), / ˈsæbətɑːʒ / (BrE)
to deliberately destroy, damage, or obstruct something, especially for political or military advantage
deliberately destroy or hinder

Examples

The employees threatened to sabotage the machinery if their demands were not met.
Someone tried to sabotage the experiment by tampering with the data.
During the strike, some workers sabotaged factory equipment to stop production.

Synset Examples

The Resistance sabotaged railroad operations during the war

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wreck - to deliberately destroy or severely damage something
  • Antonym: to protect - to keep safe from harm or injury

Etymology

From French 'sabotage', from 'saboter' (to sabotage, to botch), originally literally 'to walk noisily', from 'sabot' (wooden shoe).
