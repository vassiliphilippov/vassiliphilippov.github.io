to terrorise

1. VERB

/ ˈtɛrəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈtɛrəˌraɪz / (BrE)
to fill someone with intense fear; to frighten greatly
frighten or intimidate intensely

Examples

The gang used threats to terrorise the local residents for months.
During the war, air raids would terrorise entire cities at night.
The dictator's regime actively sought to terrorise its opponents into silence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intimidate - to frighten or threaten someone to make them do what you want
  • Antonym: comfort - to make someone feel less afraid or worried

Etymology

From French 'terroriser', from 'terreur' (terror), ultimately from Latin 'terror'.

2. VERB

/ ˈtɛrəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈtɛrəraɪz / (BrE)
to coerce or intimidate by using violence or threats of violence
coerce or intimidate with violence or threats

Examples

The gangs terrorised the neighborhood with frequent threats and violence.
During the war, civilians were terrorised into silence by the occupying forces.
Hackers tried to terrorise the company by sending threatening emails.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intimidate - to frighten or threaten someone into doing something
  • Antonym: comfort - to soothe or make someone feel less afraid

Etymology

From French terroir (to frighten), influenced by Medieval Latin terrorizare, from Latin terror (fear).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02512010-v
Synset POS: v
