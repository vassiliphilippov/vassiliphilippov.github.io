to curdle

1. VERB

/ ˈkɝː.dl̩ / (AmE), / ˈkɜː.dl̩ / (BrE)
(of a liquid, especially milk) to separate into curds and whey, often as a result of souring or the addition of acid.
separate into curds, especially of milk

Examples

If you add lemon juice to milk, it will curdle quickly.
The hot soup caused the cream to curdle on top.
She noticed the milk had curdled and could not be used for drinking.

Synset Examples

curdled milk

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulate - to change from a fluid into a thickened mass

Etymology

From Middle English 'kyrdel', related to 'curd'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00458725-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkɝːdəl / (AmE), / ˈkɜːdəl / (BrE)
to change or cause to change from a liquid into a thickened, solid mass, especially as in the souring of milk
to thicken and form curds

Examples

The milk began to curdle after being left out overnight.
Heat can cause eggs to curdle if added too quickly to a hot mixture.
The horror story was so frightening, it made my blood curdle.

Synset Examples

his blood curdled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to solidify - to make or become hard or solid
  • Synonym: coagulate - to thicken into a mass; clot

Etymology

From Middle English 'kirdlen' (to congeal), related to 'curd'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00443802-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɝːdəl / (AmE), / ˈkɜːdl / (BrE)
(of a liquid, especially milk) to become thick and partly solid, often because it is going bad or sour.
become thick or sour (milk, liquid)

Examples

If you leave milk out in warm weather, it will curdle quickly.
The sauce began to curdle after boiling for too long.
She tasted the soup and realized that the cream had curdled.

Synset Examples

The milk curdled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coagulate - to change from a fluid into a thickened mass
  • Synonym: sour - to make or become sour
  • Synonym: spoil - (of food) to become unfit for eating

Etymology

From Middle English 'curdelen', related to 'curd' (from Old English 'crūdan', meaning to press or drive).
