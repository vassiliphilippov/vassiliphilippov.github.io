decease

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈsis / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːs / (BrE)
the act or event of dying; death, especially as a formal or legal term.
formal act of dying; death

Examples

The lawyer requested a copy of the will following his client's decease.
All property will be transferred to her heirs upon her decease.
The sudden decease of the president caused uncertainty throughout the country.

Synset Examples

her death came as a terrible shock
upon your decease the capital will pass to your grandchildren

Related Terms

  • Synonym: death - the end of life; cessation of all vital functions

Etymology

From Latin 'decedere' (to die), via Old French 'deces'.

2. VERB

/ dɪˈsis / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːs / (BrE)
(formal or archaic) to die; to pass from physical life and lose all bodily functions necessary to sustain life.
(formal/archaic) die; pass away
archaic

Examples

It was reported that the monarch had deceased peacefully in her sleep.
The title passes to the heir only after the current holder has deceased.
He deceased before any medical intervention could be attempted.

Synset Examples

She died from cancer
The children perished in the fire
The patient went peacefully
The old guy kicked the bucket at the age of 102

Related Terms

  • Synonym: die - to stop living; to cease to live
  • Synonym: to perish - to die, especially in an untimely or violent way
  • Antonym: to survive - to continue to live or exist

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French *deces*, from Latin *decessus* (departure, death), from *decēdere* (to depart, die).
