instinct

1. ADJ

/ ˈɪn.stɪŋkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.stɪŋkt / (BrE)
(followed by 'with') deeply filled or permeated with a quality or feeling; suffused.
deeply filled or suffused (with)
archaic

Examples

The room was instinct with a sense of anticipation before the ceremony began.
Her letters were instinct with warmth and genuine affection for her friends.
The novel is instinct with the author's passion for social justice.

Synset Examples

imbued with the spirit of the Reformation
words instinct with love
it is replete with misery

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imbued - deeply filled or permeated with a quality
  • Synonym: replete - filled or well-supplied with something

Etymology

From Latin 'instinctus' (impulse, inspiration), from 'instinguere' (to instigate).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.stɪŋkt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.stɪŋkt / (BrE)
an inborn pattern of behavior in animals and humans that is typically a response to specific external stimuli and is not learned
natural, inborn behavior or impulse

Examples

Many birds have a strong instinct to migrate as the seasons change.
She followed her motherly instinct and comforted the crying child.
The hunting instinct in cats remains even when they are domesticated.

Synset Examples

the spawning instinct in salmon
altruistic instincts in social animals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reflex - an automatic physical response to a specific stimulus

Etymology

From Latin 'instinctus' (impulse, instinct), from 'instinguere' (to incite or instigate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05630506-n
Synset POS: n
