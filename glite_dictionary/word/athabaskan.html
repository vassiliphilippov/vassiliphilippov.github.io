Athabaskan

1. NOUN

/ ˌæθəˈbæskən / (AmE), / ˌæθəˈbæskən / (BrE)
a group of related Native American languages spoken in western North America, especially in Alaska, northwestern Canada, and the southwestern United States.
group of Native American languages

Examples

Many indigenous people of Alaska speak languages belonging to the Athabaskan family.
Navajo is one of the best-known Athabaskan languages.
Linguists have studied the similarities among Athabaskan languages across North America.

Etymology

Name introduced by linguist Edward Sapir, derived from Lake Athabasca in Canada.

2. NOUN

/ ˌæθəˈbæskən / (AmE), / ˌaθəˈbaskən / (BrE)
A member of any of the Indigenous North American peoples who speak an Athabaskan language, primarily inhabiting subarctic regions of western Canada and central Alaska.
Indigenous group of North America

Examples

Many Athabaskan communities continue to practice traditional hunting and fishing in Alaska.
The Athabaskan peoples have a rich oral history conveyed through storytelling.
Linguists study the origins of Athabaskan languages to trace the migration patterns of these groups.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Athabaskan language - a family of related Indigenous languages spoken in North America

Etymology

From Cree 'Athabaska-w' (there are reeds here), via the name for Lake Athabasca.
