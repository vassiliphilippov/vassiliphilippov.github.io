divestiture

1. NOUN

/ daɪˈvɛstɪtʃɚ / (AmE), / daɪˈvɛstɪtʃə / (BrE)
the compulsory act of selling off or disposing of assets, especially as ordered by a court to prevent monopoly or anticompetitive practices
court-ordered sale of assets
legal

Examples

The judge ordered the divestiture of several subsidiaries to restore competitive balance in the market.
After the divestiture, the corporation no longer held its dominant position in the industry.
Antitrust laws sometimes require the divestiture of acquired companies to prevent monopolies.

Synset Examples

the court found divestiture to be necessary in preventing a monopoly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disinvestment - the process of withdrawing invested funds or assets

Etymology

From Latin 'divestire' (to undress, to strip), via French 'divestiture'.

2. NOUN

/ daɪˈvɛs.tə.tʃɚ / (AmE), / dɪˈvɛs.tɪ.tjʊə / (BrE)
the act of selling off a business segment, such as a subsidiary, division, or product line, usually by a company as part of its business strategy
selling off part of a company

Examples

The company announced a divestiture of its consumer electronics division to focus on its core businesses.
Following the divestiture, the new entity operated independently under a different management team.
Regulations sometimes require a divestiture to prevent a monopoly and ensure market competition.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acquisition - the act of one company buying another
  • Antonym: merger - the act of combining two companies

Etymology

From 'divest' (from Latin divestire, to undress or deprive) plus '-ure', indicating the process or result of an action.
