sequestration

1. NOUN

/ ˌsiːkwəˈstreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsiːkwɪˈstreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of forming a stable compound with an ion, atom, or molecule so that it is no longer available to participate in reactions
chemical formation of stable compounds that prevent reactions
medical

Examples

The sequestration of heavy metal ions prevents them from interfering with biochemical reactions.
Chelating agents assist in the sequestration of calcium ions during water softening.
Understanding sequestration is important in designing drugs that target specific metal ions in the body.

Etymology

From Latin sequestrare (to separate, set aside), via Medieval Latin sequestratio.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsiːkwəˈstreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsiːkwɪˈstreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of separating or isolating someone or something from others, especially in a legal or official context.
the act of separating or isolating

Examples

The sequestration of the jury ensured a fair and unbiased verdict.
Environmental sequestration of carbon dioxide is essential in fighting climate change.
Legal sequestration may occur when sensitive documents are removed from public access.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: isolation - the process of separating something from others
  • Synonym: seclusion - the state of being private and away from other people

Etymology

From Late Latin sequestratio (a deposit, removal).

3. NOUN

/ ˌsiːkwəˈstreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsiːkwɪˈstreɪʃən / (BrE)
the legal act of seizing and holding property belonging to someone else until a debt or demand is satisfied
legal seizure of property until debts are paid
legal

Examples

The court ordered the sequestration of the company’s assets until the debt was settled.
A judge may grant sequestration when a debtor fails to pay their creditors.
Following the lawsuit, sequestration of property was used to secure payment for damages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confiscation - the act of taking property with authority
  • Synonym: forfeiture - the loss or giving up of something as a penalty

Etymology

From Latin sequestratio, from sequestrare (to set aside).

4. NOUN

/ ˌsiːkwəˈstreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsiːkwɪˈstreɪʃən / (BrE)
a legal process or writ authorizing the seizure of property until a debt has been paid or other claims have been met
legal seizure of property by writ
legal

Examples

The court issued a sequestration to secure the defendant's assets during the proceedings.
Failure to pay the fines could result in the sequestration of property by the authorities.
The lawyer filed for a sequestration to protect the claimant's interests.

Etymology

From Late Latin sequestratio (a storing up), from sequestrare (to hand over to a trustee).
