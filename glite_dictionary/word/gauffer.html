gauffer

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɔːfər / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːfə / (BrE)
an ornamental frill or trimming produced by pressing pleats, especially in fabric or paper
decorative frill made with pleats

Examples

The bonnet was adorned with a delicate gauffer along its edge.
A lace collar with a fine gauffer was popular in Victorian fashion.
She admired the skilled craftsman’s ability to create a perfect gauffer on the gown.

Etymology

From French *gaufrer* (to impress with a figured pattern).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡɔːfər / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːfə / (BrE)
a heated tool for pressing pleats and ridges into fabric, especially used in fine textile work
tool for pressing pleats into fabric

Examples

The antique linen napkin was shaped with a gauffer to create intricate patterns.
She carefully heated the gauffer before pressing it onto the delicate ruffles.
Victorian dressmakers often relied on a gauffer to add decorative ridges to collars and cuffs.

Etymology

From French *gaufrer* (to emboss or waffle), originally referring to creating ridges on fabric or paper.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03448699-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈɡɔfər / (AmE), / ˈɡɔːfə / (BrE)
to make fabric wavy or crimped by pressing it with a heated goffering iron.
create wavy fabric with a heated iron

Examples

The milliner decided to gauffer the ribbons to create a decorative effect.
Traditional costumes often feature collars or cuffs that have been gauffered for added texture.
She learned how to gauffer lace using an old-fashioned heated iron.

Synset Examples

goffer the trim of the dress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: goffer - to crimp with a heated iron; alternate spelling of 'gauffer'

Etymology

From French *gaufrer* (to emboss, wrinkle), related to *gaufre* (waffle) due to the patterned effect.
