winder

1. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪndər / (AmE), / ˈwɪndə / (BrE)
A mechanical device used for winding a spring-driven mechanism, such as a clock or watch.
device for winding mechanisms

Examples

He lost the winder for his antique clock, making it impossible to keep the time accurate.
The watch winder keeps automatic watches running even when not worn.
Please use the winder gently to avoid overwinding the mechanism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: key - a device for winding a clock by turning a spring

Etymology

From the verb 'wind' (to twist or coil) + -er (agent noun suffix).

2. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪndər / (AmE), / ˈwɪndə / (BrE)
a mechanical device around which something can be wound, such as thread, rope, or film
device for winding thread, rope, etc.

Examples

The old clock needs a winder to keep the mainspring tight.
She used a small winder to roll up the garden hose neatly.
Film cameras often have a manual winder for advancing the film.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spool - cylindrical device on which thread or wire is wound
  • Synonym: bobbin - cylinder or spindle on which yarn or thread is wound

Etymology

From Old English 'windan' (to wind, turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04593707-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈwaɪndər / (AmE), / ˈwɪndə / (BrE)
a worker whose job is to wind mechanisms such as winches, clocks, or other devices
person who winds mechanisms as a job

Examples

The clock winder visited every Monday to keep the ancient grandfather clock running.
Years ago, a textile mill employed a winder to keep the machines going smoothly.
The cable winder carefully handled heavy ropes attached to the docked ship.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wind - to turn or twist something around

Etymology

From 'wind' (to turn or twist), with the agent suffix '-er'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
