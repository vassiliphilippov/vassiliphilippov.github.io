bombard

1. VERB

/ bɑmˈbɑrd / (AmE), / bɒmˈbɑːd / (BrE)
to attack someone or something repeatedly by throwing objects at them
attack repeatedly by throwing objects

Examples

Angry protesters began to bombard the police with stones and bottles.
During the food fight, students bombarded each other with handfuls of spaghetti.
Kids love to bombard their friends with snowballs during winter.

Synset Examples

They pelted each other with snowballs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pelt - to attack repeatedly with missiles or blows

Etymology

From French *bombarder*, related to 'bombarde' (medieval cannon), from Italian *bombarda*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01510546-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ bɑmˈbɑrd / (AmE), / bɒmˈbɑːd / (BrE)
to direct a stream of high-energy particles or radiation at a target, especially for experimental or scientific purposes
direct high-energy particles or radiation at

Examples

The scientists bombarded the sample with neutrons to study the reaction.
High-energy electrons are often bombarded onto materials to alter their properties.
Physicists bombard atoms with gamma rays to investigate their internal structures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to irradiate - to expose to radiation

Etymology

From French *bombarder*, based on *bombe* (bomb).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00292386-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bɑmˈbɑrd / (AmE), / bɒmˈbɑːd / (BrE)
to attack a place or people continuously with bombs, shells, or other missiles
attack with bombs or artillery

Examples

The enemy forces began to bombard the city at sunrise, causing widespread panic among residents.
Allied planes continued to bombard enemy positions throughout the night.
During the siege, they bombarded the fortress for several days without stopping.

Synset Examples

The Americans bombed Dresden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bomb - to attack with explosive devices
  • Synonym: shell - to fire shells at; bombard with artillery
  • Antonym: ceasefire - a temporary stoppage of fighting

Etymology

From French 'bombarder', from 'bombe' (bomb) plus '-ard'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01134328-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ bämˈbɑrd / (AmE), / bɒmˈbɑːd / (BrE)
A large shawm; the bass member of the shawm family, used as an early musical wind instrument.
large bass shawm (musical instrument)

Examples

The medieval band featured a musician playing the bombard at the center.
Music historians are fascinated by the deep, reedy sound of the bombard.
In Brittany, the traditional bombard is still used in folk music ensembles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shawm - a woodwind instrument of the Renaissance period

Etymology

From Middle French 'bombarde', referring to both a cannon and a medieval musical wind instrument.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02870607-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ bɑmˈbɑrd / (AmE), / bɒmˈbɑːd / (BrE)
to direct a continuous stream of questions, requests, or other messages at someone, often in an overwhelming way
attack with persistent requests or questions

Examples

Journalists bombarded the politician with questions as she left the building.
My inbox was bombarded with promotional emails after I signed up for the newsletter.
The teacher was bombarded with complaints after announcing the new policy.

Synset Examples

The speaker was barraged by an angry audience
The governor was bombarded with requests to grant a pardon to the convicted killer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barrage - to overwhelm with a large amount of something, especially questions or criticism
  • Degree Lower: to pester - to trouble or annoy someone with frequent or persistent requests or interruptions

Etymology

From French 'bombarder', from 'bombe' (bomb), originally a military term.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00865243-v
Synset POS: v
