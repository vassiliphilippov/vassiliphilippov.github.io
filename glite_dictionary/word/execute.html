to execute

1. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkjut / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪkjuːt / (BrE)
to kill someone deliberately, especially as an official punishment or as part of a planned crime
kill deliberately; official/criminal context

Examples

The mafia leader ordered his men to execute the informer who had betrayed them.
After being found guilty of treason, the spy was executed by firing squad.
They planned to execute the rival gang member as a warning to others.

Synset Examples

The Mafioso who collaborated with the police was executed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: murder - the unlawful and intentional killing of a person
  • Antonym: pardon - to officially forgive and not punish someone

Etymology

From Latin 'exsequi' (carry out, accomplish), via Old French 'executer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02489797-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkjut / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪˌkjuːt / (BrE)
to put (someone) to death as a punishment for a crime, especially as ordered by a legal authority
to put to death as punishment

Examples

The prisoner was executed by lethal injection after a lengthy trial.
The government decided not to execute him due to new evidence.
A growing number of countries have abolished the right to execute criminals.

Synset Examples

In some states, criminals are executed

Related Terms

  • Antonym: pardon - to officially forgive someone for a crime, waiving punishment
  • Antonym: commute - to reduce a criminal sentence, especially from death penalty to another punishment

Etymology

From Latin executus, past participle of exsequi (to follow out, enforce).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02488856-v
Synset POS: v

to carry out or put into effect a plan, order, or course of action
carry out; put into effect

Examples

The company will execute the plan next month after all preparations are complete.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: abandon - to stop carrying out a plan, order, or course of action
