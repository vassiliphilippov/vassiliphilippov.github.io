Karaism

1. NOUN

/ ˈkærəˌɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈkɑːrəˌɪzəm / (BrE)
A Jewish religious movement that rejects oral tradition as a source of divine law, accepting only the Hebrew Bible as authoritative.
Jewish movement rejecting oral tradition

Examples

The history of Karaism offers a unique perspective on the diversity of Jewish belief and practice.
Scholars have studied the influence of Karaism on medieval Jewish philosophy and literature.
Unlike Rabbinic Judaism, Karaism relies solely on the Hebrew Bible for religious law.

Synset Examples

This ephemeral appearance of Karaism on Spanish soil was fruitful for Jewish historical literature, for it induced the philosophically trained Abraham ibn Daud of Toledo to write his ‘Sefer ha-Ḳabbalah’ (1161), which is invaluable for the history of the Jews in Spain.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Rabbinic Judaism - Mainstream Jewish tradition that recognizes both written and oral law

Etymology

From Hebrew 'Qara'im', meaning 'Readers' (of the Scripture), based on the root 'qara' (to read).
