Labyrinthodontia

1. NOUN

/ ˌlæb.əˌrɪn.θoʊˈdɑn.ʃi.ə / (AmE), / ˌlæb.ər.ɪnˈθɒd.n.ti.ə / (BrE)
an extinct group of large amphibians from the Devonian to Triassic periods, characterized by a solid, flattened skull and conical teeth with a complex internal structure
extinct group of prehistoric amphibians

Examples

Scientists discovered fossils of Labyrinthodontia in rocks dating back to the Triassic period.
The complex tooth patterns of Labyrinthodontia helped paleontologists identify their remains.
Labyrinthodontia are believed to have been among the earliest vertebrates to venture onto land.

Etymology

From Greek 'labyrinthos' (labyrinth, maze) and 'odous, odont-' (tooth), referring to the maze-like structure of their teeth.

2. NOUN

/ ˌlæb.əˌrɪn.θəˈdɒn.ʃi.ə / (AmE), / ˌlæb.ɪˌrɪn.θɒˈdɒn.ʃɪ.ə / (BrE)
a superorder of extinct amphibians, typically resembling large, heavy-bodied salamanders or crocodiles, known for their solid, flattened skulls and conical teeth; lived from the Devonian through Triassic periods
superorder of extinct amphibians

Examples

Fossils of Labyrinthodontia have been discovered in rock formations from the Triassic period.
Scientists believe that Labyrinthodontia were among the top predators in ancient freshwater ecosystems.
The characteristic tooth structure of Labyrinthodontia is a key feature for identifying their fossils.

Etymology

From Greek 'labýrinthos' (labyrinth) and 'odous' (tooth), referring to the complex folded structure of their teeth.
