to predict

1. VERB

/ prɪˈdɪkt / (AmE), / prɪˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to be a sign that something will happen; to indicate a future event as suggested by signs, omens, or symptoms.
be a sign of a future event

Examples

Experts say that these darkening skies predict heavy rainfall.
Some believe that animal behavior can predict an approaching earthquake.
The sudden drop in temperature seemed to predict a coming storm.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foretell - to state or indicate what will happen in the future
  • Synonym: to portend - to be a sign or warning that something is likely to happen

Etymology

From Latin praedictus, past participle of praedicere (to foretell), from prae- (before) + dicere (to say).

2. VERB

/ prɪˈdɪkt / (AmE), / prɪˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to say that something will happen in the future, often based on knowledge or reasoning
to say what will happen in the future

Examples

Experts predict that the new policy will boost the economy over the next year.
No one could predict how the weather would change so rapidly.
She predicted that the team would win, and she was right.

Synset Examples

Call the outcome of an election

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forecast - to predict or estimate a future event, especially the weather
  • Synonym: project - to estimate or predict based on present trends
  • Degree Lower: guess - to form an opinion or answer without sufficient evidence

Etymology

From Latin praedictus, past participle of praedicere (to say beforehand), from prae- (before) + dicere (to say).
