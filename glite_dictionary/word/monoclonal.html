monoclonal

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɑːnəˈkloʊnl̩ / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəˈkləʊnəl / (BrE)
A laboratory-produced antibody derived from a single clone of cells and consisting of identical antibody molecules.
lab-produced identical antibody
medical

Examples

The new cancer treatment involves injecting a monoclonal designed to target specific cells.
Researchers developed a monoclonal that can neutralize the virus efficiently in laboratory conditions.
Doctors often use a monoclonal as a targeted therapy for autoimmune diseases.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: polyclonal - Antibodies derived from multiple cell lines, not identical

Etymology

From 'mono-' (single) + 'clonal', relating to a single cell clone.

2. ADJ

/ ˌmɑːnəˈkloʊnəl / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəˈkləʊnəl / (BrE)
(of cells or antibodies) derived from a single cell or clone, and therefore identical; produced by or relating to a single clone.
derived from a single cell or clone
medical

Examples

Monoclonal antibodies have revolutionized the treatment of certain cancers and autoimmune diseases.
The lab tested a new monoclonal antibody for its effectiveness against the virus.
Researchers produced monoclonal cell lines to study the genetic mutation in detail.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: polyclonal - derived from multiple clones

Etymology

From 'mono-' (one, single) and 'clone' (organism or cell produced asexually from one ancestor).
