scavenger

1. NOUN

/ ˈskævəndʒɚ / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒə / (BrE)
a chemical substance added to a mixture to react with and neutralize impurities or unwanted substances
chemical agent removing impurities
medical

Examples

A scavenger is added to the fuel to remove traces of lead that could harm the engine.
The manufacturer recommended using a scavenger to prevent unwanted reactions during the chemical process.
Adding a scavenger ensures that impurities in the solution do not affect the final product.

Etymology

From 'scavenge', which derives from Middle English 'scawageour' (inspector of weights and measures).

2. NOUN

/ ˈskævəndʒər / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒə / (BrE)
a person who searches through and collects items discarded by others, often for reuse or resale
person who collects discarded items

Examples

The scavenger spent hours sorting through the city's trash bins in search of reusable items.
Urban scavengers often find old electronics and sell them for parts.
After the festival, a scavenger picked up bottles and cans left behind by revelers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forager - person or animal that searches widely for food or provisions
  • Synonym: collector - person who gathers things, especially as a hobby

Etymology

From Middle English scavager, from Old French escauwage (inspection), related to 'scavenge'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈskævəndʒər / (AmE), / ˈskævɪndʒə / (BrE)
an animal that feeds on dead or decaying organic matter
animal feeding on decaying matter

Examples

Vultures are a well-known type of scavenger found in many parts of the world.
After the storm, several scavengers searched the beach for anything edible.
Hyenas act as both hunters and scavengers in the African savannah.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carrion eater - animal that eats the bodies of dead animals
  • Antonym: predator - animal that hunts and kills other animals for food

Etymology

From Middle English scavager, from Anglo-Norman *scawager ('tax or street official'), later applied to animals that 'clean up' decaying matter.
