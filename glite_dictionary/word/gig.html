gig

1. NOUN

/ ɡɪɡ / (AmE), / ɡɪɡ / (BrE)
an implement with a shaft and a barbed point, used for catching fish or frogs, especially by spearing
barbed fishing spear

Examples

The fishermen used a gig to spear catfish in the shallow water.
He carved his own gig from a piece of wood and metal.
A sharp gig is essential for hunting frogs at night.

Etymology

Perhaps from obsolete French *gigue* (a type of spear or fish-spear).

3. NOUN

/ ɡɪɡ / (AmE), / ɡɪɡ / (BrE)
a fishing implement consisting of a cluster of hooks, typically without barbs, that is dragged through a group of fish to snag them by the body when they are not biting
fishing implement with clustered hooks

Examples

The fisherman used a gig to catch fish when they refused to bite on bait.
He watched as the gig was drawn swiftly through the shallow water, snagging several fish.
A gig can be effective during spawning season when traditional lures fail.

Etymology

Perhaps of Scandinavian origin; related to Old Norse gigja (fiddle, fishing implement).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ɡɪɡ / (AmE), / ɡɪɡ / (BrE)
a light, two-wheeled horse-drawn carriage for one or two people, typically without a hood
two-wheeled horse-drawn carriage

Examples

He arrived at the estate in a smart gig drawn by a single horse.
The old painting depicted a gentleman driving a gig through the countryside.
A gig was often favored for its speed and lightness over heavier carriages.

Etymology

Perhaps from French *gigue* (a type of dance or fiddle), later applied to light vehicles in the 18th century.

long, light racing or ship's rowing boat

Examples

Each naval officer was responsible for maintaining his own gig in perfect condition.
She joined a local club and learned to race a traditional Cornish gig on weekends.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whaleboat - a long, narrow, and light boat formerly used for pursuing whales

6. NOUN

/ ɡɪɡ / (AmE), / ɡɪɡ / (BrE)
a light, narrow ship's boat, often used for the personal transportation of a ship's captain
a light boat, often for a captain's use

Examples

The captain was rowed ashore in his gig to meet the local officials.
Each morning, the crew would prepare the gig for inspection.
After the storm, they found the gig undamaged and ready for use.

Etymology

Probably from Middle French *gigue* (a light boat) or related to the same origin as the 'gig' carriage.
