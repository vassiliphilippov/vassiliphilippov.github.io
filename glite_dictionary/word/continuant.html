continuant

1. NOUN

/ kənˈtɪn.ju.ənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪn.ju.ənt / (BrE)
a consonant sound produced by constricting, but not completely closing, the vocal tract, allowing the airflow to continue; includes fricatives and approximants
a consonant produced without complete closure

Examples

In English, the continuant sounds include /f/, /v/, and /l/.
A fricative is a type of continuant consonant.
Learners often struggle to distinguish between stops and continuant sounds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stop - a consonant sound produced by completely blocking and then releasing the airflow
  • Degree Lower: fricative - a continuant consonant produced by forcing air through a narrow channel
  • Degree Higher: approximant - a continuant consonant formed by narrowing the vocal tract without friction

Etymology

From Latin 'continuans', present participle of 'continuare' (to continue).

2. ADJ

/ kənˈtɪn.ju.ənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪn.ju.ənt / (BrE)
(of a speech sound) produced by continuously forcing air through a constricted passage in the vocal tract, as in the sounds 'f', 's', 'z', or 'th'.
a speech sound made with continuous airflow

Examples

In linguistics, the sound continuant refers to consonants like 's' and 'f' where airflow is not stopped.
The English sound 'th' in 'then' is classified as a continuant.
Fricatives and approximants are types of continuant consonants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stop - a speech sound made by blocking airflow and then releasing it

Etymology

From Latin continuans, present participle of continuare (to continue).
