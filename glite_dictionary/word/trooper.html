trooper

1. NOUN

/ ˈtruːpər / (AmE), / ˈtruːpə / (BrE)
a police officer who is a member of a mounted police force, especially a state police force or cavalry unit
mounted police officer

Examples

The trooper rode his horse along the parade route to keep order.
A mounted trooper assisted with traffic at the busy intersection.
The park was patrolled by a trooper on horseback, much to the delight of the children.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: state trooper - a member of a US state police force

Etymology

From troop + -er, originally referring to a member of a troop of soldiers.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtruːpər / (AmE), / ˈtruːpə / (BrE)
a soldier belonging to a motorized or armored military unit, such as cavalry or armored vehicles.
soldier in a motorized army unit

Examples

The trooper climbed into the armored vehicle before the mission began.
Each trooper in the unit was specially trained to operate military transports.
As a trooper in the mechanized brigade, he became skilled at driving tanks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soldier - a person who serves in an army

Etymology

From troop + -er, originally referring to a member of a military troop.

a police officer who is a member of a state police force, especially in the United States

A trooper assisted the stranded motorists during the snowstorm.
After ten years as a trooper, she was promoted to sergeant.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: state police - police force responsible for statewide law enforcement

From troop + -er, originally referring to a cavalry soldier.

4. NOUN

/ ˈtruːpər / (AmE), / ˈtruːpə / (BrE)
a soldier who is a member of a cavalry unit, typically one who rides a horse
cavalry soldier on horseback

Examples

The young trooper polished his saddle before every inspection.
During the charge, each trooper kept close formation with his regiment.
In the old paintings, a trooper is often shown in elaborate cavalry uniform.

Synset Examples

a cavalryman always takes good care of his mount

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cavalryman - a soldier who fights on horseback

Etymology

From troop + -er, modeled after French 'troupier' (member of a troop).
