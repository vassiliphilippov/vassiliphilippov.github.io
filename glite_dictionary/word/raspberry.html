raspberry

1. NOUN

/ ˈræzˌbɛri / (AmE), / ˈrɑːzˌbəri / (BrE)
A small, soft, red or black edible berry made up of many tiny drupelets, often eaten raw or used in cooking and baking.
a small edible red or black berry

Examples

She picked a handful of raspberries from the garden for her breakfast.
The tart flavor of raspberry goes well with dark chocolate in desserts.
He planted both raspberry and blueberry bushes in his backyard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blackberry - an edible black or dark purple berry similar to a raspberry

Etymology

From Middle English raspis, of uncertain origin, possibly from rasp (referring to its rough surface) + berry.

2. NOUN

/ ˈræzˌbɛri / (AmE), / ˈrɑːzbəri / (BrE)
a rude sound made by blowing air through the lips, used to express displeasure or contempt
a sound of derision made with the lips

Examples

The crowd greeted the unpopular speaker with a loud raspberry.
He blew a raspberry to show what he thought of the idea.
A mischievous child gave a big raspberry to her teacher.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bronx cheer - similar derisive sound made with the lips

Etymology

From rhyming slang 'raspberry tart' for 'fart'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07138578-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈræzˌbɛri / (AmE), / ˈrɑːzbəri / (BrE)
a small, soft, red or sometimes black or yellow edible fruit that separates easily from its stem, or the bush that produces such fruits
a small edible fruit or its bush

Examples

She picked a raspberry from the bush and tasted its sweet flavor.
The tartness of the raspberry complemented the rich chocolate cake perfectly.
Rabbits had eaten all the raspberry plants in their garden by midsummer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blackberry - an edible black fruit produced by a related bramble bush
  • Synonym: strawberry - a soft, red, sweet fruit with tiny seeds on its surface

Etymology

From Middle English raspis, perhaps based on rasp (the fruit's rough surface).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-12676630-n
Synset POS: n
