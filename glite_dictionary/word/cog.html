cog

1. VERB

/ kɑɡ / (AmE), / kɒɡ / (BrE)
to join pieces of wood together using cogs or projections that fit into corresponding sockets
join wood pieces using cogs

Examples

The carpenter decided to cog the beams together for extra strength.
They cogged the boards so that the floor would be more stable.
He spent the afternoon carefully cogging each piece to ensure a tight fit.

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English cogge, referring to a tooth on a gear or projection for joining.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01296521-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ kɑg / (AmE), / kɒg / (BrE)
a tooth on the rim of a gear wheel, which interlocks with those on another wheel to transmit motion
tooth on a gear wheel

Examples

A missing cog can cause an entire machine to malfunction.
Each cog of the gear must be perfectly shaped to ensure smooth operation.
Technicians replaced the worn cog to restore the clock's movement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gear tooth - a tooth on a gear, similar meaning to cog
  • Synonym: gear - a rotating machine part having cut teeth, works with cog

Etymology

From Middle English cogge, likely from Middle Dutch kogge (tooth of a gear).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03068939-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ kɑg / (AmE), / kɒg / (BrE)
a subordinate person who performs an important but routine function in a large organization or system
minor but important subordinate

Examples

She felt like just another cog in the corporate machine, easily replaced if she left.
Each worker is a vital cog in the company’s operations, ensuring everything runs smoothly.
Although he was a small cog, his role kept the team moving forward every day.

Synset Examples

he was a small cog in a large machine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: functionary - an official who performs specific duties in an organization
  • Antonym: big cheese - an important or influential person

Etymology

From Middle English cogge, from Middle Dutch kogge (cogwheel, tooth on a gear).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
