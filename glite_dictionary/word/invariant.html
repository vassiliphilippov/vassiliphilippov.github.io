invariant

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛriənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈveəriənt / (BrE)
a quantity, property, or feature that remains unchanged under certain transformations, especially in mathematics or science
unchanging quantity or property under transformation

Examples

The total energy of the system is an invariant under this transformation.
In geometry, the distance between points is an important invariant.
Physicists search for quantities that remain invariant in different frames of reference.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: constant - a quantity that does not change
  • Antonym: variable - a quantity that can change or vary

Etymology

From Latin 'invarians', present participle of 'invariare' ('in-' not + 'variare' to vary).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈvɛɹiənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛːɹɪənt / (BrE)
unchanging or unaffected by a specified operation or transformation, especially in mathematics, science, or logic.
unchanging under transformation

Examples

The total energy of the system remains invariant under this transformation.
Certain equations describe quantities that are invariant despite changes in coordinate systems.
In programming, an invariant property helps maintain code correctness.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: constant - remaining the same; unchanging
  • Antonym: variable - likely to change; not consistent

Etymology

From Latin in- (not) + varians, variant- (varying); literally 'not varying'.

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈvɛriənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛərɪənt / (BrE)
never changing; remaining constant or unchanged under specified conditions.
unchanging; constant

Examples

The results of the experiment remained invariant despite changes in the procedure.
These laws are invariant under translation and rotation.
Her attitude has been remarkably invariant over the years.

Synset Examples

maintained a constant temperature
principles of unvarying validity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: variable - likely to change or vary; not constant

Etymology

From Latin 'invarians', present participle of 'invariare' (to make unchanging): in- (not) + variare (to change).
