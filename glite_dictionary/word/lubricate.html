to lubricate

1. VERB

/ ˈluːbrɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈluːbrɪkeɪt / (BrE)
(of a substance) to have the quality of reducing friction between surfaces; to act as a lubricant.
to act as a lubricant

Examples

This oil lubricates engine parts efficiently during operation.
Special additives are used to ensure the solution lubricates well under extreme conditions.
The cream not only cleans but also lubricates the machinery surfaces.

Synset Examples

the liquid in this can lubricates well

Etymology

From Latin lubricare (to make slippery).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02757472-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈluːbrɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈluːbrɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to make something smooth or slippery by applying a substance such as oil or grease
make smooth or slippery by applying lubricant

Examples

Please lubricate the hinges if the door starts to squeak.
It's important to lubricate bicycle chains regularly to ensure a smooth ride.
The mechanic lubricated all the moving parts before reassembling the engine.

Synset Examples

lubricate the key

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oil - to put oil on something to make it work smoothly
  • Synonym: grease - to put grease on something to make it work smoothly

Etymology

From Latin *lubricatus*, past participle of *lubricare* (to make slippery).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00219438-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈluːbrɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈluːbrɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to apply oil or another substance to reduce friction between surfaces in motion
apply a substance to reduce friction

Examples

It is important to lubricate bike chains regularly to ensure smooth operation.
The mechanic will lubricate the engine parts before reassembling them.
Be sure to lubricate the hinges to stop the door from squeaking.

Synset Examples

lubricate my car

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oil - to put oil on or in something to reduce friction

Etymology

From Latin lubricatus, past participle of lubricare (to make slippery).
