to fuddle

1. VERB

/ ˈfʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˈfʌd.əl / (BrE)
to confuse or perplex someone; to make someone unable to think clearly
confuse or bewilder

Examples

The complex instructions utterly fuddled him during the exam.
She tried to explain, but her words only fuddled the children more.
Don't let all these technical details fuddle you; the process is actually straightforward.

Synset Examples

These questions confuse even the experts
This question completely threw me
This question befuddled even the teacher

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to befuddle - to make someone unable to think clearly; to confuse
  • Synonym: to confuse - to make someone unable to think clearly or to mix up
  • Antonym: to clarify - to make something clearer or easier to understand

Etymology

Probably from Low German 'fuddeln' (to work in a careless or confused way).

2. VERB

/ ˈfʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˈfʌd.əl / (BrE)
to drink alcohol, especially to excess or so as to become confused or intoxicated
drink alcohol, especially to excess
archaic

Examples

After hours at the tavern, he was completely fuddled by ale and cheer.
Some would fuddle their worries away with a bottle of strong wine.
She warned him not to fuddle his mind before the important meeting.

Synset Examples

We were up drinking all night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drink - to consume liquid, especially alcohol

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'foderen', to make drunk, of unknown origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01173463-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈfʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˈfʌd.əl / (BrE)
to confuse or stupefy (someone), especially with alcohol; to make muddled or befuddled, often as a result of drinking.
make confused, esp. due to alcohol

Examples

Too much wine can easily fuddle your mind if you aren't careful.
Late into the evening, the brandy began to fuddle his thoughts and speech.
She tried not to let the strong cocktails fuddle her judgment during the party.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to befuddle - to confuse or stupefy, especially with alcohol
  • Antonym: sober - not affected by alcohol

Etymology

Probably from dialectal English, perhaps related to 'fuddled' (confused) and possibly influenced by 'fuss' or 'puddle.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01193274-v
Synset POS: v
