whipsaw

1. VERB

/ ˈwɪpˌsɔ / (AmE), / ˈwɪpˌsɔː / (BrE)
to cut or saw (wood) with a whipsaw, typically by two people pulling the saw alternately
to saw with a whipsaw

Examples

In the early days, lumberjacks would whipsaw logs into planks by hand.
They spent the afternoon whipsawing large tree trunks for building materials.
Traditionally, teams whipsawed timber in saw pits before the advent of powered sawmills.

Etymology

From 'whip' (referring to a motion back and forth) + 'saw'; originally used for a long, two-person saw.

2. VERB

/ ˈwɪpˌsɔ / (AmE), / ˈwɪpˌsɔː / (BrE)
to subject someone to two adverse situations or actions at once, especially to victimise or defeat alternately in gambling, negotiations, or competing pressures
to subject to two opposing adverse actions

Examples

Small investors were whipsawed by the rapid swings in the stock market.
He felt whipsawed by the aggressive tactics used in the negotiation.
The company was whipsawed between rising costs and falling revenues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to victimize - to single someone out for cruel or unjust treatment
  • Synonym: to defraud - to illegally obtain money or something else from someone by deception
  • Antonym: benefit - to receive an advantage; profit

Etymology

Originally from the literal tool 'whipsaw'; figurative sense from the saw's back-and-forth motion, likening to being pulled in opposing directions.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02580033-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪpˌsɔ / (AmE), / ˈwɪpˌsɔː / (BrE)
a long saw with handles at both ends, designed to be operated by two people
long two-person saw

Examples

The two lumberjacks worked together with a whipsaw to fell the large tree.
During the demonstration, the carpenter showed how to use a whipsaw for cutting timber.
A whipsaw requires strength and coordination from both operators to work efficiently.

Etymology

From 'whip' + 'saw', likening its back-and-forth motion to cracking a whip.
