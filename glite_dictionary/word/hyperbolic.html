hyperbolic

1. ADJ

/ haɪ.pərˈbɑ.lɪk / (AmE), / haɪ.pəˈbɒl.ɪk / (BrE)
relating to or having the form of a hyperbola, especially as used in mathematics and geometry.
relating to a hyperbola (geometry)

Examples

The mathematician explained the properties of hyperbolic functions in class.
These equations describe hyperbolic trajectories rather than circular ones.
In hyperbolic geometry, a hyperbolic plane has different rules than a flat plane.

Synset Examples

hyperbolic functions

Etymology

From Greek *hyperbolikos* (extravagant, excessive), relating to *hyperbolē* (a throwing beyond; in mathematics, the curve).

2. ADJ

/ ˌhaɪ.pərˈbɑ.lɪk / (AmE), / ˌhaɪ.pəˈbɒl.ɪk / (BrE)
(of language, style, or claims) exaggerated to an extreme degree; not meant to be taken literally.
exaggerated; overstated

Examples

The politician’s hyperbolic promises were met with skepticism by the audience.
Her hyperbolic descriptions of the storm made it sound worse than it actually was.
Critics pointed out the hyperbolic style of his writing, which often relied on exaggeration.

Synset Examples

a hyperbolic style

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exaggerated - described in a way that makes something seem better, worse, or larger than it really is
  • Antonym: understated - described in a way that makes something seem less important or serious than it really is

Etymology

From Late Latin hyperbolicus, from Greek hyperbolikos (excessive), from hyperbolē (exaggeration).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00884272-s
Synset POS: s
