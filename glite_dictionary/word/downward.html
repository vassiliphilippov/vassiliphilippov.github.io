downward

1. ADV

/ ˈdaʊnwɚd / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnwəd / (BrE)
In or toward a lower place, level, or position; from a higher to a lower point.
toward a lower level or position

Examples

The leaves drifted downward gently from the trees.
After the announcement, the company's stocks moved sharply downward.
She glanced downward at the street below from her balcony.

Synset Examples

don't fall down
rode the lift up and skied down
prices plunged downward

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upward - toward a higher place, level, or position

Etymology

From Old English 'dūnweard' (toward the hill, downward), from 'dūn' (hill, down) + '-weard' (toward).

2. ADJ

/ ˈdaʊnwərd / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnwəd / (BrE)
toward a lower place, level, or position; in a direction from a higher to a lower point.
moving or directed toward a lower place

Examples

The hikers moved downward along the steep mountain trail as the sun began to set.
A downward glance revealed just how far they had climbed.
Prices took a sharp downward turn due to economic uncertainty.

Synset Examples

he lay face downward
the downward pull of gravity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upward - moving or directed toward a higher place

Etymology

From Old English 'dūnweard' (‘downward, toward the hill’), from 'dūn' (down, hill) + '-weard' (toward).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02502659-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈdaʊnwərd / (AmE), / ˈdaʊnwəd / (BrE)
moving or extending from a higher to a lower position
moving from higher to lower

Examples

The road took a downward turn as it led into the valley.
Sales have shown a downward trend over the past year.
She felt a downward pull as the elevator descended.

Synset Examples

the down staircase
the downward course of the stream

Related Terms

  • Antonym: upward - moving or extending from a lower to a higher position

Etymology

From Old English 'dūnweard', meaning 'towards the hill' (down + -ward).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02495926-s
Synset POS: s
