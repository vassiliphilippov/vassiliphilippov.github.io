verse

1. VERB

/ vɝs / (AmE), / vɜːs / (BrE)
to compose poetry or to express something in verse form
compose poetry; put into verse

Examples

The poet decided to verse the folk tale for a modern audience.
She skillfully versed her memoir, creating an emotional narrative in rhyme.
Many writers attempt to verse their stories to capture musicality and rhythm.

Synset Examples

He versified the ancient saga

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to versify - to turn into verse; to compose in verse

Etymology

From Latin 'versus' (a line, row), past participle of 'vertere' (to turn).

2. NOUN

/ vɝs / (AmE), / vɜːs / (BrE)
a line or group of lines in poetry; a piece or section of a poem
a piece or section of a poem

Examples

She recited a verse from her favorite poem in front of the class.
The songwriter wrote a new verse to add more depth to the lyrics.
Each verse in the anthology conveyed a different emotion.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: stanza - a grouped set of lines within a poem, often separated by a blank line
  • Degree Lower: line - a single row of words in a poem
  • Degree Higher: poem - a piece of writing in verse form, often with rhythm and rhyme

Etymology

From Latin 'versus' (a line, row, or turn of the plough), from 'vertere' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06393492-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ vɝːs / (AmE), / vɜːs / (BrE)
A line of poetry or metrical text; a single line in a poem or song.
single line of poetry

Examples

The poet wrote a beautiful verse that captivated everyone in the audience.
Each verse in the song adds a new layer to its story.
She memorized a verse from her favorite poem for the recital.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: line - a unit of language into which a poem or play is divided
  • Degree Higher: stanza - a group of lines forming a unit in a poem

Etymology

From Latin 'versus' (a line, literally 'a turning'), from 'vertere' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06396351-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ vɝs / (AmE), / vɜːs / (BrE)
writing arranged with a metrical rhythm, typically having a rhyme; poetry or a part of a poem.
metrical writing; poetry

Examples

Shakespeare wrote much of his work in dramatic verse, which gives it a rhythmic quality.
The first verse of the poem introduces the main theme.
Children often enjoy learning simple verse because of its rhyme and meter.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prose - written or spoken language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure
  • Synonym: poetry - literary work in which special intensity is given to the expression of feelings and ideas by the use of distinctive style and rhythm; poems collectively, regarded as a synonym in many contexts

Etymology

From Latin 'versus' (a line, row, particularly of writing), from 'vertere' (to turn).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07107220-n
Synset POS: n
