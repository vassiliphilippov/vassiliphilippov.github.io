clerk

1. NOUN

/ klɝk / (AmE), / klɑːk / (BrE)
a person whose job is to keep records, accounts, or perform general office tasks in an organization
office worker who does clerical tasks

Examples

The company hired a new clerk to manage bookkeeping duties.
As a clerk, she was responsible for filing documents and answering phones.
He started his career as a clerk in a busy insurance office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secretary - a person employed to handle correspondence and routine office tasks
  • Antonym: manager - a person who manages an office or department

Etymology

From Old English 'clerc' (cleric, scholar), from Latin 'clericus' (clergyman). Originally, educated persons (often clerics) performed such duties.

2. VERB

/ klɜrk / (AmE), / klɑːk / (BrE)
to work as a clerk, especially in a law office or legal context
work as a clerk (legal/professional)

Examples

She clerked for a Supreme Court justice after graduating from law school.
He has been clerking at a small law firm over the summer.
Many young lawyers gain experience by clerking for federal judges.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: work - to do a job or perform duties

Etymology

From Middle English *clerk*, from Old French *clerc*, from Latin *clericus* (clergyman, scribe).

3. NOUN

/ klɝk / (AmE), / klɑːk / (BrE)
a person whose job is to assist customers and handle sales transactions in a shop or store
salesperson in a store
american

Examples

I asked the clerk at the counter for help finding my size.
The clerk scanned all the items before bagging them.
Several new clerks were hired to handle the holiday rush.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: salesperson - person whose job is selling goods in a store
  • Synonym: cashier - person who handles payments and receipts in a store

Etymology

From Old English 'clerc', from Latin 'clericus' (clergyman); in American English, the meaning shifted to 'shop assistant'.
