fox

1. VERB

/ fɑks / (AmE), / fɒks / (BrE)
(of paper or books) to become discolored with yellowish or brownish spots, typically due to mildew or age.
to become discolored with spots (mildew)

Examples

The old letters began to fox after years of being stored in the attic.
Some antique books fox over time, developing brownish spots on their pages.
Humidity caused several documents in the archive to fox noticeably.

2. NOUN

/ fɑks / (AmE), / fɒks / (BrE)
a person who is shifty, clever, and deceptive
shifty or deceptive person

Examples

No one trusted the new manager, as he was known to be a fox in business dealings.
She played the part of a fox, always scheming to get ahead at others' expense.
His reputation as a fox made colleagues wary of his proposals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wolf - a fierce or cruel person (metaphorical use)
  • Antonym: simpleton - a person lacking in subtlety and cleverness

Etymology

Metaphorical use based on the animal, known for its cunning in folklore, from Old English 'fox'.

5. NOUN

/ fɑks / (AmE), / fɒks / (BrE)
a small, carnivorous mammal with a pointed muzzle, upright ears, and a bushy tail, noted for its cleverness and solitary hunting habits
small carnivorous mammal with bushy tail

Examples

A red fox darted into the undergrowth as we approached.
Many farmers consider the fox a cunning and troublesome animal.
The fox uses its keen sense of hearing to locate prey beneath the snow.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: wolf - large wild carnivorous mammal related to the dog, living and hunting in packs
  • Synonym: dog - domesticated carnivorous mammal related to wolves and foxes

Etymology

Old English *fox*, related to Dutch *vos* and German *Fuchs*.
