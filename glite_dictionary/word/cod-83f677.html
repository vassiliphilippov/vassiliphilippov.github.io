cod

1. NOUN

/ kɑd / (AmE), / kɒd / (BrE)
a large marine fish with a spotted body, found in cold or temperate waters, valued as a food source and for its white, flaky flesh
a large edible marine fish

Examples

Fried cod is a popular dish in many coastal towns.
She bought fresh cod at the fish market for dinner.
The fisherman was proud of the huge cod he caught early that morning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: haddock - another edible fish similar to cod

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'codd(e)' meaning 'bag', referring to the fish's appearance.

2. NOUN

/ kɑd / (AmE), / kɒd / (BrE)
the lean white flesh of a large North Atlantic fish, eaten as food
lean white flesh of a food fish

Examples

She ordered grilled cod with a side of vegetables at the restaurant.
The recipe calls for fresh cod fillets, lemon, and herbs.
Baked cod is a healthy dinner option rich in protein.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: haddock - lean white flesh of another North Atlantic food fish
  • Antonym: salmon - flesh of another commonly eaten fish, usually pink

Etymology

From Middle English *codde*, possibly from Old Norse *koddi* (cushion, testicle), referring to the fish's appearance.

3. VERB

/ kɑd / (AmE), / kɒd / (BrE)
to trick or deceive (someone) as a joke; to hoax
deceive or hoax (someone), esp. as a joke
british

Examples

They tried to cod me into believing the shop was haunted.
Are you codding me, or is there really a party tonight?
She realized she'd been codded only after the prank was revealed.

Synset Examples

The immigrant was duped because he trusted everyone
You can't fool me!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fool - to trick or deceive (someone)
  • Synonym: hoax - to deceive or trick, usually for a joke
  • Synonym: dupe - to deceive or trick (someone)

Etymology

Originates in British slang, possibly a shortening of 'codger' (an old man; sometimes a trickster); precise origin is uncertain.
