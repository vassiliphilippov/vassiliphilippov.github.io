to transubstantiate

1. VERB

/ ˌtræn.səbˈstæn.ʃi.eɪt / (AmE), / ˌtræn.səbˈstæn.ʃieɪt / (BrE)
(in Christian theology) to change the bread and wine of the Eucharist into the body and blood of Christ.
change bread and wine into body and blood (theology)

Examples

Catholics believe that the priest can transubstantiate the bread and wine during Mass.
The doctrine claims that the elements transubstantiate into the real presence of Christ.
Many theologians have debated how the bread and wine actually transubstantiate in the sacrament.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to transform - make a thorough or dramatic change in form or appearance
  • Synonym: convert - change (something) into a different form or property

Etymology

From Medieval Latin transubstantiāre, from Latin trans- (across) + substantia (substance).

2. VERB

/ ˌtræn.səbˈstæn.ʃi.eɪt / (AmE), / ˌtræn.sʌbˈstæn.ʃɪ.eɪt / (BrE)
to change the form or substance of something, especially to transform into a different kind or nature
change the form or substance of

Examples

The alchemist aimed to transubstantiate base metals into gold through mysterious processes.
Some philosophers believe that profound experiences can transubstantiate one's outlook on life.
According to the doctrine, the bread and wine transubstantiate into the body and blood of Christ during the ceremony.

Synset Examples

This experience transformed her completely
She transformed the clay into a beautiful sculpture
transubstantiate one element into another

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to transform - to change the form, appearance, or character of something
  • Synonym: to metamorphose - to change completely in form or nature

Etymology

From Medieval Latin transubstantiāre (to change from one substance to another), from Latin trans- (across) and substantia (substance).
