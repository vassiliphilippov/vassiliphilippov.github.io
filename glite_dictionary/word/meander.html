meander

1. NOUN

/ miˈændɚ / (AmE), / miˈændə / (BrE)
a winding or indirect path, especially one taken in a leisurely or aimless way
a winding or leisurely path or walk

Examples

Our meander through the old town lasted all afternoon.
They enjoyed a gentle meander along the riverbank, stopping whenever they pleased.
The path takes a meander through the woods, curving this way and that.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to wander - to move about aimlessly; to roam
  • Synonym: amble - to walk at a slow, relaxed pace
  • Antonym: march - to walk with a regular, measured tread, often with purpose

Etymology

From Greek 'maiandros', after the winding river Maiandros (Meander) in Asia Minor.

2. VERB

/ miˈændɚ / (AmE), / miˈændə / (BrE)
to move in a winding or indirect course, especially over a long distance
move in a winding course

Examples

The river meanders gently through the valley, creating scenic bends along its banks.
Tourists meandered through the narrow streets, discovering hidden shops and cafés along the way.
We spent the afternoon meandering around the botanical gardens, enjoying every unexpected turn.

Synset Examples

the river winds through the hills
the path meanders through the vineyards
sometimes, the gout wanders through the entire body

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wind - to follow a curving or twisting path
  • Synonym: to wander - to move aimlessly or without a fixed course

Etymology

From Greek 'maiandros', the name of a winding river (the Maeander).

3. NOUN

/ miˈændɚ / (AmE), / miˈɑːndə / (BrE)
a winding bend or curve, especially in a river or stream
bend or curve in a river or stream

Examples

The river formed several broad meanders as it wound through the valley.
Over centuries, the meanders of the stream created fertile floodplains.
We followed the meander of the creek while hiking through the forest.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bend - a curve or angle in something, especially a river or road

Etymology

From Greek 'maiandros,' referring to the winding Maeander river in Asia Minor.
