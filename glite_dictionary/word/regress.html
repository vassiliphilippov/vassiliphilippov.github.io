regress

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
a return to a previous or less developed state or condition
a return to an earlier state

Examples

After making progress in therapy, she feared a sudden regress into old habits.
The patient's regress was evident after the medication was discontinued.
Some historians worry about a cultural regress during times of crisis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: regression - the act or result of returning to a previous state

Etymology

From Latin 'regressus', from 'regredi' meaning 'to go back'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00236075-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
A process of reasoning in which one assumes the conclusion and works backward to discover the premises or evidence that support it, often leading to an infinite sequence of prior causes or explanations.
reasoning backward from a conclusion

Examples

In philosophical debates, an infinite regress can undermine an argument's validity.
The theory leads to a regress, where every proof requires another underlying proof.
They criticized the logic for resulting in a vicious regress that could never be resolved.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: progress - the process of moving forward toward a conclusion, often the opposite reasoning direction

Etymology

From Latin 'regressus', past participle of 'regredi' (to go back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05791773-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
to return to a worse or less developed state or condition; to deteriorate or revert to an earlier stage
return to a worse or earlier state

Examples

Without proper treatment, the patient's condition may regress to a more serious stage.
If you stop practicing, your skills can regress over time.
After initial improvement, the child's behavior began to regress again.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deteriorate - to become worse in quality or value
  • Antonym: progress - to move forward or improve; the opposite of regress

Etymology

From Latin regressus (a return), from regredi (to go back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00250199-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
to return to an earlier or less developed state or condition
return to an earlier state

Examples

If therapy is interrupted, patients might regress to earlier patterns of behavior.
Sometimes, under stress, children regress and start behaving as they did at a younger age.
In economic downturns, companies may regress to less innovative practices.

Synset Examples

We reverted to the old rules

Related Terms

  • Antonym: progress - move forward or develop to a more advanced state

Etymology

From Latin regressus (a return), from regredi (to go back), from re- (back) + gradi (to step, go).

5. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
to return to a less desirable or former state, especially by resuming bad or undesirable behavior after improvement
to relapse into bad behavior or a former state

Examples

After months of good behavior, he began to regress to his old, troublesome habits.
Without support, some patients may regress to negative coping strategies during stressful periods.
The child seemed to regress in his behavior after the family's move to a new city.

Synset Examples

Those who recidivate are often minor criminals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relapse - to fall back into a former state, especially after improvement
  • Antonym: progress - to move forward or improve over time

Etymology

From Latin 'regressus', past participle of 'regredi' (to go back).

Source: WordNet

6. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛs / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛs / (BrE)
(in statistics) To return to an average value or mean, or to estimate the relationship between variables using regression analysis.
(statistics) return to a mean value

Examples

In large populations, individual data points often regress toward the group mean over time.
When outliers are eliminated, results tend to regress to a statistical average.
In analyzing variables, we may regress one variable on another to understand their relationship.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: regression - the statistical process of estimating relationships among variables
  • Antonym: progress - to move forward or advance, the antonym in general sense

Etymology

From Latin 'regressus', past participle of 'regredi' (to go back). Statistical use developed from the term 'regression to the mean'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00492392-v
Synset POS: v
