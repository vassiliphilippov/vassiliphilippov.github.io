to detain

1. VERB

/ dɪˈteɪn / (AmE), / dɪˈteɪn / (BrE)
to delay or hold back someone or something, often making them late or preventing them from proceeding
to delay or hold back

Examples

I'm sorry I'm late; I was detained by a last-minute phone call.
The storm detained several flights at the airport.
She was detained at work and missed the beginning of the concert.

Synset Examples

Traffic was delayed by the bad weather
she delayed the work that she didn't want to perform

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delay - to make someone or something late or postpone progress

Etymology

From Latin 'detinere', from 'de-' (away) + 'tenere' (to hold).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈteɪn / (AmE), / dɪˈteɪn / (BrE)
to keep someone in official custody, especially in order to question them about a possible crime or to prevent their departure
keep in official custody

Examples

The police decided to detain him for further questioning after the incident.
Travelers can be detained at the border if their documents are not in order.
She was detained by customs officials due to a problem with her visa.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: release - to set free from confinement or custody
  • Synonym: to imprison - to put or keep in prison or confinement

Etymology

From Old French *detenir*, from Latin *detinere* (to hold off, keep back).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02500687-v
Synset POS: v
