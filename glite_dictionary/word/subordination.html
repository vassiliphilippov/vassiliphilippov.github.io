subordination

1. NOUN

/ səˌbɔrdnˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the grammatical relationship in which a word or phrase (the subordinate) depends on another word or phrase (the head), often forming a subordinate clause or modifier.
grammatical relationship of dependence

Examples

Relative clauses illustrate subordination in complex sentences.
In grammar, subordination helps clarify relationships between ideas in a sentence.
Understanding subordination is important for writing more nuanced and varied sentences.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: coordination - the connection of two or more elements of equal syntactic importance

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinatio, from sub- (under) + ordinatio (arrangement).

2. NOUN

/ səbˌɔrdnˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / səbˌɔːdɪˈneɪʃn / (BrE)
the act or process of placing someone or something in a lower or subordinate position; making subject to the authority or control of another
the act of making someone or something subordinate

Examples

The manager's subordination of staff needs to company policy caused resentment.
Colonial history often involved the subordination of native cultures.
Attempts at subordination were met with resistance from the workers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: domination - the exercise of power or control over others
  • Antonym: autonomy - the state of being self-governing or independent

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinatio, from Latin subordinare 'to place in order, make subordinate.'

3. NOUN

/ səbˌɔːrdəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the state of being subordinate to someone or something; a lower or secondary rank, position, or importance.
the state of being subordinate

Examples

The company's strict hierarchy led to the subordination of individual creativity.
Cultural traditions often enforced the subordination of women in public life.
His subordination to authority figures was evident throughout his career.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: submission - the act of yielding to a superior force or authority
  • Antonym: dominance - the state of having power or influence over others

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinatio (the act of placing in a lower order), from Latin subordinare (to subordinate).

the state of being subordinate to something else or occupying a lower rank or position; a condition in which someone or something is under the authority or control of another

Examples

In some cultures, there's a clear subordination of women to men, especially in leadership roles.
The military is known for its strict subordination of lower ranks to higher authority.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superiority - the state of being higher in rank or quality
  • Synonym: inferiority - the state of being lower in status or quality

Etymology

From Late Latin subordinatio (placement in a lower order); from sub- (under) + ordinatio (arrangement).

5. NOUN

/ səˌbɔrdəˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the quality or state of being obedient or submissive to authority or someone higher in rank
obedient submissiveness to authority

Examples

The army demands absolute subordination from its soldiers at all times.
She resented her constant subordination to her manager's arbitrary decisions.
Cultural norms in the village encouraged the subordination of individual wishes to the needs of the group.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obedience - compliance with an order, request, or law; submission to authority
  • Antonym: dominance - power and influence over others; the opposite of submission
  • Antonym: insubordination - defiance of authority; refusal to obey orders

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinatio, from Late Latin subordinare (to set below).
