germ

1. NOUN

/ dʒɝm / (AmE), / dʒɜːm / (BrE)
a very small living thing, especially one that can cause disease
microorganism that may cause disease

Examples

It's important to wash your hands regularly to get rid of germs.
Many parents worry about their children catching germs at school.
Doctors recommend covering your mouth when you sneeze to prevent the spread of germs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bacterium - microscopic, single-celled organism
  • Synonym: virus - infectious agent that can cause disease

Etymology

From Latin 'germen', meaning 'sprout' or 'bud'.

2. NOUN

/ dʒɝm / (AmE), / dʒɜːm / (BrE)
an initial idea or element that inspires or leads to later development; the origin of something
origin or initial inspiration for later work

Examples

The germ of his novel came from a story he heard as a child.
She had only a germ of an idea when she started, but it grew into a major project.
That conversation planted the germ for what would become their new business.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seed - the beginning or cause of a development or idea
  • Synonym: root - an origin or source of something

Etymology

From Latin 'germen' (sprout, bud, offshoot).

3. NOUN

/ dʒɝm / (AmE), / dʒɜːm / (BrE)
a minute structure, such as a fertilized egg, from which new tissue can develop into a complete organism
a basic structure from which an organism develops

Examples

The scientist observed the early development of the germ under a microscope.
Each germ has the potential to grow into a new organism under the right conditions.
In biology, the germ is crucial for understanding embryonic development.

Etymology

From Latin germen (sprout, bud).
