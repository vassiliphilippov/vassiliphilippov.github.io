accusative

1. NOUN

/ əˈkjuːzətɪv / (AmE), / əˈkjuːzətɪv / (BrE)
the grammatical case used for a noun or pronoun that serves as the direct object of a verb
grammatical case for direct objects

Examples

In Latin, the accusative case marks the direct object of an action verb.
English does not typically mark the accusative case except in some pronouns, like 'him' or 'her'.
Students of German must learn the endings used for nouns in the accusative case.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominative - the grammatical case for the subject of a verb

Etymology

From Latin 'accusativus', meaning 'accusative (case)'.

2. ADJ

/ əˈkjuːzətɪv / (AmE), / əˈkjuːzətɪv / (BrE)
(in grammar) relating to or denoting a grammatical case used to indicate the direct object of a verb or the object of certain prepositions.
relating to the grammatical object case

Examples

In Latin, the accusative case is used for direct objects of verbs.
The teacher explained how to identify accusative endings in German nouns.
Some languages, like Finnish, have an accusative distinct from other grammatical cases.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominative - grammatical case marking the subject of a verb
  • Degree Higher: dative - grammatical case marking the indirect object

Etymology

3. ADJ

/ əˈkjuːzətɪv / (AmE), / əˈkjuːzətɪv / (BrE)
expressing or implying accusation; indicating that someone is blamed or suspected of wrongdoing
expressing accusation or blame

Examples

Her accusative tone made him feel immediately guilty.
He pointed an accusative finger at his colleague during the heated meeting.
The detective noted the suspect's accusative glance at the victim.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

Etymology
