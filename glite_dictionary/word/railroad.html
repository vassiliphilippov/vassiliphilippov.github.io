railroad

1. VERB

/ ˈreɪlˌrɑd / (AmE), / ˈreɪlˌrɔːd / (BrE)
to supply (an area or region) with railroad lines
provide with railroad lines

Examples

The government invested heavily to railroad the western region during the nineteenth century.
Many small towns grew rapidly after engineers railroaded the valley.
They plan to railroad the new territory within the next decade.

Synset Examples

railroad the West

Etymology

Derived from the noun 'railroad', meaning a railway system.

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈreɪlˌroʊd / (AmE), / ˈreɪl.rəʊd / (BrE)
a line of track consisting of rails on which trains run
a track for trains
american

Examples

The children loved to watch the trains pass by on the railroad behind their house.
Workers repaired the damaged railroad after the storm.
The town grew quickly when the railroad was constructed nearby.

Synset Examples

he walked along the railroad track

Related Terms

  • Synonym: railway - a track for trains, chiefly British term

Etymology

From rail + road, coined in the early 19th century to describe the new transport system with rails for vehicle movement.

3. VERB

/ ˈreɪlˌrɑd / (AmE), / ˈreɪlˌrɔːd / (BrE)
to transport people or goods by railroad train
transport by train

Examples

The company used to railroad coal from the mines to the city in the 1920s.
Fresh produce is railroaded across the country to reach local markets.
During the gold rush, workers often railroaded supplies to remote camps.

Etymology

From rail + road, referring to the tracks and the route; verb use from the noun.

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

to force someone to do something by using pressure, threats, or unfair methods
force or pressure someone into action

Examples

He felt they tried to railroad him into signing the agreement without proper review.
The committee attempted to railroad the proposal through without discussion.
She refused to let them railroad her into making a quick decision.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to coerce - to force someone to do something by using threats or pressure
  • Synonym: to pressurize - to apply pressure in order to make someone do something
  • Degree Lower: to persuade - to cause someone to do something by reasoning or arguing

Etymology

Metaphorically from the notion of driving something forward forcefully and rapidly, like a train on rails.

to force or pressure someone into doing something or making a decision quickly, often without giving them enough time to consider it properly
to pressure into quick decision or action

Examples

Don't let them railroad you into making a hasty choice.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to coerce - to force someone to do something by using pressure
  • Synonym: bully - to pressure or intimidate someone into doing something
  • Antonym: to encourage - to give someone support or confidence to do something

Etymology

Figurative use from the rapid, unstoppable motion of trains on a railroad; derives from the noun 'railroad.'

7. VERB

/ ˈreɪlˌroʊd / (AmE), / ˈreɪl.rəʊd / (BrE)
to force something to happen or be approved in a quick or unfair way, often without proper discussion or consideration
force through quickly or unfairly

Examples

The board tried to railroad the new policy despite objections from several members.
She accused management of attempting to railroad the changes without proper debate.
Legislators warned that the government could not railroad controversial measures through parliament.

Synset Examples

The company tried to railroad the new policy without consultation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bulldoze - to push something through forcefully or ruthlessly
  • Antonym: block - to prevent progress or approval, opposite to forcing through
  • Antonym: hinder - to make it difficult for something to happen, opposite on the spectrum

Etymology

From US English, originally referring to the construction or operation of railroads, later extended metaphorically to mean forcing something through with the speed and directness of a train.
