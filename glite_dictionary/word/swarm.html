swarm

1. NOUN

/ swɔrm / (AmE), / swɔːm / (BrE)
a large group of insects or other small things moving together in the air or on the ground
large group of moving insects or things

Examples

A swarm of bees gathered around the hive.
We watched a swarm of locusts descend upon the field.
A swarm of tourists filled the small market square.

Synset Examples

a swarm of insects obscured the light
clouds of blossoms
it discharged a cloud of spores

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flock - a group of birds, sheep, or other animals kept or moving together
  • Synonym: throng - a large, densely packed crowd of people or animals
  • Antonym: solitary - being alone or without companions

Etymology

From Old English 'swarma' (mass of bees),

2. VERB

/ swɔrm / (AmE), / swɔːm / (BrE)
(of people, animals, or insects) to move somewhere in large numbers, often in a busy or active way.
move in large numbers

Examples

Fans began to swarm into the stadium as soon as the gates opened.
Bees swarm out of their hive when disturbed.
Reporters swarmed around the celebrity, eager for a statement.

Synset Examples

people were pouring out of the theater
beggars pullulated in the plaza

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to teem - to be full of or swarming with
  • Antonym: scatter - to move or spread in different directions over a wide area

Etymology

Old English swearm (mass of buzzing insects), related to German Schwarm.

3. NOUN

/ swɔrm / (AmE), / swɔːm / (BrE)
a large group of people or things moving together in a busy or uncontrolled way
large moving crowd

Examples

A swarm of tourists filled the museum lobby in the morning.
Security struggled to control the swarm rushing toward the stage after the concert.
A swarm of reporters gathered outside the courthouse waiting for a statement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - a large number of people gathered together

Etymology

From Old English 'swearm' (swarm/hive of bees), related to Proto-Germanic *swurmaz.
