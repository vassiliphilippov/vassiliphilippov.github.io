tuck

1. VERB

/ tʌk / (AmE), / tʌk / (BrE)
to push or fold something carefully into a particular place, especially so that it fits snugly or is neatly hidden
to fit something snugly by folding or pushing in

Examples

Please tuck your shirt into your pants before the meeting.
She tucked the stray hair behind her ear as she spoke.
He quickly tucked the letter into his pocket when someone walked in.

Synset Examples

insert your ticket into the slot
tuck your shirttail in

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else

Etymology

From Middle English tuken (to pull up, draw fabric together), origin uncertain.

2. NOUN

/ tʌk / (AmE), / tʌk / (BrE)
a narrow, flat fold sewn into fabric, typically for decoration or to reduce fullness
a sewn fold in fabric

Examples

The dress was decorated with delicate tucks along the hem.
She pressed each tuck carefully to make the blouse look neat.
Adding a tuck at the waist can adjust the fit of a garment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pleat - a fold in fabric that is stitched in place

Etymology

Probably from Old English *tucian* (to punish, torment), later influenced by the sense of 'pull' or 'draw together'.

7. VERB

/ tʌk / (AmE), / tʌk / (BrE)
to draw together or fold (something, especially fabric) so that it is held in place or forms a small fold or pucker

Examples

The dress was tucked at the waist to create a more flattering shape.
He tucked the napkin into his shirt collar before eating.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pleat - to fold fabric into even, narrow folds

Etymology

Probably from Middle English tuken (to pull up sharply), possibly of Germanic origin.
