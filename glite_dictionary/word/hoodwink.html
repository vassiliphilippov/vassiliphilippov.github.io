to hoodwink

1. VERB

/ ˈhʊdˌwɪŋk / (AmE), / ˈhʊdˌwɪŋk / (BrE)
to deceive or trick someone, especially by pretending to have good intentions
to deceive or trick

Examples

The company tried to hoodwink customers with misleading advertisements.
He managed to hoodwink the security guard by pretending to be an employee.
Politicians sometimes hoodwink the public with false promises.

Synset Examples

He bamboozled his professors into thinking that he knew the subject well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive or cheat someone
  • Antonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge or understanding

Etymology

From 'hood' (a covering for the head) + archaic 'wink' (to close the eyes), originally meaning to blindfold.

2. VERB

/ ˈhʊdˌwɪŋk / (AmE), / ˈhʊdˌwɪŋk / (BrE)
to deceive or trick someone, especially by slyness or cunning
deceive or trick by cunning

Examples

The company tried to hoodwink customers with false advertisements.
Despite his efforts to hoodwink the authorities, his plan was uncovered.
She felt embarrassed after realizing she had been hoodwinked by a clever scam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive someone, usually for personal gain or amusement
  • Synonym: swindle - to cheat someone out of money or possessions by deceit
  • Antonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge or understanding

Etymology

From hood (a covering for the head) + wink; originally meant 'to cover the eyes' (as with a hood) so as to prevent someone from seeing.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
