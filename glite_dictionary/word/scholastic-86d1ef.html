scholastic

1. ADJ

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlæstɪk / (BrE)
relating to schools or education; academic
relating to schools or education

Examples

The school board discussed changes to the scholastic calendar during their meeting.
Her scholastic achievements earned her a prestigious scholarship.
The university organizes several scholastic competitions for local schools each year.

Synset Examples

scholastic year

Related Terms

  • Synonym: academic - relating to education or scholarly activities

Etymology

From Medieval Latin scholasticus (of a school), from Latin schola (school).

2. NOUN

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlastɪk / (BrE)
A person who places excessive importance on formal rules and academic learning, often at the expense of practical understanding.
someone overly focused on academic rules

Examples

The university is full of scholastics who value abstract theories above practical solutions.
As a true scholastic, he corrected everyone’s grammar constantly, even in casual conversations.
Some critics argue that scholastics are out of touch with the real world.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pedant - a person excessively concerned with minor academic details or rules
  • Antonym: pragmatist - a person oriented toward practical affairs and results

Etymology

From Latin 'scholasticus' (of a school), from Greek 'skholastikos'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10431101-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlæstɪk / (BrE)
relating to scholasticism, especially the philosophical and theological system developed by medieval European scholars
of or relating to scholasticism

Examples

Many medieval universities were strongly influenced by scholastic methods of inquiry.
The debate centered on traditionally scholastic questions about faith and reason.
She specializes in the study of scholastic philosophy during the Middle Ages.

Synset Examples

scholastic philosophy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scholasticism - the philosophical and theological system of medieval European schools

Etymology

From Medieval Latin scholasticus (of a school), from Greek scholastikos (devoting one's leisure to learning).
