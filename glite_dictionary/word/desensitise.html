to desensitise

1. VERB

/ diːˈsɛnsɪtaɪz / (AmE), / diːˈsɛnsɪtaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something less sensitive to physical, emotional, or other forms of stimulation
make less sensitive
british

Examples

Repeated exposure to violence can desensitise people to its impact.
The doctor used cream to desensitise the patient's skin before the injection.
Watching horror films may desensitise viewers over time.

Synset Examples

The war desensitized many soldiers
The photographic plate was desensitized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to desensitize - American spelling of desensitise
  • Antonym: to sensitise - to make sensitive or more aware
  • Synonym: numb - to deprive of feeling or responsiveness

Etymology

From de- (expressing removal) + sensitise, from French sensibiliser (to make sensitive).

2. VERB

/ diˈsɛnsəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / diˈsɛnsɪtaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something less sensitive or less responsive to physical or emotional stimuli; to make insensitive
make less sensitive or responsive
british

Examples

Years of exposure to violence in the media can desensitise people to real-world suffering.
Repeated use of the same medication can desensitise the body's response to it.
Therapists sometimes desensitise patients to their fears through gradual exposure.

Synset Examples

His military training desensitized him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to desensitize - to make less sensitive or responsive (American spelling)
  • Antonym: to sensitise - to make sensitive or more responsive

Etymology

From 'de-' (expressing removal) + 'sensitise' (from 'sensitive' and '-ise').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00273482-v
Synset POS: v
