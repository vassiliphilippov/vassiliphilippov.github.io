to ingrain

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡreɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪn / (BrE)
to firmly and thoroughly work (something) into the surface or substance of something else, especially so that it becomes difficult to remove
to work into deeply and firmly

Examples

Years of working outdoors had ingrained dirt into the lines of his hands.
The dye was ingrained so deeply in the fabric that it never faded.
Those habits were ingrained in him from childhood.

Synset Examples

His hands were grained with dirt

Etymology

From in- (into) + grain (from Old French graine, meaning 'seed'), originally referring to dye worked into the fibers of cloth.

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡreɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪn / (BrE)
to firmly fix or establish (an idea, habit, or attitude) in a person
to fix an idea or habit firmly

Examples

It is important to ingrain good study habits in children from an early age.
The coach worked hard to ingrain discipline and teamwork in his players.
Years of training had ingrained the safety procedures into her routine.

Synset Examples

Mother tried to ingrain respect for our elders in us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to instill - to gradually but firmly establish an idea or attitude in a person's mind
  • Synonym: implant - to fix an idea or attitude firmly in someone's mind

Etymology

From obsolete adjective 'ingrain' (dyed with grain), from Middle French engrainer.

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡreɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡreɪn / (BrE)
to establish a habit, idea, or behavior so firmly that it becomes part of a person's character or routine
establish something firmly (habit, idea, behavior)

Examples

It is important to ingrain good study habits in students early on.
Regular training helps ingrain safe practices into employees' daily routines.
She worked hard to ingrain a sense of responsibility in her children.

Synset Examples

It takes time to ingrain new behaviors in children.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to instill - to gradually establish an idea or attitude, especially in someone's mind
  • Antonym: to eradicate - to remove or destroy something completely, especially a habit or idea

Etymology

From Middle English ingrain (to dye with fast colors), from Old French engrainer (to dye in grain), from Latin granum (grain, dye).
