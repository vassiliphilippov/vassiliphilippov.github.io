vesture

1. NOUN

/ ˈvɛstʃɚ / (AmE), / ˈvɛstjʊə / (BrE)
something that covers or cloaks like a garment; a covering, especially when used figuratively.
something that covers like a garment
archaic

Examples

The mountain was hidden under a vesture of mist.
Spring brings a fresh vesture of green to the valley.
The poet described the city at dusk as wrapped in a golden vesture of light.

Synset Examples

fields in a vesture of green

Related Terms

  • Synonym: garment - an item of clothing

Etymology

From Old French vesture, from Latin vestītūra (clothing).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvɛstʃər / (AmE), / ˈvɛstjʊə / (BrE)
(literary or formal) clothing or garments; something that covers or clothes the body.
clothing; something worn on the body

Examples

The king was draped in splendid vesture during the coronation ceremony.
In the painting, the goddess is depicted with flowing vesture of silken cloth.
Ancient rituals often required participants to wear special vesture signifying their role.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: garment - an article of clothing
  • Synonym: attire - clothes, especially fine or formal ones

Etymology

From Latin 'vestitura', from 'vestire' (to clothe).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03055525-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈvɛstʃɚ / (AmE), / ˈvɛstjʊə / (BrE)
to clothe or cover with a garment, especially a cloak; to dress or envelop
to clothe or cover with a garment
archaic

Examples

The statue was vestured in a flowing white robe for the ceremony.
Twilight shadows vestured the mountains in a cloak of darkness.
He vestures himself in tradition each year with his grandfather’s cloak.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to clothe - to put clothes on; to dress
  • Synonym: dress - to put clothes on oneself or someone else
  • Antonym: strip - to remove clothing from; to undress

Etymology

From Latin vestire (to clothe), via Old French vesture (clothing).
