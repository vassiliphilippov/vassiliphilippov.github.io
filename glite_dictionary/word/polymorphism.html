polymorphism

1. NOUN

/ ˌpɑːliˈmɔːrˌfɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈmɔːfɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The existence of genetic variation within a population, allowing natural selection to act on different heritable traits.
genetic variation within a population
medical

Examples

The polymorphism observed in the gene explained why some individuals were resistant to the disease.
High levels of polymorphism in a population can increase its chances of adaptation.
Researchers studied the polymorphism in butterfly wing patterns to understand natural selection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: variation - Difference or deviation in structure or character

Etymology

From Greek 'polymorphos', meaning 'having many forms' (poly- 'many' + morphē 'form').

2. NOUN

/ ˌpɑːliˈmɔːrˌfɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈmɔːfɪzəm / (BrE)
(Biology) The occurrence of two or more distinct forms, or morphs, among individuals within a single animal species, independent of sex differences.
multiple forms within a species (biology)

Examples

The butterfly population shows remarkable polymorphism in wing color and pattern.
Genetic polymorphism enables certain species to adapt effectively to different environments.
Researchers studied the polymorphism in beetles to understand how physical traits evolve.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monomorphism - the condition of having a single form within a species
  • Degree Lower: dimorphism - the occurrence of two distinct forms within a species

Etymology

From Greek 'poly-' (many) and 'morphē' (form).

3. NOUN

/ ˌpɑːliˈmɔːrfɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈmɔːfɪzəm / (BrE)
The existence of different forms or crystal structures in the same chemical compound.
different crystal forms of a compound
medical

Examples

Some minerals display polymorphism, forming different crystal structures under varying conditions.
The polymorphism of carbon allows it to appear as both graphite and diamond.
Temperature and pressure changes can induce polymorphism in certain chemical substances.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allotropy - The existence of two or more different physical forms of a chemical element.

Etymology

From Greek 'poly-' (many) and 'morphē' (form).
