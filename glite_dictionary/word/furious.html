furious

1. ADJ

/ ˈfjʊriəs / (AmE), / ˈfjʊəriəs / (BrE)
Extremely angry; full of intense rage.
extremely angry

Examples

She was furious when she discovered the mistake in her order.
The furious customer demanded to speak to the manager immediately.
He slammed the door furious with the way he was treated.

Synset Examples

the enraged bull attached
furious about the accident
a furious scowl
infuriated onlookers charged the police who were beating the boy
could not control the maddened crowd

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: angry - feeling or showing strong displeasure
  • Degree Higher: enraged - extremely angry or filled with rage

Etymology

From Latin furiosus (full of rage), from furia (fury).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfjʊriəs / (AmE), / ˈfjʊəriəs / (BrE)
displaying or marked by extreme anger or violent energy
extremely angry; violent in energy

Examples

The furious winds ripped apart the small coastal town overnight.
She was furious when she discovered the mistake that ruined her project.
A furious debate erupted in the parliament over the new law.

Synset Examples

a ferocious beating
fierce fighting
a furious battle

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: angry - feeling or showing strong annoyance or displeasure
  • Degree Higher: enraged - extremely angry; filled with rage
  • Antonym: calm - not showing or feeling anger, excitement, or agitation

Etymology

From Latin furiosus (full of rage), from furia (rage, fury).

3. ADJ

/ ˈfjʊriəs / (AmE), / ˈfjʊəriəs / (BrE)
(of the weather or natural forces) extremely violent or intense, as if showing anger.
very violent or intense (of the elements)

Examples

The furious winds battered the coastline for hours, uprooting trees and damaging houses.
A furious storm rolled in suddenly, bringing heavy rain and lightning.
The sailors struggled against the furious sea during the height of the tempest.

Synset Examples

angry clouds on the horizon
furious winds
the raging sea

Related Terms

  • Synonym: violent - using or accompanied by great physical force
  • Antonym: calm - not showing or feeling violence or agitation, especially of the weather

Etymology

From Latin 'furiosus' (full of rage), from 'furia' (rage, fury).
