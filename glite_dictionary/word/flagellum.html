flagellum

1. NOUN

/ fləˈdʒɛləm / (AmE), / fləˈdʒɛləm / (BrE)
A whip or scourge, especially one used for inflicting punishment.
a whip used for punishment

Examples

The Roman soldiers used a flagellum to enforce discipline among the ranks.
In ancient times, a flagellum was a symbol of authority and punishment.
The historian described the brutal use of the flagellum in medieval prisons.

Etymology

From Latin *flagellum* (whip, scourge), diminutive of *flagrum* (whip).

2. NOUN

/ fləˈdʒɛləm / (AmE), / fləˈdʒɛləm / (BrE)
a long, whip-like projection from a cell used for movement, found in certain bacteria, protozoa, and sperm cells
whip-like cellular appendage for movement
medical

Examples

Many bacteria move using a single flagellum that rotates like a propeller.
The flagellum of a sperm cell enables it to swim toward the egg.
Microscopes revealed the flagellum extending from the protozoan, helping it to glide through water.

Etymology

From Latin 'flagellum' (whip, scourge), diminutive of 'flagrum' (whip).
