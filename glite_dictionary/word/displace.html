to displace

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈpleɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪs / (BrE)
to remove someone from a job or position, especially as a result of restructuring, redundancy, or replacement
remove someone from a job or position

Examples

The company had to displace several managers during the reorganization.
Automation may displace many factory workers in the coming years.
She was displaced from her role after the department merged with another.

Synset Examples

The boss fired his secretary today
The company terminated 25% of its workers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dismiss - to remove someone from their job officially; to fire
  • Synonym: fire - to dismiss someone from their job
  • Antonym: hire - to employ someone for a job

Etymology

From Old French 'desplacer', from Latin 'displacere' (to remove from its place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02407967-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈpleɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪs / (BrE)
to take the place of something or someone; to have precedence over and cause removal or replacement
take the place of; supersede

Examples

The new manager will displace several old policies that are no longer effective.
In some regions, electric vehicles are beginning to displace traditional gasoline cars.
Rising water levels could displace local populations along the river.

Synset Examples

live broadcast of the presidential debate preempts the regular news hour
discussion of the emergency situation will preempt the lecture by the professor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to supersede - to take the place of; to replace
  • Synonym: to replace - to take the place of something that is no longer used or needed

Etymology

From Old French *desplacer* (to remove), based on Latin *dis-* (away, apart) and *placere* (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02411141-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈpleɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪs / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to move from its usual or original place or position, either physically or abstractly
move someone or something from original position

Examples

The construction of the new highway will displace hundreds of residents from their homes.
Technological innovations often displace older methods and practices in the industry.
She feared that a new manager would displace her from her current position.

Synset Examples

Move those boxes into the corner, please
I'm moving my money to another bank
The director moved more responsibilities onto his new assistant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: move - to change the place or position of something or someone
  • Synonym: to replace - to take the place of something or someone

Etymology

From Old French 'desplacer', from Latin 'dis-' (apart) + 'placere' (to place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01854282-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪsˈpleɪs / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪs / (BrE)
to force someone or something to move from its usual place, often because of external pressure or force
to forcefully move from usual place

Examples

Thousands of villagers were displaced by the flooding in the region.
The rapid construction of new buildings can displace local wildlife populations.
War and conflict often displace entire communities, forcing them to seek shelter elsewhere.

Synset Examples

the refugees were displaced by the war

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evict - to force someone to leave a place, especially a home
  • Synonym: to relocate - to move to a new place or position
  • Antonym: to replace - to put something new in the place of something else

Etymology

From Old French *desplacier* (to move from place) and Medieval Latin *displacare*, from Latin *dis-* (apart) + *placēre* (to please, to place).
