epanalepsis

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛpəˌnəˈlɛpsɪs / (AmE), / ˌɛpəˌnəˈlɛpsɪs / (BrE)
A rhetorical device in which a word or phrase is repeated at the beginning and end of a clause or sentence, often with intervening words.
rhetorical repetition at start and end of a clause

Examples

The phrase "The king is dead, long live the king" is an example of epanalepsis in rhetoric.
Writers often use epanalepsis to add emphasis or create a poetic effect.
In his speech, he employed epanalepsis by repeating the same word at the beginning and end of a sentence.

Etymology

From Greek epanalepsis (repetition, resumption), from epi (upon) + ana (again) + lambanein (to take).
