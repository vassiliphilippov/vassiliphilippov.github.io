detour

1. VERB

/ ˈdiːˌtʊr / (AmE), / ˈdiːtʊə / (BrE)
to travel by taking a longer or different route than usual, especially to avoid an obstacle or reach a destination indirectly.
take a longer or alternate route

Examples

We had to detour around the flooded section of the road.
Road construction forced the school bus to detour through a nearby neighborhood.
Because of the parade downtown, drivers detoured along quieter streets to reach their offices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bypass - to go around instead of through a place or obstacle

Etymology

From French 'détour' (a turning aside, digression), from 'détourner' (to turn aside).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02070131-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈdiːˌtʊr / (AmE), / ˈdiː.tʊə / (BrE)
a temporary route taken when the usual road or way is blocked or unavailable, especially due to construction or an accident
a temporary alternative route

Examples

The highway was closed for repairs, so drivers had to take a detour through side streets.
We missed our exit and ended up making a long detour before getting back on track.
Construction signs indicated a detour, forcing commuters to find an alternate way.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bypass - a road passing around a town rather than through it
  • Antonym: shortcut - a route shorter than the usual one

Etymology

From French *détour* (a turning aside, digression), from *détourner* (to turn aside).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03187172-n
Synset POS: n
