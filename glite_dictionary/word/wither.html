to wither

1. VERB

/ ˈwɪðər / (AmE), / ˈwɪðə / (BrE)
(of a plant, person, or thing) to lose freshness, vigor, or vitality; to become weak, dry, or shriveled.
lose freshness or vitality

Examples

After weeks without rain, the crops began to wither in the fields.
As the years passed, her enthusiasm seemed to wither away.
The flowers will quickly wither if you don't put them in water.

Synset Examples

Her bloom was fading

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fade - to gradually lose color, brightness, or strength
  • Synonym: wilt - to lose strength and become limp, often from lack of water (especially of plants)
  • Antonym: to thrive - to grow, develop, or be successful

Etymology

From Old English *wiðerian* (to dry up, shrivel), related to German *verwelken*.

2. VERB

/ ˈwɪðər / (AmE), / ˈwɪðə / (BrE)
(of a plant, fruit, or other organic matter) to become dry and shriveled, usually through loss of moisture; to lose freshness, vigor, or vitality.
become dry or shriveled

Examples

The leaves began to wither under the harsh summer sun.
If you don't water the flowers regularly, they will quickly wither and die.
Time and neglect caused the once-beautiful bouquet to wither in the vase.

Synset Examples

The fruit dried and shriveled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to shrivel - to become wrinkled and contracted, especially due to loss of moisture
  • Synonym: wilt - (of a plant) to become limp through heat, loss of water, or disease

Etymology

From Old English 'wiðerian' (to dry up, shrink, decay).
