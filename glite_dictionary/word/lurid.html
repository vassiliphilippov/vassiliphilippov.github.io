lurid

1. ADJ

/ ˈlʊrɪd, ˈljuːrɪd / (AmE), / ˈljʊərɪd, ˈlʊərɪd / (BrE)
shining with a vivid, unnatural red or fiery glow, especially as seen through smoke or haze
glowing with an unnatural red or fiery light

Examples

The forest was bathed in a lurid glow as the fire raged through the trees.
A lurid light came from the factory, shining through the dense evening smog.
We watched the lurid sunset paint the sky with streaks of red and orange.

Synset Examples

a lurid sunset
lurid flames

Etymology

From Latin *luridus* (pale yellow, wan).

2. ADJ

/ ˈlʊrɪd / (AmE), / ˈljʊərɪd / (BrE)
Having a pale, ghastly, or unnaturally unnatural light or appearance.
ghastly pale or unnatural in color

Examples

The ghost's face had a lurid glow in the moonlight, making it appear even more frightening.
A lurid light cast strange shadows on the old castle walls.
Her skin took on a lurid hue under the harsh fluorescent lights.

Synset Examples

moonlight gave the statue a lurid luminance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pallid - lacking color or liveliness; pale
  • Synonym: wan - pale and giving the impression of illness or exhaustion
  • Antonym: vivid - producing powerful feelings or strong, clear images in the mind

Etymology

From Latin luridus (pale yellow, wan).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00406827-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈlʊr.ɪd / (AmE), / ˈljʊə.rɪd / (BrE)
Extremely shocking or sensational, especially because of being gruesome or vividly explicit in depicting crime or violence.
gruesome; sensationally shocking

Examples

The newspaper described the lurid details of the crime in shocking detail.
She was fascinated by lurid stories of criminal exploits and scandals.
The trial exposed a lurid side of the city's nightlife that few people had seen.

Synset Examples

lurid crimes
a lurid life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ghastly - horribly shocking or frightful
  • Synonym: gruesome - causing horror or disgust; grisly
  • Antonym: pleasant - giving a sense of happy satisfaction or enjoyment

Etymology

From Latin *luridus* (pale yellow, ghastly), from *lūor* (to be pale).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02522392-s
Synset POS: s

4. ADJ

/ ˈlʊr.ɪd / (AmE), / ˈljʊə.rɪd / (BrE)
presented in a vividly shocking or sensational way, often giving explicit details of crimes or events
sensationally explicit or shocking

Examples

The newspaper was criticized for publishing lurid details about the murder case.
She was fascinated by lurid stories of scandal and crime.
The movie is full of lurid violence that some viewers found disturbing.

Synset Examples

lurid details of the accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sensational - causing great public interest and excitement
  • Antonym: restrained - subtle or not excessively showy; the opposite of lurid in this sense

Etymology

From Latin 'luridus' (pale yellow, ghastly).
