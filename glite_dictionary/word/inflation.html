inflation

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of filling something with air or gas so that it becomes larger or more inflated
the act of filling with air

Examples

The inflation of the bicycle tire took only a few seconds with the pump.
Balloon inflation should be done slowly to avoid bursting.
Rapid inflation of the life jacket can be triggered in an emergency.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deflation - the act of releasing air or gas from something inflated

Etymology

From Latin inflatio, from inflare ‘to blow into’.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (BrE)
A general and continual rise in prices across an economy, resulting in a decrease in the purchasing power of money.
general rise in prices; decreased money value

Examples

The government implemented policies to control inflation and stabilize the economy.
During periods of high inflation, the cost of everyday goods can rise quickly.
Many workers demand higher wages to keep up with inflation.

Synset Examples

in inflation everything gets more valuable except money

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deflation - a decrease in the general price level of goods and services
  • Degree Higher: hyperinflation - extremely rapid or out-of-control inflation

Etymology

From Latin 'inflare' (to blow into or inflate), via French 'inflation'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13520031-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfleɪʃən / (BrE)
(Cosmology) A period of extremely rapid exponential expansion of the universe that is theorized to have occurred just after the Big Bang, causing the size of the universe to increase faster than the speed of light.
cosmological rapid expansion after the Big Bang

Examples

According to modern theories, inflation resolved several cosmic mysteries by stretching small regions of space to immense sizes.
The concept of inflation explains why the universe appears uniform in all directions.
Scientists have proposed that inflation occurred within a fraction of a second after the Big Bang.

Etymology

From Latin inflatio (a blowing into, puffing up), used in 20th-century physics to describe universe expansion.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
