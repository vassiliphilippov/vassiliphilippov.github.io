Donatism

1. NOUN

/ ˈdoʊ.nəˌtɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈdəʊ.nə.tɪz.əm / (BrE)
A Christian doctrine and movement in northern Africa from the 4th to 7th centuries, which maintained that only blameless individuals could be true members of the Church or administer sacraments.
Christian sect and doctrine (4th–7th century North Africa)

Examples

The conflict between the Roman Church and Donatism led to decades of religious tension in North Africa.
Historians often cite Donatism to illustrate the complexities of early Christian schisms.
Augustine wrote extensively against Donatism, arguing for the unity of the church.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Catharism - A medieval Christian heresy with similar views on church purity

Etymology

From the name of Donatus, a 4th-century North African bishop and leader of the movement.
