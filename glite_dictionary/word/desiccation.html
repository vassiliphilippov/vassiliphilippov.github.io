desiccation

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɛsɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛsɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of removing moisture from something, especially to dry it completely
the process of drying out or removing moisture

Examples

Prolonged desiccation can preserve organic materials for centuries in arid environments.
Scientists studied the effects of desiccation on the survival of desert plants.
The museum uses controlled desiccation to protect its ancient manuscripts from mold.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drying - the process of becoming dry or removing moisture
  • Antonym: hydration - the process of adding moisture or water

Etymology

From Latin 'desiccare' (to dry up), from 'de-' (out) + 'siccare' (to dry).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɛsɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛsɪˈkeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the process of becoming extremely dry, especially as a result of the removal of water; the state of being dried out
the process or state of extreme dryness

Examples

Desiccation of the soil can make it difficult for plants to grow in arid regions.
The museum preserves ancient manuscripts using desiccation to prevent mold and decay.
After weeks without rain, the desiccation of the riverbed was evident to all.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dehydration - removal of water or moisture; the process causing dryness
  • Antonym: moistening - the act of making something moist or wet; the opposite of desiccation

Etymology

From Latin desiccare (to dry up), from de- (completely) + siccare (to dry).
