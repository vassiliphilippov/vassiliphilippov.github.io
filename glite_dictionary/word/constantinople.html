Constantinople

1. PROPN

/ kɒnˌstæn.tɪˈnoʊ.pəl / (AmE), / kɒn.stæn.tɪˈnəʊ.pəl / (BrE)
the former name of Istanbul, historically the capital of the Byzantine Empire, founded by Constantine I in the fourth century
former name of Istanbul, Turkey

Examples

The city of Constantinople served as the capital of the Byzantine Empire for over a thousand years.
In 1453, Constantinople was conquered by the Ottoman Turks and later became Istanbul.
Many ancient churches and monuments still stand in what was once Constantinople.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Istanbul - the current name of the city formerly known as Constantinople

Etymology

From Latin 'Constantinopolis', from Greek 'Konstantinoupolis' (city of Constantine).

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːn.stæn.tɪˈnoʊ.pəl / (AmE), / ˌkɒn.stæn.tɪˈnəʊ.pəl / (BrE)
the second ecumenical council of the Christian church, held in 381 AD in Constantinople, which expanded the Nicene Creed, particularly in regard to the Holy Spirit.
second ecumenical council (AD 381) expanding the Nicene Creed

Examples

The First Council of Constantinople clarified Christian doctrine on the Holy Spirit.
The Nicene Creed as recited today reflects changes made at Constantinople in 381.
Scholars debate the political context of Constantinople and its impact on early Christianity.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Council of Nicaea - the first ecumenical council of the Christian Church (AD 325)
  • Degree Higher: Council of Ephesus - the third ecumenical council (AD 431)

Etymology

From the name of the city Constantinople (modern Istanbul), Latin from Greek Konstantinoupolis, meaning ‘City of Constantine’.
