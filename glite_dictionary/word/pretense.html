pretense

1. NOUN

/ ˈpriːˌtɛns / (AmE), / ˈpriː.təns / (BrE)
the act of pretending, especially using imagination in play or in intellectual exercise
imaginative pretending or make-believe

Examples

Children often engage in pretense when they invent stories with their toys.
Her pretense of being a detective helped spark the group's imagination.
Through pretense, young students learn important social and problem-solving skills.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: make-believe - play involving pretending or imagination

Etymology

From Old French *pretense*, from Medieval Latin *praetensus*, past participle of *praetendere* (to pretend).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpriːˌtɛns, prɪˈtɛns / (AmE), / ˈpriːtɛns, prɪˈtɛns / (BrE)
the act of pretending or making something appear true or real when it is not; a false claim or reason given to hide the real purpose or situation.
false claim or appearance

Examples

He made no pretense of friendship.
The invitation was only a pretense for her to visit.
Under the pretense of helping, he tried to gather information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pretext - a reason given in justification of a course of action that is not the real reason
  • Synonym: deception - the act of deceiving or lying; making someone believe something that is not true
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and straightforward

Etymology

From Old French 'pretense', from medieval Latin 'praetensus', meaning 'pretended', past participle of 'praetendere' (to pretend).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpriːˌtɛns / (AmE), / ˈpriː.tɛns / (BrE)
an attempt to make something that is not the case appear true, especially with the intention to deceive
a false show or claim; deception

Examples

She made a pretense of being interested, but she was actually bored.
Under the pretense of friendship, he tried to gather information.
His cheerful attitude was just a pretense to hide his true feelings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deception - the act of deceiving or misleading
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and sincere

Etymology

From Old French 'prétens', from Medieval Latin 'pretensus', past participle of Latin 'praetendere' (to stretch forth, pretend).
