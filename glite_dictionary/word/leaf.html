leaf

1. NOUN

/ lif / (AmE), / liːf / (BrE)
a single sheet of paper in a book or manuscript, especially one with writing or printing on it
sheet of paper in a book or manuscript

Examples

She carefully turned the delicate leaf of the ancient manuscript.
A torn leaf was missing from the back of the old book.
The librarian found several loose leaves inside the cover.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: page - one side of a sheet of paper in a book or publication

Etymology

Old English *lēaf* (leaf, sheet), related to Old High German *loub* (leaf).

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ lif / (AmE), / liːf / (BrE)
a hinged or detachable flat section of an object, such as an extension of a table or a panel of a door
hinged or detachable table or door section

Examples

The dining table has an extra leaf that can be added for guests.
He folded down the leaf of the drop-leaf desk to save space.
One of the door’s leaves was removed for repair.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: panel - a flat, typically rectangular piece forming part of a surface, such as a door

Etymology

From Old English lēaf (leaf, sheet); extended metaphorical use in reference to flat objects.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03657552-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ lif / (AmE), / liːf / (BrE)
the flat, typically green part of a plant that grows from a stem or branch and is the main organ for photosynthesis and transpiration.
main photosynthetic organ of a plant

Examples

The maple tree's leaves turned brilliant red in autumn.
Photosynthesis primarily takes place in the leaf of the plant.
She pressed the leaf between the pages of her book to preserve it.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: needle - a slender, pointed leaf found in many conifers
  • Antonym: petal - a part of a flower, often brightly colored but not a true leaf

Etymology

Old English lēaf (leaf, foliage), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13173519-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ lif / (AmE), / liːf / (BrE)
to turn the pages of a book, magazine, etc., quickly, often without reading every word
turn pages quickly; browse through pages

Examples

She leafed through the magazine while waiting for her appointment.
He likes to leaf through cookbooks for dinner ideas.
After finding the old journal, I spent hours leafing through its pages.

Synset Examples

He thumbed through the report
She leafed through the volume

Related Terms

  • Synonym: browse - to look through or glance at casually
  • Antonym: to peruse - to read something, especially carefully or thoroughly

Etymology

From the noun 'leaf', meaning a page in a book.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02157453-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ lif / (AmE), / liːf / (BrE)
to quickly turn the pages of a book, magazine, or document, usually looking for something or to get a general impression
quickly turn pages, glance through

Examples

She likes to leaf through magazines in waiting rooms to pass the time.
I watched him leaf rapidly through the folder, searching for a specific document.
After dinner, I leafed through my old photo album and reminisced about childhood.

Synset Examples

leaf through a book
leaf a manuscript

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flip through - to look quickly through the pages of a book or magazine

Etymology

From Old English 'lēaf', originally meaning 'leaf' (of a plant or book page).
