slenderness

1. NOUN

/ ˈslɛndɚnəs / (AmE), / ˈslɛndənɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being slight, insufficient, or inadequate
quality of being insufficient or slight

Examples

The slenderness of the evidence was not enough to convict him.
We were worried about the slenderness of our chances of success.
Given the slenderness of my resources, I had to decline the trip.

Synset Examples

he knew the slenderness of my wallet
the slenderness of the chances that anything would be done
the slenderness of the evidence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insufficiency - the quality of being not enough
  • Antonym: abundance - a large quantity, more than enough

Etymology

From 'slender' + '-ness'; 'slender' from Middle English, possibly from Middle Dutch 'slinder' (thin).

2. NOUN

/ ˈslɛndɚnəs / (AmE), / ˈslɛndənəs / (BrE)
the quality of being thin, narrow, or gracefully slim in shape, especially as compared to length or height
the quality of being thin or narrow

Examples

The slenderness of the columns gave the pavilion an elegant appearance.
She admired the slenderness of the model's waist.
The slenderness of the tree trunks made them sway easily in the wind.

Synset Examples

the tenuity of a hair
the thinness of a rope

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thinness - the quality or state of being thin
  • Antonym: stoutness - the quality of being thick, solid, or broad

Etymology

From slender + -ness (suffix forming nouns denoting a state or quality).

3. NOUN

/ ˈslɛndɚnəs / (AmE), / ˈslɛndənɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being attractively thin or gracefully slim in appearance, especially in reference to a person
quality of being attractively slim

Examples

Grace admired the slenderness of the model as she walked down the runway.
Her slenderness is often complemented by her elegant posture.
The artist tried to capture the slenderness of the dancer in his sketch.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thinness - quality of being thin
  • Antonym: plumpness - quality of being plump

Etymology

From slender + -ness; slender from Middle English slendre, probably from Middle Dutch slinder.
