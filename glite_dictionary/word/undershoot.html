to undershoot

1. VERB

/ ˌʌndərˈʃuːt / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈʃuːt / (BrE)
to fail to reach, hit, or reach a target by falling short of it; to shoot or aim too low or too short
fail to reach a target by falling short

Examples

If you undershoot the runway, you may land in the grass instead of on the pavement.
The company tends to undershoot its sales targets to manage investor expectations.
He undershot the basketball hoop, and the ball bounced off the front rim.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to overshoot - to go beyond or exceed a target or mark

Etymology

From under- (meaning below, not enough) + shoot (to discharge a projectile).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01154998-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌʌndɚˈʃuːt / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈʃuːt / (BrE)
(of an aircraft) to land short of the intended runway or target area.
land short of a target (esp. runway)

Examples

The pilot had to abort the landing after the plane undershot the runway during bad weather.
If the aircraft undershoots its target, it may land in unsafe terrain.
Investigators found that heavy fog contributed to the jet undershooting the airport runway.

Synset Examples

The plane undershot the runway

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to overshoot - to go beyond or past a target, especially in landing or aiming

Etymology

Formed from 'under-' (not enough) + 'shoot' (move rapidly or aim for a target).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01984490-v
Synset POS: v
