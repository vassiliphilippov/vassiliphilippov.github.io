subjunctive

1. NOUN

/ səbˈdʒʌŋktɪv / (AmE), / səbˈdʒʌŋktɪv / (BrE)
a verb mood used to express wishes, suggestions, demands, or situations that are hypothetical or not factual
verb mood for wishes or hypotheticals

Examples

The teacher explained when to use the subjunctive in conditional sentences.
In English, the subjunctive often appears in phrases like 'If I were you'.
The French language uses the subjunctive much more frequently than English.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: indicative - the verb mood expressing factual statements or questions
  • Antonym: imperative - the verb mood used for commands or requests

Etymology

From Latin 'subjunctivus' (serving to subjoin).

2. ADJ

/ səbˈdʒʌŋktɪv / (AmE), / səbˈdʒʌŋktɪv / (BrE)
(of a verb or verb form) relating to or denoting a mood expressing wishes, hypotheses, demands, or suggestions, rather than statements of fact.
relating to the subjunctive mood in verbs

Examples

The teacher explained when to use the subjunctive in English sentences.
In some languages, the subjunctive mood is used more frequently than in English.
He struggled to recognize subjunctive verb forms on his grammar test.

Synset Examples

subjunctive verb endings

Related Terms

  • Antonym: indicative - relating to the verb mood expressing statements of fact
  • Antonym: imperative - relating to the verb mood used to express commands or requests

Etymology

From Late Latin *subjunctivus*, from Latin *subiunctus* (joined under), referring to subordinate clauses.
