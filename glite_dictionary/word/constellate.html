to constellate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːn.stəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.stə.leɪt / (BrE)
(formal or literary) To cluster together or form a group or collection, especially in a way that is reminiscent of stars forming a constellation.
to cluster or come together in a group

Examples

Each summer, scholars constellate at the historic university to share their latest research.
Small villages constellate along the winding river, creating a scenic landscape.
Ideas from different cultures constellate in this city, making it a vibrant center of creativity.

Synset Examples

The poets constellate in this town every summer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - to come together in a group

Etymology

From Latin 'constellatus', past participle of 'constellare' (to group together like stars).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːn.stə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.stə.leɪt / (BrE)
to scatter or spread over an area resembling stars in a constellation; to dot or stud with bright points
to scatter like stars across an area

Examples

The hillside was constellated with the golden lights of cottages.
Tiny wildflowers constellate the meadow each spring, creating a vibrant tapestry of color.
At night, lanterns constellated the festival grounds, giving the scene a magical appearance.

Synset Examples

Hills constellated with lights

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dot - to mark with small spots
  • Synonym: stud - to decorate with small objects or points

Etymology

From Latin constellatus, past participle of constellare (to group among stars), from con- (together) + stella (star).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02695724-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑn.stə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.stə.leɪt / (BrE)
to form or cause to form into a group or cluster, like a constellation
form into a cluster or group

Examples

The bright city lights constellate the night sky, creating patterns reminiscent of actual stars.
Various artists constellated around the new movement, forming a vibrant creative cluster.
Tiny islands constellate the bay, each with its unique charm and character.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - to come together into a close group
  • Antonym: to disperse - to scatter or spread widely

Etymology

From Latin 'constellatus' (starred, marked with stars), from 'con-' (together) + 'stella' (star).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00141875-v
Synset POS: v
