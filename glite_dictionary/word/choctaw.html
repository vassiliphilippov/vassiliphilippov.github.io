Choctaw

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʃɑːktɔː / (AmE), / ˈtʃɒktɔː / (BrE)
a member of a Native American people originally inhabiting areas of present-day Alabama, Mississippi, and Louisiana
member of a Native American people

Examples

Several Choctaw served as code talkers during World War I.
The Choctaw have a rich cultural heritage that is still celebrated today.
A group of Choctaw settled in what is now southeastern Oklahoma.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Muskogean - a member of a group of Native American peoples in the southeastern United States

Etymology

From the Choctaw self-designation, Chahta.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtʃɑk.tɔ / (AmE), / ˈtʃɒk.tɔː / (BrE)
the Native American language of the Choctaw people, belonging to the Muskogean language family
the language of the Choctaw people

Examples

Many elders still speak Choctaw as their first language in some communities.
She is studying Choctaw at university to help preserve her heritage.
The Choctaw language has unique features that set it apart from other Native American languages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Muscogee - the language of the Muscogee (Creek) people, closely related to Choctaw

Etymology

From the name of the Choctaw people, originally from their own autonym Chahta.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06925212-n
Synset POS: n
