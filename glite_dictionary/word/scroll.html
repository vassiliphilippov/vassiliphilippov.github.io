scroll

1. NOUN

/ skroʊl / (AmE), / skrəʊl / (BrE)
a roll of parchment, paper, or similar material, especially one with writing on it, that can be rolled up for storage or transport
rolled-up written document

Examples

The ancient library contained a rare scroll inscribed with philosophical teachings.
She gently unrolled the scroll to reveal the beautifully painted calligraphy.
Researchers discovered a scroll hidden inside the old statue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manuscript - a handwritten document, especially a book or document written before the invention of printing

Etymology

From Old French escroe, escroue (strip or scroll), ultimately from Medieval Latin scrofa (strip of parchment).

2. VERB

/ skroʊl / (AmE), / skrəʊl / (BrE)
to move text or graphics on a computer screen up, down, or across so as to display parts that do not fit on the screen
move content on a screen

Examples

You can scroll through the document to find the section you need.
She scrolled down the page to read the latest comments.
If you scroll to the bottom, you'll see the download link.

Synset Examples

Scroll down to see the entire text

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: scroll up - move displayed content upward on a screen
  • Degree Lower: scroll down - move displayed content downward on a screen

Etymology

Originally from Old French *escroe* (strip of parchment), later extended to computer usage.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00124662-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ skroʊl / (AmE), / skrəʊl / (BrE)
a spiral or coiled shape, especially as formed by leaves, petals, or in ornamental design
spiral or coiled shape

Examples

The capital of the column was decorated with an elegant scroll design.
She admired the delicate scroll of the rose petals as they unfurled.
The artist carved a wooden frame featuring a scroll pattern reminiscent of ancient Greek art.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spiral - a curve which winds around a central point gradually getting farther from it
  • Synonym: coil - a series of loops that curve around and around

Etymology

From Old French *escroe* (roll of parchment), probably of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
