to disentangle

1. VERB

/ ˌdɪsənˈtæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˌdɪsɪnˈtæŋɡ(ə)l / (BrE)
to free something or someone from being caught or twisted with something else; to untangle
free from entanglement; untangle

Examples

It took me several minutes to disentangle my headphones from the mess in my bag.
The rescue team worked tirelessly to disentangle the whale from the fishing net.
She tried to disentangle herself from the awkward conversation.

Synset Examples

Can you disentangle the cord?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to untangle - to make something that is twisted or knotted straight again

Etymology

From dis- (expressing reversal) + entangle, modeled after Old French desentaler.

/ ˌdɪsɪnˈtæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˌdɪsɪnˈtæŋɡəl / (BrE)
to smooth out and neaten (hair or fibers) by removing tangles, often using a comb or fingers
smooth or neaten by removing tangles

Examples

She carefully disentangled her long hair before going to bed.
It took him a while to disentangle the wool after it became knotted.
The hairdresser gently disentangled the child's curls with a wide-toothed comb.

5. VERB

/ ˌdɪsənˈtæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˌdɪsɪnˈtæŋɡl / (BrE)
To free something from tangles or complications; to separate something that is twisted together.
free from tangles or complications

Examples

She tried to disentangle the cords behind the television.
It took hours to disentangle her necklace from the others in the box.
The rescue team worked quickly to disentangle the whale from the fishing nets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to untangle - to make something no longer tangled; to separate entangled elements
  • Antonym: to entangle - to twist together or catch in a tangle

Etymology

From dis- (expressing reversal) + entangle (from Old French entangler).
