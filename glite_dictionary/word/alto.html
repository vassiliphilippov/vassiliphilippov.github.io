alto

1. NOUN

/ ˈæltoʊ / (AmE), / ˈæltəʊ / (BrE)
the lowest female singing voice, or the voice part written for it
lowest female singing voice

Examples

She sings alto in the church choir every Sunday.
The composer wrote a challenging piece for the alto soloist.
In this ensemble, the alto part requires precise harmony with the sopranos.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: soprano - the highest female singing voice
  • Degree Lower: tenor - a voice type higher than baritone and lower than alto in men

Etymology

From Italian alto (high), from Latin altus (high, deep).

2. NOUN

/ ˈæl.toʊ / (AmE), / ˈæl.təʊ / (BrE)
(of a musical instrument) the second highest instrument in a family of similar instruments, such as the alto saxophone or alto flute.
second highest instrument in a group

Examples

The alto saxophone is a popular choice for jazz musicians.
She plays the alto flute in the school orchestra.
In the quartet, the alto clarinet has a distinct, mellow sound.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: soprano - the highest voice or instrument in a family
  • Degree Lower: tenor - the instrument below the alto in pitch within a family

Etymology

From Italian alto, meaning 'high' (from Latin altus).

8. NOUN

/ ˈæltoʊ / (AmE), / ˈæltəʊ / (BrE)
a singer, usually a woman or boy, whose voice is lower than a soprano and higher than a tenor
singer with a low female or boy's voice

Examples

She sang as an alto in the school choir for three years.
The alto stood between the sopranos and the tenors during rehearsal.
Many famous operatic roles have been written for the alto voice.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: soprano - a singer with the highest vocal range
  • Degree Lower: tenor - a singer with a range just below alto but above baritone

Etymology

From Italian 'alto' (high), from Latin 'altus'. Originally referred to the highest male part before meaning shifted to the current voice type.
