to refract

1. VERB

/ rɪˈfrækt / (AmE), / rɪˈfrɑːkt / (BrE)
to measure the refracting power of a lens or other optical element.
measure optical refraction

Examples

The optician will refract each lens to ensure it meets specifications.
In the lab, scientists routinely refract different types of glass to study their optical properties.
The technician refracted the new lens before installing it in the microscope.

Etymology

From Latin refractus, past participle of refringere (to break up).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈfrækt / (AmE), / rɪˈfrakt / (BrE)
(of a wave, such as light or sound) to change direction when it passes at an angle from one medium into another of different density.
change direction when passing into a different medium

Examples

A prism can refract white light into the colors of the spectrum.
Waves will refract when they enter shallow water at an angle.
The atmosphere can refract starlight, making stars appear to twinkle.

Synset Examples

refract a light beam

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to reflect - to bounce back from a surface rather than passing through

Etymology

From Latin refractus, past participle of refringere (to break up).

3. VERB

/ rɪˈfrækt / (AmE), / rɪˈfrɑːkt / (BrE)
(of light or other waves) to change direction when it passes at an angle from one medium into another of different density.
(of light) bend as it passes through a medium

Examples

When sunlight enters a glass of water, the refracted rays can create colorful patterns.
A prism refracts light into the colors of the spectrum.
As light passes from air into water, it refracts and changes direction.

Synset Examples

He used a prism to demonstrate how light refracts.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to reflect - (of light or sound) to bounce off a surface
  • Degree Lower: diffuse - (of light) to spread out in many directions
  • Degree Higher: scatter - (of light) to be dispersed or spread irregularly

Etymology

From Latin 'refractus', past participle of 'refringere', meaning 'to break up'.
