explosive

1. ADJ

/ ɪkˈsploʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊsɪv / (BrE)
(of a sound or action) sudden and very loud, often startling or forceful.
sudden and very loud (of sound/action)

Examples

His explosive laugh echoed through the quiet room.
She made an explosive entrance that startled everyone at the party.
The comedian's joke was met with an explosive burst of applause.

Synset Examples

an explosive laugh

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thunderous - very loud and powerful (of a sound)
  • Antonym: quiet - making little or no noise

Etymology

From Latin explosivus, from explodere (to drive out by clapping).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01147494-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈsploʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊsɪv / (BrE)
A chemical substance that undergoes a rapid reaction, producing gas and heat, especially when heated or struck, often used for blasting or as a weapon.
a substance causing rapid explosion

Examples

The engineers carefully handled the explosive to avoid any accidental ignition.
Police discovered a small quantity of homemade explosive in the suspect's garage.
Dynamite is a well-known commercial explosive used in mining and construction.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: detonator - a device used to trigger an explosive

Etymology

From Latin 'explosivus', from 'explodere' meaning 'to drive out by clapping'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03309675-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ɪkˈsploʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊsɪv / (BrE)
able or likely to explode; causing or relating to an explosion or sudden violent outburst.
liable to explode; sudden or violent

Examples

The scientist handled the explosive chemicals with great caution.
There was an explosive argument in the meeting that caught everyone by surprise.
His explosive reaction to the news shocked his friends.

Synset Examples

an explosive device
explosive gas
explosive force
explosive violence
an explosive temper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: volatile - liable to change rapidly, especially for the worse; unstable
  • Antonym: calm - not showing or feeling nervousness, anger, or other strong emotions

Etymology

From Medieval Latin explosivus (tending to explode), from Latin explodere (to drive out by clapping).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00476610-a
Synset POS: a

4. ADJ

/ ɪkˈsploʊsɪv / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊsɪv / (BrE)
(of a situation or issue) likely to cause sudden change or violence; liable to erupt unexpectedly.
likely to cause sudden violence or change

Examples

The political climate in the region remains explosive after last week's unrest.
The manager warned that the meeting could become explosive if tempers flared.
His comments turned what was already an explosive debate into a shouting match.

Synset Examples

an explosive issue
a volatile situation with troops and rioters eager for a confrontation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: volatile - liable to change rapidly and unpredictably, especially for the worse

Etymology

From Latin 'explosivus', from 'explodere' (to drive out by clapping).
