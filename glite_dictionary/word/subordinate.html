subordinate

1. ADJ

/ səˈbɔːrdɪnət / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
(of a person or thing) lower in rank or importance; less important than someone or something else.
lower in rank or importance

Examples

In the company hierarchy, a manager is usually subordinate to a director.
The committee's recommendations were considered subordinate to the main policy.
Her needs became subordinate to those of her children after they were born.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superior - higher in rank or importance
  • Synonym: inferior - lower in status or quality; used as a synonym in some contexts

Etymology

From Latin 'subordinatus', past participle of 'subordinare' meaning 'to place in order, to rank lower'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00796546-a
Synset POS: a

2. VERB

/ səˈbɔːrdəˌneɪt / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪneɪt / (BrE)
to treat or consider someone or something as less important than someone or something else
treat as less important

Examples

The needs of the group often subordinate individual desires.
Management sometimes subordinates employee welfare to profit.
He felt that his own goals had been subordinated to the needs of the company.

Synset Examples

Art is sometimes subordinated to Science in these schools

Related Terms

  • Synonym: downgrade - to lower in rank or value
  • Antonym: to elevate - to raise something to a higher rank or position

Etymology

From Latin 'subordinare' (to arrange below), from 'sub-' (under) + 'ordinare' (to arrange).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ səˈbɔrdənət / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
a word having a more specific meaning than another word to which it is related; a hyponym.
a more specific word; hyponym

Examples

In the semantic hierarchy, 'rose' is a subordinate of 'flower'.
The word 'sparrow' functions as a subordinate of 'bird' in this taxonomy.
'Apple' is a subordinate to 'fruit' in the given classification.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superordinate - a word having a more general meaning; a hypernym

Etymology

From Latin subordinatus, past participle of subordinare (to place in a lower order).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06303754-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ səˈbɔrdəˌneɪt / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
a person who has a lower position or rank, and is subject to the authority or control of another
person of lower rank or status

Examples

The manager held a meeting with each subordinate to discuss their progress on the project.
She delegated several important tasks to her most trusted subordinate.
As a subordinate, John was responsible for following the supervisor's instructions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superior - a person of higher rank or status
  • Synonym: assistant - a person who helps another, often of lower rank

Etymology

From Latin subordinatus, past participle of subornare ('to arrange under'), from sub- ('under') + ordinare ('to arrange').

Source: WordNet

/ səˈbɔrdənət / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
(of a clause) unable to stand alone as a complete sentence; dependent on a main clause.
unable to stand alone as a complete sentence (grammar)

Examples

A subordinate clause must be attached to a main clause to form a complete sentence.
The teacher explained how a subordinate clause adds extra information to the sentence.
When the subordinate clause is removed, the remaining sentence still makes sense.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dependent - (of a clause) unable to stand alone as a sentence; dependent on another clause
  • Antonym: independent - (of a clause) able to stand alone as a sentence; not subordinate

Etymology

From Latin subordinatus, past participle of subordinare meaning 'to place in a lower order'.
