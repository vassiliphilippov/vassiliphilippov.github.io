tarnish

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɑrnɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːnɪʃ / (BrE)
A dull or discolored film that forms on the surface of a metal caused by oxidation or other chemical reactions, often resulting in loss of luster.
discoloration of a metal surface

Examples

Silver jewelry often develops a tarnish if it is not stored properly.
The antique candlesticks had a layer of tarnish that dulled their shine.
You can remove the tarnish from copper pots with a mixture of lemon juice and salt.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rust - reddish-brown coating on iron or steel from oxidation

Etymology

From Middle English ternen, from Old French ternir, from terne (dull, dark).

2. VERB

/ ˈtɑrnɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈtɑːnɪʃ / (BrE)
to make or become less bright or clean, especially by exposure to air; to spoil the good opinion or reputation of something or someone
lose brightness; damage reputation

Examples

The silver candlesticks will tarnish if they are not polished regularly.
She refused to let the scandal tarnish her good name.
Years of neglect had tarnished the once-beautiful statue.

Synset Examples

The silver was tarnished by the long exposure to the air
Her reputation was sullied after the affair with a married man

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sully - to damage the purity or integrity of; to taint
  • Antonym: clean - to make something free from dirt or marks
  • Antonym: polish - to make a surface smooth and shiny by rubbing it

Etymology

From Middle English 'tarnishen', from Old French 'ternir' (to make dull), from Vulgar Latin *ternire.
