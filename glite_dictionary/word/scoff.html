to scoff

1. VERB

/ skɑf / (AmE), / skɒf / (BrE)
to speak or laugh at someone or something in a scornful or derisive way
mock or laugh at with scorn

Examples

The critics scoffed at the idea of launching an electric car company.
He scoffed when I told him about my new business plan.
Many people scoffed at her dreams, but she proved them wrong.

Synset Examples

The crowd jeered at the speaker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to make fun of or ridicule someone or something
  • Synonym: jeer - to make rude and mocking remarks, typically in a loud voice
  • Antonym: praise - to express warm approval or admiration of someone or something

Etymology

From Middle English scoffen, probably from Middle Dutch *schoffen* (to scoff).

2. VERB

/ skɑf / (AmE), / skɒf / (BrE)
to speak about or treat something with contempt or ridicule; to mock or dismiss with scorn
treat with contempt or mockery

Examples

Many critics scoff at the idea that this technology will change the world.
He scoffed when I suggested they might win the championship this year.
She tends to scoff at traditions she doesn't understand.

Synset Examples

flout the rules

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to make fun of someone or something
  • Synonym: to flout - to openly disregard or show contempt for something

Etymology

From Middle English 'scoffen', probably of Scandinavian origin; related to Old Norse 'skopa' (mockery).

Synset POS: v
