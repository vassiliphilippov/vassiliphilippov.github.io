Phyllocladaceae

1. NOUN

/ ˌfɪloʊˌklædəˈsiː.i / (AmE), / ˌfɪləkˌlædəˈsiː.i / (BrE)
a small family of evergreen coniferous trees and shrubs, primarily found in the southern hemisphere, characterized by flattened, leaf-like branches (phylloclades).
family of evergreen coniferous trees and shrubs
medical

Examples

The Phyllocladaceae are native to regions of the southern hemisphere such as Australia and New Zealand.
Botanists classify certain conifers within the family Phyllocladaceae due to their unique leaf structures.
Research into Phyllocladaceae has helped to illuminate evolutionary adaptations among southern conifers.

Etymology

From New Latin, based on phylloclade (a flattened stem resembling a leaf) + -aceae (family suffix in botany).

2. NOUN

/ ˌfɪləˌklædəˈsiː.i / (AmE), / ˌfɪləkˌlædəˈsiː.i / (BrE)
a family of chiefly southern hemisphere evergreen trees and shrubs, characterized by branchlets that resemble leaves; in the order Ranunculales.
a family of evergreen trees and shrubs (botany)

Examples

The Phyllocladaceae are found primarily in Australia, New Zealand, and nearby islands.
Scientists classify the genus Phyllocladus within the Phyllocladaceae family.
Several members of Phyllocladaceae have unique leaf-like branches called phylloclades.

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Phyllocladus (the type genus), from Greek 'phyllon' (leaf) and 'klados' (branch).
