kinetic

1. ADJ

/ kəˈnɛtɪk, kaɪˈnɛtɪk / (AmE), / kɪˈnɛtɪk, kaɪˈnɛtɪk / (BrE)
Producing or relating to motion; supplying or capable of supplying motive force.
supplying motive force; relating to motion

Examples

The city became the kinetic force behind the country's cultural renaissance.
His kinetic leadership energized the entire organization.
The new policy served as a kinetic center for economic development.

Synset Examples

the complex civilization of which Rome was the kinetic center

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dynamic - characterized by constant change or activity; energetic

Etymology

From Greek kinetikos (pertaining to motion), from kinēin (to move).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00812733-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ kəˈnɛtɪk, kaɪˈnɛtɪk / (AmE), / kɪˈnɛtɪk / (BrE)
relating to or resulting from motion; characterized by movement.
relating to motion

Examples

The kinetic energy of the dancer captivated the audience throughout the performance.
Her paintings have a kinetic quality, seeming almost to move on the canvas.
The film's kinetic pace kept viewers on the edge of their seats from start to finish.

Synset Examples

modern dance has been called kinetic pantomime

Related Terms

  • Antonym: static - lacking in movement, action, or change

Etymology

From Greek kinetikos (relating to movement), from kinētos (movable), from kinein (to move).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01567742-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ kɪˈnɛtɪk / (AmE), / kɪˈnɛtɪk / (BrE)
relating to or resulting from motion or the movement of objects and the forces involved
relating to motion or movement

Examples

The scientist explained how kinetic energy increases as an object's speed increases.
The new sculpture captures the feeling of kinetic movement, as if it's in perpetual motion.
Engineers must consider kinetic forces when designing bridges that withstand earthquakes.

Synset Examples

kinetic energy

Related Terms

  • Antonym: static - lacking in movement, action, or change, especially in a way viewed as undesirable

Etymology

From Greek kīnētikos (of motion), from kīnein (to move).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02905079-a
