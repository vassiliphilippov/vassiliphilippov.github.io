Camelidae

1. NOUN

/ kəˈmɛlɪˌdi / (AmE), / kəˈmiːlɪˌdiː / (BrE)
the biological family comprising camels, llamas, alpacas, vicuñas, and guanacos
the camel family of mammals

Examples

The Camelidae are well adapted to arid regions across South America and Asia.
Scientists study the evolution of Camelidae to better understand their unique physiological traits.
Llamas and alpacas, both members of the Camelidae family, are important to Andean cultures.

Etymology

Modern Latin, from Latin 'camelus' (camel), after Greek 'kamēlos'.

2. NOUN

/ kəˈmɛləˌdiː / (AmE), / kəˈmiːlɪˌdiː / (BrE)
the biological family containing camels, llamas, vicuñas, alpacas, and guanacos, characterized by their long legs and adaptations to arid environments
biological family of camels and their relatives

Examples

The Camelidae are known for their ability to thrive in harsh climates, such as deserts and high mountains.
Members of the Camelidae include the domesticated camel, llama, and alpaca.
Scientists study the evolution of the Camelidae to understand adaptation to extreme environments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: camel - large, domesticated animal with a humped back
  • Synonym: llama - domesticated South American member of Camelidae

Etymology

From Latin "Camelus" (camel) + "-idae" (a standard zoological suffix denoting a family).
