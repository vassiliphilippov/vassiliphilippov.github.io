mood

1. NOUN

/ mud / (AmE), / muːd / (BrE)
a temporary state of mind or feeling, especially as expressed in behaviour or emotions
state of mind or feeling

Examples

She was in a cheerful mood after receiving the good news.
The rainy weather put everyone in a gloomy mood.
He apologized for his bad mood yesterday and promised it wouldn't happen again.

Synset Examples

whether he praised or cursed me depended on his temper at the time
he was in a bad humor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: temper - a person's state of mind seen in terms of being angry or calm
  • Synonym: humor - a mood or state of mind; disposition
  • Degree Higher: cheerful - noticeably happy and optimistic
  • Degree Lower: depressed - in a state of general unhappiness or despondency

Etymology

From Old English mōd (mind, spirit, courage), related to Dutch moed and German Mut.

2. NOUN

/ mud / (AmE), / muːd / (BrE)
the general emotional feeling or psychological atmosphere of a person, group, or situation at a particular time
general emotional state or atmosphere

Examples

The country's political mood was tense following the sudden resignation of the prime minister.
Her cheerful mood lifted the spirits of everyone in the office.
There was a festive mood in the city as people celebrated the holiday.

Synset Examples

the climate of opinion
the national mood had changed radically since the last election

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atmosphere - the pervading tone or mood of a place, situation, or creative work
  • Synonym: spirit - the prevailing or typical quality, mood, or attitude of a person, group, or period

Etymology

From Old English 'mōd' (spirit, feeling, heart), of Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ mud / (AmE), / muːd / (BrE)
(Grammar) a category of verb forms that expresses the attitude of the speaker toward the action or state, such as whether it is a statement, command, wish, or possibility.
grammatical form of verbs expressing attitude

Examples

In the sentence 'If I were rich, I would travel,' the verb 'were' is in the subjunctive mood.
The imperative mood is used to give commands or requests.
Different languages express the conditional mood in various ways.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tense - time expressed by verb inflection (past, present, future, etc.)
  • Synonym: voice - grammatical category expressing the relation between the verb's action and its participants (active, passive, etc.)

Etymology

From Latin *modus* (manner, measure, mode).

4. NOUN

/ mud / (AmE), / muːd / (BrE)
a temporary state of mind or feeling, especially when it is negative or noticeably different from the usual
temporary state of mind or feeling

Examples

She was in a terrible mood after failing her exam.
His mood seemed darker than usual this morning.
Don't take it personally; he's just in a bad mood today.

Synset Examples

His mood was terr after hearing the bad news.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: temper - a person's state of mind seen in terms of their being angry or calm
  • Synonym: emotion - a strong feeling deriving from one's mood or circumstances
  • Degree Higher: depression - a long-lasting state of negative mood and low energy

Etymology

From Old English ‘mōd’ (mind, spirit, courage), related to Old High German ‘muot’.
