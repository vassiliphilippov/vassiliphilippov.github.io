noose

1. NOUN

/ nus / (AmE), / nuːs / (BrE)
a loop of rope or cord, typically with a running knot, used as a trap for birds or small mammals
looped trap for animals

Examples

The hunter set a noose in the undergrowth to catch rabbits.
A simple noose can be made from a piece of strong string.
Several birds were found caught in a hidden noose.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snare - a device for catching birds or small animals, typically involving a noose of wire or cord

Etymology

From Middle English nose, from Old French nos, from Latin nodus (knot).

2. NOUN

/ nus / (AmE), / nuːs / (BrE)
a loop formed in a rope or cord by means of a slipknot, which tightens as the rope is pulled, traditionally used for catching animals or for hanging.
loop in a rope that tightens when pulled

Examples

The hangman tied the noose carefully before the execution.
She made a noose to trap the wild rabbit in the forest.
The sailor used a noose to secure the boat to the dock.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lasso - a rope with a loop used to catch animals

Etymology

From Middle English nose, noose, from Old French nos, from Latin nodus (knot).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ nus / (AmE), / nuːs / (BrE)
to make a loop with a running knot, especially in a rope, typically to catch or secure something
make a loop with a knot, esp. in rope

Examples

He carefully noosed the rope around the calf's neck to keep it from running away.
The fisherman expertly noosed the line to trap the eel.
Before starting the trick, she noosed the end of the cord just as she had practiced.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loop - form a shape that bends around and crosses itself

Etymology

From Middle English nose, from Old French nos, related to Old Provençal nous (knot, tie).

4. VERB

/ nus / (AmE), / nuːs / (BrE)
to fasten or catch with a looped rope (noose), especially to secure or trap someone or something.
to fasten or catch with a noose

Examples

The wrangler skillfully noosed the runaway calf before it escaped.
He noosed the rope around the criminal's wrists to restrain him.
Fishermen sometimes noose crabs to haul them out of their hiding places.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lasso - to catch with a rope loop (lasso)

Etymology

From Middle English nose, related to Old French nous (knot), ultimately of Germanic origin.
