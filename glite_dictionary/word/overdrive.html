overdrive

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈdraɪv / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdraɪv / (BrE)
to make someone or something work too hard or beyond normal limits; to force to perform at an excessive rate or intensity
to drive or work too hard

Examples

If you overdrive your employees, their productivity may actually decrease.
She tends to overdrive herself and ends up exhausted at the end of the week.
The manager was accused of overdriving his team to meet unrealistic deadlines.

Synset Examples

Overdriving people often suffer stress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overwork - to work too hard or cause someone to work too hard
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02413660-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvɚˌdraɪv / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəˌdraɪv / (BrE)
a mechanism in a vehicle's transmission that allows operation at high speed with reduced engine power and lower fuel consumption
mechanism allowing high-speed driving with less engine power

Examples

With the overdrive engaged, the engine ran more quietly on the highway.
He shifted into overdrive to save fuel during the long trip.
The car's overdrive feature helps maintain speed without overworking the engine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gear - a toothed wheel in a machine that works with others to change speed or direction

Etymology

From 'over-' (above, beyond) + 'drive', referring to driving the output shaft faster than the engine shaft.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03869303-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌdraɪv / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəˌdraɪv / (BrE)
a state of extremely high or excessive activity, effort, or concentration
state of excessive activity or focus

Examples

As the deadline approached, the whole team went into overdrive to finish the project.
Her imagination was in overdrive after watching the suspenseful movie.
With preparations for the event in full swing, the staff were working in overdrive.

Synset Examples

Troops are ready to go into overdrive as soon as the signal is given
Melissa's brain was in overdrive

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hyperdrive - a state of greatly accelerated activity or effort

Etymology

Originally from the technical term in vehicles, meaning a gear that allows a car to go faster with less effort; later used figuratively.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14032505-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈdraɪv / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdraɪv / (BrE)
to use something too often or too extensively; to overuse or exhaust through excessive application
to use excessively or overuse

Examples

The company overdrove its marketing strategy, irritating potential customers with too many advertisements.
If you overdrive your employees, they may become fatigued and less productive.
He tends to overdrive his point in arguments, repeating the same facts unnecessarily.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overuse - to use something too much
  • Antonym: underuse - to use something too little or infrequently
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01164011-v
Synset POS: v
