beak

1. NOUN

/ bik / (AmE), / biːk / (BrE)
(informal) a person's nose, especially if large or prominent.
informal: a person's nose
informal

Examples

He broke his beak in a fight outside the pub.
She laughed so hard her beak turned red.
That guy has a real Roman beak on him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nose - the part of the face used for smelling and breathing

2. NOUN

/ bik / (AmE), / biːk / (BrE)
the hard, pointed, projecting mouthpart of a bird, used for eating and other functions
hard pointed mouthpart of a bird

Examples

The eagle used its sharp beak to tear the meat from its prey.
Parrots have a curved beak which helps them crack nuts and seeds.
The robin wiped its beak on a branch after catching an insect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bill - the beak of a bird, especially a web-footed one

Etymology

From Old French bec, from Latin beccus, meaning 'beak' or 'bill.'

3. NOUN

/ bik / (AmE), / biːk / (BrE)
the hard, beaklike mouthpart of animals other than birds, such as turtles or some mollusks
beaklike mouthpart of non-bird animals

Examples

The snapping turtle has a powerful beak to catch its prey.
A squid's beak is strong enough to break through shells.
Some mollusks use their sharp beak to tear food apart.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bill - beak of a bird; also used for some animals

4. NOUN

/ biːk / (AmE), / biːk / (BrE)
a beaklike, tapering tip found on certain plant structures, such as fruits, seeds, or leaves
beaklike tip of a plant part

Examples

The seed pod narrows into a distinctive beak at the tip.
Some grasses produce grains with a slender beak at one end.
The fruit's beak helps with dispersal by attaching to animal fur.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rostrum - a beaklike projection, especially in animals or plants

Etymology

From Old French bec, from Latin beccus (beak).
