fawn

1. VERB

/ fɔn / (AmE), / fɔːn / (BrE)
to behave in an excessively flattering or submissive manner in order to gain favor or approval
to flatter or behave submissively

Examples

The employee would fawn over the manager whenever she entered the room.
Politicians sometimes fawn on influential donors to secure their support.
He fawned on his superiors in hopes of getting a promotion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to flatter - to praise excessively, often insincerely
  • Synonym: to grovel - to act in an obsequious or self-abasing manner
  • Antonym: insult - to speak or act with disrespect

Etymology

From Old English *fagnian* (to rejoice), related to Old French *faon* (young animal, fawn) as a root for the noun sense but distinct in the verb's usage.

2. VERB

/ fɔn / (AmE), / fɔːn / (BrE)
to try to gain favor or approval by behaving in an excessively flattering or submissive manner
flatter excessively or submissively

Examples

She tends to fawn over her supervisors, hoping for a promotion.
Instead of honest feedback, he would fawn on anyone in authority.
You don't have to fawn to succeed; genuine effort is more valuable.

Synset Examples

He is always kowtowing to his boss

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to flatter - to praise someone excessively, often insincerely
  • Synonym: kowtow - to act in an excessively submissive or servile manner

Etymology

From Old English *fagnian* (to rejoice), later influenced by the noun 'fawn' (young deer) due to perceived meekness.

3. NOUN

/ fɔn / (AmE), / fɔːn / (BrE)
a young deer, especially one that is less than a year old
a young deer

Examples

We spotted a fawn resting in the tall grass near the forest edge.
The fawn followed its mother closely as they crossed the field.
A frightened fawn darted away as we approached the stream.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: deer - a hoofed grazing or browsing animal, of the family Cervidae

Etymology

From Old French *faon* (young animal), from Latin *fetus* (offspring, fetus).
