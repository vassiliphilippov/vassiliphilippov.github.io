clown

1. NOUN

/ klaʊn / (AmE), / klaʊn / (BrE)
A person who behaves in a foolish, rude, or vulgar way; a fool or buffoon.
a rude or vulgar fool

Examples

He acted like a total clown during the meeting, making offensive jokes.
Nobody takes him seriously because he's known as the office clown.
Don't be such a clown; show some respect for the occasion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buffoon - a ridiculous but amusing person; a clown
  • Synonym: fool - a person who acts unwisely or imprudently

Etymology

From French *clouen*, related to dialectal English *clowne* (rustic, boorish person).

2. NOUN

/ klaʊn / (AmE), / klaʊn / (BrE)
a person who entertains others by behaving in a comical or silly way, often in a circus
comic entertainer, often in a circus

Examples

The clown made the children laugh with his silly tricks and bright costume.
Some people are afraid of clown performances at the circus.
He acted like a clown during the meeting, making everyone laugh.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jester - a historical figure who entertained at royal courts by joking and clowning

Etymology

Originally from Medieval Latin *colonus* or Italian *colonna*, but adopted in English in the late 16th century referring to a rustic or peasant, later to comic entertainers.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09950334-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ klaʊn / (AmE), / klaʊn / (BrE)
to act in a playful or silly way like a clown, often to entertain others
act playfully or foolishly

Examples

Stop clowning around and help me with this project.
He was clowning in class to make his friends laugh.
The kids clowned for hours at the birthday party.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fool around - to behave in a silly or playful way
  • Synonym: joke - to say or do things to make people laugh
  • Antonym: behave seriously - to act in a serious or earnest way

Etymology

From Middle English *clowne* (rustic, boor), later used for a stage comic performer.
