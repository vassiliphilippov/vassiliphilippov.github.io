habituation

1. NOUN

/ həˌbɪtʃuˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / həˌbɪtjʊˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of becoming accustomed to a stimulus or environment after repeated exposure, leading to a decrease in response
decreased response from repeated exposure

Examples

After several nights living near the railway, his habituation to the noise meant he hardly noticed the trains anymore.
The animal’s habituation to the presence of humans made it less likely to flee when approached.
Through repeated exposure, habituation can reduce a person's sensitivity to persistent background sounds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adaptation - the process of adjusting to new conditions

Etymology

From Latin habitūtiō (customary practice), from habitus (habit).

2. NOUN

/ həˌbɪt͡ʃuˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / həˌbɪtʃuˈeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the process of becoming abnormally tolerant to and dependent on a habit-forming substance, such as alcohol or drugs
becoming dependent on habit-forming substances
medical

Examples

After years of alcohol abuse, his habituation made it nearly impossible for him to quit.
Medical professionals monitor patients for signs of habituation when prescribing strong painkillers.
Long-term habituation to narcotics can have severe health consequences.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: addiction - the condition of being unable to stop taking a harmful substance
  • Synonym: dependence - the state of relying on or being controlled by something, especially a drug
  • Antonym: sobriety - the state of not being affected by alcohol or drugs

Etymology

From Latin habitus (habit, condition) + -ation (forming nouns of action or process).
