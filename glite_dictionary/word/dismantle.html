to dismantle

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈmæntl̩ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmæntl̩ / (BrE)
to demolish or tear down a structure completely, reducing it to ground level
tear down or demolish completely

Examples

The old factory was dismantled to make room for new apartments.
They plan to dismantle the stadium next year after its final event.
After standing for a century, the monument was finally dismantled by the city council.

Synset Examples

The building was levelled

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to demolish - to completely destroy a building or structure
  • Antonym: construct - to build or put together a structure

Etymology

From French 'dismanteler', from 'des-' (reverse) and 'manteler' (to cloak or cover).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01665698-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈmæntəl / (AmE), / dɪsˈmæntəl / (BrE)
to take something apart so that it is in separate pieces
take apart into pieces

Examples

The workers began to dismantle the old factory equipment piece by piece.
Before moving, we had to dismantle the bookshelf to fit it through the door.
The committee voted to dismantle the outdated system and replace it with a new one.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to assemble - to put together the parts of something
  • Synonym: to disassemble - to take something apart into separate pieces

Etymology

From French 'desmanteler', from 'des-' (dis-) + 'manteler' (to cloak, cover), originally meaning 'to strip of a cloak'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01661660-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈmæntl̩ / (AmE), / dɪsˈmæn.tl̩ / (BrE)
to remove coverings or equipment from something, especially as part of taking it apart or rendering it unusable
to remove coverings or equipment

Examples

They had to dismantle the stage before the next event could begin.
Workers will dismantle the old signs from the building this afternoon.
She carefully dismantled the electronics, removing each part one by one.

Synset Examples

strip a wall of its wallpaper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strip - to remove covering, clothing, or equipment from something

Etymology

From Old French mastur (to strip, take apart), probably from mantle (cloak).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00194878-v
Synset POS: v
