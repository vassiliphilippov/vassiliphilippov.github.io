to rusticate

1. VERB

/ ˈrʌstɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrʌstɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to send someone to the countryside, especially as a punishment or to remove them from a particular environment
send to the countryside

Examples

After causing trouble at school, he was rusticated to his aunt’s farm for the summer.
The university decided to rusticate the student for repeated violations.
In Victorian times, families would sometimes rusticate their children to the countryside for health reasons.

Synset Examples

He was rusticated for his bad behavior

Etymology

From Latin 'rusticari' (to live in the country), from 'rusticus' (rural).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02510065-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈrʌstɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrʌstɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to give (stone) a roughened or rustic appearance, especially by making the surfaces uneven or deeply cut, as in architectural work
give stone a rustic or roughened look

Examples

The architect decided to rusticate the lower level of the mansion to create a dramatic visual contrast.
Masons meticulously rusticated the stone blocks to achieve the desired rugged texture for the façade.
Some buildings rusticate their ground floors to evoke the style of Renaissance palazzos.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dress - to give a smooth finish to a stone surface in masonry

Etymology

From Latin rusticatus, past participle of rusticari (to live in the country), from rusticus (rural).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01558108-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈrʌstɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrʌstɪkeɪt / (BrE)
(Britain, historical) To suspend temporarily from a college or university, especially as a punishment.
temporarily suspend from a university (Britain)
british archaic

Examples

After being caught cheating on his exams, the university decided to rusticate him for a term.
Several students were rusticated for their involvement in the protest.
If you break the rules again, the college may have no choice but to rusticate you.

Etymology

From Latin rusticari (to live in the country), by extension to being sent away from college (often to the country).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈrʌstɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrʌstɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to give a building, surface, or space a rustic or country-like appearance, especially by using rough stone or similar materials
give a rustic appearance

Examples

They decided to rusticate the kitchen by exposing the original wooden beams.
The architect plans to rusticate the facade with stone cladding for a country cottage effect.
Many homeowners choose to rusticate their patios to create a warm, inviting space.

Synset Examples

rusticate the house in the country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to decorate - to make something look more attractive by adding items or color
  • Antonym: to modernize - to make more modern in appearance or character

Etymology

From Latin rusticatus, past participle of rusticari (to live in the country), from rusticus (rural, rustic).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00536749-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ ˈrʌstəˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈrʌstɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to live in the countryside, often adopting a simple, rustic lifestyle
live in the countryside; adopt a rustic life

Examples

After decades in the city, she decided to rusticate on her family's countryside estate.
Some artists rusticate during the summer to find inspiration for their work.
They rusticated for a year to escape the chaos of urban life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to retire - to withdraw from active life, especially to the country
  • Antonym: to urbanize - to make or become urban; adopt urban ways

Etymology

From Latin rusticari (to live in the country), from rusticus (rural).
