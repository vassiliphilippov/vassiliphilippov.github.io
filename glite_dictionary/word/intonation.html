intonation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.toʊˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the rise and fall in the pitch of the voice when speaking, especially as it affects meaning or expression
variation in pitch during speech

Examples

English questions often have a rising intonation at the end of the sentence.
She practiced her speech, paying careful attention to intonation to keep the audience interested.
You can change the meaning of a sentence by simply altering your intonation.

Etymology

From French *intonation*, from Late Latin *intonatio* (“a singing or sounding upon; intonation”).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.toʊˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the accuracy or exactness of pitch in producing musical tones by voice or instrument
exactness of pitch in music

Examples

The violinist was praised for her perfect intonation during the concert.
Poor intonation can ruin even the most beautiful melody.
Voice teachers often focus on improving students' intonation to achieve precise notes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pitch - the degree of highness or lowness of a musical note
  • Antonym: out of tune - not having or producing correct musical pitch

Etymology

From Latin 'intonare' (to thunder, sound), via French 'intonation'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.toʊˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təʊˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
The act of singing or speaking in a continuous, monotonous tone without variation in pitch.
singing in a monotonous tone

Examples

His intonation while chanting was flat and lacked variety.
The intonation of the choir was criticized for being dull and repetitive.
Meditation practices sometimes include intonation of mantras in a steady tone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monotone - a succession of sounds or words uttered in a single tone of voice

Etymology

From Latin 'intonare' (to thunder, utter loudly), via French 'intonation'.

4. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.toʊˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the singing by a soloist of the opening phrase or piece of plainsong in church music
opening chant sung solo in plainsong

Examples

The priest delivered the intonation before the choir joined in the chant.
During the medieval service, the intonation set the tone for the entire plainsong.
Her clear intonation of the opening phrase was met with reverent silence from the congregation.

Etymology

From Latin 'intonare' (to thunder, sing, or sound), via French 'intonation'.
