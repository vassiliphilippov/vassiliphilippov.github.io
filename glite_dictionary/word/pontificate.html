pontificate

1. VERB

/ pɑnˈtɪfɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / pɒnˈtɪfɪˌkeɪt / (BrE)
To carry out the duties or office of a pontiff, especially as Pope or bishop in the Roman Catholic Church.
hold the office of a pontiff

Examples

After being elected, he began to pontificate over the Roman Church with great solemnity.
The council gathered to witness the new pope pontificate on Easter Sunday.
For over twenty years, the bishop pontificated in the diocese, faithfully performing all his duties.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pope - the bishop of Rome, head of the Roman Catholic Church

Etymology

From Latin 'pontificatus', past participle of 'pontificare' (to perform priestly duties), from 'pontifex' (priest).

2. NOUN

/ pɑnˈtɪfɪkət / (AmE), / pɒnˈtɪfɪkət / (BrE)
The government or office of the pope, especially as the head of the Roman Catholic Church.
papal government or office

Examples

The pontificate of Pope Urban II was marked by the launch of the First Crusade.
Throughout his pontificate, the pope emphasized social justice and reform.
Historians debate the influence of the pontificate on European politics during the Renaissance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: papacy - the office or authority of the pope

Etymology

From Latin 'pontificatus', meaning 'the office of a pontiff'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08070012-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ pɑnˈtɪfɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / pɒnˈtɪfɪkeɪt / (BrE)
to speak or express opinions in a pompous or dogmatic way, often as if one is always right
speak pompously or dogmatically

Examples

The manager tends to pontificate at meetings, rarely listening to other viewpoints.
During the debate, he began to pontificate about issues he barely understood.
She often pontificates on politics even though her knowledge is limited.

Synset Examples

The new professor always pontificates

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preach - to give advice or express opinions in a way that shows one thinks they are right and others are wrong
  • Synonym: lecture - to talk to someone in a way that shows you think you are right and they are wrong

Etymology

From Latin pontificatus, past participle of pontificare (to perform priestly duties), later associated with pontiff (pope) and extended metaphorically.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
