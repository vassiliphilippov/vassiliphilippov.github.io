mineral

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɪnɚəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnərəl / (BrE)
Relating to or derived from minerals.
relating to minerals

Examples

The scientist studied the mineral content of the soil.
She bought a face mask with mineral ingredients for healthier skin.
Mining companies search for mineral resources in remote areas.

Synset Examples

mineral elements
mineral deposits

Etymology

From Medieval Latin mineralis, from minera (mine).

WordNet ID: oewn-03098402-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈmɪnərəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnərəl / (BrE)
(of substances or materials) composed of matter that is not derived from plants or animals; inorganic.
inorganic; not derived from living things

Examples

Coal is not considered a mineral material because it comes from plant remains.
The geologist studied the mineral components of the sediment.
Deserts are often characterized by mineral soils with little organic matter.

Synset Examples

the inorganic mineral world

Related Terms

  • Antonym: organic - relating to or derived from living matter

Etymology

From Medieval Latin minera (mine).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01685218-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɪnərəl / (AmE), / ˈmɪnərəl / (BrE)
a naturally occurring solid substance that is not organic and has a definite chemical composition and structure
naturally occurring inorganic solid

Examples

Quartz is a common mineral found in many types of rocks.
The geologist identified several rare minerals in the sample.
Water dissolves some minerals from the soil, enriching its content.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rock - a naturally occurring solid aggregate of minerals

Etymology

From Medieval Latin mineralis, meaning 'pertaining to mines,' from minera 'ore.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14686753-n
Synset POS: n
