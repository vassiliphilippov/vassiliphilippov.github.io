to deputise

1. VERB

/ ˈdɛp.juˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɛp.juˌtaɪz / (BrE)
to act or perform duties as a substitute for someone, especially in their official role or capacity
act as a substitute (esp. officially)
british

Examples

When the manager was away, Jane was asked to deputise for him at the meeting.
The teacher needed someone to deputise in her absence the following week.
During the concert, Ben agreed to deputise for the lead guitarist who was ill.

Synset Examples

She stood in for the soprano who suffered from a cold

Related Terms

  • Synonym: substitute - to act or serve in place of another
  • Synonym: delegate - to entrust a task or authority to another

Etymology

From French *deputer* (appoint, delegate), ultimately from Latin *deputare* (to assign).

2. VERB

/ ˈdɛpjəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɛpjʊtʌɪz / (BrE)
to appoint someone to act as a deputy or substitute for oneself or another person
appoint as a deputy or substitute
british

Examples

The manager will deputise Jane to lead the team during his absence.
She was asked to deputise for the principal at the school event.
The committee may deputise someone to act on its behalf if needed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deputize - appoint as a substitute (American spelling)

Etymology

From Medieval Latin deputare (to assign, to appoint), via French 'députer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02399587-v
Synset POS: v
