stampede

1. NOUN

/ stæmˈpid / (AmE), / stæmˈpiːd / (BrE)
A sudden, panicked rush of a herd of animals, usually as a result of fear or excitement.
wild rush of panicked animals

Examples

There was a stampede when the cattle were startled by a loud noise.
A sudden movement in the herd triggered a stampede across the plain.
The cowboys managed to stop the stampede just in time to prevent injuries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rout - A disorderly or chaotic retreat, especially of a crowd or army
Source: WordNet

2. NOUN

/ stæmˈpid / (AmE), / stæmˈpiːd / (BrE)
a sudden and uncontrolled rush of a crowd, often triggered by panic or excitement
sudden rush of a crowd

Examples

The crowd began to stampede when the fire alarm rang.
There was a desperate stampede towards the exits after the announcement.
A rumor in the marketplace triggered a stampede of shoppers.

Synset Examples

when he shouted ‘fire’ there was a stampede to the exits

Related Terms

  • Synonym: panic - sudden uncontrollable fear or anxiety
  • Antonym: calm - state of tranquility or peace, absence of panic

Etymology

From Spanish 'estampida' (crash, uproar).

3. VERB

/ stæmˈpid / (AmE), / stæmˈpiːd / (BrE)
to cause (a group of animals or people) to run suddenly in panic
cause to run in panic

Examples

A loud noise may stampede a herd of cattle across the field.
The sudden movement in the crowd could stampede the children if not controlled.
The fire alarm was enough to stampede the shoppers toward the exits.

Synset Examples

Thunderbolts can stampede animals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: panic - to suddenly become very afraid and unable to think clearly

Etymology

American English, probably from Spanish 'estampida' (crash, uproar).

4. VERB

/ stæmˈpid / (AmE), / stæmˈpiːd / (BrE)
to move or act together suddenly and hurriedly, often in response to excitement, fear, or impulse
move or act suddenly in a group

Examples

At the first hint of a sale, shoppers stampede into the store to grab bargains.
Investors began to stampede out of the market as panic spread.
When the concert doors opened, fans stampeded towards the stage.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - to move quickly and energetically
  • Antonym: retreat - to move away quickly, often in fear or danger

Etymology

From American Spanish 'estampida' (crash, uproar), related to Spanish 'estampar' (to stamp).

6. VERB

/ stæmˈpid / (AmE), / stæmˈpiːd / (BrE)
(of people or animals) to move suddenly and quickly in a group, often in panic or fear, usually away from something.
to run suddenly in a group, often in panic

Examples

When the fire alarm sounded, the crowd began to stampede toward the nearest exits.
Wild horses will stampede if startled by a loud noise.
The fans nearly stampeded onto the field after the winning goal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scatter - to move quickly in different directions

Etymology

From American Spanish 'estampida' (crash, uproar), from Spanish 'estampido' (crash, report).
