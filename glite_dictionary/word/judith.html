Judith

1. PROPN

/ ˈdʒuːdɪθ / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdɪθ / (BrE)
A Jewish heroine featured in one of the books of the Apocrypha, known for saving her people by beheading the Assyrian general Holofernes.
Jewish heroine in the Apocrypha

Examples

The story of Judith is celebrated in art and literature for her bravery.
Many paintings depict Judith holding the head of Holofernes, symbolizing triumph over tyranny.
In religious studies, Judith is often discussed as an example of faith and courage.

Etymology

From Hebrew Yehudit, meaning 'woman from Judea'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒuːdɪθ / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdɪθ / (BrE)
A book of the Apocrypha that tells the story of Judith, who saves her people from the Assyrians through courage and strategy.
apocryphal book about Judith saving her people

Examples

The story of Judith is often cited as an example of bravery in biblical literature.
Many Bible editions include the Book of Judith among the Apocryphal books.
In Judith, the heroine outwits the Assyrian general Holofernes to save her city.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Apocrypha - biblical or related writings not forming part of the accepted canon of Scripture

Etymology

From Hebrew Yəhūḏīṯ, a biblical female name; also the title character in the book.
