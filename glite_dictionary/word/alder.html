alder

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔl.dɚ / (AmE), / ˈɔːl.də / (BrE)
A deciduous tree or shrub of the birch family, found in north temperate regions, with toothed leaves and small, cone-like fruit; noted for its rot-resistant wood and use in tanning and dyeing.
deciduous tree/shrub with cone-like fruit

Examples

Several species of alder grow along the riverbanks, providing shade and shelter for wildlife.
The wood from an alder tree is prized for its resistance to decay in wet conditions.
Native Americans used alder bark to create natural dyes and medicines.

Etymology

From Old English *alor*, related to German *Erle* and Latin *alnus*.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɔldɚ / (AmE), / ˈɔːldə / (BrE)
The wood from any of several alder trees, known for its resistance to underwater decay and commonly used in construction, especially for bridges and piling.
wood from an alder tree

Examples

The old dock was supported by alder pilings that had lasted for centuries under water.
Furniture made from alder is prized for its fine grain and durability.
Traditional Venetian buildings often rest on alder foundations to withstand the moisture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oak - wood from an oak tree, hardwood used in construction and furniture
  • Synonym: willow - wood from a willow tree, used for similar purposes

Etymology

From Old English 'alor', related to Old High German 'elira' and Latin 'alnus'.
