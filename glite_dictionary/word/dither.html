dither

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɪðər / (AmE), / ˈdɪðə / (BrE)
A state of nervous excitement, agitation, or indecisiveness.
state of nervous agitation or indecision

Examples

She was in a complete dither before her job interview.
The unexpected announcement sent the office into a dither.
Don't get yourself into a dither over things you can't control.

Synset Examples

he was in a dither
there was a terrible flap about the theft

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flap - state of panic or agitation
  • Antonym: calm - absence of nervousness or agitation

Etymology

Originated in late 18th century dialect, probably related to 'didder' (tremble, quake).

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪðɚ / (AmE), / ˈdɪðə / (BrE)
to be indecisive and unable to make up one's mind, often fussing or showing agitation
be indecisive or agitated

Examples

She tends to dither over simple decisions, making it hard to get anything done.
Don't dither when it's your turn to order—just pick something.
He dithered all morning about whether to attend the meeting or not.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to hesitate - pause before acting due to indecision
  • Antonym: to decide - to make a final choice

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'dedderen' (to tremble) or related to 'didder'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01823849-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdɪðɚ / (AmE), / ˈdɪðə / (BrE)
to be indecisive or act nervously, especially when unable to make a decision quickly
be indecisive; act nervously

Examples

She tends to dither for ages before making even simple choices.
Don't dither—just pick a restaurant so we can eat!
He dithered over the job offer and nearly missed his chance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to hesitate - pause before doing something, usually because of uncertainty
  • Antonym: to decide - to make up one's mind about something

Etymology

Of imitative origin, possibly related to dialectal 'didder' meaning 'to tremble.'

Source: WordNet
