perforation

1. NOUN

/ ˌpɝːfəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpɜːfəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
a row of small holes punched or cut in paper or other material, usually to make tearing easier along that line
line of small holes for easy tearing

Examples

Please fold the paper along the perforation before tearing it off the pad.
The booklet features a perforation so you can easily remove coupons.
Tickets are easier to distribute when they have a clear perforation between each section.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tear-off - able to be torn off along a line

Etymology

From Latin 'perforare' (to bore through), via Medieval Latin 'perforatio'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌpɝːfəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌpɜːfəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act or process of making a hole or a series of holes in something, especially to make separation easier
making holes for separation

Examples

Tear along the perforation to separate the two tickets easily.
The stamps were connected by a row of tiny perforations.
The technician checked the perforation to ensure the sheets would break apart cleanly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hole - an opening through something

Etymology

From Latin per- (through) + forare (to bore).

3. NOUN

/ ˌpɝː.fəˈreɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌpɜː.fəˈreɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a hole or series of holes made in a material or structure, especially deliberately or as a result of injury or wear
a hole made in something

Examples

The doctor discovered a perforation in the patient's colon during surgery.
He made a small perforation in the paper to help it tear evenly.
Repeated stress can eventually cause perforation of thin metal sheets.

Synset Examples

a perforation of the eardrum

Related Terms

  • Synonym: puncture - a small hole made by a sharp object, typically accidentally
  • Synonym: tear - a rip or split in something, often accidental
  • Antonym: obstruction - the state of being blocked; opposite of a hole or opening

Etymology

From Latin perforatio (a piercing through), from perforare (to pierce through).
