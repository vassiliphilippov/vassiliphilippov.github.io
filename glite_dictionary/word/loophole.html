loophole

1. NOUN

/ ˈluːˌpoʊl / (AmE), / ˈluːphəʊl / (BrE)
a narrow opening in a fortification, such as a wall, used for observation or for firing weapons
narrow opening in a fortification

Examples

The soldiers watched the enemy's approach through a loophole in the castle wall.
Each loophole was strategically placed to give a wide field of fire.
He peered out from the loophole, scanning the horizon for any sign of movement.

Etymology

From Middle English 'lophole', possibly from 'loop' (narrow aperture) + 'hole'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈluːˌpoʊl / (AmE), / ˈluːpəʊl / (BrE)
an ambiguity or omission in a law, rule, or contract that allows people to avoid following it as intended
ambiguity in rules enabling evasion

Examples

The companies exploited a loophole in the tax law to reduce their payments.
Lawyers found a loophole that allowed their client to avoid prosecution.
New legislation was introduced to close the loophole in the existing regulations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: technicality - a minor detail in the law that allows avoidance of the general intent

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *lupen* (to watch) + *hole*; originally meant a narrow window in a wall for observing or shooting.
