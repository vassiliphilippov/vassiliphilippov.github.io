stodgy

1. ADJ

/ ˈstɑdʒi / (AmE), / ˈstɒdʒi / (BrE)
(of food) heavy, starchy, and hard to digest.
heavy and hard-to-digest food

Examples

After a big lunch of stodgy pasta, he felt sleepy all afternoon.
The restaurant is known for its rich, stodgy desserts.
Many people find traditional holiday dishes a bit too stodgy for their taste.

Synset Examples

stodgy food
a stodgy pudding served up when everyone was already full

2. ADJ

/ ˈstɑːdʒi / (AmE), / ˈstɒdʒi / (BrE)
(of a person, attitude, or style) old-fashioned, dull, or resistant to change, often used pejoratively.
old-fashioned or dull (pejorative)

Examples

His stodgy views on music made him seem much older than he was.
The committee's decisions were criticized as being stodgy and out of touch with contemporary needs.
She wanted to modernize the stodgy image of the company.

Synset Examples

moss-grown ideas about family life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: old-fashioned - out of date; not modern
  • Antonym: modern - relating to the present or recent times

Etymology

Probably from dialectal stodgy (stiff, heavy), related to 'stodge' (thick food).

3. ADJ

/ ˈstɑːdʒi / (AmE), / ˈstɒdʒi / (BrE)
(of people, behaviour, or things) dull and overly conventional; lacking originality or excitement.
dull, unimaginative, conventional

Examples

The lecture felt stodgy, offering nothing new to spark interest among the students.
His stodgy attitude made him resistant to any innovative suggestions at work.
They avoided restaurants with a stodgy atmosphere, preferring lively places instead.

Synset Examples

why is the middle class so stodgy, so utterly without a sense of humor?
a stodgy dinner party

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dull - lacking interest or excitement
  • Synonym: uninspired - lacking originality or imagination
  • Antonym: exciting - causing enthusiasm and eagerness

Etymology

Of uncertain origin, possibly from dialectal English 'stodgy' meaning thick or heavy (of food), probably related to 'stod' (to stud, block up).
