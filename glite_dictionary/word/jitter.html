jitter

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɪt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈdʒɪtə / (BrE)
Small rapid variations in a waveform or signal caused by fluctuations in voltage, mechanical vibrations, or other disturbances.
small rapid variations in a signal or waveform
medical

Examples

Audio engineers often try to minimize jitter to preserve sound quality in digital recordings.
Network jitter can cause delays and interruptions during video conferences.
Signal jitter in electronic devices may result from unstable power supplies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: noise - undesired random disturbances in a signal
  • Degree Lower: drift - a gradual deviation in a signal's value over time

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɪt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈdʒɪtə / (BrE)
a small, rapid, and irregular movement or fluctuation, especially in physical systems or electronic signals
small, irregular movement or fluctuation

Examples

The video feed suffered from jitter, causing the image to shake slightly.
Engineers monitor jitter to ensure stable data transmission in networks.
Seismic sensors detected a slight jitter before the main tremor occurred.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: oscillation - regular movement back and forth, especially in physics or engineering
  • Synonym: fluctuation - variation or change, especially in level or amount

Etymology

Perhaps from 'jitters', referring to feelings of nervousness, or influenced by 'chitter' (to tremble or chatter). Exact origin unclear.
