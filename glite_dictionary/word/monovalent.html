monovalent

1. ADJ

/ ˌmɑːnoʊˈveɪlənt / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəˈveɪlənt / (BrE)
(of an antibody or vaccine) effective against or containing only one specific antigen or pathogen; having a single valence.
having a single antibody type or specificity
medical

Examples

The monovalent vaccine targets only one strain of the influenza virus.
Researchers developed a monovalent antibody to fight the specific infection.
A monovalent serum may be less effective if multiple pathogens are present.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: polyvalent - effective against or containing multiple antibodies or antigens

Etymology

From Latin 'mono-' (one) + 'valentia' (power, capacity).

2. ADJ

/ ˌmoʊ.nəˈveɪ.lənt / (AmE), / ˌmɒn.əˈveɪ.lənt / (BrE)
(of a chemical element or group) having a valence of one, able to combine with or replace one atom of hydrogen.
having a valence of one (chemistry)
medical

Examples

The chemist explained that sodium is a monovalent element because it forms only one bond.
This vaccine contains a monovalent antibody targeting a single strain of the virus.
Hydrogen chloride is formed when a monovalent chlorine atom combines with hydrogen.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: divalent - having a valence of two (chemistry)

Etymology

From Latin roots: 'mono-' (one) and 'valentia' (strength, capacity).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01550389-a
Synset POS: a
