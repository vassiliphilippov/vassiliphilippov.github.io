countermarch

1. VERB

/ ˈkaʊntərˌmɑːrtʃ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntəˌmɑːtʃ / (BrE)
(of troops or a group) to march back along the same route by which they came; to turn back while marching in order to return.
turn and march back by the same route

Examples

At the sound of the commander’s whistle, the troops began to countermarch to their original position.
The parade unit practiced how to countermarch in perfect unison for the ceremony.
After reaching the end of the street, the soldiers were ordered to countermarch and retrace their steps.

Etymology

From counter- (against, opposite) + march, imitating French 'contremarche' or Italian 'contromarcia'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkaʊntərˌmɑrtʃ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntəˌmɑːtʃ / (BrE)
(of troops) to change direction or order during a march, especially by turning about and marching back the same way.
change direction/order of marching troops

Examples

The commander ordered the troops to countermarch in response to the unexpected obstacle ahead.
When signaled, the regiment would countermarch swiftly to reposition at the rear.
To confuse the enemy, the soldiers were instructed to countermarch halfway down the street.

Etymology

From counter- (against, in reverse) + march; based on military commands.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00660931-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊn.tɚˌmɑrtʃ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.təˌmɑːtʃ / (BrE)
(military) a maneuver in which troops or units march in the reverse direction or back along the same route.
military maneuver: marching in reverse or same route back

Examples

The commander ordered a countermarch to reposition the soldiers for a defensive line.
As soon as the cavalry realized the enemy's flanking move, they performed a rapid countermarch.
A well-executed countermarch can quickly change the direction and formation of troops in battle.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retreat - a withdrawal of troops from enemy forces or dangerous positions

Etymology

From counter- (against, reverse) + march, from Latin 'contra' (against) and 'marciare' (to march).
