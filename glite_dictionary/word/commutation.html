commutation

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑː.mjuˈteɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒ.mjuˈteɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the act of substituting one thing or person for another
substitution of one thing or person for another

Examples

The commutation of the original leader with a new one brought significant changes to the project.
There was a commutation of the planned materials with less expensive alternatives.
This process involves the commutation of certain chemicals for safer ones.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: substitution - the act of replacing one thing or person with another

Etymology

From Latin 'commutatio' (a change, exchange), from 'commutare' (to change, interchange).

3. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmjʊˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒmjʊˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
an official decision to reduce a criminal's punishment to one that is less severe
reduction of legal punishment
legal

Examples

The governor signed a commutation that reduced the prisoner's sentence from life to twenty years.
Legal counsel requested a commutation of the death penalty in light of new evidence.
She was granted a commutation after serving ten years of her original sentence.

Related Terms

4. NOUN

(law) The action of reducing the severity of a punishment, especially a prison sentence, imposed by law.
reduction of a legal punishment
legal

Examples

The governor granted a commutation of his sentence, reducing it from life imprisonment to twenty years.
She appealed to the president for commutation after spending a decade in prison.
The legal team argued that the harsh penalty warranted commutation due to new evidence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pardon - an official decision to free someone from punishment
  • Synonym: remission - the cancellation or reduction of a punishment or debt

From Latin commutatio (change, exchange).
