infusion

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfjuʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfjuːʒən / (BrE)
a liquid made by soaking or steeping a substance, typically in water, to extract its flavor or active ingredients
liquid made by soaking a substance

Examples

She enjoyed a hot infusion of chamomile before bed.
The herbalist recommended an infusion of mint leaves for an upset stomach.
Prepare an infusion by steeping the dried flowers in boiling water for ten minutes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decoction - a liquid resulting from boiling a substance to extract its essence

Etymology

From Latin infusio, from infundere (to pour in).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfjuʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfjuːʒ(ə)n / (BrE)
the process of extracting active ingredients from a substance (such as a plant or drug) by steeping or soaking it, usually in water
extracting properties by steeping or soaking
medical

Examples

The herbalist prepared an infusion of chamomile flowers to help with relaxation.
A hot water infusion is often used to extract medicinal compounds from roots and leaves.
This infusion should steep for at least ten minutes before drinking.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: decoction - the extraction of substances by boiling
  • Synonym: tincture - extract obtained by soaking in alcohol

Etymology

From Latin infusio (pouring in), from infundere (to pour in).

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈfjuʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈfjʊːʒ(ə)n / (BrE)
(medicine) the slow introduction of a fluid, drug, or electrolyte into a vein or between tissues, often using gravity.
slow introduction of liquid into the body (medical)
medical

Examples

The patient received an infusion of antibiotics over several hours.
Doctors started a saline infusion to treat her dehydration.
Complications can occur if the infusion is too rapid or not properly monitored.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: injection - the act of injecting a substance into the body with a syringe

Etymology

From Latin 'infusio' (pouring in), from 'infundere' (to pour in).
