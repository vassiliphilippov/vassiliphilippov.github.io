to efface

1. VERB

/ ɪˈfeɪs / (AmE), / ɪˈfeɪs / (BrE)
to remove or make disappear by or as if by rubbing or erasing
remove by rubbing or erasing

Examples

Centuries of weather had effaced the ancient carvings on the stone.
She tried to efface the memory from her mind, but it lingered.
The writing was so faint that a light touch could efface it completely.

Synset Examples

Please erase the formula on the blackboard — it is wrong!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to erase - to remove something completely, especially written or drawn marks

Etymology

From French 'effacer', from Latin 'ex-' (out) + 'facies' (face, form).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01551500-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪˈfeɪs / (AmE), / ɪˈfeɪs / (BrE)
to make oneself or something inconspicuous or withdraw from notice; to cause one to be unnoticed
make oneself inconspicuous

Examples

She tried to efface herself at the meeting, sitting in the back and speaking very little.
He prefers to efface himself rather than take credit for the team's success.
After her promotion, she made an effort not to efface her presence in the new office environment.

Synset Examples

efface oneself

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to withdraw - to remove oneself from participation or attention
  • Antonym: to obtrude - to become or make something noticeable in an unwelcome way

Etymology

From French 'effacer', from Latin 'effacere' (to erase).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01804771-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪˈfeɪs / (AmE), / ɪˈfeɪs / (BrE)
to erase or remove completely from recognition, memory, or existence
erase or remove completely

Examples

She tried to efface all traces of her former life after moving to a new city.
The tragic memories could never fully efface themselves from his mind.
Over time, the monument's inscription became effaced by wind and rain.

Synset Examples

efface the memory of the time in the camps

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to erase - to remove written or recorded marks or information
  • Synonym: to obliterate - to destroy completely; wipe out
  • Antonym: to retain - to keep in one's memory or possession

Etymology

From French 'effacer', from Latin 'ex-' (out) + 'facies' (appearance, face).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00312120-v
Synset POS: v
