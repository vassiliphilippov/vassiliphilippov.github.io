kingdom

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɪŋdəm / (AmE), / ˈkɪŋdəm / (BrE)
A country, state, or territory ruled by a king or queen.
monarchy ruled by king or queen

Examples

The United Kingdom is a kingdom with a long and complex history.
After the death of the king, the entire kingdom mourned for weeks.
He dreamed of one day ruling his own kingdom and bringing peace to its people.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: republic - country without a monarch, head of state is elected
  • Synonym: monarchy - a form of government with a monarch at the head

Etymology

From Old English cyningdōm (realm of a king), from 'cyning' (king) + 'dōm' (domain, authority).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɪŋdəm / (AmE), / ˈkɪŋdəm / (BrE)
one of the highest categories in biological classification, grouping together all forms of life with fundamental similarities, such as animals or plants
major biological classification group

Examples

All living organisms are classified into different kingdoms such as Animalia and Plantae.
Biologists debated whether fungi should be placed in their own kingdom.
In school, we learned that bacteria belong to a separate kingdom from plants and animals.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: phylum - a major taxonomic group below kingdom, grouping organisms of the same body plan
  • Degree Higher: domain - the highest taxonomic rank above kingdom in some classification systems

Etymology

From Old English cyningdōm (realm of a king), later extended in scientific use.

6. NOUN

/ ˈkɪŋdəm / (AmE), / ˈkɪŋdəm / (BrE)
the highest taxonomic rank in the biological classification of organisms, grouping together related phyla or divisions, such as Animalia, Plantae, Fungi, Protista, and Monera.
highest rank in biological classification

Examples

Biologists classify all living things into five kingdoms based on fundamental characteristics.
The kingdom Animalia includes all animals, from insects to mammals.
Fungi constitute a separate kingdom distinct from plants and animals.

Etymology

From Old English 'cyningdom' (realm of a king), here extended to biological taxonomy.
