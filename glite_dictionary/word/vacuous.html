vacuous

1. ADJ

/ ˈvæk.ju.əs / (AmE), / ˈvæk.ju.əs / (BrE)
Containing nothing; devoid of matter or empty.
empty; devoid of matter

Examples

The scientist examined the vacuous chamber under the microscope.
The room felt oddly vacuous after all the furniture was removed.
A vacuous space separated the two walls, amplifying the echo of footsteps.

Synset Examples

a vacuous space

Related Terms

  • Synonym: empty - containing nothing; not filled or occupied
  • Antonym: full - containing as much as possible; filled

Etymology

From Latin vacuus (empty) via Latin vacuōsus (full of emptiness).

2. ADJ

/ ˈvækjuəs / (AmE), / ˈvakjʊəs / (BrE)
having or showing a lack of thought, intelligence, or meaning; empty-headed or inane.
lacking thought or intelligence

Examples

Her vacuous remarks contributed nothing to the discussion.
He gave a vacuous smile and stared off into space.
The movie was visually stunning but ultimately vacuous in its content.

Synset Examples

a vacuous mind
a vacant expression

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inane - lacking sense or meaning; silly
  • Synonym: empty-headed - lacking intelligence or awareness
  • Antonym: insightful - showing deep understanding or perception

Etymology

From Latin vacuus (empty).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02581841-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈvæ.kju.əs / (AmE), / ˈvak.ju.əs / (BrE)
lacking real meaning, importance, or substance; empty or meaningless.
lacking meaning or substance

Examples

His speech was full of vacuous promises and vague reassurances.
Critics called the film's dialogue vacuous and uninspired.
She grew tired of the vacuous conversation at the party.

Synset Examples

empty promises
a hollow victory
vacuous comments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: empty - containing nothing; not filled or occupied
  • Antonym: meaningful - having significance or purpose

Etymology

From Latin vacuus (empty, void).
