to rhapsodise

1. VERB

/ ˈræp.səˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈræp.sə.daɪz / (BrE)
to speak or write about something with great enthusiasm and passion
speak or write with great enthusiasm
british

Examples

She began to rhapsodise about the breathtaking sunset she had witnessed on holiday.
Critics often rhapsodise over the beauty of his paintings.
He rhapsodised endlessly about the new restaurant's innovative cuisine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enthuse - to express eager enjoyment or interest

Etymology

From Greek rhapsōidein (to recite epic poetry), via French rhapsodiser.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00918736-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈræpsəˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈræpsədaɪz / (BrE)
to recite a rhapsody, especially in an enthusiastic or impassioned manner
recite a rhapsody passionately
british

Examples

The poet began to rhapsodise about ancient myths before the captivated audience.
She would often rhapsodise at gatherings, bringing the epic tales to life.
In the evening, the bard rhapsodised the verses with such passion that everyone was spellbound.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to rhapsodize - to recite a rhapsody (American spelling)

Etymology

From Greek *rhapsōidein* (to recite a rhapsody), via Latin and French.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00956169-v
Synset POS: v
