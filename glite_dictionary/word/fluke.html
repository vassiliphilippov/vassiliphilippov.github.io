fluke

1. NOUN

/ fluːk / (AmE), / fluːk / (BrE)
The flat, bladelike part on the arm of an anchor that digs into the ground to hold the anchor in place.
flat projection on anchor arm

Examples

The fluke of the anchor caught firmly in the sandy seabed.
Inspect the fluke for damage before lowering the anchor.
Barnacles had collected on the fluke during the long voyage.

Etymology

Possibly related to Old Norse 'floke' (flatfish); precise origin uncertain.

2. NOUN

/ fluːk / (AmE), / fluːk / (BrE)
a barb or projection on a harpoon or arrow, designed to prevent it from being easily withdrawn
barb on a harpoon or arrow

Examples

The fisherman examined the fluke on his harpoon before heading out to sea.
Ancient arrows sometimes had a single fluke to ensure they stayed lodged in the target.
The fluke of the harpoon made it especially effective for hunting large marine animals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barb - sharp projection at the end of an arrow or fishhook that makes extraction difficult

Etymology

Possibly from Old Norse *floke* (flat fish) due to shape resemblance.

3. NOUN

/ fluːk / (AmE), / fluːk / (BrE)
a parasitic flatworm with external suckers used for attaching to a host, typically found in the liver or blood of vertebrates
a parasitic flatworm

Examples

The sheep was infected by a liver fluke that caused health problems.
Scientists studied the life cycle of the blood fluke in the laboratory.
Some flukes are serious parasites in both humans and animals.

Etymology

From Old English *flóc* (a flatfish), later applied to flatworms due to similarity in shape.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ fluːk / (AmE), / fluːk / (BrE)
an unexpected stroke of luck; something good that happens by chance rather than by intention
an unexpected stroke of luck

Examples

Winning the lottery was a complete fluke and not something she ever expected.
His goal was a fluke, as the ball bounced off his knee and into the net.
They met again years later, but it was just a fluke rather than a planned reunion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chance - luck, or the occurrence of events without apparent cause
  • Synonym: accident - something that happens unexpectedly or unintentionally
  • Antonym: misfortune - bad luck; an unlucky event

Etymology

Possibly from Old English *floc* (flatfish), but precise origin uncertain. The sense of 'stroke of luck' may derive from billiards slang (19th century).

5. NOUN

/ fluːk / (AmE), / fluːk / (BrE)
either of the two lobes of the flattened tail of a whale, dolphin, or other cetacean, used for propulsion
lobe of a whale or dolphin's tail

Examples

The blue whale lifted its massive flukes high above the water before diving.
Scientists identified the whale by the scars on its right fluke.
A dolphin propels itself quickly by moving its flukes up and down.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fin - a flat appendage used by fish and other aquatic animals for movement or balance
  • Synonym: tail - the hindmost part of an animal

Etymology

Probably from Old English *floc* (flatfish, flounder), later applied to whales' tail lobes for their flattened shape.
