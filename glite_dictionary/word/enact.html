to enact

1. VERB

/ ɪˈnækt / (AmE), / ɪˈnakt / (BrE)
to perform or represent (something) as if in a play; to act out a role or scene
to act out or perform scenes

Examples

The children decided to enact their favorite scene from the play for their parents.
In therapy, clients sometimes enact situations from their past to gain insight.
At the festival, volunteers enacted a traditional wedding ceremony for the audience.

Synset Examples

She reenacted what had happened earlier that day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to perform - to present a role or an action in front of an audience
  • Degree Lower: to improvise - to perform without preparation or script

Etymology

From Middle English, from en- (make, put in) + act, from Latin actus (a doing).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈnækt / (AmE), / ɪˈnækt / (BrE)
to make into law; to formally establish or order by authority
formally make into law

Examples

Parliament voted to enact strict new regulations on emissions.
The government plans to enact a series of reforms next year.
A new law was enacted to protect consumer rights.

Synset Examples

The King ordained the persecution and expulsion of the Jews
the legislature enacted this law in 1985

Related Terms

  • Antonym: repeal - to revoke or annul a law officially
  • Synonym: to legislate - to make or pass laws

Etymology

From Latin 'enactare', from 'en-' (in) + 'actum' (act).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02432763-v
Synset POS: v
