to deracinate

1. VERB

/ dɪˈræsəˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈræsɪneɪt / (BrE)
to remove people forcibly from their homeland and place them in a new, foreign environment
forcibly remove from homeland

Examples

The civil war threatened to deracinate entire communities from the valley.
Policies aimed to deracinate native people from their ancestral lands generated widespread grief.
Generations have struggled to rebuild their identity after being deracinated by colonial expansion.

Synset Examples

The war uprooted many people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to uproot - to remove or displace people from their home or environment

Etymology

From French déraciner, from dé- (from, out) + racine (root).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈræsɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈræsɪneɪt / (BrE)
to pull up by the roots; to remove or uproot completely, especially from a native environment or culture
pull up by the roots; uproot

Examples

The invasive species threatened to deracinate the local plants from their natural habitat.
The government aimed to deracinate harmful traditions by introducing new laws.
Centuries of migration tended to deracinate families from their ancestral lands.

Synset Examples

uproot the vine that has spread all over the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to uproot - to pull (a plant) out of the ground; remove completely
  • Antonym: plant - to put (a seed or plant) in the ground so that it will grow

Etymology

From French 'déraciner', from Old French 'desraciner', from 'des-' (dis-) + 'racine' (root, from Latin 'radix').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01569697-v
Synset POS: v
