synergism

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪnɚˌdʒɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈsɪnədʒɪzəm / (BrE)
The theological doctrine that salvation is achieved through the cooperation of human will and divine grace.
doctrine: salvation via human and divine cooperation

Examples

Some Christian denominations embrace synergism, believing that humans cooperate with divine grace for salvation.
The debate between monergism and synergism has influenced Protestant theology for centuries.
His essay examines how synergism shapes concepts of responsibility and free will in religious thought.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: monergism - the doctrine that salvation is solely the work of divine grace, without human cooperation

Etymology

From Greek 'synergos' (working together), via modern Latin 'synergismus'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪn.ɚˌdʒɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈsɪn.əˌdʒɪz.əm / (BrE)
the interaction of two or more agents or forces so that their combined effect is greater than the sum of their individual effects
interaction producing a greater combined effect

Examples

The synergism between the two drugs led to a much faster recovery than expected.
Scientists are studying the synergism observed when certain chemicals are combined.
Muscles can exhibit synergism to produce more powerful movements than they could alone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: synergy - the combined power of a group that achieves more than individuals

Etymology

From Greek 'sunergos' (working together), via Late Latin 'synergismus'.
