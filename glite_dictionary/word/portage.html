portage

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɔrtɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈpɔːtɪdʒ / (BrE)
An overland route or track between two navigable waters, used for carrying boats and supplies.
overland route between waterways

Examples

The explorers carried their canoe across the portage to reach the next river.
A well-worn portage connected the two remote lakes.
They chose the shortest portage to save time on their journey.

Etymology

From French *portage* (carrying), from porter (to carry).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɔrtɪdʒ, ˈpɔrtədʒ / (AmE), / ˈpɔːtɪdʒ, ˈpɔːtɪdʒ / (BrE)
the charge or cost for carrying goods or cargo, especially over land between two waterways
cost of carrying goods

Examples

The crew was charged a high portage to move their goods across the isthmus.
In the 18th century, portage was an important expense for traders navigating rivers separated by land.
The merchant calculated the portage before agreeing to transport his wares upstream.

Etymology

From French *portage* (carrying), from *porter* (to carry).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɔrtɪdʒ, pɔrˈtɑʒ / (AmE), / ˈpɔːtɪdʒ, pɔːˈtɑːʒ / (BrE)
the act of carrying boats and supplies overland between two waterways or around an obstacle such as rapids or a waterfall
carrying boats and supplies over land

Examples

The crew made a portage to avoid the dangerous rapids ahead.
After a long portage through the forest, we finally reached the next river.
The ancient trade route required frequent portage between lakes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carry - to support and move (someone or something) from one place to another

Etymology

From French 'portage', from porter (to carry).
