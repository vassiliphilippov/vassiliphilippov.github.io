brisk

1. ADJ

/ brɪsk / (AmE), / brɪsk / (BrE)
quick, energetic, and active in movement or action.
quick and energetic

Examples

Let's take a brisk walk before dinner to energize ourselves.
She gave a brisk nod and continued her work efficiently.
The air was cool and the wind grew brisk as the sun set.

Synset Examples

a brisk walk in the park
a lively gait
a merry chase
traveling at a rattling rate
a snappy pace
a spanking breeze

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lively - full of life and energy
  • Antonym: slow - not quick or energetic; lacking speed

Etymology

Probably from Old Norse *briskr* (lively).

2. VERB

/ brɪsk / (AmE), / brɪsk / (BrE)
(of activity or trade) to become busy, lively, or energetic.
(of activity) become busy or lively

Examples

Sales began to brisk during the holiday season.
The pace of negotiations brisked up after the new proposal was introduced.
As the weather improved, business at the café brisked.

Synset Examples

business brisked up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quicken - to become faster or livelier

Etymology

From Middle English 'brisk' or 'bruske', possibly from Old French 'brusque' or Italian 'brusco' (lively, sharp).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00206174-v
Synset POS: v

4. ADJ

/ brɪsk / (AmE), / brɪsk / (BrE)
(of weather, air, or activity) stimulating and refreshing; imparting vitality and energy.
invigorating; energizing (weather/air/activity)

Examples

The brisk wind on the coast revived our spirits after a long day.
She enjoyed taking brisk morning walks to start her day with energy.
After a brisk swim in the lake, he felt refreshed and alert.

Synset Examples

the bracing mountain air

Related Terms

  • Synonym: invigorating - making one feel strong, healthy, and full of energy
  • Synonym: refreshing - serving to restore energy or cheerfulness

Etymology

From Middle English 'bruske', of uncertain origin, perhaps from Old French 'brusque'.
