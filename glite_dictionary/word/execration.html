execration

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛksɪˈkreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛksɪˈkreɪʃən / (BrE)
an object that is cursed or detested; something or someone loathed or condemned
object of cursing or detestation

Examples

In medieval times, the traitor's name quickly became an execration throughout the land.
The statue, once revered, was now an execration to the entire community.
She viewed the oppressive regime as an execration that had to be overthrown.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - a solemn utterance intended to invoke a supernatural power to inflict harm or punishment
  • Antonym: blessing - an act or words of approval, encouragement, or kindness

Etymology

From Latin execratio (a cursing), from exsecrari (to curse).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɛksɪˈkreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛksɪˈkreɪʃən / (BrE)
an appeal to a supernatural power to bring harm or evil upon someone or something; a curse
a curse or invocation of harm

Examples

The ancient text contains an execration against enemy nations.
Hearing his name spoken in execration only fueled her anger.
Superstitious villagers feared the execration might actually bring misfortune.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - a solemn utterance intended to invoke a supernatural power to inflict harm or punishment

Etymology

From Latin execrationem (a cursing, accursed thing), from execrari (to curse, denounce).

3. NOUN

/ ˌɛksəˈkreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɛksɪˈkreɪʃən / (BrE)
an intense feeling of hatred and disgust; the act of denouncing or cursing someone or something
intense hatred or curse

Examples

The public's execration of the corrupt officials was evident in the protests.
Her speech was filled with execration for the war and its devastation.
He spoke of his enemy with utter execration and contempt.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abhorrence - strong feeling of hatred mixed with disgust
  • Antonym: blessing - expression of approval or good wishes; antonym of execration in the sense of cursing

Etymology

From Latin 'execratio', from 'execrari' (to curse, detest).
