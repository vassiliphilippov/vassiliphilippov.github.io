cobble

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːbəl / (AmE), / ˈkɒbl / (BrE)
a roughly rectangular stone with a rounded top, used in the past for paving roads and streets
rectangular stone used for road paving

Examples

The old street was lined with cobbles that made walking uneven but charming.
Many European cities still have roads paved with cobbles.
Workers removed several cobbles to repair the underground pipes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cobblestone - a small, round stone used for paving streets

Etymology

From Middle English 'cobbe' (rounded lump).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑb.əl / (AmE), / ˈkɒb.əl / (BrE)
to repair or mend, especially shoes, in a rough or makeshift way
repair or mend (shoes)

Examples

The old man used to cobble shoes in his workshop every day.
In the past, poor villagers would cobble their own shoes to make them last longer.
She learned how to cobble boots to help her family save money.

Synset Examples

cobble shoes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mend - to repair something that is broken or damaged
  • Synonym: repair - to restore something that is damaged to good condition

Etymology

From Middle English 'cobelen' (to mend shoes), likely a diminutive of 'cob' (a rounded lump), used colloquially for a cobbler.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00262731-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑb.əl / (AmE), / ˈkɒb.əl / (BrE)
to pave (a street or road) with cobblestones
pave with cobblestones

Examples

The workers will cobble the main street to preserve its historical charm.
They cobbled the old alleyway last year, making it safe for pedestrians.
Plans are underway to cobble the sidewalks in the city center.

Etymology

From Middle English *coblen*, from 'cobble' (n.), originally meaning a rounded stone.
