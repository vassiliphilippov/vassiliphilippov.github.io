dugout

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʌɡˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈdʌɡ.aʊt / (BrE)
a canoe made by hollowing out and shaping a large log
canoe made from a hollowed log

Examples

The villagers paddled their dugout down the river to reach the neighboring island.
He carved a dugout from a single tree trunk, following traditional methods.
Tourists sometimes ride in a dugout canoe to explore the wetlands.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canoe - a light, narrow boat pointed at both ends and moved with a paddle

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʌɡˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈdʌɡ.aʊt / (BrE)
a shelter made by digging a hole or depression into the ground, often used for housing people or animals and sometimes covered with earth or sod
shelter dug into the ground

Examples

The early settlers built a dugout to shelter from the harsh winter winds.
During the excavation, archaeologists uncovered a prehistoric dugout that had once housed an entire family.
Many livestock owners rely on a dugout as an inexpensive way to protect animals from extreme weather.

Synset Examples

Dugouts are one of the most ancient types of human housing known to archeologists, and the same methods have evolved into modern ‘earth shelter’ technology.

Etymology

From 'dug' (past participle of 'dig') + 'out', referring to something dug out of the ground.

4. NOUN

/ ˈdʌɡ.aʊt / (AmE), / ˈdʌɡ.aʊt / (BrE)
A shelter or fortification built by digging into the ground, especially one used for protection during warfare.
an underground shelter or fortification

Examples

The soldiers took cover in a dugout during the heavy artillery bombardment.
During World War I, many troops lived for weeks at a time in cramped dugouts.
The old dugout was hidden by grass and used for emergency shelter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bunker - a reinforced underground shelter, especially for use in wartime

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'dig' and 'out', indicating something that has been dug out of the earth.

Examples

Ancient people often lived in dugouts to protect themselves from predators and extreme weather.
Some modern eco-homes are inspired by traditional dugout designs, utilizing earth for natural insulation.
