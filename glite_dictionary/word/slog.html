to slog

1. VERB

/ slɑg / (AmE), / slɒg / (BrE)
to work hard and steadily over a long period, especially at something difficult or exhausting
work hard and steadily

Examples

After hours of slogging through paperwork, she finally finished the report.
He had to slog through deep snow to reach the cabin.
Despite feeling tired, the team slogged on until the job was done.

Synset Examples

She keeps plugging away at her dissertation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toil - to work hard and continuously
  • Synonym: grind - to work hard, especially at something monotonous or tedious
  • Antonym: rest - to relax or stop working after effort

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly imitative or related to the dialect word 'slug' (to hit hard).

2. VERB

/ slɑg / (AmE), / slɒg / (BrE)
to strike heavily, especially with a fist or a bat
to hit hard with fist or bat

Examples

He slogged the ball out of the park with one powerful swing.
The boxer slogged his opponent in the jaw during the final round.
She slogged him on the shoulder to get his attention.

Synset Examples

He slugged me so hard that I passed out

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slug - to hit someone hard with a heavy blow
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01418239-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ slɑg / (AmE), / slɒg / (BrE)
to walk with slow, heavy steps, especially because of tiredness or through difficult conditions such as mud
walk with slow, heavy steps

Examples

After hours of rain, we had to slog through the muddy field to reach the house.
The exhausted hikers continued to slog up the steep, rocky path despite the downpour.
Every morning he would slog to work through the crowded, noisy streets.

Synset Examples

Mules plodded in a circle around a grindstone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trudge - to walk slowly with heavy steps, typically because of exhaustion
  • Synonym: plod - to walk heavily and slowly without much energy or excitement
  • Degree Higher: march - to walk with regular, measured steps
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01925197-v
Synset POS: v
