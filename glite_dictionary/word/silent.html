silent

1. ADJ

/ ˈsaɪlənt / (AmE), / ˈsaɪlənt / (BrE)
Implied or understood without being directly stated or expressed in words.
implied; not said aloud

Examples

Her parents gave their silent approval to the plan by not objecting.
There was a silent agreement among the team members to work extra hours if needed.
Their silent consent was clear from their actions, even though nothing was discussed.

Synset Examples

gave silent consent
a tacit agreement
the understood provisos of a custody agreement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tacit - implied or understood without being stated; unspoken

Etymology

From Latin silentem, present participle of silēre (to be quiet).

Synset POS: s

without sound; making no noise

Examples

3. ADJ

/ ˈsaɪlənt / (AmE), / ˈsaɪlənt / (BrE)
(of a letter) written but not pronounced in speech.
not pronounced (of a letter)

Examples

The silent 'k' in 'knight' often confuses young readers.
In the word 'psychology', the 'p' is silent.
Many English words borrowed from French contain a silent letter at the end.

Synset Examples

the silent ‘h’ at the beginning of ‘honor’
in French certain letters are often unsounded

Related Terms

  • Antonym: voiced - (of a letter) pronounced with vocal cord vibration

Etymology

From Latin 'silens', present participle of 'silere' (to be quiet, be still).

6. ADJ

/ ˈsaɪlənt / (AmE), / ˈsaɪlənt / (BrE)
(of a sound) having a frequency that is too low or too high to be heard by humans.
inaudible due to frequency

Examples

The trainer used a silent dog whistle to call the animals without disturbing people.
Bats can emit silent ultrasonic waves for navigation and hunting insects.
Some alarm systems work by producing silent alarms that are outside the range of human hearing.

Synset Examples

a silent dog whistle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inaudible - not able to be heard

Etymology

From Latin 'silent-' (being silent), from 'silere' (be quiet).
