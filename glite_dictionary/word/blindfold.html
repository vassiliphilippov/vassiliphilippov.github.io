blindfold

1. VERB

/ ˈblaɪndˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈblaɪndˌfəʊld / (BrE)
to cover someone's eyes with a cloth or similar covering to prevent them from seeing
cover someone's eyes to prevent sight

Examples

The kidnappers blindfolded the victim before taking him into the building.
She blindfolded her little brother so he couldn't see his birthday surprise.
Before the game, each participant was blindfolded and spun around.

Synset Examples

the hostage was blindfolded and driven away

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unblindfold - to remove a blindfold from someone

Etymology

From 'blind' + 'fold', meaning to fold something over the eyes to render blind.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01340609-v
Synset POS: v

2. ADJ

/ ˈblaɪndˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈblaɪndfəʊld / (BrE)
Wearing a piece of cloth or covering over the eyes, so as to prevent seeing.
wearing a covering over the eyes

Examples

The blindfold contestant struggled to find the hidden object.
Two blindfold players tried to tag each other in the game.
Each blindfold participant relied on touch and sound.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: sighted - having the faculty of seeing; not blind or blindfolded

Etymology

From Middle English; 'blind' + 'fold'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02166779-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ ˈblaɪnˌfoʊld / (AmE), / ˈblaɪnˌfəʊld / (BrE)
A piece of cloth used to cover a person's eyes to prevent them from seeing.
cloth that covers the eyes

Examples

The child wore a blindfold during the party game.
They gently tied a blindfold around his eyes before leading him to the surprise.
He removed the blindfold and opened his eyes to see the gift.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eye mask - a mask covering the eyes to block light or vision

Etymology

From Middle English 'blyndefolden', meaning 'to cover the eyes'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02855198-n
Synset POS: n
