tickle

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪkəl / (BrE)
a light, tingling sensation on the skin, often caused by gentle touch or stroking and sometimes causing laughter
light tingling skin sensation

Examples

She felt a sudden tickle on her arm as the breeze brushed her skin.
A faint tickle in his throat made him cough during the meeting.
The soft feather produced a tickle that made the baby giggle uncontrollably.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: itch - an uncomfortable or irritating skin sensation causing the urge to scratch
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05731362-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪkəl / (BrE)
the act of touching someone lightly to cause laughter or twitching movements
the act of tickling

Examples

He erupted in giggles at the slightest tickle on his feet.
The tickle made her squirm and laugh uncontrollably.
A gentle tickle behind the knee sent him into peals of laughter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tickling - the act of tickling (gerund form)

Etymology

From Middle English tikelen, related to Dutch 'tikelen' and German 'kitzeln', meaning to tickle.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00145265-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈtɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪkəl / (BrE)
to touch or stroke a part of the body lightly, often so as to cause laughter or a tingling sensation
touch or stroke lightly, often causing laughter

Examples

The long grass began to tickle her ankles as she walked through the field.
If you tickle his feet, he'll burst out laughing.
A feather can easily tickle your nose and make you sneeze.

Synset Examples

The grass tickled her calves

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tease - to touch or handle lightly or playfully

Etymology

From Middle English tikelen, probably of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01434620-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈtɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪkəl / (BrE)
to cause a sudden intense sensation or emotion, especially of amusement or excitement
to feel or cause a sudden emotion or sensation

Examples

The idea of traveling to Japan really tickled her.
His praise tickled me, and I couldn't help but smile.
It tickled them to be chosen for the award.

Synset Examples

he was thrilled by the speed and the roar of the engine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrill - to cause to feel intense excitement

5. VERB

/ ˈtɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈtɪkəl / (BrE)
to touch a part of the body lightly so as to cause laughter or involuntary movements
touch lightly to cause laughter

Examples

She tried not to laugh when her friend began to tickle her feet.
If you tickle a baby gently, it might start giggling.
He loves to tickle his dog behind the ears.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: scratch - to rub a surface with something sharp or rough, usually for relief from irritation

Etymology

From Middle English *tikelen*, from Old English *ticcian* (to touch lightly).
