mandarin

1. NOUN

/ ˈmændəˌrɪn / (AmE), / ˈmændərɪn / (BrE)
a small, somewhat flat citrus fruit with loose, reddish-orange skin, originally from China
small reddish-orange citrus fruit

Examples

She peeled a mandarin for her lunch.
The market sells sweet, juicy mandarins during the winter.
A bowl of mandarins brightened up the kitchen table.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tangerine - a similar small variety of citrus fruit

Etymology

From Portuguese mandarim, from Malay 'menteri', from Sanskrit 'mantrin' (counselor), originally referring to a Chinese official; fruit so named for its Chinese origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmændəˌrɪn / (AmE), / ˈmændərɪn / (BrE)
a high-ranking public official in imperial China, often selected by examination and noted for their administrative duties
high official of imperial China

Examples

The mandarin oversaw the implementation of new tax laws across the province.
Aspiring scholars studied for years to pass the exams required to become a mandarin.
A portrait of a distinguished mandarin hung proudly in the ancestral hall.

Etymology

From Portuguese 'mandarim', from Malay 'menteri' (counselor, minister), from Sanskrit 'mantrin' (counselor).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmæn.də.rɪn / (AmE), / ˈmæn.dər.ɪn / (BrE)
a member of an elite group of intellectuals or cultural leaders, especially one considered influential or authoritative
influential intellectual or cultural elite member

Examples

The academic mandarins held enormous sway over national educational policy.
Many critics argue that political mandarins are out of touch with everyday concerns.
She was considered a cultural mandarin because of her influence in artistic circles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elite - a group of people considered to be the best in a society or category

Etymology

Originally from Portuguese 'mandarim', from Malay 'menteri', from Sanskrit 'mantrin' (counselor); the meaning broadened in English to members of intellectual elites by analogy with Chinese officials.

5. NOUN

/ ˈmæn.də.rɪn / (AmE), / ˈmæn.dər.ɪn / (BrE)
A small evergreen tree or shrub native to southeastern Asia, cultivated for its sweet, easily peeled, orange fruit.
a citrus tree with sweet, loose-skinned fruit

Examples

The mandarin tree in our garden produces fruit with a thin, easy-to-peel skin.
Imported mandarins are especially popular around the Lunar New Year.
A single mandarin contains a high amount of vitamin C and is a healthy snack.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tangerine - a similar citrus fruit, often considered a variety of mandarin

Etymology

From Portuguese 'mandarim,' from Malay 'menteri,' from Sanskrit 'mantrin' (counselor), originally used for the Chinese magistrate; later transferred to the fruit via association with China.
