skepticism

1. NOUN

/ ˈskɛp.tɪˌsɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈskep.tɪ.sɪ.zəm / (BrE)
The belief that it is impossible to have absolute knowledge or certainty about anything; a doubting or questioning attitude towards claims of ultimate knowledge.
doubt about the possibility of ultimate knowledge

Examples

Her skepticism made her question every claim she encountered, no matter how established.
Philosophical skepticism argues that humans can never achieve true knowledge of reality.
While some embrace new ideas easily, others approach them with a healthy skepticism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubt - lack of conviction or certainty
  • Antonym: certainty - the state of being sure or confident

Etymology

From Greek 'skeptikos' (thoughtful, inquiring), via Latin 'scepticus'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈskɛptɪˌsɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈskɛptɪsɪzəm / (BrE)
an attitude of doubting the truth or reliability of something; a tendency to question or disbelieve accepted ideas or claims
doubt about the truth or validity of something

Examples

There was a great deal of skepticism about the new policy's effectiveness.
Despite initial skepticism, the theory was eventually accepted by the scientific community.
Her claims were met with skepticism by many experts in the field.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubt - a feeling of uncertainty or lack of conviction
  • Antonym: belief - acceptance that something exists or is true

Etymology

From Greek *skeptikos* (inquiring, reflective), via Latin *scepticus*.
