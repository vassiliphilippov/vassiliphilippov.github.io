intransitive

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtræn.sə.tɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtræn.zɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
(of a verb) not taking or requiring a direct object.
(of a verb) not taking a direct object

Examples

The verb 'arrive' is intransitive because you cannot 'arrive something'.
Many English verbs can be both intransitive and transitive, depending on the context.
In the sentence 'She sleeps', 'sleeps' is an intransitive verb.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transitive - (of a verb) taking a direct object

Etymology

From Latin intransitivus, from in- (not) + transitivus ('transitive').

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtrænzətɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈtrænsətɪv / (BrE)
A verb or verb construction that does not take a direct object.
verb not taking a direct object

Examples

The verb 'arrive' is an intransitive, as it does not require an object.
Can you give an example of an intransitive in this sentence?
In the sentence 'She laughed', 'laughed' functions as an intransitive.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: transitive - a verb that takes a direct object

Etymology

From Latin intransitivus, from in- (not) + transitivus (transitive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06342634-n
Synset POS: n
