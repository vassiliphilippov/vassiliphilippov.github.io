handicap

1. NOUN

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændikæp / (BrE)
a physical or mental condition that restricts a person's ability to perform certain activities, often used now in reference to disability but considered outdated or offensive in modern usage
physical or mental disadvantage; outdated term
offensive

Examples

She refused to let her handicap define what she could accomplish in life.
The stadium was equipped with special access for people with a handicap.
Many organizations now avoid the term handicap in favor of 'disability.'

Synset Examples

reading disability
hearing impairment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disability - a physical or mental condition that limits a person's movements, senses, or activities
  • Synonym: impairment - a loss of function or ability

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'hand in cap', a trading game, later generalized to mean a disadvantage, and later a disability.

2. VERB

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhandɪkap / (BrE)
to attempt to predict the outcome of a contest, especially a horse race, and set odds for or against a participant
predict winner and set odds (esp. in racing)

Examples

Experts will handicap the upcoming derby based on each horse's recent performances.
Bookmakers often handicap matches to set fair odds for punters.
He has been handicapping sporting events for over a decade and is known for his accuracy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to predict - to say what will happen in the future, especially based on knowledge or experience
  • Synonym: forecast - to estimate or calculate in advance, especially as regards competition outcomes

Etymology

From English noun 'handicap', originally a type of trading game, later used in horse racing to mean equalizing the chances of contestants.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01088285-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈhæn.diˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhæn.dɪ.kæp / (BrE)
an advantage or disadvantage given to a competitor in order to equalize the chances of winning
advantage/disadvantage to equalize competition

Examples

Because he was a much better golfer, he played with a handicap to make the match fair.
The tournament organizers imposed a time handicap to give the new team a better chance.
Chess matches sometimes use a handicap by starting one player with a pawn less.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advantage - something that helps one to be more successful than others
  • Antonym: disadvantage - an unfavorable circumstance that reduces the chances of success

Etymology

From English 'hand in cap', an old game involving forfeit; later applied to racing and competition.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05164205-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændiˌkap / (BrE)
to cause a permanent injury or disability; to impair someone's physical or mental abilities permanently
to injure or disable permanently

Examples

The disease may handicap a child for life if left untreated.
A serious knee injury handicapped him early in his athletic career.
Repeated concussions have the potential to handicap even the most talented players.

Synset Examples

He was disabled in a car accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disable - to make unable to use part of the body or to do something

Etymology

From 'handicap' (noun), originally from the phrase 'hand in cap,' a betting game in the 17th century; later used in the sense of disadvantage.

5. NOUN

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændikæp / (BrE)
A circumstance, condition, or factor that makes progress or success more difficult.
disadvantage; obstacle to progress

Examples

Her lack of experience proved to be a serious handicap during the interview process.
Despite his handicap, he managed to complete the project on time.
Economic inequality remains a major handicap for many people seeking education and employment.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: advantage - a condition giving greater chance of success
  • Synonym: obstacle - something that blocks progress
  • Synonym: impediment - a hindrance or obstruction

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'hand in cap,' a method of equalizing chances in games (mid 18th century).

Source: WordNet

6. VERB

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændikæp / (BrE)
to put someone or something at a disadvantage compared to others
put at a disadvantage

Examples

Wearing thick gloves can handicap a surgeon during delicate operations.
Her lack of experience may handicap her in the job interview.
Heavy rain handicapped the team's efforts to finish the match.

Synset Examples

The brace I have to wear is hindering my movements

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hinder - to make it more difficult for someone to do something; to delay; to obstruct
  • Synonym: to impede - to delay or prevent by obstructing; to hinder
  • Antonym: assist - to help or make it easier for someone to do something

Etymology

From earlier noun sense ‘hand in cap’ (an old form of betting and equalizing competitions); later adopted in sporting contexts.
