alveolar

1. ADJ

/ ælˈviələr / (AmE), / ælˈviːələ / (BrE)
Relating to the small air sacs in the lungs where gas exchange occurs.
relating to lung air sacs
medical

Examples

The alveolar structure plays a critical role in the respiratory process.
Smoking can cause damage to alveolar tissue, leading to breathing difficulties.
Doctors observed inflammation in the alveolar region of the patient's lungs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulmonary - relating to the lungs

Etymology

From Latin 'alveolus' (small cavity), diminutive of 'alveus' (trough, basin).

2. ADJ

/ ælˈvioʊlər / (AmE), / ælˈviːələ / (BrE)
relating to the sockets of the teeth or the part of the jaw that contains them, especially the upper jaw.
relating to tooth sockets in the jaw
medical

Examples

The dentist examined the alveolar bone for signs of infection.
In some mammals, the alveolar ridge is particularly pronounced.
Damage to the alveolar processes can lead to tooth loss.

Synset Examples

alveolar processes

Etymology

From Latin alveolus (small cavity) + -ar.

3. NOUN

/ ælˈviːələr / (AmE), / alˈviːələ / (BrE)
A consonant sound produced by placing the tip or blade of the tongue against or near the upper front ridge of the mouth (the alveolar ridge).
consonant articulated with tongue near gum ridge

Examples

The English sound t is classified as an alveolar consonant.
Linguists often discuss the difference between dental and alveolar sounds.
In Spanish, both alveolar and dental stops are common.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: dental - consonant articulated with tongue against the teeth
  • Degree Higher: postalveolar - consonant articulated just behind the alveolar ridge

Etymology

From Latin 'alveolus' (diminutive of 'alveus', meaning 'trough, hollow').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07130201-n
Synset POS: n
