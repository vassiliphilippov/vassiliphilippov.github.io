to wander

1. VERB

/ ˈwɑndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
(of a path, stream, or similar) to move in a winding, indirect, or meandering course.
move in a winding course

Examples

The little stream wandered across the meadow, twisting between tufts of grass.
Our hike followed a trail that wandered through the ancient forest.
The old stone wall wandered along the hillside, curving around boulders and trees.

Synset Examples

the river winds through the hills
the path meanders through the vineyards
sometimes, the gout wanders through the entire body

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meander - to follow a winding, turning course, especially of a river or path
  • Synonym: wind - (of a road, river, etc.) to follow a curving or twisting course

Etymology

From Old English 'wandrian', meaning 'to roam, go about, or wander'.

2. VERB

/ ˈwɑːndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
to move about aimlessly or without a fixed destination, often in search of food or work
move aimlessly without destination

Examples

The dog likes to wander through the fields on sunny afternoons.
She wandered the city streets with no particular destination in mind.
During the recession, many workers were forced to wander from town to town looking for jobs.

Synset Examples

The gypsies roamed the woods
roving vagabonds
the wandering Jew
The cattle roam across the prairie
the laborers drift from one town to the next
They rolled from town to town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to roam - to move about or travel aimlessly or unsystematically, especially over a wide area
  • Synonym: drift - to move passively, aimlessly, or involuntarily into a certain situation or condition
  • Synonym: stray - to move away aimlessly from a group or from the right course or place

Etymology

From Old English 'wandrian', meaning to move about aimlessly.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01885173-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈwɑndər / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
(of a person's attention, thoughts, or speech) to lose focus or go off the main subject; to digress or stray in direction or clarity.
to stray from the main topic or focus

Examples

Her mind tends to wander during long meetings, making it hard to keep up.
Try not to let your speech wander too much, or the audience may get confused.
His attention would wander whenever the discussion became too technical.

Synset Examples

She always digresses when telling a story
her mind wanders
Don't digress when you give a lecture

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to digress - to leave the main subject temporarily in speech or writing
  • Synonym: ramble - to talk or write at length in a confused or inconsequential way
  • Antonym: focus - to pay particular attention to one subject
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00782620-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈwɑːndər / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
to move from place to place without a fixed course, often in a relaxed or aimless way
move without fixed course or aim

Examples

After dinner, we wandered into town to explore the streets.
She likes to wander through the forest, enjoying the sights and sounds of nature.
Rather than following a map, he just wandered around the city all afternoon.

Synset Examples

After dinner, we wandered into town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to roam - to move about aimlessly or without any destination
  • Synonym: stray - to move away from the correct path or place
  • Antonym: march - to walk with regular steps in a specified direction

Etymology

From Old English wandrian (to move about), related to Dutch wandelen (to walk).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
