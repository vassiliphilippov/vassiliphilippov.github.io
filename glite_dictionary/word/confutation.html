confutation

1. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːnfjuˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒnfjuˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of proving a statement or theory to be wrong or false, often through evidence or argument
act of proving something false

Examples

Her carefully constructed argument was met with immediate confutation from the opposing lawyer.
The scientist provided compelling confutation of the outdated theory in her recent paper.
Despite his claims, no confutation of the allegations has been presented.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: refutation - the act of proving a statement or theory to be wrong

Etymology

From Latin confutatio (disproof, refutation), from confutare (to confute, refute).

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːnfjuˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒnfjuˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of proving a statement, argument, or theory to be wrong or false, especially in a conclusive way
conclusive act of refuting

Examples

The lawyer's confutation of the witness's testimony was both thorough and convincing.
His essay offered a powerful confutation of the opposing theory.
The scientist's confutation of the old hypothesis changed the direction of research in the field.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: refutation - the act of proving something wrong or false
  • Antonym: confirmation - the act of proving something to be true or valid

Etymology

From Latin confutatio (disproving, refutation), from confutare (to confute).
