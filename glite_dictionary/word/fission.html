fission

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˈfɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
A nuclear reaction in which a heavy atomic nucleus splits into two or more smaller nuclei, with the release of energy.
splitting of a nucleus with energy release

Examples

Nuclear fission powers many modern reactors for electricity generation.
The process of fission produces hazardous radioactive waste.
Scientists study fission to better understand how nuclear weapons work.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fusion - a nuclear reaction in which two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus

Etymology

From Latin 'fissio' (a cleaving, splitting), from 'findere' (to split).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˈfɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
Reproduction in which a single-celled organism divides into two roughly equal and independent cells.
cell division; reproduction by splitting
medical

Examples

Many bacteria reproduce rapidly through fission, allowing colonies to expand quickly.
During fission, the parent cell splits in half to form two new cells.
Unicellular organisms like amoebas multiply by binary fission.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mitosis - cell division resulting in two genetically identical nuclei
  • Antonym: budding - form of asexual reproduction where new organism grows from body of parent

Etymology

From Latin fissio (a cleaving or splitting), from the verb findere (to split).
