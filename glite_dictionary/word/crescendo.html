crescendo

1. ADJ

/ krəˈʃɛndoʊ / (AmE), / krɛˈʃɛndəʊ / (BrE)
(of music) gradually increasing in loudness or intensity.
gradually increasing in loudness (music)

Examples

The orchestra played the passage with a crescendo that built excitement in the audience.
As the music swelled in a crescendo, the dancers moved faster across the stage.
The movement ended with a dramatic crescendo that filled the concert hall with sound.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: diminuendo - gradually decreasing in loudness

Etymology

From Italian 'crescendo', gerund of 'crescere' (to grow), from Latin 'crescere'.

2. NOUN

/ krəˈʃɛn.doʊ / (AmE), / krəˈʃen.dəʊ / (BrE)
(music) a gradual increase in loudness or intensity, especially in a piece of music.
gradual increase in loudness (music)

Examples

The orchestra reached a dramatic crescendo at the end of the piece.
The thunderstorm built up to a deafening crescendo before suddenly fading away.
Her voice rose in a powerful crescendo, captivating the entire audience.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: diminuendo - a gradual decrease in loudness (music)

Etymology

From Italian 'crescendo', gerund of 'crescere' (to grow), from Latin 'crescere'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04997743-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ krəˈʃɛn.doʊ / (AmE), / krɛˈʃɛn.dəʊ / (BrE)
(of sound, especially music) to gradually increase in loudness or intensity.
grow louder (music)

Examples

The orchestra began to crescendo as the piece reached its climax.
The thunder crescendoed before finally fading away in the distance.
As the argument continued, the voices in the room crescendoed to a fever pitch.

Synset Examples

The music crescendoes here

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fade - to gradually become quieter or disappear (in sound/music)

Etymology

From Italian 'crescendo', gerund of 'crescere' (to grow), from Latin 'crescere'.
