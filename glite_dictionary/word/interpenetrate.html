to interpenetrate

1. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpɛnəˌtreɪt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpɛnɪtreɪt / (BrE)
to penetrate or pass into one another; to be interlocked or mutually infiltrated
penetrate or blend into each other

Examples

In some cultures, religious and secular beliefs interpenetrate almost seamlessly.
The themes of love and loss interpenetrate throughout the novel.
The two businesses interpenetrate each other's markets through strategic partnerships.

Synset Examples

The territories of two married people interpenetrate a lot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to penetrate - to pass into or through something

Etymology

From Latin inter- (between) + penetrare (to penetrate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01229799-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpɛnəˌtreɪt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpɛnɪtreɪt / (BrE)
to spread or diffuse through something so that elements mix or permeate each other
to spread or permeate through

Examples

The blue and red lights interpenetrated to create a mesmerizing purple glow.
Ancient philosophies often interpenetrate, sharing ideas and influences across cultures.
A sense of anxiety interpenetrated the atmosphere of the courtroom as the verdict was read.

Synset Examples

An atmosphere of distrust has permeated this administration
music penetrated the entire building
His campaign was riddled with accusations and personal attacks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to permeate - to spread throughout or be diffused through something
  • Synonym: to penetrate - to go into or through something

Etymology

From Latin 'inter-' (between) + 'penetrare' (to penetrate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01231635-v
Synset POS: v
