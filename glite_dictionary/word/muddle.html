muddle

1. VERB

/ ˈmʌdəl / (AmE), / ˈmʌdəl / (BrE)
to mix up or confuse things, making them unclear or difficult to understand
to mix up or confuse

Examples

He managed to muddle the instructions so no one knew what to do.
If you muddle your facts, the argument becomes much weaker.
Try not to muddle the details in your presentation.

Synset Examples

He muddled the issues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to confuse - to make something unclear by mixing things up
  • Antonym: to clarify - to make something easier to understand

Etymology

Probably from Middle Dutch *moddelen* (to make muddy) or related Low German forms.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌd.əl / (AmE), / ˈmʌd.əl / (BrE)
a confused or disorganized situation; a state of disorder or confusion, often causing difficulty
a confused or disorganized situation
informal

Examples

He always seems to find himself in a muddle when trying to organize his finances.
After the sudden resignation, the company was left in a bit of a muddle.
The instructions were so unclear that I ended up in a complete muddle.

Synset Examples

he got into a terrible fix
he made a muddle of his marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mess - a situation that is dirty, confused, or full of problems
  • Antonym: order - a state in which everything is arranged or organized properly

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle Dutch 'model' meaning 'dregs' or from 'mud'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14432893-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmʌdəl / (AmE), / ˈmʌdəl / (BrE)
a confused or untidy state of things; a situation where things are mixed together in a disorganized way
a confused or disorganized state

Examples

Her desk was such a muddle that she couldn't find anything she needed.
After the move, their lives were in a complete muddle for weeks.
The report was lost in a muddle of papers on his table.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mess - a situation that is dirty, disorganized, or full of problems
  • Antonym: order - a state of cleanliness, organization, or arrangement

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'model', meaning a mess, of uncertain origin.
