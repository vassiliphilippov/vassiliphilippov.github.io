freight

1. VERB

/ freɪt / (AmE), / freɪt / (BrE)
to transport goods or cargo commercially by land, sea, or air
transport goods commercially

Examples

The company freights agricultural produce across the country by rail.
Most goods are freighted overseas in large shipping containers.
They decided to freight the equipment instead of sending it by air.

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *vrecht* (transport), related to Old French *freter* (to hire for transport).

2. VERB

/ freɪt / (AmE), / freɪt / (BrE)
to load or transport goods for shipment, especially commercially
to load or transport goods

Examples

The company will freight the equipment overseas by cargo ship.
They freighted the goods by rail to reduce costs.
Several trucks were freighting supplies to the disaster area.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ship - to send or transport goods by any means
  • Antonym: to unload - to remove goods from a vehicle or container

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'vrecht' meaning 'cargo, freight'.

3. NOUN

/ freɪt / (AmE), / freɪt / (BrE)
goods transported in bulk by train, truck, ship, or aircraft
goods transported in bulk

Examples

The train was carrying a large amount of freight across the country.
Most commercial trucks in this region are used for freight transport rather than passengers.
The port specializes in handling international freight shipped by cargo vessels.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cargo - goods carried by a ship, aircraft, or vehicle
  • Antonym: passenger - a person who is traveling in a vehicle but is not operating it

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *vracht* (freight, charge), influenced by French *fret* (freight).

Examples

Due to high demand, freight shipments may take longer to arrive than usual.
Railroads have played a major role in American freight transport for over a century.

Related Terms

Examples
