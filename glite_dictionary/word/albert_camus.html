Albert Camus

1. PROPN

/ ælˌbɛr kæˈmu / (AmE), / ælˌbɛː kaˈmyː / (BrE)
(1913–1960) French writer and philosopher, noted for exploring themes of absurdity and the human condition; author of works such as 'The Stranger' and 'The Myth of Sisyphus'.
French novelist and philosopher (1913–1960)

Examples

Albert Camus won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1957 for his contribution to modern thought.
Many scholars discuss Albert Camus when analyzing existential and absurdist philosophy.
The works of Albert Camus continue to influence writers and philosophers worldwide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Jean-Paul Sartre - French philosopher and contemporary of Camus known for existentialism

Etymology

French form of the name Albert (from Germanic roots) and the surname Camus.
