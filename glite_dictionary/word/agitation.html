agitation

1. NOUN

/ ˌædʒɪˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌædʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
a state of extreme emotional disturbance, marked by restlessness, anxiety, or excitement
intense emotional disturbance

Examples

His constant agitation made it impossible for him to concentrate on his work.
The news of the accident caused great agitation among the family members.
She paced the room in growing agitation as she waited for the phone call.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: calm - a state of tranquility or absence of agitation
  • Synonym: unrest - a state of disturbance or turmoil, especially emotional

Etymology

From Latin agitare (to drive, excite), from agere (to do, drive).

2. NOUN

/ ˌædʒɪˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌadʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of stirring or shaking something, especially vigorously
the act of shaking something

Examples

The agitation of the liquid caused bubbles to form at the surface.
During washing, the agitation helps to remove dirt from clothes.
The scientist explained that proper agitation ensures all ingredients mix thoroughly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stirring - the act of moving something about in a circular way
  • Synonym: shaking - the act of moving something violently back and forth or up and down

Etymology

From Latin agitāre (to put in motion, stir) via French or directly from Late Latin agitātiō (motion, disturbance).

3. NOUN

/ ˌædʒɪˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌædʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
a state of disturbance or unrest in a group, often arising from protest or social unrest
disturbance due to protest or unrest

Examples

The city was in a state of agitation following the announcement of the new law.
Widespread agitation among workers led to a series of strikes.
Political agitation often results in changes to government policies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: unrest - a state of dissatisfaction, disturbance, and agitation in a group of people
  • Antonym: calm - a state of peace or tranquility, especially in a group setting

Etymology

From Latin 'agitare', meaning 'to stir up' or 'to agitate'.
