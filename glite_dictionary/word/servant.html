servant

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɜːrvənt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːvənt / (BrE)
a person who works for another, especially in someone's home, performing domestic duties or other services
person working for another, usually in a household

Examples

The wealthy family employed a live-in servant to help with cleaning and cooking.
As a servant, she was expected to be discreet and efficient in her duties.
In the 19th century, it was common for large households to have several servants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: master - person in authority over servants
  • Synonym: employee - someone who works for another person or a company

Etymology

From Old French 'servant' (servant, helper), from Latin 'serviens', present participle of 'servire' (to serve).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɝːvənt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːvənt / (BrE)
A person or thing that serves another or is in a subordinate position to a greater power, function, or purpose.
person or thing in a subordinate role

Examples

She saw herself as a humble servant of the community, always putting others first.
In modern democracies, the government is often described as the servant of the people.
The press, as a servant of democracy, must remain free and independent.

Synset Examples

theology should be the handmaiden of ethics
the state cannot be a servant of the church

Related Terms

  • Antonym: master - one who has control or authority over others
  • Synonym: handmaiden - a person or thing that is subservient or subordinate to another

Etymology

From Old French 'servant', present participle of 'servir' (to serve), from Latin 'servire'.
