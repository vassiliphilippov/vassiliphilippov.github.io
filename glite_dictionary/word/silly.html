silly

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɪli / (AmE), / ˈsɪli / (BrE)
showing a lack of good sense or judgment; foolish or absurd.
foolish or absurd

Examples

He looked silly wearing that giant clown wig to the party.
It was a silly mistake, but fortunately no one was hurt.
The idea sounded silly at first, but it actually worked.

Synset Examples

wore a goofy hat
a silly idea
some wacky plan for selling more books

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foolish - lacking good sense or judgment
  • Antonym: sensible - having or showing good sense or judgment

Etymology

From Old English 'sǣlig' (happy, fortunate), later developing the sense 'foolish'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɪli / (AmE), / ˈsɪli / (BrE)
Having or showing a lack of seriousness or common sense; characterized by frivolous or trivial behavior.
lacking seriousness; frivolous

Examples

Stop acting so silly during the meeting and try to focus.
The children exchanged silly jokes and giggled all afternoon.
She wore a silly hat that made everyone laugh.

Synset Examples

a dizzy blonde
light-headed teenagers
silly giggles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frivolous - not serious or sensible; carefree
  • Antonym: serious - acting or speaking sincerely and in earnest

Etymology

From Middle English *sely* (happy, innocent, pitiable), from Old English *sælig* (happy, fortunate).

Source: WordNet

5. ADJ

/ ˈsɪli / (AmE), / ˈsɪli / (BrE)
Deserving of scornful pity; so lacking in judgment or dignity as to inspire amused or contemptuous pity.
deserving scornful or pitying laughter

Examples

She felt silly standing in the rain, waiting for someone who would never come.
The old man's desperate attempts to impress the crowd seemed more silly than amusing.
His silly dreams of fame earned him nothing but quiet pity from his friends.

Synset Examples

how silly an ardent and unsuccessful wooer can be especially if he is getting on in years

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foolish - lacking good sense or judgment
  • Synonym: pitiful - arousing pity, often mingled with scorn
  • Antonym: dignified - having or showing a composed or serious manner worthy of respect

Etymology

From Old English *sælig* (happy, innocent), later evolving to 'lacking good sense'.
