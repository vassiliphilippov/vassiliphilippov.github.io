capillary

1. ADJ

/ kəˈpɪl.ɛr.i / (AmE), / kəˈpɪl.əri / (BrE)
(of a tube or vessel) long and slender with a very small internal diameter.
narrow, slender tube or vessel

Examples

The scientists observed how the liquid rose rapidly in the capillary tube.
A pipette is a type of capillary tube used in many laboratory experiments.
Water movement in soil often occurs through capillary spaces between particles.

Synset Examples

a capillary tube

Etymology

From Latin capillaris, from capillus (hair), referring to the hair-like thinness.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæpɪˌlɛri / (AmE), / kəˈpɪləri / (BrE)
any of the very small blood vessels that connect arterioles and venules, allowing exchange of substances between blood and tissues
tiny blood vessel between arteries and veins
medical

Examples

Oxygen passes from the alveoli into the capillaries to enter the bloodstream.
The doctor explained how damaged capillaries can cause small red marks on the skin.
Nutrients are exchanged for waste products in the network of capillaries.

Etymology

From Latin *capillaris* (of hair), from *capillus* (hair), referencing the fine, hair-like structure.

3. NOUN

/ kəˈpɪləri / (AmE), / kəˈpɪləri / (BrE)
A very thin tube, especially one that holds or moves liquid through capillary action.
very thin tube for holding liquid

Examples

The scientist used a capillary to transfer a tiny amount of liquid.
A capillary is often used in laboratories to measure small volumes accurately.
Because of its narrow diameter, a capillary can draw up liquid against gravity.

Etymology

From Latin capillaris (of hair), from capillus (hair), referring to the tube's hair-like thinness.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
