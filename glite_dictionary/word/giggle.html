giggle

1. VERB

/ ˈɡɪɡəl / (AmE), / ˈɡɪɡl / (BrE)
to laugh with repeated short, high sounds, often in a nervous or silly way
to laugh in a light or nervous way

Examples

The group couldn't help but giggle during the awkward silence.
She would often giggle nervously when asked a difficult question.
The children began to giggle at the funny faces their teacher made.

Synset Examples

The girls giggled when the rock star came into the classroom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chuckle - to laugh quietly or inwardly
  • Synonym: snicker - to laugh in a partly suppressed, disrespectful manner
  • Antonym: sob - to cry noisily with short, sudden breaths

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, representing the sound of such laughter.

3. VERB

/ ˈɡɪɡəl / (AmE), / ˈɡɪɡl / (BrE)
to laugh in a silly or nervous way, often with a series of short, high-pitched sounds
laugh in a silly or nervous way

Examples

The children couldn't stop giggle during the puppet show.
She tried not to giggle when the teacher mispronounced the word.
He often giggles nervously when he's asked a difficult question.

Synset Examples

She giggled at his doot during the quiet moment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chuckle - to laugh quietly or to oneself
  • Synonym: snicker - to laugh quietly in a disrespectful or covert way
  • Antonym: sob - to cry noisily, often convulsively

Etymology

Probably imitative in origin, first recorded in the 16th century.
