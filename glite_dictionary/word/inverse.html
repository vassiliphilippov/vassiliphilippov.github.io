inverse

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌvɜrs / (AmE), / ˈɪnvɜːs / (BrE)
something that is the opposite or reverse in order, direction, or effect, especially compared to something else
the opposite or reverse of something

Examples

When the standard method didn't work, he decided to try the inverse.
The inverse of the function can be difficult to compute.
Success and failure are sometimes just inverses of each other.

Synset Examples

when the direct approach failed, he tried the inverse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: opposite - a person or thing that is totally different from or the reverse of someone or something else
  • Synonym: reverse - the opposite or contrary to something

Etymology

From Latin 'inversus', past participle of 'invertere' (to turn upside down, invert).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈvɜrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɜːs / (BrE)
reversed in order, nature, or effect; opposite or contrary in position, direction, or relationship
reversed in order or effect

Examples

There is an inverse relationship between price and demand in economics.
The inverse effect was observed when the conditions were altered.
If you increase the pressure, the volume decreases in an inverse manner.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: opposite - completely different or contrary in position, direction, or meaning
  • Antonym: direct - moving in a straight line or by the shortest course; not reversed

Etymology

From Latin inversus, past participle of invertere (to turn upside down).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00203687-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈɪnˌvɜrs / (AmE), / ˈɪnvɜːs / (BrE)
(of a relationship or effect) opposite in nature or effect to something else, especially so that as one increases, the other decreases.
opposite in effect or relation

Examples

There is an inverse relationship between price and demand in many markets.
His energy levels showed an inverse pattern to his mood—he felt tired when he was happiest.
In physics, resistance and current often have an inverse connection.

Synset Examples

a term is in inverse proportion to another term if it increases (or decreases) as the other decreases (or increases)

Related Terms

  • Antonym: direct - having a relationship where one quantity increases as the other increases

Etymology

From Latin inversus, past participle of invertere 'to turn upside down'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00773049-a
