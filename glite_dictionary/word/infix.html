infix

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈfɪks / (AmE), / ɪnˈfɪks / (BrE)
(Linguistics) To insert a morpheme into the body of a word stem.
insert a morpheme into a stem word
medical

Examples

In Tagalog, linguists often infix a syllable to indicate a completed action.
The teacher showed the students how to infix certain particles into verbs to change their meaning.
Some rare English slang forms are created when speakers infix expletives for emphasis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affix - morpheme attached to a word stem in any position

Etymology

From Latin 'infixus', past participle of 'infigere' (to fasten in, insert).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01301131-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌfɪks / (AmE), / ˈɪn.fɪks / (BrE)
An affix inserted into the middle of a word, rather than at its beginning or end.
an affix inserted within a word

Examples

In Tagalog, the word 'sulat' can take the infix '-um-' to become 'sumulat'.
Some languages use an infix to convey tense or aspect within a verb stem.
The linguistics professor explained how an infix differs from a prefix or suffix.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affix - a grammatical element added to a word, including prefixes, suffixes, and infixes

Etymology

From Latin 'in-' (into) + 'figere' (to fix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06320262-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˌɪnˈfɪks / (AmE), / ˌɪnˈfɪks / (BrE)
to insert or introduce something into or between other things, especially into the middle of something
to insert or introduce into

Examples

The teacher asked the students to infix the quotation into their essays.
In some languages, you can infix particles into root words.
He tried to infix a sense of hope into the conversation.

Synset Examples

insert a picture into the text

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to place or fit something into another thing
  • Synonym: to embed - to fix or set firmly in a surrounding mass

Etymology

From Latin 'inficere', from 'in-' (into) + 'facere' (to make, to do).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01424276-v
Synset POS: v
