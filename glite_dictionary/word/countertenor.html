countertenor

1. ADJ

/ ˈkaʊn.tɚˌtɛn.ɚ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.təˌtɛn.ə / (BrE)
(of a singing voice) describing or relating to the highest male voice, with a range above that of a tenor.
having the highest male singing voice

Examples

He was cast as the lead due to his remarkable countertenor range.
The composer wrote an aria specifically for a countertenor voice.
His countertenor performance captivated the entire audience.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: tenor - the highest adult male singing voice below countertenor
  • Degree Higher: alto - the voice part above tenor but commonly sung by women or boys

Etymology

From Italian 'contratenore', from Latin 'contra' (against) + 'tenor'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊn.tɚˌtɛn.ɚ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.təˌtɛn.ə / (BrE)
a male singer with a singing voice higher than that of a tenor, typically singing in an alto range
male singer with a high voice (above tenor)

Examples

The countertenor sang the opening aria with remarkable clarity and power.
Many Baroque operas feature roles written specifically for a countertenor.
He was well-known for his unique ability as a countertenor in the choir.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: tenor - a male singer with a high adult male voice
  • Degree Higher: alto - a voice range typically higher than tenor and sung by women or boy singers

Etymology

From Latin 'contratenor', meaning 'against the tenor' (i.e., counter-melody).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊntɚˌtɛnɚ / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntətɛnə / (BrE)
an adult male singer with a voice range equivalent to a contralto or mezzo-soprano, the highest of the adult male singing voices
the highest adult male singing voice

Examples

The lead role in the opera was sung by a world-renowned countertenor.
Many baroque choral works feature parts written specifically for the countertenor.
Despite being a man, he amazed the judges with the pure tone of his countertenor voice.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: alto - a voice or instrument with a range between soprano and tenor
  • Degree Lower: tenor - a male singing voice between baritone and alto in range

Etymology

From Italian 'contratenore', via French 'contre-ténor', meaning 'against (counter) tenor'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06885835-n
Synset POS: n
