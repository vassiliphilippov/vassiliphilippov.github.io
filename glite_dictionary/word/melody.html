melody

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɛlədi / (AmE), / ˈmɛlədi / (BrE)
a sequence of musical notes arranged in a particular rhythmic and tonal pattern, forming a recognizable and distinctive tune
sequence of musical notes; tune

Examples

The violinist played a beautiful melody that brought tears to the audience's eyes.
She found herself humming the familiar melody from her childhood favorite song.
The composer experimented with a new melody to make the piece more memorable.

Synset Examples

she was humming an air from Beethoven

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tune - a melody, especially a simple and easily remembered one

Etymology

From Old French *melodie*, from Late Latin *melodia*, from Greek *melōidia* (singing, chanting).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɛlədi / (AmE), / ˈmɛlədi / (BrE)
a sequence of musical notes that are perceived as a single, coherent, and pleasant entity; the tuneful aspect of music.
a tuneful sequence of musical notes

Examples

The composer created a beautiful melody that lingered in my mind long after the concert ended.
She hummed a gentle melody while working in the garden.
The melody of the song is simple, but its lyrics are powerful.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tune - a melody, especially a simple and catchy one
  • Antonym: harmony - the combination of different musical notes played or sung at the same time

Etymology

From Old French 'melodie', from Late Latin 'melodia', from Greek 'melōidia' (singing, chant).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05719862-n
Synset POS: n
