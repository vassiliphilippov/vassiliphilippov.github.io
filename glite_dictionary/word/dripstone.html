dripstone

1. NOUN

/ ˈdrɪpˌstoʊn / (AmE), / ˈdrɪp.stəʊn / (BrE)
A form of calcium carbonate deposited from dripping water, often found in stalactites and stalagmites within caves.
calcium carbonate in stalactites or stalagmites

Examples

The cave's ceiling was covered in dripstone formations created over thousands of years.
Scientists collected samples of dripstone to study ancient climate conditions.
Some of the world's most impressive stalagmites are made from pure dripstone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: travertine - a form of limestone deposited by mineral springs, often forming cave features

2. NOUN

/ ˈdrɪpstoʊn / (AmE), / ˈdrɪpstəʊn / (BrE)
A projecting stone ledge or molding, usually over a window or door, designed to deflect rainwater away from the building’s surface.
projecting stone to deflect rainwater

Examples

The mason carefully carved the dripstone to fit above the cathedral's arched windows.
Rainwater trickled harmlessly off the dripstone, sparing the wall below from erosion.
During restoration, the original 18th-century dripstones were discovered intact above the doorways.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hoodmould - an architectural molding over a window or door, in the same function

Etymology

From 'drip' + 'stone'; refers to its purpose and material.
