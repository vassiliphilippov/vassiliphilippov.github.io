Carolingian

1. NOUN

/ ˌkærəˈlɪndʒiən / (AmE), / ˌkærəˈlɪndʒiən / (BrE)
a member of the Carolingian dynasty, a Frankish noble family that ruled parts of Western Europe from the 8th to 10th centuries
member of the Carolingian dynasty

Examples

The museum features artifacts once owned by a prominent Carolingian.
Some historians argue that no Carolingian matched Charlemagne’s influence.
The lands ruled by each Carolingian often shifted due to inheritance customs.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Merovingian - a member of the dynasty preceding the Carolingians in France

Etymology

From French Carolingien, from Medieval Latin Carolingus (relating to Charles/Charlemagne).

2. ADJ

/ ˌkɛrəˈlɪndʒiən / (AmE), / ˌkarəˈlɪndʒiən / (BrE)
of or relating to the Frankish dynasty founded by Charlemagne's father, or to its period of rule (about 751–987 AD).
of the Frankish Carolingian dynasty or era

Examples

The Carolingian Empire played a crucial role in shaping medieval Europe.
Scholars have studied Carolingian art for its unique blend of classical and Germanic influences.
A Carolingian manuscript was discovered in the monastery's ancient library.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Merovingian - of or relating to the earlier Frankish dynasty preceding the Carolingians

Etymology

From French Carolingien, from Medieval Latin Carolus (Charles) + -ingian (dynastic suffix).
