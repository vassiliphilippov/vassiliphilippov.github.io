ablative

1. NOUN

/ ˈæblətɪv / (AmE), / ˈablətɪv / (BrE)
a grammatical case used in some languages to indicate the agent, instrument, manner, or place of an action
a grammatical case indicating agent or means

Examples

In Latin, the ablative case often expresses means or manner.
The teacher explained the function of the ablative in Hungarian grammar.
Many students find the ablative case challenging when learning ancient Greek.

Etymology

From Latin 'ablativus', from 'ablat-', past participle stem of 'auferre' (to carry away).

2. ADJ

/ ˈæbləˌtɪv / (AmE), / ˈæblətɪv / (BrE)
(of a material) designed to be removed or vaporized at very high temperatures, especially to protect something from heat.
designed to be removed at high temperatures

Examples

The rocket's nose cone was coated with an ablative material to withstand reentry heat.
Engineers selected an ablative shield for the spacecraft to ensure safe passage through the atmosphere.
An ablative coating burns away gradually, carrying heat away from the underlying surface.

Synset Examples

ablative material on a rocket cone

Related Terms

  • Antonym: non-ablative - not designed to be removed or vaporized at high temperatures

Etymology

From Latin ablativus, from ablatus (removed), past participle of auferre (to carry away).

relating to or denoting the ablative case, a grammatical case used in some languages to express motion away from, origin, or means.
relating to the ablative grammatical case

Examples
