rhetorical

1. ADJ

/ rəˈtɔːrɪkəl / (AmE), / rɪˈtɒrɪkəl / (BrE)
focusing on language style or persuasive effect rather than on meaningful content or substance
relating to style over substance in speech or writing

Examples

Many politicians give rhetorical speeches that lack concrete plans or details.
Her argument was criticized as being merely rhetorical rather than substantive.
The essay was full of rhetorical flourishes but said very little of substance.

Synset Examples

mere rhetorical frippery

Related Terms

  • Antonym: substantive - focused on important or meaningful content

Etymology

From Late Latin rhetoricalis (pertaining to oratorical style), from Greek rhētorikos.

2. ADJ

/ ɹəˈtɔɹɪkəl / (AmE), / rɪˈtɒrɪkəl / (BrE)
relating to or concerned with the art of rhetoric or effective use of language; often used to describe language intended to persuade or impress.
relating to rhetoric or effective language use

Examples

Her speech was full of rhetorical flourishes that impressed the audience.
The editor made only minor rhetorical changes to the original text.
He asked a rhetorical question to emphasize his point.

Synset Examples

accepted two or three verbal and rhetorical changes I suggested
the rhetorical sin of the meaningless variation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oratorical - relating to the skill or art of public speaking
  • Antonym: literal - taking words in their usual or most basic sense without metaphor or exaggeration

Etymology

From Latin *rhetoricus*, from Greek *rhetorikos* (oratorical).
