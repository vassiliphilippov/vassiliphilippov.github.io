meiosis

1. NOUN

/ maɪˈoʊsɪs / (AmE), / mɪˈəʊsɪs / (BrE)
The deliberate use of understatement for emphasis or rhetorical effect, often achieved by negating the contrary; a type of rhetorical figure.
understatement for rhetorical effect

Examples

The author used meiosis when she described the disastrous event as 'a minor inconvenience.'
By calling the huge storm 'a bit of rain,' he employed rhetorical meiosis.
Saying someone is 'not unhappy' when they are actually thrilled is an example of meiosis.

Synset Examples

saying ‘I was not a little upset’ when you mean ‘I was very upset’ is an example of litotes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: litotes - figure of speech using negation of the contrary for emphasis

Etymology

From Greek *meíōsis* (a lessening), from *meioun* (to diminish).

2. NOUN

/ maɪˈoʊsɪs / (AmE), / mɪˈəʊsɪs / (BrE)
A type of cell division in sexually reproducing organisms that results in four daughter cells, each with half the number of chromosomes of the parent cell, producing gametes in animals or spores in plants.
cell division forming gametes/spores
medical

Examples

During meiosis, the chromosome number is reduced by half to form haploid gametes.
Errors in meiosis can lead to genetic disorders in the offspring.
The process of meiosis ensures genetic diversity in sexually reproducing organisms.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mitosis - ordinary cell division resulting in two identical cells

Etymology

From Greek meíōsis (a lessening), from meíoun ‘to make smaller’.
