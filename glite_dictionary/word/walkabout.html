walkabout

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɔkəˌbaʊt / (AmE), / ˈwɔːkəˌbaʊt / (BrE)
an informal public walk by a celebrity or public figure to meet and interact with members of the public
public stroll by a celebrity to meet people
british

Examples

The royal couple went on a walkabout to greet the crowds gathered outside the palace.
During her walkabout, the pop star signed autographs and posed for selfies with fans.
Politicians often do a walkabout before elections to connect with voters.

Etymology

Originally Australian English; from 19th-century Aboriginal English referring to a wandering or journey, later adopted for this public context.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɔk.əˌbaʊt / (AmE), / ˈwɔː.kə.baʊt / (BrE)
(in Australian Aboriginal culture) a temporary journey into the bush, especially as undertaken by an Aboriginal person as a rite or for spiritual renewal.
Aboriginal journey into the bush
british

Examples

He went on a walkabout to reconnect with his ancestral land.
The young men prepared for their traditional walkabout in the Australian outback.
During her walkabout, she followed ancient paths used by her people for generations.

Etymology

From Australian English, combining 'walk' and 'about', used since the early 20th century to describe this Aboriginal practice.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00294261-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɔkəˌbaʊt / (AmE), / ˈwɔːkəˌbaʊt / (BrE)
a journey made by walking, especially in the Australian outback, sometimes for travel or for cultural reasons; more generally, an informal walking tour or excursion
a walking journey or informal walking tour

Examples

The prime minister went on a walkabout to meet local residents and hear their concerns.
During our trip to Australia, we took a walkabout through the national park.
He decided to go on a walkabout to clear his mind and enjoy the scenery.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hike - a long walk for pleasure or exercise
  • Synonym: excursion - a short journey or trip, especially one taken as a leisure activity

Etymology

From Australian Aboriginal English, originally referring to a ritual journey in the bush.
