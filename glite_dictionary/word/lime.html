lime

1. VERB

/ laɪm / (AmE), / laɪm / (BrE)
to spread lime (a calcium-containing substance) onto soil or land to improve its quality and promote plant growth
treat soil by adding lime

Examples

Every spring, the gardener limes the vegetable beds to balance the soil's pH.
It is important to lime your lawn if the soil is too acidic.
Farmers often lime their fields to improve crop yields.

Synset Examples

lime the lawn

Etymology

From Middle English lim, from Old English līm, meaning 'sticky substance, lime'.

2. NOUN

/ laɪm / (AmE), / laɪm / (BrE)
A deciduous tree of the genus Tilia, with heart-shaped leaves and fragrant yellowish flowers, some species of which provide valuable timber.
deciduous tree of the genus Tilia

Examples

The ancient lime stands tall in the village square, its flowers perfuming the air each spring.
Carpenters highly value the wood from the lime for its fine grain and workability.
The park was lined with lime trees, providing deep shade during the hottest months.

Etymology

From Old English *lind*, via Middle English *lind*, related to the German *Linde* (lime tree).

4. NOUN

/ laɪm / (AmE), / laɪm / (BrE)
A white caustic substance (calcium oxide) produced by heating limestone, used especially in building and in agriculture.
white caustic substance from limestone

Examples

The walls were coated with lime to help preserve them from moisture.
Old kilns were used for producing lime for mortar and plaster.
Adding lime to farmland can improve soil quality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quicklime - calcium oxide, produced by heating limestone, main form of lime
  • Degree Lower: slaked lime - calcium hydroxide, produced by adding water to lime

Etymology

From Old English līm (sticky substance, birdlime, mortar), from Proto-Germanic *līmaz.

7. NOUN

/ laɪm / (AmE), / laɪm / (BrE)
a white, alkaline substance consisting mainly of calcium oxide, used especially in building materials and agriculture

Examples

The builders mixed lime with sand and water to create mortar for the walls.
Exposure to lime dust can irritate the eyes and lungs, so proper protection is advised.
When heated, limestone breaks down to form lime and carbon dioxide.

Related Terms

8. NOUN

/ laɪm / (AmE), / laɪm / (BrE)
A tropical or subtropical tree of the citrus family, especially Citrus aurantiifolia, that bears the small, greenish-yellow, acidic fruit known as a lime.
a fruit tree of the citrus family

Examples

The farmer planted several lime trees to supply his restaurant with fresh fruit.
A lime tree requires a warm climate and plenty of sunshine to thrive.
Many commercial orchards grow both lemon and lime trees for the market.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lemon - a citrus tree bearing yellow fruit

Etymology

From Arabic 'līma', via French and Spanish, originally from Persian 'limu'.
