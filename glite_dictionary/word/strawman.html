strawman

1. NOUN

/ ˈstrɔˌmæn / (AmE), / ˈstrɔːmæn / (BrE)
An effigy, often roughly in the shape of a human, made of straw and set up to scare birds away from crops.
effigy of a man made of straw to scare birds

Examples

The farmer placed a strawman in the middle of the field to protect his crops.
As we walked by the cornfield, a tattered strawman stood guarding the rows.
The children dressed the strawman in old clothes and a floppy hat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scarecrow - object shaped like a person used to scare birds

2. NOUN

/ ˈstrɔˌmæn / (AmE), / ˈstrɔːˌman / (BrE)
a person who is used as a front or cover for some questionable or illicit activity, often to conceal the identity of those actually responsible
person used as a front for questionable activities

Examples

He acted as a strawman for the real owners of the company.
The politician used a strawman to hide his financial interests.
Investigators revealed that the buyer was merely a strawman for a secretive group.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: front man - a person who serves as a cover for others in business or crime

Etymology

From 'straw man', originally a dummy made of straw, used as a figure of debate or deception.

3. NOUN

/ ˈstrɔːˌmæn / (AmE), / ˈstrɔːˌman / (BrE)
An argument or viewpoint that is misrepresented or exaggerated in order to be easily refuted or dismissed.
misrepresented, easily refutable argument

Examples

The politician set up a strawman to distract from the real issues at hand.
By attacking a strawman version of her opponent's idea, she avoided confronting its real merits.
It's a common fallacy to criticize a strawman rather than engage with someone's actual argument.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: red herring - a misleading or distracting argument
  • Degree Higher: argument - reasoning or set of reasons in support of an idea

Etymology

From the literal image of a man made of straw, easily destroyed, metaphorically applied to arguments in debate.

Source: WordNet
