to ensnare

1. VERB

/ ɛnˈsnɛr / (AmE), / ɪnˈsnɛə / (BrE)
to trap or catch someone or something, often by trickery or in a difficult situation
to trap or catch by trickery

Examples

The clever scheme was designed to ensnare unsuspecting tourists.
Her kindness was genuine, not a ploy to ensnare you in her plans.
Many animals are ensnared by nets set along the riverbank each year.

Synset Examples

I was set up!
The innocent man was framed by the police

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trap - to catch or take by a device or trick
  • Synonym: to entrap - to catch in or as in a trap

Etymology

From Middle English 'insnaren', from 'en-' (in) + 'snare' (trap).

2. VERB

/ ɛnˈsnɛr / (AmE), / ɪnˈsneə / (BrE)
to catch or trap someone or something, or cause them to become caught, as if in a trap
catch or trap

Examples

The hunter tried to ensnare the rabbit with a well-hidden net.
She found herself ensnared in a web of lies and deceit.
The company used clever marketing tactics to ensnare new customers.

Synset Examples

The men trap foxes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trap - to catch, usually by trickery or deception, in a trap
  • Antonym: free - to release from captivity or a trap

Etymology

From Old French 'ensnarer', based on Latin 'insnarare', from 'in-' (in) + 'snare' (noose, snare).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01483400-v
Synset POS: v
