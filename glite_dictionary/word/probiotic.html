probiotic

1. NOUN

/ ˌproʊ.baɪˈɑː.tɪk / (AmE), / ˌprəʊ.baɪˈɒt.ɪk / (BrE)
A bacterium or microorganism that is beneficial to health, especially those found in the digestive system.
beneficial microorganism, often in the gut

Examples

Some yogurt brands contain live probiotic cultures that support digestive health.
Taking a daily probiotic may help restore balance to your gut microbiome after antibiotics.
Scientists continue to research the effects of probiotic supplements on overall immunity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prebiotic - a substance that promotes the growth of beneficial microorganisms
  • Antonym: antibiotic - a substance that kills or inhibits the growth of microorganisms

Etymology

From Greek 'pro-' (for) + 'biotic', from 'bios' (life).

2. NOUN

/ proʊˌbaɪˈɑːtɪk / (AmE), / prəʊˌbaɪˈɒtɪk / (BrE)
A dietary supplement or food containing beneficial live bacteria, intended to restore or improve the natural balance of microorganisms in the gut.
supplement with beneficial live bacteria
medical

Examples

Many yogurts are marketed as containing added probiotic cultures to promote digestive health.
After taking antibiotics, her doctor recommended a daily probiotic to help restore her gut flora.
Researchers are studying the effects of probiotic supplements on immune system function.

Synset Examples

The first commercially sold dairy-based probiotic was Yakult, a fermented milk with added Lactobacillus casei, in 1935.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: antibiotic - a medicine that destroys or inhibits the growth of microorganisms

Etymology

From 'pro-' (for) and 'biotic' (relating to life or living things), first used in the 1960s based on analogy to 'antibiotic'.
