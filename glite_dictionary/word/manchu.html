Manchu

1. NOUN

/ ˈmæn.tʃu / (AmE), / ˈman.tʃuː / (BrE)
the Tungusic language traditionally spoken by the Manchu people of northeastern China
Tungusic language of the Manchu people

Examples

Very few people today are fluent speakers of Manchu.
The imperial court of the Qing dynasty conducted official business in Manchu.
She studied Manchu at university to research ancient documents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Tungusic - relating to a family of languages spoken in Siberia and northeastern China

Etymology

From Chinese Mǎnzhōu, the self-designation of the Manchu people.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmæn.tʃu / (AmE), / ˈman.tʃuː / (BrE)
a member of an indigenous people from Manchuria, northeastern China, who ruled China during the Qing dynasty (17th–20th centuries)
member of Manchurian ethnic group

Examples

The Manchu established the Qing dynasty after conquering China in the 17th century.
Many Manchu traditions are preserved in northeastern China today.
He traces his ancestry back to a prominent Manchu clan.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Chinese - a member of the ethnic group native to China

Etymology

From Chinese Mǎnzhōu (滿洲), the name of the ethnic group and region.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09757020-n
Synset POS: n

3. PROPN

/ ˈmæn.tʃu / (AmE), / ˈman.tʃuː / (BrE)
the imperial dynasty that ruled China from 1644 to 1912, also known as the Qing dynasty
Chinese imperial dynasty (1644–1912)

Examples

The Manchu dynasty lasted from the seventeenth to the early twentieth century.
Many significant reforms took place during the Manchu era in China.
The fall of the Manchu dynasty marked the end of imperial rule in China.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Qing dynasty - the last imperial dynasty of China, also called the Manchu dynasty

Etymology

From Manchu 'manju', the name of the people who founded the dynasty.
