corporal

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɔr.pɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈkɔː.pər.əl / (BrE)
relating to or affecting the body as opposed to the mind or spirit
relating to the body

Examples

Some illnesses produce corporal symptoms, while others are purely psychological.
He suffered corporal pain after the accident, but no mental trauma.
The court focused on the corporal punishment inflicted by the teacher.

Synset Examples

bodily needs
a corporal defect
corporeal suffering
a somatic symptom or somatic illness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bodily - relating to the body; physical rather than mental
  • Antonym: spiritual - relating to the mind or spirit rather than the body

Etymology

From Latin 'corporalis' (of the body), from 'corpus' (body).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɔr.pɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈkɔː.pər.əl / (BrE)
having or relating to a physical body; bodily, as opposed to spiritual.
relating to the physical body

Examples

The apparition took on a corporal form before vanishing into thin air.
Ancient philosophers debated the nature of corporal existence versus the immortal soul.
He suffered corporal pain as a result of the harsh conditions.

Synset Examples

what seemed corporal melted as breath into the wind
an incarnate spirit
‘corporate’ is an archaic term

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incarnate - embodied in flesh; given a bodily form
  • Antonym: spiritual - relating to the soul or spirit, rather than to the body or physical nature

Etymology

From Latin corporalis, from corpus (body).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɔr.pɚ.əl / (AmE), / ˈkɔː.pər.əl / (BrE)
a noncommissioned officer in the armed forces, ranking above private and below sergeant
military rank above private

Examples

After years of service, he was finally promoted to corporal.
The corporal instructed the new recruits during their first drill.
As a corporal, she was responsible for leading a small team of soldiers.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: sergeant - a noncommissioned military officer ranking above corporal
  • Degree Lower: private - lowest military rank, below corporal

Etymology

From Old French 'caporal', from Italian 'caporale', from 'capo' (head), ultimately from Latin 'caput' (head).
