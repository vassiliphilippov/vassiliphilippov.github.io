to saltate

1. VERB

/ ˈsæl.teɪt / (AmE), / ˈsæl.teɪt / (BrE)
to leap or skip, especially in a lively or rhythmic manner
leap or skip in a lively manner

Examples

The gazelles saltate gracefully across the open plain.
During the performance, the dancers would saltate in quick, light steps.
Some insects saltate as a means of escaping predators.

Synset Examples

These fish swim with a saltating motion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leap - to jump a long way, usually to a great height
  • Synonym: skip - to move lightly and quickly, making small jumps
  • Antonym: plod - to walk heavily or slowly

Etymology

From Latin saltare, meaning 'to dance' or 'to leap'.

2. VERB

/ ˈsæl.teɪt / (AmE), / ˈsæl.teɪt / (BrE)
(of particles, especially sand or biological organisms) to move by a series of leaps or jumps, particularly as a result of wind, water, or muscular action.
move by leaping or jumping in succession

Examples

The sand grains saltate across the desert, carried by strong gusts of wind.
During dry spells, fine dust particles may saltate before settling back on the ground.
Some insects saltate as a primary mode of movement through tall grasses.

Synset Examples

The sand grains are saltated by the wind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leap - to jump or spring a long way
  • Synonym: hop - to move by jumping on one foot

Etymology

From Latin saltare (to leap), frequentative of salire (to jump).
