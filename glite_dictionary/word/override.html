override

1. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvɚˌraɪd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəˌraɪd / (BrE)
a mechanism or control that allows a person to manually take control over or bypass the operation of an automatic device
manual control for bypassing automation

Examples

The security system has an override in case of emergency.
If the automatic doors get stuck, use the manual override.
The engineer engaged the override to stop the machine immediately.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bypass - a means of circumventing or ignoring a regular process

Etymology

From the verb 'override', meaning to ride over, later extended to mean to overrule or take precedence.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03871498-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈraɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈraɪd / (BrE)
to be more important than something else and therefore have the power to cancel or change it; to prevail over
be more important than; prevail over

Examples

Company policy will override any individual manager's decision.
Her desire to help others overrides her own personal interests.
Safety warnings should always override convenience in this laboratory.

Synset Examples

health considerations override financial concerns

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outweigh - to be more important or valuable than something else
  • Synonym: to supersede - to take the place of something; to replace
  • Antonym: yield - to give in to pressure or authority

Etymology

From Old English ofer- ('over') + rīdan ('to ride').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02650706-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈraɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈraɪd / (BrE)
to use one's authority to reject or cancel a decision, recommendation, or rule
reject or cancel by authority

Examples

The board decided to override the manager's proposal after much debate.
A two-thirds majority is required to override a presidential veto in Congress.
Despite the committee's recommendation, the director chose to override their decision.

Synset Examples

The Republicans were overruled when the House voted on the bill

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to overrule - to reject by exercising higher authority
  • Antonym: to uphold - to support or maintain a previous decision

Etymology

From Old English ofer- (over) + rīdan (to ride); originally meaning to ride over, later to dominate or set aside.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

To ride (a horse) too hard or for too long, causing exhaustion or harm to the animal.
ride a horse too hard

Examples

You must not override your mount if you wish it to recover quickly.
In the past, some couriers overrode their horses to deliver urgent messages.

5. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌraɪd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəˌraɪd / (BrE)

There is a manual override for the automatic safety system in case of emergency.
The judge issued an override to prevent the law from taking effect.

6. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈraɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈraɪd / (BrE)
to disable or take control over the normal automatic operation of something, especially a vehicle's gear shift, so as to operate it manually.
take manual control over automatic system

Examples

The driver can override the automatic transmission in certain conditions for better control.
If you need more engine braking, you can override the system and switch to manual mode.
He overrode the car's computer to shift gears himself on the steep hill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bypass - to avoid or ignore a usual control or mechanism

Etymology

From over- + ride, originally meaning 'ride over', later extended to controlling or superseding something.
