piggyback

1. ADV

/ ˈpɪɡiˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈpɪɡibæk / (BrE)
in a way that involves being carried on the back or shoulders of another person
on the back or shoulders

Examples

She carried her son piggyback across the stream.
The little girl wanted to ride piggyback on her father during the hike.
He gave his friend a piggyback home after the party.

Synset Examples

she carried her child piggyback

Etymology

From 'pickaback', a variant of 'pick pack', influenced by 'pig'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɪɡiˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈpɪɡibæk / (BrE)
the act of carrying someone or something on your back or shoulders, especially with their arms around your neck and legs around your waist
act of carrying on the back or shoulders

Examples

She gave her little brother a piggyback to the playground when he got tired.
After our hike, I offered my friend a piggyback since he twisted his ankle.
The children took turns giving each other piggyback rides in the backyard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ride - an act of being carried on the back of someone

Etymology

From 'pig', possibly influenced by the playful or childlike way a pig might be imagined carried; the use of 'back' refers to carrying on one's back.

Source: WordNet

/ ˈpɪɡiˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈpɪɡibæk / (BrE)
with one vehicle or object carried on top of another, especially of trailers or vehicles transported on a flatcar or truck.
on top of another (esp. in transport)

Examples

Many new cars are delivered piggyback on specialized railcars across the country.
The small boat traveled piggyback atop the larger vessel during the journey.

Synset Examples

Etymology

From pig + back, originally referring to a ride on someone's back like a piglet; extended to transport sense in mid-20th century.
