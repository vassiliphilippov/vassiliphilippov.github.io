to profess

1. VERB

/ prəˈfɛs / (AmE), / prəˈfɛs / (BrE)
to declare openly that you have a particular feeling, belief, or allegiance, especially when it may not be true
declare belief or allegiance openly

Examples

She professed loyalty to the organization in her public statement.
Although he professes to support the cause, his actions suggest otherwise.
They professed their dedication to the team after the victory.

Synset Examples

The terrorists professed allegiance to their country
he professes to be a Communist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to affirm - to state something firmly and publicly
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true or refuse to accept it

Etymology

From Latin professus, past participle of profiteri ‘declare publicly’.

WordNet ID: oewn-00762780-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ prəˈfɛs / (AmE), / prəˈfɛs / (BrE)
to formally admit someone into a religious order or congregation, especially after taking vows
admit into a religious order

Examples

She was professed as a nun at the age of twenty-three.
After two years of novitiate, he was finally professed into the monastic community.
The abbey will profess five new sisters at this year's ceremony.

Etymology

From Latin professus (having declared publicly), from profiteri (to declare openly).

Source: WordNet

/ prəˈfɛs / (AmE), / prəˈfɛs / (BrE)
to state something openly or freely, especially feelings, beliefs, or opinions, often when these may not be true

The politician professed support for the new law during the debate.

/ prəˈfɛs / (AmE), / prəˈfɛs / (BrE)
to claim or declare something, often insincerely or falsely
to claim or declare (often insincerely)

Examples
