terminus

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɝː.mə.nəs / (AmE), / ˈtɜː.mɪ.nəs / (BrE)
the final point or station at either end of a railroad or bus route
end point of a transport route

Examples

The train will arrive at the terminus in thirty minutes.
At the terminus, all passengers must leave the bus.
They are building a new terminus at the north end of the subway line.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: terminal - a station at the end of a transport line or a major point for arrival or departure

Etymology

From Latin terminus (limit, boundary, end).

/ ˈtɝː.mə.nəs / (AmE), / ˈtɜː.mɪ.nəs / (BrE)
the final point or place where something ends, especially a station at the end of a railway or bus route
final point; end station or boundary

Examples

Passengers disembarked at the terminus and hurried toward the exit.
This road is the terminus of the old Roman route.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: terminal - station at the end of a transport line

Etymology

From Latin terminus (end; boundary).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɜːrmɪnəs / (AmE), / ˈtɜːmɪnəs / (BrE)
a final station on a railway, bus, or other transport route, where vehicles load or unload passengers or goods
final station on a transport route

Examples

The train will arrive at its terminus in just a few minutes.
All passengers must leave the bus when it reaches the terminus.
The new terminus was built to accommodate more cargo and passengers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: station - a designated place where trains or buses stop for passengers or goods
  • Antonym: origin - starting point of a transport route

Etymology

From Latin terminus (end, boundary).

4. NOUN

/ ˈtɝː.mə.nəs / (AmE), / ˈtɜː.mɪ.nəs / (BrE)
(architecture) A statue, human bust, or animal figure carved from the top of a square pillar, originally used as a boundary marker in ancient Rome.
statue or bust atop a pillar (arch.); Roman boundary marker

Examples

The ancient villa's gardens were marked by a marble terminus at each entrance.
A terminus depicting a Roman deity was found during the excavation of the site.
In neoclassical landscaping, designers often placed a terminus at the corners to echo Roman traditions.

Etymology

From Latin 'terminus' (boundary, limit), originally the name of a Roman god of boundaries and the marker itself.
