yielding

1. NOUN

/ ˈjiːldɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈjiːldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of verbally admitting defeat or surrendering, especially in a discussion, negotiation, or contest
verbal admission of defeat

Examples

After hours of debate, his yielding signaled the end of the argument.
The general's public yielding was broadcast to the entire nation.
Her quiet yielding during the meeting surprised her colleagues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surrender - the act of giving up or yielding to another
  • Synonym: capitulation - the act of ceasing to resist; surrender
  • Antonym: resistance - the refusal to accept or comply with something; the attempt to prevent something by action or argument

Etymology

From Old English 'gieldan' (to pay, yield), with the gerund suffix '-ing'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈjiːldɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈjiːldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of conceding, giving way, or submitting, especially in a disagreement or contest
the act of conceding or giving way

Examples

His yielding to their demands was seen as a sign of weakness.
The negotiation ended with the yielding of several important points.
She was frustrated by his constant yielding in arguments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: concession - the act of conceding or granting something
  • Antonym: resistance - refusal to accept or give in

Etymology

From the verb 'yield', which comes from Old English 'gieldan' (to pay, give up).

3. ADJ

/ ˈjiːldɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈjiːldɪŋ / (BrE)
soft and easily giving way or bending under pressure; not stiff or firm.
soft; easily bends or gives way

Examples

The yielding mattress provided excellent comfort for her back.
She preferred a yielding clay for sculpting, as it was easier to shape.
His feet sank into the yielding sand as he walked along the shore.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: firm - not easily yielding to pressure; solid or stiff
  • Synonym: soft - easy to press, fold, or cut; not hard or firm

Etymology

Present participle of the verb 'yield', from Old English 'gieldan' (to pay, return, yield).
