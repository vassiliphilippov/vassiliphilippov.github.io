wrecker

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɛkər / (AmE), / ˈrɛkə / (BrE)
a person whose job is to demolish or dismantle buildings
person who demolishes buildings

Examples

The old factory was torn down by a professional wrecker last year.
After the earthquake, a wrecker was hired to safely dismantle damaged buildings.
The wrecker worked quickly to clear the site for new construction.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demolisher - person who destroys buildings or structures
  • Antonym: builder - person who constructs buildings

Etymology

From the verb 'wreck' + -er, denoting someone who wrecks.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛkər / (AmE), / ˈrɛkə / (BrE)
A truck equipped with a crane or hoist used for towing disabled or illegally parked vehicles.
tow truck for disabled or parked cars
american

Examples

When my car broke down on the highway, I had to call a wrecker to tow it to the garage.
The police ordered a wrecker to remove the car blocking the fire hydrant.
After the accident, a wrecker arrived quickly to haul away the damaged vehicles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tow truck - a vehicle used to move disabled or illegally parked cars

Etymology

From 'wreck' + '-er', originally meaning someone or something that wrecks, later specialized to a vehicle for removing wrecks.

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈrɛkɚ / (AmE), / ˈrɛkə / (BrE)
a person who deliberately causes destruction or sabotages, especially by intentionally wrecking property or causing accidents
someone who deliberately causes destruction or sabotage

Examples

The spy acted as a wrecker by sabotaging the machinery in the factory.
Several wreckers were arrested for planning to destroy the railroad bridge.
Authorities believe the shipwreck was caused by a wrecker seeking to loot the cargo.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: saboteur - a person who engages in sabotage
  • Antonym: rescuer - someone who saves others, often from wrecks

Etymology

From wreck + -er (agent noun suffix), with use for people who intentionally cause wrecks dating from the 19th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
