to discourage

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈkɝːɪdʒ / (AmE), / dɪsˈkʌrɪdʒ / (BrE)
to try to prevent something from happening by expressing disapproval or showing opposition
try to prevent or oppose

Examples

Parents often discourage their children from smoking by explaining its dangers.
The government aims to discourage reckless driving through stricter penalties.
Heavy taxes on soda are meant to discourage excessive sugar consumption.

Synset Examples

We should discourage this practice among our youth

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to encourage - to give support, confidence, or hope to someone; to help something happen

Etymology

From Old French 'descouragier' (to deprive of courage), from 'des-' (reversal) + 'corage' (courage).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00910052-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈskɝːɪdʒ / (AmE), / dɪˈskʌrɪdʒ / (BrE)
to make someone lose confidence or hope; to try to prevent something by making it less likely to happen
make someone lose hope or confidence

Examples

Please do not discourage your child from trying new things, even if they seem difficult.
Negative criticism can discourage employees from sharing innovative ideas at work.
Her failure on the test did not discourage her from studying harder next time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dissuade - to persuade someone not to do something
  • Antonym: to encourage - give support, confidence, or hope to someone

Etymology

From Old French 'descouragier' (to dishearten), based on 'courage'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01823114-v
Synset POS: v
