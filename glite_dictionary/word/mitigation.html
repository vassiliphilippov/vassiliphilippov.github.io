mitigation

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɪt̬ɪˈɡeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɪtɪˈɡeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of making an offense or fault seem less serious by providing excuses or showing mitigating circumstances
lessening the seriousness of an offense

Examples

The defendant's lawyer presented evidence in mitigation of his client's actions.
In mitigation, she explained that she was unaware of the new policy.
The court considered several factors in mitigation before passing sentence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - a reason or explanation that justifies an action
  • Antonym: aggravation - the act of making an offense or situation worse

Etymology

From Latin mitigatio (a softening, alleviation), from mitigare (to soften).

3. NOUN

/ ˌmɪt̬ɪˈɡeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɪtɪˈɡeɪʃən / (BrE)
The action of reducing the severity, seriousness, or painfulness of something.
reduction of severity or intensity

Examples

The government announced new policies aimed at the mitigation of climate change effects.
Effective mitigation of damage during natural disasters can save many lives.
The lawyer argued for mitigation of the sentence, citing the defendant's difficult circumstances.

Synset Examples

the object being control or moderation of economic depressions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alleviation - the act of making something less severe
  • Antonym: exacerbation - the act of making something worse or more severe

Etymology

From Latin mitigatio (softening, alleviation), from mitigare (to soften, alleviate).
