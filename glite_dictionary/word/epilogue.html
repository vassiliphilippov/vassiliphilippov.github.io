epilogue

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛpɪˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈɛpɪlɒg / (BrE)
a section at the end of a book, play, or other literary work that serves as a comment on or a conclusion to what has happened
concluding section of a literary work

Examples

The epilogue revealed what happened to the characters long after the story ended.
She read the epilogue to understand the author's final thoughts on the story.
In the epilogue, the narrator addresses the audience directly to wrap up loose ends.

Synset Examples

the epilogue told what eventually happened to the main characters

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prologue - an introduction at the beginning of a literary work

Etymology

From Greek epilogos (conclusion), from epi- (in addition) + logos (speech).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛpɪˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈɛpɪlɒg / (BrE)
a short speech, often in verse, spoken directly to the audience by an actor at the end of a play, usually to provide a comment or conclusion
concluding speech in a play

Examples

At the end of the performance, the actor delivered a moving epilogue that reflected on the story’s themes.
The epilogue provided a humorous summary of the play and thanked the audience for attending.
Shakespeare often included an epilogue in his comedies to offer final thoughts or moral lessons.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prologue - an introduction to a play or other literary work

Etymology

From Greek 'epilogos' (conclusion), from 'epi-' (in addition) + 'logos' (speech).
