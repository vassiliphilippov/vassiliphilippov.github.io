smudge

1. NOUN

/ smʌdʒ / (AmE), / smʌdʒ / (BrE)
a smoky fire, especially one made outdoors, used to drive away insects such as mosquitoes
smoky fire to repel insects

Examples

The farmers lit a smudge near the barn to keep mosquitoes away during the evening.
We built a smudge at the campsite so we wouldn't be bothered by bugs all night.
A thick smudge of smoke drifted over the field as they tried to protect the cattle from flies.

Etymology

Possibly from Scandinavian origin, related to Old Norse *smjuga* (to creep through, as smoke).

2. VERB

/ smʌdʒ / (AmE), / smʌdʒ / (BrE)
to make a dirty or blurred mark on something; to soil or blur by touching or rubbing
make a dirty or blurred mark

Examples

Please be careful not to smudge the freshly painted surface with your fingers.
She accidentally smudged her makeup while putting on her coat.
If you smudge the text, it might be hard to read.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stain - mark or discolor with something that is not easily removed
  • Antonym: clean - remove dirt or marks; make clean

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English 'smogen' (to soil); of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01254492-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ smʌdʒ / (AmE), / smʌdʒ / (BrE)
a dirty mark or stain that makes a surface look untidy
dirty mark or stain

Examples

The white shirt had a noticeable smudge on the sleeve.
She wiped the smudge of chocolate from the boy's face.
There was a dark smudge on the page where the ink had spread.

Synset Examples

he had a smudge on his cheek

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stain - a mark that is difficult to remove, caused by a substance
  • Synonym: blotch - an irregular patch or mark, typically on skin or material

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse 'smjuga' (to creep, slip in), referring to something that sneaks onto a surface.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
