herald

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɛr.əld / (AmE), / ˈhɛr.əld / (BrE)
a person, especially in former times, who makes official announcements or introduces important news
person who announces news

Examples

The royal herald announced the beginning of the tournament to the crowd.
In medieval times, a herald would often ride ahead to proclaim the arrival of the king.
She felt like a herald as she delivered the news of victory to the waiting villagers.

Synset Examples

the chieftain had a herald who announced his arrival with a trumpet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: messenger - a person who carries messages or news
  • Synonym: announcer - a person who announces something, especially publicly

Etymology

From Old French 'heraut', of Germanic origin, related to 'hari' (army) and 'wald' (rule).

2. VERB

/ ˈhɛrəld / (AmE), / ˈhɛr.əld / (BrE)
to greet or announce something enthusiastically or joyfully, often as a sign of what is to come
to greet or announce with enthusiasm

Examples

The town gathered to herald the arrival of the Olympic torch.
Many believe that spring is heralded by the first blooms of daffodils.
Critics heralded the artist's new work as a bold step forward.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to announce - to make known publicly or officially

Etymology

From Old French heraut, originally from Germanic *hariwald 'commander of an army'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00902139-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈhɛr.əld / (AmE), / ˈhɛr.əld / (BrE)
to praise something or someone enthusiastically, especially as an important or significant development
to praise enthusiastically or publicly

Examples

The press heralded the new novel as a literary masterpiece.
Many experts heralded the recent discovery as a turning point in cancer research.
She was heralded as one of the most promising young artists of her generation.

Synset Examples

The critics hailed the young pianist as a new Rubinstein

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hail - to praise or acclaim enthusiastically

Etymology

From Old French herault, from Germanic origin, related to proclaiming or announcing.

4. VERB

/ ˈhɛr.əld / (AmE), / ˈhɛr.əld / (BrE)
to be a sign that something important, and often good, is about to happen; to foreshadow or presage.
to foreshadow or signal an event

Examples

The sudden drop in temperature heralded the arrival of winter.
Experts believe these discoveries herald a new era in cancer treatment.
Dark clouds on the horizon heralded the approaching storm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to announce - to state or make known formally or publicly
  • Synonym: signal - to indicate or make known a sign
  • Antonym: to conceal - to keep something secret or hidden

Etymology

From Old French 'herault', from Germanic origin; related to 'harja-waldaz' meaning 'army commander'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ ˈhɛr.əld / (AmE), / ˈhɛr.əld / (BrE)
A person or thing that comes before and signals the arrival of someone or something.
forerunner; indicator of what is to come

Examples

Robins are a herald of spring, often appearing as the first sign of the changing season.
The dark clouds on the horizon were a herald of the coming storm.
Many people see the invention of the smartphone as a herald of a new era in communication.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forerunner - a person or thing that precedes the coming of someone or something else
  • Synonym: harbinger - a person or thing that announces or signals the approach of another

Etymology

From Old French *herault*, of Germanic origin, related to *hari* (army) and *wald* (rule).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
