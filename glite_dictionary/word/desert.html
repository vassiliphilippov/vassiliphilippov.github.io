desert

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛz.ɚt / (AmE), / ˈdez.ət / (BrE)
A large, dry, and often sandy area of land with very little rainfall and sparse vegetation.
arid land with little vegetation

Examples

The Sahara is the largest hot desert in the world.
Few plants can survive in such an inhospitable desert environment.
After driving for hours, we finally reached the edge of the desert.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wasteland - an area of land that is barren or unused
  • Antonym: oasis - a fertile spot in a desert where water is found

Etymology

From Old French 'desert', from Latin 'desertum' (something left waste).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈzɝːt / (AmE), / dɪˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to leave a cause, country, or military organization, often to join an opposing one, especially illegally or disloyally
leave a cause, country, or army disloyally

Examples

Several soldiers decided to desert the army after months of hardship and danger.
He was accused of plotting to desert his country and join the rebels.
If you desert your comrades in battle, you risk severe punishment.

Synset Examples

If soldiers deserted Hitler's army, they were shot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defect - to abandon one's cause or allegiance in favor of another

Etymology

From Latin 'deserere' (to forsake).

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈzɝːt / (AmE), / dɪˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to leave someone or something that you are responsible for, especially without intending to return
to leave someone or something behind

Examples

Many animals will desert their young if they sense danger nearby.
When times got tough, he felt like everyone had deserted him.
After the argument, she deserted her friends and went home alone.

Synset Examples

the students deserted the campus after the end of exam period

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abandon - to leave someone or something behind, often permanently
  • Synonym: to forsake - to abandon, especially someone you have a responsibility to
  • Antonym: stay - to remain in a place or position

Etymology

From Old French *deserter*, from Late Latin *desertare*, frequentative of Latin *deserere* (to abandon).

4. NOUN

/ dɪˈzɜrt / (AmE), / dɪˈzɜːt / (BrE)
something that a person rightfully deserves, especially a deserved reward or punishment; often used in the plural as 'just deserts'.
thing deserved, especially reward or punishment
archaic

Examples

After years of dishonesty, the politician finally got his deserts when he was exposed to the public.
She was confident that everyone would receive their just deserts in the end.
In many stories, villains meet their deserts before the tale concludes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: merit - the quality of being particularly good or worthy
  • Synonym: reward - something given in recognition of service, effort, or achievement
  • Antonym: punishment - the infliction of a penalty as retribution for an offense

Etymology

From Old French *deserte* (merit), from Latin *deservire* (to serve well or zealously, to deserve).

to leave someone who needs or depends on you, especially in a difficult situation
abandon someone in need

Related Terms

  • Antonym: support - to give help or assistance to someone
