cathode

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæθoʊd / (AmE), / ˈkæθəʊd / (BrE)
the negatively charged electrode in an electrical device from which electrons flow into the external circuit
negatively charged electrode

Examples

Electrons travel from the cathode to the anode in a typical galvanic cell.
The television's old picture tube contains a heated cathode that emits electrons.
Replacing the worn-out cathode improved the device's performance significantly.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: anode - the positively charged electrode in an electrical device

Etymology

From Greek 'kathodos' (way down), coined in the 19th century to contrast with 'anode'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæθˌoʊd / (AmE), / ˈkaθəʊd / (BrE)
the positively charged terminal of a voltaic cell or battery from which electric current flows into an external circuit
positively charged terminal of a cell or battery

Examples

The cathode of a battery is the terminal from which electrons flow outward in a circuit.
During discharge, the cathode in a lead-acid battery is made of lead dioxide.
Make sure to connect the wire to the cathode to complete the circuit properly.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: anode - the negatively charged terminal of a voltaic cell or battery

Etymology

From Greek 'kathodos' (way down), coined in 19th-century scientific terminology.
