cipher

1. NOUN

/ ˈsaɪfər / (AmE), / ˈsʌɪfə / (BrE)
a person who has no power or importance in a situation; a person of no influence
person of no influence or importance

Examples

After the takeover, Sam became a cipher in the company, with no real power.
Although he was present at all the meetings, he remained a political cipher.
She was treated like a cipher, barely acknowledged by the rest of the group.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nonentity - a person or thing with no interesting or special qualities; unimportant person

Etymology

From Arabic 'ṣifr' (empty, zero), via Medieval Latin and Old French.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsaɪfər / (AmE), / ˈsʌɪfə / (BrE)
a secret or disguised way of writing; a code used to conceal the meaning of a message
a secret code or method of writing

Examples

The spies communicated in a cipher to keep their messages safe from enemies.
Unless you can break the cipher, you won't be able to read the encrypted letter.
The diary was written in a simple cipher that took days to decode.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: code - a system of words, letters, figures, or symbols used to represent others, especially for secrecy
  • Antonym: to decipher - to convert a coded message into normal language

Etymology

From Arabic 'ṣifr' (zero, empty), via Medieval Latin 'cifra' and Old French 'cifre'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈsaɪfər / (AmE), / ˈsʌɪfə / (BrE)
a message written using a secret code, especially one that is intended to be difficult to understand without the key
a message in secret code

Examples

The spy sent a cipher to his contact, hiding the true meaning from anyone else.
Breaking the cipher took the codebreakers several hours of intense work.
The general issued orders in a cipher to ensure they remained secret.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: code - a system of words, letters, or symbols used to represent others, especially for secrecy
  • Antonym: to decipher - to succeed in understanding, interpreting, or identifying something (especially a coded message)

Etymology

From Arabic ṣifr (zero, empty) via Old French cifre, originally indicating 'zero' before taking on the meaning of code.

5. VERB

/ ˈsaɪfər / (AmE), / ˈsaɪfə / (BrE)
to convert ordinary language into a code, especially to keep a message secret
convert text into code

Examples

All sensitive documents were ciphered before being sent electronically.
Can you cipher this message so that only the team understands it?
She spent hours ciphering secret codes for the club's activities.

Synset Examples

We should encode the message for security reasons

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encode - to convert information into a different form, often for secrecy or storage
  • Antonym: to decipher - to convert code back into ordinary language

Etymology

From Arabic ṣifr (zero, empty), later used for a code; entered English via Old French 'cifrer' (to use numerals or codes).
