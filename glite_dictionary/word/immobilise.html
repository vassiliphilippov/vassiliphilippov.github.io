to immobilise

1. VERB

/ ɪˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ɪˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to make someone or something unable to move or operate normally
make unable to move

Examples

The mechanic managed to immobilise the faulty machine for repairs.
A broken leg can immobilise a patient until it heals.
Police tried to immobilise the vehicle by puncturing its tires.

Synset Examples

The sudden storm immobilized the traffic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to paralyse - to make unable to move or act
  • Synonym: to restrain - to prevent from moving freely

Etymology

From Latin 'immobilis' (not movable), via French 'immobiliser'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00270026-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ɪˈməʊbəlaɪz / (BrE)
(economics) To convert (assets) into fixed capital, making them no longer liquid.
convert assets into fixed capital

Examples

The company decided to immobilise most of its short-term assets by investing in new machinery.
After the merger, management chose to immobilise certain funds to support long-term projects.
By purchasing real estate, they effectively immobilised part of their working capital.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to liquidate - to convert assets into cash

Etymology

From French 'immobiliser', based on 'immobile' from Latin 'immobilis' (not movable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00270775-v
Synset POS: v

6. VERB

/ ɪˈmoʊbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ɪˈməʊbɪlaɪz / (BrE)
(of capital) to withdraw from active use or hold as a reserve, making it unavailable for immediate investment or transactions.
withdraw capital from use or circulation

Examples

The company decided to immobilise a large portion of its cash reserves during the economic downturn.
Banks often immobilise capital to meet regulatory requirements or prepare for uncertain markets.
Excessive investments in real estate can immobilise capital that might otherwise be used for innovation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: freeze - to prevent something, especially assets, from being used or moved
  • Antonym: release - to make available for use again, opposite of immobilise in this sense

Etymology

From French 'immobiliser', from 'immobile' (motionless) + '-iser' (verb-forming suffix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02219646-v
Synset POS: v
