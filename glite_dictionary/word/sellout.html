sellout

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɛlˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈsɛl.aʊt / (BrE)
An event, such as a concert or sports game, for which all tickets have been sold.
event with all tickets sold

Examples

The band's performance was a complete sellout, with thousands in attendance.
Every show on the tour has been a sellout so far this year.
Tickets went so quickly that the event became a sellout within hours.

Synset Examples

get your tickets for the concert early, it is sure to be a sellout

Related Terms

  • Synonym: full house - an event or performance where every available seat is taken

Etymology

Formed from the verb 'sell out', meaning to sell all available tickets or items.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɛlˌaʊt / (AmE), / ˈsɛl.aʊt / (BrE)
an act of betraying one's principles, country, or cause for personal gain or advantage.
betrayal of principles for gain

Examples

Many fans accused the band of being a sellout after they changed their musical style.
His decision to support the new policy was seen as a political sellout.
She promised never to be a sellout and always to stand by her beliefs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: betrayal - the act of betraying someone or something
  • Antonym: loyalty - faithfulness or allegiance to a person, cause, or principle
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00751514-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɛl.aʊt / (AmE), / ˈsɛl.aʊt / (BrE)
a person who betrays their principles, associates, or cause, often for personal gain
someone who betrays their principles

Examples

Many fans accused the band of being a sellout after they signed with a major label.
She refused to become a sellout even when offered a lot of money to change her art.
His colleagues viewed him as a sellout when he abandoned their campaign for a corporate job.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: traitor - a person who betrays someone or something, such as a friend, cause, or principle
  • Antonym: loyalist - a person who remains loyal to a cause or party

Etymology

From the phrasal verb 'sell out,' meaning to betray for personal advantage; first used in 19th-century American English.
