F-actin

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛf ˌæktɪn / (AmE), / ˈɛf ˌæktɪn / (BrE)
the filamentous, polymerized form of actin found in cells, forming part of the cytoskeleton
filamentous form of actin protein
medical

Examples

The cytoskeleton relies on F-actin to support cellular shape and movement.
Researchers observed the rapid assembly of F-actin during the process of cell migration.
Drugs that disrupt F-actin can inhibit the division of certain cancer cells.

Synset Examples

It can be present as either a free monomer called G-actin (globular) or as part of a linear polymer microfilament called F-actin (filamentous), both of which are essential for such important cellular functions as the mobility and contraction of cells during cell division.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: G-actin - the globular, monomeric form of actin protein

Etymology

From 'F' for 'filamentous' + 'actin', indicating its structure as a filamentous polymer of actin.
