to cauterise

1. VERB

/ ˈkɔːtəraɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɔːtəraɪz / (BrE)
to burn or sear tissue, typically to stop bleeding or prevent infection, using a hot instrument, electric current, or a chemical agent
burn tissue to stop bleeding/infection
medical british

Examples

After removing the mole, the doctor used a heated tool to cauterise the wound.
In the past, soldiers’ injuries were often cauterised on the battlefield to stop bleeding.
New advances allow surgeons to cauterise tissue more quickly and precisely during operations.

Synset Examples

The surgeon cauterized the wart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cauterize - to burn tissue, US spelling

Etymology

From Greek kautēriazein, meaning 'to burn with a branding iron'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɔːtəˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɔːtəraɪz / (BrE)
To make someone insensitive or emotionally hardened; to deaden feelings or morals.
make emotionally insensitive or deaden morals

Examples

Years of conflict had cauterised his sense of compassion.
Repeated disappointments can cauterise a person's ability to trust others.
She feared that constant criticism might cauterise her enthusiasm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to harden - make or become more emotionally callous or less sensitive
  • Antonym: to soften - make or become less severe or more sensitive

Etymology

From Greek kautēriazein (to burn, brand), via Late Latin cauterizare.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00273913-v
Synset POS: v
