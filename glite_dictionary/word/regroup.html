to regroup

1. VERB

/ riˈɡruːp / (AmE), / riˈɡruːp / (BrE)
to organize or arrange people or things in a new way, especially after a difficulty, defeat, or setback
organize again after a setback

Examples

After the unexpected loss, the team had to regroup and come up with a new strategy.
Once the hikers became separated, they stopped to regroup at a nearby clearing.
The company will regroup its staff following the recent restructuring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reorganize - to arrange something again or differently
  • Antonym: scatter - to separate or disperse a group

Etymology

From re- (‘again’) + group, first used in a military context in the early 20th century.

2. VERB

/ riˈɡruːp / (AmE), / riˈɡruːp / (BrE)
to organize people or things into new groups, especially after they have been separated or after a setback
organize into new groups again

Examples

After the failed attempt, the team needed to regroup before trying again.
The birds scattered at first, but eventually they began to regroup nearby.
The company plans to regroup its staff into more efficient working teams.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reorganize - organize again in a different way
  • Antonym: to disperse - to break up and move in different directions

Etymology

Formed from re- (again) + group (from French groupe, from Italian gruppo) in the 19th century.
