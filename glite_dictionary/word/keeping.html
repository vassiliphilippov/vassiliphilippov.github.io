keeping

1. NOUN

/ ˈkipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkiːpɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of retaining or holding something, or the state of being kept; custody or care
the act of retaining or holding something

Examples

The museum was responsible for the keeping of valuable historical documents.
She entrusted her jewelry to the bank for safe keeping.
The keeping of secrets can be difficult for some people.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retention - the continued possession or control of something
  • Antonym: release - the act of allowing something to be free or let go

Etymology

From Middle English keping, from keep + -ing.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkiː.pɪŋ / (BrE)
the responsibility or care of someone or something entrusted to a protector or guardian
care or responsibility for someone or something

Examples

The museum placed the artifact in the keeping of its most trusted curator.
She trusted her children to the keeping of her sister while she was away.
The company left the confidential files in his keeping until the audit was complete.

Synset Examples

he left his car in my keeping

Related Terms

  • Synonym: custody - protective care or guardianship of someone or something
  • Synonym: guardianship - the position of being legally responsible for someone

Etymology

From Middle English *keeping*, from the verb *keep*, ultimately from Old English *cepan* (to seize, hold).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈkipɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈkiːpɪŋ / (BrE)
the state of being in agreement, harmony, or conformity with something; often used in the phrase 'in keeping with'.
state of being in agreement or harmony

Examples

His response was perfectly keeping with company policy.
The new decorations are not in keeping with the building’s historic character.
Their actions were in keeping with the club’s long-standing traditions.

Synset Examples

his behavior was not in keeping with the occasion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accordance - agreement or conformity with a rule or request
  • Antonym: conflict - lack of agreement or harmony
