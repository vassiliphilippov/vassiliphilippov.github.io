peduncle

1. NOUN

/ pəˈdʌŋkəl / (AmE), / pɪˈdʌŋkl̩ / (BrE)
a bundle of myelinated nerve fibers connecting different parts of the brain or parts of the nervous system
bundle of myelinated nerve fibers in the brain
medical

Examples

Damage to the cerebellar peduncle can result in impaired motor coordination.
The superior peduncle carries information from the cerebellum to the midbrain.
Researchers identified lesions in the brainstem peduncle during the MRI scan.

Etymology

From Latin pedunculus (a small foot or stalk).

2. NOUN

/ pɪˈdʌŋkəl / (AmE), / pɪˈdʌŋkl̩ / (BrE)
a slender stalk of tissue that connects a polyp or other structure to its base or main body, especially in anatomy or zoology
slender stalk connecting a polyp to its base
medical

Examples

The surgeon carefully severed the peduncle attaching the polyp to the intestinal wall.
Microscopic examination revealed that the peduncle was free of malignant cells.
The jellyfish's peduncle is a vital structure for anchoring it to the ocean floor.

Etymology

From Latin pedunculus, diminutive of pes (foot).

3. NOUN

/ pɪˈdʌŋkəl / (AmE), / pɪˈdʌŋkəl / (BrE)
the stalk that supports an inflorescence or a single flower
stalk supporting a flower or inflorescence

Examples

The orchid's peduncle was sturdy enough to hold several blossoms at once.
If the peduncle is damaged, the flower may not develop properly.
Some plants have a long peduncle that raises the flowers above the leaves.

Etymology

From Latin 'pedunculus', diminutive of 'pes' (foot).
