sallow

1. NOUN

/ ˈsæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈsaləʊ / (BrE)
a shrubby or small tree of the willow genus (Salix), native to Europe and Asia, with broad leaves and large catkins, some species of which are valued for their bark and as sources of charcoal.
type of shrubby broad-leaved willow

Examples

The sallow thrives in moist soils along riverbanks and wetlands.
Some species of sallow are traditionally harvested for their tanbark.
The broad leaves of the sallow distinguish it from other types of willows.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: willow - a tree or shrub of the genus Salix with slender branches and narrow leaves

Etymology

From Old English *salh*, from Proto-Germanic *salhaz*, related to the scientific name Salix.

2. VERB

/ ˈsæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈsaləʊ / (BrE)
to make (someone or something) look yellowish or unhealthy, especially in the face
make appear yellowish or unhealthy

Examples

The long winter had sallowed his cheeks and dulled his eyes.
Her face was sallowed by months of worry and sleepless nights.
Illness and confinement began to sallow the complexion of the entire family.

Synset Examples

The illness has sallowed her face

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: pale - having a lighter or whitish color, often due to illness

Etymology

From Old English *salwe* (willow, sallow tree), relating to the pale or yellowish coloration.

3. ADJ

/ ˈsæloʊ / (AmE), / ˈsæləʊ / (BrE)
(of a person's face or skin) having an unhealthy yellow or pale brown color.
of unhealthy yellowish complexion

Examples

After weeks of illness, his skin took on a sallow hue that worried his doctor.
She noticed the sallow appearance of his cheeks under the harsh hospital lights.
Working long nights left him with a sallow complexion and tired eyes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pallid - pale, typically because of poor health
  • Antonym: ruddy - having a healthy red color

Etymology

Old English *saluw* (dusky, yellowish), of Germanic origin; related to German *selb* (yellowish).
