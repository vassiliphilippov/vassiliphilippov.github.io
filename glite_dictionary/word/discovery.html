discovery

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈskʌvəri / (AmE), / dɪˈskʌvəri / (BrE)
(law) the process by which each party in a lawsuit is required to disclose to the other parties all relevant documents and information before trial.
legal process of sharing case information
legal

Examples

During discovery, both lawyers requested access to thousands of financial records.
The judge ruled that key emails must be produced during discovery before the trial.
Failure to comply with discovery requests can result in sanctions from the court.

Etymology

From Old French *descovrir* (to uncover, reveal), from Latin *discooperire* (to uncover).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈskʌvəri / (AmE), / dɪˈskʌvəri / (BrE)
The act or process of finding or learning something that was previously unknown or hidden.
the act of finding something new

Examples

The discovery of penicillin revolutionized medicine in the twentieth century.
Her latest discovery could change how we understand human genetics.
The scientist announced a surprising discovery after years of research.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: invention - the creation of something new, especially a device or process

Etymology

From Old French 'descovrir' (to uncover), from Latin 'dis-' (apart) + 'cooperire' (to cover).

4. NOUN

/ dɪˈskʌvəri / (AmE), / dɪˈskʌvəri / (BrE)
a thing, fact, or place that has been found or learned about for the first time
something that is discovered

Examples

The archaeologists made a significant discovery in the ruins of the ancient city.
Her latest discovery has changed the way scientists think about the disease.
Finding that old letter was an unexpected discovery in my attic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finding - a result of searching or examining that is discovered
  • Antonym: invention - something newly created, not previously existing

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French *descovrir* (to discover), from Latin *discooperire* (to uncover).
