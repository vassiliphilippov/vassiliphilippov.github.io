spangle

1. NOUN

/ ˈspæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈspæŋɡl̩ / (BrE)
A small, flat, shiny piece of material used to decorate clothing, costumes, or accessories.
small shiny decorative piece

Examples

The dancer’s dress glittered with every spangle stitched into the fabric.
She sewed a gold spangle onto the costume for added sparkle.
Each spangle on the jacket caught the light, creating a dazzling effect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sequin - a small, shiny, disk-shaped ornament used to decorate clothing

Etymology

From Middle English 'spange', from Old French 'espangue', itself from Medieval Latin 'spongia' meaning 'metallic ornament'.

2. VERB

/ ˈspæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈspaŋɡ(ə)l / (BrE)
to glitter or sparkle as if covered with small shiny objects
glitter or sparkle like small shiny objects

Examples

The ballroom was spangled with a thousand lights, creating a magical atmosphere.
Her dress seemed to spangle in the sunlight, catching everyone's attention.
Tiny bits of glass spangled across the floor after the vase shattered.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sparkle - to shine with small flashes of light
  • Synonym: gleam - to shine with a soft or reflected light

Etymology

From Middle English spangel, from Old French espangon (spark), related to 'spark'.

3. VERB

/ ˈspæŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈspæŋɡl̩ / (BrE)
to decorate or cover with small, shiny objects or spangles, often to create a glittering effect
decorate with shiny objects

Examples

The designer chose to spangle the dress with silver sequins, making it shimmer under the lights.
She loved to spangle her costumes for the dance competition each year.
Tiny crystals spangled the surface of the artwork, catching every ray of light.

Synset Examples

the star-spangled banner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embellish - to make something more attractive by adding decorative details
  • Synonym: to adorn - to decorate or add beauty to something

Etymology

From Middle English *spangel*, probably from Middle Dutch *spangel* (a spark, glittering object).
