to disoblige

1. VERB

/ ˌdɪs.əˈblaɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˌdɪs.əˈblʌɪdʒ / (BrE)
to refuse or fail to do what someone wants; to disregard or ignore someone's wishes or requests
refuse to accommodate; ignore wishes

Examples

I didn't mean to disoblige you, but I simply couldn't help at that time.
She felt hurt when her request was disobliged by her colleagues.
He tried not to disoblige anyone, always making an effort to be helpful.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to oblige - to do as someone desires or wishes

Etymology

From Latin prefix 'dis-' (expressing reversal) + 'oblige' (from Latin 'obligare', to bind).

2. VERB

/ ˌdɪs.əˈblaɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˌdɪs.əˈblaɪdʒ / (BrE)
to cause inconvenience or put someone to trouble; to do something that displeases someone
cause inconvenience or trouble to someone

Examples

I don’t want to disoblige you by changing our plans at the last minute.
Please let me know if this schedule would disoblige anyone on your team.
He apologized, saying he did not mean to disoblige his hosts.

Synset Examples

Sorry to trouble you, but …

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to oblige - to do as someone wishes or requests; to help or accommodate

Etymology

From the prefix dis- (expressing the opposite) + oblige, from Latin obligare (to bind).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02513385-v
Synset POS: v
