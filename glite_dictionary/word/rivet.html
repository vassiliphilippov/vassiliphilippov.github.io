rivet

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɪvɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɪvɪt / (BrE)
A metal pin used to fasten two or more pieces together, with a head at one end and the other end hammered flat after being inserted through holes.
metal pin used for fastening

Examples

The bridge was held together by large steel rivets hammered into place.
After drilling holes in the plates, he inserted a rivet to join them securely.
Many old ships used rivets instead of welded seams for their hulls.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bolt - metal fastener with a threaded shaft, used with a nut
  • Antonym: weld - to join metals by melting them together

Etymology

From Old French *rivet* (clenched nail), of uncertain origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɪvət / (AmE), / ˈrɪvɪt / (BrE)
An ornament in the form of a small, rounded, circular protuberance, often used on vaults, shields, or belts.
small rounded decorative protuberance

Examples

The antique shield was adorned with golden rivets along its edge.
Decorative rivets lined the leather belt, giving it a medieval appearance.
Each stone in the vaulted ceiling was marked by a small rivet at the center.

Etymology

From Old French *rivet* (nail, pin), from *river* (to fix, fasten), possibly of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04350424-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈrɪvɪt / (AmE), / ˈrɪvɪt / (BrE)
to fasten or join something with a rivet or rivets
fasten with a rivet

Examples

The mechanic carefully riveted the metal plates together to reinforce the frame.
Ships were once commonly constructed by riveting large steel panels to form the hull.
Make sure you properly rivet each joint to ensure the sculpture will not fall apart.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bolt - to fasten or secure with a bolt

Etymology

From Old French *riveter* (to clinch, fasten), from *rivet* (rivet).
