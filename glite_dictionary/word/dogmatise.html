to dogmatise

1. VERB

/ ˈdɔɡməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡmətaɪz / (BrE)
to speak or assert opinions in a dogmatic, authoritative manner, often without consideration of evidence or other viewpoints
speak dogmatically or authoritatively

Examples

It's important to discuss ideas openly rather than simply dogmatise and dismiss alternatives.
The professor tended to dogmatise during lectures, rarely acknowledging other viewpoints.
She didn't want to dogmatise; instead, she encouraged a lively debate among her students.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pontificate - to express opinions in a pompous or dogmatic way
  • Synonym: to preach - to give moral advice in a dogmatic way

Etymology

From Greek 'dogmatizein' (to lay down an opinion), via Late Latin 'dogmatizare'.

2. VERB

/ ˈdɔɡməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdɒɡmətaɪz / (BrE)
to state or assert opinions as if they are absolute truth; to express beliefs in a dogmatic way
assert beliefs as absolute truth
british

Examples

It is unwise to dogmatise on complex moral issues without considering multiple perspectives.
Scholars caution against those who dogmatise scientific theories as if they are unchangeable facts.
She tends to dogmatise during debates, refusing to entertain alternative viewpoints.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dogmatize - American spelling of 'dogmatise'

Etymology

From French 'dogmatiser', from Greek 'dogmatizein' (to lay down as a doctrine).
