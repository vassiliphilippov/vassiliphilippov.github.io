to dawdle

1. VERB

/ ˈdɔdəl / (AmE), / ˈdɔːd(ə)l / (BrE)
to waste time by moving slowly or being idle, often intentionally
to waste time or move slowly

Examples

If you dawdle in the morning, you might miss your bus.
The children dawdled on their way home from school, stopping to look at every flower.
Please don't dawdle; we need to leave soon.

Synset Examples

Get busy — don't dally!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dally - to waste time or act slowly
  • Antonym: hurry - to move or act with speed

Etymology

Probably from obsolete English 'daddle', meaning to walk unsteadily.

2. VERB

/ ˈdɑdəl / (AmE), / ˈdɔːdəl / (BrE)
to move or act slowly and with little purpose, often causing delay
move or act slowly, causing delay

Examples

Please don't dawdle on your way to school or you'll be late.
He tends to dawdle over breakfast, enjoying his coffee and the morning news.
If you dawdle any longer, we'll miss the beginning of the movie.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary
  • Antonym: hurry - to move or act with speed

Etymology

Probably from obsolete English dialect 'daddle' (to walk unsteadily); origin uncertain.

3. VERB

/ ˈdɔdəl / (AmE), / ˈdɔːdl / (BrE)
to move or act slowly or waste time, often in a way that delays progress
move or act slowly; waste time

Examples

The children dawdle on their way to school, stopping to look at every flower.
If you dawdle too long, we’ll miss the beginning of the movie.
She told him not to dawdle and to finish his chores quickly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary, often because of reluctance to leave
  • Antonym: hurry - to move or act with haste

Etymology

Perhaps from dialectal English dawdle (to dally, waste time), possibly related to daddle (to walk unsteadily) or daddle (to trifle).

Source: WordNet
