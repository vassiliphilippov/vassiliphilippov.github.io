recoil

1. NOUN

/ ˈriːˌkɔɪl / (AmE), / ˈriː.kɔɪl / (BrE)
the sudden backward movement of a gun when it is fired
backward movement of a gun when fired

Examples

The recoil from the shotgun nearly knocked him over.
You must brace yourself for the recoil when firing this weapon.
Proper stance can help manage the recoil of powerful rifles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kickback - the backward movement of a firearm when fired

Etymology

From French *reculer* (to draw back), from Latin *re-* (back) + *culare* (to move).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07365354-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ rɪˈkɔɪl / (AmE), / rɪˈkɔɪl / (BrE)
to quickly move back or away as a reaction to fear, pain, or disgust
draw back quickly in fear or pain

Examples

She couldn't help but recoil at the sight of the snake in the garden.
He recoiled in horror when he heard the news of the accident.
Many people recoil from the idea of eating insects, despite their nutritional value.

Synset Examples

she flinched when they showed the slaughtering of the calf

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flinch - to make a sudden small movement because of pain or fear
  • Synonym: shrink - to move back or away suddenly, especially because of fear

Etymology

From Old French *reculer* (to move back), from Latin *culum* (the hind part).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈkɔɪl / (AmE), / rɪˈkɔɪl / (BrE)
to suddenly move or spring back, especially as a reaction to force or impact
spring back from force or impact

Examples

4. VERB

/ rɪˈkɔɪl / (AmE), / rɪˈkɔɪl / (BrE)
(of an action) to have an unintended negative effect on the person or group who initiated it; to backfire.
to have a negative effect on the originator

Examples

The new policy recoiled on the company, leading to public criticism.
His attempt to deceive others eventually recoiled upon himself.
Threatening legal action may recoil on you if the facts are not clear.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: backfire - (of an action) to have the opposite effect to that intended, causing harm to the originator

From Old French *reculer* (to draw back), from re- (back) + cul (bottom, backside).
