bridle

1. VERB

/ ˈbraɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈbraɪdəl / (BrE)
to put a bridle on (a horse or other animal)
put a bridle on an animal

Examples

It took several minutes to bridle the restless stallion.
She carefully bridled the horse before their morning ride.
As he bridled the mare, he checked the straps for any signs of wear.

Synset Examples

bridle horses

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unbridle - to remove the bridle from (an animal)

Etymology

From Old English *brīdel* (bridle, rein), ultimately from Proto-Germanic.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01303241-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈbraɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈbraɪdəl / (BrE)
a thing that restrains, controls, or limits someone or something
something that restrains or controls

Examples

The threat of sanctions acted as a bridle on the company's expansion.
Her strong ethics were a natural bridle to her ambitious tendencies.
Without an effective bridle, the new policy led to widespread abuses.

Synset Examples

his common sense is a bridle to his quick temper

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restraint - a measure or condition that keeps someone or something under control
  • Synonym: curb - a check or restraint on something

Etymology

From Old English brīdel (rein, curb), related to 'bridan' (to pull, move quickly).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01148552-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈbraɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈbraɪdəl / (BrE)
(of a horse) to respond to the reins or to be guided by the bridle.
(of a horse) respond to the reins

Examples

The young horse quickly learned to bridle during its training sessions.
If the animal refuses to bridle, it can be difficult for the rider to maintain control.
Some horses naturally bridle better than others, making them easier to ride.

Etymology

From Old English *brīdel* (‘bridle, rein’), related to the act of controlling by a bridle.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01058790-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈbraɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈbraɪdəl / (BrE)
to show sudden anger or take offense, typically by drawing up the head or becoming stiff with indignation
to show anger or take offense

Examples

She bridled at the suggestion that her work was unimportant.
He bridled when asked about his past mistakes.
The manager bridled at the criticism and defended her team firmly.

Synset Examples

She bridled at his suggestion to elope

Related Terms

  • Synonym: take offense - to feel resentment or indignation at something
  • Synonym: to flare up - to suddenly show anger

Etymology

From Old English *bridel* (reins, restraint), related to the noun 'bridle', originally referring to horse tack.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01790111-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ ˈbraɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈbraɪdəl / (BrE)
a set of straps placed over a horse's head, including a bit and reins, used to control the animal
horse's headgear for control

Examples

The rider checked the bridle to ensure the horse was comfortable before setting off.
A leather bridle hung neatly on the stable wall after yesterday's ride.
She gently adjusted the bridle to better fit the pony's head.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harness - set of straps and fittings by which a horse is fastened to a cart or plough

Etymology

From Old English *brīdel* (rein, bridle), related to the verb *bregdan* (to pull).
