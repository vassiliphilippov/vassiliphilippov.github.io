to transcribe

1. VERB

/ trænˈskraɪb / (AmE), / trænˈskraɪb / (BrE)
(in molecular biology) to convert the genetic information in a strand of DNA into a complementary strand of RNA, especially messenger RNA.
convert DNA into RNA (biology)
medical

Examples

During gene expression, the enzyme RNA polymerase transcribes the DNA sequence into messenger RNA.
The process by which DNA is transcribed into RNA is essential for protein synthesis.
Some viruses can transcribe their genetic material directly from RNA without the need for DNA.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: to translate - to synthesize a protein from an mRNA template (in biology)

Etymology

From Latin 'transcribere', meaning 'to copy out, write over'.

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ trænˈskraɪb / (AmE), / trænˈskraɪb / (BrE)
to write or represent spoken or written words in a different script or alphabet
write in a different script

Examples

The ancient manuscript was carefully transcribed into the modern Latin alphabet.
Scholars will transcribe the old Greek texts using the Cyrillic script for linguistic comparison.
It took several months to transcribe the Sanskrit prayers into the Roman script for publication.

Synset Examples

The Sanskrit text had to be transliterated

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to transliterate - to write words in the characters of another alphabet

Etymology

From Latin transcribere, from trans- ('across') + scribere ('to write').
