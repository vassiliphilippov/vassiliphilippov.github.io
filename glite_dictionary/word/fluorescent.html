fluorescent

1. ADJ

/ flʊəˈrɛsənt / (AmE), / flʊəˈrɛsənt / (BrE)
emitting visible light when exposed to radiation from an external source, such as ultraviolet light
emitting light under radiation

Examples

The jellyfish appeared fluorescent under the blue lights of the aquarium.
They used fluorescent markers to stain the cells for microscopic examination.
Some minerals are naturally fluorescent and glow brightly under ultraviolet light.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: phosphorescent - emitting light after exposure to radiation, but continuing after the source is removed

Etymology

From 'fluorescence', formed from 'fluor' (short for fluorite, a mineral) and '-escence', meaning the process of becoming.

2. ADJ

/ fluˈɒr.ə.sənt / (AmE), / flʊəˈrɛs.ənt / (BrE)
(of a color) very bright and seeming to glow, even in daylight.
brightly glowing (of color)

Examples

She wore a fluorescent yellow jacket so she could be seen at night.
The artist used fluorescent paints to make the poster stand out.
Cyclists often choose fluorescent gear for safety on the road.

Synset Examples

fluorescent colors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: neon - (of a color) extremely bright or vivid

Etymology

From Latin 'fluor' (flow) and scientific use describing light emission.

3. NOUN

/ fluˈɔːrəsɛnt / (AmE), / flʊəˈrɛsənt / (BrE)
a lighting fixture that uses a fluorescent lamp, typically producing bright, energy-efficient light for indoor spaces
lighting fixture with a fluorescent lamp

Examples

Maintenance crews replaced the old fluorescent in the hallway with an LED fixture.
He prefers working under a fluorescent because it gives off less heat than incandescent lights.
The office's fluorescent buzzed faintly throughout the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fluorescent lamp - an electric lamp that produces light by fluorescence
  • Antonym: incandescent - an electric light using a filament that glows when heated

Etymology

From fluorescence, named after fluorite (from Latin *fluor*, meaning 'flow') and the suffix '-escent' indicating 'becoming'.

Source: WordNet
