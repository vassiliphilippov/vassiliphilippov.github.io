to disarm

1. VERB

/ dɪsˈɑrm / (AmE), / dɪsˈɑːm / (BrE)
to remove weapons or the means of attack from a person, group, or country; to make harmless or less threatening.
remove weapons or means of attack

Examples

The peacekeepers worked to disarm the militia before the elections.
After the police managed to disarm the suspect, the situation quickly became less tense.
New treaties require both countries to disarm their nuclear arsenals.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: arm - to supply or equip with weapons

Etymology

From Old French 'desarmer', from Latin 'dis-' (apart) + 'arma' (arms, weapons).

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈɑrm / (AmE), / dɪsˈɑːm / (BrE)
to take weapons away from someone or something; to make someone or something harmless
remove weapons or render harmless

Examples

The peacekeepers were able to disarm the rebels before entering the village.
Her smile could easily disarm even the most suspicious people.
The police worked quickly to disarm the bomb and prevent any harm.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: arm - to provide with weapons
  • Synonym: to demilitarize - to remove military forces or weapons

Etymology

From Old French 'desarmer', from Latin prefix 'dis-' (reversal) + 'arma' (arms, weapons).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01090416-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈɑrm / (AmE), / dɪsˈɑːm / (BrE)
to make someone less hostile or angry, often by charm or kindness; to win over an opponent's goodwill
make someone less hostile or angry

Examples

Her gentle words quickly disarmed his anger, and they began to talk calmly.
The candidate's humble response disarmed critics in the audience.
His sense of humor could easily disarm even the most skeptical interviewer.

Synset Examples

Her charm disarmed the prosecution lawyer completely

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pacify - to calm or soothe someone; to bring peace
  • Antonym: to enrage - to make someone very angry or hostile

Etymology

From Old French desarmer, from Latin dis- (away) + arma (arms, weapons).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
