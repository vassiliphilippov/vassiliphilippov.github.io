weary

1. ADJ

/ ˈwɪri / (AmE), / ˈwɪəri / (BrE)
feeling or showing tiredness, especially after exertion or sustained effort; physically and mentally fatigued
physically and mentally fatigued

Examples

After a long day of work, she felt weary and ready for sleep.
His weary eyes told the story of endless nights without proper rest.
We grew weary of waiting for the delayed train.

Synset Examples

‘aweary’ is archaic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tired - lacking strength or energy because of the need for rest
  • Degree Higher: exhausted - extremely tired; drained of energy

Etymology

From Old English *wērig* (tired), related to German *wurig*.

2. VERB

/ ˈwɪri / (AmE), / ˈwɪəri / (BrE)
to make or become exhausted or tired, especially through prolonged effort or strain
become tired or exhausted

Examples

Over time, the constant noise began to weary her.
The soldiers were wearied by the long march through the mountains.
If you weary yourself now, you'll have no energy left for the evening.

Synset Examples

We wore ourselves out on this hike

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tire - to reduce strength or energy; to exhaust
  • Antonym: to refresh - to restore energy or vigor; to revive

Etymology

From Old English *wērig* (tired).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00074774-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈwɪri / (AmE), / ˈwɪəri / (BrE)
to lose interest in or become bored with something or somebody, often due to repeated exposure or fatigue
lose interest or become bored

Examples

After years on the committee, she began to weary of endless meetings.
Some readers may quickly weary of the novel's repetitive style.
He did not weary of telling his grandchildren the same stories.

Synset Examples

I'm so tired of your mother and her complaints about my food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tire (of) - to become bored or impatient with
  • Antonym: interest - to arouse curiosity or concern

Etymology

From Old English *wǣrig* (tired), related to German *wahr* (true) and Old Norse *veiri* (weather), originally meaning physically tired; verbal sense developed later.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00075867-v
Synset POS: v
