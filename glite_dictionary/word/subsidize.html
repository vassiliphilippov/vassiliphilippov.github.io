to subsidize

1. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.sɪˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.sɪ.daɪz / (BrE)
to support financially by providing a subsidy, especially from the government or another authority
to support financially with a subsidy

Examples

The government decided to subsidize renewable energy sources to encourage their use.
Many public transportation systems are subsidized to keep ticket prices low for commuters.
Farmers often rely on subsidized loans during difficult growing seasons.

Synset Examples

The arts in Europe are heavily subsidized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance to
  • Synonym: fund - to provide money for a particular purpose
  • Antonym: tax - to impose a financial charge, usually contrasted with giving a subsidy

Etymology

From Latin 'subsidium' (support, assistance).

2. VERB

/ ˈsʌb.sɪˌdaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsʌb.sɪ.daɪz / (BrE)
to provide financial support or assistance to help secure the cooperation or services of individuals, groups, or organizations, especially by granting a subsidy
provide financial support by subsidy

Examples

The government decided to subsidize local farmers to ensure food security during the drought.
During the war, the country chose to subsidize its allies' military efforts.
Several nations agreed to subsidize the peacekeeping mission to maintain regional stability.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sponsor - to support someone or something financially or by providing resources
  • Synonym: finance - to provide funding or capital
  • Antonym: to withhold - refuse to give financial support or aid

Etymology

From Latin 'subsidium' (support, assistance) via French 'subsidier'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02221793-v
Synset POS: v
