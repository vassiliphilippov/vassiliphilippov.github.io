caliche

1. NOUN

/ kəˈliːtʃi / (AmE), / kəˈliːtʃeɪ / (BrE)
a layer of hard, calcium carbonate–encrusted subsoil found in arid or semiarid regions
hard calcium-rich soil layer

Examples

The archaeologists had to dig through a thick layer of caliche before reaching the artifacts.
Plants struggle to grow in areas where caliche forms just below the surface.
After years of drought, the caliche became nearly impossible to break with standard tools.

Etymology

From Spanish 'caliche', likely from Latin 'calx' (lime).

2. NOUN

/ kəˈliːtʃi / (AmE), / kəˈliːtʃeɪ / (BrE)
A type of rock or soil deposit rich in nitrates, found especially in the sodium nitrate deposits of Chile and Peru.
nitrate-bearing rock or soil deposit

Examples

The miners extracted caliche to obtain sodium nitrate for fertilizers.
Large deposits of caliche are found in the deserts of northern Chile.
Over time, caliche layers can significantly affect soil permeability and plant growth.

Etymology

From Spanish 'caliche', referring to this type of deposit in South America.
