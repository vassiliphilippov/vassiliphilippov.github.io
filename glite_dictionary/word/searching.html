searching

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɜrtʃɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɜː.tʃɪŋ / (BrE)
showing great keenness, force, or penetration in thought, expression, or intellect; incisive or probing
keen, incisive, or probing in thought or expression

Examples

The journalist posed a searching question that left the politician momentarily speechless.
Her searching analysis revealed flaws nobody else had noticed.
The critic's review was notable for its searching honesty about the film's weaknesses.

Synset Examples

searching insights
trenchant criticism

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incisive - sharp and clear in thought or expression
  • Synonym: trenchant - vigorous or incisive in expression or style
  • Antonym: superficial - lacking depth of thought or feeling

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɝːtʃɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɜːtʃɪŋ / (BrE)
examining or exploring thoroughly and carefully, often to discover the truth or hidden details
examining thoroughly and carefully

Examples

The detective gave me a searching glance that made me uneasy.
She asked a searching question about our real intentions.
Their searching analysis revealed flaws in the original plan.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: probing - carefully investigating or examining
  • Antonym: superficial - not thorough or deep; cursory

Etymology

Present participle of 'search', from Old French 'cerchier', from Latin 'circare' (to go around).

3. ADJ

/ ˈsɜrtʃɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈsɜːtʃɪŋ / (BrE)
examining something carefully and thoroughly to discover the truth, especially so as to reveal hidden details or errors
thorough and careful in examination or investigation

Examples

The lawyer's searching questions made the witness visibly uncomfortable.
Her searching analysis of the data revealed several hidden errors.
With a searching glance, he tried to detect any sign of dishonesty.

Synset Examples

a probing inquiry
a searching investigation of their past dealings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: probing - examining closely and deeply
  • Antonym: superficial - not thorough or deep; lacking detail

Etymology

Present participle of 'search', from Old French *cerchier*, from Latin *circare* (to go about).
