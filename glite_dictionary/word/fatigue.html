fatigue

1. VERB

/ fəˈtiɡ / (AmE), / fəˈtiːɡ / (BrE)
to become bored with or lose interest in something or someone; to become weary of.
become bored or weary of

Examples

After years of routine, he began to fatigue of his daily commute.
She quickly fatigued of listening to their endless arguments.
Many students fatigue of studying the same material repeatedly.

Synset Examples

I'm so tired of your mother and her complaints about my food

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tire - to lose interest or become bored with something or someone

Etymology

From French *fatiguer* (to tire), from Latin *fatigare* (to weary).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00075867-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ fəˈtiɡ / (AmE), / fəˈtiːɡ / (BrE)
a weakened state of a material, especially metal, caused by repeated or prolonged stress
weakening of material from repeated stress

Examples

The airplane wing failed due to fatigue after years of service.
Engineers regularly inspect bridges for signs of metal fatigue.
Prolonged vibrations can cause fatigue in steel structures.

Synset Examples

metal fatigue

Etymology

From French 'fatigue', from Latin 'fatigare' (to weary).

3. NOUN

/ fəˈtiɡ / (AmE), / fəˈtiːɡ / (BrE)
A temporary loss of strength and energy caused by hard physical or mental work.
temporary loss of strength and energy

Examples

After running the marathon, a wave of fatigue washed over her.
Mental fatigue can make it hard to concentrate at work.
The team's fatigue was evident in their slow, uncoordinated movements.

Synset Examples

weariness overcame her after twelve hours and she fell asleep

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weariness - physical or mental exhaustion; tiredness
  • Degree Higher: exhaustion - extreme tiredness; complete depletion of energy
  • Degree Lower: tiredness - the feeling of needing rest; less intense than fatigue

Etymology

From French 'fatigue', from Latin 'fatigare' (to tire out).
