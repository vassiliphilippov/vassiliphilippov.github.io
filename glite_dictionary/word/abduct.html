to abduct

1. VERB

/ æbˈdʌkt / (AmE), / əbˈdʌkt / (BrE)
to take someone away illegally by force or deception, often to demand a ransom or for other criminal purposes
seize and carry off by force

Examples

The criminals planned to abduct the wealthy businessman's daughter for ransom.
Authorities believe the missing child may have been abducted on her way home from school.
He was shocked to discover a plot to abduct members of the royal family.

Synset Examples

The industrialist's son was kidnapped

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to kidnap - to take someone away illegally by force, often to get a ransom

Etymology

From Latin 'abducere' meaning 'to lead away'.

2. VERB

/ æbˈdʌkt / (AmE), / æbˈdʌkt / (BrE)
(of a limb or body part) to move away from the midline of the body or from another part.
to move a limb away from the body
medical

Examples

The deltoid muscle allows you to abduct your arm at the shoulder.
When you abduct your fingers, you spread them apart from each other.
Physical therapists may ask patients to abduct their legs during exercises.

Synset Examples

this muscle abducts

Related Terms

  • Antonym: adduct - to move a limb or part toward the midline of the body

Etymology

From Latin abducere: ab- (away) + ducere (to lead).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01452020-v
Synset POS: v
