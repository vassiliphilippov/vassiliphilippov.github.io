vitality

1. NOUN

/ vaɪˈtæl.ə.ti / (AmE), / vaɪˈtæl.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
energetic and lively quality or style; the power to impart energy and enthusiasm
energetic or lively quality

Examples

Her vitality brought excitement to every event she attended.
The painting radiates vitality and movement, capturing the energy of city life.
Even in her old age, the author wrote with remarkable vitality.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: energy - vigorous spirit or liveliness
  • Synonym: vivacity - the quality of being attractively lively and animated
  • Antonym: lethargy - lack of energy or enthusiasm

Etymology

From Latin 'vitalitas' (life, vigor), from 'vitalis' (of life).

2. NOUN

/ vaɪˈtæləti / (AmE), / vaɪˈtælɪti / (BrE)
(historical, biology) The hypothetical non-physical force once believed to cause the evolution and development of organisms; the vital force.
the vital force once thought to animate life

Examples

In early twentieth-century biology, vitality was believed to be an immaterial force guiding development.
Bergson's theory of vitality challenged purely mechanical views of evolution.
Some scientists dismissed the concept of vitality as unscientific and favored chemical explanations instead.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: élan vital - the vital force or impulse behind life, introduced by philosopher Henri Bergson

Etymology

From Latin vitalitas (vital force, life).

the power or ability of something to survive, grow, or continue to exist; the capacity for continued life and development.
ability to survive and grow

Examples

The vitality of the young tree was obvious as it quickly adapted to its new environment.
Doctors were surprised by the patient's vitality after such a serious operation.
Seeds with higher vitality are more likely to sprout and develop into healthy plants.
