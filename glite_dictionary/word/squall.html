squall

1. NOUN

/ skwɑl / (AmE), / skwɔːl / (BrE)
A sudden, violent gust of wind, often accompanied by rain, snow, or sleet.
sudden strong gust of wind

Examples

A sudden squall knocked over the boats at the harbor.
We had to take cover when a fierce squall hit during our hike.
Dark clouds warned us of an approaching squall late in the afternoon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gale - a strong wind

Etymology

Perhaps from Scandinavian (compare Norwegian 'skval', to gush, pour).

2. VERB

/ skwɑl / (AmE), / skwɔːl / (BrE)
(of a person or animal) to cry out or make high-pitched, whiny noises, especially in a loud or prolonged way.
cry out in a high-pitched, whiny way

Examples

The baby began to squall loudly when she dropped her toy.
Seagulls squall overhead as the fishermen unload their catch.
He could hear the kittens squalling for food in the alley.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scream - make a loud, high-pitched cry or sound
  • Synonym: wail - make a long, loud, high-pitched cry
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ skwɑl / (AmE), / skwɔːl / (BrE)
to utter a sudden, loud, and often harsh cry or shout, especially in fear, pain, or complaint
utter a sudden loud cry

Examples

The infant began to squall as soon as the doctor entered the room.
The cat squalled when its tail was accidentally stepped on.
The baby was squalling loudly in the middle of the night.

Synset Examples

she cried with pain when the doctor inserted the needle
I yelled to her from the window but she couldn't hear me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yell - to shout loudly, especially because of anger, pain, or excitement
  • Synonym: cry - to make a loud sound, especially to express pain or emotion
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak very quietly, using the breath rather than the voice

Etymology

Probably imitative of a loud harsh sound; similar to Scandinavian roots (e.g. Norwegian skvala 'to scream').

4. VERB

/ skwɑl / (AmE), / skwɔːl / (BrE)
(of wind) to blow suddenly and violently, typically with a sudden increase in intensity.
blow with sudden, violent wind

Examples

The wind began to squall just as our boat reached the open water.
Storm clouds gathered overhead, and it started to squall unexpectedly, forcing the sailors to take cover.
If the weather threatens to squall, experienced sailors quickly adjust their sails.

Synset Examples

When it squalls, a prudent sailor reefs his sails

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gust - a sudden strong rush of wind

Etymology

Possibly from Scandinavian; compare Swedish 'skvala' (to gush, pour out).
