heist

1. NOUN

/ haɪst / (AmE), / haɪst / (BrE)
an act of stealing, especially one involving careful planning; a robbery
act of stealing; robbery
informal

Examples

The diamond heist was meticulously planned and shocked the city.
A group of thieves pulled off a daring heist at the art museum last night.
The movie tells the story of an elaborate bank heist gone wrong.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: robbery - the taking of property unlawfully from a person or place by force or threat
  • Synonym: burglary - unlawful entry into a building with intent to commit a crime, especially theft

Etymology

Probably from earlier American English slang 'heist' (to hoist, lift), related to the verb 'hoist'.

2. VERB

/ haɪst / (AmE), / haɪst / (BrE)
to steal or rob something, especially by breaking into a building or carrying out a planned theft
to commit a robbery or burglary
informal

Examples

The gang planned to heist a shipment of rare jewels from the vault.
Three masked men heisted the art gallery late last night and escaped unseen.
They tried to heist the casino but were caught by the police.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to burgle - to enter a building illegally and steal things
  • Synonym: to rob - to take property unlawfully from a person or place by force or threat

Etymology

Probably from earlier English 'hoist' (to lift), influenced by criminal slang.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02577426-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ haɪst / (AmE), / haɪst / (BrE)
A robbery, especially one involving elaborate planning and often carried out at gunpoint.
robbery, especially a large or planned one

Examples

The gang planned a heist on the city's main jewelry store.
After the daring heist, the thieves vanished without a trace.
That movie is about a complex heist that goes wrong.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: robbery - the act of stealing from someone, typically with violence or threat
  • Synonym: theft - the act of taking someone's property without permission
  • Synonym: burglary - the illegal entry into a building, usually to steal

Etymology

Probably from American English slang, perhaps a variant of 'hoist' (to lift).
