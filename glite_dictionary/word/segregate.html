to segregate

1. VERB

/ ˈsɛɡrəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɛɡrɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to separate or set apart from others or from the main group, often for a particular purpose or characteristic
to separate from a group

Examples

During cell division, chromosomes segregate into two independent sets.
The waste material must be segregated from the recyclable items before processing.
After the merger, the smaller department was segregated into a specialized unit.

Synset Examples

Many towns segregated into new counties
Experiments show clearly that genes segregate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separate - to set or keep apart
  • Antonym: to integrate - to combine or bring together into a whole

Etymology

From Latin segregatus, past participle of segregare (to separate), from se- (apart) + grex, greg- (flock).

2. VERB

/ ˈsɛɡrəˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɛɡrɪɡeɪt / (BrE)
to separate or isolate (someone or something) from others or from a main group, often by placing in a separate category or area
separate or isolate from others

Examples

In the 1950s, laws were created to segregate black and white students in some schools.
Hospitals may segregate patients with contagious diseases to prevent the spread of infection.
The company decided to segregate hazardous materials from regular waste for safety reasons.

Synset Examples

the sun segregates the carbon
large mining claims are segregated into smaller claims

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separate - to set or keep apart
  • Antonym: to integrate - to combine or bring together into a whole

Etymology

From Latin segregare, from se- (apart) + grex (flock, herd).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00495549-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈsɛɡ.rɪˌɡeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɛɡ.rɪ.ɡeɪt / (BrE)
to separate people, especially by race, religion, or gender, and treat them differently; to enforce or practice segregation
separate people by race, religion, etc.

Examples

The government used to segregate schools by race, but this practice is now illegal.
It is wrong to segregate people in the workplace based on their religion.
Several states continued to segregate public facilities well into the 20th century.

Synset Examples

This neighborhood is segregated
We don't segregate in this county

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to integrate - to bring people or groups with different characteristics together; to end segregation
  • Synonym: separate - to set or keep apart; not together or touching

Etymology

From Latin segregatus, past participle of segregare (to separate from the flock), from se- (apart) + grex, greg- (flock).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02487728-v
Synset POS: v
