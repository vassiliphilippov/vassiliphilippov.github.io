halibut

1. NOUN

/ ˈhæl.ɪ.bət / (AmE), / ˈhal.ɪ.bət / (BrE)
A large flatfish found in northern Atlantic and Pacific waters, valued as a food fish and among the largest of its kind.
a large flatfish, edible fish

Examples

The chef prepared grilled halibut with lemon and herbs for dinner.
Pacific halibut can grow to be hundreds of pounds in weight.
He ordered a fillet of halibut at the seafood restaurant.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flounder - a type of smaller flatfish

Etymology

From Middle English *halybutte*, from 'holy' (haly) + 'butt' (flatfish), as it was eaten on holy days.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhæləbət / (AmE), / ˈhælɪbət / (BrE)
the lean, edible flesh of a very large flatfish of the Atlantic or Pacific, valued as food
large flatfish used as food

Examples

We grilled the halibut and served it with a lemon butter sauce for dinner.
Fresh halibut is often considered a delicacy in coastal restaurants.
He bought a large halibut at the fish market for the weekend barbecue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flounder - another type of flatfish
  • Synonym: sole - a different but similar flatfish valued as food

Etymology

From Middle English *halybutte*, from Old English *halig* (holy) + *butte* (flatfish), as it was eaten on holy days.

3. NOUN

/ ˈhæləˌbʌt / (AmE), / ˈhælɪbət / (BrE)
a large flatfish found in the northern Atlantic and northern Pacific oceans, valued as a food fish and known for its size.
large flatfish, marine food fish

Examples

We ordered grilled halibut at the seafood restaurant by the harbor.
Commercial fishing for halibut is strictly regulated to prevent overfishing.
The chef prepared a delicate sauce to complement the fresh halibut fillets.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: flounder - a smaller type of flatfish found in similar habitats
  • Degree Lower: sole - another type of smaller flatfish

Etymology

From Middle English 'halybutte', from Old English 'halig' (holy) + 'butte' (flatfish), because it was eaten on holy days.
