probabilism

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɑːbəˌbɪlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈprɒbəbɪˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
The ethical doctrine in Roman Catholic casuistry that allows a person to follow any opinion that has solid probability, even if another opinion is more probable, when expert views differ.
Catholic doctrine about acting on probable opinions

Examples

The principles of probabilism have long influenced Catholic ethical discussions.
Under probabilism, a confessor may recommend following any reputable opinion, not just the most probable one.
Her studies focused on the historical debates surrounding probabilism in moral theology.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: casuistry - the use of case-based reasoning in ethical decision-making

Etymology

From Latin probabilis (probable) + -ism (doctrine or practice).

2. NOUN

/ proʊˈbæb.əˌlɪz.əm / (AmE), / prəˈbæb.ɪ.lɪz.əm / (BrE)
The philosophical doctrine that, since certainty is unattainable, probability is a sufficient basis for belief and decision-making.
doctrine relying on probability for belief/action

Examples

Some philosophers argue that probabilism provides a practical basis for making decisions in the absence of certainty.
The concept of probabilism has influenced debates in both ethics and the philosophy of science.
He rejected absolute certainty, instead embracing probabilism as a guide for belief.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: skepticism - philosophical doubting of knowledge or certainty
  • Antonym: dogmatism - tendency to lay down principles as incontrovertibly true

Etymology

From Latin 'probabilis' (provable, credible) + '-ism'.
