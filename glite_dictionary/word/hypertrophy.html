hypertrophy

1. NOUN

/ haɪˈpɜr.trə.fi / (AmE), / haɪˈpɜː.trə.fi / (BrE)
the abnormal enlargement of a body part or organ due to the increased size of its cells
abnormal enlargement of an organ or tissue
medical

Examples

Cardiac hypertrophy can result from long-term high blood pressure.
Weightlifting may cause muscular hypertrophy in athletes.
The doctor explained that thyroid hypertrophy could lead to other health problems.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: atrophy - the wasting away or decrease in size of a body part or tissue

Etymology

From Greek 'hyper-' (over) + 'trophe' (nourishment).

2. VERB

/ haɪˈpɜrtrəˌfi / (AmE), / haɪˈpɜːtrəfi / (BrE)
(of an organ or tissue) to increase in size abnormally due to the enlargement of its cells.
enlarge abnormally in size (medicine/biology)
medical

Examples

Intense weight training can cause the heart muscle to hypertrophy over time.
If testosterone levels are elevated, certain tissues may hypertrophy beyond normal limits.
Doctors noticed that the patient's left ventricle had hypertrophied after years of high blood pressure.

Synset Examples

muscles can hypertrophy when people take steroids

Related Terms

  • Antonym: atrophy - to waste away or decrease in size, especially of body tissue

Etymology

From Greek 'hypertrophia' (excessive nourishment), from 'hyper-' (over) + 'trophē' (nourishment).
