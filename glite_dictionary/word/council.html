council

1. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊn.səl / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.səl / (BrE)
A formal meeting or assembly of people convened for consultation, discussion, or advice on important matters.
formal meeting for consultation

Examples

The city held a special council to address the emergency.
Delegates from each department attended the monthly council.
An international council was convened to discuss environmental policy.

Synset Examples

emergency council

Related Terms

  • Synonym: committee - A group of people appointed for a specific function
  • Synonym: board - A group of people who manage or oversee an organization

Etymology

From Latin concilium (assembly, meeting).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊnsəl / (AmE), / ˈkaʊnsəl / (BrE)
a group of people formally constituted and meeting regularly to manage or advise on administrative, legislative, or organizational matters
administrative or advisory body

Examples

The city council voted unanimously to approve the new housing project.
She was elected president of the student council this year.
A local council has been set up to oversee the festival planning.

Synset Examples

student council

Related Terms

  • Synonym: committee - a group of people appointed for a specific function
  • Synonym: board - a group of people who manage or supervise an organization

Etymology

From Latin consilium (deliberation, assembly, counsel), via Old French concile.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08327879-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊn.səl / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.səl / (BrE)
an assembly of theologians, bishops, and other representatives of various Christian churches or dioceses, convened to regulate matters of doctrine or discipline
assembly of church leaders to decide doctrine

Examples

The Nicene council was convened to address theological disputes in early Christianity.
Roman Catholic doctrine was clarified at the council of Trent.
The church historian wrote extensively about the outcomes of each major council.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: synod - an assembly of church officials for discussion and decision-making

Etymology

From Old French 'concile', from Latin 'concilium' (assembly, meeting).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08329489-n
Synset POS: n
