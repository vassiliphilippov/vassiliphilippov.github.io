plunder

1. NOUN

/ ˈplʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈplʌndə / (BrE)
goods or money taken illegally, especially by force during war or robbery.
stolen goods; loot

Examples

The pirates divided the plunder among themselves after the raid.
He was arrested in possession of plunder stolen from the museum.
The victors returned home, their carts filled with plunder.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loot - stolen goods, especially obtained by force
  • Synonym: booty - valuables taken by force or theft
  • Synonym: spoils - goods or benefits taken from others, especially in war

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'plunderen' (to plunder, pillage).

2. VERB

/ ˈplʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈplʌndə / (BrE)
to steal goods from a place or person, typically using force and causing damage; to destroy and strip of possessions
steal by force, destroy and strip of possessions

Examples

After the invasion, the soldiers began to plunder the city, taking everything of value.
Vikings would often plunder coastal villages, leaving destruction behind them.
The pirates plundered the merchant ship before disappearing into the fog.

Synset Examples

The soldiers raped the beautiful country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loot - to steal goods, especially during war or riot
  • Synonym: pillage - to rob a place using violence, especially in wartime
  • Antonym: to protect - to keep safe from harm or injury

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'plunderen' (to plunder, pillage).

/ ˈplʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈplʌndə / (BrE)
to steal goods, especially by force, during or after capturing a place
steal goods by force, esp. after capture

Examples

The army proceeded to plunder the town after breaking through the gates.
Invaders would often plunder conquered cities, taking everything of value.
During the chaos, looters began to plunder abandoned shops.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pillage - to rob using violence, especially in war
  • Antonym: to protect - to keep safe from harm or injury

Etymology

From Middle High German *plunderen* (to loot), possibly from *plunder* (household goods).

4. VERB

/ ˈplʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈplʌndə / (BrE)
to take or use something, especially ideas, work, or property, dishonestly or illegally, often without permission or credit
take or use dishonestly, esp. ideas or work

Examples

The author was accused of plundering ideas from earlier works without giving credit.
Many companies have faced lawsuits for plundering patented technology.
He openly admitted to plundering themes from classic novels in his screenplay.

Synset Examples

This writer plundered from famous authors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steal - to take something that does not belong to you without permission
  • Synonym: appropriate - to take something for one's own use, often without permission
  • Antonym: to borrow - to take and use something with the intention of returning it

Etymology

From Middle English *plunderen*, probably from Middle Dutch *plunderen* (to plunder).

/ ˈplʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈplʌndə / (BrE)
to steal goods, typically using force and during a time of disorder such as war or disaster
steal goods using force

Examples

Opportunistic thieves attempted to plunder abandoned shops after the hurricane struck.
Archaeologists discovered that the ancient tomb had been plundered centuries ago.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pillage - to rob or steal, especially during wartime
