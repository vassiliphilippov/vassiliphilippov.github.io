slew

1. NOUN

/ sluː / (AmE), / sluː / (BrE)
a large number or amount of something; a lot
large number or amount

Examples

The company received a slew of complaints after the new policy was announced.
She has written a slew of best-selling books over the last decade.
There are a slew of options available for anyone considering a career change.

Synset Examples

made lots of new friends
she amassed stacks of newspapers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lot - a large number or quantity of something
  • Synonym: pile - a large accumulation or collection of things

Etymology

Variant of "slough" (swamp), later used in American English for "large amount" possibly due to confusion with "a lot" or "a mass".

2. VERB

/ sluː / (AmE), / sluː / (BrE)
(of a vehicle or object) to slide or turn sideways or obliquely, often in an uncontrolled way.
slide or turn sideways, uncontrollably

Examples

The car began to slew dangerously on the icy road.
She lost control and the bicycle slewed sideways into the hedge.
The truck slewed across the highway to avoid the deer.

Synset Examples

the wheels skidded against the sidewalk

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skid - slide, typically sideways or obliquely, especially out of control

Etymology

Variant of 'slue', origin uncertain; possibly from Middle English 'sluwen' (to twist, roll).

Source: WordNet

/ sluː / (AmE), / sluː / (BrE)
a large number or amount of something, especially used in the phrase 'a slew of'
a large quantity or number (often 'a slew of')

Examples

There are a slew of reasons why the project was delayed.

4. VERB

/ sluː / (AmE), / sluː / (BrE)
to turn or slide sharply in a particular direction, especially making a sudden sideways movement
to turn sharply; move suddenly to the side

Examples

The car slewed across the icy road and hit the barrier.
Without warning, the boat slewed to the right as the wind picked up.
The truck's back end slewed out on the wet pavement.

Synset Examples

The car cut to the left at the intersection
The motorbike veered to the right

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swerve - to change direction suddenly, especially to avoid something
  • Synonym: to veer - to change direction suddenly or unexpectedly

Etymology

Probably an alteration of 'slue', of unknown origin, but early uses in nautical contexts.
