corrosive

1. NOUN

/ kəˈroʊsɪv / (AmE), / kəˈrəʊsɪv / (BrE)
A substance that has the tendency to cause corrosion, especially by chemical action, such as a strong acid or alkali.
a caustic or corrosive substance

Examples

Laboratory safety guidelines require that all corrosive materials be stored in special containers.
Hydrochloric acid is a common corrosive used in industrial cleaning processes.
Always wear gloves when handling any corrosive to protect your skin from injury.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caustic - a substance that burns or destroys organic tissue by chemical action
  • Antonym: neutralizer - a substance that counteracts the effect of a corrosive

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *corrosivus* (tending to corrode), from Latin *corrodere* (to gnaw away).

2. ADJ

/ kəˈɹoʊsɪv / (AmE), / kəˈɹəʊsɪv / (BrE)
(of comments or expressions) expressing harsh criticism in a bitingly sarcastic or damaging way.
spitefully sarcastic; harshly critical

Examples

Her corrosive remarks left everyone in the meeting feeling wounded.
The comedian's corrosive humor often pushed the boundaries of good taste.
After years of corrosive criticism, his confidence was badly shaken.

Related Terms

From Latin corrodere (to gnaw away).

3. ADJ

/ kəˈroʊsɪv / (AmE), / kəˈrəʊsɪv / (BrE)
(of a substance) able to destroy or eat away materials by means of a chemical reaction, especially causing damage or deterioration.
able to destroy by chemical action

Examples

Always wear protective gloves when handling corrosive chemicals in the laboratory.
The corrosive effect of saltwater can weaken metal structures over time.
Acids like hydrochloric acid are highly corrosive and must be stored safely.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: caustic - able to burn or corrode organic tissue; highly corrosive

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *corrosivus*, from Latin *corrodere* (to gnaw away).
