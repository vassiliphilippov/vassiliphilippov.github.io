to vouch

1. VERB

/ vaʊtʃ / (AmE), / vaʊtʃ / (BrE)
to give supporting evidence or to confirm the truth or reliability of something or someone
give supporting evidence; confirm

Examples

I can vouch for his honesty; he has always been trustworthy.
Several witnesses vouched that they saw the accident happen.
Before lending her money, he asked if anyone could vouch for her character.

Synset Examples

He vouched his words by his deeds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to attest - to provide or serve as clear evidence of
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true; refuse to accept

Etymology

From Old French *voucher* (to summon, call to witness), from Latin *vocare* (to call).

2. VERB

/ vaʊtʃ / (AmE), / vaʊtʃ / (BrE)
(legal, archaic) To summon someone (a vouchee) into court to warrant or defend a title.
summon someone into court (legal, archaic)
legal archaic

Examples

The defendant may vouch another party into court to defend the disputed title.
In medieval property law, it was common to vouch a vouchee to guarantee one's claim.
If challenged, the tenant could vouch the original grantor in defense of his title.

Etymology

From Old French 'voucher' (to call, summon, or guarantee), from Latin 'vocare' (to call).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00793827-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ vaʊtʃ / (AmE), / vaʊtʃ / (BrE)
to give personal assurance or guarantee the reliability or truth of someone or something
state assurance or guarantee

Examples

I can vouch for her honesty because I've known her for years.
The witness vouched for the suspect's whereabouts on the night of the crime.
Before hiring a candidate, the company asks employees to vouch for their references.

Synset Examples

Will he vouch for me?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guarantee - to provide a formal assurance for something
  • Synonym: to affirm - to assert as true; declare
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true

Etymology

From Old French 'voucher' (to summon, call as a witness), from Latin 'vocare' (to call).

4. VERB

/ vaʊtʃ / (AmE), / vaʊtʃ / (BrE)
to confirm the truth, accuracy, or reliability of something; to guarantee or affirm responsibility for someone or something
affirm or guarantee the truth or reliability

Examples

I can vouch for his honesty; he has always been trustworthy.
The manager refused to vouch for the new supplier without further investigation.
She was asked to vouch for her friend's whereabouts on the night of the incident.

Synset Examples

I vouch for the quality of my products

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guarantee - promise to take responsibility for something if necessary
  • Synonym: to certify - officially state that something is true or meets a standard
  • Antonym: to disclaim - refuse to acknowledge responsibility or involvement

Etymology

From Old French 'voucher' (to summon, call to witness), from Latin 'vocare' (to call).
