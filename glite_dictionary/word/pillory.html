pillory

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɪləri / (AmE), / ˈpɪləri / (BrE)
a wooden framework with holes for the head and hands, in which offenders were locked and exposed to public ridicule or punishment
wooden device for public punishment
archaic

Examples

The criminal was placed in the pillory and jeered at by the townspeople all afternoon.
In the medieval square, the pillory stood as a warning to potential lawbreakers.
Old records show that minor offenders were often sentenced to hours in the pillory for public humiliation.

Etymology

From Old French *pilori*, of uncertain origin; attested in English since the 13th century.

2. VERB

/ ˈpɪlɚi / (AmE), / ˈpɪləri / (BrE)
to criticize someone or something publicly and harshly, especially so as to subject them to ridicule or scorn
criticize harshly in public

Examples

The senator was pilloried in the media for his controversial remarks.
She felt unfairly pilloried by the critics after her first novel was published.
Many celebrities have been pilloried online for their public statements.

Synset Examples

The press savaged the new President
The critics crucified the author for plagiarizing a famous passage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to lambaste - to criticize someone or something severely
  • Antonym: praise - to express warm approval or admiration

Etymology

From Old French *pilori* (pillory, a wooden framework for public punishment).

3. VERB

/ ˈpɪləri / (AmE), / ˈpɪləri / (BrE)
to punish someone by placing them in a wooden framework with holes for the head and hands, as a form of public humiliation
to punish by placing in a pillory

Examples

In colonial times, criminals were pilloried in the town square as a warning to others.
The mayor was pilloried for his corrupt actions during a mock-trial festival.
Villagers gathered around to jeer at the thief who was pilloried for his crimes.

Etymology

From Old French *pilori*, possibly from Medieval Latin *pilloria* (framework for punishment).

4. VERB

/ ˈpɪləri / (AmE), / ˈpɪlɒri / (BrE)
to publicly criticize or ridicule someone, especially in a way that exposes them to scorn or contempt
critique or ridicule publicly

Examples

She felt unfairly pilloried by her colleagues after the project failed.
Online platforms can sometimes pillory people for minor mistakes, leading to intense public scrutiny.

Related Terms
