sackcloth

1. NOUN

/ ˈsækˌklɔθ / (AmE), / ˈsækˌklɒθ / (BrE)
A coarse, rough fabric often made from jute or hemp, formerly worn as a garment to show sorrow or repentance, especially in religious contexts.
coarse garment worn to show remorse
archaic

Examples

In ancient times, people wore sackcloth and ashes to express their deep sorrow.
He appeared before the assembly in sackcloth, seeking forgiveness for his actions.
The old paintings often depict prophets dressed in sackcloth as a sign of repentance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hairshirt - an uncomfortable garment worn as punishment or to show penance

Etymology

From Old English saccclāth, from sack (bag made of coarse cloth) + cloth.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsækˌklɔθ / (AmE), / ˈsæk.klɒθ / (BrE)
A coarse, rough fabric similar to sacking, traditionally used for bags or as a symbol of mourning or penitence.
coarse, rough fabric used for bags or mourning

Examples

During the ceremony, the mourners wore garments made of sackcloth to express their grief.
The farmer used old sackcloth to cover the harvested crops and protect them from the sun.
In historical times, people would dress in sackcloth during periods of sorrow or repentance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burlap - a coarse canvas woven from jute or similar fiber

Etymology

From Old English saccclāth, combining 'sack' and 'cloth'.
