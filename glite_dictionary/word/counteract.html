to counteract

1. VERB

/ ˌkaʊntɚˈækt / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntərˈækt / (BrE)
to act against something in order to reduce its force or neutralize it
act against; neutralize

Examples

The new drug is designed to counteract the effects of high blood pressure.
Government measures were put in place to counteract rising inflation.
She took quick action to counteract the spread of the virus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to neutralize - to make something ineffective by applying an opposite force or effect
  • Antonym: to reinforce - to strengthen or support, especially with additional personnel or material

Etymology

From Latin contra- (against) + agere (to act) via French 'contrecarrer'.

2. VERB

/ ˌkaʊntərˈækt / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntərˈækt / (BrE)
to act against something in order to reduce its force or neutralize it
act to reduce or neutralize

Examples

The new government measures are designed to counteract inflation.
He drank coffee to counteract the drowsiness caused by the medication.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to neutralize - to make ineffective by applying an opposite force
  • Antonym: to reinforce - to strengthen or support

Etymology

From counter- (against) + act (from Latin actus, a doing).

3. VERB

/ ˌkaʊntərˈækt / (AmE), / ˌkaʊntərˈækt / (BrE)
to act against something in order to reduce its force or neutralize its effect
to neutralize or oppose an effect

Examples

The government introduced new laws to counteract tax evasion.
Drinking water can counteract the effects of dehydration.
They hired extra staff to counteract the increased workload.

Synset Examples

This will counteract the foolish actions of my colleagues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to neutralize - to make something ineffective by applying an opposite force
  • Antonym: to reinforce - to strengthen or support, especially with additional personnel or material

Etymology

From counter- (against) + act, influenced by Latin contrā (against) and actus (act).
