Utopia

1. PROPN

/ juˈtoʊpiə / (AmE), / juːˈtəʊpiə / (BrE)
A book written by Sir Thomas More in 1516 that describes an ideal society on an imaginary island.
book by Thomas More (1516) about an ideal society

Examples

Many scholars study Utopia to understand early thoughts on social reform.
Sir Thomas More's Utopia influenced countless subsequent works of political fiction.
In Utopia, More critiques the politics and society of his own time.

Etymology

From Greek 'ou' (not) + 'topos' (place), meaning 'no place'; coined as the title by Thomas More.

2. NOUN

/ juˈtoʊpiə / (AmE), / juːˈtəʊpiə / (BrE)
an imagined place or state of things in which everything is perfect
imaginary perfect place or state

Examples

Many philosophers have tried to describe a Utopia where everyone lives in harmony.
The new city plan was inspired by the concept of a Utopia, with green spaces and no pollution.
She dismissed his vision as unrealistic, calling it a modern Utopia that could never exist.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dystopia - an imagined state or society where there is great suffering or injustice, typically a totalitarian or environmentally degraded one

Etymology

From Greek 'ou' (not) + 'topos' (place), coined by Sir Thomas More in 1516 as the title of his book.
