to divest

1. VERB

/ daɪˈvɛst / (AmE), / daɪˈvɛst / (BrE)
to take away possessions, rights, or power from someone or an organization, often as an act of authority or compulsion
take away possessions, rights, or power

Examples

The new government decided to divest the company of its land holdings.
During the reforms, many officials were divested of their positions and privileges.
The law aims to divest certain organizations of their financial assets.

Synset Examples

The Nazis stripped the Jews of all their assets

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strip - to remove property, rights, or titles from someone
  • Synonym: to deprive - to take something away from someone

Etymology

From Latin 'divestire' (to undress), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'vestire' (to clothe).

2. VERB

/ daɪˈvɛst / (AmE), / daɪˈvɛst / (BrE)
to sell off or dispose of an asset, investment, or part of a business; to cease to hold an investment
to sell off or dispose of an investment

Examples

The organization decided to divest from fossil fuel companies in response to public pressure.
After the merger, the company was required to divest some of its assets to comply with regulations.
She chose to divest her holdings in the tech sector before the market declined.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

/ daɪˈvɛst / (AmE), / dɪˈvɛst / (BrE)

Examples

4. VERB

/ daɪˈvɛst / (AmE), / dɪˈvɛst / (BrE)
to remove (someone's or one's own) clothing; to undress, especially in a formal or deliberate manner
to remove clothing; to undress

Examples

After entering the warm room, she divested herself of her heavy coat and boots.
He quickly divested the child of her wet clothes so she wouldn't catch cold.
The actors divested themselves of their costumes backstage after the performance.
