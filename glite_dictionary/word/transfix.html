to transfix

1. VERB

/ trænsˈfɪks / (AmE), / trænsˈfɪks / (BrE)
to cause someone to become motionless with amazement, terror, or awe
make motionless with amazement or fear

Examples

She was transfixed by the beauty of the sunset, unable to look away.
The audience stood transfixed as the magician performed his final trick.
He transfixed his opponents with a cold, unwavering stare.

Synset Examples

The snake charmer fascinates the cobra

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to mesmerize - to hold someone's attention completely as if by magic
  • Synonym: to petrify - to make someone unable to move out of fear

Etymology

From Latin transfixus, past participle of transfigere (to pierce through).

2. VERB

/ trænsˈfɪks / (AmE), / trænsˈfɪks / (BrE)
to pierce something or someone with a sharp stake or point so as to impale or hold fast
pierce with a sharp point or stake

Examples

The hunter used a spear to transfix the fish as it swam by.
Lightning seemed to transfix the tree during the storm, splitting it in two.
She watched as the chef transfixed each vegetable on a wooden skewer for the grill.

Synset Examples

impale a shrimp on a skewer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to impale - to pierce or transfix with a sharp instrument

Etymology

From Latin 'transfigere', from 'trans-' (across) + 'figere' (to fix, fasten).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01446945-v
Synset POS: v
