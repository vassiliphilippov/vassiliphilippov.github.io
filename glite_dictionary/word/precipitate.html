precipitate

1. VERB

/ prɪˈsɪpɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / prɪˈsɪpɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of rain, snow, etc.) to fall from clouds, usually as a result of atmospheric processes.
(of rain, snow) fall from clouds

Examples

Heavy clouds began to precipitate snow across the region by midday.
The storm will precipitate several inches of rain before moving east.
Water vapor in the air can precipitate as sleet under certain conditions.

Synset Examples

rain, snow and sleet were falling
Vesuvius precipitated its fiery, destructive rage on Herculaneum

Etymology

From Latin praecipitatus, past participle of praecipitare (to throw headlong).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02762779-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ prɪˈsɪpɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / prɪˈsɪpɪtət / (BrE)
a solid substance separated from a solution, typically as a result of a chemical reaction, often appearing as a suspension or after settling or filtering
solid formed from a solution (chemistry)
medical

Examples

After mixing the two solutions, a white precipitate formed at the bottom of the test tube.
The chemist filtered the precipitate from the liquid to analyze its composition.
A yellow precipitate indicates the presence of certain metal ions in the solution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sediment - solid material that settles at the bottom of a liquid

Etymology

From Latin praecipitatus (hurled headlong), referring to sudden falling or separation.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14615815-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ prɪˈsɪpɪtət / (AmE), / prɪˈsɪpɪtət / (BrE)
(of an action) done with excessive haste or without careful consideration.
done hastily or without thought

Examples

Critics argued that the government's decision was precipitate and poorly planned.
A precipitate withdrawal of troops could destabilize the region even further.
His precipitate response surprised everyone, as they expected a more thoughtful reply.

Synset Examples

wondered whether they had been rather precipitate in deposing the king

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hasty - done with excessive speed or urgency
  • Antonym: deliberate - done with careful thought or consideration

Etymology

From Latin 'praecipitatus', past participle of 'praecipitare' (to throw headlong).

4. VERB

/ prɪˈsɪpɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / prɪˈsɪpɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of a substance) to separate out from a solution as solid particles, usually as a result of a chemical reaction.
separate as solid particles from solution
medical

Examples

Silver chloride will precipitate out of the solution when two reagents are mixed.
When the temperature drops, calcium carbonate may precipitate from seawater.
Adding sodium hydroxide causes the metal ions to precipitate as a solid.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dissolve - to cause to become incorporated into a liquid so as to form a solution

Etymology

From Latin 'praecipitare' (to throw headlong), from 'praeceps' (headlong).

7. VERB

/ prɪˈsɪpɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / prɪˈsɪpɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of something) to fall suddenly and rapidly, especially from a height or into a particular situation.
to fall or plunge suddenly and rapidly

Examples

Heavy rainfall caused the boulders to precipitate down the mountainside.
After the announcement, the stock market began to precipitate into a sharp decline.
The boat lost its balance and threatened to precipitate everyone into the cold water.

Synset Examples

Our economy precipitated into complete ruin

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunge - to fall or move suddenly downward or forward

Etymology

From Latin praecipitare (to throw headlong), from prae- (before) + caput (head).
