regurgitation

1. NOUN

/ rɪˌɡɝːdʒɪˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌɡɜːdʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the backward flow of blood through a defective heart valve
backflow of blood in the heart
medical

Examples

Mitral valve regurgitation can lead to heart failure if left untreated.
The doctor diagnosed him with a mild case of aortic regurgitation.
Proper treatment can help manage symptoms of blood regurgitation in heart patients.

Etymology

From Latin regurgitatio (a surging back), from re- (back) + gurges (whirlpool).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˌɡɝːdʒəˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌɡɜːdʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of repeating information exactly as learned, often without understanding or analysis
rote repetition of learned information

Examples

Some students rely heavily on regurgitation of facts instead of critical thinking.
The final exam tested understanding, not just regurgitation of textbook material.
He criticized the curriculum for encouraging mere regurgitation rather than creativity.

Synset Examples

he complained that school was just memorization and regurgitation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: memorization - learning information by heart

Etymology

From Latin regurgitatio (a flooding back), from regurgitare (to flow back).

3. NOUN

/ rɪˌɡɝːdʒɪˈteɪʃən / (AmE), / rɪˌɡɜːdʒɪˈteɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of bringing swallowed food or liquid back up from the stomach and out through the mouth, often involuntarily
ejecting stomach contents through the mouth
medical

Examples

The patient experienced sudden regurgitation after eating a large meal.
Some birds feed their chicks by regurgitation of partially digested food.
Chronic acid regurgitation can be a symptom of gastroesophageal reflux disease.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vomiting - forcing the contents of the stomach out through the mouth; more forceful than regurgitation

Etymology

From Latin regurgitatio, from regurgitare (to flow back).
