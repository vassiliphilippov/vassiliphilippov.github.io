tun

1. NOUN

/ tʌn / (AmE), / tʌn / (BrE)
a large cask, especially one equal to 252 gallons or two butts, used mainly for storing wine or other liquids
large cask or barrel (252 gallons)

Examples

The cellar held a massive tun of wine dating back to the 18th century.
In the brewery, each tun was filled with freshly brewed beer for fermentation.
Historically, ships transported goods in large tuns across the ocean.

Etymology

From Old English *tunne* (barrel, cask), related to Old High German *tunna* (tub, tun).

2. NOUN

/ tʌn / (AmE), / tʌn / (BrE)
an archaic or poetic term for a garment or tunic; a long, loose piece of clothing worn on the upper body
archaic word for tunic or garment
archaic

Examples

She admired the embroidery on her tun, which shimmered in the candlelight.
In ancient times, men and women alike wore a simple tun as daily attire.
The play featured actors dressed in elaborate tuns reminiscent of medieval fashion.

Synset Examples

She wore a beautiful tun that flowed with every step.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tunic - a loose garment, typically sleeveless, reaching to the wearer's knees

Etymology

From Old English *tūn* (enclosure, tunic), related to Old Norse *tún* (enclosure, yard).
