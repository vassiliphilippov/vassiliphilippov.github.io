euphuism

1. NOUN

/ ˈjuː.fju.ˌɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈjuː.fjʊ.ɪ.zəm / (BrE)
A highly ornate and elaborate style of prose characteristic of the Elizabethan period, marked by balance, antithesis, alliteration, and extended similes with frequent allusions to nature and mythology.
elaborate Elizabethan prose style

Examples

The writer adopted a style of euphuism, delighting in ornate language and elaborate metaphors.
Many critics found the euphuism of his prose excessive and artificial.
In Elizabethan England, euphuism was admired by some and mocked by others for its affected elegance.

Etymology

From the title of John Lyly's prose romance 'Euphues' (1578), whose style it exemplifies.

2. NOUN

/ ˈjuːfjuˌɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈjuːfjʊˌɪzəm / (BrE)
A literary style marked by elaborate balance, extreme elegance, and artificiality of language, often characterized by excessive use of antithesis, alliteration, and similes.
artificially elaborate literary style

Examples

The author's reliance on euphuism made the story ornate but difficult to read.
Critics accused the poet of using unnecessary euphuism in his latest collection.
While some admire euphuism for its artistry, others find it tedious and artificial.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grandiloquence - a lofty, pompous, or extravagant manner of speaking
  • Antonym: plainness - the quality of being simple or unadorned in style

Etymology

From the title of the 16th-century novel 'Euphues' by John Lyly, which popularized the style.
