to impound

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈpaʊnd / (AmE), / ɪmˈpaʊnd / (BrE)
to take possession of something, especially by legal authority, often temporarily and as security or evidence
to take possession by legal authority

Examples

The authorities decided to impound his car after he failed to provide proper registration.
Customs officials may impound goods suspected of being smuggled.
The animal shelter has the power to impound stray dogs found in public areas.

Synset Examples

The FBI seized the drugs
The customs agents impounded the illegal shipment
The police confiscated the stolen artwork

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take hold of something suddenly and forcibly, often by legal authority
  • Synonym: confiscate - to take away something, especially by authority
  • Antonym: release - to set something free, to allow to leave impoundment

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *empounder*, from Old French *emponder* (to put into a pound, an enclosure).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈpaʊnd / (AmE), / ɪmˈpaʊnd / (BrE)
to confine or seize and keep in an enclosure, especially by legal authority or for safekeeping
confine or seize and keep in enclosure

Examples

The police had to impound his car after the accident.
Farmers sometimes impound stray cattle to prevent damage to crops.
During the investigation, authorities impounded several documents as evidence.

Synset Examples

pound the cows so they don't stray

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to confine - to keep or restrict someone or something within certain limits
  • Antonym: release - to set free from confinement

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *enpounder*, from 'in' + 'pound' (enclosure).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01304843-v
Synset POS: v
