mansard

1. NOUN

/ ˈmæn.sɑrd / (AmE), / ˈmæn.sɑːd / (BrE)
A roof with two slopes on each of its four sides, the lower slope being steeper than the upper.
type of roof with two slopes

Examples

The old hotel was topped with a distinctive mansard that gave it an elegant French appearance.
Architects often use a mansard to add a full attic or extra living space.
The city regulations permitted new constructions with a mansard to maintain historical charm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gambrel - a type of roof with two slopes on each side, most commonly seen on barns

Etymology

From French Mansart, after the architect François Mansart (1598–1666), who popularized the style.

2. ADJ

/ ˈmæn.sɑrd / (AmE), / ˈmæn.sɑːd / (BrE)
(of a roof) having two slopes on all sides, with the lower slope steeper than the upper, typically creating additional living space.
roof with double slopes on all sides

Examples

The house featured a mansard roof that added a stylish flair to its design.
Many Parisian buildings have mansard roofs with dormer windows extending from the upper level.
The architect recommended a mansard roof to maximize space in the attic.

Synset Examples

the storey formed by a mansard roof is usually called the garret

Etymology

From French mansarde, named after the French architect François Mansart (1598–1666).
