interrupt

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪntəˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈrʌpt / (BrE)
in computing, a signal that temporarily stops the execution of a program or process so that another procedure can be carried out
computing: temporary signal stopping a program

Examples

The CPU received an interrupt that caused it to pause the current task.
When a key is pressed, it generates an interrupt to alert the operating system.
Device drivers often respond to hardware interrupts that signal an event has occurred.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: signal - electronic communication alerting or triggering an action, especially in computing
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11467628-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˌɪn.təˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈrʌpt / (BrE)
to stop someone from continuing what they are saying or doing by saying or doing something yourself; to break the continuity, peace, or concentration of something
to stop or disturb the continuity of something

Examples

Please do not interrupt the speaker during the presentation.
A loud noise suddenly interrupted the peaceful evening.
Her phone call interrupted my train of thought while studying.

Synset Examples

Don't interrupt me when I'm reading

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disturb - to interrupt the normal condition or activity of something
  • Synonym: to disrupt - to interrupt by causing disorder or confusion
  • Antonym: to continue - to go on without stopping or being disturbed

Etymology

From Latin *interruptus*, past participle of *interrumpere* (to break apart).

Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ ˌɪn.təˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ˌɪn.təˈrʌpt / (BrE)
to bring something to an end or terminate it, often before its expected or natural conclusion
bring to an end; terminate

Examples

The government hopes to interrupt the spread of disease through early vaccination programs.
She decided to interrupt her studies to travel the world for a year.
New policies were introduced to interrupt the cycle of poverty in the region.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to continue - persist in an activity or process

Etymology
