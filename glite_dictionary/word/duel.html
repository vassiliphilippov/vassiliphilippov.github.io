duel

1. NOUN

/ ˈduːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
a contest or struggle between two skillful opponents, whether individuals or groups
contest between two opponents

Examples

The two chess grandmasters engaged in a fierce duel that lasted several hours.
The election campaign turned into a bitter duel between the leading candidates.
The teams' duel for the championship drew nationwide attention.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: battle - a sustained fight between opposing forces
  • Synonym: competition - a contest between rivals
  • Antonym: truce - an agreement to stop fighting

Etymology

From Latin 'duellum' (war, combat between two), also related to 'duo' (two).

2. NOUN

/ ˈduːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
a formal fight between two people, often with weapons, arranged in advance to settle a dispute or point of honor
formal fight to settle a dispute

Examples

The two generals agreed to settle their differences in a duel at dawn.
Challenging someone to a duel was once considered an honorable way to resolve disputes.
The outcome of the duel changed the course of their families’ histories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: duelist - a person who takes part in a duel
  • Synonym: combat - a fight, especially during a war
  • Degree Lower: contest - a struggle or fight to win something

Etymology

From Latin 'duellum' (war, combat), an older form of 'bellum' (war).

Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ ˈduːəl / (AmE), / ˈdjuːəl / (BrE)
to fight a formal combat with weapons, especially with another person, typically over a matter of honor
fight a formal combat with weapons

Examples

In the 19th century, gentlemen would sometimes duel to defend their honor.
The officers agreed to duel at dawn in accordance with the code.
Two rivals dueled over a perceived insult, attracting a crowd of anxious onlookers.

Synset Examples

In the 19th century, men often dueled over small matters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fight - to take part in a physical struggle, battle, or combat
  • Synonym: battle - to engage in a sustained fight or conflict

Etymology

From Latin 'duellum', an old form of 'bellum' meaning 'war'.
