must

1. ADJ

/ mʌst / (AmE), / mʌst / (BrE)
Highly recommended or essential, especially referring to something one should not miss or fail to experience.
highly recommended; essential

Examples

Her new novel is a must for anyone interested in contemporary fiction.
That museum is a must if you are visiting Paris.
The guide lists all the must restaurants in the city.

Synset Examples

a book that is must reading

Related Terms

  • Synonym: essential - extremely important or necessary
  • Antonym: optional - not compulsory or necessary
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00904721-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ mʌst / (AmE), / mʌst / (BrE)
An unpleasant smell or taste that is stale, old, or mouldy.
stale or mouldy smell or taste

Examples

The basement had an unmistakable must that lingered no matter how much she cleaned.
After years in the attic, the books gave off a strong must when opened.
He wrinkled his nose at the must of old bread left in the back of the cupboard.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mustiness - the state of being musty; the quality of smelling or tasting old or stale

Etymology

From Old English *mūst* (new wine), but this sense relates to 'musty.'

3. NOUN

/ mʌst / (AmE), / mʌst / (BrE)
something that is essential or absolutely necessary
something essential or necessary

Examples

A passport is a must if you plan to travel abroad.
For a successful interview, preparation is a must.
Good walking shoes are a must for this hike.

Synset Examples

seat belts are an absolute must

Related Terms

  • Synonym: necessity - something that is necessary or indispensable
  • Antonym: option - something that can be chosen but is not required
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09386867-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ mʌst / (AmE), / mʌst / (BrE)
freshly pressed grape juice, including skins, seeds, and stems, before or during fermentation to make wine
unfermented or fermenting grape juice

Examples

The winemaker carefully sampled the must to monitor its sugar content.
During the harvest, large vats were filled with must ready for fermentation.
In traditional methods, the skins are kept in the must to enhance color and flavor.

Etymology

From Latin mustum (fresh, new wine).
