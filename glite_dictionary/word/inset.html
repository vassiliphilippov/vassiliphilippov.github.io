inset

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌsɛt / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌsɛt / (BrE)
a piece of material sewn into a garment to strengthen or enlarge it
piece of material in a garment

Examples

The tailor added an inset to the dress to make it fit better.
A colorful lace inset decorated the neckline of her blouse.
The jacket’s sleeves have an inset that allows for extra movement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - something inserted or added separately

Etymology

From the verb 'inset', meaning to set in or insert, based on 'set'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03476313-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌsɛt / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌsɛt / (BrE)
an object or piece that is inserted into something else, often as an addition, decoration, or reinforcement
object inserted into something else

Examples

A decorative inset was added to the tabletop to enhance its appearance.
The book features a detailed map as a glossy inset page.
The craftsman carefully fit the wooden inset into the cabinet door.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else; also a noun for something inserted

Etymology

From the verb 'inset', formed from 'in-' + 'set'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03578433-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌsɛt / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌsɛt / (BrE)
a small picture, map, or illustration placed within the borders of a larger one, often to provide additional detail or information
small picture within a larger one

Examples

The atlas included an inset showing a detailed map of the city center.
A small inset in the corner displayed the route in greater detail.
The magazine featured an inset photograph to highlight the architectural details.

Etymology

From the verb 'inset', from 'in-' + 'set' (to place inside).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03578615-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈɪnˌsɛt / (AmE), / ˈɪnˌsɛt / (BrE)
to set or place something into a space or within another object, often so that it fits flush or is embedded.
to set in or embed

Examples

The designer decided to inset a panel of stained glass into the front door.
Shelves are frequently inset into alcoves to save space.
A small screen was inset at the bottom corner of the monitor for additional information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to embed - to fix (an object) firmly and deeply in a surrounding mass
  • Antonym: remove - to take something out from its place

Etymology

From Middle English, past participle of 'insetten' (to set in), from 'in' + 'setten' (to set).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00188798-v
Synset POS: v
