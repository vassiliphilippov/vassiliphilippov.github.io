predictor

1. NOUN

/ prɪˈdɪktər / (AmE), / prɪˈdɪktə / (BrE)
a factor or indicator that is used to estimate or forecast a future event or outcome
something used to estimate the future

Examples

Analysts look for the strongest predictor of economic growth in global markets.
Patient history can be a key predictor of disease risk in medical studies.
Rainfall in the spring is often a reliable predictor of the summer harvest.

Synset Examples

the weekly bulletin contains several predictors of mutual fund performance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indicator - something that indicates or signals a condition or trend

Etymology

From Latin praedictor (foreteller), from praedicere (to foretell).

2. NOUN

/ prɪˈdɪktɚ / (AmE), / prɪˈdɪktə / (BrE)
a person who forecasts future events, often using expert knowledge or data
person who forecasts the future

Examples

As an experienced predictor of economic trends, she is often asked for her opinion on the market.
The weather predictor gave a surprisingly accurate forecast for the entire month.
Many people regard him as a reliable predictor of political outcomes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forecaster - a person who predicts, especially about the weather or other future events

Etymology

From Latin praedictor, from prae- (before) + dicere (to say).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10122275-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ prɪˈdɪktər / (AmE), / prɪˈdɪktə / (BrE)
a device or computer controlling antiaircraft fire that calculates the predicted position of an aircraft at the moment a shell will arrive
device for antiaircraft fire control

Examples

The antiaircraft battery relied on its predictor to calculate where to aim.
Technicians calibrated the predictor before the exercise to ensure accuracy.
Advances in technology led to improved predictor systems for targeting fast-moving planes.

Etymology

From predict + -or, denoting 'one that predicts'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04005225-n
Synset POS: n
