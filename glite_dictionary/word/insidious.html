insidious

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɪdiəs / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɪdiəs / (BrE)
(of something unpleasant or dangerous) proceeding in a gradual, subtle way, but with harmful effects.
harming gradually and secretly

Examples

The effects of the virus were insidious, appearing only months after infection.
Corruption can have an insidious influence on a community's values.
The insidious spread of misinformation can be difficult to detect until it's too late.

Synset Examples

glaucoma is an insidious disease
a subtle poison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: subtle - so delicate or precise as to be difficult to analyze or describe

Etymology

From Latin 'insidiosus' (deceitful), from 'insidiae' (an ambush, plot).

2. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɪdiəs / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɪdiəs / (BrE)
Spreading gradually or subtly in a way that is harmful or deceptive; enticing but dangerous.
subtly harmful or deceptive

Examples

The disease can be insidious, going unnoticed until serious symptoms develop.
She was wary of the insidious influence of certain social media accounts.
The novel explores the insidious effects of unchecked power on individuals.

Synset Examples

insidious pleasures

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deceptive - Giving an impression or appearance different from the true one; misleading
  • Antonym: benign - Gentle and harmless, not causing any harm

Etymology

From Latin insidiosus (deceitful), from insidiae (an ambush, plot).

Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ɪnˈsɪdiəs / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɪdɪəs / (BrE)
intended to entrap or harm in a gradual, subtle, or stealthy way; proceeding in a gradual, subtle way but with harmful effects.
harmful in a subtle or deceptive way

Examples

There was an insidious plot to undermine the company's reputation.
The disease is insidious, spreading unnoticed until it is too late.
She noticed the insidious effects of stress only after several months.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devious - showing a skillful use of underhand tactics
  • Antonym: straightforward - honest and open; not deceitful or ambiguous

Etymology

From Latin insidiosus (deceitful, cunning), from insidiae (ambush, plot).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02067510-s
Synset POS: s
