proactive

1. ADJ

/ proʊˈæktɪv / (AmE), / prəʊˈæktɪv / (BrE)
acting in advance to influence or control future events, especially by causing effects that appear before or lead to later processes or events; anticipating and preventing problems before they arise.
acting in advance to affect future events

Examples

The team took a proactive approach to identifying risks before they became major issues.
Investigators studied the effects of proactive interference on memory recall.
In many jobs, being proactive is valued more highly than simply reacting to problems as they occur.

Synset Examples

proactive inhibition
proactive interference

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reactive - responding to an event rather than initiating it

Etymology

Formed from 'pro-' (before) and 'active,' modeled on 'reactive.'

WordNet ID: oewn-02602462-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ proʊˈæktɪv / (AmE), / prəʊˈæktɪv / (BrE)
(of a person, policy, or action) creating or controlling a situation by causing something to happen rather than just reacting to events after they have happened.
taking action to control situations in advance

Examples

The manager encouraged her team to take a proactive approach to problem-solving.
Being proactive about customer feedback can help prevent complaints from escalating.
He was praised for his proactive response to the rapidly changing market conditions.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: reactive - responding to events rather than initiating them

Etymology

Formed from 'pro-' (before) and 'active', first used in 20th-century English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00039507-s
Synset POS: s
