purport

1. NOUN

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
the intended meaning or sense of something, especially a document or speech; what something claims or appears to mean
intended meaning or sense

Examples

The purport of the letter was unclear, leaving everyone confused about its true message.
Despite the complexity of the legal language, the purport of the document was straightforward.
She spoke passionately, but the purport of her argument was lost on many in the audience.

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French *purport* (meaning, sense), from Latin *proportare* (to bring forth, convey).

2. NOUN

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
the general meaning or substance of something, especially a speech or text
general meaning or substance

Examples

The purport of her remarks was that changes needed to be made.
He did not understand the exact wording, but the general purport was clear enough.
Despite the complex language, the purport of the document was obvious to all.

Synset Examples

caught the general drift of the conversation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drift - general meaning or gist
  • Synonym: gist - the essential or main point

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French purport (intention, meaning), from Latin pro- (forth) + portare (to carry).

3. VERB

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to claim, propose, or intend to do something, often used formally or in legal contexts
to claim or intend (formally)

Examples

The organization purports to provide free legal advice to those in need.
He purports to represent the interests of the local community, but many are skeptical.
In her letter, she purported to resign for personal reasons.

Synset Examples

I aim to arrive at noon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intend - to have as a plan or purpose
  • Synonym: claim - to assert or state as a fact

Etymology

From Latin 'proportare' (to convey, bring forward), via Anglo-Norman 'purporter'.

4. VERB

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to claim to be or to do something, often falsely or with doubtful truth
claim or appear to be (often falsely)

Examples

The plan purports to improve education, but its effectiveness is questionable.
That document purported to be an original, yet experts found it was a forgery.
He purports to have inside information about the company’s finances.

Synset Examples

The letter purports to express people's opinion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: claim - to state or assert that something is the case
  • Synonym: to profess - to declare openly, often falsely
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true

Etymology

From Old French *purporter*, from Latin *proportare* (to convey, to bring forth), from *pro-* (forth) + *portare* (to carry).
