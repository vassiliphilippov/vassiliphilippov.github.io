erosion

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈroʊʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊʒən / (BrE)
The process by which material is gradually worn away, especially by the action of chemical processes.
wearing away by chemical action

Examples

Acid rain can cause the erosion of limestone buildings.
The scientist studied the erosion of metal pipes in chemical plants.
Chemical erosion can weaken infrastructure over time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrosion - gradual destruction by chemical action, especially of metals

Etymology

From Latin 'erosio', from 'erodere' meaning 'to gnaw away'.

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈroʊʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊʒən / (BrE)
The gradual destruction or wearing away of land, rock, or other material, especially by the action of wind, water, or ice; also, the gradual loss of something by repeated action or influence.
gradual wearing away, especially by natural forces

Examples

The relentless flow of the river caused significant erosion along its banks.
Farmers planted trees to help prevent soil erosion caused by heavy rains.
There is growing concern about the erosion of trust in our political system.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weathering - the breaking down of rocks and minerals by natural forces
  • Antonym: deposition - the process by which material is laid down or deposited by wind, water, or ice

Etymology

From Latin 'erosio' (a gnawing away), from 'erodere' (to gnaw away).

4. NOUN

/ ɪˈroʊʒən / (AmE), / ɪˈrəʊʒən / (BrE)
The gradual wearing away and removal of the earth's surface by natural forces such as water, wind, or ice.
wearing away of earth by natural forces

Examples

Severe erosion of the riverbank threatened nearby houses after the heavy rains.
Farmers planted trees to help prevent soil erosion caused by wind and water.
Coastal erosion has reduced the size of the beach over the past decade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weathering - the breaking down of rocks and minerals by natural forces

Etymology

From Latin erosio (a gnawing away), from erodere (to gnaw away).
