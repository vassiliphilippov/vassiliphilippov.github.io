juggle

1. VERB

/ ˈdʒʌɡəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌɡl / (BrE)
to try to manage or deal with several activities, tasks, or responsibilities at the same time
manage multiple tasks at once

Examples

She found it challenging to juggle her career, family, and social life.
He often has to juggle several projects at the same time at work.
As a student, I learned to juggle classes, assignments, and part-time jobs.

Synset Examples

She had to juggle her job and her children

Related Terms

  • Synonym: multitask - to do more than one thing simultaneously
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02443248-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒʌɡəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌɡl / (BrE)
to throw and keep several objects in the air by catching and throwing them repeatedly, usually for entertainment
throw and keep objects in the air simultaneously

Examples

She learned to juggle three balls at the same time during the circus camp.
He amazed the crowd by juggling flaming torches with ease.
It takes hours of practice to juggle five clubs without dropping any.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toss - to throw something lightly or casually
  • Antonym: drop - to let something fall

Etymology

From Old French *jogler* (to entertain by performing tricks), from Latin *joculare* (to jest).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01607735-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈdʒʌɡəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒʌɡl / (BrE)
an act of manipulating or rearranging things in order to deceive or create a misleading impression
deceptive manipulation or rearrangement

Examples

The company's financial juggle was eventually uncovered by auditors.
Political campaigns often involve a juggle of promises and facts.
Through a clever juggle of schedules, she made it appear as though she had more free time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manipulation - the act of controlling or managing something in a skillful but sometimes dishonest way
  • Synonym: fraud - wrongful or criminal deception for personal gain
