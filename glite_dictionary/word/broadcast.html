broadcast

1. NOUN

/ ˈbrɔdˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdˌkɑːst / (BrE)
a radio or television program that is transmitted to the public
radio or TV program

Examples

The live broadcast of the game attracted millions of viewers worldwide.
She appeared on a morning broadcast to discuss her new book.
Last night's broadcast was interrupted due to technical difficulties.

Synset Examples

did you see his program last night?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: program - a planned radio or television item or show
  • Antonym: narrowcast - transmission of information to a specific audience

Etymology

From broad + cast; originally referred to scattering seeds widely, later applied to radio and television transmissions.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈbrɔdˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdkɑːst / (BrE)
to send out a program by radio or television for public reception
transmit by radio or television

Examples

The game will be broadcast live on national television tonight.
The station broadcasts news updates every hour throughout the day.
They broadcast the emergency warning to all local radio channels.

Synset Examples

We cannot air this X-rated song

Related Terms

  • Synonym: air - to broadcast a program on radio or television
  • Synonym: to transmit - to send out (signals, etc.) electronically

Etymology

Originally agricultural, from 'broad' + 'cast' referring to scattering seeds; adapted to radio c. 20th century.

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈbrɔdˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdˌkɑːst / (BrE)
a radio or television programme or transmission intended for public reception
radio or television transmission

Examples

The president addressed the nation in a special broadcast last night.
Millions tuned in to watch the live broadcast of the awards ceremony.
After the broadcast, viewers called in to share their opinions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transmission - a programme or signal that is broadcast
  • Antonym: narrowcast - a programme sent to a specific, limited audience

Etymology

From 'broadcast' (verb), originally meaning 'to scatter seed widely', later applied to radio and television transmissions.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06264137-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈbrɔdˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdˌkɑːst / (BrE)
to sow seeds by scattering them widely over the surface of the ground, especially by hand
sow seeds by scattering widely

Examples

Farmers used to broadcast seeds across the fields in early spring.
She broadcast grass seed by hand over the lawn to ensure even coverage.
The traditional method involves broadcasting wheat seed before lightly raking the soil.

Synset Examples

broadcast seeds

Related Terms

  • Antonym: drill - to sow seeds by placing them in rows or small holes

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'broad cast' (wide scattering), in use since the 18th century for sowing.

5. VERB

/ ˈbrɔdˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈbrɔːdˌkɑːst / (BrE)
to make information widely known; to spread news or ideas publicly or to many people
make information widely known

Examples

The organization broadcast a warning to all its members about the scam.
They broadcast news of their engagement to friends and family.
Rumors about the politician were quickly broadcast on social media.

Synset Examples

spread information
circulate a rumor
broadcast the news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spread - to extend or distribute something over a wide area or among many people
  • Synonym: to circulate - to pass from person to person or place to place, especially information or rumors
  • Antonym: to conceal - to keep something secret or hidden

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'broad cast', meaning to scatter seeds widely (19th century); later extended to spreading information or signals.
