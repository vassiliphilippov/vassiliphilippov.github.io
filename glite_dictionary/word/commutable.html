commutable

1. ADJ

/ kəˈmjutəbəl / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːtəbəl / (BrE)
(of a punishment or penalty) able to be changed to something less severe.
able to be changed, especially to something less severe

Examples

Under certain conditions, a death sentence may be commutable to life in prison.
The penalty is commutable at the discretion of the judge.
Her sentence was found to be commutable upon further review by the court.

Synset Examples

the death sentence was commutable to life imprisonment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: convertible - able to be changed into a different form or use

Etymology

From Latin 'commutare' (to change, exchange), via Late Latin 'commutabilis'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00349300-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ kəˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːtəbl̩ / (BrE)
capable of being exchanged, substituted, or converted for another thing of equal value or effect.
able to be exchanged or substituted

Examples

Certain bonds have commutable benefits that can be converted into cash.
His sentence was commutable to a fine instead of imprisonment.
Vacation time is not always commutable for extra pay in some companies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exchangeable - able to be replaced with something else of equivalent value
  • Synonym: interchangeable - able to be swapped without making a difference

Etymology

From commute + -able, following Latin commutabilis (changeable, exchangeable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00920882-s
Synset POS: s
