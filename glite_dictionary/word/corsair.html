corsair

1. NOUN

/ kɔːrˈsɛr / (AmE), / kɔːˈsɛə / (BrE)
a pirate, especially one operating along the Barbary Coast of North Africa during the 16th to 19th centuries
pirate of the Barbary Coast

Examples

The feared corsair swept the Mediterranean, plundering merchant ships for treasure.
Many European navies tried to defend their ships from corsairs during the 17th century.
He boasted that his ancestor had once served as a notorious corsair off the coast of Tunis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pirate - a person who attacks and robs ships at sea

Etymology

From Old French 'corsaire', from Provençal 'corsaïre', probably from Medieval Latin 'cursarius' (privateer), from Latin 'cursus' (course).

2. NOUN

/ kɔrˈsɛr / (AmE), / kɔːˈseə / (BrE)
a fast pirate ship, especially one operating with official sanction in the Mediterranean during the 16th–18th centuries
fast pirate ship, often officially sanctioned

Examples

The infamous corsair terrorized merchant vessels along the Barbary Coast.
The ship's design was inspired by the legendary corsairs of the Mediterranean.
A corsair fleet, flying official colors, attacked the enemy port at dawn.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: privateer - an armed ship owned and crewed by private individuals holding a government commission to attack enemy ships

Etymology

From French 'corsaire', from Old Provençal 'corsa' (a raid or piracy) and Italian 'corsaro'.
