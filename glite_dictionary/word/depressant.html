depressant

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈprɛsənt / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛsənt / (BrE)
A drug or substance that lowers the activity of the nervous system and reduces excitability or calms a person.
drug reducing nervous system activity
medical

Examples

Alcohol acts as a depressant on the central nervous system.
Some anxiety medications are classified as depressants because they slow brain activity.
Doctors warn that mixing different depressant drugs can be dangerous.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stimulant - a drug that increases nervous system activity

Etymology

From Latin 'deprimere' meaning to press down, via the verb 'depress' + '-ant'.

2. ADJ

/ dɪˈprɛsənt / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛsənt / (BrE)
(of a substance) reducing or suppressing physiological or psychological activity or response.
reducing physiological or psychological activity
medical

Examples

Alcohol is considered a depressant because it slows down brain function and neural activity.
Some medications have a depressant effect on the central nervous system.
Caffeine is a stimulant, but antihistamines often act as a depressant on alertness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stimulant - a substance that raises levels of physiological or nervous activity

Etymology

From Latin 'deprimere' (to press down) via 'depress' + '-ant'.
