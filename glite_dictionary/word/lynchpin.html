lynchpin

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɪn(t)ʃˌpɪn / (AmE), / ˈlɪn(t)ʃ.pɪn / (BrE)
A pin inserted through the end of an axle to keep a wheel in position.
a pin holding a wheel on an axle

Examples

The wagon's wheel almost fell off when the lynchpin broke during the ride.
Before setting out, Tom checked that each lynchpin was securely fastened to the axle.
Without a proper lynchpin, the cart wheel would have slipped off easily.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: linchpin - Alternative spelling of lynchpin, with same meaning

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *linch* (axle-tree), of Old English origin, and *pin*.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɪn(t)ʃˌpɪn / (AmE), / ˈlɪn(t)ʃˌpɪn / (BrE)
A person or thing that is the most important part of a system, organization, or group, keeping it together and functioning.
vital central support or mainstay

Examples

Her insight has made her the lynchpin of the research team.
Strong leadership acted as the lynchpin holding the company together during tough times.
Education is often considered the lynchpin of a thriving society.

Synset Examples

faith is his anchor
the keystone of campaign reform was the ban on soft money
he is the linchpin of this firm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mainstay - a chief support or main element
  • Synonym: cornerstone - an important quality or feature on which a particular thing depends
  • Synonym: anchor - a person or thing providing stability or confidence

Etymology

Alteration of 'linchpin,' from Middle English 'lynspin,' possibly from Old English 'lynis' (axle tree) + 'pin.'
