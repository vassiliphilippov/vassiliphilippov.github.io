mow

1. VERB

/ moʊ / (AmE), / məʊ / (BrE)
to cut down (grass, crops, etc.) with a blade or mower
cut grass or crops with a blade or mower

Examples

Every Saturday, he mows the lawn before noon.
Farmers usually mow the fields in late summer to harvest hay.
The park worker mowed the overgrown grass to prepare for the concert.

Synset Examples

mow the grass

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to divide with a sharp instrument

Etymology

Old English *mawan* (to mow, cut down grass), from Proto-Germanic *mawanan*.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ moʊ / (AmE), / məʊ / (BrE)
a loft or upper compartment in a barn where hay or grain is stored
hayloft in a barn

Examples

The fresh hay was piled high in the mow above the barn.
We spent the afternoon throwing bales into the mow for winter storage.
Swallows darted through the open window of the mow as the sun set.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hayloft - a loft in a barn for storing hay

Etymology

From Old English mūga (heap, pile), related to the idea of a pile of hay or grain.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03505701-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ moʊ / (AmE), / məʊ / (BrE)
(archaic) To pull a sad or pouting face, often thrusting out the lower lip to show displeasure or sulkiness.
(archaic) to make a pouting face
archaic

Examples

The disappointed child began to mow when he did not get his dessert.
She would mow and sulk all afternoon if things didn't go her way.
Stop that mowing—you'll get what you want if you ask politely.

Synset Examples

mop and mow
The girl pouted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pout - to push out one's lips in a show of displeasure or sulkiness
  • Antonym: smile - to make a happy facial expression by curving the lips upward

Etymology

From Middle English *mouen*, related to Middle French *mouer* (to grimace).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00034751-v
Synset POS: v
