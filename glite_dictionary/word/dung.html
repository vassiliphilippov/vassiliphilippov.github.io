dung

1. VERB

/ dʌŋ / (AmE), / dʌŋ / (BrE)
(of animals) to defecate; to discharge excrement.
(of animals) defecate

Examples

The cattle often dung in the same part of the field every day.
Farmers manage pastures to reduce the spread of parasites where animals dung.
When threatened, elephants may dung unexpectedly as a stress response.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to defecate - to discharge feces from the bowels

Etymology

From Old English *dung* (dung, manure), also used as a verb since the 17th century.

2. VERB

/ dʌŋ / (AmE), / dʌŋ / (BrE)
to fertilize or dress soil with animal dung
apply dung as fertilizer

Examples

The farmer decided to dung the fields before planting the new crops.
It is important to dung the vegetable garden annually for better yield.
They dunged the land in spring to improve soil fertility.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manure - to apply manure as fertilizer to soil

Etymology

Old English *dung* (dung, manure); related to Old Norse *dyngja* (heap).

3. NOUN

/ dʌŋ / (AmE), / dʌŋ / (BrE)
the excrement of animals, especially when used as manure for fertilizing land
animal excrement; manure

Examples

The farmer spread cow dung across the field to improve the soil.
Beetles are often found feeding on animal dung in tropical forests.
In some cultures, dried dung is used as a fuel for cooking fires.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: manure - animal dung used for fertilizing land

Etymology

Old English *dung* (manure, fertilizer), related to Old Norse *dyngja* (heap).
