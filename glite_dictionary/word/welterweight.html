welterweight

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɛltɚˌweɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɛltəˌweɪt / (BrE)
A weight of 28 pounds, sometimes used as a handicap assigned to a horse in races such as steeplechases.
a 28-pound handicap weight in horse racing

Examples

The steeplechase required each horse to carry a welterweight as part of the handicap.
Assigning a welterweight to the favorite aimed to level the playing field for other horses.
The jockey was concerned about managing the extra welterweight during the race.

Etymology

From 'welter', an old term for a heavyweight horse, plus 'weight'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɛltərˌweɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɛltəweɪt / (BrE)
A weight classification in boxing and other combat sports for competitors who weigh more than lightweight but less than middleweight, typically not exceeding 147 or 148 pounds (67 kg).
weight class in boxing and combat sports

Examples

The new welterweight champion impressed the crowd with his speed and agility.
She began her career as a welterweight before moving up a weight class.
Several welterweight contenders are hoping for a title shot this year.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: lightweight - A lower weight class below welterweight in boxing and similar sports
  • Degree Higher: middleweight - A higher weight class above welterweight in boxing and similar sports

Etymology

From 'welter', meaning to roll, and 'weight', indicating mass; used for a middle weight class between lightweight and middleweight.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɛltərˌweɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɛltəˌweɪt / (BrE)
a weight classification in wrestling and boxing for competitors who weigh between 154 and 172 pounds (for wrestling) or between 140 and 147 pounds (for boxing)
wrestling/boxing weight class

Examples

He has been competing in the welterweight division this season.
Several famous boxers have held the welterweight title over the years.
After gaining weight, he moved up from welterweight to middleweight.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: middleweight - a weight class just above welterweight in combat sports
  • Degree Lower: lightweight - a weight class just below welterweight in combat sports

Etymology

From 'welter' (possibly from English dialect meaning 'heavyweight horse') + 'weight'.

A weight class in boxing and other combat sports, for competitors heavier than lightweights but lighter than middleweights, typically around 147 pounds (67 kg).
boxing/combat sports weight class

Examples

He is a former welterweight champion who won multiple titles during his career.
The welterweight division is often regarded as one of the most competitive in boxing.
She moved up from lightweight to challenge for the welterweight title.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: lightweight - a lighter weight class below welterweight in boxing
  • Degree Higher: middleweight - a heavier weight class above welterweight in boxing

Etymology

From 'welter', originally referring to a heavyweight horseman, and 'weight'.

5. NOUN

/ ˈwɛltɚˌweɪt / (AmE), / ˈwɛltəˌweɪt / (BrE)
a professional boxer or an athlete in combat sports who weighs between 141 and 147 pounds (64–67 kg), or the weight class itself
boxer/athlete weighing 141–147 lb weight class

Examples

He became the welterweight champion after winning the final bout.
The welterweight division is known for its fast-paced and technical fights.
Several legendary boxers have competed as welterweights throughout history.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: lightweight - a boxer or athlete in a weight class below welterweight (135–140 lb)
  • Degree Higher: middleweight - a boxer or athlete in the weight class above welterweight (154–160 lb)

Etymology

From 'welter' (to roll, toss), a term used in early boxing, and 'weight'; first used in the 19th century for prizefighting classes.
