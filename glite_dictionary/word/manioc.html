manioc

1. NOUN

/ ˈmæniˌɑk / (AmE), / ˈmæniˌɒk / (BrE)
The cassava root, which is eaten as a staple food after processing, and used as a source of tapioca.
cassava root used as food and for tapioca

Examples

Manioc is a major source of carbohydrates for millions of people in tropical regions.
The villagers harvested and processed manioc to make flour and bread.
Tapioca pudding is made from starch extracted from the manioc plant.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cassava - tropical plant with a starchy root, also called manioc

Etymology

From French *manioc*, from Tupi *mani'oka* ('manioc root').

2. NOUN

/ ˈmæniˌɑk / (AmE), / ˈmæniɒk / (BrE)
A tropical plant with long, starchy, tuberous roots, used as food or to make products such as cassiri and tapioca; also known as cassava.
tropical plant with edible starchy roots

Examples

Farmers in the region rely on manioc as one of their main sources of food.
After harvesting, the manioc roots are peeled and processed to make flour.
Traditional dishes often include manioc cooked in a variety of ways.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cassava - tropical plant with starchy tuberous roots; same species as manioc

Etymology

From French 'manioc', from Tupi 'manioka'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɑːniˌɑk / (AmE), / ˈmaniɒk / (BrE)
A starchy substance obtained from the root of the cassava plant, used as a staple food in tropical regions and as the source of tapioca.
starch from cassava root; staple food

Examples

In many African countries, manioc is ground into flour and used to make porridge.
The chef used manioc to create a gluten-free bread for the restaurant.
People in Brazil often prepare manioc by boiling or frying it as a side dish.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cassava - tropical root plant, source of manioc and tapioca
  • Degree Lower: tapioca - starch derived from manioc used as food

Etymology

From Portuguese 'mandioca', from Tupi 'mani' (a legendary figure) + 'oca' (house); via South American indigenous languages.
