to roil

1. VERB

/ rɔɪl / (AmE), / rɔɪl / (BrE)
(of a liquid) to be or make (a liquid) turbulent or agitated; to stir up or disturb.
to stir up or agitate (liquid)

Examples

A strong wind began to roil the surface of the lake, sending waves crashing ashore.
Debris started to roil in the muddy river after the heavy rain.
Tensions between the two countries continued to roil the region for months.

Synset Examples

the sea was churning in the storm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: churn - to move or stir (liquid) violently
  • Antonym: calm - not agitated or disturbed (liquid)

Etymology

Probably from French *rouiller* (to rust), from Old French *roil* (mud, sediment).

2. VERB

/ rɔɪl / (AmE), / rɔɪl / (BrE)
to make a liquid muddy or cloudy by stirring up sediment or dregs
make liquid cloudy by stirring sediment

Examples

Nets dragged through the river roil the water, making it hard to see the fish.
The storm roiled the lake, churning up mud from the bottom.
Strong winds roil the ocean's surface, reducing visibility for divers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to rile - to make water or liquid turbulent or muddy
  • Antonym: to clarify - to make a liquid clear by removing impurities or sediment

Etymology

Probably from Old French *rouiller* (to rust), or related to 'rile' (variant influenced by 'boil').

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
