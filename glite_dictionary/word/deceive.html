to deceive

1. VERB

/ dɪˈsiːv / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːv / (BrE)
to make someone believe something that is not true, typically to gain a personal advantage
cause someone to believe an untruth

Examples

It is wrong to deceive people for personal gain.
He tried to deceive his parents about what happened at school.
The advertisement was designed to deceive customers into buying unnecessary products.

Synset Examples

The insurance company deceived me when they told me they were covering my house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to mislead - to cause someone to have a wrong idea or impression
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive someone as a way of achieving a goal
  • Antonym: honest - free of deceit; truthful and sincere

Etymology

From Old French 'decevoir', from Latin 'decipere' (to ensnare, cheat).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈsiv / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːv / (BrE)
to make someone believe something that is not true, usually in order to gain some personal advantage
to mislead or trick someone

Examples

It is wrong to deceive people for personal gain.
She managed to deceive everyone with her charming smile.
He felt betrayed when he realized his friend had deceived him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to mislead - to give the wrong idea or impression and make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: to betray - to be disloyal or unfaithful to someone
  • Antonym: honest - truthful and free from deceit

Etymology

From Old French *decevoir*, from Latin *decipere* (to ensnare, cheat).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02581157-v
Synset POS: v
