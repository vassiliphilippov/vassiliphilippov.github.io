feminine

1. ADJ

/ ˈfɛmənɪn / (AmE), / ˈfɛmɪnɪn / (BrE)
having qualities or characteristics traditionally associated with women; relating to or typical of women or girls.
having qualities associated with women

Examples

She decorated her room in soft colors to create a feminine atmosphere.
His handwriting is unusually feminine for a man, with elegant loops and curves.
That dress has a very feminine style, with delicate lace and floral patterns.

Synset Examples

feminine intuition

Related Terms

  • Antonym: masculine - having qualities associated with men

Etymology

From Latin 'femininus' (of or pertaining to women), from 'femina' (woman).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɛmənɪn / (AmE), / ˈfɛmɪnɪn / (BrE)
(in grammar) a gender that refers chiefly to females or to nouns typically associated with female characteristics.
grammatical gender for females or female-associated nouns

Examples

In French, the word 'table' is considered feminine.
Some languages have both masculine and feminine nouns, and sometimes even neuter ones.
The teacher explained how to identify the feminine in Spanish nouns.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: masculine - the grammatical gender associated with males or male-associated nouns
  • Antonym: neuter - the grammatical gender for nouns that are neither masculine nor feminine

Etymology

From Latin 'fēminīnus' (female, feminine), from 'fēmina' (woman).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06339989-n
Synset POS: n

4. ADJ

/ ˈfɛmənɪn / (AmE), / ˈfɛmɪnɪn / (BrE)
(of a grammatical gender) belonging to the class of words or forms denoting female beings or grammatical gender typically marked as female.
of female grammatical gender

Examples

In French, the word for table is feminine, so it uses the article 'la'.
German nouns can be masculine, feminine, or neuter.
Many languages assign either masculine or feminine gender to inanimate objects.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: masculine - of grammatical gender typically associated with male beings; opposite of feminine gender
  • Antonym: neuter - of grammatical gender neither masculine nor feminine

Etymology

From Latin 'fēminīnus' (of or belonging to women), from 'fēmina' (woman).
