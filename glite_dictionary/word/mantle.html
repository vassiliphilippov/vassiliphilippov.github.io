mantle

1. NOUN

/ ˈmæntəl / (AmE), / ˈmæn.tl̩ / (BrE)
(Zoology) The soft, protective layer of tissue in mollusks and brachiopods that secretes the substance forming the shell.
protective tissue layer in mollusks
medical

Examples

The clam's mantle secretes calcium carbonate to build its shell.
A snail withdraws its body under the protection of its mantle when threatened.
Scientists studied the structure of the mantle in various species of mollusks.

Etymology

From Latin *mantellum* meaning cloak or covering.

6. NOUN

/ ˈmæntəl / (AmE), / ˈmantl̩ / (BrE)
the thick layer of the Earth between the crust and the core, consisting of hot, solid rock that flows slowly
layer of Earth between crust and core

Examples

Scientists use seismic waves to study the Earth's mantle and learn about its composition.
The mantle makes up about 84 percent of the volume of our planet.
Volcanic eruptions can bring rocks from the mantle to the surface.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: crust - the Earth's outermost solid shell
  • Degree Lower: core - the central, innermost layer of the Earth

Etymology

From Latin 'mantellum' (cloak, covering), referring to something that covers or envelops.
