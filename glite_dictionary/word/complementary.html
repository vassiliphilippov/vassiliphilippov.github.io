complementary

1. ADJ

/ ˌkɑːmpləˈmɛn.tə.ri / (AmE), / ˌkɒmplɪˈmɛn.təri / (BrE)
acting as or providing something that completes or enhances something else
serving to complete or enhance

Examples

These two complementary skills are essential to running a successful business.
Their personalities are complementary, making them an effective team.
The new software is complementary to our existing tools, not a replacement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supplementary - providing an addition or supplement rather than a completion

Etymology

From Latin 'complementum' (that which fills up or completes) + '-ary'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00049236-s
Synset POS: s

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmpləˈmɛnˌtɛri / (AmE), / ˌkɒmplɪˈmɛntəri / (BrE)
either of two colors that, when combined in the right proportions, produce white (for light) or gray (for pigments)
color that forms a neutral pair with another

Examples

On the color wheel, green and red are complementaries often used together in art.
Mixing two complementaries can result in a lifeless gray tone in painting.
Designers often pair complementaries to create high contrast in visual works.

Synset Examples

yellow and blue are complementaries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: complementary color - a color that, paired with another, produces white or gray

Etymology

From Latin 'complementarius', from 'complementum' (that which fills up or completes).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04983006-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˌkɑːmpləˈmɛntəri / (AmE), / ˌkɒmplɪˈmɛntəri / (BrE)
(of words or propositions) so related that each is the negation of the other, together covering all possibilities.
mutually exclusive and exhaustive (linguistics, logic)

Examples

In logic, 'alive' and 'dead' are considered complementary terms.
The words 'true' and 'false' are classic examples of complementary propositions.
In linguistics, a pair of complementary antonyms together exhaust all possible states.

Synset Examples

‘male’ and ‘female’ are complementary terms

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contradictory - mutually opposed or inconsistent (logic, linguistics)

Etymology

From Latin complementarius, from complementum (that which fills up or completes).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02390531-s
Synset POS: s
