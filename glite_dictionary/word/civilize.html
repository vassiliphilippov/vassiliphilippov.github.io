to civilize

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪv.əˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪv.ɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to refine (someone) by teaching them to develop more discriminating tastes or judgment; to cultivate.
refine tastes or judgment

Examples

Travel can civilize a person's taste in food and art.
She hoped to civilize his manners by introducing him to classical music.
The program aims to civilize young people's appreciation for literature.

Synset Examples

Cultivate your musical taste
Train your tastebuds
She is well schooled in poetry

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cultivate - to improve or develop by careful training or education

Etymology

From French *civiliser*, from Latin *civilis* (relating to a citizen).

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪvəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪvəlaɪz / (BrE)
to bring (a person, group, or society) to a stage of social, cultural, and moral development considered to be more advanced; to improve the customs or manners of.
make more cultured or refined

Examples

Efforts were made to civilize the remote tribes through education and exposure to new customs.
The teacher hoped the new rules would help civilize the students' behavior in class.
Many reformers of the past believed that art and literature could civilize the masses.

Synset Examples

The wild child found wandering in the forest was gradually civilized

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge or understanding; to educate
  • Antonym: to barbarize - to make or become less civilized; to become more barbaric

Etymology

From French *civiliser*, from Latin *civilis* (civil).

WordNet ID: oewn-00412237-v
