to eject

1. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to leave an aircraft rapidly by means of an ejection seat or capsule, usually in an emergency
leave an aircraft by ejection seat

Examples

The pilot had to eject when the engine failed over the mountains.
Two crew members ejected safely moments before the fighter jet crashed.
She was trained to eject in emergencies during her years as a test pilot.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bail out - to jump from an aircraft with a parachute

Etymology

From Latin ejectus, past participle of ejicere (to throw out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02081246-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to cause a liquid or substance to come out forcefully in a sudden stream, especially in a squirt
force out liquid or substance

Examples

The clown's flower ejected water into the startled audience.
Some animals eject ink or venom as a defense mechanism.
The malfunctioning pump ejected a stream of oil across the floor.

Synset Examples

the boy squirted water at his little sister

Related Terms

  • Synonym: squirt - to force a liquid out through a narrow opening

Etymology

From Latin *ejectus*, past participle of *eicere* (to cast out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01378302-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to force someone or something to leave a place; to expel
to force out or expel

Examples

Security was called to eject the disruptive fan from the stadium.
The airline ejected the passenger for refusing to obey the rules.
The manager threatened to eject customers who were behaving aggressively.

Synset Examples

The unruly student was excluded from the game

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to expel - to force someone to leave a place
  • Antonym: to admit - to allow entry into a place

Etymology

From Latin 'ejectus', past participle of 'eicere' (to throw out), from 'ex-' (out) + 'iacere' (to throw).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01471205-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to force a substance out from something; to expel or discharge
expel or discharge a substance

Examples

Certain air fresheners eject fragrance into the air at set intervals.
The printer will eject excess ink during its cleaning cycle.
Some plants eject seeds forcefully to aid in dispersal.

Synset Examples

combustion products are exhausted in the engine
the plant releases a gas

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to emit - to send out (a substance, typically gas or light)
  • Antonym: to absorb - to take in (a substance)

Etymology

From Latin 'ejectus', past participle of 'eicere' meaning 'to throw out'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00104622-v
Synset POS: v
