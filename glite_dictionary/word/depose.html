to depose

1. VERB

/ dɪˈpoʊz / (AmE), / dɪˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to give evidence or make a formal statement under oath, especially in a legal context
give sworn testimony
legal

Examples

The witness was required to depose before the grand jury regarding the events she had witnessed.
Several experts deposed under oath about the safety standards of the company.
After deposing in the case, he was advised not to discuss his testimony.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to testify - give evidence as a witness in a law court
  • Synonym: to swear - declare or affirm solemnly, especially under oath

Etymology

From Old French *deposer* (to put down, lay aside) and Late Latin *deponere* (lay down).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈpoʊz / (AmE), / dɪˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to remove someone forcibly from office or a position of power
remove from office by force

Examples

The military sought to depose the corrupt leader and establish a new government.
After months of protests, the president was deposed from office.
Historians studied the factors that led to the king being deposed by his rivals.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dethrone - to remove from a throne or position of authority
  • Synonym: to dismiss - to remove someone from their job or position
  • Antonym: to appoint - to assign someone to a position or office

Etymology

From Old French deposer, from Latin deponere (to lay aside).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02410394-v
Synset POS: v
