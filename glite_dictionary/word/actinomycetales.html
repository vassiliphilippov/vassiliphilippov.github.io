Actinomycetales

1. NOUN

/ ˌæk.tɪ.noʊ.maɪˈsiː.təˌliz / (AmE), / ˌæk.tɪ.nəʊ.maɪˈsiː.təˌliːz / (BrE)
an order of filamentous or rod-shaped bacteria, many of which are found in soil and some of which can cause diseases in plants and animals
order of filamentous or rod-shaped bacteria
medical

Examples

Several species within the Actinomycetales order are important producers of antibiotics.
The soil was found to contain a high population of Actinomycetales, which aid in decomposition.
Some Actinomycetales can cause infections in humans and animals, particularly if the immune system is compromised.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining 'actino-' (ray) + 'mycete' (fungus) + '-ales' (order).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæktɪnoʊˌmaɪsəˈtiːliːz / (AmE), / ˌæktɪnəʊˌmaɪsɪˈtiːliːz / (BrE)
an order of filamentous or rod-shaped bacteria, many of which are found in soil and are important in decomposition and antibiotic production
an order of filamentous bacteria
medical

Examples

Many species within the Actinomycetales play a crucial role in decomposing organic matter in soil.
Several antibiotics, such as streptomycin, are produced by bacteria belonging to Actinomycetales.
Scientists are studying the diversity of Actinomycetales to discover new bioactive compounds.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining 'actino-' (ray) and 'mycetes' (fungi), referring to the radiating form and resemblance to fungi.
