transitivity

1. NOUN

/ træn.sɪˈtɪv.ə.ti / (AmE), / træn.zɪˈtɪv.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
(Logic, mathematics) The property of a relation such that if it holds between a first and second element, and also between the second and third, then it must hold between the first and third.
property of certain relations (math/logic)

Examples

The transitivity of equality means that if a = b and b = c, then a = c.
In mathematics, transitivity is a fundamental property of certain relations like order and equivalence.
Understanding transitivity is essential when analyzing logical arguments and proofs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reflexivity - property of a relation where every element is related to itself
  • Synonym: symmetry - property of a relation where if it holds from A to B, it also holds from B to A

Etymology

From Latin transitivus, from transire (to go across).

2. NOUN

/ ˌtræn.sɪˈtɪv.ə.ti / (AmE), / ˌtræn.zɪˈtɪv.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the property of a verb that determines whether it can take a direct object, distinguishing between transitive and intransitive verbs
property of a verb relating to its objects

Examples

In English, the transitivity of a verb affects which objects can appear in a sentence.
Linguists study transitivity to analyze sentence structure and verb usage across languages.
A change in transitivity can alter the meaning of a verb phrase significantly.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intransitivity - the property of a verb not taking a direct object

Etymology

From Latin transitivus (transitive), from transire (to go across).
