bivalve

1. ADJ

/ ˈbaɪˌvælv / (AmE), / ˈbaɪˌvælv / (BrE)
(of a mollusk) having a shell consisting of two hinged parts or valves, as in clams, oysters, and mussels.
having a two-part shell (of mollusks)

Examples

The oyster is a classic example of a bivalve animal.
Most edible shellfish in this region are bivalve mollusks.
Scientists found several bivalve fossils in the ancient lake bed.

Etymology

From Latin 'bivalvis', from 'bi-' (two) + 'valvae' (leaves of a door).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbaɪˌvælv / (AmE), / ˈbaɪˌvælv / (BrE)
a marine or freshwater mollusk with a soft body enclosed within two hinged shells, such as a clam, oyster, or mussel
mollusk with two hinged shells

Examples

Oysters and clams are both types of bivalve found along the coast.
The scientist collected several bivalves to study their shells and feeding habits.
Many bivalve species are important sources of food around the world.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mollusk - invertebrate with soft, unsegmented body, often with a shell

Etymology

From Latin 'bivalvis', from 'bi-' (two) + 'valvae' (leaves of a door or folding door).
