ridicule

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɪdɪˌkjul / (AmE), / ˈrɪdɪkjuːl / (BrE)
the act of making someone or something the object of scornful laughter or mockery
mockery; scornful laughter

Examples

He became the subject of ridicule after his failed attempt at the performance.
Her ideas were once met with ridicule, but now they are widely accepted.
The politician responded to the ridicule with calm and dignity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mockery - the act of mocking or making fun of someone or something
  • Antonym: praise - expression of approval or admiration

Etymology

From Latin 'ridiculus' (laughable), from 'ridere' (to laugh).

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɪdɪˌkjuːl / (AmE), / ˈrɪdɪˌkjuːl / (BrE)
the act of making someone or something the subject of mockery or scorn, especially with the intention to humiliate
mockery or scorn to humiliate

Examples

His speech was met with ridicule from the audience.
She became the target of ridicule after the story went public.
Fear of ridicule stopped him from sharing his ideas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mockery - the act of mocking or making fun of someone or something
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling or act of admiration or regard, opposite of ridicule

Etymology

From Latin ridiculum (joke, jest), from ridiculus (laughable), from ridere (to laugh).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06728725-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈrɪdɪˌkjul / (AmE), / ˈrɪdɪkjuːl / (BrE)
to mock or make fun of someone or something in a contemptuous way
to mock or make fun of

Examples

It is unfair to ridicule someone for their accent or background.
The comedian ridiculed politicians during his stand-up show last night.
Many critics ridiculed the artist's latest work, calling it childish.

Synset Examples

The satirists ridiculed the plans for a new opera house
The students poked fun at the inexperienced teacher
His former students roasted the professor at his 60th birthday

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to make fun of someone in a cruel way
  • Synonym: to deride - to express contempt for; ridicule
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration of someone or something

Etymology

From French ridiculer, from Latin ridiculum (a joke), from ridere (to laugh).
