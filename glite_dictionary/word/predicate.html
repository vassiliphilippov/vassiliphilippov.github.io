predicate

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɛdɪkət / (BrE)
(logic) The part of a proposition or sentence that asserts something about the subject, typically following the subject and the copula.
term asserted about a sentence's subject (logic)

Examples

In the sentence 'All dogs are mammals,' 'are mammals' is the predicate.
The predicate often tells us something about the subject's state or action.
Logic students learn to identify the predicate in different types of propositions.

Synset Examples

‘Socrates is a man’ predicates manhood of Socrates

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subject - The part of a proposition about which something is stated

Etymology

From Latin praedicatum (something declared), from praedicare (to proclaim).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɛdɪkət / (BrE)
The part of a sentence or clause containing a verb and stating something about the subject, typically including the verb and its complements or objects.
part of a sentence containing the verb

Examples

In the sentence 'The dog barked loudly,' the predicate is 'barked loudly.'
Every complete sentence must contain both a subject and a predicate.
Teachers often underline the predicate to help students identify the sentence's main action.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subject - the part of a sentence about which something is said

Etymology

From Latin praedicatum (that which is said of the subject), from praedicare (to proclaim, assert).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06327619-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈpriːdɪˌkeɪt / (BrE)
to affirm or declare something as an attribute or quality of a subject
declare an attribute or quality

Examples

The expert's report predicated the claimant's success on the strength of the new evidence.
Ancient philosophers often predicated rationality as the chief virtue of mankind.
The teacher predicates her grading system on students' mastery of the coursework.

Synset Examples

The speech predicated the fitness of the candidate to be President

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assert - to state or declare positively and often forcefully
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true

Etymology

From Latin praedicare (to proclaim, affirm), from prae (before) + dicare (to declare).

4. VERB

/ ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (BrE)
(formal) to state or affirm something as a basis or condition for something else; to assert something as necessary for a consequence.
state as a condition or basis

Examples

Their plan was predicated on the cooperation of all team members.
The judge’s decision is predicated upon established legal precedent.
Success in this field is often predicated on years of hard work and dedication.

Synset Examples

solving the problem is predicated on understanding it well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: base - to use something as a foundation or support for something else
  • Synonym: to assume - to suppose to be true, especially as a basis for reasoning or action

Etymology

From Latin praedicare (to proclaim, declare).

5. VERB

/ ˈprɛdɪˌkeɪt / (AmE), / ˈprɛdɪkeɪt / (BrE)
(in grammar and logic) to assert or state (a property or quality) of a subject in a proposition.
to state a property of a subject (grammar/logic)

Examples

In the sentence 'All roses are flowers,' the word 'flowers' is predicated of 'roses.'
Many philosophers predicate existence as a fundamental property of certain objects.
The professor explained how the term 'mortal' is predicated of humans in classic logical propositions.

Synset Examples

The predicate ‘dog’ is predicated of the subject ‘Fido’ in the sentence ‘Fido is a dog’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to affirm - to state something as a fact
  • Antonym: to deny - to declare something is not true, to refuse to accept

Etymology

From Latin praedicare (to proclaim, assert).
