to syncopate

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋ.kəˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋ.kə.pɛɪt / (BrE)
to modify the rhythm of music by emphasizing beats or accents that are normally weak or unstressed
accent a weak beat in music

Examples

Jazz musicians often syncopate the rhythm to create a lively and unexpected feel.
The composer decided to syncopate the melody, giving the song a unique groove.
If you syncopate the beat, the music becomes much more interesting to dance to.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: syncopation - a shifting of the usual accent in music to a normally weak beat

Etymology

From Medieval Latin syncopare, from Greek synkoptein (to cut short).

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋ.kəˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋ.kə.peɪt / (BrE)
to omit a sound or letter from the middle of a word
omit sound or letter in a word

Examples

It is common to syncopate words in casual speech, such as 'library' pronounced as 'libry'.
Some dialects syncopate certain vowels, making the words shorter and easier to say.
Linguists may study which syllables are typically syncopated in rapid conversation.

Synset Examples

syncopate a word

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to elide - to omit a sound or syllable when speaking

Etymology

From Medieval Latin syncopatus, past participle of syncopare, from Greek synkopē (contraction, cutting up).
