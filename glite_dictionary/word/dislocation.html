dislocation

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɪs.loʊˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪs.ləʊˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
an injury in which a bone or joint is forced out of its normal position
bone or joint forced out of position
medical

Examples

The X-ray confirmed that she had a dislocation of her left shoulder.
Prompt treatment is important to reduce the risk of permanent damage after a dislocation.
He suffered a dislocation during the match and had to leave the field.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fracture - the breaking of a bone

Etymology

From Latin dislocatio (removal from a place), from dis- (apart) + locare (to place).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɪs.loʊˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪs.ləʊˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a disruption or displacement causing a break in continuity, especially in a structure, system, or physical state
a disruption or displacement; a break in continuity

Examples

The earthquake caused a significant dislocation along the fault line.
Rapid technological change can lead to the dislocation of entire industries.
After the accident, there was a visible dislocation in the structure of the bridge.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: displacement - the action of moving something from its place or position
  • Antonym: continuity - the unbroken and consistent existence of something over a period of time

Etymology

From Latin dislocatio (displacement), from dislocare (to displace).

3. NOUN

/ ˌdɪsloʊˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɪsləʊˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
a disturbance or interruption of the usual arrangement or functioning of something, especially a system or society.
disruption of an established order

Examples

The war caused a major dislocation in the nation's economic system.
Rapid technological changes can lead to dislocation in traditional industries.
The abrupt closure of the factory created social dislocation in the community.

Synset Examples

the social dislocations resulting from government policies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disruption - a disturbance that interrupts an event, activity, or process
  • Antonym: stability - the state of being steady or unchanging

Etymology

From Latin dislocare (to displace).
