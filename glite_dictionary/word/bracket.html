bracket

1. NOUN

/ ˈbræk.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈbræk.ɪt / (BrE)
either of two angle-shaped punctuation marks (‘<’ or ‘>’) used especially in computer programming or to enclose text
< or > punctuation mark

Examples

The command must be enclosed in brackets to execute properly.
In HTML, tags are written inside angle brackets.
Place the optional argument in brackets if it is not required.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: angle bracket - < or > punctuation mark used in mathematics and computing

Etymology

From Old French 'braguette', diminutive of 'brague' (codpiece or armor part), later applied to supports and then to enclosing marks.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbræk.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈbræk.ɪt / (BrE)
A support that projects from a wall and is used to bear the weight of a shelf or similar object.
wall support for a shelf

Examples

The new shelf was held securely by a metal bracket screwed into the wall.
He chose ornate brackets to support the wooden mantel above the fireplace.
A loose bracket can cause the entire shelf to fall.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shelf - a flat board for storage, often held by brackets

Etymology

From Old French 'braguette' (codpiece, bracket), diminutive of 'brague' (breeches), related to architectural supports.

4. NOUN

/ ˈbrækət / (AmE), / ˈbrækɪt / (BrE)
either of the pair of punctuation marks [ ] used to enclose words or figures, separating them from the rest of the text
punctuation mark [ ] used to enclose text

Examples

Please put the citation inside brackets at the end of the sentence.
You should not use square brackets unless you are inserting words.
The instructions in brackets are not meant to be read aloud.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parenthesis - punctuation mark ( ) used to enclose additional material
  • Degree Higher: brace - punctuation mark { } used to enclose text or groups
  • Degree Higher: angle bracket - punctuation mark < > used to enclose material

Etymology

From Middle French 'braguette', diminutive of 'brague' (codpiece), later adopted for various objects resembling supports or frames.

6. NOUN

/ ˈbræk.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈbræk.ɪt / (BrE)
a group or category of people or things whose members share a particular characteristic, especially in terms of income, age, or other measurable limits
a category within defined limits (e.g., income, age)

Examples

Many young professionals fall into the highest bracket for income taxes.
The school's sports teams compete in the same age bracket.
Families in the lower income bracket may qualify for assistance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: category - a class or division of people or things regarded as having particular shared characteristics
  • Synonym: group - a number of people or things that are located, gathered, or classed together

Etymology

From Old French *braguette* (codpiece, clamp), from Vulgar Latin *braca* (trouser), later sense shift to 'category' by association with enclosing or grouping.

7. VERB

/ ˈbræk.ɪt / (AmE), / ˈbræk.ɪt / (BrE)
to classify or group people or things together, especially because they have similar characteristics
classify or group together

Examples

The government bracketed families according to their income levels.
In the study, participants were bracketed based on age and education.
They tend to bracket all jazz musicians together, ignoring the differences between styles.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to categorize - to place in a particular class or group
  • Synonym: to classify - to arrange or order by classes or categories
  • Antonym: separate - to set or keep apart

Etymology

From Old French *braguette* (codpiece, bracket), from Old Provençal *bragueta*; noun sense was extended to grouping by analogy with the physical bracket.
