2. ADJ

/ ˈsɛpɚˌeɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈsɛpərətɪv / (BrE)
(Linguistics) Describing a word that refers singly and without exception to each member of a group.
referring separately to each member (linguistics)

Examples

In the sentence ‘Which of the men arrived?’, the word which is used in a separative sense.
Grammatical analysis often distinguishes between distributive and separative words.
The term separative applies to pronouns and determiners like ‘which’ when they select individuals from a group.

Synset Examples

whereas ‘each’, ‘every’, ‘either’, ‘neither’, and ‘none’ are distributive or referring to a single member of a group, ‘which’ in ‘which of the men’ is separative

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distributive - (of a word) referring to each member of a group separately

Etymology

From Latin separativus, from separare (to separate).

Source: WordNet

3. ADJ

/ ˈsɛpɚˌeɪtɪv / (AmE), / ˈsɛpərətɪv / (BrE)
(of an accent in Hebrew orthography) indicating that a word is marked as separated rhythmically and grammatically from the following word.
denoting separation (esp. in Hebrew orthography)

Examples

The separative accent in the text signals a grammatical break between the two phrases.
Scholars have debated how each separative mark affects the interpretation of the Hebrew sentence.
A separative accent tends to occur at important syntactic boundaries in biblical Hebrew.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disjunctive - (in linguistics) indicating separation or disjunction, often of accents or words

Etymology

From Latin separativus, from separare (to separate).
