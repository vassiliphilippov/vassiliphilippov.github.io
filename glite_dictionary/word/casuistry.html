casuistry

1. NOUN

/ ˈkæʒuˌɪstri / (AmE), / ˈkæʒuːɪstri / (BrE)
the use of theoretical ethical principles to resolve specific moral problems or dilemmas, especially in a methodical or systematic way
the application of ethical principles to resolve moral dilemmas

Examples

The professor explained how casuistry was used to address complex moral questions in history.
Some critics accuse him of using casuistry to justify questionable actions.
In medical ethics, casuistry helps practitioners make decisions in difficult cases.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ethics - the study of moral principles that govern a person's behavior
  • Synonym: moralism - the practice of judging or applying moral principles

Etymology

From French 'casuistique', from Latin 'casus' (case, occurrence) + '-istry'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkæʒuˌɪstri / (AmE), / ˈkazjuːɪstri / (BrE)
the use of clever but fallacious reasoning, especially in relation to moral questions; argumentation that is misleading or excessively subtle
misleading or overly subtle reasoning

Examples

The politician's response was full of casuistry, avoiding a direct answer to the question.
Legal debates can sometimes descend into casuistry rather than seeking real justice.
She saw through his casuistry and demanded straightforward explanations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sophistry - the use of clever but false arguments, especially with the intention of deceiving
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being truthful and free from deceit

Etymology

From French 'casuisterie', from 'casuiste' (casuist), relating to the analysis of moral cases, ultimately from Latin 'casus' (case).
