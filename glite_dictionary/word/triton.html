Triton

1. PROPN

/ ˈtraɪ.tən / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.tən / (BrE)
the largest moon of the planet Neptune, notable for its retrograde orbit and icy surface
the largest moon of Neptune

Examples

Astronomers discovered geysers of nitrogen erupting from Triton during a Voyager 2 flyby.
The retrograde orbit of Triton suggests it may have been captured by Neptune's gravity.
Scientists believe Triton's icy surface hides a subsurface ocean.

Etymology

From Greek mythology, Triton is the son of Poseidon and Amphitrite; the name was adopted for this moon in astronomy.

2. PROPN

/ ˈtraɪ.tən / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.tən / (BrE)
In Greek mythology, a sea god; the son of Poseidon and Amphitrite, often depicted as having the upper body of a human and the tail of a fish.
sea god in Greek mythology

Examples

According to legend, Triton calmed the seas by blowing his conch shell.
Ancient sculptures often depict Triton with the tail of a fish.
The myth of Triton inspired many artists in Renaissance Europe.

Etymology

From Greek Τρίτων (Trítōn), the name of the mythological sea god.
