to republish

1. VERB

/ riˈpʌblɪʃ / (AmE), / riˈpʌblɪʃ / (BrE)
(law) To revive or make effective again a document, such as a will or libel, that was previously cancelled or invalidated.
make a cancelled legal document effective again
legal

Examples

The court may republish a cancelled will if new evidence is presented.
If a document is mistakenly revoked, a judge can republish it under certain conditions.
His attorney filed a motion to republish the libel after the original was set aside.

Etymology

From re- (again) + publish, from Latin 'publicare' (to make public).

WordNet ID: oewn-00168984-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ riˈpʌblɪʃ / (AmE), / riˈpʌblɪʃ / (BrE)
to publish (a work or information) again, especially after revision or in a different form or edition
publish again

Examples

The magazine decided to republish the article in its anniversary issue.
After new evidence was discovered, the researchers had to republish their findings.
The novel was so popular that the publisher chose to republish it with additional content.

Synset Examples

The scientist republished his results after he made some corrections

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to publish - to make a document or work available to the public

Etymology

From re- (again) + publish, after Latin roots.
