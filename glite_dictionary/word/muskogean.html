Muskogean

1. NOUN

/ mʌsˈkoʊɡiən / (AmE), / mʌsˈkəʊɡiən / (BrE)
a member of any of the indigenous peoples formerly living in the southeastern United States and speaking Muskogean languages
member of a southeastern US indigenous group

Examples

The Muskogean were known for their mound-building cultures and complex societies.
Several Muskogean tribes still maintain cultural traditions in the southeastern United States.
Archaeologists have studied the ancient settlements of Muskogean peoples for evidence of their way of life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Muskogee - a specific Muskogean-speaking indigenous people

Etymology

From the name 'Muskogee,' an indigenous tribe, with the suffix -an.

2. NOUN

/ mʌsˈkoʊɡiən / (AmE), / mʌsˈkəʊɡiən / (BrE)
A family of North American Indigenous languages spoken primarily in the southeastern United States, including Creek, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and others.
language family of southeastern U.S. Native peoples

Examples

The Muskogean languages include Choctaw and Creek, spoken in the southeastern United States.
Researchers studied the structure of the Muskogean language family to compare it with other Native American languages.
Many Muskogean words have found their way into English place names across the Southeast.

Etymology

From Muskoge(e), an Indigenous tribe also known as the Creek, plus English -an (relational suffix).
