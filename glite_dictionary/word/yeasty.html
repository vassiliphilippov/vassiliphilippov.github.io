yeasty

1. ADJ

/ ˈjisti / (AmE), / ˈjiːsti / (BrE)
Full of lively excitement or enthusiasm; marked by spirited enjoyment.
full of lively excitement or enthusiasm

Examples

The party's yeasty atmosphere kept everyone laughing and dancing until dawn.
She brought a yeasty spirit to every gathering, infecting others with her high energy.
The novel captures the yeasty excitement of the city's jazz age.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ebullient - cheerful and full of energy
  • Synonym: boisterous - noisy, energetic, and cheerful

Etymology

From Middle English yesty, from yeast, meaning the frothy or fermenting quality of yeast, later figuratively used for lively enthusiasm.

2. ADJ

/ ˈjisti / (AmE), / ˈjiːsti / (BrE)
Full of restless energy, excitement, or creative ferment.
exuberantly creative or energetic

Examples

The yeasty atmosphere of the new art school encouraged experimentation and innovation.
Her yeasty imagination brought color and excitement to every project she touched.
It's a yeasty period in science, with breakthroughs occurring almost daily.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creative - having the ability to create; imaginative
  • Synonym: inspired - filled with creative or spiritual inspiration
  • Antonym: stagnant - lacking activity, movement, or creativity

Etymology

From 'yeast', by analogy with the rising, fermenting action of yeast.

3. ADJ

/ ˈjisti / (AmE), / ˈjiːsti / (BrE)
Containing, resembling, or characteristic of yeast; often used to describe something frothy, bubbly, or lively.
containing or resembling yeast

Examples

The baker's kitchen was filled with the yeasty aroma of fresh bread.
A yeasty froth appeared on top of the fermenting beer.
The festival had a yeasty energy, with everyone in high spirits.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frothy - full of or covered with a mass of small bubbles
  • Antonym: flat - not effervescent or lively; lacking bubbles or spirit

Etymology

From Middle English 'yesti', from 'yeast'.
