Phrygian

1. NOUN

/ ˈfrɪdʒiən / (AmE), / ˈfrɪdʒiən / (BrE)
a native or inhabitant of Phrygia, an ancient region in west-central Anatolia (modern-day Turkey)
native or inhabitant of ancient Phrygia

Examples

The ancient Phrygian was renowned for his skills as a musician.
Many Phrygians settled in neighboring regions after the decline of their kingdom.
Historians study Phrygian culture to better understand early Anatolian civilizations.

Etymology

From Latin Phrygius (of Phrygia), from Ancient Greek Φρύγιος (Phrúgios), relating to Phrygia.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfrɪdʒiən / (AmE), / ˈfrɪdʒɪən / (BrE)
an extinct Indo-European language once spoken by the ancient people of Phrygia, now known only from a few inscriptions
extinct language of ancient Phrygia

Examples

Scholars study Phrygian as part of the Indo-European language family.
Only a handful of Phrygian inscriptions have survived from ancient Anatolia.
The exact classification of Phrygian remains debated among linguists.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Thracian - extinct Indo-European language spoken in ancient Thrace

Etymology

From Latin 'Phrygius', from Greek 'Phrugios' (of Phrygia), relating to Phrygia, an ancient region in Anatolia.
