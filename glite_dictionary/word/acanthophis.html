Acanthophis

1. NOUN

/ əˈkænθəfɪs / (AmE), / əˈkanθəfɪs / (BrE)
A genus of highly venomous elapid snakes native to Australia and surrounding regions, commonly known as death adders.
genus of venomous Australian snakes

Examples

The Acanthophis genus is responsible for several cases of snakebite in Australia each year.
Researchers studied the habitat preferences of the Acanthophis in the wild.
Despite their appearance, Acanthophis death adders are not true vipers but belong to the elapid family.

Etymology

From Greek akantha (spine, thorn) + ophis (snake).

2. NOUN

/ əˈkænθəˌfɪs / (AmE), / əˈkanθəʊˌfɪs / (BrE)
A genus of Australian venomous snakes of the family Elapidae, commonly known as death adders.
genus of Australian venomous snakes

Examples

The Acanthophis is often referred to as the death adder due to its potent venom.
Researchers studied the habitat of several Acanthophis species in northern Australia.
Unlike many snakes, Acanthophis uses an ambush strategy to hunt its prey.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: death adder - Common name for snakes of the genus Acanthophis

Etymology

From Greek 'akantha' meaning 'thorn' and 'ophis' meaning 'snake'.
