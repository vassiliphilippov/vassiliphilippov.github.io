preemption

1. NOUN

/ priˈɛmpʃən / (AmE), / priˈɛmpʃən / (BrE)
the legal principle that federal law takes precedence over state law when both address the same subject
legal principle: federal law overrides state law
legal

Examples

The doctrine of preemption prevented the state from enforcing its own regulations in that area.
Under preemption, state laws that conflict with federal rules may be rendered invalid.
The court cited the principle of preemption when overturning the state statute.

Etymology

From Latin praeemere (to buy before), through preempt (take action in advance), adapted in U.S. legal context.

2. NOUN

/ priˈɛm(p)ʃən / (AmE), / priˈɛm(p)ʃən / (BrE)
the act of appropriating or taking possession of something before others, especially as a prior right or intervention
prior appropriation; act of taking priority

Examples

The company's preemption of the market left little opportunity for competitors.
Rapid preemption of fresh water sources can lead to conflicts between neighboring countries.
Critics argue that the preemption of public space by private events reduces community access.

Synset Examples

the preemption of bandwidth by commercial interests

Related Terms

  • Synonym: appropriation - the act of taking something for one's own use, often without permission
  • Synonym: allocation - the action or process of distributing something

Etymology

From Latin 'praeemere', meaning 'to buy before' or 'anticipate'.

3. NOUN

/ priˈɛmpʃən / (AmE), / priːˈɛmpʃən / (BrE)
the right to purchase or acquire something before others are given the opportunity
right to purchase before others

Examples

The contract granted the company preemption on all mineral rights in the area.
She exercised her preemption to buy the land before it was put on the open market.
Under the agreement, we have preemption for any resale of their shares.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: first refusal - the right of being offered something before others

Etymology

From Latin praeemere (to buy before), from prae- (before) + emere (to buy).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05185921-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ priˈɛmpʃən / (AmE), / priːˈɛmpʃən / (BrE)
(law) the right of a government to seize or appropriate property before others; the act of taking possession in advance of others.
government's right to seize or acquire property
legal

Examples

The government exercised its preemption to acquire land for the new highway.
Laws regarding preemption can override property rights in certain circumstances.
Through preemption, a state may seize assets deemed necessary for public use.

Etymology

From Latin praeemptio (purchase beforehand), from prae- (before) + emere (to buy).
