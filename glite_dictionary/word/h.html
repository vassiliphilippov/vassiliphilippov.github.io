2. NOUN

/ eɪtʃ / (AmE), / eɪtʃ / (BrE)
the symbol for hydrogen, a colorless, odorless, highly flammable diatomic gas; the lightest and most abundant chemical element in the universe
chemical symbol for hydrogen element

Examples

Water is composed of two atoms of H and one atom of oxygen.
The periodic table lists H as the first chemical element.
Rocket fuel often uses liquid H for its high energy output.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hydrogen - a colorless, odorless, highly flammable gas; the simplest element

Etymology

From the initial letter of New Latin hydrogenium, meaning hydrogen.

3. NOUN

/ eɪtʃ / (AmE), / eɪtʃ / (BrE)
The symbol for enthalpy, a thermodynamic quantity equal to the internal energy of a system plus the product of its pressure and volume.
thermodynamics: symbol for enthalpy
medical

Examples

The change in H during the reaction determines whether heat is absorbed or released.
Scientists use H to calculate the enthalpy change of chemical processes.
In thermodynamic equations, H is often used to represent the heat content of a system.

Synset Examples

enthalpy is the amount of energy in a system capable of doing mechanical work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enthalpy - thermodynamic quantity: internal energy plus pressure-volume product

Etymology

Used as the symbol for 'enthalpy', from the Greek 'enthalpein' (to warm in).

4. NOUN

/ eɪtʃ / (AmE), / eɪtʃ / (BrE)
the symbol for henry, the SI unit of inductance, equal to the inductance for which an electromotive force of one volt is produced when the current changes at the rate of one ampere per second
the SI unit of inductance (henry)

Examples

The inductance of the coil is measured in H (henries).
A value of 2 H means the coil will generate 2 volts if the current changes at one ampere per second.
Capacitors are rated in farads, while inductors are rated in H.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: henry - SI unit of inductance (H)

Etymology

Named after Joseph Henry, American physicist (1797–1878).
