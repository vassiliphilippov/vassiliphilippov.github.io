mendicant

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (AmE), / ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (BrE)
(of a person or group) practicing begging or depending on charitable donations for support.
practicing or characteristic of begging

Examples

The city was known for its mendicant monks, who depended on alms for their survival.
He joined an order of mendicant friars and renounced all personal possessions.
In medieval times, mendicant orders were different from traditional monastic communities.

Synset Examples

mendicant friars

Related Terms

  • Synonym: begging - asking for charity or money, especially as a practice
  • Antonym: self-sufficient - independent; not relying on others for support

Etymology

From Latin mendicant-, present participle of 'mendicare' (to beg).

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (AmE), / ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (BrE)
a member of a religious order who relies chiefly or exclusively on alms for subsistence, especially in reference to certain orders in the Christian tradition
member of a religious order living by alms

Examples

The Franciscan order is well known for its tradition of mendicant life.
He studied the history of mendicant monks during the Middle Ages.
As a mendicant, he depended on the charity of others for his daily needs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: friar - a member of a mendicant religious order
  • Synonym: monk - a member of a religious community living under vows of poverty, chastity, and obedience

Etymology

From Latin 'mendicans', present participle of 'mendicare' (to beg), from 'mendicus' (beggar).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (AmE), / ˈmɛn.dɪ.kənt / (BrE)
a person, often poor, who lives by begging for money or food
person who begs for a living

Examples

After losing his job, he became a mendicant wandering the city streets.
The old mendicant sat at the church steps, hoping for charity from passersby.
In medieval times, some religious orders required their members to live as mendicants.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beggar - a person who begs, typically for money or food
  • Synonym: pauper - a very poor person

Etymology

From Latin mendicant-, present participle of mendicare (to beg).

Synset POS: n
