weird

1. ADJ

/ wɪrd / (AmE), / wɪəd / (BrE)
very strange or unusual in a way that is surprising or difficult to understand
strikingly odd or unusual

Examples

The abandoned house had a weird atmosphere at night.
She heard a weird sound coming from the basement.
His behavior was so weird that it made everyone uncomfortable.

Synset Examples

some trick of the moonlight; some weird effect of shadow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strange - unusual or surprising; not normal
  • Synonym: bizarre - very strange or unusual
  • Antonym: normal - usual or expected; not strange

Etymology

From Old English *wyrd* (fate, destiny), later influenced by 19th-century usage.

2. ADJ

/ wɪrd / (AmE), / wɪəd / (BrE)
suggesting supernatural influences; strange in a way that seems unearthly or beyond the ordinary
suggesting supernatural influences

Examples

The full moon gave the forest a weird glow that made everything look otherworldly.
She felt a weird presence in the old mansion, as if someone was watching her.
In the play, the weird sisters prophesy Macbeth’s rise and fall.

Synset Examples

an eldritch screech
the three weird sisters
stumps … had uncanny shapes as of monstrous creatures
an unearthly light
he could hear the unearthly scream of some curlew piercing the din

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supernatural - attributed to some force beyond scientific understanding or the laws of nature
  • Synonym: uncanny - strange or mysterious, especially in an unsettling way
  • Antonym: ordinary - with nothing strange or supernatural, normal

Etymology

From Old English *wyrd* (fate, destiny), influenced by use in Shakespeare's 'Macbeth' as 'weird sisters'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01579817-s
Synset POS: s
