iris

1. NOUN

/ ˈaɪrɪs / (AmE), / ˈaɪərɪs / (BrE)
a plant with long, narrow leaves and tall stems bearing brightly colored flowers with three upright petals and three drooping sepals
a plant with showy, colorful flowers

Examples

The garden was full of blooming iris in shades of purple, yellow, and white.
She planted a row of irises along the walkway to add color in spring.
The delicate petals of the iris glistened with morning dew.

Etymology

From Greek iris (rainbow, iris) via Latin, referring to the variety of flower colors.

2. NOUN

/ ˈaɪrɪs / (AmE), / ˈaɪərɪs / (BrE)
the circular, colored membrane in the eye, composed of muscle, that controls the size of the pupil and thus the amount of light entering the eye
colored membrane controlling pupil size in the eye

Examples

The color of her iris changed slightly under different lights.
A doctor can diagnose certain diseases by examining the iris.
The contraction of the iris causes the pupil to shrink in bright sunlight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pupil - the opening in the center of the iris that regulates light entry

Etymology

From Greek *iris* (rainbow), applied to the eye's colored part because of its appearance.

3. NOUN

/ ˈaɪrɪs / (AmE), / ˈaɪərɪs / (BrE)
A mechanical diaphragm consisting of thin, overlapping plates that can be adjusted to change the size of a central opening, as used in devices like cameras and microscopes to regulate light.
adjustable diaphragm in optical devices

Examples

The photographer adjusted the iris of the lens to control the exposure.
A small iris within the microscope allowed for precise light regulation.
An automatic iris helps modern cameras adapt to changing lighting conditions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: diaphragm - a device that controls the passage of light or other substances, often by varying the size of an opening

Etymology

From the resemblance to the iris of the eye; the name is borrowed from the anatomical term.
