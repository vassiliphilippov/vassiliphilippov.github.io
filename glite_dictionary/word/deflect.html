to deflect

1. VERB

/ dɪˈflɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkt / (BrE)
to cause (someone's attention or focus) to be directed away from something; to distract
divert attention or focus

Examples

The politician tried to deflect attention from the scandal by focusing on economic issues.
She deflected questions about her personal life with a joke.
His sudden outburst was meant to deflect the teacher's criticism away from his mistakes.

Synset Examples

The thief distracted the bystanders
He deflected his competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to distract - to prevent someone from concentrating

Etymology

From Latin 'deflectere' (to bend away), from 'de-' (away) + 'flectere' (to bend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01795785-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈflɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkt / (BrE)
to stop or prevent the forward movement of an opponent or ball, especially in sports; to block or turn aside
block or turn aside (opponent or ball)

Examples

The goalkeeper deflected the ball with his fingertips to prevent a goal.
She skillfully deflected the opponent's attack during the match.
The defender managed to deflect the pass before it reached the striker.

Synset Examples

block an attack

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to stop or prevent movement, especially in games or sports
  • Antonym: to allow - to let through, not block or impede

Etymology

From Latin *deflectere* (to bend away, turn aside), from *de-* (away) + *flectere* (to bend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01149526-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈflɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkt / (BrE)
to prevent something undesirable from happening, especially by taking action to avoid or ward it off
prevent or ward off something undesirable

Examples

The company introduced new policies to deflect potential lawsuits.
Good communication can often deflect unnecessary arguments before they escalate.
She tried to deflect criticism by explaining her decision in detail.

Synset Examples

Let's avoid a confrontation
head off a confrontation
avert a strike

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to avert - to prevent something bad from happening
  • Synonym: to avoid - to keep away from or stop something from happening
  • Antonym: invite - to bring on or make more likely (in context of danger or risk)

Etymology

From Latin deflectere, from de- ‘away from’ + flectere ‘to bend’.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪˈflɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkt / (BrE)
to cause something to change direction or turn from its original course or focus
cause to change direction

Examples

The shield was designed to deflect arrows away from the soldiers.
She skillfully deflected the reporter's difficult questions.
Wind can sometimes deflect the flight of a golf ball.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to divert - to cause something or someone to change direction
  • Synonym: deviate - to turn aside from a route, way, course, etc.
  • Antonym: direct - to cause to move in a straight line or toward a point

Etymology

From Latin deflectere, from de- (away) + flectere (to bend).

5. VERB

/ dɪˈflɛkt / (AmE), / dɪˈflɛkt / (BrE)
to cause something to change direction by interposing something; to turn aside from a straight course or intended direction
cause to change direction

Examples

The goalkeeper managed to deflect the ball away from the goal.
She used her hand to deflect the branch as she walked through the forest.
He tried to deflect attention from the issue during the interview.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to divert - cause to change course or direction
  • Synonym: to reflect - cause to bounce back or turn aside, especially light or sound
  • Antonym: to straighten - to make or become straight; not to turn aside

Etymology

From Latin 'deflectere', from 'de-' (away) + 'flectere' (to bend).
