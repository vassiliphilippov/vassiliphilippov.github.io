devolution

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɛvəˈluʃən / (AmE), / ˌdiːvəˈluːʃən / (BrE)
the transfer or delegation of power or authority from a central government to regional or local governments
transfer of power to regional governments

Examples

The Scottish Parliament was created as a result of political devolution from Westminster.
Many countries pursue devolution to give regions more control over local matters.
The debate on devolution highlights tensions between national and regional priorities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decentralization - the process of distributing or dispersing functions, powers, or people away from a central location or authority
  • Antonym: centralization - the concentration of control and decision-making authority in a central organization or body

Etymology

From Latin devolutio, from devolvere, meaning to roll down or transfer.

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɛvəˈluːʃən / (AmE), / ˌdiːvəˈluːʃən / (BrE)
a gradual decline or degeneration from a higher to a lower state in effectiveness, quality, or vitality
decline from higher to lower state

Examples

The devolution of the empire was marked by widespread corruption and loss of power.
Many historians debate the causes of the devolution of ancient civilizations.
Environmental damage often leads to the devolution of once-thriving ecosystems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: decline - gradual loss of strength or quality
  • Synonym: degeneration - the process of declining to a less functional state
  • Antonym: evolution - gradual development to a higher or more advanced state

Etymology

From Latin devolutio (a rolling down, decline), from devolvere (to roll down).
