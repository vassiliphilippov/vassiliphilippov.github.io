pinion

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɪnjən / (AmE), / ˈpɪnjən / (BrE)
any of the larger flight feathers on the wing or tail of a bird, especially those crucial for flight
large feather of a bird's wing or tail

Examples

The eagle spread its pinions wide as it soared over the valley.
A broken pinion can prevent a bird from flying.
The biologist examined the structure of the falcon's pinions under a microscope.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flight feather - a large feather necessary for a bird's flight

Etymology

From Old French 'pignon' (feather, wing), from Latin 'pinnio' (feather, wing), from 'penna' (feather).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɪnjən / (AmE), / ˈpɪnjən / (BrE)
a small gear with few teeth that meshes with a larger wheel or rack in a mechanical system
small gear in mechanical systems

Examples

The technician replaced the worn pinion to restore smooth movement to the machine.
A pinion gear is essential in the operation of many clocks and watches.
When the pinion meshes with the rack, the rotational motion is converted into linear motion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gear - a toothed machine part, such as a wheel or cylinder, that meshes with another toothed part

Etymology

From Old French *pignon* (cogwheel), from Latin *pinea* (pine cone), from the shape resemblance.

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈpɪniən / (AmE), / ˈpɪnjən / (BrE)
to bind or restrain someone's arms or legs, especially to prevent movement; to confine or immobilize
bind or restrain arms or legs

Examples

The guards quickly pinioned the prisoner's arms behind his back.
She was pinioned to the chair so she couldn't escape.
The child felt pinioned by the heavy winter coat and scarf.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to restrain - to prevent someone from moving freely or acting freely

Etymology

From Old French 'pignon' (wing, feather), from Latin 'pinnionem', diminutive of 'penna' (feather).

4. NOUN

/ ˈpɪnjən / (AmE), / ˈpɪnjən / (BrE)
the outer part of a bird's wing including the flight feathers, or the wing itself
outer part or whole of a bird's wing

Examples

The eagle spread its powerful pinions and soared above the mountains.
A falcon with injured pinions soon loses its graceful flight.
In poetry, the word pinion is often used to evoke the image of majestic wings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wing - the limb of a bird or aircraft used for flying

Etymology

From Old French *pignon* (feather, wing), from Latin *pinnio* (wing feather), from *penna* (feather, wing).

5. VERB

/ ˈpɪnjən / (AmE), / ˈpɪnjən / (BrE)
to cut or remove the wing feathers or part of the wing of a bird to prevent it from flying.
cut or remove bird's wing feathers

Examples

Farmers sometimes pinion the geese to prevent them from escaping the enclosure.
The keepers pinioned the swans as part of routine management at the reserve.
He objected to the practice of pinioning birds, considering it cruel and unnecessary.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pinion - to bind the wings or arms of; to restrain or immobilize

From Old French *pignon* (feather, wing), from Latin *pinna* (feather, wing).
