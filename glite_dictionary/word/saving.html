saving

1. ADJ

/ ˈseɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈseɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
bringing about salvation or redemption from sin; leading to spiritual deliverance
leading to salvation from sin

Examples

Many believers place their hope in saving grace during difficult times.
The preacher spoke about the importance of saving faith to her congregation.
He credited his transformation to the saving power of divine love.

Synset Examples

saving faith
redemptive (or redeeming) love

Related Terms

  • Synonym: redemptive - serving to redeem or save, especially from sin
  • Antonym: damning - bringing condemnation or spiritual ruin

Etymology

From save + -ing, used in religious contexts since the 14th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈseɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈseɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of protecting something or someone from loss, harm, or danger
the act of protection from harm or loss

Examples

Firefighters are dedicated to the saving of lives during emergencies.
The saving of historical artifacts is important for cultural preservation.
The charity focuses on the saving of endangered species.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rescue - the act of saving someone from danger or harm
  • Antonym: endangerment - the act of putting someone or something at risk

Etymology

From Old French 'sauver' and Latin 'salvāre' meaning to save, preserve.

5. ADJ

/ ˈseɪvɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈseɪvɪŋ / (BrE)
(of a person) showing or characterized by thriftiness or careful use of resources.
characterized by thriftiness

Examples

Despite his wealth, Robert remained a saving man who avoided unnecessary expenses.
Her saving habits helped the family get through difficult times.
They admired her saving ways, especially in a culture that valued extravagance.

Synset Examples

wealthy by inheritance but saving by constitution

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrifty - using money and resources carefully and not wastefully
  • Antonym: spendthrift - a person who spends money in an extravagant, irresponsible way

Etymology

From the verb 'save', influenced by the idea of preserving resources.
