to slake

1. VERB

/ sleɪk / (AmE), / sleɪk / (BrE)
to cause something, especially lime, to heat and break down by adding water
cause to heat and crumble with water (esp. lime)

Examples

To produce mortar, you must slake quicklime by adding water.
The workers slaked the lime before mixing it with sand.
When the powder was slaked, it released intense heat and crumbled into a fine paste.

Synset Examples

slack lime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hydrate - to combine with water or cause to absorb water

Etymology

From Old English slacian (to become slack or remit); technical sense later specialized.

WordNet ID: oewn-00215050-v

2. VERB

/ sleɪk / (AmE), / sleɪk / (BrE)
to satisfy (thirst, desire, etc.) or to quench; especially by drinking
satisfy or quench, especially thirst

Examples

The hikers stopped by a stream to slake their thirst.
After hours in the sun, nothing could slake her craving for water.
He drank deeply, hoping to slake his parched throat.

Synset Examples

The cold water quenched his thirst

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quench - to satisfy (thirst) or put an end to something

Etymology

From Old English 'slacian' (to become slack), related to 'slack'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01206641-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ sleɪk / (AmE), / sleɪk / (BrE)
to make less active or intense; to satisfy or quench, especially thirst or desire
make less intense; quench

Examples

The rain helped to slake the heat of the summer afternoon.
After running the marathon, he needed water to slake his thirst.
Her curiosity was slaked by the detailed explanation provided by the guide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to quench - to satisfy thirst or extinguish; similar meaning
  • Antonym: to intensify - to make more intense; opposite meaning

Etymology

From Old English *slacian* (to slacken), related to *slæc* (slack).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00246175-v
Synset POS: v
