to stabilise

1. VERB

/ ˈsteɪbəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsteɪbəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make something unlikely to change, fall, or fail; to make or become stable
make or become stable
british

Examples

The government acted quickly to stabilise the national currency after the economic crisis.
Doctors are working to stabilise the patient before performing surgery.
Strict regulations can help stabilise food prices during periods of high inflation.

Synset Examples

The drug stabilized her blood pressure
stabilize prices

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stabilize - to make something stable or steady; American spelling
  • Antonym: to destabilize - to make unstable or less steady

Etymology

From French 'stabiliser', based on Latin 'stabilis' (stable).

2. VERB

/ ˈsteɪ.bə.laɪz / (AmE), / ˈsteɪ.bɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to become stable or to make something stable; to stop changing, increasing, or getting worse
become or make stable

Examples

The government implemented policies to help the economy stabilise after a period of rapid inflation.
Doctors worked quickly to stabilise the patient's condition in the emergency room.
After months of turbulence, the company's share price began to stabilise.

3. VERB

/ ˈsteɪ.bə.laɪz / (AmE), / ˈsteɪ.bɪ.laɪz / (BrE)
to make something steady or less likely to move, change, or fall; to support or hold steady
make steady or stable
british

Examples

Engineers worked hard to stabilise the bridge after last winter’s floods.
She placed her hand on the table to stabilise herself before standing up.
Measures were taken to stabilise the currency and restore confidence in the economy.

Synset Examples

brace your elbows while working on the potter's wheel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stabilize - American English spelling of 'stabilise'
  • Synonym: steady - make or become steady
  • Antonym: to destabilise - make unstable; cause to become unsteady

Etymology

From French 'stabiliser', from Latin 'stabilis' (firm, stable).
