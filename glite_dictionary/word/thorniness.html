thorniness

1. NOUN

/ ˈθɔrni.nəs / (AmE), / ˈθɔː.ni.nəs / (BrE)
the quality or state of being covered with sharp thorns or spines
the state of being covered in thorns

Examples

The thorniness of the bushes made it difficult to pick the berries without gloves.
We underestimated the thorniness of the path and got several scratches along the way.
The thorniness of the plant protects it from most herbivores.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thorny - covered with or having many thorns

2. NOUN

/ ˈθɔrniˌnɪs / (AmE), / ˈθɔːniˌnɪs / (BrE)
The quality of being complicated, difficult, or full of controversial points.
difficulty or complexity; controversial nature

Examples

The thorniness of the debate made it difficult to find a consensus.
He underestimated the thorniness of the negotiations with the labor union.
Her book addresses the thorniness of modern political discourse.

Synset Examples

And, given the thorniness of the issues on the table, a work stoppage at all West Coast ports is not to be ruled out.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: difficulty - state or quality of being hard to do, understand, or deal with
  • Synonym: complexity - the state of being complex or hard to analyze
  • Antonym: simplicity - the quality of being easy to understand or do

Etymology

From 'thorny' + '-ness', meaning the state or quality of being thorny (difficult or troublesome).

3. NOUN

/ ˈθɔr.ni.nəs / (AmE), / ˈθɔː.ni.nəs / (BrE)
the quality of being rough, harsh, or bitter in manner or character
harsh or bitter manner

Examples

Despite his thorniness, he was devoted to his friends and family.
She was surprised by the thorniness in his voice when discussing the past.
The thorniness of their interactions made collaboration difficult.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acerbity - sharpness or bitterness of temperament
  • Antonym: gentleness - the quality of being kind and mild in manner

Etymology

From 'thorny' (from Old English 'þorn' meaning thorn) + '-ness' (forming nouns from adjectives).
