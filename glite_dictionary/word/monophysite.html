Monophysite

1. ADJ

/ məˈnɑːfəˌsaɪt / (AmE), / məˈnɒfɪsaɪt / (BrE)
relating to or supporting Monophysitism, the doctrine that Christ has a single, divine nature
relating to Monophysitism (Christian doctrine)

Examples

The Monophysite position was rejected at the Council of Chalcedon in 451 CE.
Several Eastern churches have Monophysite roots that distinguish them from other Christian traditions.
The debate between Monophysite and Chalcedonian views shaped early Christian theology.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Monophysitism - the doctrine that Christ has a single, divine nature

Etymology

From Greek monos (single) + physis (nature).

2. NOUN

/ məˈnɒfəˌsaɪt / (AmE), / ˌmɒnəˈfɪsaɪt / (BrE)
A person who adheres to the doctrine that Christ has a single, divine nature rather than both a divine and a human nature.
adherent of Monophysitism (one-nature doctrine)

Examples

The council condemned the Monophysite doctrine as heretical in the sixth century.
Several Eastern churches had many Monophysite members after the debates over Christology.
A noted theologian of the time was accused of being a Monophysite by his opponents.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Nestorian - adherent of the doctrine that Christ exists as two persons, one divine and one human
  • Antonym: Dyophysite - adherent of the doctrine that Christ has two natures, divine and human

Etymology

From Late Latin Monophysita, from Greek monophysitēs: monos (single) + physis (nature).
