to benight

1. VERB

/ bɪˈnaɪt / (AmE), / bɪˈnaɪt / (BrE)
to envelop with intellectual, social, or moral darkness; to make ignorant or unenlightened
envelop in darkness or ignorance

Examples

The regime sought to benight its citizens by restricting access to information.
Centuries of isolation served to benight the rural communities, leaving them far behind the rest of the country.
Misleading propaganda can easily benight a population if left unchecked.

Synset Examples

The benighted peoples of this area

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge and understanding

Etymology

From Middle English 'bynyghten', from 'be-' (thoroughly) + 'night', meaning to cover with night.

2. VERB

/ bəˈnaɪt / (AmE), / bɪˈnaɪt / (BrE)
to make dark or obscure, especially making something difficult to see or understand
make dark or obscure
archaic

Examples

Clouds benight the landscape, hiding the mountains from view.
The ancient forest was benighted by the setting sun, casting long shadows everywhere.
Her memory of that night was benighted by fear, making it hard to recall details.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obscure - make unclear, difficult to see or understand

Etymology

From Middle English 'bynighten' (to cover with night), from 'by' + 'night'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00314661-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ bɪˈnaɪt / (AmE), / bɪˈnaɪt / (BrE)
(of night or darkness) to overtake or envelop (someone or something); to plunge into darkness.
to be overtaken by darkness or night
archaic

Examples

The travelers were benighted in the forest after sundown.
We hurried to make camp before the valley was benighted.
He feared the party would be benighted on the mountain trail.

Etymology

From Middle English benighten, from be- (intensive prefix) + night.
