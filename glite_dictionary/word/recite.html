to recite

1. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to list or specify items individually, often in detail
specify or list items individually

Examples

During her speech, she recited every reason for her decision, leaving nothing unsaid.
The teacher recited all the steps required to complete the assignment.
He calmly recited the names of each team member who contributed to the project.

Synset Examples

She enumerated the many obstacles she had encountered
The doctor recited the list of possible side effects of the drug

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enumerate - to mention things one by one

Etymology

From Latin recitare (read out, recite).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00948137-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to repeat aloud from memory, especially before an audience
repeat from memory aloud

Examples

At the assembly, she recited a classic Shakespearean sonnet from memory.
Children are often asked to recite poems or multiplication tables in school.
He stood confidently and recited the national anthem without hesitation.

Synset Examples

she recited a poem
The pupil recited his lesson for the day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: read - to look at and comprehend the meaning of written or printed matter
  • Degree Lower: to memorize - to commit to memory; to learn by heart

Etymology

From Latin recitare (to read out), from re- (again) + citare (to summon, to call).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00947287-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to say aloud or repeat from memory, usually for an audience
say from memory or aloud

Examples

She stood in front of the class and began to recite the poem.
He can recite all fifty states in alphabetical order.
At the competition, each participant was asked to recite a passage from Shakespeare.

Synset Examples

recite a poem
retell a story

Related Terms

  • Synonym: read - to look at and understand the meaning of written or printed words or symbols
  • Degree Lower: to memorize - to commit something to memory; to learn by heart

Etymology

From Latin recitare, meaning 'to read out' or 'to recite'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to narrate or give a detailed account of something, often by listing facts or events one after the other
to narrate or give a detailed account

Examples

The witness was asked to recite exactly what occurred that night.
She could recite every detail of the trip from memory.
During the meeting, he recited a long list of their recent achievements.

Synset Examples

Tell what happened
The father told a story to his child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to narrate - to give a spoken or written account of something
  • Antonym: to summarize - to give a brief statement of the main points

Etymology

From Latin recitare (read aloud, repeat from memory), from re- (again) + citare (summon, call).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00955248-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to say aloud or repeat something, especially from memory, often in a public or formal setting
to say something aloud from memory

Examples

She was asked to recite the poem in front of the entire class.
Each student must recite a passage from Shakespeare by heart.
He practiced every day to recite his lines perfectly at the competition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to declaim - to speak aloud in a formal, theatrical, or rhetorical way
  • Degree Lower: to memorize - to commit something to memory
  • Antonym: to improvise - to perform without preparation or memory

Etymology

From Latin recitare (to read out), from re- (again) + citare (to summon).

Source: WordNet
