to recede

1. VERB

/ rɪˈsid / (AmE), / rɪˈsiːd / (BrE)
to move back or away from a previous position; to retreat or become more distant
move back; retreat

Examples

The floodwaters began to recede after several days of heavy rain.
His hairline has started to recede over the past few years.
As the pain receded, she was finally able to get some rest.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retreat - to move back or withdraw
  • Antonym: advance - to move forward

Etymology

From Latin 'recedere' (to go back, withdraw), from 're-' (back) + 'cedere' (to go, yield).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈsid / (AmE), / rɪˈsiːd / (BrE)
to move back or withdraw from a previous position; to become more distant.
move back or withdraw

Examples

After the flood, the waters slowly began to recede from the streets.
As he grew older, his hairline started to recede steadily.
The footsteps receded into the distance until they were no longer audible.

Synset Examples

The enemy withdrew
The limo pulled away from the curb

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retreat - to move away from a place or situation, especially because it is dangerous or unpleasant
  • Antonym: advance - to move forward or make progress

Etymology

From Latin recedere (to go back), from re- (back) + cedere (to go).

3. VERB

/ rɪˈsid / (AmE), / rɪˈsiːd / (BrE)
to become gradually fainter or more distant; to move back or away in memory, perception, or space
become fainter or more distant

Examples

As time passed, the pain of his loss began to recede, allowing him to move forward.
The sound of the train's whistle receded into the distance until it was barely audible.
Her fears slowly receded as she grew more confident in her new surroundings.

Synset Examples

the unhappy memories of her childhood receded as she grew older

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fade - to gradually become less clear or strong
  • Synonym: to diminish - to become less or smaller

Etymology

From Latin recedere (to go back, withdraw), from re- (back) + cedere (to go).
