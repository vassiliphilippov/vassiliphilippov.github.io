to entertain

1. VERB

/ ˌɛn.tɚˈteɪn / (AmE), / ˌɛn.təˈteɪn / (BrE)
to provide amusement or enjoyment for others
provide amusement or enjoyment

Examples

The comedian did his best to entertain the audience throughout the evening.
She often invites friends over and tries to entertain them with games and music.
Children's performers entertain at birthday parties with magic tricks and songs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to amuse - to make someone laugh or smile; to entertain
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel weary and uninterested by being dull or tedious

Etymology

From Latin 'intertenere' (to hold together), via Old French 'entretenir'.

2. VERB

/ ˌɛn.tərˈteɪn / (AmE), / ˌɛn.təˈteɪn / (BrE)
to hold or consider in the mind (an idea, thought, or feeling)
hold or consider an idea or feeling

Examples

She refused to entertain the possibility of failure.
He often entertains doubts about his decisions late at night.
It's difficult not to entertain thoughts of revenge in such a situation.

Synset Examples

bear a grudge
entertain interesting notions
harbor a resentment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harbor - to keep (a thought or feeling) in one's mind
  • Synonym: to cherish - to hold (a belief or feeling) deeply and dearly

Etymology

From Old French *entretenir* (to hold together, support), from Latin *intertenere* (to hold mutually).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01777051-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˌɛn.tɚˈteɪn / (AmE), / ˌɛn.təˈteɪn / (BrE)
to consider or allow oneself to think about (an idea, suggestion, or possibility)
consider (an idea or proposal)

Examples

She decided to entertain the possibility of applying for the new job.
He never seriously entertained their objections to the plan.
Despite her doubts, Julia entertained the idea of going back to school.

Synset Examples

He entertained the notion of moving to South America

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to consider - to think about carefully
  • Antonym: to dismiss - to decide not to consider

Etymology

From Old French *entretenir* (to hold together, support, maintain), from Latin *intertenēre* (to hold mutually).
