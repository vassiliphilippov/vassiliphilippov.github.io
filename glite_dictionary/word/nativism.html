nativism

1. NOUN

/ ˈneɪ.t̬ɪˌvɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈneɪ.tɪˌvɪz.əm / (BrE)
the policy or practice of preserving or reviving the culture of long-established inhabitants, often in opposition to outside influences or acculturation
policy supporting native cultures

Examples

Many activists supported nativism to protect indigenous languages and traditions from disappearing.
The government's policy of nativism faced criticism for resisting multicultural influences.
Critics argue that excessive nativism can lead to social isolation and prejudice against newcomers.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acculturation - the process of adopting the cultural traits or social patterns of another group

Etymology

From Latin 'nativus' (native) + English '-ism'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈneɪt̬ɪˌvɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈneɪtɪˌvɪzəm / (BrE)
(Philosophy) the theory that certain ideas, concepts, or mental capacities are innate or inborn rather than acquired through experience.
theory that ideas are innate

Examples

Many philosophers have debated whether language ability is a result of nativism or learned behavior.
Chomsky's theory of universal grammar is often cited as an example of nativism in linguistics.
The theory of nativism suggests that some concepts exist in the mind independently of experience.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: empiricism - the theory that all knowledge is derived from experience

Etymology

From Latin 'nativus' meaning 'innate, produced by birth'.
