invention

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (BrE)
The act of creating something new, especially as a result of original thought or imagination.
the act of creating something new

Examples

Her brilliant invention completely changed the way we communicate.
Imagination is essential for any great invention to take shape.
The novel's entire plot was a pure invention of the author's mind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innovation - the introduction of something new

Etymology

From Latin 'inventio' meaning 'invention, discovery', from 'invenire' meaning 'to come upon'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (BrE)
A new device, process, or method that has been created as a result of study and experimentation.
creation of a new device or process

Examples

The telephone was a groundbreaking invention that changed the way people communicate.
Her invention won first prize at the science fair for its ingenuity.
Many modern conveniences stem from the invention of electricity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innovation - the introduction of something new or a new idea, method, or device

Etymology

From Latin 'inventio' (a finding, discovery), from 'invenire' (to come upon, find).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03587827-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛnʃən / (BrE)
the act of creating or designing something that has not existed before; the process of inventing.
the act of inventing

Examples

Thomas Edison is famous for his invention of the electric light bulb.
The company's latest invention has revolutionized the tech industry.
Patents encourage the invention of new and useful devices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creation - the action or process of bringing something into existence
  • Antonym: discovery - the act of finding something for the first time

Etymology

From Latin inventio (a finding, discovery).
