comose

1. ADJ

/ ˈkoʊˌmoʊs / (AmE), / ˈkəʊ.məʊs / (BrE)
(of certain seeds or plants) having a tuft or tufts of hair.
having a tuft of hair (botany)

Examples

The seeds of the willow are comose, allowing them to be carried by the wind.
Some plants produce comose fruits that aid in their dispersal.
Cotton is known for its comose seeds, which are surrounded by soft fibers.

Synset Examples

comate (or comose) seeds
a comal tuft

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comate - having a tuft of hair, especially botanical

Etymology

From Latin comosus, meaning 'full of hair, leafy', from coma ('hair').

2. ADJ

/ ˈkoʊˌmoʊs / (AmE), / ˈkəʊˌməʊs / (BrE)
(of a plant or plant part) having a tuft or crown of long hairs, bracts, or leaves at the apex.
having a tuft of hairs or bracts
medical

Examples

The seeds of the willow tree are comose, each crowned with silky hairs that aid in wind dispersal.
Many tropical plants produce comose fruits to aid seed movement and protection.
The comose flower heads of some grasses help them catch the breeze for pollination.

Synset Examples

comate royal palms
pineapples are comate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comate - bearing a tuft or a crown; another term for 'comose' in botany

Etymology

From Latin comosus (full of leaves or hair), from coma (hair of the head).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00657417-s
Synset POS: s
