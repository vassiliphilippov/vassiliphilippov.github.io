Judas

1. PROPN

/ ˈdʒuːdəs / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdəs / (BrE)
(in the New Testament) one of Jesus' apostles, considered to be a brother of St James and invoked in prayer for desperate or hopeless cases.
apostle invoked for hopeless cases

Examples

Many people pray to Judas when facing situations that seem impossible to resolve.
Religious paintings sometimes depict Judas holding a staff or a book.
The feast day of Judas is celebrated by some Christian denominations in late October.

Etymology

From Latin 'Judas', from Greek 'Ioudas', from Hebrew 'Yehudah' (Judah).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdʒuːdəs / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdəs / (BrE)
a person who betrays a friend or companion, especially under the appearance of loyalty or friendship
traitor under the guise of friendship

Examples

He was branded a Judas after revealing confidential information to the rival company.
She felt like a Judas when she broke her promise to her closest friend.
Calling him a Judas, the team never trusted him again after the betrayal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: traitor - a person who betrays someone or something, such as a friend, cause, or principle
  • Antonym: loyalist - a person who remains loyal to a cause or person

Etymology

From the biblical figure Judas Iscariot, one of Jesus's disciples who betrayed him.

3. PROPN

/ ˈdʒuːdəs / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdəs / (BrE)
In the New Testament, the Apostle who betrayed Jesus Christ to his enemies for thirty pieces of silver.
Apostle who betrayed Jesus

Examples

Many artists have depicted Judas reaching for the silver coins in classical paintings.
The name Judas has become synonymous with betrayal in Western culture.
In the biblical account, Judas identified Jesus to the authorities with a kiss.

Etymology

From Latin Judas, from Greek Ioudas, ultimately from Hebrew Yehudah (Judah).
