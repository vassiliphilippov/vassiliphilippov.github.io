reprimand

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɛprɪˌmænd / (AmE), / ˈrɛprɪˌmɑːnd / (BrE)
a formal expression of disapproval or criticism, especially by someone in authority
formal criticism or rebuke

Examples

The manager gave him a strong reprimand for being late to the meeting.
After the incident, the student received a formal reprimand from the principal.
Her mistake resulted in a written reprimand placed in her personnel file.

Synset Examples

he had to take the rebuke with a smile on his face

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuke - an expression of sharp disapproval or criticism
  • Antonym: commendation - praise or official approval

Etymology

From French 'réprimande', from Latin 'reprimenda' (to be repressed)

2. VERB

/ ˈrɛprəˌmænd / (AmE), / ˈrɛprɪmɑːnd / (BrE)
to rebuke or express disapproval to someone, especially formally or officially
to formally rebuke or scold

Examples

The manager decided to reprimand the employee for repeatedly arriving late.
If you break the rules, the teacher may reprimand you in front of the class.
He was reprimanded by his supervisor for not following safety procedures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuke - to express sharp disapproval or criticism
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From French *réprimander* and Latin *reprimandare* (to reprove, check).

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛprɪˌmænd / (AmE), / ˈrɛprɪˌmɑːnd / (BrE)
to formally express sharp disapproval or criticism, especially officially or with authority
formally express disapproval

Examples

The teacher reprimanded the student for cheating on the test.
He was reprimanded by his boss for arriving late to the meeting.
Parents may need to reprimand their children when they break important rules.

Synset Examples

The mother scolded the child for entering a stranger's car
The deputy ragged the Prime Minister
The customer dressed down the waiter for bringing cold soup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scold - to angrily rebuke or criticize someone, especially a child
  • Antonym: to commend - to praise or express approval

Etymology

From French *réprimander*, from Latin *reprimenda* (things to be repressed) from *reprimere* (to repress).
