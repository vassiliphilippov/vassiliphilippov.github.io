constraint

1. NOUN

/ kənˈstreɪnt / (AmE), / kənˈstreɪnt / (BrE)
a device or mechanism that restricts or slows the motion of something
device restricting movement

Examples

The car did not have proper constraints installed to protect the passengers.
Safety constraints are essential in modern elevators to prevent accidents.
Engineers tested several types of constraint before selecting the best one for the machinery.

Synset Examples

the car did not have proper restraints fitted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restraint - a device that limits or prevents movement

Etymology

From Latin 'constringere' (to bind tightly), via Old French 'constreindre'.

Source: WordNet

2. NOUN

/ kənˈstreɪnt / (AmE), / kənˈstreɪnt / (BrE)
the act of constraining or restricting someone or something; the use or threat of force to limit freedom or behavior
act of restricting or limiting

Examples

The law acts as a constraint on government power.
Social constraint shaped his behavior at the formal event.
He acted under severe constraint imposed by the threat of punishment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restraint - a measure or condition that keeps someone or something under control
  • Antonym: freedom - the power or right to act, speak, or think without hindrance or restraint

Etymology

From Latin 'constringere' (to bind together or restrain).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01152116-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ kənˈstreɪnt / (AmE), / kənˈstreɪnt / (BrE)
the state of being restricted or held back, especially physically, by something that limits movement or freedom
physical restriction or limitation

Examples

The patient wore a special vest as a constraint to prevent accidental falls.
Legal constraints prevented him from leaving the building until the investigation was complete.
Despite the constraint of handcuffs, he managed to stand up and move around.

Synset Examples

dogs should be kept under restraint

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restraint - the action of holding something back or limiting it; also refers to physical restriction

Etymology

From Latin 'constringere' (to bind tightly), via Old French 'constreinte'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
