forerunner

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌrʌnər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːˌrʌnə / (BrE)
a person who goes before or announces the coming of someone or something else
one who precedes or announces another

Examples

The colonial courier acted as a forerunner, delivering news ahead of the official delegation.
Many regarded John the Baptist as a forerunner to Jesus in the New Testament.
The high winds were a forerunner of the approaching storm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precursor - a person or thing that comes before another of the same kind
  • Antonym: successor - a person who comes after another

Etymology

From Old English 'fore' (before) + 'runner' (one who runs).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌrʌnər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːˌrʌnə / (BrE)
a person or thing that comes before and indicates the approach of someone or something similar or related; a precursor.
something preceding a similar thing

Examples

The telephone was a forerunner of modern communication technology.
Some consider the steam engine to be the forerunner of the internal combustion engine.
This ancient festival was a forerunner of many customs we celebrate today.

Synset Examples

phrenology was an antecedent of modern neuroscience

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precursor - something that comes before and leads to another
  • Antonym: successor - a person or thing that follows another

Etymology

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌrʌnər / (AmE), / ˈfɔːˌrʌnə / (BrE)
A person or thing that comes before and signals or prepares for the arrival of someone or something else.
person or thing preceding another

Examples

The invention was seen as a forerunner to modern technology.
Swallows are often viewed as a forerunner of spring.
The first compact discs were the forerunner of today's digital music formats.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precursor - something that comes before another of the same kind; a forerunner
  • Antonym: successor - person or thing that succeeds another

Etymology

From Old English 'forerennere', combining 'fore-' (before) and 'runner'.
