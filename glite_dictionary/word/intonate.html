to intonate

1. VERB

/ ˈɪn.toʊ.neɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪn.təʊ.neɪt / (BrE)
to recite or utter with musical or rhythmic intonation, especially as a chant or psalm
recite with musical intonation

Examples

The priest began to intonate the evening prayers as the congregation listened.
During the ceremony, the monks intonated ancient hymns in unison.
He stood at the podium and intonated the chant solemnly, filling the hall with sound.

Synset Examples

The rabbi chanted a prayer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chant - to sing or say something in a repetitive, rhythmic, and musical manner

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *intonare* (to thunder, to sound, to intone).

2. VERB

/ ˈɪntoʊneɪt / (AmE), / ˈɪntəneɪt / (BrE)
to speak or utter words with careful attention to the rise and fall of pitch; to speak in a particular tone or manner
speak with varied pitch or tone

Examples

She tried to intonate the poem with emotion, pausing at just the right moments.
The actor was asked to intonate the lines with a sense of urgency.
He would intonate each phrase as if telling a dramatic story.

Synset Examples

please intonate with sadness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intonation - the rise and fall of the voice in speaking

Etymology

From Medieval Latin intonatus, past participle of intonare, meaning 'to thunder, to intone'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01052185-v
Synset POS: v
