tarmacadam

1. NOUN

/ ˌtɑːrməˈkædəm / (AmE), / ˌtɑːməˈkædəm / (BrE)
A road or pavement surface made by compacting layers of crushed stone and binding them with tar.
paved surface bound with tar and stones

Examples

The workers laid fresh tarmacadam on the country lane after repairing the water pipes.
Our driveway was resurfaced with tarmacadam last summer for a smoother finish.
Early roads were often constructed using tarmacadam instead of modern asphalt.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: macadam - road surface made by compacting small stones without tar

Etymology

From the surname MacAdam, after John Loudon McAdam (1756–1836), combined with 'tar', referring to the tar-based version of McAdam's road-building method.

2. NOUN

/ ˌtɑːr.məˈkæd.əm / (AmE), / ˌtɑː.məˈkæd.əm / (BrE)
A paving material made from tar and broken stone, usually mixed in a factory and laid as a surface for roads or driveways.
paving material of tar and stone

Examples

The driveway was resurfaced with tarmacadam to provide a smooth finish.
They laid tarmacadam on the new road to improve its durability.
Modern airports often use tarmacadam for their runways and taxiways.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: asphalt - a mixture of bitumen and aggregates used for road surfaces
  • Synonym: macadam - compact layered small stones used for paving roads

Etymology

Blend of 'tar' and the surname 'Macadam', after John Loudon McAdam, who pioneered road construction methods.
