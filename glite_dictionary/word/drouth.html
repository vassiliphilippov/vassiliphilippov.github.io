drouth

1. NOUN

/ draʊθ / (AmE), / draʊθ / (BrE)
A prolonged period of shortage, especially of rainfall; a drought. Sometimes used metaphorically for any lengthy lack.
prolonged shortage (esp. of rain); drought
archaic

Examples

The severe drouth lasted for two years, devastating crops across the region.
After a ten-year drouth of championships, the team finally claimed victory.
Many families left the area during the drouth, searching for better living conditions elsewhere.

Synset Examples

when England defeated Pakistan it ended a ten-year drought

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drought - a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall

Etymology

Old variant spelling of 'drought,' from Old English *drūgath* (dryness).

2. NOUN

/ draʊθ / (AmE), / draʊθ / (BrE)
an extended period of unusually low rainfall, resulting in a lack of water
period of low rainfall; drought
archaic

Examples

The region suffered a severe drouth that ruined many crops.
Old diaries describe how families coped during the drouth of 1924.
Desperate for relief, the townspeople prayed for rain to end the long drouth.

Synset Examples

farmers most affected by the drought hope that there may yet be sufficient rain early in the growing season

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drought - a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall; a shortage of water

Etymology

Earlier form and dialectal variant of "drought," from Old English *drūgaþ* (dryness).
