stony

1. ADJ

/ ˈstoʊni / (AmE), / ˈstəʊni / (BrE)
(of ground or an area) full of stones or rocks.
full of stones or rocks

Examples

The path became almost impassable over the stony hillside, slowing our progress.
Farmers struggled to plant crops in the stony soil left after the flood.
We found a quiet spot to sit among the stony outcrops overlooking the valley.

Synset Examples

rocky fields
stony ground
bouldery beaches

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rocky - covered with or full of rocks

Etymology

From Old English stānig, from stān (stone) + -ig (adjective suffix).

2. ADJ

/ ˈstoʊni / (AmE), / ˈstəʊni / (BrE)
Extremely hard or unyielding, like stone or granite.
extremely hard or unyielding

Examples

Her gaze was stony, revealing no hint of emotion or pity.
The bread had gone so stale that it was almost stony in texture.
He clenched his hand into a stony fist, refusing to back down.

Synset Examples

a granitic fist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rock-hard - extremely hard, like a rock
  • Antonym: soft - not hard or unyielding

Etymology

From Old English stāniġ, from stān (stone) + -y.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01155454-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈstoʊni / (AmE), / ˈstəʊni / (BrE)
(of a person or their expression) showing no sympathy or emotion; unfeeling or hard.
showing no sympathy or emotion

Examples

She gave him a stony glare that made her feelings perfectly clear.
His stony silence told us we would get no help from him.
Despite his friend's pleading, his response remained cold and stony.

Synset Examples

his flinty gaze
the child's misery would move even the most obdurate heart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hard-hearted - lacking sympathy or compassion
  • Antonym: compassionate - showing sympathy and concern for others

Etymology

From Old English 'stānig' (made of stone), from 'stān' (stone).
