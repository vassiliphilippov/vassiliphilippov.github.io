spook

1. VERB

/ spuːk / (AmE), / spuːk / (BrE)
to suddenly frighten or disturb (a person or animal), often causing a startled or panicked reaction
to frighten or startle (especially animals)

Examples

The loud thunder spooked the cattle, causing them to run.
She didn't want to spook the deer, so she approached quietly.
Unexpected movements can easily spook young horses.

Synset Examples

The noise spooked the horse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to startle - cause someone to feel sudden shock or alarm
  • Antonym: calm - make tranquil or soothe, especially after fright

Etymology

Probably from Dutch 'spook' (ghost, specter).

2. NOUN

/ spuːk / (AmE), / spuːk / (BrE)
An image or memory that haunts the mind, often associated with fear or anxiety.
haunting memory or mental image

Examples

Old traumas can sometimes return as spooks to trouble our sleep.
For years, the spook of his past mistakes haunted him in quiet moments.
She tried to forget the spook that lingered after the accident, but it was never far away.

Synset Examples

he looked like he had seen a ghost
it aroused specters from his past

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ghost - the spirit of a dead person; apparition

Etymology

From Dutch 'spook' meaning ghost, apparition.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05906778-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ spuːk / (AmE), / spuːk / (BrE)
a person who is regarded as unpleasantly strange, odd, or eccentric
strange or eccentric person

Examples

Everyone at the party thought the new guy was a bit of a spook because of his bizarre stories.
She avoided Jack, thinking he was a spook due to his odd habits and mysterious ways.
The old house was owned by a spook who rarely spoke to anyone in the village.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weirdo - person who behaves strangely or unusually
  • Synonym: eccentric - person with unconventional or slightly strange views or behavior

Etymology

From Dutch 'spook' meaning 'ghost, apparition'.
