triangulation

1. NOUN

/ traɪˌæŋɡjuˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / traɪˌaŋɡjʊˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
a method of surveying or determining locations by dividing an area into triangles and measuring the length and angles of one side to calculate the others
surveying method using triangles

Examples

The engineers used triangulation to determine the precise location of the new bridge.
Traditional land surveys relied heavily on triangulation before the advent of GPS technology.
Accurate triangulation requires clear lines of sight between the measurement points.

Etymology

From French 'triangulation', from 'triangle', ultimately from Latin 'triangulus' (triangle).

2. NOUN

/ traɪˌæŋɡjuˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / traɪˌæŋɡjʊˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
a method of determining the location of a point by measuring angles to it from two known points at either end of a fixed baseline
method for finding location by measuring angles

Examples

Navigators use triangulation to determine the precise location of a ship at sea.
Modern surveying often relies on triangulation techniques to map large areas.
By applying triangulation, the hikers pinpointed their position on the map.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: trilateration - finding location by measuring distances rather than angles

Etymology

From Latin triangulum (triangle) + -ation, meaning the process related to triangles.
