to comport

1. VERB

/ kəmˈpɔrt / (AmE), / kəmˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to behave or conduct oneself in a particular manner
behave or conduct oneself

Examples

She always comports herself with great dignity at public events.
You are expected to comport yourself professionally during the conference.
He failed to comport himself in a manner befitting a leader.

Synset Examples

You should act like an adult
Don't behave like a fool
What makes her do this way?
The dog acts ferocious, but he is really afraid of people

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to behave - to act in a particular way, especially to be good
  • Antonym: to misbehave - to behave badly or improperly

Etymology

From Middle French comporter, from Latin comportare (to bring together, endure).

2. VERB

/ kəmˈpɔrt / (AmE), / kəmˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to behave or conduct oneself in a particular way; to act in accordance with expected standards
to behave oneself

Examples

He always tries to comport himself with dignity, even in difficult situations.
As an ambassador, she must comport herself with professionalism at all times.
The students failed to comport themselves appropriately at the ceremony.

Synset Examples

The children must learn to behave

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to behave - to act in a particular way, especially to be good by following rules or expectations

Etymology

From French 'comporter' (to conduct oneself), from Latin 'comportare' (to bring together, to collect).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02525332-v
Synset POS: v
