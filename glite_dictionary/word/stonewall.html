to stonewall

1. VERB

/ ˈstoʊnwɔl / (AmE), / ˈstəʊnwɔːl / (BrE)
to delay or obstruct by refusing to answer questions or by giving evasive replies, especially to avoid giving information or cooperating
deliberately obstruct or delay by refusing to cooperate

Examples

The committee accused the witness of trying to stonewall the investigation.
When reporters pressed him for details, he continued to stonewall and offered no clear answers.
They were determined to stonewall any attempts to learn the truth about the deal.

Synset Examples

The President stonewalled when he realized the plot was being uncovered by a journalist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to obstruct - to block or hinder progress
  • Antonym: to cooperate - to work together willingly

Etymology

Originally from the noun 'stone wall'; figurative sense from the idea of being as unyielding as a stone wall. Used prominently in U.S. politics since the 20th century.

2. VERB

/ ˈstoʊnˌwɔl / (AmE), / ˈstəʊnˌwɔːl / (BrE)
to deliberately obstruct or hinder the progress of a discussion, investigation, or request by refusing to answer questions or cooperate
deliberately block progress or discussion

Examples

The company spokesperson continued to stonewall journalists seeking answers about the incident.
When asked about the missing funds, the manager chose to stonewall rather than explain.
She has a habit of stonewalling whenever uncomfortable topics are raised during meetings.

Synset Examples

Nixon stonewalled the Watergate investigation
When she doesn't like to face a problem, she simply stonewalls

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to obstruct - to block or get in the way of something
  • Synonym: block - to prevent movement, progress, or action
  • Antonym: to facilitate - to make a process or action easier or more likely to happen

Etymology

From 'stone wall,' originally meaning a literal wall of stones, used metaphorically from the late 19th century for obstruction in US politics.
