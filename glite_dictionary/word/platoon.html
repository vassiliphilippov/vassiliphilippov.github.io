platoon

1. NOUN

/ pləˈtun / (AmE), / pləˈtuːn / (BrE)
a division of police officers working together under a military-style system, typically led by a commanding officer
division of police officers

Examples

The new police platoon was assigned to patrol the downtown area every night.
Each platoon consists of twenty officers led by a lieutenant.
After the shift change, a fresh platoon took over the investigation scene.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: squad - a small military or police unit

Etymology

From French 'peloton' (small ball or group), originally from Middle French.

2. NOUN

/ pləˈtun / (AmE), / pləˈtuːn / (BrE)
a group of people who work, move, or act together for a particular purpose, especially in the military or sports teams
group acting together, often military or sports

Examples

The first platoon on the scene quickly secured the area and awaited reinforcements.
During the game, the coach sent in a new defensive platoon for the second half.
Several platoons of volunteers assembled to clean the park after the festival.

Synset Examples

platoons of tourists poured out of the busses
the defensive platoon of the football team

Related Terms

  • Synonym: squad - a small organized group, especially of soldiers or police
  • Synonym: team - a group of people working together in sports or at work
  • Degree Higher: company - a large group of soldiers in the military

Etymology

From French *peloton* (small ball or group), diminutive of *pelote* (ball).

3. NOUN

/ pləˈtun / (AmE), / pləˈtuːn / (BrE)
a subdivision of a military company, typically made up of several squads and commanded by a lieutenant
military unit below company level

Examples

The platoon moved cautiously through the forest during their training exercise.
He was recently promoted to lead his own platoon in the army.
Three platoons were assigned to secure the perimeter of the base.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: company - a larger military unit composed of several platoons
  • Degree Lower: squad - a smaller military unit within a platoon

Etymology

From French 'peloton' (small ball or group), originally from Middle French 'pelote' (ball).
