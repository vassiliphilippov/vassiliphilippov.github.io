rhyme

1. NOUN

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
correspondence of sounds at the ends of words, especially in poetry, usually at the ends of lines
matching sounds at line ends in poetry

Examples

The poet used rhyme to give his verses a musical quality.
A perfect rhyme occurs when two lines end with exactly matching sounds.
Her favorite poems always have a strong sense of rhyme.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhythm - a strong, regular, repeated pattern of movement or sound

Etymology

From Old French 'rime', from Latin 'rhythmus', influenced by association with 'rhythm'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07111327-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
A short poem or verse, especially one in which the sounds at the ends of lines correspond with each other.
a short poem or verse with rhyming lines

Examples

She wrote a clever rhyme for her friend's birthday card.
Children often learn simple rhymes to help with language development.
The poet composed a rhyme about the changing seasons.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: verse - a line of poetry, or poetry as a genre
  • Synonym: poem - a piece of writing in verse form

Etymology

From Old French *rime*, from Latin *rythmus*, from Greek *rhythmos* (measured flow, rhythm).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06393492-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
to compose lines, verses, or poems in which the endings of lines sound alike; to write in rhyme
compose rhyming lines or poems

Examples

Many poets rhyme their verses to create a musical quality.
Children enjoy trying to rhyme words as a game in class.
The songwriter skillfully rhymed every line of the chorus.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: verse - writing arranged with a metrical rhythm, typically having rhyme
  • Antonym: prose - written or spoken language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure

Etymology

From Old French *rime*, of Germanic origin; related to Old High German *rīm* (series, number).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01705717-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ raɪm / (AmE), / raɪm / (BrE)
to have or end with a sound that corresponds to another, especially at the end of words
have a similar ending sound

Examples

'Cat' and 'hat' both rhyme with each other.
In poetry, children often learn words that rhyme to help remember lines.
These two lines don't rhyme, so the poem sounds awkward.

Synset Examples

‘hat’ and ‘cat’ rhyme

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sound alike - to sound similar or identical

Etymology

From Old French *rime* (series, sequence), from Latin *rhythmus* (measured flow, rhythm).
