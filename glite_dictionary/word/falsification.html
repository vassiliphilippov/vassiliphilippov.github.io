falsification

1. NOUN

/ ˌfælsɪfəˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɔːlsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of proving that something is false or incorrect
the act of proving something false

Examples

The scientist's experiments led to the falsification of the previous theory.
Careful analysis resulted in the falsification of several long-held beliefs.
Peer review is an important step that can lead to the falsification of flawed research.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disproof - the act of showing that something is false

Etymology

From Latin 'falsificatio', from 'falsificare' (to falsify).

3. NOUN

/ ˌfælsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɔːlsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
The act of deliberately altering, distorting, or misrepresenting facts or evidence.
deliberate misrepresentation of facts

Examples

The investigation uncovered deliberate falsification of financial records.
The scientist lost her credibility after accusations of falsification in her research data.
Laws are in place to punish the falsification of important documents.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fabrication - the act of inventing false information
  • Antonym: truthfulness - quality of being true or honest

Etymology

From Latin falsificatio (falsifying), from falsificare (to falsify).

4. NOUN

/ ˌfɔlsəfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfɔːlsɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of making something false, especially by altering documents or information fraudulently or counterfeiting
fraudulent alteration or counterfeiting

Examples

The accountant's falsification of records led to a major financial scandal.
Laws impose serious penalties for the falsification of official documents.
Scientific progress depends on resisting the falsification of data.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forgery - the crime of falsely making or altering a document

Etymology

From Latin falsificatio (act of falsifying), from falsificare ('to falsify').
