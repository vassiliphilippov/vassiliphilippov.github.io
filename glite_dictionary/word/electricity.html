electricity

1. NOUN

/ ɪˌlɛkˈtrɪsəti / (AmE), / ɪˌlɛkˈtrɪsəti / (BrE)
a form of energy produced by the movement of electrons, widely used to power devices, machines, and lighting
a form of energy from moving electric charge

Examples

During the thunderstorm, the electricity in our house went out for hours.
Many household appliances would not work without electricity.
Solar panels can generate electricity from sunlight.

Synset Examples

they built a car that runs on electricity
The power went out around midnight

Related Terms

  • Synonym: power - energy that is used to provide light, heating, or to work machines

Etymology

From Latin 'ēlectricus', meaning 'produced by amber', via New Latin 'ēlectricitas'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪˌlɛkˈtrɪsəti / (AmE), / ɪlˌɛkˈtrɪsɪti / (BrE)
a strong, shared feeling of excitement or tension in a group or situation
intense shared excitement or tension

Examples

You could feel the electricity in the room as the finalists waited for the announcement.
There was a real sense of electricity between the two leading actors in the play.
The crowd's electricity was palpable as the band walked onto the stage.

Synset Examples

the stage crackled with electricity whenever she was on it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excitement - a feeling of great enthusiasm and eagerness
  • Synonym: tension - mental or emotional strain or suspense

Etymology

From Greek elektron (amber), source of the concept due to static electric charge observed on amber.

3. NOUN

/ ɪˌlɛkˈtrɪsəti / (AmE), / ɪˌlɛkˈtrɪsɪti / (BrE)
A form of energy resulting from the existence and movement of charged particles such as electrons or protons, which can be static or flow as a current.
energy from charged particles

Examples

The power outage left the entire neighborhood without electricity for several hours.
Modern households rely on electricity to operate appliances and lighting.
During the experiment, the students observed how electricity flows through a simple circuit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: current - flow of electric charge
  • Degree Lower: static electricity - electric charge at rest

Etymology

From New Latin *ēlectricus* (like amber) + English noun suffix *-ity*, influenced by the earlier term 'electric'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
