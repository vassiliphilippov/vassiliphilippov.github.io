derivative

1. NOUN

/ dəˈrɪv.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈrɪv.ə.tɪv / (BrE)
A word that is formed from another word, usually by adding a prefix or suffix or through other morphological processes.
word formed from another word

Examples

‘Electricity’ is a derivative of ‘electric’.
Many scientific terms are derivatives of Latin or Greek words.
The word ‘happiness’ is a derivative of ‘happy’.

Synset Examples

‘electricity’ is a derivative of ‘electric’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: derived - formed or developed from something else; not original (linguistic sense)

Etymology

From Medieval Latin derivativus, from Latin derivare (to derive).

2. NOUN

/ dəˈrɪvətɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈrɪvətɪv / (BrE)
a quantity representing the rate at which a function changes at a particular point, found by the process of differentiation in calculus
mathematical rate of change in calculus

Examples

The derivative of the function at this point represents its slope.
To solve the problem, you first need to compute the derivative of the equation.
Physicists often use the derivative to analyze rates of change in motion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: differential - infinitesimal change in a function, related to derivatives in calculus

Etymology

From Medieval Latin derivativus, from Latin derivatus, past participle of derivare (to draw off).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06024079-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈrɪv.ə.tɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈrɪv.ə.tɪv / (BrE)
a financial instrument whose value depends on the value of another asset, such as a stock, bond, or commodity
financial instrument based on another asset

Examples

Many investors use derivatives to hedge against changes in currency values.
The bank was criticized for its heavy trading in complex derivative products.
Understanding how a derivative works is crucial for anyone entering financial markets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: futures - contracts to buy or sell assets at a future date
  • Synonym: option - a financial contract giving the right to buy or sell an asset

Etymology

From Medieval Latin derivativus, from Latin derivare (to derive), influenced by finance in the late 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06492177-n
Synset POS: n

4. ADJ

/ dəˈrɪvətɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈrɪvətɪv / (BrE)
(of something, especially a work of art or literature) imitative of the work of another person, and lacking originality.
lacking originality; imitative

Examples

The novel was criticized as a derivative work that offered little new to readers.
Her painting style was considered too derivative to stand out in the competition.
Many pop songs today are accused of being derivative rather than innovative.

Synset Examples

a derivative process
a highly derivative prose style

Related Terms

  • Antonym: original - new or inventive, not copied or imitative

Etymology

From Medieval Latin derivativus, from Latin derivare (to derive).
