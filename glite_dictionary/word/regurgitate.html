to regurgitate

1. VERB

/ rɪˈɡɝːdʒɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡɜːdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of a liquid, especially in a medical or scientific context) to flow or rush back into a place, especially in the wrong direction.
to flow or rush back (medical/scientific)
medical

Examples

In certain heart conditions, blood may regurgitate into the chamber it just left.
Valve defects can cause fluid to regurgitate rather than flow in the proper direction.
If the structure is damaged, some contents might regurgitate unexpectedly.

Synset Examples

The blood regurgitates into the heart ventricle

Etymology

From Latin 'regurgitare' (to overflow or surge back), from 're-' (back) + 'gurgitare' (to engulf, flood).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02073324-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈɡɜrdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡɜːdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to repeat information from memory, especially without understanding or originality
repeat memorized information

Examples

Some students simply regurgitate facts for the test without truly grasping the concepts.
He could regurgitate the entire speech word for word from memory.
During the discussion, she regurgitated arguments she had read online without adding any personal insight.

Synset Examples

For the exam, you must be able to regurgitate the information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parrot - to repeat mechanically without understanding

Etymology

From Latin regurgitat-, past participle of regurgitare 'to overflow, gush back'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00961210-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈɡɝdʒəˌteɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡɜːdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
(of some animals, especially birds) to bring previously swallowed food back up into the mouth or throat, typically to feed young.
bring up swallowed food to feed young

Examples

Some bird species regurgitate food to feed their chicks during the early stages of life.
Parent penguins often regurgitate partially digested fish for their young.
When food is scarce, flamingos regurgitate a nutrient-rich liquid for their offspring.

Synset Examples

many birds feed their young by regurgitating what they have swallowed and carried to the nest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vomit - to eject stomach contents through the mouth
  • Antonym: swallow - to cause or allow (something) to pass down the throat

Etymology

From Latin regurgitat-, past participle of regurgitare (to overflow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01180632-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ rɪˈɡɜrdʒɪˌteɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡɜːdʒɪteɪt / (BrE)
to eject the contents of the stomach through the mouth; to vomit
eject stomach contents; vomit

Examples

The patient began to regurgitate his lunch unexpectedly during the examination.
Many birds regurgitate food to feed their young.
She felt nauseous and had to regurgitate after eating spoiled seafood.

Synset Examples

After drinking too much, the students vomited
He purged continuously
The patient regurgitated the food we gave him last night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vomit - to eject the contents of the stomach through the mouth
  • Synonym: retch - to make the sound and action of vomiting

Etymology

From Latin regurgitare (to overflow, to flow back), from re- (back) + gurges (whirlpool).
