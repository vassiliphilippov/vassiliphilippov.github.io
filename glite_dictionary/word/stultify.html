to stultify

1. VERB

/ ˈstʌltɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈstʌltɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to cause someone to appear foolish or absurd; to make someone seem ridiculous
cause to appear foolish

Examples

His constant interruptions only served to stultify his position during the debate.
She worried that asking basic questions might stultify her in front of her colleagues.
The complicated procedure could easily stultify an otherwise intelligent participant.

Synset Examples

He stultified himself by contradicting himself and being inconsistent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to humiliate - make someone feel ashamed or foolish
  • Antonym: to dignify - make someone or something seem worthy or impressive

Etymology

From Latin stultificare (‘to make foolish’), from stultus (‘foolish’) + facere (‘to make’).

2. VERB

/ ˈstʌltɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈstʌltɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to prove someone to be of unsound mind or to render someone incompetent, especially in a legal context; to make someone appear foolish or absurd
prove someone incompetent or of unsound mind
legal

Examples

In rare legal cases, the defense may attempt to stultify the defendant in order to challenge a contract.
The policy seemed designed to stultify innovation rather than encourage it.
She feared that admitting such an error would stultify her in front of her colleagues.

Synset Examples

nobody is legally allowed to stultify himself

Etymology

From Latin stultus (foolish) + facere (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01805062-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈstʌltɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈstʌltɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to lose energy, enthusiasm, or effectiveness; to make useless or ineffective
make ineffective or useless

Examples

The tedious routine at work can stultify even the most creative employees.
Heavy regulations may stultify a company’s ability to innovate.
Her constant criticism began to stultify his enthusiasm for the project.

Synset Examples

This measure crippled our efforts
Their behavior stultified the boss's hard work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cripple - to cause complete or severe loss of function or effectiveness
  • Synonym: hamper - to make progress or movement difficult
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to make stronger or more effective

Etymology

From Latin stultificare, from stultus (foolish) + facere (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00226258-v
Synset POS: v
