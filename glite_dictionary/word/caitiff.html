caitiff

1. NOUN

/ ˈkeɪ.tɪf / (AmE), / ˈkeɪ.tɪf / (BrE)
A cowardly and despicable person; someone regarded with contempt for their lack of courage or morals.
cowardly, despicable person
archaic

Examples

Many in the court saw him as a mere caitiff, undeserving of either trust or respect.
In the old story, the caitiff betrayed his comrades and fled the battlefield.
No noble soul would commit such acts as that caitiff did in secret.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coward - a person who lacks courage
  • Antonym: hero - a person noted for courageous acts or nobility of character

Etymology

From Old French *caitif* (captive, wretch), from Latin *captive* (captive).

2. ADJ

/ ˈkeɪ.tɪf / (AmE), / ˈkeɪ.tɪf / (BrE)
Despicably mean and cowardly; contemptibly base or craven.
despicably mean and cowardly
archaic

Examples

The knight refused to yield to the caitiff foe who beset him with treachery.
He was reviled as a caitiff for betraying his comrades in their time of need.
Many a caitiff heart quailed before the king’s stern gaze.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cowardly - lacking courage
  • Antonym: brave - showing courage

Etymology

From Old French 'caitif' (captive, wretched), from Latin 'captivus' (captive).
