Achaean

1. NOUN

/ əˈkiːən / (AmE), / əˈkiːən / (BrE)
A member of the ancient Greek people who lived in the region of Achaea or, more broadly, any Greek in the Homeric period.
ancient Greek inhabitant of Achaea

Examples

The Achaeans played a central role in the stories of the Iliad.
Many legends describe the conflicts between the Achaeans and the Trojans.
Archaeological sites in Achaea provide insights into the lives of the ancient Achaeans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Greek - a native or inhabitant of Greece
  • Antonym: Trojan - an inhabitant of ancient Troy (Homeric period; literary contrast with Achaean)

Etymology

From Latin Achaeus, from Ancient Greek Akhaiós (Ἀχαιός), meaning 'Achaean'.

3. NOUN

/ əˈkiən / (AmE), / əˈkiːən / (BrE)
a member of a group of prehistoric Greeks forming one of four major ethnic divisions, living on the Greek mainland, Crete, and Cyprus from the 16th to 12th centuries BC
member of a prehistoric Greek ethnic group

Examples

The ancient Achaeans played a significant role in early Greek civilization.
According to Homer, the Achaeans besieged Troy for ten years.
Archaeological evidence of Achaean settlements has been found in both Crete and the Greek mainland.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Ἀχαιός (*Akhaiós*), the mythological ancestor of the Achaeans.
