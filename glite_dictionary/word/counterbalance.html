counterbalance

1. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊn.tɚˌbæl.əns / (AmE), / ˈkaʊn.təˌbæl.əns / (BrE)
a weight that balances another weight, often used to offset forces or loads
a balancing weight

Examples

The elevator uses a heavy counterbalance to reduce the energy required to lift and lower the car.
A counterbalance was added to the crane to ensure stability during operation.
In old clocks, a counterbalance is used to regulate the movement of the pendulum.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: balance - a state in which opposing forces or influences are balanced

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman 'countrebalancer', from 'countrer-' (counter-) + 'balancer' (to balance).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈkaʊntərˌbæl.əns / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntəbæl.əns / (BrE)
a weight or force that offsets or compensates for another; something that has an effect opposite to another to maintain balance or equilibrium
thing that offsets or balances another

Examples

The new tax policy was designed as a counterbalance to rising income inequality.
Weights were added to the elevator as a counterbalance to ensure smooth operation.
Charity work can serve as a counterbalance to the demands of a high-stress job.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterweight - a weight used to balance another weight
  • Synonym: offset - something that serves to counterbalance or compensate
  • Antonym: imbalance - a lack of proportion or relation between corresponding things

Etymology

From 'counter-' (against) + 'balance', modeled on French 'contrebalancer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13312212-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈkaʊntərˌbæləns / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntəbæləns / (BrE)
to offset or compensate for something so that it has a neutral or desired effect
to offset or compensate for

Examples

The company implemented new policies to counterbalance economic losses from the previous year.
Good nutrition can counterbalance the negative effects of stress on the body.
Engineers must counterbalance the increased weight with stronger materials in the design.

Synset Examples

engineers will work to correct the effects of air resistance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compensate - to make up for something, especially a loss or disadvantage
  • Synonym: offset - to balance the effect of something, so that there is no advantage or disadvantage

Etymology

From Middle French 'contresbalance', from contre- (against) + balance (scales for weighing).

4. VERB

/ ˌkaʊn.tɚˈbæl.əns / (AmE), / ˌkaʊn.təˈbæl.əns / (BrE)
to have an equal effect on something in the opposite direction, so that it balances or neutralizes it
to offset or balance by opposing force

Examples

The benefits of the new law counterbalance the potential drawbacks.
She tried to counterbalance her stress with regular exercise and meditation.
Stricter regulations may counterbalance the risks posed by emerging technologies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: offset - to counteract by having an opposing effect
  • Synonym: to neutralize - to make ineffective by applying an opposite force or effect
  • Antonym: to intensify - to make something stronger or more extreme

Etymology

From 'counter-' (against) and 'balance', modeled after Medieval Latin 'contrabalançare'.

5. VERB

/ ˈkaʊntərˌbæl.əns / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntəbæl.əns / (BrE)
To have an effect that opposes and reduces the impact of something else; to act as a counteracting force.
offset or neutralize by opposing action

Examples

Strict regulations are needed to counterbalance the risks posed by new technologies.
A healthy diet can help counterbalance the effects of a stressful lifestyle.
His optimism counterbalanced her doubts about the project.

Synset Examples

This will counteract the foolish actions of my colleagues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to counteract - to act against something to reduce its effect
  • Synonym: to neutralize - to cancel the effect of something

Etymology

From 'counter-' (against) + 'balance' (to poise or weigh), first recorded in the late 16th century.
