thane

1. NOUN

/ θeɪn / (AmE), / θeɪn / (BrE)
a man, especially in Anglo-Saxon England, ranking above an ordinary freeman and below a noble, often granted land in exchange for military service
Anglo-Saxon nobleman below a earl

Examples

In Macbeth, the character is named the thane of Cawdor by King Duncan.
During Anglo-Saxon times, a thane was granted land in return for military service.
The local thane held significant influence over the surrounding villages.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: noble - a person of high rank or aristocratic status
  • Degree Lower: freeman - a person who is not a serf or slave and who is free

Etymology

Old English 'þegn' meaning servant, attendant, later also a noble title; related to Old Norse 'þegn'.

2. NOUN

/ θeɪn / (AmE), / θeɪn / (BrE)
a man, especially in Scotland or Anglo-Saxon England, who held land granted by the king or by a nobleman, ranking below an earl and above an ordinary freeman; a feudal lord or baron.
feudal lord or baron (historical)
archaic

Examples

The Scottish thane pledged allegiance to his king.
In Shakespeare's Macbeth, the title of thane of Cawdor plays a central role.
During the Middle Ages, a thane controlled land and commanded soldiers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: baron - a member of the lowest order of the British nobility

Etymology

Old English *þegn* (servant, attendant, retainer), later referring to a man holding land in return for services.
