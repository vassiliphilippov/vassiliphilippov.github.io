to depress

1. VERB

/ dɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to cause something, especially prices or markets, to fall or become lower
cause prices or values to fall

Examples

The oversupply of wheat helped depress prices worldwide.
A sudden drop in demand can depress the entire stock market.
Heavy exports of steel may depress domestic prices for months.

Synset Examples

The glut of oil depressed gas prices

Related Terms

  • Antonym: raise - to cause prices or values to increase

Etymology

From Latin 'deprimere', meaning 'to press down'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01977946-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to press down or to push something, especially a button or key, downwards
press down; push a button or key downwards

Examples

Please depress the red button to reset the machine.
You need to depress the clutch pedal before shifting gears.
The elevator won't move until you depress the floor selection button.

Synset Examples

Depress the space key

Related Terms

  • Synonym: press - to push something firmly, typically with a finger

Etymology

From Latin 'deprimere', from 'de-' (down) + 'premere' (to press).

WordNet ID: oewn-01577597-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ dɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to make someone feel sad and without hope or enthusiasm
make someone feel sad or down

Examples

The endless rain can depress even the most optimistic people.
Hearing about the layoffs really depressed her.
It's easy to feel depressed during the long winter months.

Synset Examples

These news depressed her
The bad state of her child's health demoralizes her

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to cheer up - to make or become more cheerful or happy
  • Synonym: to discourage - to cause someone to lose confidence or enthusiasm

Etymology

From Latin 'deprimere' (to press down), from 'de-' (down) + 'premere' (to press).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01818363-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ dɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to cause something to drop, sink, or become lower in level
cause to drop or sink

Examples

The drought greatly depressed the water levels in the lake.
Freezing temperatures can depress the pressure inside a tire.
High taxes may depress business investment in the region.

Synset Examples

The lack of rain had depressed the water level in the reservoir

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lower - to make or become less in amount, level, or value

Etymology

From Latin depressus (pressed down), past participle of deprimere.

5. VERB

/ dɪˈprɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈprɛs / (BrE)
to reduce the level, strength, or activity of something
reduce activity or force

Examples

The government raised taxes to depress consumer spending.
High interest rates can depress investment in the housing market.
Certain drugs may depress the activity of the nervous system.

Synset Examples

The rising inflation depressed the economy

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to stimulate - to increase the activity or force of something

From Latin deprimere (to press down), from de- (down) + premere (to press).
