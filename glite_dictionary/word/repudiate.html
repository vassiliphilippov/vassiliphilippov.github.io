to repudiate

1. VERB

/ rɪˈpjuːdiˌeɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈpjuːdɪeɪt / (BrE)
to refuse to accept, acknowledge, or recognize as valid or true
refuse to accept or acknowledge

Examples

He chose to repudiate the allegations made against him in the press.
The company may repudiate any agreement that is not legally binding.
Many historians repudiate the theory as lacking sufficient evidence.

Synset Examples

The woman repudiated the divorce settlement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reject - to refuse to accept, consider, or submit to something
  • Antonym: to accept - to consent to receive or undertake something offered

Etymology

From Latin repudiatus, past participle of repudiare 'to cast off, reject'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00687940-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈpjuːdiˌeɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈpjuːdieɪt / (BrE)
to refuse to accept, acknowledge, or be associated with someone or something; to reject or disown formally
formally reject or disown

Examples

After the scandal, the politician was quick to repudiate any involvement.
His family chose to repudiate his actions and cut all ties with him.
The organization decided to repudiate its controversial past.

Synset Examples

She renounced her husband
The parents repudiated their son

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reject - to refuse to accept or consider
  • Antonym: embrace - to accept or support willingly and enthusiastically

Etymology

From Latin repudiare (to cast off, reject), from repudium (divorce, rejection).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deny - to declare something is not true
  • Antonym: to accept - to consent to receive or believe

From Latin repudiare (to reject, cast off), from repudium (divorce, rejection).
