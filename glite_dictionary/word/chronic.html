chronic

1. ADJ

/ ˈkrɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / ˈkrɒn.ɪk / (BrE)
(of an illness or problem) lasting for a long time or constantly recurring; persistent.
long-lasting or recurring (especially illness)

Examples

She suffered from chronic back pain that made everyday activities difficult.
The country has faced a chronic shortage of clean water for decades.
His chronic lateness eventually led to disciplinary action at work.

Synset Examples

chronic indigestion
a chronic shortage of funds
a chronic invalid

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acute - (of a disease or condition) severe but of short duration

Etymology

From Greek *khronikos* (of time), from *khronos* (time).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00045343-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈkrɑː.nɪk / (AmE), / ˈkrɒn.ɪk / (BrE)
(of a person or behavior) having a particular bad habit or characteristic for a long time and difficult to change.
consistent or habitual (often negative)

Examples

He's a chronic liar and no one trusts what he says anymore.
The company has a chronic habit of missing deadlines.
She's a chronic worrier, always expecting the worst.

Synset Examples

a chronic smoker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: habitual - done or doing constantly or as a habit

Etymology

From Greek *khronikos* (concerning time), from *khronos* (time).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00491759-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈkrɑːnɪk / (AmE), / ˈkrɒnɪk / (BrE)
(of a problem, illness, or situation) lasting for a long period or constantly recurring.
long-lasting or recurring (problem, illness, situation)

Examples

He struggled with chronic back pain for most of his adult life.
The city faces chronic housing shortages that have lasted for decades.
Her chronic tardiness eventually led to her losing the job.

Synset Examples

chronic money problems

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acute - (of a disease or problem) severe but short-term

Etymology

From Greek *khronikos* (of time), from *khronos* (time).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01441837-s
Synset POS: s
