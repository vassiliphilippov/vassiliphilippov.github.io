Mycoplasmataceae

1. NOUN

/ ˌmaɪkoʊˌplæzməˈteɪsiː / (AmE), / ˌmʌɪkəʊˌplazməˈtasɪiː / (BrE)
A family of pleomorphic, Gram-negative, nonmotile microorganisms that are parasitic in mammals and are similar to both viruses and bacteria.
family of parasitic bacteria-like microorganisms
medical

Examples

The Mycoplasmataceae family includes several species that cause respiratory infections in humans and animals.
Scientists studied the structure of Mycoplasmataceae to develop new antibiotics.
Some Mycoplasmataceae are known for lacking a cell wall, making them unique among bacteria.

Etymology

From New Latin, combining 'Mycoplasma' (the genus name) with the suffix '-aceae' denoting a family.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmaɪ.kəˌplæz.məˈteɪ.siˌiː / (AmE), / ˌmaɪ.kəˌplæz.məˈteɪ.siː / (BrE)
A family of pleomorphic, Gram-negative, nonmotile microorganisms similar to both viruses and bacteria, which are parasitic in mammals.
family of parasitic microorganisms
medical

Examples

The Mycoplasmataceae have unique structures that distinguish them from other bacterial families.
Scientists studied the role of Mycoplasmataceae in respiratory infections among cattle.
Members of the Mycoplasmataceae are known for lacking a rigid cell wall.

Etymology

From Greek 'mykes' (fungus) and 'plasma' (formed, molded), plus the taxonomic suffix '-aceae'.
