self-inductance

1. NOUN

/ sɛlf ɪnˈdʌktəns / (AmE), / sɛlf ɪnˈdʌktəns / (BrE)
the property of a circuit, especially a coil, by which a changing electric current induces an electromotive force in itself; measured as the ratio of induced electromotive force to the rate of change of current, and expressed in henries
property of a circuit to induce emf in itself

Examples

The self-inductance of the coil determines how much opposition it provides to a changing current.
Engineers calculated the self-inductance to ensure the circuit operated safely.
Increasing the number of turns in a solenoid raises its self-inductance.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mutual inductance - inductance between two separate circuits or coils

Etymology

From 'self' + 'inductance', reflecting the circuit inducing voltage in itself.
