moodiness

1. NOUN

/ ˈmuːdinəs / (AmE), / ˈmuːdɪnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of being prone to frequent changes in mood, especially sulky or gloomy moods
tendency to frequent mood changes, often sulky or gloomy

Examples

His moodiness made it difficult for his friends to know how he would react each day.
The rainy weather seemed to intensify her moodiness and reluctance to socialize.
Parents sometimes struggle with the moodiness of teenagers during adolescence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: irritability - the tendency to become easily annoyed or angered
  • Antonym: cheerfulness - the quality of being happy and optimistic

Etymology

From 'moody' + '-ness'; 'moody' from Middle English 'modie', meaning having a particular mood or temper.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmuːdi.nəs / (AmE), / ˈmuːdɪnɪs / (BrE)
the quality of having frequent changes in mood, especially becoming easily gloomy or irritable
tendency to change moods often

Examples

Her moodiness made it difficult for her friends to predict how she would react to news.
The teenager's moodiness was attributed to the pressures of school and social life.
Despite his moodiness, he remained a loyal and caring friend.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: irritability - the quality of being easily annoyed or angered
  • Antonym: cheerfulness - the quality of being happy and optimistic

Etymology

From 'moody' + '-ness', with 'moody' from Old English *mōdig* (brave, high-spirited, later 'disposed to mood').
