concretion

1. NOUN

/ kənˈkriːʃən / (AmE), / kənˈkriːʃən / (BrE)
an increase in the density of a substance or material, especially through compacting or solidification.
increase in density or compacting

Examples

The concretion of sediments in the rock layer led to its hardened structure.
Therapies aim to prevent further concretion of material in the arteries.
Over time, the concretion of mineral deposits made the cave walls much denser.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consolidation - the process of making something physically stronger or more solid
  • Antonym: rarefaction - the process of becoming less dense

Etymology

From Latin 'concretio', from 'concrescere' (to grow together, make hard).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈkriːʃən / (AmE), / kənˈkriːʃən / (BrE)
the formation of a stonelike mass in a body organ, such as the kidneys or bladder
formation of a stonelike mass in the body
medical

Examples

Ultrasound imaging revealed a concretion in the patient's gallbladder.
The formation of a concretion in the kidney can lead to severe pain and urinary blockage.
Doctors removed a large concretion during the surgery.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: calculus - a stonelike concretion formed in the body, especially in the kidneys or bladder

Etymology

From Latin 'concretio' (growing together, compact mass).

3. NOUN

/ kənˈkriːʃən / (AmE), / kənˈkriːʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the union or coming together of different things into a single body, form, or group; the growing together of separate parts
union or merging into a single group or form

Examples

The concretion of different political parties formed a new alliance.
In geology, a concretion occurs when minerals grow together into a solid mass.
Medical conditions can sometimes result from the concretion of tissues that are normally separate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aggregation - grouping of individuals or things into a whole
  • Antonym: separation - the act of moving things apart

Etymology

From Latin 'concretio' (a growing together), from 'concrescere' (to grow together).
