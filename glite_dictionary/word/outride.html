to outride

1. VERB

/ ˌaʊtˈraɪd / (AmE), / ˌaʊtˈraɪd / (BrE)
to survive, endure, or get through a difficult situation or period successfully
survive or endure a difficulty

Examples

The small company managed to outride the recent economic crisis.
It's important to stay calm and outride the turbulence during difficult times.
They hoped their friendship would outride any misunderstandings and challenges.

Synset Examples

ride out the storm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to survive - to remain alive or continue to exist, especially in spite of hardship
  • Synonym: weather - to come safely through a difficult experience or period

Etymology

From out- (beyond) + ride; originally meaning to ride faster or beyond, later extended figuratively to enduring hardship.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02625202-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ aʊtˈraɪd / (AmE), / aʊtˈraɪd / (BrE)
to ride better, faster, or further than someone or something else
ride better or faster than others

Examples

As the group approached the final hill, she managed to outride all her rivals.
No one could outride the old cowboy on his trusty horse.
The young cyclist trained hard so he could outride his competition in the next race.

Synset Examples

The champion bicyclist outrode all his competitors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outperform - to do better than someone

Etymology

From out- (outside, beyond) + ride, paralleling other verbs like outdo.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01962137-v
Synset POS: v
