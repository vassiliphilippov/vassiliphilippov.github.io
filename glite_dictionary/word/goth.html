Goth

1. NOUN

/ ɡɑθ / (AmE), / ɡɒθ / (BrE)
a member of a Germanic people who invaded the Roman Empire in the 3rd to 5th centuries AD, playing a major role in its downfall
member of an ancient Germanic people

Examples

The Goths played a significant role in the decline of the Western Roman Empire.
In the fifth century, the Goth Alaric famously sacked Rome.
Historians study how the migration of the Goths changed European history.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Visigoth - member of the western branch of the Goths
  • Synonym: Ostrogoth - member of the eastern branch of the Goths

Etymology

From Latin Gothi (plural), of uncertain origin, possibly related to a Germanic tribal name.

2. NOUN

/ ɡɑθ / (AmE), / ɡɒθ / (BrE)
(Archaic) A crude, uncouth, or ill-bred person who lacks culture or refinement.
uncouth or ill-bred person (archaic)
archaic

Examples

The critics dismissed him as a mere Goth with no appreciation for literature or art.
In the refined company, his manners revealed him to be something of a Goth.
Some Victorians called their less cultured colleagues Goth as a playful insult.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barbarian - an uncivilized or uncultured person
  • Synonym: philistine - a person who is indifferent to culture or the arts
  • Antonym: gentleman - a courteous, cultured, or refined man

Etymology

From the name of the East Germanic people, the Goths, used figuratively for a barbarous person.
