Plataea

1. PROPN

/ pləˈtiːə / (AmE), / pləˈtiːə / (BrE)
an ancient town in Boeotia, Greece, famous as the site of a decisive battle in 479 BC where Greek forces defeated the Persians.
ancient town in Greece; site of Greek-Persian battle

Examples

Historically, Plataea is known for the pivotal battle that ended the Persian invasion of Greece.
The ruins of Plataea attract historians interested in ancient Greek warfare.
Many scholars have written about the significance of Plataea in classical history.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Πλάταια (Plátaia).

2. PROPN

/ pləˈtiːə / (AmE), / pləˈtiːə / (BrE)
the site of a decisive battle in 479 BC where the Greek city-states defeated the Persian army, marking the end of the Persian invasion of Greece
site of major Greek victory over Persians, 479 BC

Examples

The Battle of Plataea was a turning point in the Greco-Persian Wars.
Historians often cite Plataea as the final blow to Persian ambitions in Greece.
After their victory at Plataea, the Greeks secured their independence from Persian rule.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Battle of Marathon - a major Greek victory over Persia in 490 BC
  • Synonym: Thermopylae - site of a famous last stand of the Greeks against Persia in 480 BC

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Πλαταιαί (Plataiaí), the name of the city-state near the battlefield.
