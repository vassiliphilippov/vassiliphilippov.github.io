Ceratodus

1. NOUN

/ sɛˈrætəˌdəs / (AmE), / sɛˈrætədəs / (BrE)
an extinct genus of lungfish known from fossil records, particularly from the Mesozoic era
extinct genus of lungfish

Examples

Fossils of Ceratodus have been found in rocks dating back millions of years.
Many paleontologists study Ceratodus to understand the evolution of lungfish.
The distinctive teeth of Ceratodus help scientists identify its fossils.

Etymology

From Greek keras (horn) + odous (tooth), referring to the hornlike shape of the teeth.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛrəˈtoʊdəs / (AmE), / ˌsɛrəˈtəʊdəs / (BrE)
A genus of extinct lungfishes, formerly found in various parts of the world, and the type genus of the family Ceratodontidae.
genus of extinct lungfishes

Examples

The fossils of Ceratodus have provided valuable insight into the evolution of lungfishes.
Some species of Ceratodus existed more than 100 million years ago.
The distinctive tooth plates of Ceratodus are often found in Cretaceous rock formations.

Etymology

From Greek 'keras' (horn) + 'odous, odont-' (tooth), referring to the hornlike teeth of the fish.
