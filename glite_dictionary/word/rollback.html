rollback

1. NOUN

/ ˈroʊlˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈrəʊlbæk / (BrE)
a reduction of prices, wages, or rates to a previous or earlier level, especially as an official measure
return to lower price or rate

Examples

The grocery store announced a rollback on several essential items to help customers save money.
After much public pressure, the government implemented a rollback of the recent tax increases.
The company is offering a temporary rollback of fuel prices to attract more drivers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reduction - the act of making something less or smaller
  • Antonym: increase - an instance of growing or making greater

Etymology

From the phrasal verb 'roll back', meaning to reverse or reduce; first used in U.S. English in the mid-20th century in economic and policy contexts.

2. NOUN

/ ˈroʊlˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈrəʊlˌbæk / (BrE)
the act of driving back an enemy or causing them to withdraw, especially in military or strategic contexts
forcing an enemy to withdraw

Examples

The military launched a massive rollback of enemy forces along the border.
Officials described the operation as a successful rollback of the insurgents' advances.
The general planned a strategic rollback to reclaim lost territory.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: retreat - the act of withdrawing forces from enemy contact

Etymology

From 'roll' + 'back', originally used in U.S. military and political jargon (mid-20th century).
