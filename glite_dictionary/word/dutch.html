Dutch

1. ADJ

/ dʌtʃ / (AmE), / dʌtʃ / (BrE)
of or relating to the Netherlands, its people, or its culture
relating to the Netherlands

Examples

The exhibition features Dutch paintings from the seventeenth century.
She enjoys Dutch cheese and other traditional foods.
The team wore Dutch orange jerseys during the world cup.

Synset Examples

Dutch painting
Dutch painters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Netherlands - a country in Western Europe, also called Holland

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *duutsch*, meaning 'of the people, vernacular,' historically referring to the Germanic peoples.

2. NOUN

/ dʌtʃ / (AmE), / dʌtʃ / (BrE)
the people of the Netherlands; the inhabitants or natives of the Netherlands collectively
the people of the Netherlands

Examples

The Dutch are renowned for their expertise in water management.
Many Dutch traditions date back hundreds of years.
The Dutch celebrate King’s Day with lively festivals throughout the country.

Synset Examples

the Dutch are famous for their tulips

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Netherlands - the country in northwestern Europe, also called Holland

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'duutsch', from Old High German 'diutisc' meaning 'popular' or 'of the people'.

3. NOUN

/ dʌtʃ / (AmE), / dʌtʃ / (BrE)
the West Germanic language spoken in the Netherlands and parts of Belgium and Suriname
West Germanic language of the Netherlands

Examples

Many people in Belgium also speak Dutch as their first language.
She studied Dutch in college to prepare for her trip to Amsterdam.
The Dutch language uses a lot of compound words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: German - West Germanic language of Germany and Austria
  • Degree Higher: Dutchman - a native or inhabitant of the Netherlands

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'duutsch', 'dietsch' (vernacular), ultimately from Proto-Germanic *þiudiskaz (of the people).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06965334-n
Synset POS: n
