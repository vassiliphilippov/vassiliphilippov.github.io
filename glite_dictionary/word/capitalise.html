to capitalise

1. VERB

/ ˈkæpɪtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkæpɪtəlaɪz / (BrE)
to use a situation or opportunity to gain an advantage or profit
take advantage of an opportunity
british

Examples

The company plans to capitalise on the growing demand for renewable energy.
She tried to capitalise on her opponent's mistake during the debate.
Many businesses capitalise quickly on changing consumer trends.

Synset Examples

he is capitalizing on her mistake
she took advantage of his absence to meet her lover

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to capitalize - to use a situation to gain advantage (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin 'capitalis' (of the head, principal), via French 'capitaliser'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02284617-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkæpɪtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkapɪtəlaɪz / (BrE)
to write or print a letter or word using capital letters
write in capital letters
british

Examples

Please capitalise the first letter of every sentence.
She accidentally capitalised the entire word in her email.
Double-check the report to make sure all titles are capitalised correctly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to capitalize - to write in capital letters (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin 'capitalis' (of the head), via French and late Latin forms; see also 'capital + ise'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01696161-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈkæpɪtəlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkapɪtəlaɪz / (BrE)
to convert (a company's reserve funds) into capital.
convert reserves into capital

Examples

After the merger, the company decided to capitalise its substantial reserves to fund new projects.
The board voted to capitalise part of the profits, increasing investor shares.
When firms capitalise their reserves, they strengthen their financial structure.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to capitalize - convert (a company's reserve funds) into capital (American spelling)

Etymology

From capital + -ise, influenced by Latin root 'capitalis' (principal, chief).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00161975-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈkæpɪtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkæpɪtəlaɪz / (BrE)
to provide a business with capital, using funds from investors and loans from lenders
provide capital to a business
british

Examples

The new company was capitalised by a mix of investor funding and bank loans.
If we want to expand our operations, we need to capitalise the business more effectively.
The firm capitalised itself through both equity and debt to support its growth plans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to capitalize - provide a business with capital, especially using a mix of equity and debt (US spelling)

Etymology

From capital + -ise, modeled on the noun 'capital' (wealth for investment) and the verb-forming suffix -ise/-ize.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02335738-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ ˈkæpɪtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkapɪtəlaɪz / (BrE)
to treat an expenditure as a capital asset, rather than an expense, on financial statements
treat as capital (finance)

Examples

The company decided to capitalise its investment in new equipment over five years.
Accounting rules determine when firms must capitalise certain costs rather than expense them immediately.
If you capitalise research and development spending, it appears as an asset on the balance sheet.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: expense - to record an expenditure as a cost rather than as an asset

Etymology

From capital, influenced by financial terminology; see also U.S. spelling 'capitalize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00732985-v
Synset POS: v
