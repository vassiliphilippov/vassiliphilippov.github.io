to luxuriate

1. VERB

/ lʌgˈʒʊriˌeɪt / (AmE), / lʌgˈzjʊərɪeɪt / (BrE)
to take great pleasure in something; to enjoy oneself extravagantly or abundantly
enjoy oneself greatly; take abundant pleasure

Examples

After a long day at work, she likes to luxuriate in a hot bubble bath.
They luxuriated in the comfort of the five-star hotel, enjoying every amenity.
On weekends, he enjoys luxuriating in bed with a good book and coffee.

Synset Examples

She indulges in ice cream

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to indulge - to allow oneself to enjoy pleasures, often to excess
  • Antonym: to abstain - to deliberately avoid something enjoyable

Etymology

From Latin luxuriari (to grow rank, revel in excess), from luxuria (excess, luxury).

2. VERB

/ lʌgˈʒʊriˌeɪt / (AmE), / lʌgˈzjʊərɪeɪt / (BrE)
(of plants or living things) to thrive or flourish abundantly; to grow or develop vigorously, often with rich profusion.
to grow or thrive abundantly

Examples

The tropical rainforest allowed rare orchids to luxuriate in the moist, warm air.
Grapevines luxuriate in the fertile soil of the valley each spring.
The city gardens luxuriated after a season of abundant rain and sunshine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to thrive - to grow, develop, or be successful; flourish
  • Antonym: to wither - to shrivel or decline, often due to lack of vitality

Etymology

From Latin luxuriari (be luxuriant, indulge oneself), from luxuria (luxury, excess).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ lʌɡˈʒʊriˌeɪt / (AmE), / lʌɡˈzjʊərɪeɪt / (BrE)
to enjoy oneself in a luxurious way; to take great pleasure, especially in comfort or indulgence
enjoy luxury or great pleasure

Examples

After a long week, she loved to luxuriate in a hot bubble bath.
He luxuriated in the beautiful garden, enjoying the warm sunlight and gentle breeze.
They spent the afternoon luxuriating by the hotel pool, sipping cocktails and reading novels.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to indulge - to allow oneself to enjoy the pleasure of something
  • Antonym: to abstain - to deliberately avoid or refrain from something pleasurable

Etymology

From Latin luxuriari (to be excessive, indulge), from luxuria (luxury, excess).
