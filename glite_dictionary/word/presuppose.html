to presuppose

1. VERB

/ ˌpriːsəˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌpriːsəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to assume or require something as a necessary precondition or antecedent
require as a precondition

Examples

The theory presupposes a basic understanding of quantum mechanics.
Effective communication presupposes mutual trust between the parties involved.
Her argument presupposed that everyone shared the same values.

Synset Examples

This step presupposes two prior ones

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to suppose something to be true without proof
  • Synonym: to imply - to suggest something as a logical consequence

Etymology

From Latin praesupponere, from prae- (before) + supponere (to put under, suppose).

2. VERB

/ ˌpriːsəˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌpriːsəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to assume or accept something as true or existing before beginning further reasoning or discussion; to suppose beforehand
to assume in advance

Examples

The theory seems to presuppose a basic understanding of biology.
Effective communication presupposes a shared language and common ground.
Her argument presupposed that everyone agreed with her values.

Synset Examples

I presuppose that you have done your work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assume - to suppose something is true without proof as a starting point
  • Synonym: to imply - to suggest something indirectly without stating it directly

Etymology

From Latin prae- (before) + supponere (to suppose).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00718436-v
Synset POS: v
