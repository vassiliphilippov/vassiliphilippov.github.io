to indulge

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (BrE)
to allow oneself or someone else to enjoy something, especially something considered to be a pleasure or luxury, often without restraint
allow oneself or others to enjoy fully

Examples

She tried not to indulge her cravings for chocolate every night.
Parents sometimes indulge their children by buying them too many toys.
He likes to indulge in a long, hot bath after a stressful day.

Synset Examples

The writer indulged in metaphorical language

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to restrain - to hold back or keep under control
  • Synonym: spoil - to treat with excessive indulgence

Etymology

From Latin indulgere (to be kind or yield).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (BrE)
to allow oneself to have or do something enjoyable, especially more than is good for one
enjoy something to excess

Examples

After a long week, she likes to indulge in a decadent dessert.
On vacation, we sometimes indulge ourselves by sleeping in late every morning.
He knew he shouldn't indulge his craving for chocolate, but he couldn't resist.

Synset Examples

She indulges in ice cream

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to restrain - to hold oneself back from doing something enjoyable

Etymology

From Latin 'indulgere' (to be kind or lenient).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01194178-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (BrE)
to allow someone to have or do whatever they want, especially too much or to an excessive degree
treat with excessive permissiveness

Examples

She tends to indulge her children, giving them anything they ask for.
If we indulge his every whim, he will never learn self-control.
The grandparents are always indulging the kids with sweets and presents.

Synset Examples

grandparents often pamper the children
Let's not mollycoddle our students!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to pamper - treat with excessive care or attention
  • Synonym: spoil - give a child everything they want, making them behave badly
  • Antonym: discipline - to train someone to obey rules, often by punishment

Etymology

From Latin indulgere (to be kind or lenient).

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌldʒ / (BrE)
to allow oneself or someone else to enjoy the pleasure of something, especially something considered to be a luxury or not strictly necessary
allow oneself/someone to enjoy something

Examples

She likes to indulge in a piece of chocolate after dinner.
He decided to indulge his children with an extra hour of playtime.
Even on a strict diet, she sometimes indulges in dessert.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deny - to refuse to grant or allow something
  • Synonym: to pamper - to treat with excessive care or indulgence

Etymology

From Latin *indulgere* (to be kind or yielding).
