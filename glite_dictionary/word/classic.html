classic

1. NOUN

/ ˈklæsɪk / (AmE), / ˈklasɪk / (BrE)
a work of art, literature, or other creation recognized as outstanding and of lasting significance
an outstanding and enduring work

Examples

Her first novel quickly became a classic of modern literature.
That movie is considered a classic of the horror genre.
Every holiday season, we watch the old classic It's a Wonderful Life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: masterpiece - an outstanding work of art or skill

Etymology

From Latin classicus (belonging to the highest class of citizens).

2. ADJ

/ ˈklæsɪk / (AmE), / ˈklæsɪk / (BrE)
recognized as a model of its kind; well-known and long-established in form or style
well-known and traditional in style

Examples

Her black dress was a classic choice for the elegant dinner.
The film has become a classic of the horror genre.
That building is a classic example of Victorian architecture.

Synset Examples

the classic struggle between good and evil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: classical - related to ancient Greek and Roman art or literature; traditional form or style
  • Antonym: modern - relating to the present or recent times as opposed to the remote past; opposite of traditional

Etymology

From Latin classicus (belonging to the highest class of citizens), later meaning of recognized excellence.

3. ADJ

/ ˈklæsɪk / (AmE), / ˈklæsɪk / (BrE)
Judged to be of the highest quality and outstanding of its kind, serving as a standard or model of lasting worth.
of the highest quality and lasting significance

Examples

The museum displayed a classic painting from the Renaissance period.
Her little black dress is considered a classic in the world of fashion.
Many believe that Shakespeare's works are the classic examples of English literature.

Synset Examples

a classic car
‘War and Peace’ is a classic novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: timeless - not affected by the passage of time or changes in fashion; classic
  • Synonym: iconic - widely recognized and well-established; classic
  • Antonym: modern - relating to the present or recent times; often contrasted with classic

Etymology

From Latin classicus (belonging to a class or division), especially relating to the highest class of citizens.

Examples
