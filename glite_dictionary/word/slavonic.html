Slavonic

1. NOUN

/ sləˈvɑnɪk / (AmE), / sləˈvɒnɪk / (BrE)
a branch of the Indo-European family of languages, including Russian, Polish, Czech, and others spoken in Eastern Europe and northern Asia
branch of Indo-European languages

Examples

Russian and Polish are considered Slavonic languages spoken in Eastern Europe.
The Slavonic language family includes a variety of tongues such as Czech and Bulgarian.
Linguists study the origins and development of Slavonic languages.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Slavic - the group of languages spoken by the Slavic peoples; often used interchangeably with 'Slavonic'

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'Slavonicus', from 'Slavonia' (land of the Slavs).

2. ADJ

/ sləˈvɑnɪk / (AmE), / sləˈvɒnɪk / (BrE)
of or relating to Slavic languages or the peoples who speak them.
relating to Slavic languages or peoples

Examples

The Slavonic languages include Russian, Polish, and Czech.
She studied ancient Slavonic manuscripts for her thesis.
The church offered services in Old Slavonic for traditional ceremonies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Slavic - of or relating to the Slavic peoples or languages

Etymology

From New Latin 'Slavonicus', from Medieval Latin 'Sclavonia' (Slavonia), relating to the Slavs.
