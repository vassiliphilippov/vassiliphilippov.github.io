hearsay

1. ADJ

/ ˈhɪrˌseɪ / (AmE), / ˈhɪə.seɪ / (BrE)
(of information or evidence) received from others rather than directly or first-hand.
received indirectly, not first-hand

Examples

The report was based solely on hearsay evidence, not direct observation.
Much of the so-called news he shared was just hearsay and unverified.
The judge ruled the hearsay testimony inadmissible in court.

Synset Examples

hearsay information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secondhand - obtained from others, not directly from the source

Etymology

From archaic 'hear say', meaning 'something one hears said'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhɪrˌseɪ / (AmE), / ˈhɪəseɪ / (BrE)
information received from other people that one cannot adequately verify; rumor or unverified report passed from person to person.
unverified rumor or information

Examples

Much of the news about the celebrity's private life is based on hearsay rather than direct evidence.
The court ruled the statement was inadmissible because it was considered hearsay.
Try not to trust hearsay; always check the facts before spreading information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rumor - a currently circulating story or report of uncertain or doubtful truth
  • Synonym: gossip - casual or unconstrained conversation or reports about people, typically unverified
  • Antonym: fact - a thing that is known or proved to be true

Etymology

From Middle English 'her sey' (that which is said or reported), from hear + say.
