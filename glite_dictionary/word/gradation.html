gradation

1. NOUN

/ ɡrəˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɡrəˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Linguistics) A systematic variation in the vowel sounds of a word, especially as a degree of ablaut.
systematic vowel variation; ablaut degree

Examples

The verb forms 'sing', 'sang', and 'sung' display gradation through changes in the vowel sound.
Many Indo-European languages feature gradation as part of their regular verb conjugations.
Linguists study gradation to understand patterns of sound change over time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ablaut - systematic vowel change in word forms

Etymology

From Latin gradatio (a step, a gradation), from gradus (step).

2. NOUN

/ ɡrəˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɡreɪˈdeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
a slight difference or change in degree or intensity seen as part of a continuous sequence; the relative position within such a series
relative position in a graded series

Examples

The artist carefully blended the colors to achieve subtle gradations on the canvas.
There are many gradations of responsibility within the company, each with distinct duties.
The sky displays beautiful gradations of blue as the sun sets.

Related Terms

3. NOUN

/ ɡrəˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ɡrəˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of arranging or classifying things in stages or degrees according to a scale or series
arrangement in stages or degrees

Examples

The gradation of students by ability can be a controversial topic.
The artist created a beautiful gradation of colors in her painting.
Job responsibilities are often assigned based on a clear gradation of experience and skill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gradation - a series of gradual changes or stages
  • Synonym: gradation - a change by small steps rather than large jumps

Etymology

From Latin 'gradatio', from 'gradus' meaning 'step'.
