silk

1. NOUN

/ sɪlk / (AmE), / sɪlk / (BrE)
A fine, soft, and luxurious fabric made from the threads produced by the larvae of certain insects, especially silkworms.
fine fabric from insect larvae

Examples

She wore a beautiful dress made of silk to the wedding.
The scarf felt smooth and cool, as only real silk can.
Traditional kimonos are often crafted from brightly colored silk.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: satin - a smooth, glossy fabric often made from silk or synthetic fibers
  • Antonym: cotton - a soft, white fibrous substance that surrounds the seeds of the cotton plant, used as a fabric

Etymology

From Old English 'seoloc', from Latin 'sericum', from Greek 'sērikon', of Asian origin.

2. NOUN

/ sɪlk / (AmE), / sɪlk / (BrE)
A fine, soft, lustrous fiber produced by silkworms and some other larvae, as well as spiders, used in making textiles and various products.
fine fiber from silkworms and some larvae

Examples

Traditional Chinese garments are often made of silk because of its natural shine and softness.
A spider spun a delicate web with threads of silk glistening in the morning dew.
They exported raw silk to Europe where it was woven into luxurious fabrics.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: satin - a smooth, glossy fabric, often made from silk
  • Antonym: cotton - a soft white fiber from the cotton plant, used in textiles
  • Antonym: wool - fibers from the fleece of sheep, used in textiles

Etymology

From Old English 'silc', from Latin 'sericum', from Greek 'sērikon' (silk), ultimately from the Seres, an ancient people of Asia.
