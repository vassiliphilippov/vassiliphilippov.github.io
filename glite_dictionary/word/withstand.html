to withstand

1. VERB

/ wɪðˈstænd / (AmE), / wɪðˈstænd / (BrE)
to remain undamaged or unaffected by; to resist successfully or endure through a force, difficulty, or attack
remain unaffected by; resist successfully

Examples

This material can withstand extreme temperatures.
The old fortress managed to withstand several sieges over the centuries.
She had to withstand strong criticism from her colleagues.

Synset Examples

The politician defied public opinion
The new material withstands even the greatest wear and tear
The bridge held

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to resist - to withstand the action or effect of something
  • Antonym: to succumb - to fail to withstand; to yield to a superior force

Etymology

From Old English withstandan ('stand against') from 'wiþ' (against) + 'standan' (to stand).

2. VERB

/ wɪðˈstænd / (AmE), / wɪðˈstænd / (BrE)
to remain undamaged or unaffected by something; to resist successfully
resist or stand up to

Examples

The bridge was designed to withstand earthquakes and strong winds.
He managed to withstand the intense pressure from his opponents and kept calm.
These materials are engineered to withstand extreme temperatures without deteriorating.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endure - to suffer patiently or remain firm under hardship
  • Antonym: to succumb - to fail to resist pressure or temptation

Etymology

Old English *wiþstandan* ('wiþ' meaning 'against' + 'standan' meaning 'to stand').
