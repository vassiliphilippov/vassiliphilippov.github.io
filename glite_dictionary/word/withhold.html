to withhold

1. VERB

/ wɪθˈhoʊld / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊld / (BrE)
to refuse to give something that someone wants or needs; to keep something back
refuse to give or hold back

Examples

The company decided to withhold payment until the project was completed.
She chose to withhold her opinion during the meeting.
The government may withhold information in the interest of national security.

Synset Examples

The father is withholding the allowance until the son cleans his room

Related Terms

  • Antonym: give - to hand over or provide what is requested
  • Antonym: grant - to give or allow something, especially officially

Etymology

From Old English wiðhǣldan, from wið (against) + healdan (to hold).

2. VERB

/ wɪðˈhoʊld / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊld / (BrE)
to refuse to give or pay something that is due or expected; to retain and not disburse (a payment, information, etc.)
refuse to give or pay; retain

Examples

The company decided to withhold the bonus until the target was met.
If you withhold payment, your account may be suspended.
She withheld important information from the committee.

Synset Examples

My employer is withholding taxes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: release - to allow or set free something that was held back
  • Antonym: give - to present or provide what is due or requested

Etymology

From Old English 'wiðholdan', from 'wið' (against) + 'holdan' (to hold).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02219783-v
Synset POS: v
