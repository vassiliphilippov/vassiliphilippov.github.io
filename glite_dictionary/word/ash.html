ash

1. NOUN

/ æʃ / (AmE), / æʃ / (BrE)
a deciduous tree of the genus Fraxinus, valued for its strong wood and often grown as an ornamental or for timber
tree of the genus Fraxinus

Examples

The old ash tree stood alone at the edge of the field.
Carpenters often use ash wood for making strong furniture.
Several ash species are at risk due to a destructive beetle infestation.

Etymology

Old English æsc, from Proto-Germanic *askaz (ash tree).

2. NOUN

/ æʃ / (AmE), / æʃ / (BrE)
The tough, elastic wood of any of several ash trees, used for making furniture, tool handles, and sporting goods.
tough wood from ash trees

Examples

The handle of the hammer was made of ash for durability and strength.
Many baseball bats are crafted from ash because of its flexibility and resilience.
He admired the fine grain of the ash used in the old cabinet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: oak - hard wood from oak trees, used for furniture and building

Etymology

From Old English æsc, referring to the ash tree.

3. NOUN

/ æʃ / (AmE), / æʃ / (BrE)
the gray or black powdery residue left after the burning of a substance
powdery residue after burning

Examples

After the wildfire, everything was covered in a thick layer of ash.
He sifted through the ash to find anything that could be salvaged from the fireplace.
The letter burned quickly, leaving only ash behind.

Etymology

From Old English æsce (ash tree, ash), related to Old High German asca and Old Norse aska.

4. VERB

/ æʃ / (AmE), / æʃ / (BrE)
to convert into ashes by burning or incineration
reduce to ashes; incinerate

Examples

After the wildfire, the firefighters found that the entire forest had been ashed.
In the crematorium, bodies are ashed and their remains are carefully stored.
Scientists sometimes ash plant samples in the lab to analyze their mineral content.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to incinerate - to destroy something by burning, especially waste

Etymology

From Old English 'æsce', related to the noun 'ash'.
