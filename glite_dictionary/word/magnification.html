magnification

1. NOUN

/ ˌmæɡ.nə.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌmaɡ.nɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
The act of making something seem more important, significant, or impressive than it really is; exaggeration.
exaggeration of importance or significance

Examples

His constant magnification of minor issues made the meeting last twice as long as necessary.
There was considerable magnification of the risks involved, causing unnecessary worry.
The politician's statement was a clear magnification of the problem to influence public opinion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exaggeration - the act of making something seem larger or more important than it is

Etymology

From Latin 'magnificare' (to magnify) + '-ation'.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmæɡ.nə.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌmæɡ.nɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A photographic print that has been made larger than the original image or negative.
enlarged photographic print

Examples

The photographer displayed a magnification of the landscape in the gallery.
He ordered a magnification of the old family portrait for the living room.
This magnification reveals more details than the original photograph.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enlargement - a photograph that has been printed at a larger size than the original

Etymology

From Latin 'magnificare' (to magnify) plus the suffix '-ion'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌmæɡnəfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmaɡnɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the ratio of the size of an image produced by an optical instrument to the actual size of the object
ratio of image size to object size

Examples

A microscope with a 1000x magnification is used to study bacteria.
The magnification of the lens allows us to see tiny details clearly.
You can adjust the magnification to get a closer view of the specimen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enlargement - the process or result of making something appear larger, especially in optics

Etymology

From Latin 'magnificare' (to magnify) plus English '-ation'.
