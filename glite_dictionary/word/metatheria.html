Metatheria

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɛtəˈθɪriə / (AmE), / ˌmiːtəˈθɪəriə / (BrE)
A group of mammals that includes all marsupials, characterized by giving birth to relatively undeveloped young, many of which are carried and suckled in a pouch.
group of mammals including marsupials

Examples

The kangaroo is a well-known member of the Metatheria.
Scientists continue to study how the reproductive systems of Metatheria differ from those of placental mammals.
Ancestors of modern Metatheria are thought to have originated in South America.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marsupial - a mammal carried and suckled in a pouch after birth

Etymology

From Greek meta- (after, beyond) + thēria (wild animals), referring to mammals more advanced than monotremes.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɛtəˈθɪriə / (AmE), / ˌmɛtəˈθɪərɪə / (BrE)
A major subclass of mammals that includes marsupials, characterized by the presence of a pouch in which the young are carried and nourished.
subclass of pouched mammals (marsupials)

Examples

The subclass Metatheria includes all living marsupials such as kangaroos and koalas.
Paleontologists have found fossil evidence of early Metatheria in South America.
Unlike placental mammals, members of Metatheria give birth to relatively undeveloped young.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marsupial - a mammal of the order Marsupialia, typically carrying young in a pouch

Etymology

From New Latin, based on Greek 'meta' (after, beyond) and 'thērion' (wild animal).
