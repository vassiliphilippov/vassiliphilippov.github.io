sect

1. NOUN

/ sɛkt / (AmE), / sɛkt / (BrE)
A smaller group that has separated from a larger established religion, often with its own distinct beliefs and practices.
subdivision of a religious group

Examples

Several new sects emerged during the religious revival of the 19th century.
The leader of the sect preached a doctrine very different from that of the mainstream faith.
Each sect observes unique rituals and traditions distinct from others within the same religion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: denomination - a recognized autonomous branch of the Christian Church
  • Antonym: cult - a system of religious veneration and devotion directed toward a particular figure or object

Etymology

From Latin secta (following, way, school of thought), originating from sequi (to follow).

2. NOUN

/ sɛkt / (AmE), / sɛkt / (BrE)
A group of people with somewhat different religious, philosophical, or political beliefs from those of a larger group to which they belong, typically regarded as dissenting or heretical.
dissenting religious or ideological group

Examples

The new religious sect grew quickly, attracting followers from neighboring towns.
During the 17th century, several dissenting sects broke away from the established church.
She joined a small philosophical sect that questioned traditional values.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: denomination - a recognized autonomous branch of a religion
  • Synonym: cult - a relatively small group with religious beliefs regarded as strange or sinister
  • Antonym: mainstream - the ideas, attitudes, or activities regarded as normal or conventional

Etymology

From Latin 'secta' (a following, faction, or mode of life), from 'sequi' (to follow).
