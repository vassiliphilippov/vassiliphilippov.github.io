ballast

1. NOUN

/ ˈbæl.əst / (AmE), / ˈbæl.əst / (BrE)
a quality, characteristic, or influence that provides mental or moral stability, helping to steady and support a person's character or emotions.
stabilizing influence on character or emotions

Examples

Her family provided the emotional ballast she needed during difficult times.
A sense of humor can serve as a ballast against the stresses of everyday life.
His strong principles acted as a ballast, keeping him steady amid pressure.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anchor - something that provides stability or confidence

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'ballast' meaning 'ballast, heavy material for stability'. Originally a nautical term, now also used metaphorically.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbæləst / (AmE), / ˈbæləst / (BrE)
an electrical device used to start and regulate the current in fluorescent and discharge lamps
device for controlling current in lamps

Examples

The fluorescent light stopped working because its ballast failed.
Electricians often check the ballast when troubleshooting lighting issues.
Modern LED fixtures do not require a ballast like fluorescent lights do.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: choke - a coil used to limit current in electrical circuits

3. VERB

/ ˈbæl.əst / (AmE), / ˈbæl.əst / (BrE)
to stabilize or make steady by adding ballast, especially in a vessel or aircraft
to stabilize by adding ballast

Examples

The engineers ballasted the ship to ensure a smoother voyage.
They ballast the hot air balloon before every flight for safety.
The old railroad tracks were carefully ballasted to withstand heavy loads.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stabilize - to make or become stable

Etymology

From Middle English 'balast', possibly of Low German origin.

4. NOUN

/ ˈbæl.əst / (AmE), / ˈbæl.əst / (BrE)
Coarse gravel or similar material used to form the bed for streets, railroads, or other structures, providing stability and drainage.
coarse gravel base for streets or railways

Examples

The workers spread ballast along the tracks to stabilize the new railway line.
A thick layer of ballast was laid before paving the road to ensure proper drainage.
Maintenance crews routinely check the ballast to keep the railroad in good condition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gravel - small, loose stones often used in construction or landscaping

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'balast', meaning 'bare load' or 'weight for stability'.

5. NOUN

/ ˈbæləst / (AmE), / ˈbæl.əst / (BrE)
Heavy material placed in a ship, airship, or balloon to improve stability or control the craft's balance.
material used for stabilizing a vessel

Examples

The ship's hold was filled with ballast to prevent it from rolling in the rough seas.
The hot-air balloon pilot released some ballast to help the balloon rise higher.
Engineers checked the amount of ballast in the submarine to ensure its stability.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: cargo - goods carried on a ship, aircraft, or motor vehicle

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'ballast', originally meaning 'bare load' or 'useless cargo'.

6. NOUN

/ ˈbæl.əst / (AmE), / ˈbæl.əst / (BrE)
a device, typically a resistor or inductor, placed in an electric circuit to regulate current and compensate for changes such as temperature fluctuations
device controlling current in a circuit

Examples

The technician installed a new ballast to maintain the proper current in the circuit.
Fluorescent lamps require a ballast to function safely and efficiently.
A faulty ballast can cause flickering or overheating in lighting fixtures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: resistor - an electrical component that limits or regulates current in a circuit

Etymology

Originally from Old French 'balaste' (ballast, heavy material), from Middle Low German 'ballast'.
