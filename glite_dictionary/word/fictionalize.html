to fictionalize

1. VERB

/ ˈfɪkʃənəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɪkʃənəlaɪz / (BrE)
to present a real event, person, or fact in the form of fiction, especially as a novel; to make something appear as if it is fiction
to turn real events into fiction

Examples

The director chose to fictionalize the documentary for broader appeal.
She often fictionalizes her childhood experiences in her novels.
The film fictionalized real political events to craft a compelling story.

Synset Examples

The author novelized the historical event

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to novelize - to convert a story or script into a novel

Etymology

From 'fiction' + '-alize', modeled after similar verbs like 'nationalize'; first recorded in the early 20th century.

2. VERB

/ ˈfɪk.ʃə.nəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈfɪk.ʃə.nə.laɪz / (BrE)
to present (a real event, person, etc.) in the form of fiction, often by altering or embellishing details
make into fiction; turn real events into stories

Examples

The film fictionalizes real events from the director's life to craft a compelling narrative.
She chose to fictionalize her childhood experiences in her debut novel.
Many authors fictionalize historical figures to explore 'what-if' scenarios in their books.

Synset Examples

The writer fictionalized the lives of his parents in his latest novel

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to dramatize - make a story more dramatic, often by exaggeration or adding events

Etymology

From 'fictional' (based on Latin 'fictio', meaning 'a shaping, a feigning') + '-ize' (verb-forming suffix).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01638718-v
Synset POS: v
