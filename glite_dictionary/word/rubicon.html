Rubicon

1. PROPN

/ ˈruː.bɪˌkɑn / (AmE), / ˈruː.bɪˌkɒn / (BrE)
a river in northern Italy which, in ancient times, marked the boundary between Italy and Gaul; famously crossed by Julius Caesar in 49 BC, signifying the start of civil war.
ancient river marking Italy-Gaul boundary

Examples

Julius Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon is regarded as a turning point in Roman history.
The phrase 'crossing the Rubicon' refers to taking an irreversible step, just as Caesar did.
Historians debate the exact location of the ancient Rubicon.

Etymology

From Latin Rubicon, the name of the river.

2. NOUN

/ ˈruː.bɪ.kɑn / (AmE), / ˈruː.bɪ.kɒn / (BrE)
a point of no return, where a decision or action commits one irrevocably to a course of events
point of no return; decisive commitment

Examples

Crossing the Rubicon meant accepting whatever consequences would follow.
When she resigned from her job, she knew she had crossed her personal Rubicon.
After signing the contract, the team had passed the Rubicon and could not back down.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: point of no return - the stage at which reversal is no longer possible

Etymology

From Latin Rubico, the Italian river crossed by Julius Caesar, signifying irrevocable decision.
