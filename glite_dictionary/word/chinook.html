chinook

1. NOUN

/ ʃɪˈnʊk / (AmE), / ʃɪˈnʊk / (BrE)
a warm, dry wind that blows down the eastern slopes of the Rocky Mountains, especially in North America
warm, dry wind from the Rockies

Examples

The arrival of a chinook melted much of the snow overnight.
Farmers in Alberta welcomed the chinook after a long spell of cold weather.
Temperatures can rise dramatically when a chinook sweeps through the region.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foehn - a warm dry wind descending a mountain slope, especially in the Alps

Etymology

From Salish (Indigenous language), name for a group living near the Columbia River, later applied to the wind.

2. NOUN

/ ʃɪˈnʊk, tʃɪˈnʊk / (AmE), / ʃɪˈnʊk, tʃɪˈnʊk / (BrE)
The pink or white flesh of a large Pacific salmon, valued as food.
flesh of Pacific salmon

Examples

The chef served grilled chinook with a lemon-dill sauce for dinner.
Fresh chinook is prized for its delicate flavor and rich texture in gourmet cuisine.
We bought a fillet of chinook at the fish market for our weekend barbecue.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chinook salmon - a large Pacific salmon known for its size and value as food

Etymology

From Chinook Jargon, from Salishan (Chinook) people.

3. NOUN

/ tʃɪˈnʊk, ʃɪˈnʊk / (AmE), / tʃɪˈnʊk, ʃɪˈnʊk / (BrE)
a large Pacific salmon (Oncorhynchus tshawytscha) highly valued as food, whose adults die after spawning
large Pacific salmon valued as food

Examples

The fisherman proudly displayed a 20-pound chinook he had caught in the river.
Wild-caught chinook is prized by chefs for its rich flavor and large size.
After spawning, the chinook salmon complete their life cycle and die in the stream.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: king salmon - another name for the chinook, a large Pacific salmon
  • Degree Lower: sockeye - a different species of Pacific salmon
  • Degree Lower: coho salmon - a closely related but generally smaller Pacific salmon

Etymology

From Chinook Jargon, originating from the name of the Chinook people of the Pacific Northwest.
