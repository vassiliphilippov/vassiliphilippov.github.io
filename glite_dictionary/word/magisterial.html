magisterial

1. ADJ

/ ˌmædʒɪˈstɪriəl / (AmE), / ˌmadʒɪˈstɪəriəl / (BrE)
relating to a magistrate or a magistrate's duties or authority.
relating to a magistrate

Examples

The council discussed several magisterial duties in their weekly meeting.
New reforms gave greater oversight over magisterial appointments and responsibilities.
She attended the debate on the expansion of magisterial powers in the local district.

Synset Examples

official magisterial functions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magistrate - a civil officer who administers the law

Etymology

From Latin magisterialis, from magister (master, chief, teacher).

2. ADJ

/ ˌmædʒəˈstɪriəl / (AmE), / ˌmadʒɪˈstɪəriəl / (BrE)
having or showing the manner of someone who expects to be obeyed, often in an overbearing or domineering way; arrogantly authoritative.
overbearingly authoritative; domineering

Examples

The principal spoke to the students in a magisterial tone, leaving no room for debate.
Her magisterial attitude quickly alienated her colleagues at the new job.
He managed the discussion in a magisterial manner, interrupting and overruling others without hesitation.

Synset Examples

an autocratic person
autocratic behavior
a bossy way of ordering others around
a rather aggressive and dominating character
managed the employees in an aloof magisterial way
a swaggering peremptory manner

Related Terms

  • Synonym: authoritative - showing confidence expected to be obeyed; commanding
  • Synonym: domineering - asserting one's will over another in an arrogant way
  • Antonym: submissive - ready to conform to the authority or will of others

Etymology

From Latin magisterialis, from magister (master, teacher).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00791150-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˌmædʒəˈstɪriəl / (AmE), / ˌmadʒɪˈstɪəriəl / (BrE)
having or showing great authority or dignity in manner or appearance; befitting an eminent or commanding person
having an authoritative or dignified manner

Examples

The professor spoke in a magisterial tone that commanded the attention of everyone in the room.
Her magisterial presence at the ceremony gave the event a sense of gravity and importance.
He walked onto the stage with a magisterial air, confident and dignified.

Synset Examples

his distinguished bearing
the monarch's imposing presence
she reigned in magisterial beauty

Related Terms

  • Synonym: authoritative - commanding and self-confident; likely to be respected and obeyed
  • Synonym: imperious - assuming power or authority without justification; arrogant and domineering
  • Antonym: humble - having or showing a modest or low estimate of one's importance

Etymology

From Latin 'magisterialis', from 'magister' meaning 'master'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00755631-s
Synset POS: s
