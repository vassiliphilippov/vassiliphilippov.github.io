to drown

1. VERB

/ draʊn / (AmE), / draʊn / (BrE)
to die through being submerged in and inhaling water, leading to suffocation
die by suffocating under water

Examples

Several swimmers tragically drowned after being caught in the sudden storm.
If you cannot swim, there is a real risk you might drown in deep water.
The rescue team arrived just in time to save the child from drowning.

Synset Examples

The child drowned in the lake

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suffocate - to die or have serious difficulty breathing due to a lack of air or obstruction

Etymology

From Middle English 'drounen', related to Old English 'druncnian' (to be swallowed up by water).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ draʊn / (AmE), / draʊn / (BrE)
to get rid of a feeling or thought, especially an unpleasant one, by overwhelming it with something else, often used in expressions like 'drown one's sorrows'
overwhelm or get rid of (feeling, thought)

Examples

He tried to drown his loneliness by keeping himself busy all day.
After the breakup, she would often drown her sadness in work.
Many people drown their worries in drink, though it rarely helps in the long run.

Synset Examples

She drowned her trouble in alcohol

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppress - to put an end to something, such as a feeling or activity
  • Synonym: to smother - to completely cover or stifle, as in 'smother doubts'

/ draʊn / (AmE), / draʊn / (BrE)

to cover or submerge in liquid

  • Synonym: to immerse - to dip or submerge in a liquid

Etymology

4. VERB

/ draʊn / (AmE), / draʊn / (BrE)
to kill (a person or animal) by holding them under water so that they cannot breathe
kill by submerging in water

Examples

The farmer drowned the sick puppies, unable to find homes for them.
It is illegal to drown unwanted animals in most countries today.
She was horrified to learn someone had drowned the kittens in the river.

Synset Examples

He drowned the kittens

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suffocate - to die or kill by preventing breathing
  • Antonym: rescue - to save from a dangerous or distressing situation, especially from water

Etymology

From Old English druncnian (be drowned) and Old Norse drukna (be drowned), related to 'drink'.
