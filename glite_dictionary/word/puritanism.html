Puritanism

1. NOUN

/ ˈpjʊr.ə.tənˌɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈpjʊə.rɪ.tən.ɪz.əm / (BrE)
the beliefs and practices of the Puritans, a Protestant group—mainly Calvinist—that sought to purify the Church of England of Roman Catholic influences, especially during the 16th and 17th centuries
religious movement seeking to purify the Church of England

Examples

Many early American settlers practiced Puritanism and sought to create a godly community in the New World.
The strict moral code of Puritanism influenced laws and social norms in colonial New England.
Some critics view Puritanism as an overly rigid approach to religion and personal behavior.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Calvinism - the Protestant theological system of John Calvin, influential on Puritanism

Etymology

From Puritan + -ism, referring to the Puritan movement within the Church of England.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpjʊr.ə.t̬ən.ɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈpjʊə.rɪ.tə.nɪz.əm / (BrE)
the belief in or practice of strict moral or religious behavior and self-discipline, especially associated with the Puritans; strictness and austerity in conduct and religion
strict religious or moral discipline

Examples

The community's Puritanism was evident in its strict laws and social norms.
He criticized the Puritanism of his upbringing, calling it repressive and joyless.
The novel explores the tension between Puritanism and personal freedom in early American society.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: austerity - sternness or severity in manner or attitude

Etymology

From 'Puritan' (a member of a 16th–17th century English Protestant group) + '-ism' (denoting a practice or philosophy).
