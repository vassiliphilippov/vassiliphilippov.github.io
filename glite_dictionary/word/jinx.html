jinx

1. NOUN

/ dʒɪŋks / (AmE), / dʒɪŋks / (BrE)
a person who is believed to bring bad luck to others around them
a bringer of bad luck

Examples

He started to believe that he was a jinx after his team lost every game he attended.
The superstitious sailors refused to let the supposed jinx join their crew again.
They called her a jinx because every project she touched seemed to go wrong.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - a cause of harm or misfortune
  • Antonym: charm - a person or thing believed to bring good luck

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from late 19th-century American English, influenced by 'jynx', a bird used in witchcraft and the Latin 'jynx' or Greek 'iunx'.

2. NOUN

/ dʒɪŋks / (AmE), / dʒɪŋks / (BrE)
An evil spell or charm believed to cause bad luck.
evil spell causing bad luck

Examples

The team believed there was a jinx on their stadium after years of bad luck.
Some fans wear special charms to ward off a jinx before important games.
Breaking a mirror is thought to bring a jinx that lasts for seven years.

Synset Examples

a witch put a curse on his whole family
he put the whammy on me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - a solemn utterance intended to invoke a supernatural power to inflict harm or punishment
  • Antonym: blessing - a prayer or utterance asking for divine favor and protection; opposite of a curse

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from late 19th-century American English, related to 'jynx' (a charm or spell), itself from Latin 'iynx', a wryneck bird used in witchcraft.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07175075-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ dʒɪŋks / (AmE), / dʒɪŋks / (BrE)
to bring bad luck to someone or something, often by a supposed magical or supernatural means
bring bad luck to

Examples

Don’t say we’re sure to win—you’ll jinx it!
He believed his rival had jinxed his chances of success.
She worried that talking about her plans would jinx them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to invoke or wish evil upon someone or something
  • Antonym: to bless - to confer good fortune or happiness upon

Etymology

Origin uncertain, possibly from early 20th-century American English, related to 'jynx', a Latin genus name for the wryneck bird, linked with spells and omens.

4. VERB

/ dʒɪŋks / (AmE), / dʒɪŋks / (BrE)
to bring bad luck to someone or something; to cause something to be doomed to failure by a supposed curse or superstition
to bring bad luck to

Examples

Don't say anything before the results are announced—you might jinx it!
Some people believe that mentioning a no-hitter during a game can jinx the pitcher.
He felt that buying the tickets in advance would jinx their chances of winning.

Synset Examples

This project is jinxed!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to cause misfortune or bad luck by invoking a supernatural power
  • Antonym: to bless - to bring good luck or divine favor to

Etymology

Origin uncertain; perhaps from the 17th-century 'jynx', a wryneck bird used in witchcraft, or from 19th-century American English slang.
