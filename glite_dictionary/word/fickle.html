fickle

1. ADJ

/ ˈfɪk.əl / (AmE), / ˈfɪk.əl / (BrE)
changing often and suddenly, especially in regard to feelings, loyalties, or preferences; not constant or reliable.
liable to sudden change; inconstant

Examples

Her fickle affections made it hard for anyone to trust her promises.
Spring weather can be so fickle, sunny in the morning and stormy by afternoon.
The public’s fickle attention quickly shifted to a new scandal.

Synset Examples

erratic behavior
fickle weather
mercurial twists of temperament
a quicksilver character, cool and willful at one moment, utterly fragile the next

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inconstant - not staying the same, changing frequently
  • Antonym: steady - not changing; stable and consistent

Etymology

From Old English *ficol* (deceitful), related to *fæc* (fraud, deceit).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfɪkəl / (AmE), / ˈfɪkəl / (BrE)
liable to sudden and unpredictable changes, especially in affection, loyalty, or interests.
changing easily; unpredictable in feelings

Examples

Her fickle moods made it difficult to make plans together.
He quickly lost interest in hobbies, proving just how fickle his enthusiasm could be.
The public can be fickle, showing great support one day and forgetting the next.

Synset Examples

fickle friends
a flirt's volatile affections

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inconstant - not faithful or dependable in affection or loyalty
  • Antonym: steady - firm and unwavering in attitude or affection

Etymology

From Old English *ficol* (deceitful), related to Old English *fician* (to deceive).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00587124-s
Synset POS: s
