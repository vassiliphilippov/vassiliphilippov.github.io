Judah

1. PROPN

/ ˈdʒuːdə / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdə / (BrE)
an ancient kingdom in southern Palestine, centered in Jerusalem, that existed from around the 10th century BCE until its conquest in the 6th century BCE
ancient southern Palestinian kingdom

Examples

Scholars have found artifacts from Judah that shed light on religious life in the region.
The kingdom of Judah fell to the Babylonians in the 6th century BCE.
Many biblical stories are set in the ancient land of Judah.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Israel - ancient kingdom in northern Palestine, contemporaneous with Judah

Etymology

From Hebrew Yəhūdāh, meaning 'praised', also the name of a son of Jacob in the Hebrew Bible.

2. PROPN

/ ˈdʒuːdə / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːdə / (BrE)
(in the Bible) the fourth son of Jacob and Leah, forebear of one of the twelve tribes of Israel, and an ancestor of the line from which the Messiah was prophesied to come.
biblical son of Jacob; tribe ancestor

Examples

Many biblical stories center on Judah and his brothers.
According to tradition, the royal line of David descended from Judah.
The tribe of Judah played a significant role in the history of ancient Israel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Judah (tribe) - the Israelite tribe descended from Judah, son of Jacob

Etymology

From Hebrew Yĕhūḏāh, meaning 'praised.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-11113459-n
Synset POS: n
