to forego

1. VERB

/ fɔrˈɡoʊ / (AmE), / fɔːˈɡəʊ / (BrE)
to occur or exist before something in time; to precede
to occur or come before

Examples

Debate on the new law must forego the vote in parliament.
Ancient customs often forego written records in many cultures.
The festival foregoes the harvest season, marking the start of celebrations.

Synset Examples

Stone tools precede bronze tools

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to precede - to come before in time, order, or position
  • Antonym: to follow - to come after in time or order

Etymology

From Old English 'forgān', meaning 'to go before'; related to 'fore-' (before) and 'go'.

2. VERB

/ fɔrˈɡoʊ / (AmE), / fɔːˈɡəʊ / (BrE)
to decide not to have or do something; to give up or go without something voluntarily
voluntarily go without

Examples

She decided to forego dessert after dinner to stick to her diet.
Many people chose to forego their morning coffee during the fasting period.
He will forego his right to speak at the meeting in favor of a written statement.

Synset Examples

We are dispensing with formalities
relinquish the old ideas

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forfeit - to lose or give up as a penalty or choice
  • Synonym: to relinquish - to voluntarily cease to keep or claim; give up
  • Synonym: to relinquish - to voluntarily cease to keep or claim; give up

Etymology

From Middle English 'forgoon', from Old English 'forgān' (to go past, abstain from).
