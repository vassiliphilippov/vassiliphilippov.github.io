emasculate

1. VERB

/ ɪˈmæskjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈmæskjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to make something weaker or less effective; to deprive of strength, power, or effectiveness
make weaker or less effective

Examples

The amendments served to emasculate the environmental protection bill.
Heavy regulation can sometimes emasculate innovation in a company.
Many critics say the decision will only emasculate the power of the organization.

Synset Examples

The Senate emasculated the law

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to weaken - to make or become weaker
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to make or become stronger

Etymology

From Latin *emasculatus*, past participle of *emasculare* (to castrate, weaken), from *e-* (out) + *masculus* (male).

2. ADJ

/ ɪˈmæskjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈmæskjʊlət / (BrE)
(of a person, action, or thing) having qualities regarded as unmanly or lacking traditional masculine strength.
having unmanly or feminine qualities

Examples

Critics called his writing style too emasculate for a magazine aimed at men.
The leader worried that compromise would make the government appear emasculate.
In the novel, the once powerful king is portrayed as emasculate after his defeat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: effeminate - having or showing qualities regarded as more typical of women than men

Etymology

From Latin emasculatus, past participle of emasculare (to castrate), from e- (out of) + masculus (male).

3. VERB

/ ɪˈmæskjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪˈmæskjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to remove the testicles of a male animal; to castrate.
remove the testicles of a male animal

Examples

The veterinarian had to emasculate the bull for health reasons.
Farmers sometimes emasculate young animals to control breeding.
After the procedure, the surgeon explained how he had safely emasculated the ram.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: castrate - remove the testicles or ovaries; make incapable of reproduction

Etymology

From Latin emasculatus, past participle of emasculare (to castrate), from e- (out) + masculus (male).
