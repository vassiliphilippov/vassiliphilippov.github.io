blitz

1. NOUN

/ blɪts / (AmE), / blɪts / (BrE)
In American football, a defensive play in which players rush towards the quarterback in an attempt to disrupt or tackle him.
a defensive rush in American football
american

Examples

The coach called for a blitz on third down to try to sack the quarterback.
When the defense executed a blitz, the offense struggled to protect their quarterback.
A well-timed blitz can completely change the momentum of a football game.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - a quick advance on the quarterback in football

Etymology

Originally from German 'Blitz', meaning 'lightning'. Term popularized in English during World War II as 'Blitzkrieg'; adopted in American football for its sense of a sudden attack.

2. NOUN

/ blɪts / (AmE), / blɪts / (BrE)
a swift and intense military attack, especially one involving heavy aerial bombardment
intense military attack with bombing

Examples

The city suffered a severe blitz during the closing months of the war.
The sudden blitz caught the defenders completely off guard.
Residents took shelter in underground bunkers to survive the nightly blitz.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bombardment - an attack by dropping bombs or shelling

Etymology

From German Blitz (lightning), short for Blitzkrieg (lightning war).

3. VERB

/ blɪts / (AmE), / blɪts / (BrE)
to attack suddenly and with great force, especially without warning
attack suddenly and forcefully

Examples

The army decided to blitz the enemy at dawn, catching them off guard.
The football team blitzed their opponents with a series of unexpected plays.
During the World War, several cities were blitzed overnight, leaving many buildings destroyed.

Synset Examples

Hitler blitzed Poland

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - to take aggressive action against; to assault
  • Antonym: to defend - to protect from harm or danger; to guard

Etymology

From German 'Blitz' meaning 'lightning'; popularized in English during World War II (as in 'Blitzkrieg').
