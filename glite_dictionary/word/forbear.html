to forbear

1. VERB

/ fɔrˈbɛr / (AmE), / fɔːˈbeə / (BrE)
to refrain from doing or saying something, especially through self-control or patience
to refrain from doing or saying something

Examples

He tried to forbear making any comment during the heated discussion.
She could not forbear laughing at the unexpected joke.
Although tempted by the cake, he managed to forbear eating a second slice.

Synset Examples

He refrained from hitting him back
she could not forbear weeping

Related Terms

  • Synonym: refrain - to stop oneself from doing something
  • Antonym: yield - to give way to something; opposite of resist

Etymology

From Old English 'forberan', meaning to endure or abstain.

2. VERB

/ fɔrˈbɛr / (AmE), / fɔːˈbɛə / (BrE)
To politely or patiently restrain oneself from an action or refrain from doing something.
refrain from doing something

Examples

He managed to forbear from commenting on the mistake.
She could hardly forbear a smile at his enthusiasm.
We should forbear making judgments until we know the whole story.

Synset Examples

she forbore a snicker

Related Terms

  • Synonym: refrain - to stop oneself from doing something
  • Antonym: to indulge - to allow oneself to enjoy the pleasure of something

Etymology

From Old English 'forberan' (to endure, do without), from 'for-' (away, off) + 'beran' (to bear).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02731919-v
Synset POS: v
