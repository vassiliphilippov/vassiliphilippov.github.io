clack

1. VERB

/ klæk / (AmE), / klæk / (BrE)
(of a person) to talk rapidly and continuously about trivial or unimportant matters.
talk rapidly about trivial matters

Examples

The office workers would clack about their weekends for hours before getting any work done.
She tends to clack on the phone with her friends late into the night.
He grew tired of listening to them clack about nothing in particular.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chatter - talk rapidly about trivial matters
  • Synonym: prattle - talk at length in a foolish or inconsequential way
  • Synonym: babble - talk rapidly and continuously in a foolish, excited, or incomprehensible way

Etymology

Imitative of a sharp sound; used figuratively for gossiping or idle talk.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01038676-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ klæk / (AmE), / klæk / (BrE)
a sharp, abrupt noise, often caused by two objects striking together; can be repeated
sharp, repeated striking noise

Examples

The clack of her high heels echoed down the marble hallway.
We heard the clack of billiard balls from the next room.
The clack of typewriter keys filled the otherwise silent office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: click - a short, sharp sound as made by a switch or latch
  • Degree Higher: clatter - a continuous rattling sound as of hard objects falling or striking each other

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound itself; influenced by Middle English 'clakken' (to chatter, make noise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07394563-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ klæk / (AmE), / klæk / (BrE)
(of a person or animal, especially a hen) to make short, sharp, clicking or clucking sounds.
make short, sharp clucking sounds

Examples

The old hen began to clack loudly as soon as she saw someone approach the coop.
During the performance, the actors would clack their tongues to imitate chickens.
All morning, the birds would clack and chatter under the eaves, filling the air with noise.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluck - make a characteristic short, low sound like a hen

Etymology

Imitative of the sound itself; related to Middle English 'clacken'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01056721-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ klæk / (AmE), / klæk / (BrE)
to make a short, sharp, loud, or repeated rattling sound, or to cause something to make this sound
make a rattling sound

Examples

The old train wheels clacked loudly against the iron tracks as it sped through the countryside.
She clacked her knitting needles together while telling stories by the fire.
As the wind picked up, the shutters clacked against the windows throughout the night.

Synset Examples

clattering dishes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clatter - make a loud rattling or banging sound
  • Degree Lower: click - make a short, sharp sound
  • Synonym: rattle - make or cause to make a rapid succession of short, sharp noises

Etymology

From Middle English 'clacken' (to chatter, clatter), imitative of the sound.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02176571-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ klæk / (AmE), / klæk / (BrE)
a simple valve with a hinge on one side that allows fluid to flow in only one direction
simple one-way valve

Examples

The water pump uses a clack to prevent backflow.
Engineers replaced the worn clack with a new, more efficient one.
A simple clack design can be found in many old water systems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: check valve - a valve that allows fluid to flow in one direction only

Etymology

From Middle English clak, from Old English claccian (to make a noise); influenced by the sound it describes.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03040686-n
Synset POS: n
