liberalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɪbərəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈlɪbərəlɪzəm / (BrE)
An economic theory advocating free competition, a self-regulating market, and minimal state intervention in the economy.
economic theory advocating free markets

Examples

Many economists argue that liberalism leads to greater innovation through competition.
The country's shift towards liberalism in the late twentieth century significantly impacted its economic growth.
Critics of liberalism claim it can lead to greater inequality without appropriate regulation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: laissez-faire - policy or attitude of letting things take their own course, without interfering
  • Antonym: protectionism - the theory or practice of shielding a country's domestic industries from foreign competition

Etymology

From French *libéralisme*, from Latin *liberalis* (of freedom).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlɪb.ɚ.əˌlɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈlɪb.ər.əl.ɪz.əm / (BrE)
a political philosophy or orientation advocating social progress and reform through legal and gradual change rather than revolutionary means
political philosophy favoring reform and progress

Examples

Many voters support liberalism because it promotes equality and gradual social change.
The rise of liberalism in the nineteenth century led to significant political reforms.
Her beliefs in liberalism encouraged her to advocate for new education policies.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: conservatism - political orientation favoring tradition and resisting change
  • Synonym: progressivism - political philosophy advocating progress and reform

Etymology

From French 'libéralisme', from 'libéral' (liberal), ultimately from Latin 'liberalis' (of freedom).
