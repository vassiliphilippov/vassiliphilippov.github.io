retentive

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈtɛntɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛntɪv / (BrE)
having the ability to remember things easily or keep information in mind for a long time
able to remember things easily

Examples

She has a remarkably retentive memory for faces and names.
His retentive mind made him an excellent scholar.
Even after many years, her retentive brain could recall small details from childhood.

Synset Examples

a retentive mind
tenacious memory

Related Terms

  • Antonym: forgetful - having a tendency to forget things

Etymology

From Latin retentivus, from retinere (to retain).

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈtɛntɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛntɪv / (BrE)
Having the ability to retain or remember things easily; able to hold or keep something.
able to retain or remember easily

Examples

She has a remarkably retentive memory for names and dates.
The sponge proved very retentive and held much more water than expected.
A retentive soil keeps moisture longer and benefits certain crops.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: forgetful - unable to remember things; tending not to retain information

Etymology

From Latin 'retentivus', from 'retinere' (to retain).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03122620-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ rɪˈtɛntɪv / (AmE), / rɪˈtɛntɪv / (BrE)
having the ability or tendency to retain something, especially water or moisture
able to retain, especially moisture

Examples

The clay in the garden soil is highly retentive of water, making it prone to puddling.
A retentive sponge can hold large amounts of liquid without dripping.
Farmers prefer retentive soils for crops that require consistent moisture.

Synset Examples

soils retentive of moisture

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorbent - able to soak up liquids easily
  • Antonym: permeable - allowing liquids or gases to pass through

Etymology

From Latin retentivus, from retinere (to retain).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01777459-s
Synset POS: s
