license

1. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪ.səns / (AmE), / ˈlaɪ.səns / (BrE)
excessive freedom or lack of restraint, especially that which leads to disregard for rules or conventions
excessive freedom; lack of restraint

Examples

He warned the students that academic freedom should not be confused with license to disrupt the university.
The new law was criticized for giving the authorities too much license in conducting surveillance.
Without discipline, artistic expression can devolve into mere license rather than meaningful creativity.

Synset Examples

when liberty becomes license, dictatorship is near
the intolerable license with which the newspapers break … the rules of decorum

Related Terms

  • Synonym: liberty - the state of being free within society from oppressive restrictions
  • Antonym: restraint - moderation or self-control; opposite of excessive freedom

Etymology

From Latin 'licentia' (freedom, liberty, unrestrained behaviour).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14018863-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪsəns / (AmE), / ˈlaɪsəns / (BrE)
the formal act of giving permission or authorization, usually in writing, to do something
formal act of authorization

Examples

Obtaining a license for the new business required months of paperwork and waiting.
The city council granted a license to operate the restaurant after a thorough inspection.
Without an official license, the company was not permitted to broadcast on public airwaves.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permit - an official document giving authorization
  • Antonym: prohibition - the act of forbidding something, especially by law

Etymology

From Latin 'licentia' (freedom, liberty, leave), via Old French 'licence'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01141515-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪ.səns / (AmE), / ˈlaɪ.səns / (BrE)
an official document that gives someone permission to do, own, or use something, especially after having met certain requirements
official document granting permission

Examples

You need a license to drive a car in most countries.
The restaurant's license to serve alcohol was revoked last year.
She applied for a business license before opening her shop.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permit - an official document allowing someone to do something
  • Antonym: prohibition - the act of forbidding something by law or authority

Etymology

From Old French 'licence', from Latin 'licentia' (freedom, allowability), from 'licet' (it is permitted).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06561652-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈlaɪ.səns / (AmE), / ˈlaɪ.səns / (BrE)
freedom to depart from conventional rules or norms, especially in behavior, speech, or artistic expression
freedom to ignore normal rules

Examples

He took the license to improvise several lines in the play.
Some artists use license when representing historical events to make the story more engaging.
The director exercised license in adapting the novel for the screen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poetic license - the freedom of a writer or artist to disregard rules of grammar, syntax, or fact for effect

Etymology

From Latin licentia (freedom, liberty, license).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14018291-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈlaɪsəns / (AmE), / ˈlaɪsəns / (BrE)
to give official permission or authorization to do something, especially by granting a license
give official permission

Examples

The state licenses doctors to ensure they meet strict professional standards.
She is licensed to teach in three different countries.
After passing several tests, the company was finally licensed to operate.

Synset Examples

I am licensed to practice law in this state

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permit - to give formal consent to do something
  • Antonym: to prohibit - to formally forbid something

Etymology

From Old French 'licence', from Latin 'licentia' (freedom, liberty, permission).
