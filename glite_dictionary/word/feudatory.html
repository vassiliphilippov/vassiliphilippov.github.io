feudatory

1. ADJ

/ ˈfjuːdəˌtɔri / (AmE), / ˈfjuːdəˌtəri / (BrE)
relating to the relationship between a feudal vassal and their lord; pertaining to feudal vassalage.
relating to a feudal vassal's status or duties

Examples

The lord maintained strict control over the feudatory nobility in his realm.
This castle was once the center of a feudatory state within the kingdom.
The historian examined the feudatory obligations recorded in medieval manuscripts.

Synset Examples

a feudatory relationship

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feudal - relating to the social system of feudalism

Etymology

From Medieval Latin feudatarius, from feodum (fee, fief).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfjuːdəˌtɔri / (AmE), / ˈfjuːdəˌtɔːri / (BrE)
(historical) Owing feudal allegiance or being subject to a sovereign or overlord.
subject to a sovereign in feudal system

Examples

The state remained feudatory to the empire for over two centuries.
Several feudatory princes pledged loyalty to the king.
Under the treaty, the principality became a feudatory territory of the crown.

Synset Examples

it remained feudatory to India until 1365

Etymology

From Medieval Latin feudatorius, from feudum (feudal estate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02337598-s

3. NOUN

/ ˈfjuːdəˌtɔːri / (AmE), / ˈfjuːdətri / (BrE)
a person who holds a fief and owes allegiance and service to a feudal lord
a feudal vassal or landholder

Examples

The feudatory was required to provide military service to his liege lord.
Many medieval kingdoms consisted of numerous feudatory states owing allegiance to the monarch.
The rights and duties of a feudatory were defined by the feudal system.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vassal - a person in a subordinate position to a feudal lord; a holder of land by feudal tenure
  • Antonym: lord - a feudal superior who grants land in exchange for service

Etymology

From Medieval Latin feudatorius, from feudum (fief).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10766249-n
Synset POS: n
