windage

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (BrE)
the slowing force exerted by air friction on a moving object
slowing force from air friction

Examples

Engineers must account for windage when designing high-speed trains.
The windage on a moving car increases with speed, affecting fuel efficiency.
Reducing windage is crucial in competitive cycling to maximize performance.

Etymology

From 'wind' + '-age', modeled after similar words like 'breakage' and 'shrinkage'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (BrE)
the area of a vessel or object that is exposed to the wind, especially as it affects wind resistance
area exposed to wind (wind resistance)

Examples

The ship's windage increased as more of its hull rose above the waterline.
Reducing windage is crucial for improving a sailboat's performance.
Engineers calculated the windage of the structure to ensure it could withstand strong gusts.

Etymology

From 'wind' + '-age', a suffix forming nouns denoting a result or condition.

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (BrE)
the deflection of a projectile caused by the effect of wind on its course
deflection of a projectile by wind

Examples

Snipers must constantly adjust for windage to ensure their bullets hit the target accurately.
The shooter failed to compensate for windage, causing the shot to miss slightly to the right.
Accurately predicting windage is crucial in long-range marksmanship competitions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drift - the sideways movement of a projectile in flight

Etymology

From 'wind' + '-age', patterned after similar nouns meaning 'the result of' or 'amount of'.

4. NOUN

/ ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈwɪndɪdʒ / (BrE)
the space between the projectile of a smoothbore gun and the interior surface of the gun's bore, allowing for gases to escape around the projectile when fired
gap between projectile and bore in gun

Examples

The excessive windage reduced the musket's firing efficiency.
Gunmakers worked to minimize windage to improve accuracy and power.
When the windage is too great, much of the gunpowder's force is lost.

Etymology

From 'wind' (in the sense of air moving through or around something) + '-age'.
