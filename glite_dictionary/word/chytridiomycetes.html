Chytridiomycetes

1. NOUN

/ kaɪˌtrɪd.i.oʊ.maɪˈsiːtiːz / (AmE), / kaɪˌtrɪd.i.əʊ.maɪˈsiːtiːz / (BrE)
a class of mostly aquatic fungi that are typically saprophytic or parasitic on algae, other fungi, or plants.
class of mostly aquatic fungi
medical

Examples

Chytridiomycetes play a crucial role in the decomposition of organic matter in aquatic environments.
Scientists have discovered that some Chytridiomycetes are responsible for diseases in amphibians.
The unique reproductive structures of Chytridiomycetes distinguish them from other classes of fungi.

Etymology

From Modern Latin, based on Greek 'chytra' (pot) and 'mykēs' (fungus), referring to the characteristic shape of some spores.

2. NOUN

/ kaɪˌtrɪdi.oʊˈmaɪsitiːz / (AmE), / kaɪˌtrɪdɪ.əˈmaɪsiːtiːz / (BrE)
a class of mostly aquatic fungi, many of which are saprophytic or parasitic on algae, fungi, or plants.
a class of mostly aquatic fungi
medical

Examples

Scientists have discovered several new species within the Chytridiomycetes in freshwater habitats.
Diseases caused by Chytridiomycetes can have devastating effects on amphibian populations.
The Chytridiomycetes are known for producing motile spores called zoospores.

Etymology

From Greek 'chytra' (earthen pot) + 'mycetes', the suffix used for fungal classes.
