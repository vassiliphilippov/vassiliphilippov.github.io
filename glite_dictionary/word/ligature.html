ligature

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɪɡətʃər / (AmE), / ˈlɪɡətʃə / (BrE)
a metal band that secures the reed to the mouthpiece of a clarinet or saxophone
metal band for securing reed on woodwind mouthpiece

Examples

The clarinetist tightened the ligature to ensure the reed was in place.
If your ligature is too loose, the reed may not vibrate properly.
She bought a new ligature made of gold-plated metal for her saxophone.

Etymology

From Latin 'ligatura' (a binding), from 'ligare' (to bind).

3. NOUN

/ ˈlɪɡətʃər / (AmE), / ˈlɪɡətʃə / (BrE)
A thread, wire, or other material used by surgeons to tie and bind blood vessels or tissues, especially to constrict blood flow during a medical procedure.
thread used in surgery to tie vessels
medical

Examples

The surgeon tied a ligature tightly around the artery to prevent further bleeding.
Absorbable ligatures are often used in surgical procedures to avoid the need for later removal.
A ligature must be securely fastened to ensure that the vessel does not reopen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: suture - stitch or thread used to close a wound or incision in surgery

Etymology

From Latin 'ligatura' (a binding), from 'ligare' (to tie).
