recombination

1. NOUN

/ ˌriː.kəmˌbaɪˈneɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌriː.kɒm.baɪˈneɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(Genetics) The process by which genetic material is rearranged, resulting in the production of offspring with combinations of traits differing from those found in either parent.
genetic mixing; new combination of genes
medical

Examples

During meiosis, recombination increases genetic diversity in the offspring.
Scientists study recombination to understand how traits are inherited and mixed.
Mistakes in recombination can sometimes lead to genetic disorders.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: mutation - a change in the DNA sequence
  • Synonym: crossing over - exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes

Etymology

From re- + combination, referring to the process of combining again in a new way.

2. NOUN

/ ˌriːkəmˌbaɪˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌriːkɒmˌbɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
(Physics) The process by which positive and negative ions or electrons in a gas combine to form neutral atoms or molecules, often following the passage of high-energy particles.
neutralization of ions in physics

Examples

When the charged particles in the plasma experience recombination, they release energy as light.
The process of recombination is essential for understanding how auroras form in the Earth's atmosphere.
Scientists observed recombination after exposing the gas to high-energy radiation in the laboratory.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ionization - the process of forming ions by adding or removing electrons

Etymology

From re- (again) + combination, meaning to combine again.
