flinch

1. NOUN

/ flɪntʃ / (AmE), / flɪntʃ / (BrE)
an involuntary movement or shrinking back, especially as a reaction to sudden pain or fear
involuntary shrinking movement

Examples

She gave a visible flinch when the doctor touched her injured ankle.
There was a collective flinch in the audience as the loud explosion echoed through the hall.
His unexpected question caused a slight flinch from the usually calm speaker.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wince - an involuntary movement in response to pain or distress

Etymology

Probably from Old French *flenchir* (to turn aside, give ground).

2. VERB

/ flɪntʃ / (AmE), / flɪntʃ / (BrE)
to make a quick, involuntary movement or draw back slightly, usually in response to pain, fear, or surprise
draw back quickly, as from pain or fear

Examples

She couldn't help but flinch at the loud bang from the fireworks.
Whenever the dentist approached with the drill, he would flinch slightly in the chair.
He tried not to flinch as the nurse gave him the injection.

Synset Examples

she flinched when they showed the slaughtering of the calf

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cringe - to react with discomfort or fear by shrinking or recoiling
  • Synonym: recoil - to suddenly spring or draw back, especially in fear or disgust

Etymology

From Old French *flenchir* (to turn aside), of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02064997-v
Synset POS: v
