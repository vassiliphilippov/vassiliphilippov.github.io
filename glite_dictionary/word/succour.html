succour

1. VERB

/ ˈsʌkər / (AmE), / ˈsʌkə / (BrE)
to give help or assistance to someone in a difficult or distressing situation
to help in difficulty
archaic

Examples

The Red Cross volunteers succoured the victims of the flood with food and shelter.
He was grateful to the neighbors who succoured his family after the accident.
In the old tales, brave knights would succour those in distress.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aid - to help or assist someone
  • Synonym: rescue - to save from a dangerous or distressing situation
  • Antonym: neglect - to fail to care for someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'sucourre', from Latin 'succurrere' (to run to help).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌkər / (AmE), / ˈsʌkə / (BrE)
help or assistance given to someone in difficulty or distress, especially in a time of need.
assistance in time of difficulty

Examples

Volunteers brought much-needed succour to communities affected by the flood.
Her presence was a great succour to him during his darkest days.
The charity was formed to offer succour to homeless children.

Synset Examples

the contributions provided some relief for the victims

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aid - help or support, especially of a practical nature
  • Synonym: relief - help given to those in urgent need, especially food, money, or clothing
  • Antonym: neglect - lack of care or assistance when needed

Etymology

From Old French 'socour', from Latin 'succursus', past participle of 'succurrere' (to run to help).

Synset POS: n
