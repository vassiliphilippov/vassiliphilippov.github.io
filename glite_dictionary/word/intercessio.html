intercessio

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪntɚˈtʃɛsioʊ / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈtʃɛsɪəʊ / (BrE)
(in ancient Rome) the right of veto possessed by certain magistrates, allowing them to prevent the actions of other magistrates or bodies.
Roman magistrate's right of veto

Examples

During the Roman Republic, the tribunes exercised their intercessio to block laws they deemed unfair.
An understanding of intercessio is crucial to studying the checks and balances of Roman governance.
The use of intercessio was one way that the plebeians could counter the power of patrician officials.

Synset Examples

The intercessio was thus a supervision, exercised by closely-related magistrates, who were theoretically irresponsible during their year of office over one another's functions, for the prevention of illegal or inequitable actions; and on the part of the tribune a general supervision over all other magistracies in the interests, originally of the plebs, later of the whole community.

Etymology

Latin intercessio (intercession, veto; from intercedere 'intervene').
