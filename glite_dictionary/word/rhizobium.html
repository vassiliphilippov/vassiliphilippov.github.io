Rhizobium

1. NOUN

/ raɪˈzoʊbiəm / (AmE), / raɪˈzəʊbɪəm / (BrE)
A genus of bacteria that live in the root nodules of leguminous plants and are capable of fixing nitrogen from the atmosphere.
genus of nitrogen-fixing bacteria in plant roots
medical

Examples

Rhizobium bacteria form symbiotic relationships with legumes to help fix nitrogen in the soil.
Many farmers use crops rich in Rhizobium to naturally fertilize their fields.
Microscopists observed Rhizobium colonies developing on pea roots after several weeks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bacterium - single-celled microorganism
  • Degree Lower: Azotobacter - a genus of free-living nitrogen-fixing bacteria

Etymology

From Greek 'rhiza' (root) + 'bios' (life), referencing its role in plant roots.

2. NOUN

/ raɪˈzoʊbiəm / (AmE), / rɪˈzəʊbiəm / (BrE)
A genus of bacteria that form nodules on the roots of legumes and are capable of fixing atmospheric nitrogen.
a genus of nitrogen-fixing bacteria

Examples

Rhizobium bacteria are essential for the nitrogen cycle in agriculture.
Legumes often contain Rhizobium in nodules on their roots, improving soil fertility.
Scientists study Rhizobium to enhance sustainable farming practices.

Etymology

From Greek 'rhiza' (root) + 'bios' (life), referring to its association with plant roots.
