to aggrieve

1. VERB

/ əˈɡriv / (AmE), / əˈɡriːv / (BrE)
to treat unjustly by infringing upon someone's rights; to cause legal or personal harm or injury
to treat unjustly or injure

Examples

The new law seemed to aggrieve the rights of minority groups.
Employees who feel aggrieved by unfair treatment can file a complaint.
His actions were found to aggrieve the complainant, resulting in compensation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wrong - to do unfair or unjust harm to someone
  • Synonym: to injure - to cause harm or damage to someone's rights or person

Etymology

From Old French 'agriever', from Latin 'gravare' (to burden).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02519545-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ əˈɡriv / (AmE), / əˈɡriːv / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel sorrow, distress, or grief; to afflict or oppress
cause to feel sorrow or distress

Examples

The unfair treatment will only aggrieve the employees further.
She did not mean to aggrieve her friends with her harsh words.
It would aggrieve him to hear such sad news about his hometown.

Synset Examples

his behavior grieves his mother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to grieve - to feel or cause to feel deep sorrow
  • Antonym: console - to comfort someone who is distressed

Etymology

From Old French 'agrever', from Latin 'aggravo' (to make heavy, aggravate).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01801593-v
Synset POS: v
