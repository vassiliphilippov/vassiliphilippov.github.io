wizard

1. ADJ

/ ˈwɪzɚd / (AmE), / ˈwɪzəd / (BrE)
Relating to or characteristic of magic or supernatural powers; magical.
magical; relating to supernatural powers
archaic

Examples

The old book was filled with wizard spells and mysterious symbols.
He wore a wizard cloak that shimmered in the moonlight.
The children listened to tales of wizard feats and legendary heroes.

Synset Examples

charming incantations
magic signs that protect against adverse influence
a magical spell
'tis now the very witching time of night
wizard wands
wizardly powers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: magical - Relating to, using, or resembling magic

Etymology

From Middle English 'wysard', meaning a wise man or magician.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwɪzɚd / (AmE), / ˈwɪzəd / (BrE)
a person who is dazzlingly skilled in a particular field or activity, often showing great ingenuity or expertise
an exceptionally skilled person

Examples

She is a wizard at solving complex mathematical problems.
The young programmer proved himself a real wizard with computer code.
Everyone in the office knows Mark is the financial wizard who saved the company.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expert - a person with a high level of knowledge or skill
  • Antonym: novice - a person new to a field or activity

Etymology

From Middle English 'wysard' (wise man), from 'wise' + '-ard'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09781932-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈwɪzərd / (AmE), / ˈwɪzəd / (BrE)
a person who is believed to have magical powers and practice sorcery
person with magical powers

Examples

The wizard cast a powerful spell over the kingdom, causing everyone to fall asleep for a hundred years.
As a child, she loved reading stories about a wise old wizard who helped heroes with his magic.
Dressed in a long robe and pointed hat, the wizard amazed the crowd with his mysterious tricks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sorcerer - a person who claims or is believed to have magic powers; a wizard
  • Synonym: witch - a woman thought to have magic powers, especially evil ones

Etymology

From Middle English *wysarde*, from Anglo-Norman *wisart*, from *wis* (wise).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10645222-n
Synset POS: n
