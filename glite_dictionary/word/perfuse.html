to perfuse

1. VERB

/ pərˈfjuz / (AmE), / pəˈfjuːz / (BrE)
to force a fluid to flow through a body part or tissue, especially for medical or scientific purposes
force fluid through tissue or organ
medical

Examples

In the transplant procedure, the surgeon had to perfuse the kidney with a special preservation solution.
Researchers perfused the organ with nutrients to maintain its viability during the experiment.
It is crucial to perfuse tissues adequately during cardiac bypass surgery to prevent cell damage.

Synset Examples

perfuse a liver with a salt solution

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infuse - to introduce a substance into something, typically a liquid into the body

Etymology

From Latin 'perfusus', past participle of 'perfundere' meaning 'to pour through'.

2. VERB

/ pərˈfjuz / (AmE), / pəˈfjuːz / (BrE)
to cause (a liquid, color, or substance) to spread or flow through, over, or across something
cause to spread or flow through

Examples

A warm light perfused the entire room at sunset, making the walls glow.
The technician will perfuse the tissue sample with a special dye to highlight blood vessels.
Vivid hues perfused the sky as the sun began to rise.

Synset Examples

The sky was suffused with a warm pink color

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suffuse - to spread over with liquid, color, or light
  • Synonym: to infuse - to fill or pervade with something
  • Synonym: flood - to overflow or fill completely with liquid

Etymology

From Latin perfundere (to pour over) via French perfuser.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00455206-v
Synset POS: v
