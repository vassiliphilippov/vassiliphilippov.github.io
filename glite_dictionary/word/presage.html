presage

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (BrE)
a feeling or sign that something, especially something unpleasant, will happen; an omen or foreboding
an omen or feeling about the future

Examples

The gathering storm clouds were seen as a presage of impending disaster.
His sudden illness was a presage that troubled everyone in the office.
Many believed that the eclipse served as a dark presage for the king's fate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: omen - a sign of what will happen in the future
  • Synonym: foreboding - a strong feeling that something bad will happen

Etymology

From Latin praesagium (omen, foreboding), from praesagire (to foretell).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (BrE)
a sign or warning that something, typically something bad, will happen
a sign or warning of a future event

Examples

The sudden drop in temperature was seen as a presage of the coming storm.
Many regarded the black cat crossing their path as a presage of misfortune.
The strange lights in the sky were believed to be a presage of disaster.

Synset Examples

he looked for an omen before going into battle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: omen - an event regarded as a portent of good or evil
  • Synonym: prediction - a statement about what will happen in the future
  • Synonym: harbinger - a person or thing that announces the approach of another

Etymology

From Latin 'praesagium' (omen, foreboding), from 'praesagire' (to forebode).

3. VERB

/ ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈprɛsɪdʒ / (BrE)
to be a sign or warning of; to foreshadow or indicate as an omen.
to be a sign or omen of

Examples

A sudden drop in temperature may presage a coming storm.
Many feared the economic downturn would presage further job losses.
The ominous clouds presaged the arrival of the hurricane.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foreshadow - to indicate or warn of a future event
  • Synonym: to portend - to be a sign or warning that something momentous is likely to happen
  • Synonym: to predict - to declare or indicate in advance, especially on the basis of special knowledge

Etymology

From Latin praesagium (prognostication, foreboding), from praesagire (to foreknow, forebode).
