footing

1. NOUN

/ ˈfʊtɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈfʊtɪŋ / (BrE)
a secure position for the foot when standing, walking, or climbing, especially to provide stability or support
secure position for the foot

Examples

Make sure you have a firm footing before climbing any higher.
The rocks were slippery, making it difficult to get a good footing.
He lost his footing and nearly fell down the hill.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: foothold - a secure position from which further progress may be made, especially in climbing

Etymology

From Old English *fōting*, from *fōt* (foot) + *-ing* (action, result).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfʊtɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈfʊtɪŋ / (BrE)
the basis or foundation for a relationship, position, or arrangement, especially regarding how people relate to each other
basis or foundation for a relationship

Examples

The two companies are now on a much better footing since the merger.
After the argument, they struggled to return to a friendly footing.
Negotiations resumed, but both parties approached them on a different footing than before.

Synset Examples

they were on a friendly footing
he worked on an interim basis

Related Terms

  • Synonym: basis - the underlying support or foundation for an idea, argument, or process

Etymology

Derived from the noun 'foot' + '-ing', originally referring to having one's feet securely placed; figurative sense develops in the 16th century.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfʊt̬ɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈfʊtɪŋ / (BrE)
the basis or status of a relationship between people or groups, especially in terms of friendliness or equality
relationship status or basis

Examples

While they had some differences, the two companies are now on a solid footing for further cooperation.
She hoped to establish a more friendly footing with her new colleagues.
After the argument, it took a while to get back on a good footing with his friend.

Synset Examples

on good terms with her in-laws
on a friendly footing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: terms - mutual relations or conditions in which people or groups interact
  • Synonym: standing - status or position, especially in a community or group

Etymology

From Old English 'fōtung', from 'fōt' (foot), originally meaning position of the feet but later extended figuratively.
