to harry

1. VERB

/ ˈhæri / (AmE), / ˈhæri / (BrE)
to repeatedly harass or bother someone over a period of time, especially in a persistent or aggressive way
repeatedly harass or bother

Examples

The reporters continued to harry the politician with questions even after the press conference ended.
If you harry your classmates constantly, you might lose their respect.
The soldiers harried the retreating enemy throughout the night.

Synset Examples

He is known to harry his staff when he is overworked
This man harasses his female co-workers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to harass - to subject to aggressive pressure or intimidation
  • Synonym: torment - to cause severe and persistent distress to someone

Etymology

From Old English *hergian* (to ravage, plunder).

2. VERB

/ ˈhæri / (AmE), / ˈhæri / (BrE)
(of soldiers or attackers) to raid or attack a place or people repeatedly, causing destruction or distress, especially during war.
raid and cause destruction repeatedly

Examples

Viking invaders would often harry coastal settlements, leaving devastation in their wake.
During the war, enemy troops harried the countryside, burning villages and taking supplies.
The city was constantly harried by raiders, forcing its inhabitants to fortify their defenses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: raid - a sudden attack on a place, often causing damage or theft

Etymology

Old English *hergian* (to ravage, plunder), related to Old Norse *herja* and German *hehren*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00389754-v
Synset POS: v
