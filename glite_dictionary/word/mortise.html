mortise

1. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrtɪs / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪs / (BrE)
to cut a hole in a piece of wood or other material to receive a matching projection (tenon) from another piece for joining them together
cut a hole for a tenon (woodworking)

Examples

The carpenter had to mortise the beams before assembling the frame.
You can mortise the ends of the board to fit tightly with the adjoining piece.
She carefully mortised the door for the new lock plate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tenon - a projecting piece prepared for insertion into a mortise

Etymology

From Old French *mortaise*, of uncertain origin; used in carpentry since the Middle Ages.

2. VERB

/ ˈmɔːrtɪs / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪs / (BrE)
to join or fasten (wood or other materials) by means of a mortise and tenon joint
join with a mortise and tenon

Examples

The cabinetmaker decided to mortise the joints for added strength.
Experts often mortise the beams before fitting the tenons.
If you mortise the shelves properly, the bookcase will last for years.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tenon - a projecting piece of wood made for insertion into a mortise to make a joint

Etymology

From Old French 'mortaise', probably from Arabic 'murtazz' (fastened).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01296247-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɔːrtɪs / (AmE), / ˈmɔːtɪs / (BrE)
a rectangular hole cut into a material to receive a matching projection (tenon), forming a joint
rectangular hole for a tenon

Examples

The carpenter cut a mortise into the door frame to fit the hinge snugly.
Each leg of the table fits tightly into a mortise for stability.
She used a chisel to shape the mortise in the oak beam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tenon - a projection on the end of a piece of wood that fits into a mortise

Etymology

From Old French *mortaise*, probably from Arabic *murtazz* (fastened).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03792841-n
Synset POS: n
