triangulate

1. VERB

/ traɪˈæŋɡjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / traɪˈaŋɡjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to divide (something) into triangles or to give it a triangular form, especially for purposes of measurement, construction, or analysis
divide or shape into triangles

Examples

To create a stable structure, the engineers decided to triangulate the framework.
The computer program can triangulate complex shapes to make them easier to analyze.
Artists sometimes triangulate a canvas by drawing lines from corner to corner for better composition.

Synset Examples

triangulate the piece of cardboard

Etymology

From Latin triangulus (triangle) + -ate, meaning 'to make into a triangle'.

2. VERB

/ traɪˈæŋ.jə.leɪt / (AmE), / traɪˈaŋ.gjʊ.leɪt / (BrE)
to survey, map, or determine a location by using the principles of triangulation
survey or locate using triangulation

Examples

The engineer was asked to triangulate the exact position of the new building site.
Scientists used GPS data to triangulate the epicenter of the earthquake.
The research team triangulated the area using satellite imagery and reference points.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: triangulation - the process of surveying by measuring triangles

Etymology

From Latin triangulum (triangle) + -ate (forming verbs), after 'triangulation'.

3. VERB

/ traɪˈæŋɡjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / traɪˈaŋɡjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to measure the location or distance of something by forming triangles and applying trigonometry, especially in surveying and navigation
measure using triangles and trigonometry

Examples

Surveyors triangulate the position of landmarks to create accurate maps.
The team will triangulate the height of the hill using measurements from three fixed points.
Astronomers often triangulate the distance to nearby stars by observing them from different positions in Earth's orbit.

Synset Examples

triangulate the angle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: measure - to ascertain the size, amount, or degree of something

Etymology

From Latin triangulum (triangle), after the formation of 'triangle'.
