to stigmatise

1. VERB

/ ˈstɪɡ.mə.taɪz / (AmE), / ˈstɪɡ.mə.taɪz / (BrE)
to treat someone or something as deserving disgrace or disapproval; to mark with a stigma of shame
mark with disgrace or disapproval
british

Examples

Society should not stigmatise people living with mental illness.
She felt stigmatised because of her background.
Policies that stigmatise certain groups can increase discrimination.

Synset Examples

They wanted to stigmatize the adulteress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stigmatize - American spelling of 'stigmatise'
  • Synonym: disgrace - to bring shame upon
  • Antonym: honour - to regard with great respect (opposite meaning)

Etymology

From Greek stigma (mark, puncture) via Late Latin and Medieval Latin.

2. VERB

/ ˈstɪɡ.mə.taɪz / (AmE), / ˈstɪɡ.mə.taɪz / (BrE)
to describe or regard someone or something as worthy of disgrace or great disapproval
describe as disgraceful or shameful
british

Examples

The media often stigmatise people with mental health issues.
She felt stigmatised in her workplace because of her background.
It's wrong to stigmatise entire communities because of the actions of a few.

Synset Examples

He denounced the government action
She was stigmatized by society because she had a child out of wedlock

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stigmatize - American spelling of stigmatise
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Greek *stigma* (mark, brand) via Latin *stigmatisare* (to brand), then French *stigmatiser*.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02513894-v
Synset POS: v
