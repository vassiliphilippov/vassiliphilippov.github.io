soul

1. NOUN

/ soʊl / (AmE), / səʊl / (BrE)
deep feeling or emotional intensity, especially as expressed in art, music, or performance
deep feeling or emotion

Examples

The singer performed with such soul that the audience was moved to tears.
Her painting is full of soul and raw emotion.
He plays the guitar with real soul, making every note count.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: passion - strong emotion or enthusiasm
  • Synonym: emotion - a strong feeling such as love, anger, or joy
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ soʊl / (AmE), / səʊl / (BrE)
a person who is regarded as the embodiment or perfect example of a particular quality or characteristic
embodiment or perfect example of something

Examples

She was the soul of kindness, always ready to help anyone in need.
He is considered the soul of discretion at the company.
The old teacher was the very soul of patience with his students.

Synset Examples

the soul of honor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: epitome - a person or thing that is a perfect example of a particular quality or type
  • Synonym: embodiment - the tangible or visible form of an idea, quality, or feeling

Etymology

From Old English sāwol, sāwel, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch ziel and German Seele.

3. NOUN

/ soʊl / (AmE), / səʊl / (BrE)
A style of popular music originating in the African-American community, blending elements of gospel, rhythm and blues, and jazz, especially prominent in the 1960s and 1970s.
genre of African-American popular music

Examples

Classic soul artists like Aretha Franklin and Marvin Gaye had a huge influence on popular music.
The powerful vocals and emotional depth are hallmarks of soul music.
She grew up listening to soul records from the 1970s on her parents' stereo.

Synset Examples

soul was politically significant during the Civil Rights movement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: R&B - popular music genre combining jazz, gospel, and blues influences
  • Degree Lower: gospel - style of Christian music influential on soul music

Etymology

From American English 'soul', reflecting a sense of deep emotion and feeling in music; ultimately from Old English 'sāwol'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07076008-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ soʊl / (AmE), / səʊl / (BrE)
the immaterial or spiritual part of a human being, regarded as the essential, immortal core of a person
immaterial or spiritual part of a person

Examples

Many religions teach that the soul continues to exist after the body has died.
She poured her heart and soul into her music, giving every performance deep emotion.
Philosophers have long debated whether the soul is immortal or bound to the body.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spirit - the non-physical part of a person regarded as their true self; similar meaning to 'soul'

Etymology

From Old English sāwol, related to Old High German sêula, of uncertain origin.
