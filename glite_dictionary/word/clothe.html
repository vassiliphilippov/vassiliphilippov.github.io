to clothe

1. VERB

/ kloʊð / (AmE), / kləʊð / (BrE)
to invest (someone) with power, authority, or a particular quality, especially in a formal or symbolic way
invest with power or authority

Examples

The constitution clothes the president with certain emergency powers.
They were clothed in authority by the king himself.
Tradition clothes the office with great dignity and respect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to empower - to give someone the power or authority to do something

Etymology

From Old English 'clāðian' (to clothe, provide with clothing); figurative senses developed in Middle English.

2. VERB

/ kloʊð / (AmE), / kləʊð / (BrE)
to provide someone with clothes or to put clothes on someone
provide with clothes; dress

Examples

The charity helps to clothe hundreds of children every year.
She carefully clothed her baby before taking him outside.
Some families struggle to feed and clothe their children.

Synset Examples

Parents must feed and dress their child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dress - to put clothes on someone

Etymology

From Old English *clāþian* (to clothe), from *clāþ* (cloth).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00047662-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kloʊð / (AmE), / kləʊð / (BrE)
to cover or furnish as if with clothing; to envelop or dress, often used figuratively
to cover or envelop as with clothing

Examples

The mountain was clothed in dense, creeping fog by midday.
Spring rains clothe the hills with wildflowers each year.
The ancient castle stood atop a hill, its walls clothed in ivy.

Synset Examples

the mountain was clothed in tropical trees

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cover - to put something over or on top of
  • Antonym: bare - to uncover or leave unprotected

Etymology

From Old English *clāðian*, related to *clāð* (cloth).

Source: WordNet
