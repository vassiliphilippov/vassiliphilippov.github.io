mahoe

1. NOUN

/ məˈhoʊ / (AmE), / məˈhəʊ / (BrE)
An erect forest tree native to Cuba and Jamaica, with hairy leaves and orange-yellow or orange-red flowers, yielding moderately dense timber used for cabinetwork and gunstocks.
tree of Cuba and Jamaica; timber source

Examples

The carpenter crafted a beautiful cabinet from mahoe wood.
In Jamaica, mahoe trees are valued for their sturdy timber.
We saw vibrant orange-red blossoms on the mahoe near the edge of the forest.

Etymology

From Taino, possibly via Spanish 'mahoe'.

2. NOUN

/ məˈhoʊ / (AmE), / məˈhəʊ / (BrE)
A tropical shrubby tree, especially of the genera Hibiscus or Thespesia, found along tropical shores; valued for its light, tough wood used in canoe outriggers and for fiber used in cordage and caulking.
tropical shrubby tree used for wood and fiber

Examples

Local craftsmen used mahoe wood to carve canoes and fishing tools.
The dense foliage of the mahoe creates welcome shade along the beach.
Ropes made from mahoe fibers were once essential for island navigation.

Etymology

From Caribbean English, possibly via Taino or Arawak, denoting trees of the genera Hibiscus and Thespesia.
