to polarise

1. VERB

/ ˈpoʊləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊləraɪz / (BrE)
to cause people or opinions to divide into two sharply contrasting groups or sets of beliefs, especially in a conflict or argument
divide into opposing groups or opinions
british

Examples

The debate over the new policy began to polarise the community, creating two opposing camps.
Social media can quickly polarise opinions and escalates conflicts between groups.
The election results tended to polarise voters along political lines.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unite - to come together or make things join into a single entity

Etymology

From French polariser, from pôle (pole) + -iser (-ize).

2. VERB

/ ˈpoʊ.ləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊ.ləˌraɪz / (BrE)
to cause light or other waves to vibrate in a definite pattern, especially in one plane
cause waves to vibrate in a specific pattern

Examples

Scientists can polarise light to study its properties more effectively.
Special filters are used to polarise the light passing through sunglasses.
The experiment required them to polarise the laser beam before it entered the crystal.

Synset Examples

polarize light waves

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to depolarise - to remove polarization; to make non-polarized

Etymology

From French polariser, from pôle (pole), referring to fixed points or axes in physics.

3. VERB

/ ˈpoʊ.ləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈpəʊ.lə.raɪz / (BrE)
to cause people or opinions to be divided into two sharply contrasting groups or sets of beliefs
cause division into opposing groups

Examples

The debate on climate change tends to polarise public opinion.
The introduction of the new policy has polarised the community.
Social media often polarises discussions, making compromise more difficult.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to unify - to bring together into a single group or whole
  • Synonym: divide - to separate into parts or groups, especially causing disagreement

Etymology

From French polariser, from pôle (pole), referring to the physical poles.
