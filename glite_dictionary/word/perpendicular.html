perpendicular

1. ADJ

/ ˌpɜːrpənˈdɪkjələr / (AmE), / ˌpɜːpənˈdɪkjʊlə / (BrE)
at an angle of 90° to a given line, plane, or surface; intersecting or forming right angles
at right angles; intersecting at 90°

Examples

The two walls are perpendicular to each other, creating a perfect corner.
Draw a line perpendicular to the base of the triangle from the top vertex.
The flagpole stands perpendicular to the ground.

Synset Examples

the axes are perpendicular to each other

Related Terms

  • Antonym: parallel - lines or planes that are always the same distance apart and never meet

Etymology

From Latin perpendicularis, from perpendiculum (a plumb line, a perpendicular line).

2. ADJ

/ ˌpɚ.pənˈdɪk.jə.lɚ / (AmE), / ˌpɜː.pənˈdɪk.jʊ.lə / (BrE)
(of a slope or surface) so steep as to be nearly vertical.
nearly vertical; very steep

Examples

The hikers were daunted by the perpendicular wall of rock before them.
A perpendicular drop separated the mountain trail from the valley below.
The perpendicular cliffs made access by boat nearly impossible.

Synset Examples

the great perpendicular face of the cliff

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vertical - upright or at a right angle to the horizon
  • Antonym: horizontal - parallel to the horizon; not vertical

Etymology

From Latin perpendicularis (vertical), from perpendiculum (plumb line), from pendere (to hang).

4. NOUN

/ ˌpɜr.pənˈdɪk.jə.lɚ / (AmE), / ˌpɜː.pənˈdɪk.jʊ.lə / (BrE)
A straight line or plane that is at a right angle to another line or surface.
a line or plane at right angles

Examples

The architect drew a perpendicular to mark the exact midpoint of the wall.
If two lines meet to form a right angle, one is a perpendicular to the other.
We dropped a perpendicular from the point to the base of the triangle.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: parallel - A line or surface always the same distance from another; not meeting at any point

Etymology

From Latin 'perpendicularis', from 'perpendiculum' (a plumb line), from 'per-' (through) + 'pendere' (to hang).

5. NOUN

/ ˌpɝː.pənˈdɪk.jə.lɚ / (AmE), / ˌpɜː.pənˈdɪk.jʊ.lə / (BrE)
a style of English Gothic architecture from the 14th and 15th centuries, characterized by strong vertical lines, four-centered arches, and elaborate fan vaulting
English Gothic architectural style, 14th–15th centuries

Examples

The choir was rebuilt in the Perpendicular style, featuring soaring windows and intricate tracery.
Many English cathedrals display elements of Perpendicular architecture, especially in their towers and nave arcades.
The use of four-centered arches distinguishes the Perpendicular period from earlier phases of Gothic design.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Decorated - an earlier English Gothic style with curvilinear ornament, preceding Perpendicular
  • Synonym: fan vaulting - a type of vaulting characteristic of the Perpendicular style

Etymology

From Latin perpendicularis, meaning 'vertical'; applied to this style for its emphasis on vertical lines.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05851763-n
Synset POS: n
