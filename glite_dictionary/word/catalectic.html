catalectic

1. NOUN

/ ˌkætəˈlɛktɪk / (AmE), / ˌkatəˈlɛktɪk / (BrE)
A line of verse in poetry that is missing one or more syllables in its final metrical foot.
a line missing final syllables (in poetry)

Examples

The final line of the poem is catalectic, giving it a sense of abruptness.
In classical poetry, a catalectic line often signals the end of a stanza.
Scholars debated whether the irregular rhythm was intentional or merely a catalectic error.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acatalectic - (of a line of verse) having the full number of syllables in the final foot
  • Antonym: hypercatalectic - (of a line of verse) having extra syllables in the final foot

Etymology

From Greek katalēktikos (incomplete in respect of syllables), from katalēgein (to leave off).

2. ADJ

/ ˌkætəˈlɛktɪk / (AmE), / ˌkatəˈlɛktɪk / (BrE)
(of a line of verse) metrically incomplete, especially lacking one or more syllables in the final metrical foot.
(of verse) lacking syllables at the end

Examples

The final line of the poem is catalectic, giving it an abrupt ending.
Many traditional hymns use catalectic lines to create a distinctive rhythm.
A catalectic verse can alter the overall flow and sound of a poem.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: acatalectic - (of verse) metrically complete; having the full number of syllables

Etymology

From Greek katalēktikos, from katalēgein (to leave off).
