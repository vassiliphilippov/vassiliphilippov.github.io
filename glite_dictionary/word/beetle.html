beetle

1. VERB

/ ˈbiːtəl / (AmE), / ˈbiːtəl / (BrE)
to move quickly or scurry away, especially in a hurried or furtive manner
move quickly or scurry away

Examples

The children beetled out of the classroom when the bell rang.
She beetled off before anyone could ask her a question.
We beetled up the hill to catch the sunset.

Synset Examples

He beetled up the staircase
They beetled off home

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scurry - to move quickly with short steps
  • Antonym: to dawdle - to move or act too slowly
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02107890-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈbiːtəl / (AmE), / ˈbiːtəl / (BrE)
to strike or pound with a heavy mallet known as a beetle
strike with a heavy mallet

Examples

The linen was beetled to give it a smooth, glossy finish.
Workers used to beetle the cloth after weaving it.
In the old mill, teams would beetle the fabric with heavy wooden mallets.

Etymology

From Middle English betel, from Old English bētel (‘a mallet’).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01402475-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈbiːtəl / (AmE), / ˈbiːtəl / (BrE)
a heavy tool with a large, usually wooden head, used for driving wedges, ramming paving stones, or crushing and beating objects
a heavy mallet-like tool

Examples

The stonemason used a beetle to settle the slabs into place.
The carpenter struck the wedge with a wooden beetle to split the log.
A beetle is essential for compacting paving stones during installation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mallet - a hammer with a large, usually wooden head, used to strike a surface without damaging it

Etymology

From Old English *beatel* (a mallet), related to 'beat'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈbiːtəl / (AmE), / ˈbiːt(ə)l / (BrE)
(of an object) to project or hang over something, often in a threatening or noticeable way.
jut or hang over
archaic

Examples

The ancient castle beetles over the narrow valley below.
Jagged cliffs beetled over the road, casting deep shadows.
Tall trees beetled above the cottage, giving it a gloomy air.

Synset Examples

This huge rock beetles over the edge of the town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overhang - to hang or extend over something
  • Synonym: jut - to stick out or project

Etymology

From Old English *bītol*, related to 'projecting' or 'jutting' (distinct from the insect meaning).

5. ADJ

/ ˈbiːtl̩ / (AmE), / ˈbiːtl̩ / (BrE)
(of a part of the body or an object) projecting or overhanging in a way that is noticeable or prominent.
projecting or overhanging (rare, as in 'beetle brows')
archaic

Examples

He had beetle brows that cast a shadow over his deep-set eyes.
The beetle edge of the cliff loomed over the valley below.
Her beetle forehead gave her a rather severe appearance.

Synset Examples

beetle brows

Etymology

Likely from Old English *bitela*, meaning ‘biter’, from *bītan* (to bite), possibly referring to the beetle insect's projecting jaws and transferred to describe projecting features.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01356364-s

6. NOUN

/ ˈbiːtəl / (AmE), / ˈbiːtl̩ / (BrE)
A type of insect with hard front wings that cover and protect its softer hind wings, and biting mouthparts.
insect with hard wing covers

Examples

A bright green beetle landed on the windowsill, its shell shining in the sunlight.
The scientist examined the beetle under a microscope to study its unique wing covers.
Many farmers use traps to control beetle populations that damage their crops.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ladybird - small beetle with red or yellow domed back and black spots
  • Synonym: weevil - a type of beetle with a long snout

Etymology

From Old English 'bitela', from 'bētan' (to bite), referring to its biting mouthparts.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02167116-n
Synset POS: n
