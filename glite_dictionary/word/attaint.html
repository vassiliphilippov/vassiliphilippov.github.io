to attaint

1. VERB

/ əˈteɪnt / (AmE), / əˈteɪnt / (BrE)
to bring shame, dishonor, or disgrace upon someone or something
bring shame or dishonor upon
archaic

Examples

The scandal threatened to attaint his reputation in the court.
The knight was attainted by rumors of treachery.
Fearing he might attaint his family's good name, he confessed his mistakes.

Synset Examples

he dishonored his family by committing a serious crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - cause shame or loss of respect
  • Antonym: honor - to show great respect or bring pride to

Etymology

From Old French *ateindre*, from Latin *attingere* (to touch upon, reach).

2. VERB

/ əˈteɪnt / (AmE), / əˈteɪnt / (BrE)
(historical) to condemn someone by attainder, especially resulting in the loss of civil rights and property following a conviction for treason or felony.
condemn by attainder (historical, legal)
archaic legal

Examples

In medieval England, nobles could be attainted for treason, losing all their lands and titles.
The parliament attainted the rebel leader without a fair trial.
Her family's estate was seized after her father was attainted for conspiracy.

Synset Examples

the man was attainted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: convict - to declare someone guilty of a crime in a court of law
  • Antonym: to acquit - to declare someone not guilty of a criminal charge

Etymology

From Old French *ateindre* (to convict), from Latin *attingere* (to touch upon, reach).
