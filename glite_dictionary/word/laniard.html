laniard

1. NOUN

/ ˈlæniərd / (AmE), / ˈlæniəd / (BrE)
A cord worn around the neck, shoulder, or wrist to carry an object such as a knife, whistle, or badge.
cord for carrying a small object

Examples

Police officers often wear their whistles attached to a laniard for easy access.
He looped the laniard around his neck to keep his knife secure while climbing.
Conference attendees received a badge on a blue laniard at registration.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lanyard - alternate spelling, more common, for the same object

Etymology

From French 'lanière' (thong, strap), from Old French 'lasniere'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈlæn.jɚd / (AmE), / ˈlæn.jəd / (BrE)
(nautical) A line or short rope used for extending, fastening, or securing rigging or other items on a ship.
nautical rope for rigging

Examples

Each sailor secured the mast with a laniard as the storm approached.
The old ship’s rigging included a weathered laniard that held the blocks in place.
He pulled the laniard tight to prevent the sail from flapping in the wind.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lanyard - alternative spelling; a cord or strap used to secure or fasten something

Etymology

From Old French *laniere* (strap, thong), probably via Middle English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03646616-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈlænjərd / (AmE), / ˈlanjəd / (BrE)
A cord with a hook used to fire certain types of cannon.
cord and hook to fire cannon

Examples

The gunner gripped the laniard and awaited the command to fire.
A sudden jerk of the laniard sent the cannon roaring into action.
To avoid premature ignition, he handled the laniard with great caution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lanyard - a cord, usually worn around the neck or wrist, used to secure something

Etymology

Variant spelling of "lanyard"; from Middle French *laniere* (strap).
