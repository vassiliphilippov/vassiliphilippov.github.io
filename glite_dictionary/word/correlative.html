correlative

1. ADJ

/ kəˈrɛlətɪv / (AmE), / kəˈrɛlətɪv / (BrE)
expressing or involving a reciprocal or complementary relationship, especially in grammar referring to conjunctions used in pairs, such as ‘either...or’ or ‘neither...nor’.
expressing reciprocal or complementary relationship

Examples

The two statements have a correlative relationship and support each other's validity.
In English grammar, 'both...and' are correlative conjunctions.
Her interest in mathematics was correlative with her aptitude for logical reasoning.

Synset Examples

correlative conjunctions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reciprocal - mutually corresponding or complementary

Etymology

From Medieval Latin correlativus, from Latin correlatio (reciprocal relation).

2. ADJ

/ kəˈrɛlətɪv / (AmE), / kəˈrɛlətɪv / (BrE)
(of two or more things) having a reciprocal or mutual relationship; corresponding in some way.
mutually related; having reciprocal relationship

Examples

Economic growth and environmental protection are often seen as correlative goals in sustainable development.
The witness's testimony was correlative with the evidence presented by the prosecution.
Mental and physical health can have correlative effects on each other.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reciprocal - given, felt, or done in return; mutual
  • Antonym: independent - not influenced by others; not mutually related

Etymology

From Latin correlativus, from co- (together) + relativus (having reference).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01981362-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ kəˈrɛləˌtɪv / (AmE), / kəˈrɛlətɪv / (BrE)
either of two or more things that are closely related, often in a complementary or corresponding way
one of two related or complementary things

Examples

In the pair 'either...or,' each word is a correlative of the other.
Rights and responsibilities are often regarded as legal correlatives.
The concept of cause has its correlative in the concept of effect.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: complement - something that completes or goes well with another
  • Antonym: opposite - something entirely different or contrary

Etymology

From Latin correlativus, from correlatio (relation together).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05866991-n
Synset POS: n
