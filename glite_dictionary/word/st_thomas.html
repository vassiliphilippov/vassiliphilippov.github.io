St. Thomas

1. PROPN

/ seɪnt ˈtɑːməs / (AmE), / seɪnt ˈtɒməs / (BrE)
(in Christianity) one of the twelve apostles of Jesus, known for doubting the resurrection until he saw Jesus himself.
the Apostle who doubted Jesus's resurrection

Examples

Many paintings depict St. Thomas touching the wounds of the resurrected Christ.
In Christian tradition, St. Thomas is often remembered for doubting until he saw proof.
The story of St. Thomas teaches the value of faith and the struggle with doubt.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Doubting Thomas - a person who refuses to believe without direct evidence

Etymology

St. Thomas, from Greek Thomas, from Aramaic ta’oma’ (twin).

2. PROPN

/ seɪnt ˈtɑːməs / (AmE), / seɪnt ˈtɒməs / (BrE)
A name often referring to St. Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274), Italian Dominican friar, philosopher, and theologian, recognized as a Doctor of the Church for his influential work reconciling faith and reason.
Italian theologian and philosopher, Doctor of the Church

Examples

Many scholars study the writings of St. Thomas to understand medieval philosophy.
The teachings of St. Thomas have shaped Catholic doctrine for centuries.
As a Doctor of the Church, St. Thomas is still influential today.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Thomas Aquinas - Italian theologian and philosopher considered a Doctor of the Church

Etymology

From the Latin 'Sanctus Thomas', referring to Thomas Aquinas, with 'Thomas' from the Aramaic word for 'twin'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10840394-n
Synset POS: n
