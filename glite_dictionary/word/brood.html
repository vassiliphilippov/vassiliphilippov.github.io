brood

1. VERB

/ brud / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
(of something threatening, dark, or menacing) to hang or hover over; to loom persistently.
hang or hover menacingly

Examples

A thick fog brooded over the city, making the morning feel eerie and still.
A sense of unease brooded above the meeting as tensions ran high.
Storm clouds brooded on the horizon, warning of the coming rain.

Synset Examples

The terrible vision brooded over her all day long

Etymology

From Old English *brōd* (brood), later extended figuratively to describe an ominous presence.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02749936-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ bruːd / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
to think deeply and anxiously or moodily about something, often over a prolonged period
think deeply and anxiously

Examples

She tends to brood over her mistakes long after everyone else has forgotten them.
After hearing the news, he sat alone, quietly brooding for hours.
It's not healthy to brood about problems you can't change.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ruminate - to think deeply about something; to ponder
  • Synonym: to dwell - to keep thinking or talking about something, especially something troubling
  • Synonym: worry - to feel or cause to feel anxious or troubled

Etymology

From Old English *brōd* (offspring, brood), related to German *Brut* (brood), later extended to refer metaphorically to persistent, worried thinking.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00706015-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ bruːd / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
a group of young animals, especially birds, produced or cared for at one time by a parent or parents
a group of young animals born at one time

Examples

The hen protected her brood from danger as they wandered through the yard.
The duck and her brood swam across the pond in a neat line.
When the fox approached, the mother bird gathered her brood under her wings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: litter - a group of young animals born to an animal at one time, especially mammals
  • Synonym: clutch - a group of eggs or young birds hatched at one time

Etymology

From Old English *brōd* (offspring, hatch), related to breed.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08007266-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ brud / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
(of a bird) to sit on eggs to keep them warm until they hatch.
sit on eggs to incubate them

Examples

The hen began to brood as soon as she laid her eggs.
Some species of penguins brood their eggs for weeks in harsh conditions.
If the bird does not brood the eggs, they will not hatch.

Synset Examples

Birds brood
The female covers the eggs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to incubate - to sit on eggs and keep them warm so they will hatch

Etymology

From Old English 'brōd' (offspring, hatch, brood), related to the idea of hatching.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ brud / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
to think gloomily or remain in a state of sulky dissatisfaction, often showing displeasure
to think gloomily or sulk

Examples

He tends to brood whenever things don't go his way.
After the argument, she would brood alone in her room for hours.
Instead of brooding over the loss, he tried to move on with his life.

6. VERB

/ brud / (AmE), / bruːd / (BrE)
to remain silent and sullen, often thinking deeply about something that is upsetting or worrying.
be silent and moody; dwell on negative thoughts

Examples

After the argument, he sat in his room and brooded for hours.
She tends to brood over small mistakes instead of moving on.
The dark clouds outside mirrored his brooding mood.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to mope - to be gloomy, miserable, and silent
  • Synonym: to ruminate - to think deeply about something, often in a contemplative or troubled way
  • Antonym: to cheer up - to become happier or less gloomy

Etymology

From Old English *brōd* (offspring, brood), later used figuratively of mental incubation.
