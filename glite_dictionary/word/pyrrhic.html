pyrrhic

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɪrɪk / (AmE), / ˈpɪrɪk / (BrE)
(in prosody) relating to or consisting of a metrical foot with two short or unstressed syllables.
metrical foot of two unstressed syllables

Examples

The poem makes use of pyrrhic feet to create a particular rhythm.
A pyrrhic substitution in the second line disrupts the natural stress pattern.
Scholars debate the frequency of pyrrhic meters in English verse.

Synset Examples

pyrrhic verses

Etymology

From Greek *purrhikhios* (pertaining to a war dance), later applied to this metrical foot.

5. ADJ

/ ˈpɪrɪk / (AmE), / ˈpɪrɪk / (BrE)
(of a victory) won at such great cost that it is almost tantamount to defeat; relating to or resembling the exploits of Pyrrhus, especially victories with heavy losses.
(of a victory) gained at heavy cost

Examples

Winning the championship turned out to be a pyrrhic victory, as most of the team was injured.
The CEO's decision was a pyrrhic triumph; profits rose briefly but morale plummeted.
Historians often use the term pyrrhic to describe battles where both sides suffered heavy losses.

Synset Examples

a Pyrrhic victory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Pyrrhic victory - a victory gained at too great a cost
  • Degree Lower: costly - involving great loss or sacrifice

Etymology

From the name Pyrrhus, King of Epirus, whose costly military victories over the Romans gave rise to the term.
