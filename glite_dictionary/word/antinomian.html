antinomian

1. ADJ

/ ˌæn.tɪˈnoʊ.mi.ən / (AmE), / ˌæn.tɪˈnəʊ.mi.ən / (BrE)
relating to or characterized by the rejection of established laws, especially in the context of religious or moral laws.
rejecting established religious or moral laws

Examples

Some radical groups in the 17th century were considered antinomian because they challenged traditional religious laws.
Her antinomian beliefs led her to reject the authority of church doctrine.
The preacher warned against antinomian interpretations of scripture that encouraged lawlessness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: legalist - one who adheres strictly to law or religious rules

Etymology

From Medieval Latin antinomianus, from Greek anti- (against) + nomos (law).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæntɪˈnoʊmiən / (AmE), / ˌæntɪˈnəʊmiən / (BrE)
A person who believes that, under the gospel dispensation of grace, the moral law is of no use or obligation because faith alone is necessary to salvation.
a follower of the doctrine that faith alone is necessary for salvation

Examples

The theologian was accused of being an antinomian after questioning the ongoing role of moral law in Christian life.
During the Reformation, some antinomians argued that faith alone guaranteed salvation, dismissing traditional rules.
He denied that his teachings were antinomian, insisting he still valued moral conduct.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antinomianism - the doctrine that Christians are released by grace from the obligation of obeying the moral law
  • Antonym: legalist - a person who adheres strictly to the law or to a particular set of rules

Etymology

From Greek anti- (against) + nomos (law) + -ian.
