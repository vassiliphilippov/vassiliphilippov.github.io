jostle

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʒɑːsəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒɒsəl / (BrE)
an act of pushing or shoving, especially in a crowd to make your way through
an act of pushing (in a crowd)

Examples

There was a sudden jostle at the entrance as people tried to get out.
He gave me a friendly jostle as we moved through the busy crowd.
The commuters experienced the usual jostle during rush hour on the subway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shove - an act of pushing someone or something forcefully
  • Synonym: bump - an act of knocking into someone or something

Etymology

From Middle English justelen, frequentative of justen (to joust).

2. VERB

/ ˈdʒɑsəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒɒsəl / (BrE)
to push, elbow, or bump against someone or something, typically in a crowd, to make one's way or to compete for space
push or shove to move or compete

Examples

We had to jostle through the crowd to catch a glimpse of the performers.
Commuters often jostle for space on the busy subway trains during rush hour.
She skillfully jostled her way to the front of the line without causing offense.

Synset Examples

We had to jostle our way to the front of the platform

Related Terms

  • Synonym: push - move someone or something away by pressing against it
  • Synonym: shove - push someone or something roughly

Etymology

From late Middle English, apparently from Old French *jostiller*, based on *joster* (to joust).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01918446-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈdʒɑsəl / (AmE), / ˈdʒɒsəl / (BrE)
to push, elbow, or bump against someone roughly, typically in a crowd
push or bump against someone

Examples

Shoppers began to jostle for position as the store doors opened.
He tried not to jostle anyone as he made his way through the busy hallway.
The runners jostled for the lead during the chaotic start of the race.

Synset Examples

The passengers jostled each other in the overcrowded train

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shove - to push someone or something roughly
  • Synonym: bump - to collide or come into contact with someone or something

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English justlen, frequentative of justen ‘to joust’.
