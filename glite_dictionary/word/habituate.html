to habituate

1. VERB

/ həˈbɪtʃuˌeɪt / (AmE), / həˈbɪtjueɪt / (BrE)
to make someone or oneself used to something, especially so that it no longer seems strange or new
to make used to something

Examples

Wild animals can habituate to human presence if they are exposed often enough.
Over time, residents became habituated to the constant noise from the nearby highway.
It takes several weeks to habituate yourself to a new work schedule.

Synset Examples

She became habituated to the background music

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accustom - to make familiar with something through repeated experience
  • Synonym: to desensitize - to make less sensitive to something, especially through repeated exposure

Etymology

From Latin habituare (to make accustomed), from habitus (condition, appearance).

2. VERB

/ həˈbɪtʃuˌeɪt / (AmE), / həˈbɪtjʊeɪt / (BrE)
to take or consume (a substance) regularly or as a habit
consume regularly as a habit

Examples

After years of smoking, he became habituated to nicotine and found it hard to quit.
Some people habituate themselves to caffeine by drinking several cups of coffee a day.
Patients may gradually habituate to certain medications if they use them regularly.

Synset Examples

She uses drugs rarely

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accustom - to make familiar by use or experience

Etymology

From Latin habituatus, past participle of 'habituare' (to make accustomed).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01167359-v
Synset POS: v
