some

1. ADV

/ sʌm / (AmE), / sʌm / (BrE)
used before a number or quantity to mean 'approximately' or 'about', when the exact amount is not known or exact value is not important
imprecise quantity; approximately

Examples

There were some thirty students present at the meeting.
The trip lasted some forty-five minutes, give or take a few.
His collection numbers some three hundred rare books.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: about - approximately; close to a number or amount, not exact
  • Synonym: roughly - not exactly but close in amount or value

2. DET

/ sʌm / (AmE), / sʌm / (BrE)
used to refer to an unspecified amount or number of something, especially when more than one or a little is intended
an unspecified quantity or number

Examples

Would you like some tea with your breakfast?
We bought some flowers to brighten up the room.
Some books were missing from the library shelf.

Synset Examples

have some milk
some roses were still blooming
having some friends over
some apples
some paper

Related Terms

  • Antonym: any - used to refer to one or more of a thing, with a sense sometimes opposed to 'some' in questions and negatives

Etymology

From Old English sum (some, a certain one).

3. DET

/ sʌm / (AmE), / sʌm / (BrE)
an unspecified but considerable number or amount of people or things; more than a few, but not a large number
an unspecified, relatively large number or amount

Examples

We waited here for some weeks before we got any news.
After some years, he returned to his hometown.
Some people find it difficult to adapt to change.

Synset Examples

they were here for some weeks
we did not meet again for some years

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: several - more than two but not many, a small number
  • Degree Higher: many - a large number of people or things

Etymology

Old English 'sum', of Germanic origin, related to Dutch 'som', German 'summe', and Latin 'summa' (sum, total).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01556991-s
Synset POS: s

4. ADJ

/ sʌm / (AmE), / sʌm / (BrE)
(informal) Used to emphasize that someone or something is remarkable or impressive in some way.
remarkable or impressive (emphatic)
informal

Examples

That was some party—they really went all out with the decorations and entertainment.
He is some drummer; I've never seen anyone play like that before!
The storm last night was some storm, with thunder shaking the whole house.

Synset Examples

that was some party
she is some skier

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remarkable - worthy of attention; striking
  • Antonym: ordinary - not distinctive or remarkable; commonplace
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01683245-s
Synset POS: s
