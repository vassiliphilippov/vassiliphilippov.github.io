epidural

1. NOUN

/ ˌɛpɪˈdʊrəl / (AmE), / ˌɛpɪˈdjʊərəl / (BrE)
A type of regional anesthesia in which an anesthetic is injected into the epidural space of the spinal cord, causing loss of sensation in the lower body; commonly used to relieve pain during childbirth or certain surgeries.
regional anesthesia injected near the spinal cord
medical

Examples

She decided to have an epidural to help manage the pain during labor.
The doctor explained the risks and benefits of receiving an epidural before the surgery.
Many women choose an epidural for pain relief during childbirth.

Etymology

From Greek 'epi-' (upon) and Latin 'dura mater' (tough mother, outer membrane of the spinal cord).

2. ADJ

/ ˌɛpɪˈdʊrəl / (AmE), / ˌɛpɪˈdjʊərəl / (BrE)
Situated on or outside the dura mater, the outermost membrane covering the brain and spinal cord.
on or outside the dura mater
medical

Examples

The surgeon injected the anaesthetic into the epidural space before beginning the procedure.
An epidural catheter may be used to deliver pain relief during childbirth.
The scan revealed an epidural abscess pressing on the spinal cord.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intrathecal - within the sheath—the area under the dura mater

Etymology

From Greek epi- (upon) + Latin duram mater (hard mother, referring to the dura mater).
