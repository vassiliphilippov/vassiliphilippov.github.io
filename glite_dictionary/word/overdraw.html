to overdraw

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvɚˈdrɔ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdrɔː / (BrE)
to describe something as more significant, impressive, or dramatic than it really is; to exaggerate
to exaggerate or enlarge beyond the truth

Examples

The documentary tended to overdraw the importance of this political movement.
She warned him not to overdraw the achievements of the project in his report.
In his speech, the mayor overdrew the city’s recovery after the disaster.

Synset Examples

tended to romanticize and exaggerate this ‘gracious Old South’ imagery

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exaggerate - to represent something as greater or more important than it actually is
  • Antonym: to understate - to describe something as less important or serious than it actually is

Etymology

From Old English 'oferdragan', meaning to drag over or draw excessively.

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈdrɔ / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈdrɔː / (BrE)
to withdraw more money from a bank account than is available, causing the account to go into negative balance
withdraw more than is available in an account

Examples

She realized she would overdraw her account if she wrote another check.
If you overdraw your account, the bank may charge a fee.
He accidentally overdrew his account by making a large withdrawal.

Synset Examples

She overdrew her account

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overdraft - a deficit in a bank account caused by drawing more money than the account holds

Etymology

From Old English 'oferdragan' (to drag over), combining 'over' and 'draw'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02317166-v
Synset POS: v
