indigo

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪndɪˌɡoʊ / (AmE), / ˈɪndɪɡəʊ / (BrE)
a deep blue dye obtained from certain plants or produced synthetically, used especially for coloring fabrics
deep blue dye from plants or synthetic sources

Examples

The traditional fabric was dyed with indigo to achieve its rich blue color.
Farmers in India have cultivated plants for indigo production for centuries.
Modern jeans are often colored using synthetic indigo.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dye - a substance used to change the color of something

Etymology

From Spanish 'índigo' and Latin 'indicum', meaning 'from India', referring to the origin of the dye.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪndɪˌɡoʊ / (AmE), / ˈɪndɪɡəʊ / (BrE)
A deep blue-violet color, typically positioned between blue and violet in the visible spectrum.
deep blue-violet color

Examples

The artist chose indigo for the night sky in her painting.
He wore a shirt of deep indigo to the party.
The flowers bloomed in brilliant shades of indigo and purple.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: blue - color between green and violet in the spectrum
  • Degree Higher: violet - bluish-purple color in the spectrum

Etymology

From Latin 'indicum', from Greek 'indikon', meaning ‘Indian (dye)’, referring to dye brought from India.

4. NOUN

/ ˈɪn.dɪˌɡoʊ / (AmE), / ˈɪn.dɪ.ɡəʊ / (BrE)
A deciduous subshrub of southeastern Asia with pinnate leaves and clusters of red or purple flowers, used as a source of indigo dye.
plant source of indigo dye

Examples

The indigo plant was cultivated extensively for use in dyeing cloth.
Farmers in India once relied on indigo as a major cash crop.
Clusters of purple flowers cover the indigo during the spring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indigofera - genus of plants that includes species used to produce indigo dye

Etymology

From Latin 'indicum', from Greek 'indikon' (blue dye from India).
