to forestall

1. VERB

/ fɔrˈstɔl / (AmE), / fɔːˈstɔːl / (BrE)
to act in advance to prevent something from happening or to deal with it before it arises
prevent or deal with in advance

Examples

By warning her colleagues early, she was able to forestall any major objections to the proposal.
The government acted quickly to forestall a banking crisis.
Proper maintenance can forestall many common car problems before they become serious.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening
  • Synonym: to anticipate - to regard as probable or expect and prepare for

Etymology

From Old English 'forstǣlan', meaning 'to intercept, anticipate'.

2. VERB

/ fɔrˈstɔl / (AmE), / fɔːˈstɔːl / (BrE)
to prevent or hinder (an event or action) by taking action ahead of time
prevent in advance

Examples

The government introduced new regulations to forestall a financial crisis.
He took quick action to forestall any potential objections from the committee.
They tried to forestall criticism by explaining their decision early.

Synset Examples

My sense of tact forbids an honest answer
Your role in the projects precludes your involvement in the competitive project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to preclude - to make impossible, especially by taking action in advance
  • Synonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening
  • Antonym: to allow - to permit or make possible

Etymology

From Old English 'fore-' (before) + 'stall' (to place), meaning to act in advance.
