to choreograph

1. VERB

/ ˈkɔriəˌɡræf / (AmE), / ˈkɒrɪəɡrɑːf / (BrE)
to compose and arrange the movements and steps for a dance, especially for a performance or production
compose and arrange dance steps

Examples

The director asked her to choreograph the opening number for the show.
He spent weeks trying to choreograph a waltz for the wedding scene.
They plan to choreograph the dance routine to match the upbeat music.

Synset Examples

Balanchine choreographed many pieces to Stravinsky's music

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to compose - to create or put together music, poetry, or other works
  • Antonym: to improvise - to perform or create spontaneously without preparation

Etymology

From French 'chorégraphier', from Greek 'khoros' (dance) + 'graphein' (to write).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɔːriəˌɡræf / (AmE), / ˈkɒriəˌɡrɑːf / (BrE)
to plan and control the details and movements of an event, activity, or process, often in a careful or elaborate way
plan and coordinate details carefully

Examples

The negotiations were so complex that every move was choreographed ahead of time.
The director choreographed the event down to the smallest detail.
Her campaign appearances are carefully choreographed to maximize media coverage.

Synset Examples

The meeting between the two Presidents had been carefully choreographed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to organize - to arrange systematically or put in order
  • Antonym: to improvise - to create or perform without preparation

Etymology

From French *chorégraphie*, from Greek *khoreia* (dance) + *-graphia* (writing).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00712244-v
Synset POS: v
