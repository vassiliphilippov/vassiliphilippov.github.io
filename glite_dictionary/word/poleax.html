poleax

1. NOUN

/ ˈpoʊˌlæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊˌlæks / (BrE)
A weapon from the Middle Ages consisting of a long-handled ax, often combined with a hammer or spike, used in battle.
medieval long-handled battle ax

Examples

The knight wielded a heavy poleax during the tournament.
Museum visitors admired the ornate poleax on display in the medieval armor collection.
During the siege, soldiers used a poleax to breach the castle doors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: halberd - A combined spear and battle-ax from the same era

Etymology

From Old French 'poleis' or Middle English 'polax', from 'poll' (head) + 'ax'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpoʊˌlæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊˌlæks / (BrE)
A heavy ax with a flat hammer on the back of the blade, formerly used to slaughter cattle.
slaughterhouse tool; ax with hammer head

Examples

The butcher used a poleax to humanely slaughter the cattle.
The old poleax on display at the museum showed signs of long use.
He cleaned the poleax at the end of the workday to prevent rust.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: axe - a tool with a heavy bladed head mounted across a handle

Etymology

From Old French 'poleis', variation of 'pollax', from 'poll' (head) + 'ax'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈpoʊˌlæks / (AmE), / ˈpəʊˌlæks / (BrE)
to hit, knock down, or fell someone or something as if with a poleax; to strike or overwhelm decisively
to knock down or strike decisively

Examples

The boxer was poleaxed by a sudden uppercut in the third round.
The shocking news poleaxed him into silence for several minutes.
A stray branch poleaxed the hiker, knocking him to the ground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fell - to cut down or knock down

Etymology

From poleaxe, a type of medieval weapon; 'pole' + 'axe'.
