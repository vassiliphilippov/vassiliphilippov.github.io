votary

1. NOUN

/ ˈvoʊ.tə.ri / (AmE), / ˈvəʊ.təri / (BrE)
a person who is bound by solemn vows to a religion or a life of devotion or service
person bound by vows, often religious

Examples

As a votary, she spent years in the sanctuary, dedicated to spiritual practice.
The monastery housed dozens of votaries who had pledged a life of service and prayer.
He became a votary of the order, taking solemn vows to dedicate his life to the cause.

Synset Examples

monasteries of votaries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devotee - a person very devoted to a religion, cause, or leader

Etymology

From Latin 'votarius', from 'votum' (vow).

2. NOUN

/ ˈvoʊ.tə.ri / (AmE), / ˈvəʊ.tə.ri / (BrE)
a person who is ardently devoted or dedicated to a particular cause, person, or activity, often in a way resembling religious zeal
devoted adherent or follower

Examples

She is a passionate votary of classical music, attending every major concert in the city.
As a votary of environmental causes, he dedicates much of his time to local activism.
Many a scientific votary has spent years in pursuit of a single breakthrough.

Synset Examples

the cultured votary of science

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devotee - a person who is very enthusiastic about someone or something
  • Synonym: disciple - a follower or student of a teacher or leader

Etymology

From Latin 'votarius', from 'votum' (vow).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈvoʊtəˌri / (AmE), / ˈvəʊtəɹi / (BrE)
a devoted worshipper, especially a priest or priestess in a non-Christian religion or cult
devoted worshipper in a non-Christian religion

Examples

In the ancient city, a votary of Isis tended to the temple's sacred flames.
As a votary of Demeter, she participated in yearly harvest rituals.
He became a dedicated votary after years of study in the mysteries of the cult.

Synset Examples

a votary of Aphrodite

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devotee - an enthusiastic follower or supporter
  • Synonym: priest - a religious leader authorized to perform sacred rituals
  • Antonym: skeptic - a person inclined to doubt or question accepted opinions

Etymology

From Latin 'votarius', from 'votum' meaning 'vow' or 'pledge'.
