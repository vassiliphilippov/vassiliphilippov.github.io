hard-boiled

1. ADJ

/ hɑrd ˈbɔɪld / (AmE), / hɑːd ˈbɔɪld / (BrE)
(of a person) tough and unemotional, showing little sympathy or sentiment.
tough, unemotional person
informal

Examples

The hard-boiled detective never let emotions interfere with his work.
She gave a hard-boiled response to the heartbreaking story, showing no sign of pity.
Despite his hard-boiled exterior, he deeply cared about his friends.

Synset Examples

faced a case-hardened judge

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soft-hearted - kind and sympathetic; easily moved emotionally

Etymology

Derived from the literal 'hard-boiled' (eggs), used figuratively in American slang from the late 19th and early 20th centuries to describe someone with a tough or unsentimental character.

2. ADJ

/ ˈhɑrd ˌbɔɪld / (AmE), / ˈhɑːd ˌbɔɪld / (BrE)
(of an egg) cooked in boiling water until both the white and yolk are solid.
egg cooked until yolk is solid

Examples

He likes his eggs hard-boiled with a pinch of salt.
She prepared a hard-boiled egg for breakfast.
To make a salad, chop up one hard-boiled egg and mix it in.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soft-boiled - (of an egg) cooked so the white is set and the yolk remains soft

3. ADJ

/ ˌhɑrdˈbɔɪld / (AmE), / ˌhɑːdˈbɔɪld / (BrE)
(of a person) tough and unsentimental, especially as a result of life experience; showing little emotion or sympathy.
tough and unsentimental (personality)

Examples

The hard-boiled detective refused to let his emotions get in the way of the case.
She gave a hard-boiled response, showing no sympathy for his excuses.
Years of experience in the newsroom had made him hard-boiled and cynical.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soft-hearted - kind and sympathetic; gentle in temperament

Etymology

Originally used (mid-19th century) for eggs; figurative sense of 'tough, cynical' attested from early 20th century.
