lackey

1. NOUN

/ ˈlæki / (AmE), / ˈlæki / (BrE)
A male servant, especially one employed as a footman, often used to refer to someone who behaves servilely towards someone in power.
male servant; servile follower

Examples

The wealthy count was always accompanied by his loyal lackey.
Treated by his boss as a mere lackey, he grew resentful of the constant demands.
In the play, the king's lackey provided comic relief with his bumbling behavior.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: servant - a person employed to perform duties for others, especially in a house
  • Antonym: master - a person who has people working for them, especially servants or slaves

Etymology

From French laquais (footman, servant).

2. NOUN

/ ˈlæki / (AmE), / ˈlæki / (BrE)
a person who acts obsequiously toward someone important in order to gain personal advantage
obsequious person; sycophant

Examples

He was treated as nothing more than a lackey by the powerful CEO.
The senator was surrounded by lackeys eager to do his bidding.
She refused to become a lackey for the corrupt management.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sycophant - a person who flatters someone powerful for personal gain
  • Synonym: toady - a person who behaves in a servile way toward someone in power
  • Synonym: flatterer - someone who gives excessive compliments to gain favor
  • Antonym: rebel - a person who opposes authority

Etymology

From Middle French laquais (servant, footman), possibly from Turkish ulak (messenger).
