to obtrude

1. VERB

/ əbˈtruːd / (AmE), / əbˈtruːd / (BrE)
to force oneself or something upon others, often without invitation or in an unwelcome manner
impose or force upon others

Examples

He tends to obtrude his opinions during every meeting, even when no one asks.
Please don't obtrude your personal matters into our professional discussions.
A good host ensures that their presence does not obtrude on the comfort of their guests.

Synset Examples

The colors don't intrude on the viewer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intrude - to put oneself deliberately into a place or situation where one is unwelcome
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove oneself or something from a place, situation, or involvement

Etymology

From Latin 'obtrudere' (to thrust at or against).

2. VERB

/ əbˈtruːd / (AmE), / əbˈtruːd / (BrE)
to push or thrust outward or forward, especially in a way that is unwelcome or intrusive
push or thrust outward

Examples

He tends to obtrude his opinions during meetings, even when no one asks for them.
Tall weeds obtruded from the cracks in the sidewalk, making the path difficult to walk.
Bright lights from the street obtruded into the darkness of the bedroom at night.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intrude - to enter or put oneself into a place or situation unwelcome or inappropriate
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to move away or back, often the opposite of thrusting forward

Etymology

From Latin 'obtrudere' (to thrust against, to push forward).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01877150-v
Synset POS: v
