wearing

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɛrɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɛə.rɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of having something on your body as clothing or an accessory
the act of wearing clothing or adornment

Examples

The wearing of uniforms is required in many private schools.
Her wearing of bright colors made her stand out in the crowd.
Daily wearing of sunscreen can help protect your skin from damage.

Synset Examples

she bought it for everyday wear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wear - the act of wearing clothes (noun)
  • Antonym: removal - the act of taking off something worn

Etymology

From Old English 'werian' (to wear).

2. ADJ

/ ˈwɛrɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɛːrɪŋ / (BrE)
causing fatigue or exhaustion; physically or mentally tiring
causing exhaustion or fatigue

Examples

After a wearing day at work, she just wanted to relax at home.
The long journey was particularly wearing for the small children.
Dealing with constant changes can be emotionally wearing over time.

Synset Examples

an exhausting march
the visit was especially wearing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exhausting - causing extreme tiredness
  • Synonym: tiring - making someone feel tired
  • Antonym: invigorating - making one feel stronger and more energetic

Etymology

Present participle of 'wear,' from Old English 'werian' (to wear out, consume).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00840788-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ ˈwerɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈwɛərɪŋ / (BrE)
The process of being gradually reduced or smoothed by friction, pressure, or repeated action, especially by natural forces such as water or wind; erosion or abrasion.
gradual reduction or erosion by friction

Examples

The wearing of rocks by the constant flow of the river forms smooth pebbles over time.
Years of wearing from wind and sand had shaped the mountain into a unique formation.
The wearing caused by daily use can gradually dull the sharpest blade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erosion - the gradual destruction or diminution of something by natural forces like wind or water
  • Synonym: abrasion - the process of scraping or wearing something away

Etymology

Derived from the verb 'wear', from Old English 'werian' (to carry, adorn, or use).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13496741-n
Synset POS: n
