iambic

1. NOUN

/ aɪˈæmbɪk / (AmE), / aɪˈæmbɪk / (BrE)
A line of verse composed of iambs (metrical feet with an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable).
line of verse made of iambs

Examples

The poet composed the sonnet in perfect iambic, giving the lines a rhythmic flow.
Many English poems are written in iambic to achieve a natural speaking cadence.
Shakespeare’s plays often use iambic for both dialogue and verse.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: iamb - a metrical foot with an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable

Etymology

From Latin 'iambicus', from Greek 'iambikos', relating to 'iambos' (iamb).

2. ADJ

/ aɪˈæmbɪk / (AmE), / aɪˈæmbɪk / (BrE)
Of, relating to, or consisting of iambs (metrical feet with an unstressed syllable followed by a stressed syllable), especially in poetry.
relating to iambs in poetry

Examples

Shakespeare often wrote his sonnets in iambic pentameter.
Many English nursery rhymes use iambic rhythms for their sing-song quality.
She analyzed the iambic structure of each line in the poem.

Synset Examples

iambic pentameter

Related Terms

  • Antonym: trochaic - relating to trochees (metrical feet with a stressed syllable followed by an unstressed syllable)

Etymology

From Latin *iambicus*, from Greek *iambikos* (relating to an iamb).
