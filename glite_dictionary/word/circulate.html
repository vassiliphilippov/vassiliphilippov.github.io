to circulate

1. VERB

/ ˈsɝːkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to cause information, news, or ideas to become widely known or spread among many people
make information widely known

Examples

Rumors began to circulate after the announcement was made.
The company quickly circulated the memo to all employees.
False stories can circulate rapidly on social media.

Synset Examples

spread information
circulate a rumor
broadcast the news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spread - to cause something to become widely distributed or known
  • Synonym: to disseminate - to spread or distribute information widely
  • Antonym: to suppress - prevent from becoming known or circulated

Etymology

From Latin circulare (to make circular), from circulus (circle).

/ ˈsɝːkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːkjʊleɪt / (BrE)

move freely from person or place to place

Examples

Related Terms

3. VERB

/ ˈsɝːkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
(of information, stories, or news) to become widely known and passed from person to person.
become widely known (news, rumor, etc.)

Examples

The news of their engagement circulated quickly through the small town.
False stories can circulate online before anyone has a chance to check the facts.

Synset Examples

the rumor spread
the story went around in the office

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spread - (of information) to become known by more people

Etymology

From Latin circulare (to make round, to go around), from circulus (circle).

4. VERB

/ ˈsɝːkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
to cause (a liquid, air, or gas) to move continuously or freely through a closed system or area
cause to move around in a system

Examples

The ceiling fan helps to circulate the air throughout the house.
Doctors use a machine to circulate blood during heart surgery.
He installed a pump to circulate the water in the fish tank.

Synset Examples

The fan circulates the air in the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flow - to move steadily and continuously in a current or stream
  • Antonym: to stagnate - to stop moving or flowing and become inactive or still

Etymology

From Latin circulatus, past participle of circulare 'to make circular', from circulus 'small ring'.

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ ˈsɜːrkjəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈsɜːkjʊleɪt / (BrE)
(of a liquid or gas) to move continuously through a closed system or area, often returning to the starting point.
move continuously through a system

Examples

The heating system ensures that warm air circulates throughout the whole building.
Blood circulates in the body to deliver oxygen to every cell.
Without an open window, the air doesn't properly circulate in the room.
