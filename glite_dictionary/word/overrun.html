overrun

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊ.vɚˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊ.vəˈrʌn / (BrE)
(of a vehicle, person, or process) to go beyond or past the intended limit or boundary.
to go past a limit or boundary

Examples

The plane overran the runway during landing because of the wet conditions.
If you overrun the end of the page, your text will be cut off.
The meeting overran its scheduled time by half an hour.

Synset Examples

The plane overran the runway

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to exceed - to go beyond a set limit

Etymology

From Middle English 'overrennen', from Old English 'oferrennan' ('over' + 'rennan' meaning 'to run').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00707690-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈrʌn / (BrE)
(of a liquid or contents) to flow or run over the edge of something or beyond a defined limit.
to flow or run over a limit or brim

Examples

The river began to overrun its banks after the heavy rainfall.
The saucepan will overrun if you keep boiling the milk unattended.
If you add too much data, the buffer might overrun and cause an error.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overflow - (of a liquid) to flow over the brim or limit of a container

Etymology

From Old English ofer- (over) + rinnan (to run).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02076087-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvərˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈrʌn / (BrE)
an amount produced or present that exceeds what was expected or required
excess beyond expected amount

Examples

The factory experienced an overrun in production, so there were more parts than needed.
They donated the overrun of brochures to a local school rather than waste them.
Due to printing overrun, we have extra copies available for distribution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: excess - more than is necessary or allowed
  • Antonym: shortage - the state of not having enough of something needed
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00916174-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˌoʊ.vɚˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊ.vəˈrʌn / (BrE)
to spread over or occupy a place in large numbers, often causing damage or nuisance
invade or spread in great numbers

Examples

Locusts can overrun entire fields in a matter of hours, destroying crops as they go.
By the end of summer, weeds had overrun the garden, choking out the flowers.
The abandoned building was quickly overrun by stray cats and rats.

Synset Examples

the roaches infested our kitchen

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infest - to be present in large numbers, typically so as to cause damage or disease
  • Synonym: swarm - to move in or form a large group, especially insects

Etymology

From Old English oferyrnan, from ofer (over) + rinnan (to run).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02024265-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈrʌn / (BrE)
to occupy or spread over a place in large numbers, often in a way that is difficult to control or causes harm
to occupy or infest in large numbers

Examples

The garden was quickly overrun by weeds after just a few weeks of neglect.
Tourists have overrun the small island, making it crowded during the summer season.
The farmhouse became overrun with mice after the cold weather set in.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infest - to be present in large numbers so as to cause damage or discomfort
  • Synonym: to invade - to enter a place in large numbers, often causing harm or disruption

Etymology

From Old English 'oferrinnan', meaning 'to run over or invade'.

6. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈrʌn / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈrʌn / (BrE)
(especially of armed forces) to seize control of and defeat a place or position by force, typically quickly and completely.
take control by force; defeat

Examples

The invading army overran the capital in just two days.
During the war, enemy troops overran several border towns before being stopped.
Rebels overran the government stronghold after a fierce battle.

Synset Examples

the Crusaders overran much of the Holy Land

Related Terms

  • Synonym: capture - to take control of a place or person by force
  • Antonym: retreat - to withdraw from enemy forces as a result of defeat

Etymology

From Old English 'oferrinnan': 'ofer' (over) + 'rinnan' (to run).
