cushion

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈkʊʃən / (BrE)
the layer of air that supports a hovercraft or similar vehicle, keeping it above the surface
layer of air under a hovercraft

Examples

The hovercraft glided smoothly over the lake on a cushion of air.
A thin cushion beneath the vehicle allows it to traverse both land and water.
Engineers improved the cushion to reduce friction and increase speed.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈkʊʃən / (BrE)
a device that absorbs shocks or reduces the impact of sudden movements, especially in machinery or vehicles
shock-absorbing device in machines or vehicles

Examples

The train's suspension uses a cushion to absorb the impact of the rails.
A new cushion was installed in the elevator system to ensure smoother stops.
The faulty cushion caused the machinery to vibrate excessively during operation.

Synset Examples

the old car needed a new set of shocks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: damper - a device that lessens vibrations or controls movement
  • Synonym: shock absorber - a device designed to absorb and damp shock impulses

3. VERB

/ ˈkʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈkʊʃ.ən / (BrE)
to lessen the force or effect of a hit, impact, or unpleasant situation
lessen the force of impact

Examples

The airbags are designed to cushion passengers in the event of a crash.
She tried to cushion the blow by breaking the news gently.
Pillows were used to cushion the fragile items during shipping.

Synset Examples

cushion the blow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to soften - to make less hard or severe

Etymology

From Old French coissin, from Vulgar Latin *coxinum (pillow).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈkʊʃ.ən / (BrE)
a soft bag filled with padding such as feathers, foam, or air, used to make a seat or surface more comfortable
soft bag for comfort or decoration

Examples

Sarah arranged a bright cushion on her favorite armchair.
He bought a new cushion to make his office chair more comfortable.
The cat curled up on the soft cushion by the window.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pillow - a soft support for the head, especially in bed

Etymology

From Old French *coissin*, from Latin *coxa* (hip, thigh).
