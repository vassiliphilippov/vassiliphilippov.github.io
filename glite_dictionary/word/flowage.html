flowage

1. NOUN

/ ˈfloʊɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfləʊɪdʒ / (BrE)
the gradual internal motion or deformation within a solid body, such as rock, especially due to heat or pressure
gradual deformation of a solid body

Examples

Geologists observed flowage in the rock samples due to intense heat over time.
The slow flowage of glacier ice shapes valleys and landscapes over centuries.
Unlike sudden breakage, flowage in metals occurs gradually under stress.

Synset Examples

rock fracture and rock flowage are different types of geological deformation

Related Terms

  • Antonym: fracture - break or crack in a solid body, often opposed to flowage in geology

Etymology

From 'flow' + '-age', indicating action or result of flowing.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfloʊɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfləʊɪdʒ / (BrE)
an area of land covered by water as a result of deliberate flooding, especially for reservoirs or water management.
artificially created flooded area

Examples

Several rare bird species nest along the flowage each spring.
The park was established to protect the wetlands surrounding the flowage.
Residents were relocated when the valley was turned into a flowage for flood control.

Synset Examples

many campsites were located near the flowage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reservoir - a large natural or artificial lake used to store water
  • Degree Lower: pond - a small body of still water, often artificial

Etymology

From 'flow' + '-age', denoting the result of flowing (19th century, United States).

3. NOUN

/ ˈfloʊ.ɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈfləʊ.ɪdʒ / (BrE)
the act of flooding or the state of being filled to overflowing, especially with water
flooding; overflowing with water

Examples

The recent dam construction resulted in significant flowage throughout the valley.
Flowage permits are required for man-made reservoirs affecting large tracts of land.
Heavy rains caused flowage well beyond the riverbanks, damaging nearby homes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flooding - the covering or submerging of normally dry land with a large amount of water

Etymology

From 'flow' (to move as a liquid) + '-age' (forming nouns denoting action or process).
