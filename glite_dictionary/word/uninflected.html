uninflected

1. ADJ

/ ˌʌnɪnˈflɛktɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌnɪnˈflɛktɪd / (BrE)
(of a word or form) not changed by inflection; remaining in its base or basic form.
not changed by inflection

Examples

In English, most nouns are uninflected in the plural form unless they end with 's' or another marker.
The word 'fish' is often uninflected, serving as both singular and plural.
Some languages have more uninflected words, while others rely heavily on inflection for grammatical meaning.

Synset Examples

‘boy’ and ‘swim’ are uninflected English words

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inflected - changed by inflection; having endings or internal changes to convey grammatical information

Etymology

From un- (not) + inflected, based on Latin inflectere (to bend).

2. ADJ

/ ˌʌnɪnˈflɛktɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌnɪnˈflɛktɪd / (BrE)
(of a word or language) not altered in form to indicate grammatical functions such as tense, number, or case.
not changed in form (grammar)

Examples

Some English nouns, such as 'sheep', are uninflected for plural.
In analytic languages, verbs often remain uninflected and use auxiliary words instead.
The word 'fast' is uninflected regardless of its role as adjective or adverb.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inflected - changed in form to express grammatical function

Etymology

From un- (not) + inflected, from Latin inflectere (to bend).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ˌʌn.ɪnˈflɛk.tɪd / (AmE), / ˌʌn.ɪnˈflɛk.tɪd / (BrE)
(of speech, words, or forms) not varied by inflection; monotonous or not altered in pitch, tone, or grammatical form.
not inflected; monotone or unchanged in form

Examples

Her uninflected speech made the lecture difficult to follow.
In some languages, many words remain uninflected regardless of their grammatical role.
The actor was criticized for delivering lines in an uninflected tone.

Synset Examples

uninflected words
monotonic uninflected speech

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inflected - (of words or forms) having undergone grammatical change (inflection)
  • Synonym: monotone - (of speech) having a single unvaried tone or pitch

Etymology

Formed from the prefix 'un-' (not) + 'inflected' (from Latin 'inflectere', to bend).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: a
