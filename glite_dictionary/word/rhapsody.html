rhapsody

1. NOUN

/ ˈræp.sə.di / (AmE), / ˈræp.sə.di / (BrE)
an expression of great enthusiasm, excitement, or joy, often in speech, writing, or music
enthusiastic expression of emotion

Examples

Her speech was a rhapsody of happiness that left everyone smiling.
He broke into a rhapsody about his favorite city, unable to contain his enthusiasm.
The novel ended with a rhapsody on the beauty of nature.

Synset Examples

rhapsodies of joy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ecstasy - a state of overwhelming joy or delight

Etymology

From Greek rhapsōidia (recitation of epic poetry).

2. NOUN

/ ˈræp.sə.di / (AmE), / ˈrap.sə.di / (BrE)
(Music) A free-flowing instrumental composition, often in one extended movement, typically expressing strong emotion or exuberance.
emotional, free-form musical composition

Examples

The composer's latest rhapsody was performed to standing ovations across Europe.
Liszt's Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2 is a famous example of this musical form.
The orchestra captured every shifting mood of the rhapsody, from gentle lyricism to exuberant outbursts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fantasia - a musical composition with a free form and often improvisational style
  • Antonym: concerto - a classical composition for solo instrument(s) and orchestra, often in three movements

Etymology

From Greek *rhapsōidia* (recitation of epic poetry), via Latin *rhapsodia*.

3. NOUN

/ ˈræp.sə.di / (AmE), / ˈrap.sə.di / (BrE)
(in ancient Greece) An epic poem or a portion of an epic poem that was composed for public recitation.
epic poem for recitation (Ancient Greece)

Examples

In ancient times, a rhapsody was performed at festivals in honor of the gods.
The poet specialized in adapting Homeric tales into a rhapsody for public audiences.
Historians study how the rhapsody influenced Greek culture and oral tradition.

Etymology

From Greek rhapsōidia (recitation of epic poetry), from rhaptein (to stitch) + ōidē (song).
