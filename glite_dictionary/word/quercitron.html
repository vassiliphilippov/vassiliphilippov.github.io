quercitron

1. NOUN

/ kwɜːrˈsɪtrɑn / (AmE), / kwəːˈsɪtrɒn / (BrE)
a medium to large deciduous tree native to eastern North America, with dark outer bark and yellow inner bark used for dyeing and tanning
North American tree with dye-producing bark

Examples

The inner bark of the quercitron was once widely used to produce a yellow dye.
Many hardwood forests in the eastern United States contain the quercitron tree.
Researchers studied how the quercitron affects the diversity of deciduous forests.

Etymology

From French *quercitron*, from Latin *quercus* (oak) + *citron* (lemon), referencing the yellow dye.

2. NOUN

/ kwərˈsɪtrən / (AmE), / kwəːˈsɪtrən / (BrE)
a yellow dye obtained from the bark of the black oak tree, especially Quercus velutina, used in textiles and art
yellow dye from the black oak tree

Examples

The weaver used quercitron to achieve a vibrant yellow hue in the fabric.
In the 19th century, quercitron was a popular dye for coloring wool and silk.
Extracts from the bark of the tree are processed to obtain quercitron for textile industries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dye - a substance used to color materials

Etymology

From New Latin 'quercitron,' combining 'quercus' (oak) and possibly 'citron' (lemon, for color)
