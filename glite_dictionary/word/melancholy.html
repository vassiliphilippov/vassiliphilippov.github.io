melancholy

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɛlənˌkɑli / (AmE), / ˈmɛlənˌkɒli / (BrE)
(archaic) In ancient and medieval physiology, a bodily humor once believed to be secreted by the kidneys or spleen and to cause sadness or depression.
archaic: bodily humor causing sadness
archaic

Examples

Ancient physicians believed an excess of melancholy affected both mood and health.
In medieval times, the humor melancholy was thought to originate in the spleen.
He attributed her persistent sadness to a surplus of melancholy in her body.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melancholia - severe sadness or depression, also formerly considered a medical condition
  • Antonym: sanguine - one of the four bodily humors, associated with a cheerful temperament

Etymology

From Greek *melankholia* (black bile), from *melas* (black) + *khole* (bile).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɛl.ənˌkɑ.li / (AmE), / ˈmel.ən.kə.li / (BrE)
A deep, pensive, and long-lasting sadness, often with a thoughtful or reflective quality.
thoughtful sadness

Examples

A wave of melancholy swept over him as he remembered his childhood home.
The music had an air of melancholy that lingered long after it ended.
Despite the celebration, she felt a quiet melancholy in her heart.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sadness - the state of being sad; sorrow
  • Degree Higher: depression - a medical condition of severe despondency and dejection
  • Antonym: cheerfulness - the state of being noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

From Greek melankholia, from melas 'black' and kholē 'bile'; originally linked to medieval medical theory.

5. NOUN

/ ˈmɛlənˌkɑli / (AmE), / ˈmɛlənkəli / (BrE)
A deep, persistently gloomy or depressed state of mind, sometimes seen as a constitutional or habitual tendency.
habitual gloominess or sadness

Examples

His melancholy was apparent to everyone who saw his somber expression and slow movements.
There was a sense of melancholy in the air after the team lost the final match.
Despite her outward smile, she was burdened by an inner melancholy that would not lift.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: depression - A state of severe despondency and dejection
  • Antonym: cheerfulness - The quality or state of being noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

From Greek 'melankholia', from 'melas' (black) + 'khole' (bile), once believed in medieval medicine to cause sadness.
