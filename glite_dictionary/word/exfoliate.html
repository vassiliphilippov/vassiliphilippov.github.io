to exfoliate

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈsfoʊ.li.eɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈsfəʊ.li.eɪt / (BrE)
(of a material or layer) to come off in thin, flat pieces or layers.
to come off in thin layers or sheets

Examples

The paint began to exfoliate after years of exposure to harsh sunlight.
As the tree aged, its bark would exfoliate in delicate sheets.
The mineral tends to exfoliate easily, revealing the fresh surface beneath.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flake - to break or come off in thin, small pieces or layers

Etymology

From Latin exfoliare, from ex- (out) + folium (leaf).

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈsfoʊ.li.eɪt / (AmE), / ɛksˈfəʊ.lɪ.eɪt / (BrE)
(of a plant) to grow by producing or unfolding leaves.
grow by unfolding leaves (plants)

Examples

In spring, many trees exfoliate, revealing fresh green leaves.
The new variety of fern exfoliates rapidly during the rainy season.
As the climate warms, some plants begin to exfoliate earlier each year.

3. VERB

/ ɛksˈfoʊliˌeɪt / (AmE), / ɛksˈfəʊliˌeɪt / (BrE)
(of a material, surface, or organism) to shed or peel off layers, scales, or laminae.
shed or peel off layers or scales

Examples

Over time, the bark of the birch tree will exfoliate in thin, papery strips.
Certain lizards exfoliate their skin as they grow.
During drought, some rocks naturally exfoliate, revealing fresh surfaces beneath.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peel - to remove or come off in strips or layers

Etymology

From Late Latin exfoliare, from Latin ex- (out) + folium (leaf).
