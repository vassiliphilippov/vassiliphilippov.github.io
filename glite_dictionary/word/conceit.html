conceit

1. NOUN

/ kənˈsit / (AmE), / kənˈsiːt / (BrE)
an elaborate or extended metaphor in poetry, often involving a far-fetched or surprising comparison between two very different things
elaborate poetic metaphor or comparison

Examples

The poet used a brilliant conceit to compare love to a compass, making the poem memorable.
John Donne is famous for his metaphysical conceits that connect the physical and spiritual worlds.
The teacher explained that the poem's conceit lies in likening the soul to a spinning wheel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metaphor - a figure of speech comparing two unrelated things
  • Synonym: simile - a figure of speech comparing two unlike things using 'like' or 'as'

Etymology

From Middle English 'conceit', from Old French 'conceite', from Latin 'concipere' (to take in, conceive).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈsit / (AmE), / kənˈsiːt / (BrE)
Excessive pride in oneself or one's abilities; an overly high opinion of one's worth or skill.
excessive pride or self-importance

Examples

His constant conceit made it difficult for others to work with him.
She was criticized for her conceit and unwillingness to accept advice.
The artist's conceit was evident in the way he talked about his own work.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vanity - excessive pride in one's appearance, qualities, or achievements
  • Antonym: humility - the quality of having a modest or low view of one's importance

Etymology

From Latin 'conceptus' (concept, conceived), via Old French 'conceite' (thought, fancy).
