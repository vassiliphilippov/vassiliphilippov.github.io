coordination

1. NOUN

/ ˌkoʊ.ɔːr.dɪˈneɪ.ʃn̩ / (AmE), / kəʊˌɔː.dɪˈneɪ.ʃn̩ / (BrE)
the organization of different elements or activities into a functioning, harmonious whole
organization of diverse elements

Examples

Effective coordination between departments is essential for the project’s success.
The lack of coordination resulted in confusion during the event.
She is responsible for the coordination of all marketing activities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: integration - the process of combining parts into a whole
  • Antonym: fragmentation - the process of breaking into parts

Etymology

From Latin 'coordinatio', from 'coordinare' (to arrange in order).

3. NOUN

/ koʊˌɔːrdɪˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / kəʊˌɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
The grammatical relationship between two or more elements of the same type, usually joined by a coordinating conjunction such as 'and' or 'or'.
grammatical linking of equal elements

Examples

Proper coordination joins two clauses with the word 'and' or 'but'.
In English grammar, coordination occurs when two nouns like 'cats and dogs' are linked.
The teacher explained that coordination uses conjunctions to link equal parts of a sentence.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: subordination - grammatical relationship where one clause is dependent on another

Etymology

From Latin 'coordinatio' (arrangement, combination), from 'co-' (together) + 'ordinare' (arrange).

4. NOUN

/ koʊˌɔrdɪˈneɪʃən / (AmE), / kəʊˌɔːdɪˈneɪʃən / (BrE)
the ability to use different parts of the body together smoothly and efficiently
skillful movement control

Examples

Good hand-eye coordination is essential for playing video games effectively.
Physical coordination often improves with practice and repetition.
The gymnast impressed the judges with her exceptional coordination during the routine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agility - ability to move quickly and easily
  • Antonym: clumsiness - lack of coordination or skill in movement

Etymology

From French 'coordination', from Latin 'coordinatio' (arrangement, combination).
