folio

1. NOUN

/ ˈfoʊlioʊ / (AmE), / ˈfəʊliəʊ / (BrE)
the system or method of numbering the pages of a book or manuscript
system of numbering pages

Examples

The folio used Roman numerals instead of Arabic ones in this edition.
Please check if the folio aligns with the table of contents.
Antique books often display their folio at the top corner of each page.

Etymology

From Latin 'folium' (leaf, sheet of paper).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfoʊlioʊ / (AmE), / ˈfəʊliəʊ / (BrE)
a single sheet of paper, especially one of a manuscript or book; also, a book made up of large sheets of paper folded once to form two leaves (four pages)
a sheet of paper in a book or manuscript

Examples

The original Shakespearean plays were published in a large folio format.
The museum displayed a medieval folio written by hand on vellum.
He carefully turned each folio of the ancient manuscript.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leaf - a single sheet or page in a book
  • Degree Lower: quarto - a book or page size made from folding a sheet twice
  • Degree Lower: octavo - a book or page size made by folding a sheet three times

Etymology

From Latin foliō (ablative of folium 'leaf'), referring to a leaf or sheet of paper.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfoʊlioʊ / (AmE), / ˈfəʊliəʊ / (BrE)
a book or manuscript made with large sheets of paper folded in the middle, producing two leaves or four pages; especially used to refer to an early or historical edition of a work
large-format book or manuscript

Examples

The library owns an original folio edition of Shakespeare's plays.
He purchased a rare folio containing medieval maps.
Scholars examined the ancient folio for marginal notes by its previous owners.

Synset Examples

the first folio of Shakespeare's plays

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: quarto - a book or manuscript format where each sheet is folded twice to make four leaves
  • Degree Lower: octavo - a book or manuscript format with sheets folded three times to make eight leaves

Etymology

From Latin folium (leaf, sheet of paper).
