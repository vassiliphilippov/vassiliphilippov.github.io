Polynesian

1. ADJ

/ ˌpoʊliˈniʒən / (AmE), / ˌpɒlɪˈniːziən / (BrE)
relating to Polynesia, its people, or its culture
of Polynesia or its people or culture

Examples

The traditional Polynesian tattoo designs are admired around the world.
She cooked a delicious meal using authentic Polynesian recipes.
The museum is hosting an exhibition of Polynesian artifacts this month.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Melanesian - relating to Melanesia, its people, or culture
  • Antonym: Micronesian - relating to Micronesia, its people, or culture

Etymology

From Polynesia, from Greek polús (many) + nēsos (island).

2. NOUN

/ ˌpɑː.liˈniː.ʒən / (AmE), / ˌpɒ.lɪˈniː.zi.ən / (BrE)
a person who is a native or inhabitant of Polynesia, a region of the central and southern Pacific Ocean
a native or inhabitant of Polynesia

Examples

The Polynesian welcomed us with a warm smile when we arrived on the island.
Many Polynesians maintain traditional customs despite the influence of modern culture.
She studied the history and cultural practices of Polynesian societies for her thesis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Micronesian - a native or inhabitant of Micronesia
  • Synonym: Melanesian - a native or inhabitant of Melanesia

Etymology

From French *Polynésien*, from Polynésie (Polynesia), ultimately from Greek *polys* (many) + *nēsos* (island).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˌpɑː.ləˈniː.ʒən / (AmE), / ˌpɒl.ɪˈniː.ʒən / (BrE)
the group of Austronesian languages spoken throughout the islands of Polynesia and parts of the Pacific, including Hawaiian, Maori, and Samoan
Austronesian language group of Polynesia

Examples

The Polynesian languages include Maori, Hawaiian, and Tongan.
Many Polynesian words have been borrowed into English, especially in the Pacific region.
Linguists study the similarities between different Polynesian languages to trace their origins.

Etymology

From Polynesia, formed from Greek 'polys' (many) + 'nēsos' (island) + English suffix -an.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
