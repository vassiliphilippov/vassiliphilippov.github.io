to distrain

1. VERB

/ dɪˈstreɪn / (AmE), / dɪˈstreɪn / (BrE)
to legally seize someone's property in order to obtain payment of money owed, especially rent
legally seize property for a debt
legal

Examples

The landlord threatened to distrain the tenant's goods unless the overdue rent was paid.
If the company fails to pay its debts, creditors may distrain upon its assets.
Historically, landlords had the right to distrain for unpaid rent without a court order.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take possession of something by force or legal right

Etymology

From Old French *destreindre*, from Latin *distringere* (to stretch apart, to seize).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈstreɪn / (AmE), / dɪˈstreɪn / (BrE)
(of a person with legal authority) to seize someone's goods in order to obtain payment of rent or other money owed.
legally seize goods for debt
legal

Examples

The landlord had the right to distrain the tenant’s furniture for unpaid rent.
If you fail to pay the council tax, the authorities may distrain upon your property.
Under the old law, a creditor could distrain goods to recover the money owed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take possession of something by force or legal right
  • Antonym: release - to set free or return property seized

Etymology

From Old French 'destreindre', from Latin 'distringere' (to stretch apart, seize).

3. VERB

/ dɪˈstreɪn / (AmE), / dɪˈstreɪn / (BrE)
to legally seize someone's property in order to obtain payment of a debt, typically under a court order or legal right
legally seize property for debt
legal

Examples

If the tenant fails to pay rent, the landlord may distrain upon the tenant's goods.
The sheriff was authorized to distrain the debtor's assets under the court's order.
The company threatened to distrain her furniture unless the overdue payments were made.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to seize - to take hold of something forcibly or legally

Etymology

From Old French 'destreindre', from Latin 'distringere' (to pull apart, hinder, constrain).
