rationalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈræʃənəlˌɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈræʃənəˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
(philosophy) the belief that reason rather than experience is the primary source of knowledge.
belief that reason is the main source of knowledge

Examples

Some philosophers argue that rationalism provides a more reliable foundation for scientific inquiry than empiricism.
Her commitment to rationalism led her to distrust knowledge that could not be logically proven.
Throughout history, rationalism has influenced mathematics and the development of theoretical science.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: empiricism - the theory that all knowledge is derived from sense-experience

Etymology

From French 'rationalisme', from Latin 'rationalis' (of reason).

2. NOUN

/ ˈræʃənəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈræʃənəlɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The belief or doctrine that reason should be the primary source for determining action or regulating conduct, rather than emotion, tradition, or authority.
the doctrine that reason should guide conduct

Examples

Many philosophers argued that rationalism offers a superior framework for making ethical decisions.
As a supporter of rationalism, she believed that logic, not tradition, should shape our actions.
The movement toward rationalism in the 18th century changed how people approached moral and social issues.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: empiricism - the theory that experience and observation should guide knowledge or conduct

Etymology

From French 'rationalisme', from Latin 'rationalis' (rational).

3. NOUN

/ ˈræʃənəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈræʃənəlɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
The belief that human reason, rather than divine revelation, is the chief source and test of religious truth.
belief that reason determines religious truth

Examples

Some theologians reject rationalism, insisting on the importance of faith and divine revelation.
Enlightenment thinkers often embraced rationalism in their interpretations of religion.
The rise of rationalism led to new debates about the role of reason in spiritual matters.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: empiricism - the theory that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience
  • Degree Lower: skepticism - doubt or disbelief in religious doctrines

Etymology

From French 'rationalisme', from Latin 'rationalis' (reasonable).
