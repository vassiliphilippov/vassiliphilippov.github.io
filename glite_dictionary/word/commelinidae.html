Commelinidae

1. NOUN

/ kəˌmiː.ləˈnaɪ.diː / (AmE), / kɒˌmɛl.ɪˈniː.diː / (BrE)
a subclass or superorder of monocotyledonous flowering plants, including about 19,000 species in 25 families, such as the grasses, sedges, bromeliads, and ginger family
subclass of monocotyledonous flowering plants
medical

Examples

The family Gramineae, which includes most grasses, is part of the Commelinidae.
Many wetland plants found in tropical regions belong to the Commelinidae.
Recent botanical studies have revised the classification within Commelinidae to reflect new genetic evidence.

Etymology

From the genus name Commelina and the suffix -idae, used in botanical classification.

2. NOUN

/ ˌkɑːmɛlɪˈnaɪdiː / (AmE), / ˌkɒməlɪˈniːdɪiː / (BrE)
a subclass of monocotyledonous plants comprising about 19,000 mostly terrestrial herb species in families such as Cyperaceae, Gramineae, Bromeliaceae, and Zingiberaceae, often found in moist environments
subclass of mostly herbaceous monocotyledonous plants

Examples

The family of grasses is included within the Commelinidae subclass.
Botanists often study the ecological adaptations of Commelinidae in wet habitats.
Recent research has led to a reclassification of some species within Commelinidae.

Etymology

From the plant family name 'Commelinaceae', after the botanist Jan Commelijn, combined with the standard botanical suffix '-idae' for subclasses.
