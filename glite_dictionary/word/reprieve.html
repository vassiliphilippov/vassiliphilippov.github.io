reprieve

1. VERB

/ rɪˈpriv / (AmE), / rɪˈpriːv / (BrE)
to postpone the punishment of a convicted criminal, especially to delay an execution
postpone criminal punishment
legal

Examples

The governor decided to reprieve the prisoner just hours before the scheduled execution.
After new evidence was presented, the court agreed to reprieve the man's punishment.
If the president does not reprieve her, the sentence will be carried out tomorrow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pardon - official forgiveness of a crime
  • Antonym: to execute - to carry out a death sentence

Etymology

From late Middle English, from Old French *reprendre* (to take back), from Latin *reprehendere* (to hold back, check).

6. NOUN

/ rɪˈpriv / (AmE), / rɪˈpriːv / (BrE)
an official delay or temporary suspension of a punishment, especially the death penalty
official delay of punishment

Examples

The governor granted a reprieve to the prisoner hours before the execution was to take place.
The court's decision offered a welcome reprieve from an otherwise severe sentence.
The family was relieved to hear about the temporary reprieve as they prepared the appeal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pardon - the action of forgiving or being forgiven for an error or offense
  • Antonym: sentence - a punishment assigned to a defendant found guilty by a court

Etymology

From Old French 'reprever' (to remonstrate, to reproach), later influenced by English legal usage.
