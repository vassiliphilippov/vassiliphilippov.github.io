contingent

1. NOUN

/ kənˈtɪndʒənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪndʒənt / (BrE)
a group of people representing a larger organization or country, especially at a meeting, event, or assembly
a representative group of people

Examples

A French contingent arrived at the summit to participate in the climate discussions.
The university sent a large contingent to compete in the championships.
Each company contributed a small contingent of experts to the industry taskforce.

Synset Examples

each nation sent a contingent of athletes to the Olympics

Etymology

From Latin 'contingentem', present participle of 'contingere' (to happen, befall).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07992867-n
Synset POS: n

2. ADJ

/ kənˈtɪndʒənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪndʒɪənt / (BrE)
(of something) depending on or determined by specific conditions or circumstances.
dependent on conditions

Examples

His promotion is contingent on the completion of the project.
The offer is contingent upon your agreement to the terms.
Our travel plans are contingent on the weather.

Synset Examples

arms sales contingent on the approval of Congress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conditional - subject to, or depending on, a condition or conditions
  • Antonym: unconditional - not subject to any conditions or limitations

Etymology

From Latin contingent-, contingere (to touch, befall).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00558079-s
Synset POS: s

3. NOUN

/ kənˈtɪndʒənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪndʒənt / (BrE)
a group of soldiers forming a temporary military unit, often as part of a larger force
temporary military unit or group

Examples

A French contingent participated in the peacekeeping mission alongside other nations.
The base welcomed a new contingent of engineers and medics yesterday.
Each country contributed a small contingent to the allied forces.

Synset Examples

the peacekeeping force includes one British contingent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: detachment - a group of soldiers separated from a larger group for a specific duty

Etymology

From Latin 'contingent-', from 'contingere' meaning 'to touch, befall, happen'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08421506-n
Synset POS: n

4. ADJ

/ kənˈtɪndʒənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪndʒ(ə)nt / (BrE)
possible but not certain to occur; depending on chance or conditions
possible but not certain to occur

Examples

The bonus will be paid only if certain contingent conditions are met.
The project's success is contingent on receiving sufficient funding.
We have budgeted for contingent expenses that might arise unexpectedly.

Synset Examples

they had to plan for contingent expenses

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possible - able to be done or likely to happen
  • Antonym: certain - sure to happen; definite

Etymology

From Latin 'contingent-', present participle of 'contingere' (to befall, happen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01827915-s
Synset POS: s

5. ADJ

/ kənˈtɪndʒənt / (AmE), / kənˈtɪndʒ.ənt / (BrE)
(of events or outcomes) subject to chance, dependent on conditions or circumstances that cannot be controlled; uncertain.
uncertain; dependent on chance

Examples

Her acceptance to the university is contingent on her final exam results.
The success of the event is contingent upon the weather staying dry.
Investment decisions are often contingent on economic forecasts.

Synset Examples

the results of confession were not contingent, they were certain

Related Terms

  • Antonym: certain - sure to happen; not uncertain
  • Synonym: conditional - subject to a condition

Etymology

From Latin 'contingens', present participle of 'contingere' (to touch, befall).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00342831-s
Synset POS: s
