banian

1. NOUN

/ ˈbæniən / (AmE), / ˈbæniən / (BrE)
a loose-fitting jacket or gown, originally of cotton and worn in India, later adopted in Europe as informal indoor wear in the 18th century
loose-fitting jacket, originally from India

Examples

In colonial portraits, the scholar was often depicted wearing a comfortable banian.
Elegant gentlemen in the 18th century sometimes chose a silk banian for casual mornings at home.
The museum exhibit displayed a finely embroidered banian once owned by a British officer in India.

Etymology

From Hindi banyān, referring to a type of vest or jacket, ultimately from Portuguese 'banian' (Gujarati merchant), possibly via Arabic.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbæniən / (AmE), / ˈbanɪən / (BrE)
An East Indian fig tree (Ficus benghalensis) with aerial roots that descend to the ground and develop into additional trunks, forming a large canopy.
East Indian fig tree with aerial roots

Examples

The ancient banian in the village stretched its branches over the entire square.
A banian can cover a surprisingly large area due to its multiple trunks.
Many people gathered in the shade of the sprawling banian on hot afternoons.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: banyan - East Indian tree with aerial roots (alternative spelling)

Etymology

From Portuguese 'baniano', from Gujarati 'vāṇiyo' (a Hindu trader), because the tree was often found near bazaars.
