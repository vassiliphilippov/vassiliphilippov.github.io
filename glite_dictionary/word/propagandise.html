to propagandise

1. VERB

/ prɑːˈpæɡənˌdaɪz / (AmE), / prɒpəˈɡændaɪz / (BrE)
to subject (someone) to propaganda; to spread propaganda to influence someone's beliefs or actions
to subject to propaganda
british

Examples

The regime tried to propagandise the youth with nationalist ideals.
She accused the media of attempting to propagandise the public during the election.
It is unethical to propagandise children with biased information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to propagandize - to spread or promote propaganda, especially in American English

Etymology

From 'propaganda,' formed with the verb suffix '-ise/-ize.'

2. VERB

/ prɑːˈpæɡənˌdaɪz / (AmE), / prɒpəˈɡændaɪz / (BrE)
to spread or promote an idea, belief, or information, especially in a way intended to influence people's opinions; to subject to propaganda
spread or promote propaganda
british

Examples

The organization was accused of trying to propagandise young people with biased educational materials.
Several articles were written to propagandise the new government reforms.
Opponents claim that public broadcasters should not propagandise on behalf of any political party.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to propagandize - to spread propaganda (American spelling)

Etymology

From 'propaganda', ultimately from Latin 'propagare' (to spread). British spelling of 'propagandize'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00972764-v
Synset POS: v
