cope

1. NOUN

/ koʊp / (AmE), / kəʊp / (BrE)
a long, loose cloak worn by priests or bishops during religious ceremonies
ceremonial cloak worn by clergy

Examples

The bishop wore a richly embroidered cope at the Easter service.
The priest's cope caught the candlelight as he processed down the aisle.
She admired the golden threads woven into the ceremonial cope.

Etymology

From Latin cappa (cloak).

2. NOUN

/ koʊp / (AmE), / kəʊp / (BrE)
A brick or stone that is placed sideways at the top of a wall to form a protective cap or finish.
brick or stone capping at a wall's top

Examples

The mason carefully positioned the cope on top of the new garden wall.
After years of weather, the cope needed to be replaced to prevent leaks.
They chose a decorative stone cope for the boundary wall to enhance its appearance.

Etymology

From Old French 'cope', from Latin 'capa' meaning 'cape' or 'covering'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03508241-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ koʊp / (AmE), / kəʊp / (BrE)
to manage to do or achieve something, especially when there are limited resources or difficult circumstances
manage with limited resources

Examples

With only a few ingredients left in the fridge, she managed to cope and prepare dinner.
During the power outage, we had to cope with candles and bottled water.
He learned how to cope on a tight budget during college.

Synset Examples

We got by on just a gallon of gas
They made do on half a loaf of bread every day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to get by - to manage with difficulty to survive or accomplish something
  • Synonym: to make do - to manage with what is available rather than what is ideal

Etymology

From Latin 'colapus', used in the sense 'to meet in battle, contend with'.

Source: WordNet
