torpedo

1. NOUN

/ tɔrˈpidoʊ / (AmE), / tɔːˈpiːdəʊ / (BrE)
A small explosive device placed on a railroad track, which explodes when a train passes over it to warn the engineer of danger ahead.
railway warning explosive device

Examples

In the early days of railroads, a torpedo was used to warn trains of danger ahead.
The crew placed a torpedo on the track to notify the approaching train of an obstruction.
Upon hearing the loud bang of the torpedo, the engineer immediately slowed down the train.

2. NOUN

/ tɔrˈpiːdoʊ / (AmE), / tɔːˈpiːdəʊ / (BrE)
a slow-moving, bottom-dwelling ray of the order Torpediniformes, with a rounded body and electric organs on each side of the head that can emit strong electric discharges
electric ray of the Torpediniformes order

Examples

The torpedo hides under the sand, waiting for prey to come close before using its electric shock.
Marine biologists studied the torpedo to understand how its electric organs functioned.
A diver spotted a large torpedo near the rocky seabed during the expedition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: electric ray - a ray fish capable of producing electric discharges

Etymology

From Latin *torpedo* (numbness, paralysis), from *torpēre* (to be numb).

3. VERB

/ tɔrˈpiːdoʊ / (AmE), / tɔːˈpiːdəʊ / (BrE)
to attack or hit something with torpedoes, typically in a military context
attack with torpedoes

Examples

The submarine torpedoed the enemy vessel during the night attack.
Several ships were torpedoed off the coast during the conflict.
The navy planned to torpedo the blockade to open up the harbor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bombard - to attack with bombs or heavy artillery

Etymology

From torpedo (noun), which derives from Latin 'torpedo' (numbness, electric ray).

7. NOUN

/ tɔrˈpiːdoʊ / (AmE), / tɔːˈpiːdəʊ / (BrE)
A long, cylindrical, self-propelled underwater weapon that explodes on contact with or near its target.
self-propelled underwater explosive weapon

Examples

The submarine launched a torpedo at the enemy ship during the naval exercise.
Early torpedoes were relatively slow, but modern versions travel at high speeds underwater.
The destroyer managed to evade the incoming torpedo just in time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: missile - a self-propelled weapon for use in the air or space
  • Synonym: depth charge - an underwater anti-submarine explosive weapon

Etymology

From Italian 'siluro' and earlier from Latin 'torpere' (to be numb or paralyzed), referring originally to the electric ray fish, whose shock could stun.
