quid

1. NOUN

/ kwɪd / (AmE), / kwɪd / (BrE)
An informal term for one pound sterling (the basic unit of money in the UK and Northern Ireland).
informal term for a pound sterling
informal british

Examples

He paid fifty quid for that jacket, which seems like a bargain.
Can you lend me a quid until tomorrow?
She earns about three hundred quid a week in her new job.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pound - the basic unit of money in the UK

Etymology

Possibly from Italian 'scudo' (a gold or silver coin), or from Latin 'quid' (what); precise origin uncertain.

2. NOUN

/ kwɪd / (AmE), / kwɪd / (BrE)
A small, chewable lump of something, especially tobacco.
wad of something chewable, esp. tobacco

Examples

He tucked a quid of tobacco into his cheek before starting work.
Farmers were known to keep a quid in their mouths while tending the fields.
He spat out his quid after a long chew.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plug - a piece of chewing tobacco shaped into a solid mass

Etymology

Origin unknown; possibly from dialect or earlier forms meaning a lump or mass.

3. NOUN

/ kwɪd / (AmE), / kwɪd / (BrE)
something given or received in return for something else; especially a consideration in a transaction or exchange.
something given in exchange
archaic

Examples

Each party agreed to provide a quid for the other's services.
There must be some kind of quid for any business arrangement to be fair.
The court examined the quid offered in the proposed contract.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quid pro quo - something given or received for something else, especially as part of a deal
  • Synonym: consideration - something of value exchanged in a contract
  • Antonym: gift - something given without expecting anything in return

Etymology

From Latin 'quid' (what), commonly used in the phrase 'quid pro quo' (something for something).
