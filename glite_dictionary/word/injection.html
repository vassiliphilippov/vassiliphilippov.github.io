injection

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (BrE)
the act of forcefully inserting a substance into something, especially by using pressure
forceful insertion of a substance

Examples

The nurse prepared the injection and asked the patient to roll up his sleeve.
The injection of contrast dye made the scan results much clearer.
Fuel injection allows the engine to run more efficiently in modern cars.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: infusion - the process of delivering a liquid, usually medication, into the body, often slowly

Etymology

From Latin 'injectio', from 'injicere' (to throw in).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛk.ʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛk.ʃən / (BrE)
the act of putting a liquid, especially medicine, into a person's body using a syringe or similar device
putting liquid into the body by syringe
medical

Examples

The doctor administered the injection quickly and painlessly.
Many travelers need an injection before visiting certain countries to prevent disease.
The child was scared of getting an injection at the clinic.

Synset Examples

the nurse gave him a flu shot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shot - an injection of a drug or vaccine using a syringe
  • Synonym: vaccination - an injection given to protect against disease

Etymology

From Latin injectio, from injicere (to throw in).

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (BrE)
A liquid substance introduced into the body, especially under the skin, by means of a syringe.
a liquid introduced by syringe
medical

Examples

The doctor administered an injection to help relieve her pain.
Children often dread getting an injection at the clinic.
This medication is available as both tablets and an injection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shot - an injection of a drug or vaccine

Etymology

From Latin 'iniectio', from 'inicere' meaning to throw in or inject.
