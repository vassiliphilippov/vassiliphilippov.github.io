serenade

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (AmE), / ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (BrE)
a song or piece of music played or sung outdoors, especially at night, by a man for the woman he loves
a song performed for someone, often outdoors at night

Examples

He stood beneath her window and performed a serenade on his guitar.
The soft notes of the serenade floated up to her balcony.
At midnight, the band played a romantic serenade in the quiet street.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ballad - a narrative poem or song

Etymology

From Italian *serenata* (an evening song), from *sereno* (serene, evening).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (AmE), / ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (BrE)
a musical composition, typically in several movements, written for a small orchestra or ensemble, and often intended to be light and entertaining
musical composition in several movements

Examples

Mozart's serenade in G major remains a favorite among classical music enthusiasts.
The orchestra performed a delightful serenade during the summer concert.
He composed a serenade that featured string instruments and a gentle melody.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: divertimento - an instrumental composition in several movements, light and entertaining in character

Etymology

From Italian *serenata*, from *sereno* ‘calm, clear’; related to Latin *serenus* ‘clear, serene’.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07056332-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (AmE), / ˌsɛrəˈneɪd / (BrE)
to sing or perform music for someone, especially as a form of courtship or admiration, typically outdoors in the evening
sing or play music for someone

Examples

A group of students serenaded their teacher after graduation with her favorite song.
He serenaded her under her window with his guitar every evening.
The band serenaded the wedding guests as the couple shared their first dance.

Synset Examples

She was serenaded by her admirers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sing - to produce musical tones with the voice
  • Synonym: to perform - to present music, a play, etc. to an audience

Etymology

From Italian 'serenata', from 'sereno' (calm, clear) and ultimately from Latin 'serenus' (clear, fair).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
