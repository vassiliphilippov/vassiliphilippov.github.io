gilded

1. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪldɪd / (AmE), / ˈɡɪldɪd / (BrE)
having a rich and showy appearance; ostentatiously luxurious or superior in quality.
ostentatiously rich or luxurious

Examples

The guests dined in the gilded ballroom, surrounded by crystal chandeliers and gold-trimmed walls.
Many old theaters feature gilded balconies and ornate decorations from a more prosperous era.
The gilded lifestyle of the celebrities was on full display during the lavish gala.

Synset Examples

a princely sum
gilded dining rooms
these architecture magazines are full of the lush interiors of the rich and famous

Related Terms

  • Synonym: luxurious - showing luxury; very comfortable and expensive
  • Synonym: opulent - rich and luxurious
  • Antonym: austere - plain and without decoration or luxury

Etymology

From Middle English gilded, past participle of gild, meaning to cover with a thin layer of gold.

2. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪldɪd / (AmE), / ˈɡɪldɪd / (BrE)
covered with a thin layer of gold or having the appearance of gold
covered with gold

Examples

The palace was adorned with gilded mirrors and ornate chandeliers.
She admired the gilded edges of the ancient manuscript.
Tourists marveled at the gilded statues in the grand lobby.

Synset Examples

gold coins
the gold dome of the Capitol
the golden calf
gilded icons

Related Terms

  • Synonym: golden - having the color or shine of gold

Etymology

From Middle English gilded, past participle of 'gilden' (to gild), from Old English gyldan, related to 'gold'.

3. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪldɪd / (AmE), / ˈɡɪldɪd / (BrE)
Having a pleasing or showy appearance that is deceptive or superficial; appearing attractive on the surface but lacking real value or sincerity.
deceptively attractive or superficial

Examples

The politician gave a gilded speech full of empty promises and flashy rhetoric.
Behind the gilded facade, the company was struggling financially.
The gilded invitations looked impressive, but the event itself was disappointing.

Synset Examples

the gilded and perfumed but inwardly rotten nobility
meretricious praise
a meretricious argument

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meretricious - apparently attractive but with no real value

Etymology

From Middle English 'gilden', from Old English 'gyldan', meaning 'to overlay with gold'. The figurative use refers to covering something unattractive with superficial attractiveness.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s

4. ADJ

/ ˈɡɪldɪd / (AmE), / ˈɡɪldɪd / (BrE)
Having the deep, slightly brownish color of gold.
having the color of gold

Examples

The palace walls were adorned with gilded frames that glimmered in the candlelight.
He loved her gilded hair, which shone with a warm, golden hue in the sun.
A gilded glow bathed the valley at sunset, making everything look magical.

Synset Examples

long aureate (or golden) hair
a gold carpet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: golden - having the color of gold
  • Degree Lower: bronzed - having a color resembling bronze

Etymology

From Middle English gilded, from Old English gyldan (to gild) + -ed.
