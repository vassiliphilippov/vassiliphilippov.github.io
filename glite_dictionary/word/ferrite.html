ferrite

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɛraɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɛraɪt / (BrE)
a magnetic form of iron, stable below 906°C, in which iron atoms are arranged in a body-centered cubic structure; commonly found in steels and alloys
magnetic form of iron, stable below 906°C

Examples

The presence of ferrite in steel affects its magnetic properties.
At room temperature, pure iron is mostly found in the ferrite phase.
Metallurgists study how ferrite and other microstructures influence the strength of alloys.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: austenite - a non-magnetic allotrope of iron, stable above 906°C

Etymology

From Latin ferrum (iron) + -ite (mineral suffix).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfer.aɪt / (AmE), / ˈfɛr.aɪt / (BrE)
A solid solution of alpha iron with dissolved elements, forming a soft, magnetic phase present in steel and cast iron.
phase of iron in steel; magnetic iron alloy

Examples

The microstructure of this steel consists mainly of ferrite and a small amount of pearlite.
Magnetic properties are often achieved by using soft ferrite materials in electronic components.
Above 912°C, ferrite transforms into austenite as the crystal structure changes.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: austenite - a solid solution of gamma iron and carbon, a phase in steel
  • Antonym: cementite - an iron carbide present in steel and cast iron

Etymology

From Latin 'ferrum' (iron) + '-ite' (mineral suffix).
