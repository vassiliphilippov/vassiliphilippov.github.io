recapture

1. NOUN

/ riˈkæptʃər / (AmE), / riˈkæptʃə / (BrE)
the act of taking back something that was previously lost or taken
the act of taking something back

Examples

The army planned a swift recapture of the lost territory.
Her recapture of the championship title surprised everyone.
The authorities celebrated the recapture of the escaped prisoner.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to regain - to obtain possession of something again after losing it
  • Antonym: surrender - the act of giving up something to another

Etymology

From re- (again) + capture, ultimately from Latin capere (to seize).

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ riˈkæp.tʃɚ / (AmE), / riˈkæp.tʃə / (BrE)
the legal seizure by a government of profits exceeding a fixed limit, especially under financial or tax regulations
government seizure of excess profits
legal

Examples

The company faced a recapture of excess profits after an audit by the tax authorities.
Tax recapture provisions limit how much profit can be retained by private entities.
The new law broadened the scope of recapture to include various investment gains.

Etymology

From re- (again) + capture, originally meaning to take back possession; financial sense developed in 20th-century legal contexts.

/ riˈkæptʃər / (AmE), / riˈkæptʃə / (BrE)
to take back possession or control of something by force, especially after having lost it in a battle or conflict
take back by force

Examples

Authorities worked tirelessly to recapture the escaped prisoners before they could cross the border.
