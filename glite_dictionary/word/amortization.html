amortization

1. NOUN

/ əˌmɔrtəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌmɔːtəˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of gradually reducing the value of an asset or the amount of a debt by allocating its cost over a period of time
gradual reduction of asset or debt value

Examples

The company uses straight-line amortization to distribute the cost of equipment over its useful life.
Regular amortization ensures that assets are accounted for accurately in financial statements.
Interest and amortization payments on the loan are clearly outlined in the schedule.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: depreciation - the reduction in value of a tangible asset over time

Etymology

From Medieval Latin amortisatio, from Old French amortiser 'to kill or alienate property'.

2. NOUN

/ əˌmɔrtəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / əˌmɔːtəˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of paying off a debt or obligation over a period of time through a series of regular payments
gradual payment of a debt

Examples

The mortgage will be paid off over thirty years through amortization.
Amortization schedules help borrowers understand their monthly payment breakdown.
The company's financial report included details about the amortization of its loans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repayment - the action of paying back a loan or debt
  • Antonym: prepayment - the settlement of a debt or part of a debt before it is due

Etymology

From Late Latin 'amortizationem', from 'amortizare', meaning 'to kill' or 'to extinguish'.
