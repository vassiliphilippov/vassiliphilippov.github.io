invasion

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈveɪʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪʒən / (BrE)
(medicine) the spread of pathogenic microorganisms or malignant cells into new areas of the body.
spread of disease or cancer in the body
medical

Examples

The rapid invasion of healthy tissue by the bacteria made the infection difficult to treat.
Doctors were concerned about the tumor's invasion into nearby blood vessels.
Early detection reduces the risk of widespread invasion by cancer cells.

Synset Examples

the tumor's invasion of surrounding structures

Related Terms

  • Synonym: metastasis - the spread of cancer cells to new areas of the body

Etymology

From Latin invasio (an attack), from invadere (to go into, attack).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈveɪʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪʒən / (BrE)
an action in which people, animals, or other things enter an area in large numbers, especially when not previously present
large-scale entry into an area

Examples

Every spring, we experience an invasion of ants in the kitchen.
The sudden invasion of rodents caused concern among the residents.
There was an invasion of photographers outside the celebrity's hotel.

Synset Examples

an invasion of tourists
an invasion of locusts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incursion - a sudden or brief invasion or attack
  • Synonym: occupation - control of an area by entering and remaining

Etymology

From Latin invasio (an attack, assault), from invadere (to go into, attack).

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈveɪʒən / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪʒən / (BrE)
an instance of entering a country or region with an armed force in order to conquer or plunder
armed entry into a country or region

Examples

The ancient city fell after a massive invasion by enemy forces.
The country fortified its borders to prevent another invasion.
The sudden invasion came as a shock to the peaceful villagers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incursion - a sudden or brief invasion or attack
  • Synonym: occupation - the action, state, or period of occupying a place
  • Antonym: defense - the action of resisting attack

Etymology

From Latin invasio, from invadere (to go into, attack).
