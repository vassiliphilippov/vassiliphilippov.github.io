to mislead

1. VERB

/ mɪsˈliːd / (AmE), / mɪsˈliːd / (BrE)
to cause someone to believe something that is not true; to give someone false or misleading information
cause to believe something untrue

Examples

The manager was accused of trying to mislead shareholders about the company's financial health.
Her statement might mislead readers into thinking the problem is solved.
We must ensure the advertising does not mislead the public.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: to misinform - to give someone false or inaccurate information
  • Antonym: to inform - to provide someone with facts or information

Etymology

From Old English 'mis-' (wrongly) + 'lēdan' (to lead).

2. VERB

/ mɪsˈlid / (AmE), / mɪsˈliːd / (BrE)
to give someone false or incorrect information, causing them to go in the wrong direction or make a wrong decision
give false or wrong directions/information

Examples

He did not intend to mislead anyone, but his directions were confusing.
The outdated map misled travelers heading to the park.
Her explanation was so vague that it misled most of the new employees.

Synset Examples

The pedestrian misdirected the out-of-town driver

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Antonym: guide - to show someone the right way or give correct directions
  • Synonym: to misguide - to guide someone wrongly

Etymology

From Middle English 'misleden', from 'mis-' (wrongly) + 'lead'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02004320-v
Synset POS: v
