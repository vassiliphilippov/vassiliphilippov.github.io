fundamentalism

1. NOUN

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (BrE)
strict adherence to the basic principles or doctrines of a religion, ideology, or system, often characterized by a literal interpretation of foundational texts or beliefs.
strict adherence to basic principles, esp. in religion

Examples

The rise of fundamentalism has influenced politics in several countries.
Some people fear that fundamentalism leads to intolerance of other beliefs.
Not all religious groups agree with the principles of fundamentalism.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: orthodoxy - generally accepted or traditional beliefs, especially in religion
  • Degree Higher: extremism - the holding of extreme political or religious views

Etymology

From 'fundamental' + '-ism'; first used in the early 20th century in the context of Protestant Christianity.

2. NOUN

/ ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌfʌndəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (BrE)
A form of Protestant Christianity that insists on the literal interpretation of every word in the Bible or sacred texts.
strict literal interpretation in Protestant Christianity

Examples

Many churches in the area adhere to fundamentalism and reject modern scientific theories.
The rise of fundamentalism in the early twentieth century changed the landscape of American Protestantism.
Debates over evolution versus fundamentalism continue in some school districts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: literalism - interpretation of words in their most basic sense

Etymology

From fundamental +‎ -ism, referring to adherence to the 'fundamentals' of faith; originated in early 20th-century American Protestantism.
