nark

1. NOUN

/ nɑrk / (AmE), / nɑːk / (BrE)
a police officer or detective who deals with narcotics violations
narcotics officer or detective
informal british

Examples

The undercover nark infiltrated the drug ring to gather evidence.
A seasoned nark can often spot suspicious behavior in a crowd.
Local dealers became wary once they suspected a nark was among them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: narc - A police officer investigating illegal drugs (especially in US English)

Etymology

Possibly shortened from 'narcotics officer' or influenced by the earlier slang 'nark' (informer), origin uncertain.

2. NOUN

/ nɑrk / (AmE), / nɑːk / (BrE)
a person who informs the police about criminal activities; an informer or spy for the police
police informer or spy
british informal

Examples

Sam turned out to be a nark who told the police about the gang's plans.
They suspected there was a nark in the group leaking information to the authorities.
The criminals were caught after a nark provided a tip-off to the police.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snitch - a person who informs authorities, especially police, about others
  • Synonym: grass - a British informal term for a police informer
  • Synonym: mole - a spy who infiltrates an organization, often passing information to the police

Etymology

Possibly from British slang, related to the Romani word 'nāk' (nose, to sniff out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10365390-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ nɑrk / (AmE), / nɑːk / (BrE)
to inform or act as a spy, especially for the police; to betray someone's secrets to the authorities
to inform or spy for the police
slang british

Examples

He threatened to hurt anyone who tried to nark on him to the police.
The gang suspected that someone had narked about their plans.
If you nark, you'll lose everyone's trust.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snitch - to secretly inform on someone, especially to the police
  • Synonym: grass - to inform or act as a police informer, especially in British slang
  • Synonym: to tip off - to give secret information to the authorities

Etymology

Probably from Romani *nāk* (nose), possibly via Polari slang.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
