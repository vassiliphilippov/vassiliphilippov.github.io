competent

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːmpətənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmpɪtənt / (BrE)
having the necessary ability, knowledge, or skill to do something successfully
having the required skill or ability

Examples

She is a competent engineer, capable of solving complex problems quickly.
Although he was new to the role, Jack proved himself to be competent.
Only competent drivers are allowed to operate the vehicle on this site.

Synset Examples

a competent typist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: capable - having the ability or qualities necessary for something
  • Antonym: incompetent - not having the necessary abilities or skills

Etymology

From Latin 'competens', present participle of 'competere' (to be suitable, to come together).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00511975-a
Synset POS: a

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːmpətənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmpɪtənt / (BrE)
adequate for the intended purpose but not outstanding or exceptional
adequate but not exceptional

Examples

The actor gave a competent performance that met expectations but did not impress the critics.
She is a competent pianist, though not considered particularly gifted.
His work is competent, but lacks the creativity needed to stand out.

Synset Examples

a competent performance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adequate - satisfactory or acceptable in quality or quantity
  • Antonym: exceptional - unusually good; outstanding

Etymology

From Latin competens, competentis (suitable, sufficient, capable).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00051791-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːm.pə.tənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.pɪ.tənt / (BrE)
(of a person, court, authority, etc.) having the legal authority or qualification to do something.
legally qualified or authorized
legal

Examples

The judge determined that the case should be heard by a competent court.
Only a competent witness is permitted to give testimony in a legal proceeding.
The contract can only be signed by a competent authority under the law.

Synset Examples

a competent court
competent testimony

Related Terms

  • Antonym: incompetent - not legally qualified or lacking authority

Etymology

From Latin 'competens', present participle of 'competere' (to be suitable, to strive for).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00514055-a
Synset POS: a
