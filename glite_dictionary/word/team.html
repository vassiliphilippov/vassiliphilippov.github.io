team

1. NOUN

/ tim / (AmE), / tiːm / (BrE)
a group of people who work together to achieve a common goal, especially in sports or at work
group working together (often in sports)

Examples

The basketball team practiced every evening to prepare for the championship.
She joined a project team at her new job to help develop a software update.
A strong team spirit can make a big difference in achieving success.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: group - a number of people or things that are together or in the same place
  • Antonym: individual - a single person, considered separately from a group

Etymology

From Old English 'tēam' (family, offspring, lineage).

2. VERB

/ tim / (AmE), / tiːm / (BrE)
to come together or join with others to form a team, especially for a particular purpose or activity
join others to form a team

Examples

Many companies team with local artists to promote cultural events.
Researchers often team up to tackle complex scientific problems.
We decided to team together for the charity run.

Synset Examples

We teamed up for this new project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to collaborate - to work jointly with others especially in an intellectual endeavor
  • Antonym: to compete - to strive to gain or win something by defeating others

Etymology

Old English 'team' (family, offspring), later meaning a group that works together.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01091696-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ tim / (AmE), / tiːm / (BrE)
A group of two or more draft animals harnessed and working together to pull a vehicle or implement.
group of draft animals working together

Examples

The farmer hitched the team of oxen to the plow at sunrise.
A team of horses pulled the wagon through the muddy field.
In the old days, travel often depended on a strong team of mules.

Etymology

Old English *team* (family, offspring, line of descent); later also 'line of draft animals'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08001826-n
Synset POS: n
