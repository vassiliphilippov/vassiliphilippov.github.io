Secotiaceae

1. NOUN

/ sɛˌkoʊʃiˈeɪsiː / (AmE), / sɛˌkəʊtɪˈeɪsiː / (BrE)
a family of fungi characterized by having a stalk and cap, with a wrinkled mass of tissue (gleba) where spores are produced; often considered deformed forms of other fungi
family of fungi with stalk, cap, and wrinkled gleba

Examples

The Secotiaceae are often mistaken for distorted forms of more familiar mushrooms.
Mycologists recently discovered a new species within the Secotiaceae in Australia.
The unique gleba structure of Secotiaceae distinguishes them from other fungal families.

Etymology

From the genus name Secotium, based on Latin roots.

2. NOUN

/ sɛˌkoʊtiˈeɪsiː / (AmE), / sɛˌkəʊtɪˈeɪsɪiː / (BrE)
A family of fungi characterized by having a stalk and cap with a wrinkled mass of tissue (gleba) where spores are produced; often considered to be misshapen forms of other fungi.
family of fungi with stalk, cap, wrinkled gleba

Examples

The Secotiaceae exhibit unique adaptations in their spore-producing structures compared to other agarics.
Mycologists often debate whether members of the Secotiaceae are true species or deformed relatives of other fungi.
Several species within the Secotiaceae are found in arid regions, where their distinctive gleba helps protect spores.

Etymology

From the genus name Secotium, combined with the standard botanical family suffix -aceae.
