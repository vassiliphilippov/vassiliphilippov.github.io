Hebrews

1. NOUN

/ ˈhiˌbroʊz / (AmE), / ˈhiːbruːz / (BrE)
An ancient people traditionally descended from Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, regarded as the ancestors of the Jewish people and recipients of God's covenant according to the Hebrew Bible.
ancient people descended from Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob

Examples

The Hebrews are often described as the ancestors of the Jewish people in religious texts.
According to tradition, Moses led the Hebrews out of slavery in Egypt.
The language spoken by the ancient Hebrews developed into what is now known as Hebrew.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Israelites - members of the ancient Hebrew nation, especially in the context of the Exodus
  • Synonym: Jews - members of the Jewish people, descendants of the Hebrews

Etymology

From Latin 'Hebraeus', from Greek 'Hebraios', from Hebrew 'ʿIvri' (Hebrew).

2. PROPN

/ ˈhiː.bruːz / (AmE), / ˈhiː.bruːz / (BrE)
a book of the New Testament, traditionally attributed to Saint Paul, but now generally regarded as of unknown authorship, addressed to Jewish Christians
book of the New Testament, addressed to Jewish Christians

Examples

Many scholars debate who actually wrote Hebrews, as its style differs from Paul's other letters.
The theme of Hebrews emphasizes the superiority of Christ over previous religious traditions.
During our Bible study, we focused on the opening verses of Hebrews.

Etymology

From Latin 'Hebraei', from Greek 'Hebraioi', meaning 'Hebrews'; the title refers to the people of Hebrew descent, addressed in the epistle.
