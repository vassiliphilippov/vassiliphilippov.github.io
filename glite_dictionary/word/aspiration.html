aspiration

1. NOUN

/ ˌæspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌaspɪˈreɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
a strong desire, longing, or ambition to achieve something or succeed
desire or ambition to succeed

Examples

Her aspiration to become a doctor kept her motivated throughout medical school.
Many young people have the aspiration to travel the world and experience different cultures.
Despite facing many challenges, his aspiration for success never faded.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambition - strong desire to achieve success or distinction
  • Antonym: apathy - lack of interest, enthusiasm, or concern

Etymology

From Latin aspiratio (a breathing toward, longing) from aspirare (to breathe upon, to desire).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæspɪˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of drawing breath or other gases into the lungs
the act of inhaling (breathing in)
medical

Examples

The doctor monitored the patient's aspiration to ensure proper lung function.
During meditation, slow and deep aspiration can help calm the mind and body.
Mechanical failure of the respirator interrupted the flow of aspiration in the intensive care unit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inhalation - the act of breathing in; inhaling
  • Antonym: exhalation - the act of breathing out; exhaling

Etymology

From Latin aspiratio (a breathing, blowing, inspiration), from aspirare (to breathe upon, desire).

4. NOUN

/ ˌæspəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌaspɪˈreɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(Phonetics) The audible release of breath that accompanies or follows some spoken sounds, especially certain consonants.
release of breath in speech (phonetics)

Examples

In English, the aspiration of the "p" sound distinguishes "pat" from "bat".
Linguists often study the effects of aspiration on accent and pronunciation.
Korean consonants typically show a clear difference between aspiration and unaspirated forms.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plosive - a consonant sound characterized by complete closure in the vocal tract followed by a release; often aspirated
  • Antonym: unaspirated - pronounced without an audible release of breath

Etymology

From Latin aspiratio (a blowing, breathing upon), from aspirare (to breathe upon).
