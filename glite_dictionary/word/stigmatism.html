stigmatism

1. NOUN

/ ˈstɪɡ.məˌtɪz.əm / (AmE), / ˈstɪɡ.mə.tɪz.əm / (BrE)
(Optics) A condition of an optical system, such as a lens, in which light rays from a single point converge at a single focal point, resulting in a clear image.
optical condition where rays converge at one point

Examples

The new microscope design achieved perfect stigmatism for improved image clarity.
A high-quality camera lens is engineered to minimize distortion and maintain stigmatism.
Optical engineers work to ensure stigmatism so that all rays focus precisely on the sensor.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: astigmatism - defect in optical systems where rays do not converge to a single focal point

Etymology

From Greek 'stigma' (mark, point) + '-ism'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈstɪɡməˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈstɪɡmətɪzəm / (BrE)
Normal condition of the eye in which light rays are accurately focused on the retina, resulting in clear vision; the absence of astigmatism.
normal eyesight (opposed to astigmatism)
medical

Examples

During the eye exam, the doctor confirmed that he had stigmatism and did not need corrective lenses.
Stigmatism allows the eyes to focus light correctly, ensuring sharp and clear vision.
Most people hope to maintain stigmatism as they age, avoiding common vision problems.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: astigmatism - visual defect in which vision is blurred due to irregular curvature of the cornea

Etymology

From Greek 'stigma' (mark, point) + '-ism'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈstɪɡməˌtɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈstɪɡmətɪzəm / (BrE)
the condition of having or being marked by stigmata, often referring to apparent wounds resembling those of Christ’s crucifixion
condition of having stigmata

Examples

Some devotees were said to exhibit stigmatism after intense periods of prayer.
The phenomenon of stigmatism is often reported in the accounts of certain saints.
Skeptics questioned whether her stigmatism could be explained by natural causes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stigmata - bodily marks, sores, or sensations of pain in locations corresponding to the crucifixion wounds of Jesus

Etymology

From Greek 'stigma' (mark, puncture), via Late Latin 'stigmat-', stem of 'stigma'.
