specious

1. ADJ

/ ˈspiːʃəs / (AmE), / ˈspiːʃəs / (BrE)
seeming to be true, correct, or plausible, but actually false or misleading.
seemingly true but actually false

Examples

The politician presented a specious argument that persuaded many people at first.
Her specious reasoning hid the real flaws in her plan.
The advertisement made specious claims about the product's effectiveness.

Synset Examples

a specious claim
spurious inferences

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spurious - not genuine, false or fake
  • Antonym: genuine - truly what something is said to be, authentic

Etymology

From Latin speciosus (beautiful, plausible), from species (appearance).

2. ADJ

/ ˈspiʃəs / (AmE), / ˈspiːʃəs / (BrE)
seeming to be true, correct, or attractive but actually false or deceptive.
deceptively attractive or plausible

Examples

The lawyer's specious arguments briefly convinced the jury, but the truth soon emerged.
His proposal sounded convincing, but it was based on specious assumptions.
She saw through the specious charm and refused to trust his promises.

Synset Examples

the gilded and perfumed but inwardly rotten nobility
meretricious praise
a meretricious argument

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meretricious - apparently attractive but having no real value
  • Antonym: genuine - truly what something is said to be; authentic

Etymology

From Latin 'speciosus' (beautiful, plausible), from 'species' (appearance, form).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02189932-s
Synset POS: s
