dory

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɔri / (AmE), / ˈdɔːri / (BrE)
a pike-like freshwater fish of the perch family, typically with an elongated body and sharp spines
a pike-like freshwater perch

Examples

The fisherman proudly showed off the large dory he caught at the lake.
Many anglers prefer to fish for dories in quiet, inland rivers.
That species of dory is known for its elongated shape and sharp dorsal fin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: perch - a freshwater fish of the family Percidae

Etymology

From Middle French *dorée*, from *dorer* (to gild), referring to its golden color.

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɔri / (AmE), / ˈdɔːri / (BrE)
a marine fish of several species with deep, laterally compressed bodies, found in mid-waters and deep slope waters; especially known for the John Dory.
marine fish with a deep, flat body

Examples

The dory is prized by chefs for its delicious white flesh.
Fishermen caught several dory in the deep waters off the coast.
A dory can often be identified by its large, round body and distinctive markings.

Etymology

Of uncertain origin, possibly from Dutch 'dorie' or French 'dorée' (golden).

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɔːri / (AmE), / ˈdɔːri / (BrE)
a small, shallow-draft boat with flat bottom, high sides, and cross seats, designed to be rowed
small, shallow-draft rowing boat

Examples

The fisherman rowed his dory out to sea before sunrise.
He repaired the old dory in his backyard each spring.
Several dories were tied to the dock, bobbing gently in the tide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skiff - a small, light, shallow boat for rowing or sailing
  • Synonym: canoe - a light, narrow boat pointed at both ends, paddled rather than rowed

Etymology

Perhaps from Portuguese 'dóri', of uncertain origin; first used in American English in the 18th century.
