Gnathostomata

1. NOUN

/ næθəˈstɑːmətə / (AmE), / ˌnæθəʊˈstɒmətə / (BrE)
a major group of vertebrates that have both upper and lower jaws, including fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals
jawed vertebrates group

Examples

The Gnathostomata include most vertebrates, with exceptions such as lampreys and hagfish.
In biology class, we learned that humans are members of the Gnathostomata.
Fossil records show that Gnathostomata first appeared during the Silurian period.

Etymology

From Greek gnathos (jaw) + stoma (mouth), referring to jawed animals.

2. NOUN

/ næθ.əˈstɑː.mə.tə / (AmE), / nath.əˈstɒ.mə.tə / (BrE)
A superclass of vertebrates that possess both upper and lower jaws, including fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.
jawed vertebrate superclass

Examples

Most modern vertebrates, including humans, belong to the Gnathostomata.
The evolution of jaws marked the emergence of the Gnathostomata in the fossil record.
Sharks, as members of the Gnathostomata, are distinguished from jawless fishes.

Etymology

From Greek gnathos (jaw) + stoma (mouth), referring to jawed vertebrates.
