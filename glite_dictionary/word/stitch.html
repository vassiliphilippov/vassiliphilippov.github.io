stitch

1. NOUN

/ stɪtʃ / (AmE), / stɪtʃ / (BrE)
a sudden, sharp pain in the side, often caused by exercise such as running
sharp pain in the side from exercise

Examples

Halfway through the race, David got a stitch in his side and had to slow down.
It's common for beginners to experience a stitch when they start jogging.
She pressed her hand to her ribs as the stitch made it hard to breathe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cramp - a sudden, involuntary muscle contraction causing pain
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-14355744-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ stɪtʃ / (AmE), / stɪtʃ / (BrE)
a single loop, knot, or link of thread or yarn created by a needle or similar tool in sewing, knitting, embroidery, or crocheting
single loop or knot in sewing or knitting

Examples

She dropped a stitch while knitting the scarf and had to fix it.
Each stitch in the embroidery was carefully placed to form the delicate pattern.
If you lose your place, count the stitches on the needle to make sure you're on track.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seam - a line where two pieces of fabric are joined by stitching

Etymology

From Old English *stycce* (piece, bit) and *stician* (to pierce, stab).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04328376-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ stɪtʃ / (AmE), / stɪtʃ / (BrE)
to fasten, join, or repair using a needle and thread; to sew sections of cloth or material together
fasten or sew with needle and thread

Examples

She carefully stitched the torn seam on her dress before the party.
The doctor stitched the wound after the surgery.
He stitch patches onto his old jeans to give them new life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to sew - to join with needle and thread
  • Antonym: unstitch - to undo stitches

Etymology

From Old English 'stycce' (a piece, stitch), related to Old High German 'stëcchi' (stitch).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01331921-v
Synset POS: v
