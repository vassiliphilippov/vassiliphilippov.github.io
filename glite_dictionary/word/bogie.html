bogie

1. NOUN

/ ˈboʊɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊɡi / (BrE)
an aircraft of unknown identity, often considered possibly hostile in military or aviation contexts
unidentified or possibly enemy aircraft

Examples

Radar has picked up a bogie approaching from the north.
The fighter pilots were ordered to intercept the incoming bogie.
A bogie was detected at high altitude near the airbase.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: bandit - identified enemy aircraft (military slang)

2. NOUN

/ ˈboʊ.ɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊ.ɡi / (BrE)
(archaic) A bogle, goblin, or other mythical creature; sometimes used as a name for the Devil.
mythical goblin or devil (archaic)
archaic

Examples

The children were scared the bogie would come for them in the night.
In old legends, the bogie lurked in the misty moors, waiting for travelers.
Some villagers believed misfortune meant the bogie was angered.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bogle - a ghost or goblin in northern English and Scottish folklore
  • Synonym: goblin - a mischievous, ugly creature of folklore
  • Synonym: devil - the chief evil spirit in Christian and other religions

Etymology

Variant of 'bogle', likely of northern English or Scottish origin, meaning a goblin or specter.

3. NOUN

/ ˈboʊɡi / (AmE), / ˈbəʊɡi / (BrE)
A chassis or framework carrying wheels, attached to a vehicle such as a train or trolley, to provide stability and enable it to turn corners smoothly.
wheel assembly for vehicles (especially trains)

Examples

The engineer replaced the old bogie to improve the train's ride quality.
Each carriage is supported by a bogie at either end.
After the accident, inspectors found a damaged bogie beneath the wagon.

Synset Examples

They installed a new bogie to improve stability.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: truck (railroad) - North American term for a bogie, a wheel assembly under rail vehicles

Etymology

Probably from Scottish Gaelic 'bogaidh' (a truck or low cart).
