bunch

1. NOUN

/ bʌntʃ / (AmE), / bʌntʃ / (BrE)
A number of things of the same type that are held or grouped together.
group of similar things

Examples

She handed me a bunch of fresh flowers from her garden.
A bunch of keys dangled from his belt as he walked.
We saw a bunch of kids playing soccer in the park.

Synset Examples

a bunch of trees
a cluster of admirers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - a group of similar things or people positioned or occurring closely together

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle French 'bounche' (a swelling, bump).

2. NOUN

/ bʌntʃ / (AmE), / bʌntʃ / (BrE)
(informal) a group of people, especially friends, considered together.
group of friends (informal)
informal

Examples

I went to the movies last night with a bunch of friends from work.
She and her bunch have been inseparable since college.
After the meeting, the whole bunch decided to grab dinner together.

Synset Examples

he still hangs out with the same crowd

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - a group of people (often informal friendship group)
  • Synonym: gang - an informal group of people, often friends

3. VERB

/ bʌntʃ / (AmE), / bʌntʃ / (BrE)
to gather or cause to gather closely together in a cluster or group
gather into a cluster or group

Examples

She bunched her scarf around her neck to keep warm.
The children bunched together in the doorway to avoid the rain.
He bunches the papers neatly before handing them out.

Synset Examples

She bunched her fingers into a fist

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - to come together in a group; to form a cluster
Source: WordNet

4. VERB

/ bʌntʃ / (AmE), / bʌntʃ / (BrE)
to form or gather into a tight group or cluster
form into a cluster or group

Examples

The hikers bunched together as the fog grew thicker.
His shirt bunches up around his waist when he sits down.
The sheep bunched in the corner of the field when the dog approached.

Synset Examples

The frightened children bunched together in the corner of the classroom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - to come together in a small group or mass
  • Antonym: scatter - to separate and go in different directions

Etymology

From Middle English 'bunche' (protuberance, swelling); origin uncertain.
