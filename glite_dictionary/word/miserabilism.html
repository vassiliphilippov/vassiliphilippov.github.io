miserabilism

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɪzərəˈbɪlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌmɪzərəˈbɪlɪzəm / (BrE)
A style or movement in French art and literature, particularly in the mid-20th century, characterized by a focus on themes of misery, gloom, and existential despair.
French art/literature movement focusing on misery and gloom

Examples

The painter's works were seen as classic examples of miserabilism, reflecting the despair of postwar France.
Some critics argue that miserabilism provided an artistic voice for the existential anxieties of the 1950s.
The novel's tone of unrelenting bleakness aligns it with the principles of miserabilism.

Synset Examples

Along with the briefly popular painters of a style known as Miserabilism in the early 1950s, Buffet's pictures were easily digestible accompaniments to the existential revulsion described in Jean-Paul Sartre's novel Nausea (La Nausée), 1938, and the fiction of Albert Camus, melded with the sordid underbelly of Georges Simenon's detective stories.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: existentialism - A philosophy emphasizing individual existence, freedom, and choice, influential to miserabilism

Etymology

From French 'misérabilisme', based on 'misérable' (miserable).
