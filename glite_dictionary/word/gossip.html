gossip

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑːsɪp / (AmE), / ˈɡɒsɪp / (BrE)
Light, informal conversation, especially about social matters or events.
light informal conversation

Examples

They enjoyed some gossip over coffee after the meeting.
The afternoon was filled with light gossip and laughter.
At parties, he prefers engaging in harmless gossip to serious discussions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chitchat - casual or trivial conversation

Etymology

From Old English 'godsibb' (a godparent, later 'close friend'), originally referring to a sponsor at baptism, later extended to a person who chats, then to the act itself.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07149788-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ ˈɡɑsɪp / (AmE), / ˈɡɒsɪp / (BrE)
to talk about other people's private lives, often involving secrets or personal details, typically in a disapproving way
talk about others' private matters

Examples

People in the office love to gossip about their coworkers during lunch breaks.
She refused to gossip about her friend's personal problems.
It's easy to gossip without realizing the damage it can do to someone's reputation.

Synset Examples

She won't dish the dirt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rumor - to spread unverified information about others
  • Antonym: to keep quiet - to refrain from talking about something, especially secrets

Etymology

From Old English 'godsibb' (godparent, familiar acquaintance).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑːsɪp / (AmE), / ˈɡɒsɪp / (BrE)
information about the private affairs of others, often of a sensational or malicious nature, shared for entertainment or intrigue.
rumor or talk about others' private affairs

Examples

The latest gossip at the office spread quickly among the employees.
She was often the subject of village gossip after moving in with her boyfriend.
Their conversation turned into an hour of sharing juicy gossip about celebrities.

Synset Examples

the divorce caused much gossip

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rumor - an unverified piece of information spread among people
  • Antonym: fact - a thing known or proved to be true, not mere report or speculation

Etymology

From Old English 'godsibb' (godparent, a person related in God), later used for a person who chats; sense shifted to 'idle talk' by the 16th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07237830-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈɡɑs.ɪp / (AmE), / ˈɡɒs.ɪp / (BrE)
a person who enjoys talking about the private details of other people's lives, often by spreading rumors or personal information
a person who spreads rumors or private details

Examples

Everyone knows Lily is the gossip of the office, always eager to share the latest news.
He quickly earned a reputation as a gossip among his classmates for revealing their secrets.
At every party, there's at least one gossip who can't resist stirring up drama.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rumormonger - someone who spreads rumors or unverified information
  • Synonym: blabbermouth - someone who cannot keep secrets
  • Antonym: confidant - a person entrusted with private matters (contrasting sense)

Etymology

From Old English *godsibb* (a sponsor at baptism, later 'one who talks familiarly'), from 'God' + 'sibb' (relative).

5. VERB

/ ˈɡɑsɪp / (AmE), / ˈɡɒsɪp / (BrE)
to talk casually, often about the personal affairs of others, sometimes spreading rumors or unverified information
talk casually, often about others

Examples

The neighbors love to gossip about each other's families.
They were gossiping in the kitchen when I arrived.
Even though she promised not to gossip, she couldn't help telling him the latest news.

Synset Examples

the men were sitting in the cafe and shooting the breeze

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chatter - to talk quickly and continuously about unimportant things
  • Synonym: rumor - information that is not confirmed and is spread about people or events
  • Antonym: to keep quiet - to refrain from talking or revealing information

Etymology

From Old English 'godsibb' (godparent), later evolving to mean a person who talks idly and, by the 17th century, the act of talking idly itself.
