to maturate

1. VERB

/ ˈmætʃəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmatjʊreɪt / (BrE)
to develop and reach a stage of full maturity; especially to undergo the process of maturation in a biological or medical context
reach maturity; undergo maturation

Examples

The stem cells maturate over time into specialized tissues.
Certain bacterial cultures maturate rapidly under optimal conditions.
As the abscess maturates, it eventually forms pus and may rupture.

Synset Examples

He matured fast
The child grew fast

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mature - to become fully developed or grown

Etymology

From Latin 'maturatus', past participle of 'maturare' (to ripen, bring to maturity).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00251067-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈmætʃəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmatjʊreɪt / (BrE)
(of a wound or tissue) to ripen and form or discharge pus as a result of infection.
form or discharge pus (medical)
medical

Examples

The doctor observed that the abscess had begun to maturate over the weekend.
If an infection sets in, the wound may maturate and require drainage.
It is essential to treat injuries promptly so they do not maturate and complicate healing.

Synset Examples

her wounds are festering

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suppurate - to form or discharge pus
  • Synonym: fester - (of a wound) to become infected and generate pus

Etymology

From Latin 'maturatus', past participle of 'maturare' (to make ripe).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00096520-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈmætʃəˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmatjʊreɪt / (BrE)
To grow old or older; to undergo the process of aging.
grow old or older

Examples

Plants maturate at different rates depending on species and environmental factors.
As humans maturate, their perspectives on life often change.
Some scientists study how brain cells maturate and eventually deteriorate with age.

Synset Examples

She aged gracefully
we age every day — what a depressing thought!
Young men senesce

Related Terms

  • Synonym: age - become or cause to become old
  • Synonym: to senesce - begin to show signs of old age

Etymology

From Latin 'maturatus', past participle of 'maturare' (to mature, to ripen).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
