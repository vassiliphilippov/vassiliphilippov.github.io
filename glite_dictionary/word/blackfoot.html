Blackfoot

1. NOUN

/ ˈblækˌfʊt / (AmE), / ˈblækˌfʊt / (BrE)
the Algonquian language or languages spoken by the Blackfoot people of North America
Algonquian language of the Blackfoot people

Examples

She is studying Blackfoot as part of her research on indigenous languages.
The Blackfoot language is taught in certain schools to help preserve cultural heritage.
He can speak Blackfoot fluently thanks to his grandparents' teachings.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: Algonquian - a family of indigenous languages of North America

Etymology

Named after the Blackfoot people, whose name derives from their tradition of wearing black-dyed moccasins.

2. NOUN

/ ˈblækˌfut / (AmE), / ˈblækˌfuːt / (BrE)
a member of an Indigenous group of Algonquian peoples living in the northwestern plains of North America, especially in Montana (USA) and Alberta (Canada)
member of an Indigenous group in North America

Examples

The Blackfoot were known for their skill as horsemen and warriors.
She is proud of her Blackfoot heritage and traditions.
Many Blackfoot still live in communities in Montana and Alberta today.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Blackfeet - alternative plural form of Blackfoot people

Etymology

From English 'black' + 'foot', possibly referring to a traditional practice of blackening moccasins or to burned prairie areas.
