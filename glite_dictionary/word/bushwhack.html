to bushwhack

1. VERB

/ ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (AmE), / ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (BrE)
to live in the bush as a fugitive or as a guerrilla, often to avoid authorities or conduct surprise attacks
live in the bush as a fugitive or guerrilla

Examples

After escaping the prison, the outlaws had to bushwhack for weeks to avoid capture.
To survive, the fugitives bushwhacked in the dense forests along the border.
The rebels bushwhacked in remote mountain regions until the conflict ended.

Etymology

Originally American English; from 'bush' + 'whack', referring to traveling or hiding in wild or untamed areas.

2. VERB

/ ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (AmE), / ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (BrE)
to attack someone suddenly from a concealed position; to ambush
attack from hiding; ambush

Examples

The rebels planned to bushwhack the convoy as it passed through the mountains.
He was bushwhacked by bandits while traveling along the old trail.
Armed with nothing but an old rifle, he waited in the dark to bushwhack any trespassers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ambush - to make a surprise attack from a concealed position

Etymology

Originally American English, probably from 'bushwhacker', an early 19th-century term for one who ambushes from the brush or woods.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01140630-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (AmE), / ˈbʊʃˌwæk / (BrE)
to make one's way through dense woods or undergrowth by cutting or pushing through it
force a path through bush or woods

Examples

We had to bushwhack our way through the overgrown forest to reach the lake.
The explorers spent hours bushwhacking along the riverbank, creating a new path.
After bushwhacking through thick vines and brambles, they finally found an open clearing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hack - to cut with heavy blows in a rough way

Etymology

American English, from 'bush' (wooded area) + 'whack' (to strike), mid-19th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02057647-v
Synset POS: v
