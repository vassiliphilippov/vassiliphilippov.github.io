Hamamelidae

1. NOUN

/ ˌhæməˌmiːlɪˈdiː / (AmE), / ˌhæməˌmiːlɪˈdiː / (BrE)
a former subclass or superorder of chiefly woody, primitive angiosperms with poorly developed or absent perianth, often unisexual and wind-pollinated flowers, including families such as Betulaceae and Fagaceae
botanical group of primitive woody angiosperms

Examples

Many trees within the Hamamelidae produce their flowers in catkins, which are adapted for wind pollination.
Some botanists now divide the Hamamelidae among several other modern plant groups.
In older classifications, oaks and birches were united under Hamamelidae due to similarities in floral structure.

Etymology

From the genus name Hamamelis (witch hazel) + -idae, a taxonomic suffix.

2. NOUN

/ hæməˌmiːlɪˌdiː / (AmE), / hæməˌmiːlɪˌdiː / (BrE)
a group of mostly woody flowering plants considered among the most primitive angiosperms, often with poorly developed perianths and unisexual, wind-pollinated flowers; includes families such as Betulaceae and Fagaceae
group of primitive woody flowering plants

Examples

Many trees in the Hamamelidae produce their flowers in catkins, which are adapted for wind pollination.
The family Fagaceae, which includes oaks and beeches, belongs to the Hamamelidae.
Botanists debate whether to classify the Hamamelidae as a subclass or a superorder.

Etymology

From genus name Hamamelis (witch hazel) + taxonomic suffix -idae.
