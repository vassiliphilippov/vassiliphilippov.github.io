to redden

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛdən / (AmE), / ˈrɛdən / (BrE)
to make or become red, or more red in color
make or become red

Examples

The rising sun began to redden the eastern horizon.
When she was praised in front of the class, her cheeks reddened.
Exposure to wind can redden dry skin.

Synset Examples

The setting sun reddened the sky

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blush - to become red in the face, often from embarrassment
  • Antonym: pale - to become lighter or less red in color

Etymology

From 'red' + '-en', Old English '-ian' (verb-forming suffix).

2. VERB

/ ˈrɛdən / (AmE), / ˈrɛd(ə)n / (BrE)
to become or make red, especially as a result of emotion such as embarrassment or shame
to become or make red

Examples

Her cheeks reddened when the compliment caught her by surprise.
The leaves redden each autumn as the temperature drops.
He tried to hide his embarrassment, but his face quickly reddened.

Synset Examples

The girl blushed when a young man whistled as she walked by

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blush - to become red in the face from embarrassment or shame
  • Antonym: pale - to become pale or less red in the face

Etymology

From Middle English 'redden', from Old English 'rēad' (red) + '-en' (verb suffix).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛd.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛd.ən / (BrE)
to become or make something red or redder in color
become or make red

Examples

The leaves begin to redden in autumn as the weather cools.
He felt his cheeks redden with embarrassment during the speech.
The setting sun caused the clouds to redden dramatically over the mountains.

Synset Examples

The sky reddened

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blush - to become red in the face from embarrassment or shame
  • Antonym: pale - to become or make pale; to lose color

Etymology

From Middle English 'redden', from 'red' + '-en' (verb forming suffix).
