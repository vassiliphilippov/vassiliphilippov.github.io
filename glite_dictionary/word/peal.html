peal

1. VERB

/ pil / (AmE), / piːl / (BrE)
(of bells) to ring loudly and repeatedly, especially in a series of sounds.
ring repeatedly (of bells)

Examples

Church bells pealed joyfully to announce the wedding.
Thunder pealed across the mountains, echoing among the valleys.
Every Christmas Eve, the town’s carillon peals through the snowy streets.

Synset Examples

bells were pealing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chime - to make a melodious ringing sound, often at set times (of a bell or clock)

Etymology

From Old French 'pel', from Medieval Latin 'pella' (a bell), influenced by Old English 'pæl' (bell).

2. VERB

/ pil / (AmE), / piːl / (BrE)
(of a bell or bells) to ring loudly and clearly; to make a loud, sonorous sound.
to ring loudly (esp. bells)

Examples

Church bells pealed across the town on Christmas morning.
Laughter pealed through the quiet corridor, startling everyone nearby.
Thunder pealed overhead as the storm intensified.

Synset Examples

the bells rang

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ring - to produce a clear, resonant sound, especially of a bell
  • Synonym: chime - to make a musical ringing sound, especially of bells

Etymology

From Middle English 'pel', from Old French 'apel' (call) and related to the noun 'peal'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02185344-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ pil / (AmE), / piːl / (BrE)
a deep, long, and loud sound, especially made by bells or thunder
deep, prolonged sound of bells or thunder

Examples

A loud peal of thunder shook the windows during the storm.
The church bells rang out in a joyful peal on Christmas morning.
Suddenly, a peal of laughter erupted from the children in the playground.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clang - a loud, resonant metallic sound
  • Synonym: chime - a series of musical sounds made by bells
  • Antonym: silence - absence of sound

Etymology

From Old French *pel*, from Latin *pella* (a stroke or blow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07403930-n
Synset POS: n
