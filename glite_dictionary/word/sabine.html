Sabine

1. ADJ

/ səˈbiːn / (AmE), / səˈbiːn / (BrE)
of or relating to the Sabines, an ancient people of central Italy; characteristic of the Sabines.
relating to the ancient Sabines

Examples

The museum exhibits several Sabine artifacts dating back to ancient Italy.
Scholars are fascinated by the Sabine influence on early Roman culture.
The legend of the Sabine women has been depicted in many works of art.

Etymology

From Latin Sabinus, referring to the people of central Italy.

2. PROPN

/ səˈbin / (AmE), / sæˈbiːn / (BrE)
a river in eastern Texas that flows south into the Gulf of Mexico, forming part of the border between Texas and Louisiana.
river forming part of Texas-Louisiana border

Examples

The Sabine is an important waterway for shipping and transportation in the region.
Heavy rains caused the Sabine to overflow its banks and flood nearby communities.
The boundary between Texas and Louisiana follows the course of the Sabine for much of its length.

Etymology

Named after the Spanish word 'sabina' (juniper), referring to the trees found along its banks.

3. NOUN

/ səˈbiːn / (AmE), / səˈbiːn / (BrE)
a member of an ancient Oscan-speaking people from central Italy, north of Rome, who were conquered and assimilated by the Romans in 290 BC
member of an ancient Italian people

Examples

Archaeologists have discovered artifacts from the ancient Sabine settlements north of Rome.
The Romans' assimilation of the Sabine people greatly influenced their culture and traditions.
Legends such as the Rape of the Sabine Women are central to early Roman history.

Etymology

From Latin Sabinus (of the Sabines), referring to the ancient people.
