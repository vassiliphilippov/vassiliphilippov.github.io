boogie

1. VERB

/ ˈbuːɡi / (AmE), / ˈbʊɡi / (BrE)
to dance energetically to boogie or upbeat music
dance energetically to music
informal

Examples

The DJ started playing funky tunes, and everyone began to boogie on the dance floor.
We used to boogie all night at the underground clubs downtown.
She loves to boogie whenever her favorite song comes on.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dance - move rhythmically to music

Etymology

From boogie (noun), referring to a style of blues music.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01901196-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ ˈbuːɡi / (AmE), / ˈbʊɡi / (BrE)
a style of blues music, especially for piano, characterized by a strong, fast rhythm and repetitive bass patterns
piano-based style of blues music

Examples

The pianist played a lively boogie that had everyone clapping along.
She learned to play boogie on the old upright piano in her living room.
Many early rock songs were influenced by the rhythmic patterns of classic boogie.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boogie-woogie - a style of blues piano with a strong, fast beat

Etymology

Possibly an alteration of 'boogie-woogie', an African American musical style originating in the early 20th century.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07077289-n
Synset POS: n
