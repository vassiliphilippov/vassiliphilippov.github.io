to condense

1. VERB

/ kənˈdɛns / (AmE), / kənˈdɛns / (BrE)
to cause a gas or vapor to change into a liquid, usually by cooling
change gas or vapor into liquid

Examples

The sudden drop in temperature caused the water vapor to condense on the windows.
Engineers use cooling systems to condense steam back into water in power plants.
When you breathe out on a cold day, your breath will condense into tiny droplets.

Synset Examples

The cold air condensed the steam

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to evaporate - to change from a liquid into a vapor

Etymology

From Latin condensare (to make thick, condense), from condensus (thick, dense).

2. VERB

/ kənˈdɛns / (AmE), / kənˈdɛns / (BrE)
to remove water or moisture from a substance, usually to make it thicker or more concentrated
remove water to concentrate

Examples

To make fudge, you often condense the milk by simmering it slowly.
The recipe says to condense the soup before adding it to the casserole.
They condensed the tomato sauce by boiling off the excess water.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: concentrate - make a substance denser by removing liquid

Etymology

From Latin 'condensare', meaning to make thick or dense.

In the early universe, matter started to condense into galaxies and stars.
