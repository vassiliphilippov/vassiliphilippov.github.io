kinship

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɪnʃɪp / (AmE), / ˈkɪnʃɪp / (BrE)
a strong connection or similarity between people, groups, or things, especially because of shared interests or character
close connection or similarity

Examples

She felt a strong kinship with the other artists in the group.
There is a clear kinship between jazz and blues music.
Many people experience a sense of kinship when they share similar values.

Synset Examples

found a natural affinity with the immigrants
felt a deep kinship with the other students
anthropology's kinship with the humanities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affinity - a natural liking or connection with someone or something
  • Synonym: relationship - the way in which two or more concepts, objects, or people are connected

Etymology

From Middle English 'kynship', from 'kyn' (kin) + '-ship'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɪnˌʃɪp / (AmE), / ˈkɪnʃɪp / (BrE)
(in anthropology) the state of being related by blood, marriage, or adoption; relationships among family members.
family relationship by blood, marriage, or adoption

Examples

Anthropologists study patterns of kinship to understand how societies organize families.
The sense of kinship between the two tribes was strengthened by years of intermarriage.
Adoption can create legal kinship ties where no biological connection exists.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: relationship - the way in which two or more people or things are connected

Etymology

From Middle English 'kin' + 'ship', modeled on relationship.
