to conclude

1. VERB

/ kənˈkluːd / (AmE), / kənˈkluːd / (BrE)
to reach or make an agreement, especially one that is formal or official
formally reach an agreement

Examples

The two countries finally concluded a peace treaty after months of negotiations.
The committee concluded an agreement with the union representatives last night.
After weeks of discussion, they concluded a contract that benefited both parties.

Synset Examples

They concluded an economic agreement
We concluded a cease-fire

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to agree - to have the same opinion or come to an arrangement

Etymology

From Latin 'concludere' (to shut up, enclose, finish) via Old French 'concludre'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01073784-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kənˈkluːd / (AmE), / kənˈkluːd / (BrE)
to bring something to an end, especially in a formal or definite way
bring to an end

Examples

The board decided to conclude the discussion after everyone had given their opinions.
She will conclude her speech with a call to action.
The ceremony concluded with a moment of silence.

Synset Examples

The committee concluded the meeting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finish - to bring something to an end
  • Synonym: to terminate - to bring something to a final end
  • Antonym: to commence - to begin or start something

Etymology

From Latin concludere, from con- (together) + claudere (to shut, close).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00716979-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kənˈklud / (AmE), / kənˈkluːd / (BrE)
to come to an end; to be brought to a close
to come to an end

Examples

The meeting will conclude at 5 p.m. sharp.
The ceremony concluded with a standing ovation from the audience.
Once negotiations conclude, the agreement will be signed.

Synset Examples

The concert closed with a nocturne by Chopin

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finish - to bring or come to an end
  • Synonym: end - to bring to a final point; terminate
  • Antonym: to begin - to start; to come into being

Etymology

From Latin 'concludere', from 'con-' (together) + 'claudere' (to shut).

4. VERB

/ kənˈkluːd / (AmE), / kənˈkluːd / (BrE)
to decide or come to a judgment after considering all the information and arguments
decide after discussion or consideration

Examples

After hours of debate, the committee concluded that the project should continue.
The scientists concluded from the evidence that the drug was safe.
We conclude that more research is needed before making a recommendation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to decide - to make a final choice after considering options
  • Synonym: to determine - to establish something after calculation or investigation
  • Antonym: to begin - to start or commence something

Etymology

From Latin concludere, from con- (together) + claudere (to shut).

5. VERB

/ kənˈkluːd / (AmE), / kənˈkluːd / (BrE)
to decide something after considering all the information; to reach a decision or judgment by reasoning
decide by reasoning; reach a conclusion

Examples

After careful analysis, the scientist concluded that the two chemicals react under heat.
The investigation concluded that the fire was caused by faulty wiring.
Based on the evidence, the judge concluded that the defendant was not guilty.

Synset Examples

We reasoned that it was cheaper to rent than to buy a house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infer - to deduce or conclude information from evidence and reasoning
  • Synonym: to decide - to make a final choice or judgment after considering options
  • Degree Lower: to speculate - to form a theory or conclusion without firm evidence

Etymology

From Latin 'concludere' (to shut up, enclose, end, finish, conclude), from 'con-' (together) + 'cludere' (to shut).
