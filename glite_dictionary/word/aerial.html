aerial

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛriəl / (AmE), / ˈeəriəl / (BrE)
a device for sending or receiving radio or television signals, typically consisting of a wire or rod
radio or TV signal receiver/sender
british

Examples

The old television needed a new aerial to get a good signal.
I climbed onto the roof to adjust the aerial during the storm.
Portable radios often come with a built-in aerial for better reception.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: antenna - a device for transmitting or receiving radio waves; common in American English

Etymology

From Latin 'aerius' (of the air); sense relating to radio first attested in the early 20th century.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛriəl / (AmE), / ˈeəriəl / (BrE)
In sports, especially American football, a forward pass thrown high through the air to a receiver down the field.
forward pass in football

Examples

The quarterback threw a perfect aerial that sailed forty yards down the field.
His ability to catch long aerials makes him a threat to any defense.
The team's winning touchdown came from a dramatic aerial in the final seconds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pass - an act of throwing the ball to a teammate in sports

Etymology

From Latin 'aerius' (of the air), via French 'aérial', referring to the air; used metaphorically for things occurring in or through the air.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00562263-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈɛriəl / (AmE), / ˈeəriəl / (BrE)
existing, happening, living, or operating in the air rather than on the ground or in water
in, happening, or operating in the air

Examples

The forest was surveyed using aerial photography from a low-flying plane.
Some orchids have aerial roots that absorb moisture from the air.
Modern militaries often invest heavily in aerial defense systems.

Synset Examples

aerial roots of a philodendron
aerial particles
small aerial creatures such as butterflies
aerial warfare
aerial photography
aerial cable cars

Related Terms

  • Antonym: terrestrial - existing or operating on land or the ground

Etymology

From Latin 'aerius' (airy), from Greek 'aērios' (of the air), from 'aēr' (air).

light, insubstantial, or intangible

Examples

Her thoughts drifted into aerial fancies, far removed from reality.
A soft, aerial glow surrounded the sculpture, making it seem almost intangible.
