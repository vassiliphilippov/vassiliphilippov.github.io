kirtle

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɝːtəl / (AmE), / ˈkɜːtl / (BrE)
a loose-fitting garment resembling a tunic, worn by men in the Middle Ages and later by women
medieval/later tunic-like garment

Examples

The knight donned his kirtle before fastening his armor.
Her green kirtle trailed softly over the stone floor of the manor hall.
Medieval peasants often wore a simple woolen kirtle for daily work.

Etymology

From Old English *cyrtel*, probably from Latin *curtus* (shortened, cut off).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɝːtəl / (AmE), / ˈkɜːtəl / (BrE)
A long, often loose-fitting dress or gown worn by women, especially in the Middle Ages and Renaissance.
long dress worn by women, esp. historically
archaic

Examples

The peasant girl wore a simple kirtle as she worked in the fields.
In the play, the actress was dressed in a green kirtle and a white apron.
Historical reenactors often sew their own kirtles to achieve accuracy in their costumes.

Etymology

From Old English *cyrtel*, related to Old Norse *kyrtill* (tunic).
