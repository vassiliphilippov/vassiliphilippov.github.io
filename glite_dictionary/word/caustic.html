caustic

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɔstɪk / (AmE), / ˈkɔːstɪk / (BrE)
A curved envelope of light formed by rays reflected or refracted by a curved surface or object, often seen as a bright pattern.
curve formed by focused light rays

Examples

The cup produced a striking caustic pattern on the table as sunlight passed through it.
Physicists studied the shape of the caustic formed by the curved mirror.
Ripples in the swimming pool created dancing caustic lines on the bottom.

Etymology

From Greek *kaustikos* (able to burn), referring in this sense to the burning effect of concentrated light.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɔstɪk / (AmE), / ˈkɔːstɪk / (BrE)
a chemical substance that burns, corrodes, or destroys living tissue by chemical action
substance that burns or destroys tissue

Examples

The laboratory strictly limits access to any caustic to prevent accidents.
Lye is a common caustic used in many cleaning products.
Always wear gloves when handling a caustic to protect your skin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrosive - a substance that can destroy or irreversibly damage another substance by chemical action

Etymology

From Latin causticus, from Greek kaustikos, from kaustos (burnt), from kaiein (to burn).

4. ADJ

/ ˈkɔstɪk / (AmE), / ˈkɔːstɪk / (BrE)
(of a substance) able to burn, corrode, or destroy organic tissue by chemical action, especially a strong acid or alkali.
able to burn or corrode by chemical action

Examples

The laboratory stored all caustic substances in clearly labeled containers.
The drain cleaner contains a caustic compound that dissolves organic matter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrosive - tending to cause corrosion

Etymology

From Latin causticus, from Greek kaustikos (capable of burning), from kaiein (to burn).
