to rarefy

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈreərɪˌfaɪ / (BrE)
to make or become less dense or solid; to thin out.
make less dense or thin out

Examples

As the air rose, it began to rarefy, making it harder for climbers to breathe.
The scientist used a vacuum pump to rarefy the gas inside the chamber.
With osteoporosis, the bones gradually rarefy and become more fragile.

Synset Examples

The bones are rarefied

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to condense - to make denser or more compact

Etymology

From Latin 'rarefacere', from 'rarus' (rare) + 'facere' (to make).

2. VERB

/ ˈrɛrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈreərəfʌɪ / (BrE)
to make or become less dense or more thin; to weaken the consistency of a substance, especially by reducing its density or concentration
make less dense or more thin

Examples

The chemist needed to rarefy the solution before continuing the experiment.
High altitudes rarefy the air, making it harder to breathe.
Certain processes can rarefy the mixture, resulting in a lighter consistency.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dilute - to make (a liquid) thinner or weaker by adding another substance

Etymology

From Latin 'rarefacere', from 'rarus' (thin, rare) + 'facere' (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00226805-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛrəˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈreərəˌfaɪ / (BrE)
to make something more subtle, refined, or abstract in character
make more subtle or refined

Examples

The philosopher sought to rarefy his arguments to achieve greater intellectual rigor.
Critics say that modern art can sometimes rarefy expression to the point of incomprehensibility.
Through years of training, the chef managed to rarefy traditional recipes into works of culinary art.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refine - to make something more pure, polished, or elegant

Etymology

From Latin 'rarefacere' (to make rare), from 'rarus' (rare) + 'facere' (to make).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00553215-v
Synset POS: v
