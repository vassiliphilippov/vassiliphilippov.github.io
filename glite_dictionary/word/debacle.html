debacle

1. NOUN

/ dəˈbɑkəl / (AmE), / deɪˈbɑːkəl / (BrE)
A sudden flooding caused by the violent breakup of ice in a river, especially during the spring or summer thaw.
flooding from river ice breakup

Examples

The spring debacle caused flooding along the riverbanks after the ice suddenly broke up.
Residents prepared for the annual debacle by moving valuables away from the flood-prone areas.
During the 19th century, a debacle could devastate entire riverside communities.

Etymology

From French 'débâcle' (breaking up of ice in a river, disaster), from 'débâcler' (to unbar, unleash).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈbɑkəl / (AmE), / deɪˈbɑːkəl / (BrE)
A sudden and complete failure; a total collapse, often in a violent or dramatic way.
sudden, disastrous collapse

Examples

The company’s new product launch turned into a complete debacle when the software malfunctioned.
The politician’s campaign was a debacle, ending in public embarrassment and financial loss.
After the team’s debacle in the finals, morale was at an all-time low.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disaster - a sudden event causing great damage or loss
  • Synonym: catastrophe - an event causing sudden and great damage or suffering
  • Antonym: success - the accomplishment of an aim or purpose

Etymology

From French 'débâcle' (breaking up of ice in a river; disaster), from 'débacler' (to unbar, free).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈbɑkəl / (AmE), / deɪˈbɑːkəl / (BrE)
a sudden and complete failure; a fiasco or a disastrous defeat
a sudden and disastrous failure or defeat

Examples

The team's 6-0 loss was a total debacle that shocked their fans.
After the financial debacle, the company struggled to recover its reputation.
The campaign ended in a political debacle for the once-popular candidate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fiasco - a complete failure, especially in a ludicrous or humiliating way
  • Synonym: catastrophe - an event causing great and usually sudden damage or suffering; a disaster
  • Antonym: triumph - a great victory or achievement

Etymology

From French débâcle (breaking up of ice, disaster), from débacler (to unbar, free).
