herd

1. NOUN

/ hɜrd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
a large group of wild or domestic animals of the same species that stay or move together
group of animals living together

Examples

A large herd of elephants moved across the plain at sunrise.
We watched as a herd of antelope grazed near the river.
Marine biologists tracked a herd of whales as they migrated south.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flock - a group of birds or sheep kept together
  • Synonym: pack - a group of hunting animals, especially wolves or dogs

Etymology

Old English heord (group of domestic animals), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08010997-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ hɜrd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
to move or cause people or animals to gather into a group, often by directing or controlling their movement
to gather or drive into a group

Examples

The teachers herded the students onto the bus for the field trip.
Security personnel herded the crowd towards the exits after the concert ended.
We herded our sheep into the barn before the storm began.

Synset Examples

We herded the children into a spare classroom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to round up - to gather together people or animals into one place

Etymology

From Old English heord (herd, flock).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02032568-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ hɝd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
a large group of animals, especially cattle or sheep, that live, feed, or are kept together
group of domestic animals kept together

Examples

The farmer led the herd of cattle back to the barn before sunset.
A herd of sheep grazed peacefully on the hillside.
When threatened, the animals in the herd moved closer together for protection.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flock - a group of birds, sheep, or goats kept together

Etymology

From Old English heord (herd, flock), related to German Herde.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-08010773-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ hɝd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
(of people or animals) to move together as a group in the same direction, especially in a manner resembling a herd.
move together as a group

Examples

Tourists began to herd through the museum when it opened.
During the emergency, people herded toward the nearest exit.
The sheep herded into the barn as the storm approached.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gather - to come together in one place; assemble
  • Antonym: to disperse - to scatter in different directions; to break up
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02032462-v
Synset POS: v

5. NOUN

/ hɝd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
a large group of people or things, especially considered as ordinary, undistinguished, or lacking individuality
large group of ordinary people or things

Examples

The artist refused to follow the herd and developed her own unique style.
Politicians sometimes address the herd rather than focusing on individual needs.
He prided himself on standing out from the herd in his thinking.

Synset Examples

his brilliance raised him above the ruck
the children resembled a fairy herd

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crowd - a large number of people gathered together
  • Antonym: elite - a group of people considered to be the best or most distinguished

Etymology

From Old English 'heord' (flock, herd), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

6. VERB

/ hɝd / (AmE), / hɜːd / (BrE)
to keep, move, or drive animals, especially as a group
move or keep animals as a group

Examples

The farmer will herd the sheep into the barn before sunset.
Ranchers often herd cattle across great distances during the season.
She learned to herd goats as a child on her family's farm.

Synset Examples

Who will be herding the cattle when the cowboy dies?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shepherd - to tend, herd, or guard sheep

Etymology

From Old English heordian (to attend a herd), from heord (herd).
