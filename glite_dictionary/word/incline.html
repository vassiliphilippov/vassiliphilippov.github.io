incline

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈklaɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈklaɪn / (BrE)
to be at an angle to the horizontal or vertical; to slope
be or cause to be at an angle; slope

Examples

The hillside inclines gently towards the river.
As the road inclines upward, trucks often slow down.
The roof inclined sharply to allow snow to slide off.

Synset Examples

The terrain sloped down

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slope - to be at an angle, especially sharply downward or upward

Etymology

From Latin inclinare, from in- (towards) + clinare (to lean)

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02040935-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈklaɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈklaɪn / (BrE)
to have a tendency or disposition to do or be something; to be likely to behave in a particular way
tend or be likely to do something

Examples

Even as a child, he would incline toward helping others in need.
She inclines to pessimism when facing new challenges.
Given his background, he naturally inclined to support the proposal.

Synset Examples

She tends to be nervous before her lectures
These dresses run small
He inclined to corpulence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to tend - to regularly or frequently behave in a particular way; be likely
  • Synonym: lean - to have a tendency toward (a particular opinion, action, etc.)
  • Antonym: to resist - to oppose or have a tendency against doing something

Etymology

From Latin inclinare, from in- (toward) + clinare (to bend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02725274-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈklaɪn / (AmE), / ɪnˈklaɪn / (BrE)
to feel willing or favorably disposed to do or believe something; to tend toward a particular opinion or course of action
feel favorably disposed; tend toward an opinion

Examples

I incline to agree with your suggestion, as it seems reasonable.
She often inclines toward a more optimistic view of the situation.
When faced with conflicting evidence, he inclined to trust his instincts.

Synset Examples

She inclines to the view that people should be allowed to express their religious beliefs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lean toward - to be likely to prefer or support a particular idea or opinion
  • Antonym: to disincline - to make or become unwilling or reluctant

Etymology

From Latin 'inclinare' (to lean toward, bend), via Old French 'incliner'.

Source: WordNet

5. NOUN

/ ˈɪnˌklaɪn / (AmE), / ˈɪn.klaɪn / (BrE)
a sloping surface or ramp that connects two different levels
a sloping surface between levels

Examples

The incline up to the bridge was steep and challenging for cyclists.
They built a wooden incline to make the entrance accessible for wheelchairs.
Walkers paused to catch their breath halfway up the incline.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slope - an area of ground that slopes upward or downward
  • Degree Lower: decline - a downward slope or bend
  • Degree Higher: escarpment - a steep slope or long cliff separating two levels

Etymology

From Latin inclinare (to lean, bend towards), via Old French incliner.
