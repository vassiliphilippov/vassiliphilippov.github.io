ensemble

1. NOUN

/ ɑnˈsɑmbəl / (AmE), / ɒnˈsɒmbəl / (BrE)
An assemblage of parts or details, especially in a work of art, regarded as forming a unified whole.
whole formed by assembled parts

Examples

The architect designed each room so that the ensemble creates a sense of harmony.
The painter’s attention to every element resulted in an ensemble that was both balanced and captivating.
Each detail in the sculpture contributes to the powerful ensemble seen by visitors.

Etymology

From French 'ensemble' (together, whole), from Latin 'insimul' (at the same time).

3. NOUN

/ ɑnˈsɑmbəl / (AmE), / ɒnˈsɒmbəl / (BrE)
The group of singers or dancers who perform as the chorus in a ballet or musical, supporting the main performers.
chorus group in ballet or musical

Examples

The ensemble rehearsed their routine alongside the principal dancers before opening night.
She joined the ensemble of the ballet company to gain more stage experience.
Although not in the spotlight, the ensemble plays a vital role in every performance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chorus - a group of performers who sing or dance together in a show

Etymology

From French 'ensemble' meaning 'together', originally from Latin 'insimul'.

5. NOUN

/ ɑnˈsɑmbəl / (AmE), / ɒnˈsɒmbəl / (BrE)
The members of a theatrical, musical, or dance group who perform together, excluding the principal performers; the supporting cast.
supporting cast in a production

Examples

The theater company had a talented ensemble that brought energy to every scene.
Unlike traditional musicals, the play featured a strong ensemble without a single star.
She was proud to be part of the ensemble, contributing to the show's success alongside her colleagues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cast - the actors in a play or film
  • Synonym: chorus - group of performers singing or dancing together, often not the principals

Etymology

From French ensemble (together), from Latin insimul (at the same time).
