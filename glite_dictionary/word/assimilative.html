assimilative

1. ADJ

/ əˈsɪməˌleɪtɪv / (AmE), / əˈsɪmɪlətɪv / (BrE)
able to absorb or incorporate substances such as gases, light, or liquids into a solution or into itself.
able to absorb or take in substances

Examples

Chlorophyll is an assimilative pigment in plants that captures light energy for photosynthesis.
The assimilative properties of activated charcoal help it remove toxins from the digestive tract.
Soil with higher assimilative capacity can filter and break down pollutants more effectively.

Synset Examples

an assimilative substance

Etymology

From Latin assimilativus, from assimilare (to make like).

2. ADJ

/ əˈsɪmɪlətɪv / (AmE), / əˈsɪmɪlətɪv / (BrE)
able to take in and incorporate information, ideas, or experiences mentally; capable of absorbing and integrating.
capable of mental absorption or integration

Examples

The child's assimilative abilities make learning languages easier at a young age.
His assimilative approach to research allowed him to merge different theoretical perspectives.
Successful adaptation requires an assimilative mindset, open to new experiences and knowledge.

Synset Examples

assimilative processes
assimilative capacity of the human mind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorptive - able to absorb or take in
  • Synonym: receptive - willing or able to receive new ideas

Etymology

From assimilate + -ive, after Latin assimilativus.
