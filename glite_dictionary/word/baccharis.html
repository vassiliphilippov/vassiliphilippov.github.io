Baccharis

1. NOUN

/ ˈbæk.ə.rɪs / (AmE), / ˈbak.ə.rɪs / (BrE)
A genus of shrubs native to the Americas, often with honey-scented flowers, whose species are used for erosion control and ornamental purposes.
genus of shrubs native to the Americas

Examples

Several species of Baccharis are planted to help prevent soil erosion along riverbanks.
The Baccharis shrubs filled the air with a pleasant, honey-like fragrance during late summer.
Gardeners often choose Baccharis for its resilience and ability to attract pollinators.

Etymology

From Latin Baccharis, from Greek bakkharis (a kind of fragrant plant).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbæk.ə.rɪs / (AmE), / ˈbak.ə.rɪs / (BrE)
a genus of shrubs native to the western hemisphere, often with honey-scented flowers and silky thistlelike fruit heads, sometimes used for erosion control
genus of honey-scented shrubs

Examples

Baccharis shrubs are commonly planted along highways for erosion control in the southwestern United States.
The honey-scented flowers of Baccharis attract many pollinators during spring and summer.
After blooming, Baccharis produces silky, thistlelike heads filled with tiny seeds.

Etymology

From Latin 'baccharis', from Greek 'bakcharis', a kind of plant.
