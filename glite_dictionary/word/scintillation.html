scintillation

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɪn(t)ɪˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪn.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a flash or sparkle of light; the quality of shining with a bright or sparkling reflected light
a flash or sparkle of light

Examples

The scintillation of the diamond caught everyone's eye under the bright lights.
Stars twinkled with a gentle scintillation in the clear night sky.
The scintillation of sunlight on the water made the lake appear magical.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sparkle - a glittering flash of light
  • Degree Lower: glow - a steady radiance of light or heat

Etymology

From Latin scintillatio (sparkling, glittering), from scintillare (to sparkle).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɪn(t)əˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪn.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
A flash of light produced in a phosphor or similar material when it absorbs a photon or an ionizing particle, especially in the context of detecting radiation.
flash of light in certain materials (physics)
medical

Examples

The detector registered a scintillation when it was struck by a gamma ray.
In nuclear physics, scintillation counters are used to measure radiation levels.
A single scintillation can indicate the absorption of a high-energy photon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flash - a sudden brief burst of bright light

Etymology

From Latin scintillatio (sparkle), from scintillare (to sparkle).

5. NOUN

/ ˌsɪn.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɪn.tɪˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the twinkling or sparkling of stars, caused when variations in the Earth's atmosphere produce uneven refraction of their light
twinkling of stars due to atmospheric effects

Examples

The scintillation of the stars made the night sky seem alive.
Astronomers must account for scintillation when making precise observations.
Atmospheric turbulence increases the scintillation that causes stars to twinkle.

  • Synonym: twinkle - shine with a gleam that changes constantly
