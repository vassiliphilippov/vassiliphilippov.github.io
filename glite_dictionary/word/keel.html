keel

1. NOUN

/ kil / (AmE), / kiːl / (BrE)
a main longitudinal beam or plate running along the bottom of a ship's hull, which can extend downward to provide stability and structural strength
longitudinal beam of a ship's hull

Examples

The ship's keel was laid in the dry dock before construction began.
A deep keel helps prevent a sailing yacht from capsizing in strong winds.
After the collision, divers inspected the vessel's keel for damage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hull - the main body of a ship or boat

Etymology

From Old English 'ceol' (ship, keel), of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'kiel' and German 'Kiel'.

2. NOUN

/ kil / (AmE), / kiːl / (BrE)
a ridge along the breastbone (sternum) of most birds, to which the flight muscles are attached
ridge on birds' breastbone for flight muscles

Examples

The large keel of the eagle supports the powerful muscles needed for flight.
Birds that do not fly generally lack a prominent keel on their breastbone.
The ornithologist explained how the keel distinguishes flying and flightless bird species.

Etymology

From Old Norse 'kjölr', meaning 'ship's keel'; transferred to anatomy because of resemblance to a ship's keel.

3. NOUN

/ kil / (AmE), / kiːl / (BrE)
a projecting ridge or structure resembling the keel of a boat, especially in anatomy, botany, or engineering.
a projecting ridge or structure

Examples

On the breastbone of many birds, there is a pronounced keel for flight muscle attachment.
Some flowers have a central keel formed by fused petals.
The dinosaur fossil displayed a bony keel running along its back.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridge - a long narrow raised part of a surface

Etymology

From Old English cēol (ship, ship's keel); extended metaphorical sense in scientific contexts.
