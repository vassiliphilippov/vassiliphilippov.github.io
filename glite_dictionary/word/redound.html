to redound

1. VERB

/ rɪˈdaʊnd / (AmE), / rɪˈdaʊnd / (BrE)
to contribute or lead to a particular result, especially as an advantage or disadvantage to someone
to contribute to a result, often as benefit or disadvantage

Examples

Her generosity will surely redound to her reputation in the community.
These policies may redound to the benefit of future generations.
Any success the project achieves will redound to all of the team members.

Synset Examples

Everything redounded to his glory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to contribute - to help to cause or bring about
  • Antonym: to detract - to diminish the worth or value of

Etymology

From Old French 'redonder', from Latin 'redundare' (to overflow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00155835-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ rɪˈdaʊnd / (AmE), / rɪˈdaʊnd / (BrE)
(of an action or event) to have an effect, especially as a consequence that rebounds for good or ill, often used in the phrase 'redound to (someone's) benefit/credit/discredit'.
to have a consequential effect

Examples

The company's generous donations will redound to its reputation in the community.
Any mistakes made here will redound to your disadvantage.
His hard work and innovation will redound to the benefit of the whole team.

Synset Examples

Her efforts will redound to the general good

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to contribute - to help to cause or bring about

Etymology

From Latin 'redundare' (to overflow).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00138037-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɪˈdaʊnd / (AmE), / rɪˈdaʊnd / (BrE)
(formal) To contribute greatly to or result in a particular consequence for someone or something, especially in the phrases 'redound to one's credit/honor/etc.'
contribute to or result in (formal)

Examples

Her generosity will redound to her honor.
The success of the project redounded to the entire team's credit.
Such actions rarely redound to anyone's benefit.

Synset Examples

Fame redounds to the heroes

Etymology

From Latin 'redundare' (to flow back, overflow).
