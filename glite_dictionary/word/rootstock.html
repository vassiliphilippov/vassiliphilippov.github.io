rootstock

1. NOUN

/ ˈrutˌstɑk / (AmE), / ˈruːtˌstɒk / (BrE)
the root or part of a plant, often from a different variety or species, used as the base onto which another plant is grafted and which supplies the roots
base root used in grafting plants

Examples

The gardener chose a hardy rootstock to improve the resilience of the grafted rose.
Many commercial fruit trees are grafted onto a rootstock that is resistant to soil-borne diseases.
Using the right rootstock can influence the size and productivity of the plant.

Etymology

Formed from 'root' + 'stock', referring to the stock (base) providing the root system.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrutˌstɑk / (AmE), / ˈruːt.stɒk / (BrE)
A horizontal plant stem, usually underground, with shoots growing above and roots growing below, serving as a reproductive structure; a rhizome.
horizontal plant stem with shoots and roots

Examples

The bamboo spreads quickly thanks to its extensive rootstock underground.
Gardeners often divide the rootstock in spring to propagate new plants.
Many perennials survive winter by storing energy in their thick rootstock.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhizome - an underground stem that sends out roots and shoots

Etymology

From root + stock, meaning the base of a plant's root system.
