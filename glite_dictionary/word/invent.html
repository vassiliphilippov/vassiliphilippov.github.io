to invent

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛnt / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛnt / (BrE)
to create or fabricate something, especially something false or untrue
to fabricate; make up (a lie, story, etc.)

Examples

The author decided to invent an entirely fictional town for her latest novel.
He was accused of trying to invent an alibi that never existed.
Some children like to invent stories about imaginary friends.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fabricate - to make up (a story, information, etc.) with intent to deceive
  • Synonym: lie - to say or write something untrue or misleading
  • Antonym: to discover - to find something that already exists rather than create it

Etymology

From Latin 'inventus', past participle of 'invenire' (to find, to devise).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈvɛnt / (AmE), / ɪnˈvɛnt / (BrE)
to create or devise (an idea, plan, explanation, theory, or principle) through mental effort or ingenuity
devise or create an idea or theory

Examples

She managed to invent a clever solution to the problem.
Scientists often invent new theories to explain unusual phenomena.
He had to invent an excuse for being late to the meeting.

Synset Examples

excogitate a way to measure the speed of light

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devise - to plan or invent by careful thought
  • Synonym: to conceive - to form or devise (a plan or idea) in the mind

Etymology

From Latin 'inventus', past participle of 'invenire' (to come upon, find).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
