to pierce

1. VERB

/ pɪrs / (AmE), / pɪəs / (BrE)
(of a sound) to go through something sharply or shrilly, especially so as to be strongly noticed or felt.
(of a sound) go through sharply or shrilly

Examples

A siren pierced the calm of the early morning.
Her shrill laughter pierced the silence in the hall.
Thunderous applause pierced the stillness of the theater.

Synset Examples

The scream pierced the night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to penetrate - (of a sound) to enter or pass through something

Etymology

From Old French *percer*, of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02139952-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ pɪrs / (AmE), / pɪəs / (BrE)
to make a hole in or through something, especially with a sharp object
make a hole in or through

Examples

The sharp thorn pierced his finger, making him yelp in pain.
Sunlight pierced the clouds, illuminating the valley below.
She watched as the surgeon skillfully pierced the swollen tissue with a needle.

Synset Examples

The needle pierced her flesh

Related Terms

  • Synonym: puncture - to make a small hole in something
  • Synonym: to penetrate - to go into or through something

Etymology

From Old French 'percer', from Vulgar Latin '*pertusiare', from Latin 'pertusus' (bored through).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01443719-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ pɪrs / (AmE), / pɪəs / (BrE)
to penetrate or cut through something with a sharp instrument
penetrate with a sharp object

Examples

The needle pierced his skin quickly but painlessly.
A loud scream pierced the silence of the night.
Sunlight pierced through the dense clouds as the storm cleared.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to penetrate - to go into or through something, especially with force or effort
  • Synonym: stab - to push a pointed object into someone or something
  • Antonym: seal - to close tightly so as not to let in or out; opposite of pierce in some uses

Etymology

From Old French *percer*, of uncertain origin, perhaps from Vulgar Latin *pertusiare*, from Latin *pertusus* (bored through).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01444129-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ pɪrs / (AmE), / pɪəs / (BrE)
to affect someone's emotions or sensations deeply or sharply, often in a sudden or powerful way
affect deeply or sharply (feelings/sensations)

Examples

His harsh criticism pierced her confidence, leaving her feeling vulnerable.
A sudden sense of loneliness pierced him as he entered the empty house.
The news of her friend's illness pierced her heart.

Synset Examples

The cold pierced her bones
Her words pierced the students

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stab - to wound or pierce with a pointed object; can be used figuratively to mean deeply hurt emotionally
  • Synonym: touch - to affect emotionally, to move someone

Etymology

From Old French *percer*, from Vulgar Latin *perciāre* (to pierce through).

Source: WordNet

5. VERB

/ pɪrs / (AmE), / pɪəs / (BrE)
to make a way through or into something, especially by cutting or penetrating
make a way through; penetrate

Examples

The headlights pierced the thick fog as the car moved forward.
A narrow path pierced the dense woods, leading to the river.
Sunlight pierced the clouds after the heavy rain.

Synset Examples

the knife cut through the flesh
The path pierced the jungle
Light pierced through the forest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to penetrate - to go into or through something, especially when difficult
  • Synonym: to perforate - to make a hole through something

Etymology

From Old French *percer*, from Vulgar Latin *pertusiare*, from Latin *pertusus* (bored through).
