to contradict

1. VERB

/ ˌkɑntrəˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌkɒntrəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to state that something is not true or to assert the opposite of what someone else has said
deny the truth of; assert the opposite

Examples

The witness contradicted the defendant's statement in court.
He tends to contradict everything I say, even when I'm right.
Several facts contradict the official version of events.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deny - to declare untrue or refuse to accept as true
  • Antonym: to affirm - to state something is true or confirm positively

Etymology

From Latin 'contradictus', past participle of 'contradicere' (to speak against), from 'contra' (against) + 'dicere' (to say).

2. VERB

/ ˌkɑːn.trəˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌkɒn.trəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to say or suggest that something is not true, or to be in conflict with something
deny or be in conflict with

Examples

The witness's statement contradicts the evidence presented in court.
His actions often contradict his words, making him difficult to trust.
The results of the new study contradict earlier findings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to deny - to state that something is not true
  • Antonym: to affirm - to state as a fact; assert strongly

Etymology

From Latin 'contradictus', past participle of 'contradicere' (to speak against), from 'contra-' (against) + 'dicere' (to say).

3. VERB

/ ˌkɑːntrəˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌkɒntrəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to say or prove that something is wrong or untrue; to show by argument or evidence that something is false
to prove that something is wrong or false

Examples

The new evidence seemed to contradict the witness's earlier testimony.
Several scientists published data that contradict the previous theory.
Her actions contradict her stated values.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disprove - to show that something is false or incorrect
  • Antonym: to confirm - to establish the truth or correctness of something

Etymology

From Latin 'contradictus', past participle of 'contradicere' (to speak against), from 'contra' (against) + 'dicere' (to say).

4. VERB

/ ˌkɑːn.trəˈdɪkt / (AmE), / ˌkɒn.trəˈdɪkt / (BrE)
to oppose or resist something, especially by expressing or taking a different position
oppose or resist; disagree

Examples

Some members of the committee chose to contradict the proposed policy changes.
He was not afraid to contradict his supervisor when he disagreed with the plan.
The results of the new study contradict previous research in the field.

Synset Examples

The board opposed his motion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to oppose - to act against or resist something
  • Antonym: support - to give approval or assistance to something

Etymology

From Latin 'contradictus', past participle of 'contradicere' (to speak against), from 'contra' (against) + 'dicere' (to speak).
