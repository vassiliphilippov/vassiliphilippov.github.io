motility

1. NOUN

/ moʊˈtɪlɪti / (AmE), / məʊˈtɪlɪti / (BrE)
the ability of an organism or cell to move spontaneously and independently
ability to move spontaneously
medical

Examples

Bacterial motility enables them to move toward nutrients and away from harmful substances.
The doctor tested the patient's intestinal motility to diagnose digestive problems.
Certain medications can affect the motility of sperm, which is important for fertility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mobility - ability to move easily from one place to another

Etymology

From Latin 'mōtus' (movement) + '-ility', modeled after 'motility' in French scientific vocabulary.

2. NOUN

/ moʊˈtɪl.ə.ti / (AmE), / məʊˈtɪl.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the ability or power of an organism or part of an organism to move spontaneously and actively, without external force
ability to move spontaneously
medical

Examples

Gastrointestinal motility disorders can cause symptoms like bloating and abdominal pain.
Certain bacteria have flagella that contribute to their motility and ability to infect hosts.
The doctor assessed the sperm motility as part of the fertility examination.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mobility - the ability to move or be moved easily

Etymology

From Latin 'motus' (motion, movement) + '-ility' (indicating ability or capacity).
