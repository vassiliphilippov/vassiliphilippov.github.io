liquidation

1. NOUN

/ ˌlɪkwɪˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌlɪkwɪˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the deliberate killing or elimination of a rival or competitor, often for political or criminal reasons
murder or elimination of a rival

Examples

The gang leader ordered the liquidation of his main rival to consolidate power.
Several witnesses disappeared after the liquidation of the political dissident.
The secret police were reportedly responsible for the liquidation of opposition members.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assassination - the targeted killing of an important person, often for political reasons

Etymology

From Latin 'liquidatio' (a melting, dissolution), extended figuratively to mean elimination.

2. NOUN

/ ˌlɪkwɪˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌlɪkwɪˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of destroying or eliminating people, often in large numbers, especially in a violent or systematic way
extermination; mass killing

Examples

The regime's brutal liquidation of political dissidents shocked the international community.
After the war, evidence of the liquidation of entire villages was uncovered.
Historians still debate the circumstances surrounding the liquidation of the ethnic minority.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extermination - the complete destruction or killing of a group

Etymology

From French 'liquidation', from Latin 'liquidare' (to melt, make liquid, clear up).

3. NOUN

/ ˌlɪkwəˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌlɪkwɪˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of bringing a business to an end and distributing its assets to claimants, typically by selling off its assets to pay debts
the closing and settling of a business's debts
legal

Examples

After months of losses, the company began the process of liquidation to pay its creditors.
Creditors were notified that a court-ordered liquidation would be initiated next week.
During liquidation, the assets of the business were sold to cover outstanding debts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bankruptcy - legal state of being unable to pay debts
  • Antonym: solvency - the ability to meet long-term financial obligations

Etymology

From French 'liquidation', from Latin 'liquidare' (to melt, clarify, liquidate).
