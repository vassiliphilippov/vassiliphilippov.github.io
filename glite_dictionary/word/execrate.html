to execrate

1. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪkrɛɪt / (BrE)
to feel or express great loathing or hatred for someone or something; to denounce strongly
feel or express intense hatred

Examples

Historians often execrate the brutality of the war.
She publicly execrated the corrupt regime in her speech.
Many people execrate cruelty to animals and actively oppose it.

Synset Examples

I loathe that man
She abhors cats

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to loathe - to feel intense dislike or disgust for
  • Synonym: to abhor - to regard with disgust and hatred
  • Synonym: to detest - to dislike intensely
  • Antonym: love - to feel deep affection; opposite of hatred

Etymology

From Latin 'execrari' (to curse), from 'ex-' (out) + 'sacrare' (to devote to holiness, to curse).

2. VERB

/ ˈɛk.sɪˌkreɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛk.sɪˌkreɪt / (BrE)
To denounce or express vehement hatred for someone or something; to curse or declare to be evil.
to denounce or curse as evil

Examples

The preacher would execrate the corruption he saw in society from the pulpit.
Many citizens openly execrated the actions of the oppressive regime.
He could not help but execrate those who betrayed their friends.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to call down harm or misfortune upon; to wish evil upon
  • Antonym: to bless - to confer or invoke divine favor upon; to make holy

Etymology

From Latin execrari, past participle execratus, from ex- 'out' + sacrare 'to dedicate as sacred'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
