overbid

1. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvɚˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈbɪd / (BrE)
(Bridge) a bid that is higher than an opponent's, especially when the bid exceeds the value of one's hand.
bid higher than appropriate (in cards)

Examples

Her overbid in the last round surprised everyone at the table.
Making an overbid can sometimes put your side at a disadvantage in bridge.
The expert warned against an overbid when your partner has not entered the auction.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underbid - in cards, a bid lower than required or appropriate
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06750507-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ ˌoʊvərˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈbɪd / (BrE)
a bid that exceeds the highest previous bid, especially in auctions or card games such as bridge
a bid higher than previous ones

Examples

The item was sold to the highest overbid at the auction.
In bridge, his aggressive overbid put the opponents under pressure.
She made an overbid and ended up paying more than the painting was worth.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underbid - a bid that is lower than another or than is necessary

Etymology

From 'over-' (above) + 'bid' (offer a price), formed in English.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07179949-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to bid for more tricks or a higher amount than one can reasonably expect to achieve, especially in card games or auctions
bid more than can be won or achieved

Examples

He overbid during the bridge game and ended up losing more tricks than he could win.
At the auction, several bidders overbid, driving the price above the item's value.
She regretted overbidding after realizing her hand was too weak to win so many tricks.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underbid - to make a lower bid than an opponent or than expected

Etymology

From over- (excessive) + bid (make an offer).

Source: WordNet

4. VERB

/ ˌoʊvɚˈbɪd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈbɪd / (BrE)
to offer a price for something that is higher than its actual value or worth, especially in an auction or competitive bidding
to bid more than something is worth

Examples

If you overbid at the auction, you might regret your purchase later.
She realized too late that she had overbid for the painting and paid twice its value.
They tried not to overbid during the heated negotiation for the contract.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to underbid - to offer a lower price than others, especially in a competitive bidding process
  • Synonym: to outbid - to offer more than another person in a bidding process

Etymology

From over- (excessively) + bid (offer a price), modeled after other over- formation verbs.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02304513-v
Synset POS: v
