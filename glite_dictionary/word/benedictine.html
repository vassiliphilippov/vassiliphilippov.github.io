Benedictine

1. NOUN

/ ˌbɛnəˈdɪktin / (AmE), / ˌbɛnɪˈdɪktiːn / (BrE)
a monk or nun who belongs to the monastic order founded by Saint Benedict
member of Saint Benedict's monastic order

Examples

She spent several years as a Benedictine in a hilltop abbey.
The Benedictine monks are known for their contributions to education and music.
He met a Benedictine while traveling through Italy who shared stories about monastic life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: monk - a man who has taken religious vows and lives in a monastery
  • Synonym: nun - a woman who has taken religious vows and lives in a convent or monastery

Etymology

From Latin Benedictinus, from Benedictus (Saint Benedict).

2. ADJ

/ ˌbɛnəˈdɪktin / (AmE), / ˌbɛnɪˈdɪktiːn / (BrE)
of or relating to Saint Benedict or to the monastic order founded by him or his teachings
of Saint Benedict or his order

Examples

He devoted his thesis to the study of Benedictine spirituality in medieval Europe.
The monks followed the strict Benedictine rule of life within the abbey.
The library contains a rare collection of Benedictine manuscripts dating back to the 12th century.

Etymology

From Latin 'Benedictus', the name of Saint Benedict of Nursia (c.480–c.550), founder of the Benedictine order.

3. ADJ

/ ˌbɛnəˈdɪktin / (AmE), / ˌbɛnɪˈdɪktiːn / (BrE)
relating to the Benedictines, a religious order following the rule of St. Benedict
relating to the Benedictine religious order

Examples

The monks followed Benedictine traditions in their daily prayers and routines.
She researched Benedictine history for her thesis on medieval monasteries.
The Benedictine rule emphasizes balance, moderation, and reason in monastic life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Cistercian - relating to the Cistercians, another religious order
  • Synonym: Jesuit - relating to the Jesuits, a Catholic religious order

Etymology

From Medieval Latin Benedictinus, from Benedictus (St. Benedict).

4. NOUN

/ ˌbɛnəˈdɪktin / (AmE), / ˌbɛnɪˈdɪktɪn / (BrE)
a French herbal liqueur originally made by Benedictine monks, known for its distinctive flavor and aroma
French herbal liqueur

Examples

He ordered a glass of Benedictine after dinner for its unique flavor.
The recipe calls for a splash of Benedictine to enhance the cocktail.
Many collectors value vintage bottles of Benedictine for their history and taste.

Etymology

Named after the Benedictine monks who first produced it in Normandy, France.
