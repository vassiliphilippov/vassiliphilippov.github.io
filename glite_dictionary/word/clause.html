clause

1. NOUN

/ klɔz / (AmE), / klɔːz / (BrE)
A group of words containing a subject and a predicate, forming part of a sentence or constituting a whole simple sentence.
part of a sentence with subject and predicate

Examples

A relative clause often begins with a word like 'who' or 'which'.
Complex sentences usually contain more than one clause.
In the sentence, 'When it rains, we stay inside,' the first clause is dependent.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: phrase - a group of words without a subject-predicate structure

Etymology

From Old French 'clause', from Medieval Latin 'clausa' (close, conclusion), from Latin 'claudere' (to close).

2. NOUN

/ klɔz / (AmE), / klɑːz / (BrE)
a distinct section or provision in a legal document, such as a contract, statute, or will
section of a legal document
legal

Examples

Each clause in the agreement must be carefully reviewed for accuracy and completeness.
The contract contains a confidentiality clause that prohibits sharing sensitive information.
If either party fails to meet the conditions in this clause, the contract will be considered void.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: article - a numbered section of a legal document or contract
  • Synonym: provision - a specific requirement or stipulation in a legal document

Etymology

From Latin clausula (a closing, conclusion), diminutive of clausus (closed), from claudere (to close).
