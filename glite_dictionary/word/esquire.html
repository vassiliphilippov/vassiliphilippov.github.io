esquire

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈskwaɪər / (AmE), / ɪˈskwaɪə / (BrE)
a title of respect placed after a man's name, originally for members of the English gentry ranking just below a knight
title of respect for a man, below a knight

Examples

He signed the letter as John Smith, Esquire, to show his professional status.
The title Esquire was traditionally used for members of the English gentry.
Most British lawyers use Esquire after their names in formal correspondence.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: squire - young nobleman acting as an attendant to a knight; also a country gentleman

Etymology

From Old French *esquier* (squire), from Late Latin *scutarius* (shield-bearer).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈskwaɪər / (AmE), / ɪˈskwaɪə / (BrE)
(historical) A young man acting as an attendant and shield-bearer to a knight, regarded as a candidate for knighthood in the Middle Ages.
attendant and candidate for knighthood
archaic

Examples

During the Middle Ages, an esquire would accompany a knight and learn the ways of chivalry.
He served as an esquire before being dubbed a knight for his bravery in battle.
The young esquire polished his master's armor in preparation for the tournament.

Etymology

From Old French *escuier* (shield-bearer), from Late Latin *scutarius* (shield-bearer).
