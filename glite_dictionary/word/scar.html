scar

1. VERB

/ skɑr / (AmE), / skɑː / (BrE)
to mark the skin or a surface permanently with a scar, often as a result of injury, disease, or surgery
leave a permanent mark on skin or surface

Examples

The accident scarred her for life, both physically and emotionally.
Years of conflict have scarred the landscape, leaving visible traces of destruction.
If you pick at the wound, it may scar and never fully heal.

Synset Examples

The skin disease scarred his face permanently

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mark - to make a visible impression or spot on something

2. NOUN

/ skɑr / (AmE), / skɑː / (BrE)
A mark left on the skin or other tissue after a wound, burn, or sore has healed.
mark left by healed wound

Examples

After the surgery, a small scar remained above his eyebrow.
The scar on her arm reminded her of a childhood accident.
He wore his scar with pride, viewing it as a sign of survival.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blemish - a small mark or flaw that spoils the appearance of something
  • Degree Lower: wound - an injury to body tissue, typically involving a cut or break in the skin
  • Degree Higher: disfigurement - the action or process of spoiling the appearance or shape of something

Etymology

From Old French *escharre*, from Late Latin *eschara* (scab), from Greek *eskhara* (scab, scar).

3. NOUN

/ skɑr / (AmE), / skɑː / (BrE)
a mark left on the skin or other surface after a wound, injury, or damage has healed; a lasting sign of past damage
mark left by injury or damage

Examples

The car accident left a permanent scar on his forehead.
Her childhood experience left an emotional scar that she carried into adulthood.
A deep scar cut across the hillside where the landslide occurred.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blemish - a small mark or flaw that spoils the appearance of something
  • Synonym: wound - an injury to body tissue, especially that involving a cut or break in the skin
  • Antonym: to heal - to become sound or healthy again after damage or injury

Etymology

From Old Norse 'skarð' (notch, gap, chasm), related to Old French 'escharre' (scab), and late Latin 'eschara' (scab, scar).
