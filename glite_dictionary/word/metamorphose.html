to metamorphose

1. VERB

/ ˌmɛtəˈmɔrfoʊz / (AmE), / ˌmɛtəˈmɔːfəʊz / (BrE)
to change completely in form, nature, or appearance, especially into something very different
change completely in form or nature

Examples

The caterpillar will metamorphose into a butterfly after several weeks.
The city began to metamorphose into a vibrant cultural hub.
Her experiences abroad helped her metamorphose into a confident and independent person.

Synset Examples

In Kafka's story, a person metamorphoses into a bug
The treatment and diet transfigured her into a beautiful young woman
Jesus was transfigured after his resurrection

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to transform - to change the form, appearance, or structure of
  • Synonym: to transfigure - to change completely the appearance or form of, often in a way that inspires awe
  • Antonym: preserve - to keep something in its original state or in good condition

Etymology

From Greek metamorphoun, from meta- (change) + morphē (form).

2. VERB

/ ˌmɛtəˈmɔrˌfoʊz / (AmE), / ˌmɛtəˈmɔːfəʊz / (BrE)
to change completely in form or nature, especially in a way that seems magical or surprising
undergo or cause complete change of form

Examples

The caterpillar will metamorphose into a beautiful butterfly after several weeks.
Her shy personality seemed to metamorphose during college, and she became much more outgoing.
Over the years, the city centre has metamorphosed into a bustling commercial district.

Synset Examples

He transformed into a monster
The salesman metamorphosed into an ugly beetle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to transform - to change the form, appearance, or structure of something
  • Synonym: to transmute - to change or alter in form, appearance, or nature, often for the better

Etymology

From Greek 'metamorphoun' (to transform), from 'meta-' (change) + 'morphē' (form).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00382272-v
Synset POS: v
