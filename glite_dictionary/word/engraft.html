to engraft

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡræft / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡrɑːft / (BrE)
to fix or implant something securely or deeply; to establish firmly
to fix or implant firmly

Examples

Modern surgeons can engraft healthy tissue onto damaged areas to aid recovery.
Over time, the traditions became engrafted into the local culture.
The dentist managed to engraft the artificial tooth seamlessly into her gum.

Synset Examples

He planted a knee in the back of his opponent
The dentist implanted a tooth in the gum

Related Terms

  • Synonym: implant - to insert or fix in a person's body, especially by surgery
  • Synonym: to embed - to fix firmly and deeply in a surrounding mass

Etymology

From Late Latin *ingraftare*, from Latin *in-* (in) + *graftare* (to graft).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡræft / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡrɑːft / (BrE)
to insert or join a part of one plant into another so that they grow together as a single plant
join plant parts to grow together

Examples

In spring, skilled gardeners often engraft apple branches onto sturdy rootstocks.
The horticulturist showed us how to engraft a healthy stem onto a diseased tree.
They successfully engrafted a rare rose onto a more common variety.

Synset Examples

graft the cherry tree branch onto the plum tree

Related Terms

  • Synonym: graft - to insert a shoot or twig into another plant so that they grow together

Etymology

From Old French en- (in) + grafter (to graft), ultimately from Latin graphium (stylus), referring to incision.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01533466-v
Synset POS: v
