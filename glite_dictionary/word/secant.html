secant

1. NOUN

/ ˈsiːkænt / (AmE), / ˈsiːkənt / (BrE)
a straight line that intersects a curve at two or more points
line cutting a curve at two+ points

Examples

In geometry, a secant of a circle is a line that cuts the circle at two points.
The teacher asked the students to draw a secant through the given parabola.
Unlike a tangent, a secant actually passes through the curve instead of just touching it.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tangent - a straight line that touches a curve at a single point without crossing it

Etymology

From Latin secant-, secans, present participle of secare (to cut).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsiːkənt / (AmE), / ˈsiːkənt / (BrE)
A trigonometric function equal to the ratio of the length of the hypotenuse to that of the adjacent side in a right-angled triangle; the reciprocal of the cosine.
trigonometric function; hypotenuse to adjacent side

Examples

The secant of 30 degrees is equal to two over the square root of three.
In trigonometry, secant is written as 'sec' and is the reciprocal of the cosine function.
To solve the equation, you need to find the value of the secant at various angles.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cosine - trigonometric function; adjacent side over hypotenuse
  • Synonym: cosecant - reciprocal of the sine in trigonometry

Etymology

From Latin secans, secantis, present participle of secare (to cut).
