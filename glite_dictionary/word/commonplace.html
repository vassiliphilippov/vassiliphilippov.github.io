commonplace

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɑːmənˌpleɪs / (AmE), / ˈkɒmənˌpleɪs / (BrE)
Ordinary and unremarkable; not unusual or special.
ordinary; unremarkable

Examples

For many people, remote work has become commonplace in recent years.
Smartphones are now so commonplace that nearly everyone owns one.
The use of email for business communication is entirely commonplace today.

Synset Examples

air travel has now become commonplace
commonplace everyday activities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ordinary - with no special or distinctive features; normal
  • Antonym: remarkable - worthy of attention; striking

Etymology

From common + place, originally meaning a topic or passage for general use, later 'ordinary'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01678501-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ˈkɑː.mənˌpleɪs / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.ənˌpleɪs / (BrE)
Not challenging, dull, and lacking excitement; unremarkable or routine.
dull; lacking excitement or challenge

Examples

The work became commonplace after years of repeating the same tasks.
Her days felt commonplace and lacking in any real adventure.
He wanted to escape his commonplace routine and find excitement elsewhere.

Synset Examples

an unglamorous job greasing engines

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mundane - lacking interest or excitement; dull
  • Antonym: exciting - causing great enthusiasm and eagerness
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00926081-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈkɑː.mənˌpleɪs / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.ənˌpleɪs / (BrE)
(of a remark, idea, or phrase) overused and therefore unoriginal; lacking freshness or originality through frequent use.
lacking originality; overused

Examples

The speech was full of commonplace expressions that failed to inspire the audience.
Critics described the movie's plot as predictable and commonplace.
He avoided commonplace topics in his writing, seeking fresh and unique ideas instead.

Synset Examples

his remarks were trite and commonplace
hackneyed phrases
a stock answer
repeating threadbare jokes
parroting some timeworn axiom
the trite metaphor ‘hard as nails’

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trite - lacking originality or freshness, due to overuse
  • Synonym: hackneyed - (of a phrase or idea) lacking significance through having been overused
  • Antonym: original - new and not derived from anything else

Etymology

From Middle French 'commun place', calque of Latin 'locus communis' (general topic, common place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01693210-s
Synset POS: s

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɑmənˌpleɪs / (AmE), / ˈkɒmənpleɪs / (BrE)
A remark, idea, or statement that is unoriginal, trite, or obvious and lacks novelty.
a trite or obvious remark

Examples

His speech was full of commonplace about hard work and determination.
It's disappointing when a novel only offers commonplace instead of original insights.
She tried to avoid making any commonplace during the discussion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: platitude - a remark or statement, especially one with a moral content, that has been used too often to be interesting or thoughtful
  • Synonym: cliché - a phrase or opinion that is overused and betrays a lack of original thought
  • Antonym: witticism - a clever or witty remark (opposite of dull or trite)

Etymology

From Middle French 'commun place', from Latin 'locus communis' (general or common topic).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07168754-n
Synset POS: n
