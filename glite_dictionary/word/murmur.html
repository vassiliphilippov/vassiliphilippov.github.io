murmur

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɝː.mɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɜː.mə / (BrE)
An abnormal sound heard during a heartbeat, sometimes indicating a problem with the heart valves or blood flow.
abnormal heart sound
medical

Examples

The doctor detected a faint murmur during the physical examination.
A heart murmur can be harmless or a sign of an underlying condition.
Children are sometimes born with a murmur that disappears as they grow older.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: heart sound - a normal sound of the heart produced by the closing of heart valves

Etymology

From Latin murmurar (to murmur, mutter).

2. VERB

/ ˈmɝː.mɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɜː.mə / (BrE)
to speak in a soft, low, and often indistinct voice
speak softly or indistinctly

Examples

She murmured a few soothing words to calm the frightened child.
He murmured something under his breath and quickly walked away.
Lying in bed, she murmured a quiet goodnight before drifting off to sleep.

Synset Examples

She murmured softly to the baby in her arms

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whisper - to speak very quietly so that only someone close can hear you

Etymology

From Latin 'murmurare' (to murmur), from 'murmur' (a low sound).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01045986-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɝː.mɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɜː.mə / (BrE)
a low and indistinct expression of complaint or discontent
a quiet complaint or protest

Examples

There was a murmur of discontent among the employees after the new policy was announced.
She heard a faint murmur of protest from the back of the room.
Despite the harsh decision, no more than a quiet murmur was heard from the students.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grumble - a muted complaint or mutter, especially about something minor
  • Degree Lower: whisper - speak very softly using one's breath without vocal cords
  • Degree Higher: complaint - an expression of dissatisfaction or annoyance

Etymology

From Latin 'murmur' (a low, continuous sound; a muttered complaint).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07225752-n
Synset POS: n

4. NOUN

/ ˈmɝː.mɚ / (AmE), / ˈmɜː.mə / (BrE)
a low, continuous, indistinct sound, often made by people speaking quietly or by soft movement
low, continuous indistinct sound

Examples

A gentle murmur drifted through the audience as the speaker approached the podium.
She could hear the soft murmur of voices from the next room.
The forest was filled with the murmur of leaves rustling in the breeze.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whisper - soft and low spoken sound
  • Synonym: babble - continuous low, murmuring sound, especially of water or indistinct speech
  • Antonym: roar - loud, deep, continuous sound

Etymology

From Latin murmur (a murmur, muttering).

to say something in a low, quiet, often complaining or discontented voice, especially so that others can barely hear

Examples

He murmured a complaint, hoping no one would hear.
