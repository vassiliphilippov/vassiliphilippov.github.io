carnival

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑrnəvəl / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnɪvəl / (BrE)
A traveling entertainment show with sideshows, amusement rides, and games of skill, often set up temporarily in a town or city.
traveling amusement show with rides and games

Examples

The carnival set up in the town square attracted visitors from neighboring cities.
We enjoyed the roller coasters and games at the carnival last weekend.
Every summer, a traveling carnival brings excitement and color to the small rural communities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: funfair - a temporary outdoor event with rides and amusements; especially British usage

Etymology

From Italian 'carnevale', originally a festival marking the beginning of Lent.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑrnɪvəl / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnɪvəl / (BrE)
A public festival typically involving street processions, music, dancing, and general merrymaking, often held before Lent in many cultures.
festival with processions and celebrations

Examples

The annual carnival attracts thousands of visitors from across the country.
Colorful floats and costumes are a highlight of the city's famous carnival.
After the parade, everyone joined the joyous carnival celebrations in the main square.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: festival - a day or period of celebration

Etymology

From Italian 'carnevale', from Medieval Latin 'carnelevamen' (removal of meat), referring to the period before Lent when meat was traditionally given up.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑrnəvəl / (AmE), / ˈkɑːnɪvəl / (BrE)
A situation characterized by wild, noisy, and disorganized activity, often with a playful or chaotic atmosphere, similar to that of a public festival.
wild, noisy, disorganized activity

Examples

The debate quickly turned into a carnival of shouting and laughter.
After the results were announced, a carnival of confusion swept through the office.
Her birthday party was a carnival of noise, games, and excited children.

Synset Examples

it was so funny it was a circus
the whole occasion had a carnival atmosphere

Related Terms

  • Synonym: circus - A scene of wild confusion or uproar; used similarly to describe chaotic situations

Etymology

From Italian 'carnevale', ultimately from Latin 'carne vale' meaning 'farewell to meat', referring to feasting before Lent.
