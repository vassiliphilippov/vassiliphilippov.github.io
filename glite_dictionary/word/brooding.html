brooding

1. ADJ

/ ˈbruːdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbruːdɪŋ / (BrE)
showing deep or serious thought, often with a slightly dark or somber mood
deeply or seriously thoughtful

Examples

The novel's brooding protagonist spends hours contemplating his fate.
She sat by the window, lost in a brooding silence as the rain fell.
There was a brooding intensity in his eyes that made everyone uneasy.

Synset Examples

Byron lives on not only in his poetry, but also in his creation of the ‘Byronic hero’ - the persona of a brooding melancholy young man

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thoughtful - absorbed in thought; reflective
  • Antonym: cheerful - noticeably happy and optimistic

Etymology

Present participle of 'brood', from Middle English broden, from Old English brōdian.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbruː.dɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbruː.dɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of a bird sitting on eggs to incubate and hatch them by the warmth of its body
incubation of eggs by a bird

Examples

The mother hen's brooding ensured that all the chicks hatched safely.
Proper brooding conditions are essential for the health of young poultry.
Scientists observed the brooding behavior of penguins in the wild.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incubation - the process of keeping eggs warm so they develop and hatch

Etymology

From the verb 'brood' (to incubate eggs), which comes from Old English brōd (offspring, brood).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13462985-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈbruːdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbruːdɪŋ / (BrE)
a state of dwelling persistently and gloomily on a problem or difficulty; deep, somber, and often morbid reflection.
persistent gloomy meditation; deep reflection

Examples

His constant brooding over lost opportunities kept him from moving forward.
You could sense a mood of brooding in the room after the bad news was announced.
Prolonged brooding about your mistakes won't help you solve them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rumination - deep or considered thought about something, often persistent
  • Synonym: musing - a period of reflection or thought

Etymology

From the verb 'brood', originating in Old English 'brōdian' (to sit on eggs, hatch; to ponder).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07548995-n
Synset POS: n
