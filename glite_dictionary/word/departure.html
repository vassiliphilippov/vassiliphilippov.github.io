departure

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈpɑrtʃɚ / (AmE), / dɪˈpɑːtʃə / (BrE)
the act of leaving, especially to start a journey
the act of leaving

Examples

Our train's departure is scheduled for 8:30 a.m.
She was sad about his sudden departure from the company.
Please arrive at the airport two hours before your departure.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: arrival - the act of arriving
  • Synonym: exit - an act of going out or leaving

Etymology

From Old French 'departir' (to divide), from Latin 'departire'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00043495-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈpɑrtʃər / (AmE), / dɪˈpɑːtʃə / (BrE)
A polite or formal expression meaning death; the act of leaving life.
euphemism for death

Examples

The family struggled to come to terms with his sudden departure.
Many people attended the memorial to honor the departure of their beloved friend.
She spoke tenderly about her grandmother’s peaceful departure from this world.

Synset Examples

thousands mourned his passing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: passing - Euphemistic term for death
  • Synonym: demise - Death, especially in a formal context

Etymology

From Middle English 'departen', from Old French 'departir' (to divide, depart), from Latin 'departire'.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈpɑrtʃɚ / (AmE), / dɪˈpɑːtʃə / (BrE)
a change or difference from what is usual, expected, or standard
deviation from the norm or standard

Examples

Her latest novel is a significant departure from her earlier work.
The new policy represents a major departure from traditional practices.
This modern design is quite a departure from the classic style we usually see.

Synset Examples

the deviation from the mean

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deviation - the action of departing from an established course or accepted standard

Etymology

From Old French 'departir' (to divide), from Latin 'departire'.
