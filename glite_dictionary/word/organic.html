organic

1. ADJ

/ ɔrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡænɪk / (BrE)
(of food or farming) produced or involving production without the use of artificial chemicals, especially fertilizers, pesticides, or hormones.
grown without artificial chemicals

Examples

Many people prefer to buy organic vegetables for their families.
The farm was certified organic after it stopped using chemical pesticides.
She always looks for organic eggs at the grocery store.

Synset Examples

organic eggs
organic vegetables
organic chicken

Related Terms

  • Antonym: conventional - produced with the use of synthetic chemicals

Etymology

From Greek organikos (serving as an organ or instrument), but this sense ('chemical-free food') developed in the 20th century as part of the organic farming movement.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02569537-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ɔːrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡænɪk / (BrE)
relating to or denoting chemical compounds containing carbon, especially as found in living things; pertaining to compounds that are carbon-based.
relating to carbon-based chemical compounds

Examples

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds commonly found in fuel and natural gas.
Chemists study the properties of organic molecules to understand living systems.
Plastic materials can be derived from organic sources such as plants.

Synset Examples

hydrocarbons are organic compounds

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inorganic - not containing carbon or not derived from living matter (opposite of organic in chemistry)

Etymology

From Latin 'organicus', from Ancient Greek 'organikos' (relating to an organ or instrument).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01683714-a
Synset POS: a

3. ADJ

/ ɔrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡænɪk / (BrE)
(of a disease or disorder) relating to or caused by physical changes in an organ or tissue of the body, rather than psychological causes.
of a disease, due to physical bodily causes
medical

Examples

The doctor diagnosed her with an organic disorder, indicating a physical problem with the brain.
Distinguishing between organic and functional illnesses is important for proper medical treatment.
Some forms of dementia are considered organic diseases because they result from physical changes in brain tissue.

Synset Examples

an organic disease

Related Terms

  • Antonym: functional - of a disease, with no known physical cause; due to psychological or unknown factors

Etymology

From Greek organikos, from organon meaning ‘instrument, organ’.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01096835-a
Synset POS: a

4. ADJ

/ ɔrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡænɪk / (BrE)
(of products, lifestyle, or practices) natural, simple, and healthful, often involving minimal processing or artificial additives and close to nature.
natural and healthful; close to nature

Examples

She prefers an organic lifestyle, choosing homemade meals and natural cleaning products.
Many shoppers select organic foods in search of a healthier and more natural diet.
The retreat offered an organic approach to living that emphasized mindfulness and simplicity.

Synset Examples

an organic lifestyle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: natural - existing in or derived from nature; not artificial
  • Antonym: processed - subjected to a special process, especially one involving artificial substances or methods
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01170419-s
Synset POS: s

5. ADJ

/ ɔrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡænɪk / (BrE)
forming an essential part of the structure or constitution of something; inherent or fundamental to its makeup
inherent; fundamental to structure

Examples

The desire for freedom seemed organic to the nation's character.
There are certain organic qualities to his leadership that cannot be taught.
Trust is organic to effective teamwork—it develops naturally from shared experience.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inherent - existing as a natural or basic part of something
  • Synonym: fundamental - forming a necessary base or core; of central importance
  • Antonym: extrinsic - not part of the essential nature; coming from outside

Etymology

From Greek organikos (relating to an organ or instrument), via Latin organicus.

Source: WordNet

relating to living organisms

Related Terms

7. NOUN

/ ɔrˈɡænɪk / (AmE), / ɔːˈɡanɪk / (BrE)
A fertilizer produced from animal or vegetable matter, rather than from synthetic chemicals.
fertilizer made from biological matter

Examples

Many gardeners prefer to use organic to enrich their soil naturally.
The farm applies only organic during the growing season.
You can buy bags of organic at most garden centers.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: inorganic - fertilizer produced from synthetic chemicals

Etymology

From Latin organicus (serving as an organ or instrument), via French organique.
