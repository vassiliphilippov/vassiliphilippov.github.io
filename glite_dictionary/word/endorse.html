to endorse

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈdɔrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɔːs / (BrE)
to give support or formal approval to something or someone
give support or approval

Examples

Many public figures endorse environmental protection efforts to encourage action.
The committee unanimously endorsed the new proposal for expansion.
She refused to endorse any candidate in the election.

Synset Examples

I'll second that motion
I can't back this plan
endorse a new project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance or encouragement to
  • Antonym: to oppose - to act against or refuse to support

Etymology

From Latin 'indorsare' (to write on the back), from 'in-' (on) + 'dorsum' (back).

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈdɔrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɔːs / (BrE)
to officially approve or guarantee something as meeting a specified standard
to officially approve or guarantee

Examples

The organization endorsed the company's eco-friendly products as meeting strict quality standards.
This label endorses the appliance as energy efficient according to federal guidelines.
The tour operator is endorsed by the National Tourism Board for its high safety standards.

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈdɔrs / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɔːs / (BrE)
to sign (a cheque, bill, or other document) to make it payable to someone other than the stated payee or to evidence legal transfer
sign a cheque or bill to transfer it
legal

Examples

You must endorse the cheque before depositing it in your account.
The lawyer explained how to properly endorse the negotiable instrument.
He forgot to endorse the back of the check, so the bank would not accept it.

Synset Examples

endorse cheques

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sign - to write one's signature on a document

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *indorsare*, from Latin *in* (on) + *dorsum* (back), referring to signing on the back of a document.
