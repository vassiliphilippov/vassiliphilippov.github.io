scramble

1. VERB

/ ˈskræmbəl / (AmE), / ˈskræmbl / (BrE)
to mix or stir something, especially eggs, vigorously so that the parts are combined
mix or stir vigorously (esp. eggs)

Examples

He likes to scramble eggs every morning for breakfast.
You should scramble the batter thoroughly before pouring it into the pan.
First, scramble the eggs until they are light and fluffy.

Synset Examples

beat the egg whites
beat the cream

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beat - to stir vigorously, especially in cooking

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English 'scrambelen', frequentative of 'scram', meaning to struggle.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01420833-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈskræm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈskræm.bəl / (BrE)
to make (a message or signal) unintelligible, especially to prevent it from being understood without special equipment or knowledge
make a message or signal unintelligible

Examples

The radio operator had to scramble the transmission to secure its contents.
They used a device to scramble the television signal.
The software can scramble your emails so only the recipient can read them.

Synset Examples

scramble the message so that nobody can understand it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encrypt - to convert information or data into a code to prevent unauthorized access
  • Antonym: to decode - to convert (a coded message) into intelligible language
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00538396-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈskræm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈskræm.bəl / (BrE)
to climb or move quickly, often with difficulty or in an awkward way, especially using one's hands as well as feet
move/climb awkwardly using hands and feet

Examples

The children tried to scramble up the steep hillside before sunset.
She had to scramble over the rocks to reach the other side of the stream.
We watched the goats scrambling along the narrow mountain path.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clamber - to climb with effort or difficulty, using hands and feet

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'scrambelen', variant of 'scramble', related to 'scrabble' (to scratch or scrape).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01925765-v
Synset POS: v

4. NOUN

/ ˈskræmbəl / (AmE), / ˈskræmbəl / (BrE)
A hurried and disorderly struggle for something, often involving multiple people competing at once.
a disorderly struggle

Examples

There was a frantic scramble for tickets when they went on sale.
After the announcement, a scramble for seats erupted in the auditorium.
The sudden storm caused a scramble for shelter among the hikers.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - a sudden movement towards something, often in competition
  • Synonym: fight - a violent struggle between people

Etymology

From the verb 'scramble', likely from Middle English *scram* (to clutch at) and related to Old Norse *skramla* (to rattle).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00791052-n
Synset POS: n

5. VERB

/ ˈskræm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈskræm.bəl / (BrE)
to mix or arrange things in a disordered or random fashion
mix or arrange in a disorderly way

Examples

She likes to scramble the letters before starting the word puzzle.
The teacher asked the students to scramble their desks for the group activity.
He accidentally scrambled the papers, so they were out of order.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shuffle - to mix up or arrange in a random order, especially cards

Etymology

From Middle English scramblen, probably related to 'scramble' (to move hurriedly) and 'scram' (to go away quickly).

to move quickly or clumsily, often with difficulty or in a disorderly way, especially using hands as well as feet
move quickly or clumsily

Examples

7. NOUN

/ ˈskræmbəl / (AmE), / ˈskræmbəl / (BrE)
a hurried or disorganized attempt to achieve or get something, often by moving quickly or competing with others
hurried, disorganized rush

Examples

There was a scramble for the best seats as soon as the doors opened.
The team's victory led to a scramble for tickets to the next game.
In the scramble to finish before the deadline, several errors were overlooked.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - a sudden quick movement towards something
  • Synonym: scrabble - a hurried, disorganized attempt or search
  • Antonym: order - a situation where things are arranged neatly or methodically (opposite of a scramble)

Etymology

From Middle English *skramblen*, frequentative form of *scramble* (to struggle).
