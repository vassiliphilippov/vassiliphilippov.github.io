cross-fertilisation

1. NOUN

/ krɔs ˌfɜrt̬ əl əˈzeɪ ʃən / (AmE), / krɒs ˌfɜː tɪ laɪˈzeɪ ʃən / (BrE)
The exchange of ideas, influences, or styles between different fields, groups, or cultures, resulting in productive or beneficial outcomes.
the exchange of ideas benefiting all parties
british

Examples

The cross-fertilisation of technology and medicine has led to innovative new treatments.
Cultural cross-fertilisation can enrich societies and broaden perspectives.
Many breakthroughs in art come from the cross-fertilisation of different disciplines.

Synset Examples

the cross-fertilization of science and the creative arts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cross-pollination - the transfer of ideas between fields or groups, resulting in beneficial outcomes

Etymology

From 'cross-fertilize', originally describing biological reproduction, extended metaphorically to the exchange of ideas.

2. NOUN

/ krɔs fɚtɪləˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / krɒs fəːtɪlʌɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The fertilization of an organism by the union of male and female gametes from different individuals of the same species.
fertilization between different individuals

Examples

Many plants rely on cross-fertilisation to increase genetic diversity and resilience to disease.
Biologists observed that cross-fertilisation led to healthier populations in controlled experiments.
The absence of pollinators can greatly reduce the chance of cross-fertilisation in flowering plants.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-fertilisation - fertilization by union of gametes from the same individual

Etymology

From 'cross' (indicating between individuals) and 'fertilisation' (from Latin 'fertilis', fruitful).
