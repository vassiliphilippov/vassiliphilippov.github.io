coax

1. NOUN

/ koʊ.æks / (AmE), / kəʊ.æks / (BrE)
A type of electrical cable with a central conductor, used to transmit high-frequency signals, especially in telecommunications and broadcasting.
type of cable for high-frequency signals

Examples

The technician replaced the old coax connecting the television to the antenna.
High-speed internet is often delivered to homes using coax cables.
Before installing the new router, check if the coax is properly connected to the wall outlet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coaxial cable - an electrical cable with an inner conductor and a tubular insulating layer, used to transmit signals

Etymology

Abbreviation of 'coaxial cable,' from 'coaxial' (sharing a common axis).

2. VERB

/ koʊks / (AmE), / kəʊks / (BrE)
to persuade someone to do something gently and patiently, often by being kind or offering encouragement
gently persuade or influence

Examples

She tried to coax the kitten out from under the sofa with a treat.
He finally coaxed his friend into joining the dance.
The teacher patiently coaxed an answer from the shy student.

Synset Examples

He palavered her into going along

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to persuade - to convince someone to do something
  • Synonym: to cajole - to persuade with repeated appeals, teasing, or flattery
  • Antonym: force - compel someone to do something against their will

Etymology

Possibly from obsolete 'cocks', a fool, of unknown origin.
