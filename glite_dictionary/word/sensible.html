sensible

1. ADJ

/ ˈsɛnsəbəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛnsɪbəl / (BrE)
able to feel or perceive sensations; having the capacity for physical sensation
able to feel or perceive sensations

Examples

The more sensible areas of the skin are located at the fingertips.
Some simple organisms are sensible to light and touch.
Her hand was no longer sensible after being numbed by the cold.

Synset Examples

even amoeba are sensible creatures
the more sensible parts of the skin

Etymology

From Latin sensibilis (perceptible by the senses), from sensus (sense).

2. ADJ

/ ˈsɛnsəbəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛnsɪbəl / (BrE)
Perceptible or readily noticed by the senses; able to be detected or observed through physical sensation.
perceptible by the senses

Examples

Through the thick fog, only a sensible glow from the streetlights could be made out.
A sensible change in temperature signaled that the weather was about to shift.
The perfume had a barely sensible scent, almost impossible to distinguish.

Synset Examples

the sensible universe
a sensible odor

Related Terms

  • Antonym: imperceptible - not able to be felt or perceived by the senses

Etymology

From Latin 'sensibilis' (that can be perceived by the senses), from 'sens-', 'sentire' (to feel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01752636-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈsɛnsəbəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛnsɪbəl / (BrE)
aware of or conscious of something, especially through intuition or intellect
aware or conscious of something

Examples

She became sensible of someone watching her from across the street.
He was sensible that his remarks had made an impact on the team.
I am sensible of the difficulties faced by the organization.

Synset Examples

made sensible of his mistakes
I am sensible that the mention of such a circumstance may appear trifling
sensible that a good deal more is still to be done

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conscious - aware of and responding to one's surroundings
  • Antonym: unaware - not aware or conscious of something

Etymology

From Latin sensus (feeling, sense) via Old French sensible (perceptible by the senses).

4. ADJ

/ ˈsɛnsɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈsɛnsəbəl / (BrE)
Having or showing good sense or judgment; practical and reasonable rather than foolish.
showing good judgment or reason

Examples

She made a sensible decision to save her money rather than spend it all at once.
It's always sensible to bring an umbrella when there is a chance of rain.
He offered some sensible advice that helped the team solve its problems.

Synset Examples

a sensible choice
a sensible person

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rational - based on or in accordance with reason or logic
  • Synonym: reasonable - having sound judgment; fair and sensible
  • Antonym: foolish - lacking good sense or judgment

Etymology

From Old French 'sensible' and Latin 'sensibilis' (perceptible, sensitive), from 'sentire' (to feel).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01950023-a
Synset POS: a
