restoration

1. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of returning something to its original owner, place, or condition
returning something to its former state or owner

Examples

The museum celebrated the restoration of the stolen painting to its collection.
After years of legal battles, the restoration of property to its original owner was finally completed.
Environmental groups applauded the restoration of the wetlands to their natural state.

Synset Examples

upon the restitution of the book to its rightful owner the child was given a tongue lashing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restitution - the act of restoring something to its rightful owner

Etymology

From Latin restitutio (restoring), via Old French restauration.

2. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the process or state of being returned to a former, original, or unimpaired condition
return to former condition

Examples

The museum's restoration of the damaged sculpture took several months to complete.
After the storm, restoration of power to the entire neighborhood was a top priority.
Thanks to an extensive restoration, the old theater looks almost exactly as it did fifty years ago.

Synset Examples

the inn was a renovation of a Colonial house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: renovation - the action of renovating a building or object
  • Antonym: decay - the process of rotting or deterioration

Etymology

From Latin restauratio (a restoring), from restaurare (to restore).

3. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of returning something or someone to a satisfactory or original state
the act of returning to a satisfactory state

Examples

The restoration of the ancient painting took several months to complete.
After years of neglect, the castle is finally undergoing restoration.
The team worked tirelessly on the restoration of power after the storm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repair - the process of fixing or mending something that is damaged or broken
  • Antonym: deterioration - the process of becoming worse or less satisfactory

Etymology

From Latin 'restauratio' (a renewing, repairing), from 'restaurare' (to renew).

5. NOUN

/ ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌrɛstəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)

Examples

The museum features a detailed restoration of a prehistoric forest.
Researchers created a restoration of the extinct saber-toothed tiger for the exhibit.
A new restoration shows how the region looked during the Jurassic period.

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin 'restauratio' (a renewal, restoration), via Old French and Middle English.
