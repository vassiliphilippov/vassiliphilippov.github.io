to dilate

1. VERB

/ ˈdaɪ.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈdaɪ.leɪt / (BrE)
to become wider, larger, or more open
become wider or larger

Examples

The doctor explained that your pupils dilate in the dark to let in more light.
When heated, metal pipes can dilate slightly, affecting the fit of connections.
Her eyes began to dilate with excitement as she heard the news.

Synset Examples

His pupils were dilated

Related Terms

  • Antonym: contract - to become smaller or narrower

Etymology

From Latin 'dilatare' (to enlarge, spread out).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00306318-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ daɪˈleɪt / (AmE), / daɪˈleɪt / (BrE)
to speak or write at length or in detail about a subject; to elaborate or expand upon a topic
elaborate or expand on a subject

Examples

During the lecture, the professor began to dilate on the importance of critical thinking.
The author likes to dilate upon historical events in great detail.
She was asked to dilate further on her initial comments during the interview.

Synset Examples

She elaborated on the main ideas in her dissertation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elaborate - to add details or expand on a subject
  • Synonym: to expand - to give more details, to make something more comprehensive
  • Antonym: to summarize - to state briefly or in summary form

Etymology

From Latin 'dilatare' (to enlarge, extend).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00957633-v
Synset POS: v
