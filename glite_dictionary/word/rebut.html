to rebut

1. VERB

/ rɪˈbʌt / (AmE), / rɪˈbʌt / (BrE)
to prove that a statement or accusation is false; to refute
prove something is false; refute

Examples

The lawyer attempted to rebut the witness’s testimony with new evidence.
She quickly rebutted their claims during the interview.
In his article, he skillfully rebuts the popular misconceptions about the topic.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refute - to prove a statement, theory, or person to be wrong or false
  • Synonym: to contradict - to assert the opposite of a statement made by someone

Etymology

From Old French 'reboter', from Latin 're-' (back) + 'battuere' (to beat).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈbʌt / (AmE), / rɪˈbʌt / (BrE)
to argue against or prove that a statement, accusation, or argument is false; to refute by evidence or reasoning
to argue against; refute

Examples

During the debate, she rebutted his claims with solid evidence and clear reasoning.
The lawyer skillfully rebutted the prosecution's accusations during the trial.
Whenever challenged, he is quick to rebut any criticism with facts.

Synset Examples

The speaker refuted his opponent's arguments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to refute - to prove a statement or theory to be wrong or false
  • Antonym: to confirm - to establish the truth or correctness of something

Etymology

From Old French *rebouter* (to repel, push back), from re- (again) + bouter (to push).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00816512-v
Synset POS: v
