unison

1. NOUN

/ ˈjuː nɪ sən / (AmE), / ˈjuː nɪs ən / (BrE)
the state of doing or saying something at the same time as someone else
simultaneous occurrence

Examples

The choir sang the song in unison, creating a powerful effect.
The crowd cheered in unison as the team scored a goal.
Both children answered the question in unison, surprising their teacher.

Synset Examples

the two spoke in unison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harmony - the combination of simultaneously sounded musical notes

Etymology

From Latin 'unisonus' (having the same sound), from 'uni-' (one) + 'sonus' (sound).

2. NOUN

/ ˈjuːnəsən / (AmE), / ˈjuːnɪsən / (BrE)
the state of two or more people or things doing something at exactly the same time or in perfect agreement
state of acting together at the same time

Examples

The children recited the poem in unison during the assembly.
The audience clapped in unison after the performance ended.
The soldiers marched in perfect unison across the parade ground.

Synset Examples

marching in unison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harmony - agreement or concord
  • Antonym: discord - lack of agreement or harmony

Etymology

From Italian 'unisono', from Latin 'uni-' (one) + 'sonus' (sound).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13995952-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈjunəsən / (AmE), / ˈjuːnɪsən / (BrE)
(music) the simultaneous sounding of two or more notes at the same pitch or in octaves.
musical notes sounding at the same pitch

Examples

The choir sang in unison, creating a powerful and harmonious sound.
The violinists played the melody in unison during the performance.
When the band joined in unison, the audience was thrilled by the effect.

Synset Examples

singing in unison

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: octave - (music) the interval between one musical pitch and another with half or double its frequency

Etymology

From Latin "unisonus" (having the same sound), from "uni-" (one) + "sonus" (sound).
