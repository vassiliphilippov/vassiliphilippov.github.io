correlate

1. VERB

/ ˈkɔːrəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒrɪləˌteɪt / (BrE)
to establish or show a mutual or reciprocal relationship between two or more things
show a relationship between things

Examples

The researchers attempted to correlate environmental factors with disease rates.
It's difficult to correlate these findings with previous studies.
He tried to correlate his past experiences with his current behavior.

Synset Examples

I cannot correlate these two pieces of information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to relate - to show or make a connection between things

Etymology

From Medieval Latin correlat-, from Latin co- (together) + relata (brought back, related).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00715901-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈkɔːrəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒrɪˌleɪt / (BrE)
to have a mutual relationship or connection, in which one thing affects or depends on another
have a mutual relationship or connection

Examples

The increase in temperature does not necessarily correlate with higher levels of pollution.
Researchers found that the two variables correlate strongly in most cases.
You should check whether the symptoms correlate with those described in previous studies.

Synset Examples

Do these facts correlate?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: associate - to connect or bring into relation, as in thought
  • Antonym: disconnect - to separate or break the relationship between things

Etymology

From Medieval Latin correlatus, past participle of correlare (to relate together), from Latin com- (together) + relatus (related).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02665057-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɔːrəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒrɪleɪt / (BrE)
either of two or more things that are closely related or complementary, especially as variables whose values change together
thing closely related to another; complementary variable

Examples

Temperature and humidity are often considered correlates in climate research.
In their experiment, pain intensity and stress level acted as significant correlates.
Geneticists identified several correlates that may influence the development of the disease.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: variable - an element, feature, or factor that is liable to vary or change
  • Degree Lower: independent variable - a variable whose variation does not depend on another
  • Degree Higher: dependent variable - a variable whose value depends on another

Etymology

From Medieval Latin correlatum (something related with another), from Latin co- (together) + relatus (carried back, related).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-05866991-n
Synset POS: n

4. ADJ

/ ˈkɔːr.ə.leɪt / (AmE), / ˈkɒr.ə.leɪt / (BrE)
Having a mutual or reciprocal relationship; related so that when one changes, the other changes as well.
mutually related, interconnected

Examples

The two symptoms are correlate, often occurring together in patients.
Their financial success appears to be correlate with market growth.
Social unrest and unemployment are frequently correlate factors in studies of economic crisis.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unrelated - not connected or related to each other

Etymology

From Medieval Latin correlātus, past participle of Latin corrēlātus ('related together').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01981362-s
Synset POS: s
