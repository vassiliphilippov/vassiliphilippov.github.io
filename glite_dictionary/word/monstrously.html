monstrously

1. ADV

/ ˈmɑnstrəsli / (AmE), / ˈmɒnstrəsli / (BrE)
In a way that is shockingly or terribly evil or wicked.
in a terribly evil or wicked manner

Examples

The dictator ruled his country monstrously, showing little regard for his people's lives.
The villain in the story behaved monstrously, committing unspeakable crimes.
She was shocked that anyone could act so monstrously without remorse.

Synset Examples

the child was heinously murdered

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heinously - in a shockingly evil or wicked manner
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00357543-r
Synset POS: r

2. ADV

/ ˈmɑːnstrəsli / (AmE), / ˈmɒnstrəsli / (BrE)
In a manner that is shockingly unnatural, grotesque, or extremely bad or large.
in a grotesque or unnatural manner

Examples

The villain laughed monstrously as chaos erupted around him.
The cake she baked was monstrously large—enough to feed fifty people.
His actions were monstrously cruel, leaving everyone in shock.

Synset Examples

behind the house lay two nude figures grotesquely bald, with deliberate knife-slashes marking their bodies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grotesquely - in a grotesque or distorted manner
  • Synonym: horribly - in a way that is horrible or shocking

Etymology

From 'monstrous' + '-ly', ultimately from Latin 'monstrum' (monster, omen).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00353338-r
Synset POS: r

3. ADV

/ ˈmɑːnstrəsli / (AmE), / ˈmɒnstrəsli / (BrE)
In a hideous, extremely ugly, or shockingly unnatural manner.
in a hideous or unnatural way

Examples

The villain was monstrously cruel to his captives throughout the story.
Her face was monstrously distorted after the accident, shocking everyone who saw her.
The machine malfunctioned and made a monstrously loud noise in the quiet laboratory.

Synset Examples

her face was hideously disfigured after the accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hideously - in a repulsive, ugly, or shocking manner
  • Antonym: beautifully - in a beautiful or attractive manner

Etymology

From 'monstrous' + '-ly'; 'monstrous' from Latin 'monstruosus' (unnatural, strange).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00358561-r
Synset POS: r
