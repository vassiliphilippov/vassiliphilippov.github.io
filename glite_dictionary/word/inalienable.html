inalienable

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈeɪliənəbəl / (AmE), / ɪnˈeɪliənəbl / (BrE)
(of a right or property) unable to be taken away from or given away by the possessor.
cannot be taken away

Examples

The Constitution protects people's inalienable rights to freedom and happiness.
She believes that the dignity of each individual is inalienable.
Certain moral rights are considered inalienable regardless of laws or governments.

Synset Examples

an unforfeitable right

Related Terms

  • Antonym: alienable - able to be transferred or given away

Etymology

From Latin inalienabilis, from in- (not) + alienare (to transfer) + -abilis (-able).

2. ADJ

/ ɪˈneɪ.li.ə.nə.bəl / (AmE), / ɪˈneɪ.lɪ.ə.nə.bəl / (BrE)
(of a right or privilege) unable to be taken away or transferred to another; non-transferable.
cannot be taken away or transferred

Examples

The Constitution protects your inalienable rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.
Freedom of speech is considered an inalienable right in many democracies.
Some philosophers argue that human dignity is an inalienable characteristic, regardless of circumstances.

Synset Examples

endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights

Related Terms

  • Antonym: alienable - able to be transferred to another's ownership

Etymology

From French *inaliénable*, from Latin prefix *in-* (not) + *alienare* (to transfer, make over, from *alius* 'other').

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00094663-a
Synset POS: a
