intoxicant

1. ADJ

/ ɪnˈtɑksɪkənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɒksɪkənt / (BrE)
(of a substance or drink) causing intoxication; making someone drunk or exhilarated.
causing intoxication

Examples

The pub served several intoxicant liquors to its patrons.
Many countries strictly regulate the sale of intoxicant beverages.
You must be above a certain age to purchase any intoxicant drink.

Synset Examples

an intoxicating drink

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intoxicating - causing someone to lose control due to alcohol or drugs

Etymology

From Latin 'intoxicare', meaning 'to poison'.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtɑksɪkənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɒksɪkənt / (BrE)
a substance, especially an alcoholic drink, that produces intoxication by affecting the mind or body
substance causing intoxication (esp. alcohol)

Examples

The sale of intoxicants is strictly regulated in many countries.
He claimed that he had never consumed any intoxicant in his life.
The law prohibits driving under the influence of intoxicants.

Synset Examples

alcohol (or drink) ruined him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alcohol - a colorless volatile flammable liquid which is the intoxicating constituent of wine, beer, spirits, and other drinks
  • Antonym: stimulant - a substance that raises levels of physiological or nervous activity

Etymology

From Latin 'intoxicare' (to poison).

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈtɑksɪkənt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɒksɪkənt / (BrE)
A substance, especially an alcoholic drink or drug, that produces a state of intoxication when consumed.
substance causing intoxication

Examples

Alcohol is the most commonly used intoxicant in many societies.
The sale of any intoxicant to minors is strictly prohibited by law.
Certain herbs were once used as a mild intoxicant in traditional rituals.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stimulant - a substance that raises levels of physiological or nervous activity
  • Synonym: alcohol - a common intoxicating beverage

Etymology

From Latin intoxicare (to poison), influenced by toxicum (poison).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03586803-n
Synset POS: n
