mustard

1. NOUN

/ ˈmʌstərd / (AmE), / ˈmʌstəd / (BrE)
the leaves of the mustard plant, used as cooked greens in various cuisines
leaves of mustard plant used as greens

Examples

She sautéed a bunch of fresh mustard with garlic and onions for dinner.
Southern cuisine often features mustard as a flavorful cooked green.
The farmers' market had bundles of organic mustard next to the kale and spinach.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: collard greens - leafy greens used as cooked vegetables

Etymology

From Old French 'mostarde', from Latin 'mustum' (must, new wine), originally referring to the condiment.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌs.tɚd / (AmE), / ˈmʌs.təd / (BrE)
a pungent yellow or brown powder or paste made from the seeds of a mustard plant, used as a spice or condiment
pungent spice or condiment from mustard seeds

Examples

Could you please pass the mustard for my sandwich?
She added a spoonful of mustard to the salad dressing for extra flavor.
This recipe calls for both dry mustard and mustard seeds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ketchup - a thick, sweet-and-sour sauce made from tomatoes, used as a condiment

Etymology

From Old French *mostarde*, from Latin *mustum* (new wine), originally mixed with crushed mustard seeds.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˈmʌstərd / (AmE), / ˈmʌstəd / (BrE)
any of several cruciferous plants of the genus Brassica, cultivated for their pungent seeds and sometimes for their leaves
a plant of the genus Brassica

Examples

The farmer planted several rows of mustard in the spring.
Some species of mustard are grown for their edible leaves as well as their seeds.
Wild mustard can quickly take over a neglected field.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Brassica - a genus of plants in the cabbage and mustard family
  • Synonym: cabbage - a cruciferous plant cultivated for its edible leaves

Etymology

From Old French *mostarde*, from Latin *mustum* (new wine) because the condiment was originally prepared with must.
