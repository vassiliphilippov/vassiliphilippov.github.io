cacodyl

1. NOUN

/ ˈkækəˌdɪl / (AmE), / ˈkakədɪl / (BrE)
A univalent radical (–As(CH₃)₂) derived from arsine, used in chemistry.
univalent group from arsine (chemistry)

Examples

The toxic properties of cacodyl make it important to handle with care in the laboratory.
Researchers studied a series of reactions involving cacodyl and its derivatives.
The compound cacodyl was historically known for its extremely unpleasant odor.

Etymology

From French cacodyle, from Greek kakōdēs (evil-smelling).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkækəˌdɪl / (AmE), / ˈkakədɪl / (BrE)
a poisonous, oily, and volatile chemical compound with a strong garlic-like odor, composed of two cacodyl groups, which ignites spontaneously in dry air
poisonous oily compound with garlic odor

Examples

Cacodyl is notable for its extremely unpleasant garlic-like odor and high toxicity.
The laboratory experiment demonstrated that cacodyl ignites spontaneously when exposed to dry air.
Researchers handled cacodyl with extreme caution due to its poisonous and volatile nature.

Etymology

From French 'cacodyle', from Greek 'kakos' (bad) and 'odymē' (smell).
