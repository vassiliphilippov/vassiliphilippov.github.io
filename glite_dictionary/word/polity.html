polity

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑː.lə.ti / (AmE), / ˈpɒl.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
a particular form or system of government or political organization in a country, state, or society
form or system of government

Examples

The ancient Greek city-state was a unique polity with its own customs and laws.
Each polity within the federation retained some degree of autonomy.
Today, the nation is considered a democratic polity with regular elections.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: government - the governing body of a nation, state, or community

Etymology

From Greek polītīa (citizenship, government), from polītēs (citizen), from polis (city).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːləti / (AmE), / ˈpɒləti / (BrE)
an organized society or state, especially one with a specific form of government or administrative structure
an organized political unit or state

Examples

The ancient Greek polity laid the groundwork for modern democracy.
Every polity develops its own laws and customs over time.
The report analyzes the structure of each European polity in detail.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: state - an organized political community under one government
  • Synonym: nation - a large group of people united by common descent, history, culture, or language, inhabiting a particular country

Etymology

From Greek politeia (citizenship, government), from polites (citizen).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːləti / (AmE), / ˈpɒlɪti / (BrE)
shrewd or skillful management of public affairs, especially in a political sense; craftiness or artfulness in statecraft.
shrewd management of public affairs
archaic

Examples

The senator was admired for his polity in handling the delicate negotiation.
Ancient rulers often relied on polity to maintain their authority over diverse populations.
Without a hint of polity, the committee failed to navigate the political crisis successfully.

Synset Examples

we were innocent of stratagems and polity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: statecraft - the skillful management of state affairs
  • Synonym: policy - a course or principle of action adopted by a government
  • Antonym: naiveté - innocence or lack of experience, often contrasted with craftiness

Etymology

From Greek 'politeia' (citizenship, government), from 'polites' (citizen), from 'polis' (city).
