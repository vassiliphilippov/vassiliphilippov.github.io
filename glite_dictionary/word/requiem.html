requiem

1. NOUN

/ ˈriː.kwi.əm / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.wi.əm / (BrE)
a musical composition or Mass for the repose of the souls of the dead
music for a Mass for the dead

Examples

Mozart's famous requiem was unfinished at the time of his death.
A choir sang a moving requiem during the memorial service.
The composer dedicated his latest requiem to the victims of war.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Mass - a religious ceremony in Christian churches commemorating the Last Supper

Etymology

From Latin 'requiem' (rest), the first word of the Mass for the dead: 'Requiem aeternam dona eis, Domine.'

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɛkwiəm / (AmE), / ˈrɛkwɪəm / (BrE)
a Mass celebrated in the Roman Catholic Church for the repose of the souls of the dead
Mass for the dead

Examples

The choir sang a beautiful requiem in memory of the deceased.
Mozart's final composition was the famous Requiem Mass in D minor.
Families often request a requiem for their loved ones who have passed away.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Mass - a religious ceremony in the Roman Catholic Church

Etymology

From Latin 'requiem', the accusative of 'requies' meaning 'rest', the first word of the Mass for the dead ('Requiem aeternam dona eis, Domine').

3. NOUN

/ ˈriː.kwi.əm / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.wi.əm / (BrE)
A musical composition or hymn commemorating the dead, especially one sung at a funeral or memorial service.
song for the dead; funeral hymn

Examples

The choir performed a moving requiem at the memorial service.
Mozart's requiem is considered one of his greatest and most haunting works.
After the tragedy, the community gathered to hear a solemn requiem in remembrance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dirge - a mournful song, especially for a funeral
  • Synonym: elegy - a poem or song of lamentation for the dead
  • Antonym: anthem - a rousing or uplifting song, typically not specifically for the dead

Etymology

From Latin 'requiem', the accusative of 'requiēs' meaning 'rest'.
