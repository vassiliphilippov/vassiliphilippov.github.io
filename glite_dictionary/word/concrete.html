concrete

1. ADJ

/ ˈkɑn.krit / (AmE), / ˈkɒŋ.kriːt / (BrE)
existing in a material or physical form; able to be perceived by the senses and not abstract or imaginary.
existing in material or physical form

Examples

The explanation lacked concrete examples and was difficult to understand.
She prefers dealing with concrete facts rather than abstract theories.
Photographs can provide concrete proof of your presence at the event.

Synset Examples

concrete objects such as trees

Related Terms

  • Antonym: abstract - existing in thought or as an idea but not having a physical or material existence

Etymology

From Latin 'concretus' (grown together, hard, condensed), past participle of 'concrescere' (to grow together).

/ ˈkɑn.krit / (AmE), / ˈkɒŋ.kriːt / (BrE)
to cover or pave with concrete, especially in building or construction.
to cover with concrete (construction)

Examples

Synset Examples

concrete the walls

Related Terms

to form into a solid mass

Examples

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɑn.krit / (AmE), / ˈkɒŋ.kriːt / (BrE)
a hard, strong building material made from a mixture of cement, sand, gravel, and water, used for construction
building material of cement, sand, gravel, and water

Examples

The workers poured concrete to form the foundation of the new building.
A cracked concrete driveway can be repaired with patching material.
Modern bridges are often made of reinforced concrete for extra strength.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cement - powdered substance used as a binder in concrete and mortar
  • Antonym: asphalt - mixture used for road surfaces, often confused with concrete

Etymology

From Latin 'concretus' (condensed, hardened), past participle of 'concrescere' (to grow together).
