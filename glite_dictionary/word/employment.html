employment

1. NOUN

/ ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (BrE)
the act of giving someone a job or the process of hiring someone
the act of hiring someone

Examples

The company’s recent employment of several new managers has changed the team dynamic.
Her duties included the employment of seasonal workers during the busy holiday period.
Regulations ensure the fair employment of qualified candidates from diverse backgrounds.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recruitment - the process of finding and hiring new employees
  • Antonym: dismissal - the act of removing someone from their job

Etymology

From Old French 'employement', from 'employer' (to employ).

2. NOUN

/ ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (BrE)
the act of using something for a particular purpose; utilization
the act of using; utilization

Examples

The employment of advanced technology has streamlined many production processes.
Strict guidelines govern the employment of chemicals in the laboratory.
Her employment of humor helped put the audience at ease.

Synset Examples

he warned against the use of narcotic drugs
skilled in the utilization of computers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: utilization - the act of using something effectively
  • Synonym: use - the action of using something
  • Antonym: misuse - the wrong or improper use of something

Etymology

From Old French 'emploier' and Latin 'implicare' (to entangle, employ).

3. NOUN

/ ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (AmE), / ɪmˈplɔɪmənt / (BrE)
The state of having paid work or the occupation for which someone receives payment.
paid work; occupation

Examples

She found employment as a teacher in a local school.
Many recent graduates struggle to secure employment in their chosen field.
Due to economic downturns, employment rates dropped significantly last year.

Synset Examples

he is looking for employment
a lot of people are out of work

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unemployment - the state of not having paid work
  • Synonym: job - a paid position of regular employment
  • Synonym: occupation - a person's regular work or profession

Etymology

From Old French *employer* (to make use of), from Latin *implicare* (to involve).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00585404-n
Synset POS: n
