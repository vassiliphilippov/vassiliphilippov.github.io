detachment

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈtætʃmənt / (AmE), / dɪˈtætʃmənt / (BrE)
the state of avoiding emotional involvement or personal interest; a lack of emotional attachment or bias
lack of emotional involvement

Examples

She maintained a sense of detachment during the heated discussion.
Journalists are often expected to write with detachment and objectivity.
His detachment from the situation allowed him to make fair decisions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indifference - lack of interest, concern, or sympathy
  • Antonym: engagement - emotional or personal involvement

Etymology

From French détachement, from détacher (to detach).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈtætʃmənt / (AmE), / dɪˈtatʃmənt / (BrE)
a small group of troops that is separated from a larger military unit for a particular purpose
small military unit separated from main force

Examples

A detachment of soldiers was sent ahead to scout the enemy position.
The general ordered a detachment to secure the bridge overnight.
A small detachment remained behind to guard the supplies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: platoon - a subdivision of a company of soldiers, usually containing two or more squads
  • Synonym: squad - a small military unit or group

Etymology

From French *détachement*, from *détacher* (to detach).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈtætʃmənt / (AmE), / dɪˈtætʃmənt / (BrE)
the act of separating something or someone from an attachment or connection
the act of separating; disconnection

Examples

The detachment of the trailer from the truck took only a few minutes.
After years of working there, her detachment from the company was bittersweet.
Proper detachment of the wires is necessary before repairs can be made.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separation - the action or state of moving or being moved apart
  • Antonym: attachment - the state of being attached or connected

Etymology

From French *détachement*, from détacher (to detach), itself from Old French des- + attacher (to attach).
