drachm

1. NOUN

/ dræm / (AmE), / dræm / (BrE)
a unit of capacity in the British imperial system, equal to 60 minims or approximately 3.55 milliliters, used for liquid or dry measure
British unit of capacity (3.55 ml)

Examples

The old apothecary carefully measured one drachm of the tincture for the prescription.
A recipe from the 19th century called for half a drachm of saffron.
In historical texts, measurements such as the drachm are commonly found.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dram - variant spelling of drachm, also a unit of mass or capacity

Etymology

From the Greek 'drachmē', adopted into Latin as 'dragma', influencing measurement terms in English.

2. NOUN

/ dræm / (AmE), / dræm / (BrE)
a unit of apothecary weight equal to an eighth of an ounce or to 60 grains
apothecary weight unit (1/8 ounce, 60 grains)
archaic

Examples

The apothecary prescribed two drachms of the powdered herb for each dose.
Old recipes sometimes call for a drachm of spice in their ingredient lists.
A gold coin weighing a drachm was once standard in ancient Greece.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dram - a unit of weight, also called drachm, commonly used in Apothecaries' systems

Etymology

From Latin 'drachma', from ancient Greek 'drakhmē' (a coin and weight).

3. NOUN

/ dræm / (AmE), / dræm / (BrE)
A unit of capacity in the apothecary system, equal to one eighth of a fluid ounce.
apothecary volume unit (1/8 fluid ounce)
medical

Examples

The pharmacist measured out three drachms of the liquid medicine.
Traditional recipes sometimes call for quantities listed in drachms.
A drachm was once a common unit in apothecary prescriptions.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: fluid ounce - a unit of liquid capacity equal to 1/16 of a pint

Etymology

From Greek *drachmē* (a weight, coin), via Latin and Old French.
