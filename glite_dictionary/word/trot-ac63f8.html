trot

1. NOUN

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
a word-for-word translation of a foreign text, especially one used secretly by students to help with studying
word-for-word translation for study

Examples

He kept a trot under his desk to help with the Latin translation.
Students often resorted to using a trot when they struggled with Greek texts.
The teacher confiscated the trot after noticing several students glancing at it during the exam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crib - a translation used to aid study, especially illicitly
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-06358595-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
to run at a moderately swift pace, especially referring to animals such as horses, or to move briskly at a pace between a walk and a run
run at a moderately swift pace

Examples

The horses began to trot as they left the starting line.
She trotted across the field to greet her friends.
As soon as he heard his name, the dog started trotting toward its owner.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: walk - to move at a regular, relatively slow pace by lifting and setting down each foot in turn
  • Degree Higher: gallop - to move or run fast, especially of a horse, at its fastest gait

Etymology

From Old French *trotter* (to run, trot), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01905440-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
a pace of running that is faster than a walk but slower than a canter, typically used to describe the gait of a horse or similar animal
a slow run, especially of a horse

Examples

The horse broke into a steady trot as it moved down the lane.
During their morning workout, the runners kept a gentle trot around the park.
With a click of her tongue, she urged the pony into a quick trot across the field.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: canter - a moderate, easy pace of a horse between a trot and a gallop
  • Degree Lower: walk - a slow pace of movement on foot

Etymology

From Middle English *troten*, from Old French *trotter* (to trot).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00295034-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
to cause (a horse or other animal) to move at a trot
make an animal move at a trot

Examples

The rider skillfully trotted the horse around the arena for the judges.
She trotted her pony over to the stable after the trail ride.
He trots his dog in the park every morning, teaching it to move with grace.

Synset Examples

She trotted the horse home

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: walk - to make an animal move at a walking pace
  • Degree Higher: gallop - to make a horse move at a gallop

5. NOUN

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
A gait faster than a walk, in which diagonally opposite legs move together, commonly seen in horses and some other animals.
a gait faster than a walk (animals)

Examples

The horse broke into a brisk trot as it approached the finish line.
Joggers moved at the same pace as the dog’s steady trot through the park.
At the command, the riders urged their mounts into a trot across the field.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: walk - a slower gait where each foot is lifted and set down separately
  • Degree Higher: canter - a gait faster than a trot but slower than a gallop

Etymology

From Old French troter (to trot), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00288405-n
Synset POS: n

6. VERB

/ trɑt / (AmE), / trɒt / (BrE)
to ride a horse at a trot; to move a horse forward at a gait faster than a walk but slower than a canter
ride a horse at a moderate gait

Examples

She loves to trot her horse around the ring every morning.
The instructor told him to trot before moving on to a canter.
They trotted slowly along the winding country path.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: canter - to ride at a gait faster than a trot
  • Degree Lower: walk - to ride a horse at a gait slower than a trot
