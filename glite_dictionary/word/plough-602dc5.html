plough

1. NOUN

/ plaʊ / (AmE), / plaʊ / (BrE)
a large farming tool with one or more heavy blades used for turning and breaking up soil in preparation for planting seeds
farm tool for turning soil
british

Examples

The farmer used a plough to prepare the field for the spring planting.
Before tractors, oxen often pulled the plough through the heavy soil.
The old wooden plough is now displayed in the village museum.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plow - alternate American spelling for 'plough'

Etymology

From Old English *plōh* (plough), related to Old Norse *plógr*.

2. VERB

/ plaʊ / (AmE), / plaʊ / (BrE)
to move forcefully or steadily through a substance or area as if cutting through it, like a plow through soil
move forcefully through something

Examples

The ferry ploughed through the thick fog, barely visible from the shore.
He ploughed his way through the crowd to reach the exit.
The runner ploughed on through the mud despite the heavy rain.

Synset Examples

The ship plowed through the water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plow - US spelling of 'plough', same verb sense

Etymology

From Old English *plōh* (plow, plough), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ plaʊ / (AmE), / plaʊ / (BrE)
to turn over and break up the soil with a plough, especially to prepare it for planting crops
turn over soil with a plough
british

Examples

The farmer used a tractor to plough the field after the harvest.
Every spring, they plough the soil before planting new crops.
She watched as the oxen slowly ploughed the old pasture land.

Synset Examples

Farmer Jones plowed his east field last week
turn the earth in the spring

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plow - American spelling of 'plough' (to turn earth)

Etymology

From Old English *plōh* (plough, plow).
