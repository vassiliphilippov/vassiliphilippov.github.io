monopoly

1. NOUN

/ məˈnɑːpəli / (AmE), / məˈnɒpəli / (BrE)
the exclusive control or possession of the supply of or trade in a service or commodity
exclusive control; sole possession

Examples

The telecommunications company once held a monopoly on all local phone services.
No one group should have a monopoly over public information.
The government introduced new laws to break up the monopoly in the energy sector.

Synset Examples

They have no monopoly on intelligence

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: oligopoly - market structure dominated by a small number of firms
  • Antonym: competition - the act of competing, especially in business, as the opposite of monopoly

Etymology

From Greek monōpolion, from monos (alone) + polein (to sell).

2. NOUN

/ məˈnɑːpəli / (AmE), / məˈnɒpəli / (BrE)
(Economics) A situation in which there is only one seller supplying a particular product or service in a market with many buyers, giving the seller significant control over price and competition.
exclusive control by one seller in a market

Examples

The company maintained a monopoly on electricity in the region for decades.
Anti-trust laws are designed to break up monopoly power and encourage competition.
Because the publisher held a monopoly on the textbook, prices remained very high.

Synset Examples

a monopoly on silver
when you have a monopoly you can ask any price you like

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: oligopoly - a market dominated by a small number of sellers
  • Antonym: competition - a market with many buyers and many sellers

Etymology

From Latin monopolium, from Greek monopōlion (exclusive sale), from monos (single) + polein (to sell).
