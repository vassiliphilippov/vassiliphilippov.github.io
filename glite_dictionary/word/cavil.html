cavil

1. NOUN

/ ˈkævəl / (AmE), / ˈkævəl / (BrE)
A petty or unnecessary objection or criticism, especially one that evades the main point of an argument.
petty or trivial objection

Examples

The lawyer's endless cavils did little to address the real issues of the case.
She dismissed his cavil as irrelevant to the main argument.
Rather than answering the question directly, he raised a minor cavil to stall the discussion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quibble - a slight objection or criticism; a trivial argument
  • Synonym: objection - an expression of opposition or protest; an argument against something

Etymology

From Latin 'cavillari' (to jeer, mock), via Old French 'caviller'.

2. VERB

/ ˈkævəl / (AmE), / ˈkæv.əl / (BrE)
to make petty or unnecessary objections, especially by finding fault with unimportant details
raise trivial objections

Examples

The editor continued to cavil at every tiny detail in the manuscript.
Rather than work toward a solution, he preferred to cavil about insignificant problems.
If you cavil over minor points, we'll never finish this project.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quibble - to argue or raise objections about a trivial matter
  • Degree Higher: object - to express or feel disapproval or opposition

Etymology

From Latin 'cavillari' (to jest, criticize), from 'cavilla' (mockery).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00810333-v
Synset POS: v
