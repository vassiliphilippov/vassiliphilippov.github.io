to invade

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈveɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪd / (BrE)
to enter (a country or region) by force with an armed force in order to conquer or occupy it
enter by force for conquest

Examples

The army plans to invade the neighboring country at dawn.
Allied forces began to invade France on D-Day in 1944.
The general warned that any attempt to invade sovereign territory would not be tolerated.

Synset Examples

Hitler invaded Poland on September 1, 1939

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to occupy - to take control of a place, especially by military force
  • Antonym: to defend - to protect from attack or invasion

Etymology

From Latin 'invadere' meaning 'to go into, attack' (in- ‘into’ + vadere ‘to go’).

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈveɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪd / (BrE)
(of animals, plants, or disease) to enter and spread within a place or organism in large numbers, often causing harm.
spread rapidly in large numbers; infest

Examples

The crabgrass began to invade the entire lawn by midsummer.
Unwanted bacteria can quickly invade the open wound if not properly cleaned.
After a few years, non-native insects started to invade the garden and damage the crops.

Synset Examples

the Kudzu plant infests much of the South and is spreading to the North

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to infest - to spread or be present in large numbers, typically causing damage

Etymology

From Latin 'invadere' (to go into, attack), from 'in-' (in) + 'vadere' (to go).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02660781-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈveɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪd / (BrE)
to enter or encroach upon a place, situation, or someone's rights or privacy, often without permission and in an intrusive or unwelcome way
enter or intrude without permission

Examples

I don't like it when someone invades my personal space on the train.
New technologies often invade our privacy in unexpected ways.
She felt her rights were being invaded by the constant surveillance.

Synset Examples

This new colleague invades my territory
The neighbors intrude on your privacy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intrude - to put oneself deliberately into a place or situation where one is unwelcome or uninvited
  • Synonym: to encroach - to gradually or stealthily take possession of something not one's own, especially territory or rights
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to leave a place or cease to be involved, especially as the opposite of invading or intruding

Etymology

From Latin invadere (to go into, attack), from in- (in, into) + vadere (to go).

Source: WordNet

4. VERB

/ ɪnˈveɪd / (AmE), / ɪnˈveɪd / (BrE)
to enter a place, area, or body in large numbers or by force, often causing harm or damage
enter or attack harmfully

Examples

The cancer had invaded her lungs, making it difficult for her to breathe.
Several invasive species invade local habitats, threatening native plants and animals.
As the virus invades the body, it begins to attack healthy cells.

Synset Examples

The cancer had invaded her lungs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: attack - to take aggressive action against; to assault
  • Antonym: retreat - to move back or withdraw, especially from danger

Etymology

From Latin invadere (to go into, attack), from in- (into) + vadere (to go).
