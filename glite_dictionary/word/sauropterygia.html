Sauropterygia

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɔːroʊpˌtɛˈrɪdʒiə / (AmE), / ˌsɔːrəpˈtɛrɪdʒɪə / (BrE)
an extinct order of marine reptiles that includes plesiosaurs and nothosaurs, known for their long necks and flipper-like limbs
extinct order of marine reptiles

Examples

Many fossils of Sauropterygia have been discovered in marine sedimentary rocks from the Mesozoic era.
The Sauropterygia were highly adapted for life in ancient seas, with their elongated bodies and paddled limbs.
Modern studies of Sauropterygia help paleontologists understand the evolution of aquatic reptiles.

Etymology

From Greek 'sauros' (lizard) + 'pterygion' (little wing, fin), referring to their lizard-like bodies and flippered limbs.

2. NOUN

/ sɔːroʊptəˈrɪdʒiə / (AmE), / sɔːrəʊptəˈrɪdʒiə / (BrE)
an extinct order of marine reptiles that included plesiosaurs and nothosaurs, known for their paddle-like limbs and aquatic adaptations
extinct order of marine reptiles

Examples

The Sauropterygia thrived during the Mesozoic Era, dominating ancient seas as formidable predators.
Fossils of Sauropterygia have been found on every continent, providing insight into prehistoric marine life.
Scientists believe that Sauropterygia evolved specialized fins for efficient swimming in open water.

Etymology

From Greek 'sauros' (lizard) + 'pteryx' (wing, fin), referring to their limb adaptations for swimming.
