Syracuse

1. PROPN

/ ˈsɪr.ə.kjus / (AmE), / ˈsɪr.ə.kjuːs / (BrE)
a city in southeastern Sicily, Italy, founded by ancient Corinthians in the 8th century BC, known for its rich classical history and archaeological sites.
historic city in southeastern Sicily, Italy

Examples

The ancient city of Syracuse was once the most important city in the Greek world.
Archaeologists have discovered remarkable ruins in Syracuse, including a well-preserved Greek theatre.
Many tourists visit Syracuse to explore its historic streets and stunning coastline.

Etymology

From Greek Syrakousai (Συρακοῦσαι), the ancient name of the city.

2. PROPN

/ ˈsɪr.əˌkjuːz / (AmE), / ˈsɪr.ə.kjuːs / (BrE)
an ancient city on the eastern coast of Sicily, famous for its role in Greek and Roman history; now a city in modern Italy
ancient city in Sicily, Italy

Examples

The ancient city of Syracuse was a powerful center of trade and culture in the Mediterranean.
Archimedes, the famous mathematician, was born in Syracuse.
Many tourists visit the ruins of Syracuse to explore its rich archaeological heritage.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Συράκουσαι (Syrákousai), Latin Syracuse.

4. PROPN

/ ˈsɪrəˌkjus / (AmE), / ˈsɪərəkjuːs / (BrE)
an ancient Greek city on the eastern coast of Sicily, noted for its historical and cultural significance and as the site of a famous siege during the Second Punic War
ancient city in Sicily, site of famous siege

Examples

The Roman army besieged Syracuse for two years before finally conquering it.
Archimedes famously lost his life during the fall of Syracuse.
Today, tourists visit Syracuse to explore its ancient ruins and rich history.

Etymology

From Latin Syracusae, from Ancient Greek Συράκουσαι (Syrákousai).
