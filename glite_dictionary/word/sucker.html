2. NOUN

/ ˈsʌkɚ / (AmE), / ˈsʌkə / (BrE)
A shoot that grows from the roots or lower stem of a plant, often at some distance from the main stem.
shoot from roots of a plant

Examples

The rose bush produced several suckers that needed to be removed to keep the plant healthy.
Gardeners often cut away suckers to prevent them from drawing energy from the main plant.
A new sucker appeared near the base of the apple tree after pruning.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsʌkɚ / (AmE), / ˈsʌkə / (BrE)
a person who is easily deceived or tricked; someone who is readily taken advantage of
a gullible or easily deceived person
informal

Examples

He’s such a sucker—he believed every word of their story.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dupe - a person who is easily tricked or deceived
  • Synonym: fool - a person lacking good sense or judgment
  • Antonym: cynic - a person who believes people are motivated purely by self-interest

Etymology

Originally from 'to suck,' used slangily in American English by the late 19th century to mean 'dupe, simpleton.'

4. NOUN

/ ˈsʌkər / (AmE), / ˈsʌkə / (BrE)
an organ, typically found in certain animals, that is specialized for sucking nourishment or attaching to objects by suction
organ for suction or adhesion

Examples

The octopus uses its suckers to grasp onto rocks and pry open shells.
Some leeches attach themselves to hosts using their anterior sucker.
The lamprey feeds by attaching its sucker to the body of fish.

Etymology

From Middle English *suker*, from Old English *sūcan* (to suck).
