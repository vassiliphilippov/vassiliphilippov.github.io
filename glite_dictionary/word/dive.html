dive

1. NOUN

/ daɪv / (AmE), / daɪv / (BrE)
a steep, nose-down descent by an aircraft, often under control as part of a maneuver or attack
steep nose-down aircraft descent

Examples

The pilot initiated a dive to avoid enemy fire.
During the air show, the stunt plane performed a dramatic dive towards the crowd.
A sudden dive can result in a rapid loss of altitude if not carefully managed.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: climb - an aircraft's upward ascent; opposite of dive
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00327503-n
Synset POS: n

2. NOUN

/ daɪv / (AmE), / daɪv / (BrE)
A headlong plunge into water, especially from a diving board or similar structure.
jump or plunge into water

Examples

She executed a perfect dive from the ten-meter platform.
His first dive was hesitant, but he quickly gained confidence.
The competition was won with a flawless dive in the final round.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunge - an act of jumping or diving into water

Etymology

From Old English dӯfan (to dip, submerge).

3. NOUN

/ daɪv / (AmE), / daɪv / (BrE)
a cheap, disreputable bar, nightclub, or dance hall, often considered shabby or unrespectable
cheap, disreputable bar or club

Examples

We ended up in a dive on the edge of town, listening to live blues music.
That old dive has sticky floors but the friendliest staff around.
They avoided the fancy bars and preferred a local dive with cheap drinks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bar - a place where alcoholic drinks are served

Etymology

Originally slang, possibly from 'dive' as a verb from the idea of 'diving' into a low place.

4. VERB

/ daɪv / (AmE), / daɪv / (BrE)
(of prices, values, or amounts) to fall or decrease quickly and steeply.
drop or fall steeply

Examples

Tech stocks dived last week after disappointing earnings reports.
The value of the currency dived to a historic low overnight.
As soon as interest rates rose, house prices began to dive.

Synset Examples

the stock market plunged

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunge - to fall suddenly and steeply
  • Antonym: to soar - to rise rapidly and extremely

Etymology

From Old English dȳfan (to dip, submerge); current figurative use developed in the 19th-20th centuries.
