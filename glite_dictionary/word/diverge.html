to diverge

1. VERB

/ dɪˈvɜrdʒ / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːdʒ / (BrE)
(of lines, roads, or paths) to separate and go in different directions from a common point.
separate and go in different directions

Examples

At this junction, the two roads diverge and head in opposite directions.
As they grew older, their career paths gradually diverged.
The river diverges into several smaller streams just outside the town.

Synset Examples

The lines start to diverge here
Their interests diverged

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to converge - to come together from different directions

Etymology

From Latin 'divergere', from 'dis-' (apart) + 'vergere' (to turn).

3. VERB

/ daɪˈvɝdʒ / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːdʒ / (BrE)
(of a mathematical series) to increase without bound or have no finite limit.
(mathematics) to have no finite limit
medical

Examples

If the sum of the terms grows indefinitely, the series is said to diverge.
A harmonic series will diverge, as its partial sums do not approach a finite value.
In calculus, it's important to determine whether a series will diverge or converge.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to converge - (of a mathematical series) to approach a finite limit

Etymology

From Latin divergere, from dis- (apart) + vergere (to bend, turn).
