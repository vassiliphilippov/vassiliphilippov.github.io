segregation

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɛɡ.rɪˈɡeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛɡ.rɪˈɡeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
(Genetics) The process by which paired alleles separate during meiosis, resulting in their distribution into different gametes.
separation of alleles during meiosis (genetics)
medical

Examples

The principle of segregation explains how offspring inherit one allele from each parent.
During meiosis, segregation ensures that genetic variation is passed to the next generation.
Errors in segregation can lead to genetic disorders due to abnormal distribution of alleles.

Etymology

From Latin segregatio, from segregare (to separate from the flock).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɛɡ.rɪˈɡreɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛɡ.rɪˈɡreɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the action or state of setting someone or something apart from others or the act of separating groups, often by law or policy
the act of separating or setting apart

Examples

Racial segregation was enforced in some schools during the 20th century.
The segregation of waste materials helps improve recycling efficiency.
Jury segregation was ordered to prevent outside influence during the trial.

3. NOUN

/ ˌsɛɡ.rəˈɡeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌsɛɡ.rɪˈɡeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
the enforced separation of different racial, religious, or other groups in a society, especially by providing separate facilities for minority groups
enforced social separation by group

Examples

The policy of segregation in schools was declared unconstitutional by the Supreme Court.
During the 1950s, buses in some cities operated under strict segregation laws.
The museum displays artifacts from the era of racial segregation in the United States.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: integration - the act of combining or incorporating groups previously separated
  • Synonym: discrimination - unfair treatment based on group membership

Etymology

From Latin segregatio (separation), from segregare (to separate from the flock).
