frigate

1. NOUN

/ ˈfrɪɡət / (AmE), / ˈfrɪɡət / (BrE)
a medium-sized, fast, square-rigged warship used in the 18th and 19th centuries, typically armed and employed for escorting and patrol duties
18th–19th century square-rigged warship

Examples

The British navy deployed a frigate to patrol the newly discovered waters.
Historians consider the frigate a crucial vessel in 18th-century naval warfare.
The old harbor museum features a restored frigate from the Napoleonic Wars.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: man-of-war - a powerful warship of the 17th to 19th centuries

Etymology

From French 'frégate,' from Italian 'fregata' (a light, fast ship).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfrɪɡət / (AmE), / ˈfrɪɡɪt / (BrE)
A warship that is typically larger than a destroyer and smaller than a cruiser, especially in the United States Navy.
type of warship

Examples

The navy deployed a new frigate to patrol the disputed waters.
During the ceremony, the admiral praised the crew of the frigate for their bravery.
Compared to a destroyer, a frigate offers more advanced anti-submarine capabilities.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: destroyer - a fast, maneuverable warship, smaller than a frigate
  • Degree Higher: cruiser - a large warship, larger than a frigate

Etymology

From French 'frégate', from Italian 'fregata', meaning a kind of light ship.
