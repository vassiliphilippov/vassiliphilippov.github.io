culverin

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌlvərɪn / (AmE), / ˈkʌlvərɪn / (BrE)
a heavy cannon with a long barrel, used in the 16th and 17th centuries, especially in siege warfare and on ships
16th–17th century long-barrel cannon

Examples

The army deployed a massive culverin to break through the city walls.
Old ships were sometimes armed with several culverins on their decks.
The culverin was prized for its ability to fire projectiles over long distances.

Etymology

From Middle French *couleuvrine*, from Old French *couleuvre* (snake), referring to the long, slender shape.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkʌlvərɪn / (AmE), / ˈkʌlvərɪn / (BrE)
An early type of musket or long cannon used in the medieval and early modern periods.
early type of musket or cannon
archaic

Examples

The soldiers loaded the culverin before taking aim at the castle walls.
During the Middle Ages, a culverin was considered advanced artillery on the battlefield.
Historians discovered a rusted culverin among the ruins of the fortress.

Etymology

From Old French 'couleuvrine', from 'couleuvre' (grass snake), from Latin 'colubra', referencing the weapon's long shape.
