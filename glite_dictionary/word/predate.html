to predate

1. VERB

/ priˈdeɪt / (AmE), / priˈdeɪt / (BrE)
to exist or occur at a time earlier than something else; to precede in time
to exist or happen earlier than

Examples

These ancient fossils clearly predate any known human artifacts.
Her research shows that the custom predates the arrival of Europeans by centuries.
The ruins predate most recorded history in the region.

Synset Examples

Stone tools precede bronze tools

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to precede - to come before in time or order
  • Antonym: to follow - to come after in time or order

Etymology

From Latin prae- (before) + date (from Latin data, given), meaning 'given before.'

3. VERB

/ priˈdeɪt / (AmE), / priˈdeɪt / (BrE)
(of an animal) to hunt, kill, and eat another animal; to prey on.
hunt or prey on (animals)

Examples

Larger fish often predate on smaller species in the reef.
The birds predate insects that hide beneath the leaves.
Wolves typically predate deer during the harsh winter months.

Synset Examples

These mammals predate certain eggs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prey - to hunt and eat other animals
  • Antonym: to scavenge - to feed on dead or decaying matter

Etymology

From Latin praedari (to plunder, to prey), from praeda (prey).

4. VERB

/ priˈdeɪt / (AmE), / priˈdeɪt / (BrE)
to exist or occur earlier than something else; to establish as coming before in time
to exist or happen before something else

Examples

These fossils predate the arrival of humans by thousands of years.
The tradition predates the written record of the region.
This law predates the current constitution by several decades.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to precede - to come before something in time or order

Etymology

From Latin prae- (before) + date (from data, given), meaning 'to assign an earlier date.'
