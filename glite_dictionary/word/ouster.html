ouster

1. NOUN

/ ˈaʊstər / (AmE), / ˈaʊstə / (BrE)
the act of wrongfully dispossessing someone of property or rights, especially in a legal context
wrongful dispossession
legal

Examples

The landlord's attempt at ouster was challenged in court by the tenant.
She claimed ouster after being excluded from property she jointly owned.
Legal proceedings for ouster may result in the rightful owner regaining possession.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dispossession - the action of depriving someone of land or property

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *ouster*, from Old French *oster* (to take away), from Latin *augere* (to increase).

2. NOUN

/ ˈaʊstər / (AmE), / ˈaʊstə / (BrE)
a person who removes or supplants someone from a position or place
someone who ousts another

Examples

The board considered the new CEO an ouster after he replaced his predecessor so abruptly.
History often paints the revolutionary as both a hero and an ouster of the old regime.
Some saw the deputy as an ambitious ouster waiting for his chance to take control.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: usurper - a person who takes a position of power illegally or by force

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Middle French ouster (to oust), from Latin obstare (to stand in the way).

3. NOUN

/ ˈaʊstər / (AmE), / ˈaʊstə / (BrE)
the act of removing someone from a position, place, or job, especially by force or legal action
the act of forcing someone out

Examples

The board's sudden ouster of the CEO shocked the entire industry.
After months of political tension, the parliament voted in favor of her ouster.
The lawsuit ended with the ouster of the tenant from the property.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: removal - the act of taking someone or something away
  • Synonym: eviction - the act of expelling someone from a property
  • Antonym: installation - the act of putting someone in a position

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *ouster* (to oust, expel), from Old French *oster* or *ouster*, from Latin *obstinere* (to exclude, keep out).
