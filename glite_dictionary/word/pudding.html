pudding

1. NOUN

/ ˈpʊd.ɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpʊd.ɪŋ / (BrE)
(British) The dessert course of a meal; any sweet course served after the main course.
British: dessert course of a meal
british

Examples

Would you like some pudding after your meal?
For pudding, we're having apple crumble and custard.
In Britain, the word pudding often means any dessert, not just sweetened milk dishes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dessert - the sweet course eaten at the end of a meal

Etymology

Originally from Old French *boudin* (sausage, black pudding), later generalized in British English to mean any dessert course.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpʊdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpʊdɪŋ / (BrE)
A soft, thick, unsweetened baked dish, often made from cereals, vegetables, or other ingredients, and served as part of a main meal or as a side dish.
soft, thick unsweetened baked dish

Examples

She brought her famous corn pudding to the family dinner.
Savory bread pudding made with cheese is a popular side dish.
The recipe calls for baking the pudding until it is golden and firm.

Synset Examples

corn pudding

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Yorkshire pudding - A baked unsweetened batter dish served as part of a meal

Etymology

From Middle English *poding*, likely from Old French *boudin* (sausage).

3. NOUN

/ ˈpʊdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈpʊdɪŋ / (BrE)
A sweet, soft dessert, often thickened with flour, and baked, boiled, or steamed.
soft, sweet dessert

Examples

For dessert, she served chocolate pudding with whipped cream on top.
British pudding is traditionally steamed rather than baked.
He made banana pudding from an old family recipe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dessert - sweet course eaten at the end of a meal

Etymology

From Old French *boudin* (black pudding, sausage), from Latin *botellus* (sausage).
