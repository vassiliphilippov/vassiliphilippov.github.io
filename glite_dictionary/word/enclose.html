to enclose

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to surround something completely, especially with a wall, fence, or barrier
surround completely

Examples

Thick stone walls enclose the ancient city.
The garden was enclosed by a tall wooden fence.
A layer of mist enclosed the valley at dawn.

Synset Examples

Darkness enclosed him
They closed in the porch with a fence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Synonym: to surround - to be all around; encircle
  • Antonym: open - not closed or surrounded on all sides

Etymology

From Old French *enclos* (enclosed), from Latin *inclaudere* (to shut in).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to surround or cover something completely; to enfold on all sides
to surround or cover completely

Examples

The dense mist began to enclose the bridge as night fell.
A stone wall encloses the ancient garden, keeping it private and serene.
Once the capsule doors closed, the astronauts were enclosed in the small cabin for the duration of the mission.

Synset Examples

Fog enveloped the house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to envelop - to wrap up, cover, or surround completely
  • Synonym: to surround - to be all around something
  • Antonym: to expose - to leave uncovered or unprotected

Etymology

From Middle English enclosen, from Old French enclos, past participle of encloser, from Medieval Latin includere (to shut in).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01583316-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to surround or close off on all sides; to shut in
to surround or shut in

Examples

A thick fog began to enclose the city, making it difficult to see.
The garden is enclosed by a tall wooden fence for privacy.
As the sun set, darkness enclosed the valley in shadows.

Synset Examples

darkness enclosed him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Synonym: to surround - to be all around; encircle on every side
  • Antonym: to expose - to leave without protection or covering

Etymology

From Old French 'enclos', past participle of 'enclore', from Latin 'includere' (to shut in).

Source: WordNet
