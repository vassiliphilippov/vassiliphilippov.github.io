epilog

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛpɪˌlɒg / (AmE), / ˈɛpɪˌlɒɡ / (BrE)
a short speech or section, often addressed directly to the audience, delivered at the end of a play, book, or other literary work
concluding speech or section in a work

Examples

The actor delivered the epilog with great emotion, bringing the play to a satisfying close.
In Shakespearean times, it was common for a character to speak an epilog directly to the audience.
The novel ends with an epilog that ties up all the remaining loose threads.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: prologue - an introductory speech or section before the main action of a play or book
  • Synonym: epilogue - alternative spelling, standard U.S. and U.K. form of 'epilog'

Etymology

From Greek epilogos, meaning 'conclusion' or 'summary'; via Latin epilogus.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛpəˌlɔg / (AmE), / ˈɛpɪlɒg / (BrE)
A short section added at the end of a literary work, usually to comment on or conclude the story.
final section of a literary work

Examples

The novel's epilog reveals what happened to the characters years later.
After the main play ended, the epilog offered the playwright's final thoughts.
Shakespeare often included a brief epilog in his dramatic works.

Synset Examples

the epilogue told what eventually happened to the main characters

Related Terms

  • Synonym: epilogue - alternative spelling, more common variant
  • Antonym: prologue - an introduction at the start of a literary work

Etymology

From Greek 'epilogos' (conclusion), from 'epi' (upon) + 'logos' (word, speech).
