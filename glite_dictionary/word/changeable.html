changeable

1. ADJ

/ ˈtʃeɪndʒəbl / (AmE), / ˈtʃeɪndʒəbl / (BrE)
capable of changing or of being changed; likely to alter in form, quality, or nature.
able or likely to change

Examples

The weather in this region is notoriously changeable, so pack clothing for all seasons.
Her changeable mood made it difficult to predict how she would react.
Plastic is a changeable material that can be molded into various shapes.

Synset Examples

a mutable substance
mutable weather patterns
a mutable foreign policy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mutable - liable to change; capable of change or of being changed
  • Antonym: invariable - never changing; constant and unchanging

Etymology

From Middle English 'changeable', from Old French, from 'changer' (to change).

2. ADJ

/ ˈtʃeɪndʒəbəl / (AmE), / ˈtʃeɪndʒəbl / (BrE)
(of a material or surface) showing different colors when viewed from different angles or in different lights; iridescent.
varying in color when viewed from different angles

Examples

The changeable silk dress shimmered in hues of blue and green as she moved.
Dragonfly wings appear changeable, reflecting different colors with every shift of the light.
We admired the changeable surface of the gemstone, which sparkled in many shades.

Synset Examples

changeable taffeta
chatoyant (or shot) silk
a dragonfly hovered, vibrating and iridescent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: iridescent - showing luminous colors that seem to change when seen from different angles
  • Synonym: chatoyant - having a changeable luster or color, like a cat's eye

4. ADJ

/ ˈtʃeɪndʒəbl̩ / (AmE), / ˈtʃeɪndʒəbl̩ / (BrE)
likely to change or to be changed; able to undergo alteration.
able or likely to change

Examples

The weather in this region is notoriously changeable throughout the year.
Her mood is quite changeable, so you never know how she'll react.
Stock prices are extremely changeable during economic uncertainty.

Synset Examples

changeable behavior
changeable moods
changeable prices

Related Terms

  • Synonym: variable - able to change or be changed; not consistent
  • Antonym: constant - remaining the same over time; unchanging

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French 'changier' (to change) plus '-able'.
