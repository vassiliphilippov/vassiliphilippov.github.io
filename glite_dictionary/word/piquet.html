piquet

1. NOUN

/ piˈkɛt / (AmE), / piˈkɛt / (BrE)
A card game for two players, played with a pack reduced to 32 cards, in which players score points by forming combinations and winning tricks.
two-player card game with 32 cards

Examples

We spent the evening playing piquet by the fire.
He taught me the rules of piquet during our trip to France.
Although less common today, piquet was once a very popular game among the nobility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bezique - a card game played with two decks and similar rules

Etymology

From French 'piquet', likely from the verb 'piquer' (to prick, pierce), referring to scoring in the game.

2. NOUN

/ pɪˈkɛt / (AmE), / pɪˈkɛt / (BrE)
A form of military punishment, formerly used in Britain in the late 17th century, in which a soldier was forced to stand on one foot on a pointed stake.
historical military punishment involving a pointed stake
archaic british

Examples

British troops feared the cruel piquet punishment inflicted for minor offenses.
The officer sentenced the deserter to endure the piquet for several hours.
Historical records describe piquet as both painful and humiliating for soldiers.

Etymology

From French 'piquet' (stake), diminutive of 'pic' (pick, pointed object).
