Whig

1. NOUN

/ wɪɡ / (AmE), / wɪɡ / (BrE)
a member of a political group in colonial America that supported the American Revolution against British rule
supporter of the American Revolution

Examples

As a proud Whig, he argued fervently for the colonies' independence.
Several leading Whigs gathered to plan the next steps of the revolution.
Her grandfather was a devoted Whig who fought against British rule.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Tory - a colonist in America who supported the British side during the American Revolution

Etymology

Originally from Scottish 'whiggamore' (a cattle driver), later applied to a British political group and adopted in America.

2. NOUN

/ wɪɡ / (AmE), / wɪɡ / (BrE)
A member of a British political party active from the late 17th to the early 19th centuries, which advocated for social reform and constitutional monarchism, and was opposed to the Tories.
member of historic British political party

Examples

In the early 1800s, Whig leaders pushed for parliamentary reforms.
Charles Fox was a prominent Whig who championed civil liberties.
The Whig party played an important role during the reign of George III.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Tory - member of the rival British political party to the Whigs

Etymology

Probably from Whiggamore, a term for Scottish cattle drivers, later applied to political factions.

3. NOUN

/ wɪɡ / (AmE), / wɪɡ / (BrE)
a member of the Whig Party, a major political party in the United States during the early and mid-19th century, before the Civil War
member of historical U.S. political party

Examples

Abraham Lincoln began his political career as a Whig before joining the Republican Party.
During the 1840s, the Whigs opposed the expansion of slavery into new territories.
Many Whig leaders later played significant roles in the formation of the Republican Party.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Democrat - a member of the Democratic Party in the United States
  • Synonym: Whig - a member of the British Whig Party, active in the 17th–19th centuries

Etymology

From the British political term 'Whig', originally used for a political faction in England in the late 17th century.
