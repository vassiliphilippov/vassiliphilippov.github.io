rationalisation

1. NOUN

/ ˌræʃ.ə.nəl.əˈzeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃ.ə.nə.laɪˈzeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
The systematic organization of something according to a specific plan, logic, or rationale.
systematic organization or planning
british

Examples

The company's rationalisation of its workflow led to greater efficiency and productivity.
Government rationalisation of public spending has affected many social programs.
The rationalisation of the library's cataloging system simplified the search process for users.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rationalization - systematic organization; American spelling of rationalisation

Etymology

From rational + -isation, influenced by French rationalisation and Latin rationalis (rational).

2. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Mathematics) The process of simplifying an expression or equation by removing radicals, without altering its value or the roots of the equation.
simplification by removing radicals (maths)

Examples

The rationalisation of the denominator is a common technique in algebra.
Through rationalisation, the square root was removed from the fraction.
Students are often asked to practise rationalisation when simplifying mathematical expressions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: simplification - the process of making something simpler or easier to do or understand

Etymology

From Latin 'ratio' (reason) via French 'rationalisation', influenced by 'rational' in mathematics.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01214715-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of attempting to explain or justify behavior or an attitude with logical reasons, even if these are not true or appropriate
making something appear reasonable
british

Examples

He offered a detailed rationalisation for his decision, though it seemed forced.
Her rationalisation of her lateness was that traffic had been unusually heavy.
Sometimes, people use rationalisation to defend actions they secretly regret.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rationalization - American spelling of rationalisation

Etymology

From Latin ratio (reason) via French rationaliser and English rationalize; spelling follows British conventions.

4. NOUN

/ ˌræʃ.ə.nəl.əˈzeɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃ.ə.nəlaɪˈzeɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
The reorganization of a business or system according to scientific principles of management to increase efficiency.
making business more efficient by reorganizing

Examples

The rationalisation of the manufacturing process led to significant cost reductions for the company.
After the merger, management began a programme of rationalisation to eliminate overlapping roles.
Many staff were concerned that rationalisation would result in job losses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: streamlining - making a business or process more efficient
  • Antonym: inefficiency - lack of efficiency, especially in business processes

Etymology

From rational + -isation, influenced by Latin ratio (reason).

5. NOUN

/ ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(Psychology) The process of offering reasonable-sounding explanations for behavior or feelings to conceal true motives, often as a defense mechanism.
defense mechanism: making justifying excuses
medical

Examples

After failing the exam, Mark used rationalisation to convince himself that he never wanted to pass in the first place.
Her rationalisation of her anger masked the deeper insecurities she was unwilling to admit.
Psychologists often encounter rationalisation when patients refuse to acknowledge their true feelings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - the act of showing something to be right or reasonable
  • Antonym: denial - a defense mechanism where unpleasant realities are ignored or refused to be accepted

Etymology

From rational + -isation, modelled on French 'rationalisation'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13567372-n
Synset POS: n
