to rouse

1. VERB

/ raʊz / (AmE), / raʊz / (BrE)
to become active or alert, especially after sleep or inactivity
to become active or alert

Examples

It took her a few minutes to rouse after a long nap.
He finally managed to rouse from his lethargy and joined the meeting.
The noise in the street was enough to rouse him from his daydreams.

Synset Examples

He finally bestirred himself

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to awaken - to wake up or make someone wake up
  • Synonym: stir - to begin to move or become active

Etymology

Probably from Old French *reuser* (to shake, stir up).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02088053-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ raʊz / (AmE), / raʊz / (BrE)
to force someone to leave a place, especially by waking or startling them
force to leave or get up

Examples

The fire alarm roused everyone from their sleep in the middle of the night.
She roused her brother out of bed to catch the early train.
The soldiers roused the villagers and told them to evacuate immediately.

Synset Examples

The police routed them out of bed at 2 A.M.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evict - to force someone to leave, especially from a property
  • Degree Lower: to awaken - to cause someone to stop sleeping

Etymology

From Middle English rousen (to shake the feathers), probably from Old French ruser (to drive back, push).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02060390-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ raʊz / (AmE), / raʊz / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel excited, agitated, or strongly motivated
provoke excitement or agitation

Examples

The coach's passionate speech roused the team's fighting spirit.
Protesters were roused by the government's controversial decision.
His anger roused the room to a fever pitch of excitement.

Synset Examples

The speaker charged up the crowd with his inflammatory remarks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to arouse - to stir up or evoke a feeling or response
  • Antonym: calm - to make someone quiet and relaxed

Etymology

From Old French 'reuser' (to shake the feathers), probably of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01766380-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ raʊz / (AmE), / raʊz / (BrE)
to cause someone to wake up or become conscious; to awaken from sleep or inactivity
to cause to wake up or become conscious

Examples

The noise in the hallway managed to rouse him from a deep sleep.
It usually takes several alarms to rouse me in the morning.
She was gently roused by the nurse after her operation.

Synset Examples

He was roused by the drunken men in the street
Please wake me at 6 AM.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wake - to stop sleeping and become conscious
  • Synonym: to awaken - to make someone stop sleeping

Etymology

From Old French 'reuser' (to shake, stir up), of uncertain origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00018806-v
Synset POS: v
