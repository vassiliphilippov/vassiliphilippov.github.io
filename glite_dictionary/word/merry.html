merry

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɛri / (AmE), / ˈmɛri / (BrE)
Full of fun and gaiety; characterized by lively celebration and cheerful enjoyment.
cheerful, lively, and full of fun

Examples

The city was filled with merry celebrations during the festival.
We enjoyed a merry evening with music and dancing.
Her merry laughter brightened the whole room.

Synset Examples

a festive (or festal) occasion
gay and exciting night life
a merry evening

Related Terms

  • Synonym: joyful - feeling or showing great happiness
  • Synonym: cheerful - noticeably happy and optimistic
  • Antonym: gloomy - dark or poorly lit, especially so as to appear depressing or frightening

Etymology

From Old English 'myrge' (pleasant, agreeable).

2. ADJ

/ ˈmɛri / (AmE), / ˈmɛri / (BrE)
(of an action or event) quick and energetic; lively.
quick and energetic; lively

Examples

They set off at a merry pace, eager to reach the summit before noon.
He led his dog on a merry chase through the tall grass.
The children played a merry game of tag in the backyard.

Synset Examples

a brisk walk in the park
a lively gait
a merry chase
traveling at a rattling rate
a snappy pace
a spanking breeze

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brisk - quick and active; lively
  • Synonym: lively - full of energy and enthusiasm

Etymology

From Old English *myrge* (pleasant, agreeable), with later extension to lively activity.

3. ADJ

/ ˈmɛri / (AmE), / ˈmɛri / (BrE)
cheerful and lively; full of high-spirited joy or happiness.
cheerful and lively

Examples

The children were especially merry during the holiday festivities.
His merry laugh could be heard across the room.
Wishing you a merry Christmas and a happy New Year!

Synset Examples

the jolly crowd at the reunion
jolly old Saint Nick
a jovial old gentleman

peals of merry laughter
a mirthful laugh

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cheerful - feeling or showing happiness and optimism
  • Synonym: jovial - cheerful and friendly
  • Antonym: gloomy - sad or pessimistic in mood

Etymology

From Old English 'myrge' (pleasant, agreeable), related to Old High German 'murgi' (pleasant).
