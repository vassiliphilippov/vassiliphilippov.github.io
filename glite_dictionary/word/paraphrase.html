paraphrase

1. NOUN

/ ˈpærəˌfreɪz / (AmE), / ˈpærəˌfreɪz / (BrE)
a restatement of a text or passage giving the meaning in another form, usually for clarity
restatement of a text in other words

Examples

The teacher asked the students to write a paraphrase of the poem.
A good paraphrase makes complex ideas much easier to understand.
In her essay, the student included a paraphrase of the scientist's explanation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rewording - the act of expressing something in different words
  • Degree Lower: summary - a brief statement of the main points
  • Degree Higher: translation - rendering text from one language to another

Etymology

From Greek paraphrasis, from para- (beside) and phrazein (to tell).

2. VERB

/ ˈpærəˌfreɪz / (AmE), / ˈpærəfreɪz / (BrE)
to express the same meaning of something spoken or written using different words, especially to make it clearer
express in different words

Examples

Can you paraphrase this sentence to make it easier to understand?
She paraphrased the professor's comments during the discussion.
When writing essays, students often need to paraphrase information from sources.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to rephrase - to say or write something in different words
  • Antonym: quote - to repeat or copy out a phrase or passage exactly

Etymology

From Greek 'paraphrasis' (additional explanation), from 'para-' (beside) + 'phrazein' (to tell).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
