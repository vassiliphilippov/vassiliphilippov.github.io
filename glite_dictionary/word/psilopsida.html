Psilopsida

1. NOUN

/ saɪˌlɒpˈsɪdə / (AmE), / saɪˌlɒpˈsɪdə / (BrE)
A former class of simple vascular plants, including whisk ferns, characterized by the absence of true roots and leaves, and bearing rudimentary spore sacs.
class of simple vascular plants (whisk ferns)

Examples

The Psilopsida were once considered some of the most primitive vascular plants.
Research on Psilopsida provides insight into the evolution of early land plants.
Few living plants today resemble the ancient Psilopsida in structure.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Psilotaceae - family of whisk ferns, to which Psilopsida belongs

Etymology

From New Latin, combining 'Psilo-' (bare) and '-psida' (from Greek 'opsis', appearance).

2. NOUN

/ saɪˌlɒpˈsɪdə / (AmE), / saɪˌlɒpˈsɪdə / (BrE)
A class of primitive vascular plants, commonly known as whisk ferns, with no true roots, limited leaf differentiation, and simple spore sacs, comprising the family Psilotaceae.
a class of primitive vascular plants (whisk ferns)

Examples

The Psilopsida are notable for their lack of true roots and primitive vascular structure.
Scientists study Psilopsida to understand the early evolution of land plants.
Whisk ferns, classified under Psilopsida, are found in tropical and subtropical regions.

Etymology

From Greek 'psilos' (bare, naked) + 'opsis' (appearance), referring to their simple structure.
