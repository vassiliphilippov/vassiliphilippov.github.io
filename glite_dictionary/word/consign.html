to consign

1. VERB

/ kənˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kənˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to commit or deliver something irrevocably, often permanently or irretrievably, to a particular fate, place, or person
commit irrevocably; hand over permanently

Examples

After the scandal, the politician was consigned to a life outside of public office.
The damaged artwork was consigned to the attic, never to be seen again.
She felt as though her dreams had been consigned to oblivion.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to commit - to carry out or perpetrate; to consign in official use

Etymology

From Latin consignare (to seal, mark, or deliver officially), from com- (together) + signare (to sign).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02353320-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ kənˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kənˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to give something over to another person, organization, or place for care, safekeeping, or custody
give over for care or safekeeping

Examples

Before the trip, please consign your valuables to the hotel safe.
The old documents were consigned to the archives for preservation.
He decided to consign his childhood toys to a trusted friend.

Synset Examples

consign your baggage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to entrust - assign responsibility for doing something or for the care of someone or something to someone else

Etymology

From Latin consignare (to seal, register), via Old French consigner.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02352633-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ kənˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kənˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to send or deliver something, especially goods, to a person or place, often officially or for sale
send or deliver goods to a place

Examples

The company will consign the packages to your new address next week.
He consigned the clothing to a local shop for resale.
All orders are consigned by courier to minimize the risk of loss.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to send - to cause something to go or be taken to a particular destination

Etymology

From Latin consignare (to mark, seal), via French consigner.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
