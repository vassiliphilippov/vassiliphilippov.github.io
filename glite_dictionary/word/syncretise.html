to syncretise

1. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋkrəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋkrɪtʌɪz / (BrE)
to unite or combine different beliefs, practices, or principles, often to form a new system
unite different beliefs or systems

Examples

In an effort to syncretise their traditions, the two communities blended their festivals.
The philosopher tried to syncretise conflicting theories into a unified framework.
Early Christianity often syncretised pagan rituals with its own practices.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to synthesize - to combine different elements into a whole
  • Synonym: to integrate - to combine parts into a whole or make them work together

Etymology

From Greek 'synkretismos', meaning union of communities.

2. VERB

/ ˈsɪŋkrəˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈsɪŋkrɪtʌɪz / (BrE)
to fuse or combine different beliefs, practices, or systems, especially in religion or language
to fuse or combine (beliefs, practices, etc.)

Examples

The scholar argued that the local religion had syncretised with foreign beliefs over centuries.
Many languages have syncretised grammatical forms through regular contact and change.
During migration, cultures often syncretise traditions to create something new.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to merge - to combine two or more things into one
  • Synonym: blend - to mix different things together

Etymology

From Greek 'synkretismos' (union of communities), via French 'syncrétisme' and modern Latin 'syncretismus'.
