archaic

1. ADJ

/ ɑrˈkeɪɪk / (AmE), / ɑːˈkeɪɪk / (BrE)
very old or old-fashioned; belonging to an earlier period and no longer in everyday use or practice
very old or from an earlier period

Examples

Many English legal terms are considered archaic today.
The historian studied archaic tools used by ancient civilizations.
Some old traditions are now seen as archaic but are still respected in certain cultures.

Synset Examples

a ramshackle antediluvian tenement
antediluvian ideas
archaic laws

Related Terms

  • Antonym: modern - relating to the present or recent times
  • Synonym: obsolete - no longer produced or used; out of date

Etymology

From Greek archaïkos (ancient), from archaios (old, ancient), from archē (beginning).

2. ADJ

/ ɑːrˈkeɪ.ɪk / (AmE), / ɑːˈkeɪ.ɪk / (BrE)
belonging to or characteristic of an early or original stage of development, especially referring to living organisms or forms that have changed little from their ancestors.
early or primitive in development or form

Examples

Many fossils represent archaic forms of life that no longer exist today.
The platypus is considered an archaic mammal due to its primitive features.
Some scientists study archaic species to understand the course of evolution.

Synset Examples

archaic forms of life
primitive mammals
the okapi is a short-necked primitive cousin of the giraffe

Related Terms

  • Synonym: primitive - relating to an early stage of evolutionary or historical development

Etymology

From Greek arkhaīkos (ancient), from arkhē (beginning).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00821300-s
Synset POS: s
