incumbrance

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (BrE)
a thing that obstructs, impedes, or is burdensome
obstruction or burden

Examples

The fallen tree was an incumbrance that blocked the narrow road for hours.
They viewed the outdated regulations as an unnecessary incumbrance to business growth.
When hiking, it's best to travel without any incumbrance to make fast progress.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: encumbrance - something that impedes or is burdensome; a burden

Etymology

Variant of 'encumbrance', from Old French encombrer (to hinder), from Latin incombrare (to block up).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (BrE)
a legal claim or liability attached to property, such as a lien or mortgage, that may reduce its value or restrict its use
legal claim or liability on property
legal

Examples

The property was sold free of any incumbrance or legal claim against it.
Before purchasing land, make sure to check for any incumbrance such as mortgages or liens.
He could not transfer the title because there was an outstanding incumbrance on the estate.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: encumbrance - a claim or liability on property, same as incumbrance

Etymology

From Medieval Latin incumbrantia, from Latin incumbere (to lie upon).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13424198-n
Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (AmE), / ɪnˈkʌm.brəns / (BrE)
A burden or obstacle; something that makes a situation more difficult or weighs one down.
burden; something that hinders

Examples

After selling the house, she felt free from the incumbrance of property maintenance.
The new law removed many legal incumbrances to starting a small business.
He viewed his financial debt as a serious incumbrance on his plans for the future.

Synset Examples

the burden of responsibility
that's a load off my mind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burden - a load, especially a heavy one
  • Synonym: encumbrance - something that impedes or burdens
  • Antonym: relief - the alleviation or removal of a burden

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *incumbrantia*, from Latin *incumbere* (to lie upon). Variant of 'encumbrance.'
