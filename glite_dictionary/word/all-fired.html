all-fired

1. ADJ

/ ˈɔlˌfaɪrd / (AmE), / ˈɔːlˌfaɪəd / (BrE)
Used to emphasize the intensity or extremity of something, often as an intensifier before adjectives or adverbs.
extremely, intensely (intensifier)
informal american

Examples

I don't see why you're in such an all-fired rush to leave.
She was all-fired determined to win the competition, no matter the cost.
That was the most all-fired ridiculous idea I've ever heard.

Synset Examples

why is he in such an all-fired hurry?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: damned - used for emphasis, expressing extreme degree
  • Synonym: very - to a high degree

Etymology

Probably an alteration of 'hell-fired', used as an intensifier in American English.

2. ADV

/ ˈɔlˌfaɪɚd / (AmE), / ˈɔːlˌfaɪəd / (BrE)
(informal, chiefly US) Used to emphasize a quality; extremely or excessively.
extremely; very (informal, US)
informal american

Examples

I can't understand why he's being so all-fired stubborn about it.
She was all-fired eager to prove everyone wrong at the meeting.
Do you have to get all-fired upset over such a small mistake?

Synset Examples

you are bloody right
Why are you so all-fired aggressive?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bloody - British informal intensifier meaning 'very'
  • Synonym: damned - Informal intensifier used for emphasis
  • Degree Lower: slightly - To a small degree; not much

Etymology

Originally a euphemism for 'hell-fired,' used to avoid strong language in the 19th century US.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: r
