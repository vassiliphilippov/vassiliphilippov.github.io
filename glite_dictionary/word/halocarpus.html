Halocarpus

1. NOUN

/ ˌhæləˈkɑrpəs / (AmE), / ˌhaləˈkɑːpəs / (BrE)
A genus of dioecious trees and shrubs native to New Zealand, belonging to the family Podocarpaceae.
genus of New Zealand coniferous trees and shrubs

Examples

Halocarpus species are well-adapted to the cool, wet climate of southern New Zealand.
Conservation efforts have focused on protecting Halocarpus habitats from deforestation.
The timber of Halocarpus is sometimes used in local construction and woodworking projects.

Etymology

From Greek 'halos' (salt, sea) and 'karpos' (fruit), referring to the salty habitat or structure of the fruit.

2. NOUN

/ ˌhæləˈkɑrpəs / (AmE), / ˌhaləˈkɑːpəs / (BrE)
A genus of dioecious trees and shrubs native to New Zealand, similar in habit to Dacrydium.
genus of New Zealand trees and shrubs

Examples

Several species in the genus Halocarpus are found exclusively in New Zealand’s forests.
The foliage of Halocarpus is similar in appearance to that of Dacrydium.
Botanists have studied Halocarpus to understand the evolution of southern conifers.

Etymology

From Greek 'halo-' (salt, sea) and 'karpos' (fruit), referring to the plant's habitat and fruit type.
