bullrush

1. NOUN

/ ˈbʊlˌrʌʃ / (AmE), / ˈbʊl.rʌʃ / (BrE)
a tall plant with soft, erect or arching stems, found in wet areas of Eurasia, Australia, New Zealand, and commonly in North America
tall wetland plant with soft stems

Examples

We saw a flock of ducks hiding among the bullrushes at the edge of the pond.
The bullrush is known for its long, flexible stems that grow in marshy soils.
Gardeners often plant bullrushes to improve water quality in wetland areas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cattail - a similar tall, reed-like wetland plant, especially Typha species
  • Synonym: reed - a tall, slender-leaved plant of the grass family, growing in water or marshy ground

2. NOUN

/ ˈbʊlˌrʌʃ / (AmE), / ˈbʊl.rʌʃ / (BrE)
A tall marsh plant with cylindrical seed heads that burst when mature, releasing down; its long leaves are used for making mats and chair seats.
tall marsh plant with bursting seed heads

Examples

The bullrush grows densely along the edges of the pond, providing shelter for birds.
Artisans still use bullrush leaves to weave durable baskets and chair seats.
When the seed heads of the bullrush mature, they explode to release fluffy down.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reed - a tall, slender-leaved plant of the grass family
  • Synonym: cattail - marsh plant with cylindrical seed heads, also known as bulrush in American English

Etymology

From 'bull' + 'rush', referring to its robust rush-like appearance.
