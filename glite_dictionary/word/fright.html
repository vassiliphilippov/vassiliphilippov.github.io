fright

1. NOUN

/ fraɪt / (AmE), / fraɪt / (BrE)
A sudden, intense feeling of fear caused by the expectation of pain or danger; an experience of being very afraid.
sudden intense fear

Examples

The loud crash downstairs gave her a terrible fright.
He recovered quickly after the initial fright.
It was only a fright, but he could still feel his heart pounding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fear - an unpleasant emotion caused by the threat of danger, pain, or harm
  • Degree Higher: terror - extreme fear
  • Degree Lower: apprehension - anxiety or fear that something bad or unpleasant will happen

Etymology

From Old English 'fyrhto' (fear), related to 'frighten'.

2. VERB

/ fraɪt / (AmE), / fraɪt / (BrE)
to make someone feel afraid; to cause fear in someone
cause fear in
archaic

Examples

The sudden storm frighted the children and made them run inside.
It takes more than a shadowy figure to fright her.
He tried to fright me with ghost stories, but I just laughed.

Synset Examples

The stranger who hangs around the building frightens me
Ghosts could never affright her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scare - to cause fear in someone
  • Antonym: to reassure - to make someone feel less afraid or anxious

Etymology

From Old English 'fyrhtan' (to frighten), related to 'fear'.
