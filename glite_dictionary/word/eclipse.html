eclipse

1. VERB

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
to make someone or something seem less important or less impressive by comparison
surpass in importance or prominence

Examples

Her achievements quickly eclipsed those of her colleagues.
The band's fame eventually eclipsed that of their mentors.
His generosity eclipses anything I've seen before.

Synset Examples

the tragedy overshadowed the couple's happiness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to outshine - to be much more impressive or important than someone or something
  • Synonym: to overshadow - to make someone or something seem less important by being more successful or impressive

Etymology

From Greek ekleipsis (a leaving out, an eclipse), via Old French and Latin.

2. VERB

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
to cause an eclipse of (a celestial body), by having one celestial body move into the shadow of another
to cause an eclipse (of a celestial body)

Examples

The Moon will eclipse the Sun during tomorrow's solar eclipse.
As the planet passed in front, it momentarily eclipsed the distant star.
In rare cases, one moon can eclipse another as seen from Earth.

Synset Examples

The Sun eclipses the moon today
Planets and stars often are occulted by other celestial bodies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: occult - to block the light from (a celestial body), as in astronomy

Etymology

From Greek ekleipsis, via Latin eclipsis, meaning 'a failing' or 'abandonment'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02162770-v
Synset POS: v

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
an occasion when one celestial body moves into the shadow of another body, temporarily obscuring it from view
when one celestial body blocks another

Examples

We watched the solar eclipse through special glasses to protect our eyes.
A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth passes directly between the sun and the moon.
Many ancient cultures recorded their observations of a total eclipse in detailed writings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: occultation - an event in which one object is hidden by another, especially an astronomical body
  • Synonym: transit - the passage of a smaller celestial body across the face of a larger one

Etymology

From Greek 'ekleipsis' (abandonment, failure to appear), from 'ekleipō' (to fail to appear).

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
