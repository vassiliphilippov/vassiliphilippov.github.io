mortmain

1. NOUN

/ ˈmɔrtˌmeɪn / (AmE), / ˈmɔːt.meɪn / (BrE)
An oppressive or stifling influence exerted by past events, customs, or decisions; a dead hand.
oppressive influence of the past

Examples

The school's curriculum seemed stuck in mortmain, unable to adapt to new ideas.
Legal reforms were difficult to achieve due to the mortmain of tradition.
He argued that innovation was stifled by the mortmain of bureaucracy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dead hand - an oppressive controlling power from the past

Etymology

From Old French 'mortemain' (dead hand), from Latin 'mortua manus'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmɔrtˌmeɪn / (AmE), / ˈmɔːt.meɪn / (BrE)
(historical, legal) The perpetual, inalienable ownership of real property, especially by a corporation such as an ecclesiastical institution.
perpetual ownership of property by a corporation
archaic legal

Examples

During the Middle Ages, lands held in mortmain could not be inherited or sold.
Many monarchs passed laws to limit the spread of mortmain by religious orders.
The church accumulated vast wealth through mortmain before reforms were introduced.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: fee simple - absolute ownership of land free of limitations
  • Antonym: alienation - the act of transferring property to another

Etymology

From Old French mortemain (literally 'dead hand'), referring to property that remains perpetually with a non-natural person.
