sorghum

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɔr.ɡəm / (AmE), / ˈsɔː.ɡəm / (BrE)
A genus of tropical and subtropical grasses grown chiefly for their edible grain, also used as animal fodder and in the production of sweet syrup.
a genus of cereal grasses; edible grain

Examples

Farmers in arid regions often grow sorghum because it tolerates drought better than many other grains.
The syrup made from sweet sorghum is a popular ingredient in southern U.S. cooking.
Researchers are developing new varieties of sorghum to improve crop yields and disease resistance.

Etymology

From Italian sorgo, probably of Mediterranean origin, perhaps ultimately from Latin surgum.

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɔrɡəm / (AmE), / ˈsɔːɡəm / (BrE)
A sweet syrup obtained from the juice of the sweet sorghum plant, used as a sweetener or in cooking.
sweet syrup from sorghum juice

Examples

Many southern recipes use sorghum as a natural sweetener instead of refined sugar.
Farmers harvested the sweet sorghum to make syrup for local markets.
The chef drizzled sorghum over the pancakes for an earthy, rich flavor.

Etymology

From Modern Latin Sorghum, the genus name, which is from Italian sorgo.

3. NOUN

/ ˈsɔr.ɡəm / (AmE), / ˈsɔː.ɡəm / (BrE)
a tall cereal grass of warm regions, widely grown for its grain used as food, livestock feed, and in the production of syrup and alcohol
a tropical cereal grass grown for its grain

Examples

Sorghum is a major crop in many African and Asian countries due to its drought resistance.
Farmers often rotate crops with sorghum to improve soil health and manage pests.
Gluten-free flours made from sorghum are increasingly popular among people with wheat allergies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: millet - a small-seeded grass cultivated as a cereal crop

Etymology

From modern Latin 'sorghum', from Italian 'sorgo', possibly from a Sudanic source.
