to enfranchise

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈfrænˌtʃaɪz / (AmE), / ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz / (BrE)
to give someone the right to vote in an election; to grant voting rights
grant the right to vote

Examples

After years of activism, lawmakers decided to enfranchise all citizens regardless of race or gender.
Efforts to enfranchise women in the early twentieth century changed the course of history.
The new constitution aimed to enfranchise previously excluded groups within the population.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to disenfranchise - to deprive someone of the right to vote

Etymology

From Old French 'enfranchir' (to set free), from 'franc' (free).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz / (AmE), / ɪnˈfræn.tʃaɪz / (BrE)
to grant freedom or rights, especially the right to full citizenship, including from slavery or servitude
to grant freedom or rights

Examples

The government moved to enfranchise thousands of former slaves following the revolution.
After years of activism, the group was finally enfranchised and gained equal rights.
Many nations struggled with when and how to enfranchise different segments of their population.

Synset Examples

Slaves were enfranchised in the mid-19th century

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to emancipate - to set someone free from legal, social, or political restrictions
  • Antonym: to disenfranchise - to deprive someone of rights or privileges, especially the right to vote

Etymology

From Old French enfranchir (to set free), from en- (make) + franc (free).
