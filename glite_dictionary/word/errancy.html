errancy

1. NOUN

/ ˈɛrənsi / (AmE), / ˈɛrənsi / (BrE)
the tendency to make mistakes or to be fallible
tendency to err; fallibility

Examples

The judge acknowledged the errancy of human decisions in the legal process.
Philosophers debate whether errancy is an essential quality of human nature.
Her repeated errancy in critical tasks led to a review of procedures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fallibility - the ability or tendency to make mistakes
  • Antonym: infallibility - the inability to be wrong; absolute trustworthiness

Etymology

From Latin 'errantia' (wandering, straying), from 'errare' (to wander, to err).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɛrənsi / (AmE), / ˈɛrənsi / (BrE)
the holding of beliefs or views that are considered to deviate from accepted doctrine, especially in the context of Christian theology and papal infallibility.
belief in the fallibility of doctrine or teaching

Examples

Some theologians have written extensively about the errancy of certain church teachings.
The debate over biblical errancy continues to divide scholars and believers alike.
He was criticized by church leaders for promoting the idea of papal errancy.

Synset Examples

he denies the errancy of the Catholic Church

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heresy - holding beliefs that disagree with established doctrine, especially in religion

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'errantia' (wandering, straying), from Latin 'errare' (to wander, err).
