flail

1. NOUN

/ fleɪl / (AmE), / fleɪl / (BrE)
A tool consisting of a handle with a free-swinging stick attached at the end, traditionally used for threshing grain by hand.
manual threshing tool

Examples

The farmer used a flail to separate the grain from the husks.
Before threshing machines were invented, people relied on the flail during harvest.
A wooden flail hangs on the wall of the agricultural museum.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to thresh - to separate grain from husks using a flail or machine

Etymology

From Old French flael, from Latin flagellum (whip, scourge).

2. VERB

/ fleɪl / (AmE), / fleɪl / (BrE)
to wave or swing something, or move oneself, about wildly or energetically, especially in a desperate or uncontrolled way
wave or thrash about wildly

Examples

He began to flail his arms to signal for help.
The boxer started to flail wildly after losing his balance.
When she slipped on the ice, her legs flailed beneath her.

Synset Examples

Her arms were flailing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrash - to move or swing about violently

Etymology

From Old English *flegel*, from Latin *flagellum* (whip).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02098198-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ fleɪl / (AmE), / fleɪl / (BrE)
to beat or strike something or someone repeatedly and violently; to thrash
to beat or thrash hard

Examples

The angry farmer began to flail the sacks with a stick to loosen the grain.
He flailed his opponent mercilessly during the heated argument.
In old tales, heroes are sometimes said to flail their enemies with great fury.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrash - to beat repeatedly with a stick or whip
  • Synonym: strike - to hit someone or something forcibly
  • Antonym: caress - to touch or stroke gently

Etymology

From Old French 'flael', from Latin 'flagellum' (a whip, scourge).
