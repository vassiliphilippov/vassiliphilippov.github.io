resistant

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈzɪstənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪstənt / (BrE)
Not affected or harmed by something; able to withstand the effects or influence of something.
not affected or harmed by something

Examples

The bacteria are increasingly resistant to antibiotics, making them harder to treat.
She remained resistant to all their arguments for change.
This material is highly resistant to water and stains.

Synset Examples

resistant to the effects of heat
resistant to persuasion

Related Terms

  • Antonym: susceptible - likely or liable to be influenced or harmed by something

Etymology

From Latin 'resistere' (to withstand, oppose) via French 'résistant'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01779414-s
Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈzɪstənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪstənt / (BrE)
Able to tolerate or withstand specific environmental conditions, stresses, or harmful agents without being affected.
able to withstand stress or adverse conditions

Examples

The scientists developed a resistant strain of wheat that thrives in dry climates.
These bacteria are resistant to many common antibiotics, making infections harder to treat.
Some plastics are highly resistant to heat and chemicals, making them suitable for industrial use.

Synset Examples

the plant is tolerant of saltwater
these fish are quite tolerant as long as extremes of pH are avoided
the new hybrid is more resistant to drought

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tolerant - able to endure specified conditions or treatment
  • Antonym: susceptible - likely to be harmed or affected by a particular thing

Etymology

From Latin 'resistent-', present participle of 'resistere' meaning 'to withstand.'

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02444201-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ rɪˈzɪstənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪstənt / (BrE)
disposed to or engaging in active opposition or defiance, especially against established authority
opposing or defiant of authority

Examples

The students became increasingly resistant to the new regulations imposed by the school administration.
Her resistant attitude often led to conflict with supervisors at work.
Some groups remain resistant to changes in government policy, staging protests and marches.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebellious - refusing to obey authority, rules, or tradition
  • Antonym: compliant - willing to agree with or obey rules or authority

Etymology

From Latin 'resistere' (to withstand, oppose) via Old French and Middle English.

4. ADJ

/ rɪˈzɪstənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪstənt / (BrE)
not allowing or affected by the penetration, absorption, or mixing of a specified substance or agent; unaffected by or impervious to something
not affected or penetrated by a substance

Examples

This raincoat is resistant to water, keeping you dry even in a heavy downpour.
The new phone case is highly resistant to scratches and stains.
Engineers developed a plastic that is resistant to both heat and chemicals.

Synset Examples

a water-repellent fabric
plastic highly resistant to steam and water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impervious - not allowing a substance to pass through or be affected by
  • Antonym: absorbent - able to soak up liquids easily

Etymology

From Latin 'resistens', present participle of 'resistere' (to resist).

5. ADJ

/ rɪˈzɪstənt / (AmE), / rɪˈzɪstənt / (BrE)
(of people, animals, or organisms) not affected by, or able to withstand, a disease, infection, drug, or other damaging agent.
not affected by disease, drug, or force

Examples

Some bacteria have become resistant to antibiotics, making infections harder to treat.
The new crop variety is resistant to drought and several common plant diseases.
People who are vaccinated are generally more resistant to flu viruses.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: susceptible - likely or liable to be influenced or harmed by a disease or agent
  • Synonym: immune - protected or exempt from a disease or the effects of something

Etymology

From Latin 'resistens', present participle of 'resistere' (to withstand, resist).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02372182-s
Synset POS: s
