to transaminate

1. VERB

/ trænˈsæmɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / trænˈsæmɪneɪt / (BrE)
(of a chemical group) to undergo transfer from one compound to another, especially an amino group in biochemistry.
to transfer a chemical group to another compound
medical

Examples

During metabolism, certain enzymes help amino acids transaminate to form new compounds.
Some drugs act by inhibiting proteins that transaminate amino groups in the liver.
The process by which glutamate can transaminate varies depending on cellular requirements.

Synset Examples

amino groups can transaminate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: transamination - the process of transferring an amino group from one molecule to another

Etymology

From 'trans-' (across) and 'aminate' (relating to amine groups); used in biochemical contexts since the early 20th century.

2. VERB

/ trænˈsæm.əˌneɪt / (AmE), / trænˈsæm.ɪ.neɪt / (BrE)
(of a chemical process) to transfer an amino group from one molecule to another, especially in biological reactions.
transfer an amino group between molecules
medical

Examples

Enzymes that transaminate amino acids are essential for cellular metabolism.
During the reaction, the compound will transaminate to form a new product.
Scientists observed that the liver can transaminate certain chemicals efficiently.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to deaminate - remove an amino group from a molecule

Etymology

From 'trans-' (across) and 'aminate' (to introduce an amino group), based on Latin roots.
