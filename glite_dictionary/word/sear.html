sear

1. VERB

/ sɪr / (AmE), / sɪə / (BrE)
to become superficially or partially burned or scorched, especially by intense heat
become superficially burned

Examples

The intense heat caused the surface of the bread to sear quickly.
A careless spark from the campfire made her sleeve sear at the edge.
When exposed to the open flame, the paper began to sear before catching fire.

Synset Examples

my eyebrows singed when I bent over the flames

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something slightly
  • Degree Higher: char - to burn something so that it is blackened

Etymology

From Old English *sēarian* (to wither, dry up).

2. VERB

/ sɪr / (AmE), / sɪə / (BrE)
to make something very hot and dry, often by applying intense heat quickly
make very hot and dry

Examples

The intense sun can sear the ground, leaving it cracked and lifeless.
Chefs sear the steak to lock in juices before roasting it.
A forest fire can sear entire landscapes, reducing greenery to ash.

Synset Examples

The heat scorched the countryside

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something with heat
  • Synonym: char - to burn and blacken by heat
  • Antonym: to moisten - to make something slightly wet

Etymology

From Old English 'searian' (to wither, dry up).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00378386-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ sɪr / (AmE), / sɪə / (BrE)
To cause something, such as a plant or surface, to become withered, dried out, or scorched by intense heat.
to wither or parch by heat

Examples

The relentless desert winds sear the grasslands every summer.
Drought and heat seared the once-lush fields into a barren wasteland.
If you leave the leaves in direct sun, the heat will quickly sear them.

Synset Examples

The sun parched the earth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something with heat
  • Synonym: to parch - to make or become very dry by heat

Etymology

From Old English *searian* (to be dry, wither).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00218756-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ sɪr / (AmE), / sɪə / (BrE)
to burn the surface of something quickly with intense heat, so that it becomes brown or black on the outside
burn the surface with intense heat

Examples

The chef used a hot pan to sear the steak and lock in the juices.
Be careful not to sear the vegetables for too long or they will become bitter.
I could smell the aroma as the grill began to sear the meat.

Synset Examples

The cook blackened the chicken breast
The fire charred the ceiling above the mantelpiece
the flames scorched the ceiling

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorch - to burn the surface of something with flame or heat
  • Synonym: char - to burn something so that it turns black and partly into charcoal

Etymology

From Old English *searian* (become dry or withered).

5. ADJ

/ sɪr / (AmE), / sɪə / (BrE)
(of plants or vegetation) dried up, withered, or scorched, often due to loss of moisture.
dried or withered (of plants)

Examples

The sear leaves rustled quietly in the autumn wind.
After weeks without rain, the fields were covered in sear grass.
He walked past sear branches that cracked beneath his feet.

Synset Examples

dried-up grass
the desert was edged with sere vegetation
shriveled leaves on the unwatered seedlings
withered vines

Related Terms

  • Synonym: withered - dried up, shriveled, or faded (of plants)
  • Antonym: lush - (of vegetation) rich and abundant (opposite sense)

Etymology

From Old English sēar (dry, withered).
