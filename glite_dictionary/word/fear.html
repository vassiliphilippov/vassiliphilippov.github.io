fear

1. NOUN

/ fɪr / (AmE), / fɪə / (BrE)
An unpleasant emotion caused by the belief that someone or something is dangerous, likely to cause pain, or a threat.
anxious or unpleasant emotion about danger or threat

Examples

She felt a sudden surge of fear when she heard the footsteps behind her.
His fear of public speaking made presentations very difficult for him.
The child's fear of the dark disappeared as he grew older.

Synset Examples

care had aged him
they hushed it up out of fear of public reaction

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anxiety - a feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease
  • Antonym: courage - the ability to do something that frightens one

Etymology

Old English 'fǣr' (sudden calamity, danger).

2. NOUN

/ fɪr / (AmE), / fɪə / (BrE)
A feeling of profound respect or awe, often mixed with apprehension, especially toward someone or something regarded as powerful.
profound respect or awe

Examples

His actions were guided by a deep fear of his ancestors' judgment.
The old traditions instilled a fear of breaking sacred customs.
There is a sense of fear and reverence in the way they approach the law.

Synset Examples

the Chinese reverence for the dead
the French treat food with gentle reverence
his respect for the law bordered on veneration

Related Terms

  • Synonym: veneration - great respect or reverence
  • Synonym: reverence - deep respect; regard with deep respect
  • Antonym: disrespect - lack of respect or courtesy

Etymology

From Old English *fǣr* (calamity, danger, sudden attack), related to *fēran* (to frighten).

5. NOUN

/ fɪr / (AmE), / fɪə / (BrE)
An unpleasant emotion caused by the belief that someone or something is dangerous, likely to cause pain, or a threat.
emotion of being afraid or anxious

Examples

She could not hide her fear as she walked through the dark alley.
His fear of heights stopped him from climbing the mountain.
The sound of thunder filled the children with fear during the storm.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: terror - intense, overpowering fear
  • Degree Lower: anxiety - feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease
  • Antonym: courage - ability to face fear or danger

Etymology

Old English 'fǣr' (sudden calamity, danger); related to Old High German 'fara' (ambush) and Old Norse 'færa' (to frighten).
