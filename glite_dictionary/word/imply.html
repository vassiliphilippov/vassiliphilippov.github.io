to imply

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈplaɪ / (AmE), / ɪmˈplaɪ / (BrE)
(In logic) to indicate or suggest something as a logically necessary consequence; to mean that something must be true if something else is true.
to suggest as a logical consequence

Examples

In this logical statement, 'p' implies 'q', meaning if 'p' is true, then 'q' must also be true.
Mathematically, if x > 2, this implies that x is also greater than 1.
The premise implies the conclusion by the rules of deductive logic.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to infer - to deduce or conclude from evidence and reasoning

Etymology

From Latin implicare (to entangle, involve).

2. VERB

/ ɪmˈplaɪ / (AmE), / ɪmˈplaɪ / (BrE)
to express or state something indirectly rather than explicitly
express indirectly

Examples

Her tone seemed to imply that something was wrong, even if she didn't say it outright.
Are you trying to imply that I made a mistake?
The article implies that the new policy may fail without directly saying so.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to suggest - to indicate or mention indirectly
  • Antonym: to infer - to deduce or conclude from evidence and reasoning rather than from explicit statements

Etymology

From Latin 'implicare' meaning 'to entwine, entangle, involve'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00931801-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ɪmˈplaɪ / (AmE), / ɪmˈplaɪ / (BrE)
to have as a necessary result or logical consequence
have as a logical consequence

Examples

Higher taxes imply less disposable income for most people.
Winning this match implies that the team will advance to the finals.
If safety is your main concern, that implies you are willing to pay more.

Synset Examples

The water shortage means that we have to stop taking long showers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: entail - to involve something as a necessary part or result
  • Synonym: to suggest - to indicate something indirectly or as a possible consequence
  • Antonym: to deny - to state that something is not true or does not apply (opposite in meaning)

Etymology

From Latin implicāre (to entangle, involve).

/ ɪmˈplaɪ / (AmE), / ɪmˈplʌɪ / (BrE)

By questioning her alibi, the detective was implying that she could be the culprit.

  • Antonym: to infer - to deduce or conclude from evidence and reasoning

involve as a necessary condition
