week

1. NOUN

/ wik / (AmE), / wiːk / (BrE)
the number of hours or days that someone works in a calendar week, regarded as a unit of work time
unit of work hours or days in a week

Examples

She prefers a shorter week with flexible hours.
Many employees work a standard 40-hour week in the office.
He recently switched to a part-time week to spend more time with his family.

Synset Examples

they worked a 40-hour week

Etymology

From Old English 'wice', from Proto-Germanic 'wikōn'.

2. NOUN

/ wik / (AmE), / wiːk / (BrE)
a period of seven consecutive days, usually from Sunday to Saturday or Monday to Sunday
period of seven days

Examples

I will see you in a week when you return from your trip.
The new restaurant opens next week, and I'm excited to try it.
We have been working on this project for a week straight.

Synset Examples

it rained for a week

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: fortnight - a period of two weeks, especially used in British English

Etymology

From Old English wice, from Latin vicis (change, succession), influenced by Old Norse vika (week, turn), originally meaning a turn or series of seven days.

3. NOUN

/ wiːk / (AmE), / wiːk / (BrE)
a period of seven consecutive days, typically considered to start on Sunday or Monday and ending on Saturday or Sunday
period of seven days

Examples

I go grocery shopping once a week.
She plans to visit her parents next week.
A standard work week in many countries lasts five days.

Etymology

From Old English *wice*, from Proto-Germanic *wikōn-, related to Latin *vigilia* (watch).
