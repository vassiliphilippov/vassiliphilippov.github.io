to spiritualise

1. VERB

/ ˈspɪrɪtʃuəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈspɪrɪtjʊəlaɪz / (BrE)
to give a spiritual meaning to something or interpret in a spiritual sense
interpret spiritually or give deeper meaning
british

Examples

She tried to spiritualise her daily struggles by finding deeper meaning in them.
Some theologians spiritualise biblical stories rather than interpreting them literally.
He often spiritualises historical events, seeking lessons about faith and the human soul.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to spiritualize - to give a spiritual meaning to something (American spelling)

Etymology

From Latin 'spiritus' (spirit), via French 'spiritualiser'.

Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈspɪrɪt͡ʃuəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈspɪrɪtʃʊəlaɪz / (BrE)
to make more spiritual in character or outlook; to purify from the influences of the material world
make more spiritual or purified

Examples

The time he spent meditating helped to spiritualise his everyday life.
Many believe that music can spiritualise the human spirit and lift it above worldly concerns.
She sought to spiritualise her outlook by practicing mindfulness and detachment from material things.

Synset Examples

During his stay at the ashram he was spiritualized

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to materialise - make material or worldly, to become real or concrete
  • Synonym: to purify - remove contaminants; make pure

Etymology

From spiritual + -ise, after Latin 'spiritualis' (spiritual).
