discursive

1. ADJ

/ dɪsˈkɜːrsɪv / (AmE), / dɪsˈkɜːsɪv / (BrE)
proceeding to a conclusion by means of logical reasoning or argument rather than intuition; analytical.
analytical, logical reasoning

Examples

The essay adopted a discursive approach, carefully analyzing each argument before drawing a conclusion.
Her discursive reasoning made the debate more structured and logical.
Unlike intuition, discursive thinking requires time and careful analysis of evidence.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intuitive - using instinct or immediate understanding, not reasoning

Etymology

From Medieval Latin discursus (a running about, discourse), from Latin discurrere (to run about).

2. ADJ

/ dɪsˈkɜrsɪv / (AmE), / dɪsˈkɜːsɪv / (BrE)
(of speech or writing) moving from topic to topic without strict order or coherence; rambling or digressive.
moving from topic to topic; rambling

Examples

The professor's discursive lectures often wandered far from the main topic.
Her discursive essay covered everything from art history to quantum mechanics.
He tended to give discursive answers that made it hard to follow his argument.

Synset Examples

amusingly digressive with satirical thrusts at women's fashions among other things
a rambling discursive book
his excursive remarks
a rambling speech about this and that

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rambling - (of speech or writing) lengthy and confused or inconsequential; similar meaning to discursive
  • Antonym: concise - giving a lot of information clearly and in a few words; opposite of discursive

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'discursivus', from Latin 'discursus' (running about).
