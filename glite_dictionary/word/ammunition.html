ammunition

1. NOUN

/ ˌæmjəˈnɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌamjʊˈnɪʃən / (BrE)
Objects such as bullets, shells, or missiles that are fired from guns or other weapons.
bullets and projectiles for firing from guns

Examples

The soldiers ran low on ammunition during the battle.
Hunting rifles require a different type of ammunition than shotguns.
He collected spent ammunition casings from the firing range.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: munition - military weapons, equipment, and supplies, particularly ammunition

Etymology

From French 'ammunition', earlier alteration of 'munition', from Latin 'munitio' (fortification).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæmjəˈnɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌæmjʊˈnɪʃən / (BrE)
nuclear, chemical, or biological material that can be used as a weapon of mass destruction.
material for weapons of mass destruction

Examples

The country was suspected of stockpiling ammunition capable of causing mass destruction.
International treaties aim to prevent the spread of ammunition such as chemical and nuclear weapons.
Inspectors searched the facility for any signs of biological ammunition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weapon - an object or device used to inflict harm or damage

Etymology

From French 'ammunition', earlier 'la munition', from Latin 'munire' (to fortify).

Synset POS: n

3. NOUN

/ ˌæm.jəˈnɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˌæm.jʊˈnɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
information or evidence used to support or defend an argument, or to attack someone else's argument
information used in argument or debate

Examples

The report gave the opposition plenty of ammunition to criticize the government’s handling of the crisis.
She refused to share details about her plan, not wanting to provide her rivals with ammunition.
His careless comment became ammunition for his detractors in the debate.

Synset Examples

his admission provided ammunition for his critics

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evidence - facts or information indicating whether a belief or proposition is true or valid

Etymology

From French *amunition*, a variant of *munition*, from Latin *munire* (to fortify).
