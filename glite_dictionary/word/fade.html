fade

1. VERB

/ feɪd / (AmE), / feɪd / (BrE)
to become less visible or distinct, or to disappear gradually
become less visible; disappear gradually

Examples

As the sun set, the landscape began to fade into darkness.
The colors on the old painting faded over decades of sunlight.
Her voice faded as she walked away down the long corridor.

Synset Examples

The scene begins to fade
The tree trunks are melting into the forest at dusk

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vanish - to disappear suddenly and completely
  • Synonym: to disappear - to cease to be visible

Etymology

From Old French 'fader' (to fade, become weak), from Vulgar Latin *fatidare.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00225077-v
Synset POS: v

2. NOUN

/ feɪd / (AmE), / feɪd / (BrE)
(in golf) a shot that curves slightly to the right for a right-handed player, as a result of sidespin.
golf shot curving right (right-handed)

Examples

He adjusted his swing to achieve a gentle fade instead of a straight shot.
Many professional golfers use a fade to control the placement of the ball on the fairway.
Her coach taught her how to hit a reliable fade when playing into the wind.

Synset Examples

he took lessons to cure his slicing

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: slice - a golf shot that curves sharply to the right for a right-handed player
  • Antonym: draw - a golf shot that curves to the left for a right-handed player
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00573080-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ feɪd / (AmE), / feɪd / (BrE)
to gradually lose strength, power, or effectiveness; to become feeble or weak
become weak or feeble

Examples

His energy seemed to fade as the illness progressed.
The old traditions have started to fade in this modern society.
She slowly faded away after years of poor health.

Synset Examples

The prisoner has been languishing for years in the dungeon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to weaken - to become or make weaker
  • Antonym: to strengthen - to make or become stronger

Etymology

From Old French 'fader' (to become weak), from Latin 'fatuus' (foolish, insipid).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00093345-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ feɪd / (AmE), / feɪd / (BrE)
to gradually disappear or become less noticeable, especially until nothing remains
disappear or become less visible gradually

Examples

The sound of the music faded as we walked away from the concert.
As the years passed, memories of his childhood slowly faded.
The words on the old sign have almost completely faded from view.

Synset Examples

The pain eventually passed off

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to vanish - to disappear suddenly and completely
  • Synonym: to disappear - to cease to be visible or present
  • Antonym: to appear - to come into sight or become visible

Etymology

From Latin 'fatuare' (to fade, make foolish) via Old French 'fader' (become weak).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00422644-v
Synset POS: v

5. VERB

/ feɪd / (AmE), / feɪd / (BrE)
to gradually lose freshness, strength, or vitality
lose freshness or vitality

Examples

Over time, the brilliant color of the painting began to fade.
As his energy started to fade, he found it hard to finish the race.
The enthusiasm for the new policy didn't fade as quickly as expected.

Synset Examples

Her bloom was fading

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wilt - to become limp or droop, usually due to lack of water or vitality
  • Synonym: to wither - to dry up and lose freshness, often referring to plants
  • Antonym: to revive - to become strong or lively again after fading or declining

Etymology

From Latin 'fatuare' (to become weak), via Old French 'fader'.

Source: WordNet
