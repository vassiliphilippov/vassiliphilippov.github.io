throwback

1. NOUN

/ ˈθroʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈθrəʊˌbæk / (BrE)
an individual or organism that displays characteristics of a more primitive or ancestral form, especially one that appears after several generations of absence
an organism showing ancestral traits

Examples

The bird's unusual features made researchers suspect it was a throwback to an earlier evolutionary stage.
Some consider the platypus a living throwback because of its primitive reptilian traits.
Genetic mutations can sometimes result in a throwback with characteristics absent for many generations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atavism - the reappearance of ancestral characteristics

Etymology

From the verb 'throw' + 'back', originally used in the sense of reverting to a previous type.

2. NOUN

/ ˈθroʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈθrəʊˌbæk / (BrE)
a person or thing that shows characteristics or features of an earlier time or ancestor; a reversion to an earlier type or style
reappearance of an earlier characteristic

Examples

Her fashion sense is a true throwback to the 1970s.
The rare animal is considered a genetic throwback to its ancient ancestors.
That band's music is a throwback to the classic rock era.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reversion - a return to a previous condition, state, belief, or practice

Etymology

Formed from the verb phrase 'throw back', originally referring to returning or reverting to a former condition.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-07358174-n
Synset POS: n

3. ADJ

/ ˈθroʊˌbæk / (AmE), / ˈθrəʊˌbæk / (BrE)
(of a person or thing) resembling an earlier type or displaying characteristics from a previous time; atavistic.
resembling an earlier type or era

Examples

Her fashion style is a true throwback to the 1970s.
The building has a throwback charm reminiscent of Victorian architecture.
His manners are a throwback to a more formal era.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atavistic - relating to or characterized by reversion to something ancient or ancestral

Etymology

From the verb 'throw back', referring to reversion to a former state or type; first used in late 19th century.
