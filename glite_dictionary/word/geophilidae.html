Geophilidae

1. NOUN

/ dʒiːˈɑːfɪlɪˌdiː / (AmE), / dʒiːˈɒfɪlɪˌdiː / (BrE)
A family of small, extremely elongated centipedes typically found living in soil or leaf litter.
family of soil-dwelling centipedes

Examples

Members of the Geophilidae family are often found burrowing deep within garden soil.
The scientist specialized in classifying various species of Geophilidae from around the world.
Many Geophilidae have bodies adapted to squeezing through narrow spaces underground.

Etymology

From New Latin, from Greek 'geo-' (earth) + 'philos' (loving), referring to earth-dwelling habits.

2. NOUN

/ dʒiˌɑːfɪˈlɪdi / (AmE), / dʒiːˌɒfɪˈlɪdiː / (BrE)
a family of small, extremely elongate centipedes that live in soil or earth, characterized by numerous leg pairs and a burrowing lifestyle
a family of elongate, soil-dwelling centipedes

Examples

The Geophilidae family includes centipedes with more than 30 pairs of legs.
Scientists discovered a new species belonging to the Geophilidae in underground caves.
Members of Geophilidae are adapted for burrowing through loose soil.

Etymology

From Greek 'geō' (earth) + 'philos' (loving), referring to earth-loving habits.
