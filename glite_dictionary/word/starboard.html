starboard

1. ADJ

/ ˈstɑːrˌbɔrd / (AmE), / ˈstɑːbəd / (BrE)
relating to or located on the right-hand side of a ship or aircraft when facing forward
right side of a ship or aircraft

Examples

Every passenger must keep clear of the starboard lifeboat during the drill.
The cargo was stowed to the starboard side of the vessel.
From the bridge, you can see the coastline to starboard as we approach the harbor.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: port - the left side of a ship or aircraft when facing forward

Etymology

From Old English 'steorbord', meaning the side on which the ship is steered.

2. NOUN

/ ˈstɑːrˌbɔrd / (AmE), / ˈstɑːbəd / (BrE)
the right side of a ship or aircraft when facing forward toward the bow or nose
right side of a ship or aircraft

Examples

The captain ordered the crew to shift the cargo to starboard to balance the ship.
Please keep clear of the starboard side during docking procedures.
When the whale appeared off the starboard bow, everyone rushed to the rail.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: port - the left side of a ship or aircraft when facing the bow or nose

Etymology

From Old English 'steorbord', meaning the side on which a ship is steered.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-04310810-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈstɑːrˌbɔrd / (AmE), / ˈstɑːbəd / (BrE)
(of a ship's helm or rudder) to turn or steer to the right (the starboard side).
to turn a ship or rudder to the right

Examples

The captain ordered the helmsman to starboard as the ship approached the harbor entrance.
You must starboard quickly to avoid that floating debris.
He starboarded the rudder at the signal from the bridge.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: port - to turn a ship or rudder to the left (the port side)

Etymology

From Old English 'steorbord', literally ‘steer-board’ side; the verb derives from the noun.
