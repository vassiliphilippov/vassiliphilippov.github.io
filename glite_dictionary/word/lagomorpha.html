Lagomorpha

1. NOUN

/ ˌlæɡəˈmɔrfə / (AmE), / ˌlæɡəˈmɔːfə / (BrE)
an order of mammals comprising rabbits, hares, and pikas, characterized by two pairs of upper incisors; formerly grouped with rodents
order of mammals: rabbits, hares, pikas

Examples

The order Lagomorpha includes familiar animals such as rabbits and hares.
Scientists debated whether Lagomorpha should be classified separately from rodents.
Both pikas and hares belong to the Lagomorpha order.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Rodentia - the order of rodents

Etymology

From modern Latin, from Greek 'lagōs' (hare) + 'morphē' (form).

2. NOUN

/ ˌlæɡəˈmɔrfə / (AmE), / ˌlæɡəˈmɔːfə / (BrE)
a mammalian order that includes rabbits, hares, and pikas, formerly classified as a suborder of rodents
order of mammals: rabbits, hares, and pikas

Examples

Scientists studying mammals often focus on the Lagomorpha because of their unique dental structure.
Rabbits and hares both belong to the order Lagomorpha.
The Lagomorpha are distinct from rodents, despite some similarities.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: Rodentia - order of mammals including rodents such as mice, rats, squirrels

Etymology

From Greek 'lagōs' (hare) + 'morphē' (form).
