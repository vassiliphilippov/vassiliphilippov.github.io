Manichean

1. ADJ

/ ˌmænɪˈkiːən / (AmE), / ˌmænɪˈkiːən / (BrE)
Relating to Manichaeism, an ancient dualistic religious system dividing the world into forces of good and evil.
of or relating to Manichaeism

Examples

The novel presents a Manichean conflict between light and darkness.
Some philosophers reject the Manichean division of morality into absolute good and evil.
Historians debate whether the empire's attitude toward heresy was truly Manichean in nature.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dualistic - characterized by two opposing principles

Etymology

From Late Latin Manichaeus (follower of Mani, the Persian founder of Manichaeism) + -an.

2. NOUN

/ ˌmænɪˈkiːən / (AmE), / ˌmanɪˈkiːən / (BrE)
An adherent of Manichaeism, a dualistic religious philosophy founded in the 3rd century AD by the prophet Mani.
follower of Manichaeism

Examples

Augustine was a Manichean before converting to Christianity.
The beliefs of a Manichean are based on the eternal struggle between good and evil.
Historians have found evidence of Manichean communities along the Silk Road.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Manichaeism - a dualistic religious philosophy founded by Mani

Etymology

From Latin Manichaeus, from Greek Manichaios, from Mani (the Persian founder of Manichaeism).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-10310474-n
Synset POS: n
