Cynoscephalae

1. PROPN

/ saɪˌnɒsəˈfiːli / (AmE), / sɪˌnɒsɪˈkiːfəliː / (BrE)
the site of a decisive battle in 197 BC, where Roman forces defeated Philip V of Macedon, ending the Second Macedonian War and ending Macedonian dominance in Greece.
site of major Roman victory over Macedon, 197 BC

Examples

The Battle of Cynoscephalae marked a turning point in ancient Greek history.
Roman tactics at Cynoscephalae were instrumental in their decisive victory over Macedon.
Historians often study Cynoscephalae to understand the rise of Roman power in Greece.

Etymology

From Greek Κυνὸς κεφαλαί (Kynos kephalai), meaning 'dog's heads', the name of the hills where the battle occurred.

2. PROPN

/ saɪˌnɒsəˈfɑːli / (AmE), / saɪˌnɒsɪˈfiːliː / (BrE)
the fields in Thessaly, Greece, where the Romans defeated the Macedonians in 197 BC, marking a decisive battle in ancient history
site of a key ancient battle in Greece

Examples

The Battle of Cynoscephalae marked the end of Macedonian dominance in Greece.
Historians often cite Cynoscephalae as a turning point in Roman military history.
During his lecture, the professor explained the tactics used at Cynoscephalae.

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Κυνὸς κεφαλαί (Kynos kephalai), meaning 'dog's heads,' referring to the shape of the hills.
