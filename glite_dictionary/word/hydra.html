Hydra

1. PROPN

/ ˈhaɪdrə / (AmE), / ˈhʌɪdrə / (BrE)
a large and faint constellation in the southern sky, located near the celestial equator between Virgo and Cancer
large constellation in the southern sky

Examples

The star Alphard marks the brightest point in the constellation Hydra.
Astronomers often observe Hydra to study its unique arrangement of stars.
Hydra stretches across a vast area of the night sky between Virgo and Cancer.

Etymology

From Latin Hydra, from Greek Hydra, a mythical water serpent.

2. PROPN

/ ˈhaɪdrə / (AmE), / ˈhaɪdrə / (BrE)
In Greek mythology, a serpent-like monster with nine heads; when one head was cut off, two more grew in its place. It was ultimately slain by Hercules.
mythical many-headed serpent, slain by Hercules

Examples

Many legends tell of Hercules battling the fearsome Hydra in the swamps of Lerna.
The Hydra grew two new heads each time one was cut off, making it a nearly invincible foe.
In classical art, the hero is often shown locked in fierce combat with the monstrous Hydra.

Synset Examples

Hydra was slain by Hercules

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Chimera - mythical fire-breathing creature in Greek mythology
  • Synonym: Cerberus - three-headed dog from Greek mythology guarding the underworld

Etymology

From Ancient Greek Ὕδρα (Hydra), meaning 'water serpent.'

3. NOUN

/ ˈhaɪdrə / (AmE), / ˈhʌɪdrə / (BrE)
A genus of small, freshwater animals with tentacles, known for their regenerative ability, belonging to the phylum Cnidaria.
genus of small freshwater animals

Examples

Scientists observed the Hydra under a microscope to study its regenerative abilities.
A single Hydra can reproduce asexually by budding off new individuals.
The Hydra extends its tentacles to capture tiny prey in the water.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hydra - any animal of the genus Hydra

Etymology

From Latin Hydra, from Greek húdra (water serpent).
