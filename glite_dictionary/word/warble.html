warble

1. NOUN

/ ˈwɔrbəl / (AmE), / ˈwɔːbəl / (BrE)
A lumpy swelling or abscess under the skin of domestic mammals, caused by the larvae of a botfly or warble fly.
swelling in animals caused by botfly larvae
medical

Examples

The veterinarian examined the cow and discovered a large warble on its back.
Each spring, farmers check their cattle for signs of warbles.
Treatment is necessary to prevent complications caused by the presence of a warble under the skin.

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *werbel*, meaning 'knob' or 'lump', also related to Old High German *werbil* (wart).

2. VERB

/ ˈwɑrbəl / (AmE), / ˈwɑːbəl / (BrE)
to sing or play with melodic trills or quavering notes, often alternating between pitches
sing or play with trills or quavers

Examples

The bird began to warble joyfully as the sun rose over the meadow.
She loves to warble her favorite tune with playful trills.
During the concert, the flutist skillfully warbled each note.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trill - rapid alternation between two adjacent musical notes
  • Synonym: whistle - to produce a clear, high-pitched sound by forcing air through the lips

Etymology

From Old French *werbler* (to trill), probably of Frankish origin.

3. VERB

/ ˈwɑrbəl / (AmE), / ˈwɔːbəl / (BrE)
to sing with a series of rapidly changing notes, often moving smoothly or quickly between pitches, sometimes resembling yodeling
sing with trills or rapid note changes

Examples

She began to warble a joyful tune as she walked through the garden.
The bird warbled high in the branches, delighting everyone nearby.
He loves to warble popular songs in the shower, hitting all the playful notes.

Synset Examples

The Austrians were yodeling in the mountains

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yodel - to sing by rapidly alternating between normal voice and falsetto

From Old French *werbler* (to trill), likely of Frankish origin.
