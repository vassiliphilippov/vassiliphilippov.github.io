to predestine

1. VERB

/ priˈdɛstɪn / (AmE), / priːˈdɛstɪn / (BrE)
to decide or determine something in advance, especially by divine will or fate
to determine in advance by fate or will

Examples

Many theologians believe that God has predestined certain events in history.
They felt their meeting was predestined rather than the result of chance.
The church teaches that some souls are predestined for salvation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foreordain - to determine or decree beforehand
  • Synonym: predestined - having been determined in advance

Etymology

From Latin praedestinare, from prae- (before) + destinare (to determine).

2. VERB

/ priˈdɛstɪn / (AmE), / priˈdɛstɪn / (BrE)
to decide or determine an outcome or event in advance, especially by divine will or fate
to determine (something) in advance

Examples

Many believe that certain events are predestined and cannot be changed by human action.
Some theologians argue that God predestines each person's fate before they are born.
The outcome seemed almost predestined from the very start of the competition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foreordain - to appoint or decree something beforehand
  • Synonym: to predetermine - to decide in advance

Etymology

From Late Latin praedestinare, from Latin prae- (before) + destinare (to determine).

3. VERB

to decide or determine (an outcome or course of events) in advance, especially by divine will or fate

Examples

Predestine is a concept often discussed in religious philosophy.
Some people believe that our paths are predestined and cannot be changed.
The king felt it was his destiny, predestined by a higher power, to rule the land.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to foreordain - to appoint or decide in advance, especially by divine will

Etymology

From Latin praedestinare 'to predetermine', from prae- 'before' + destinare 'to determine'.
