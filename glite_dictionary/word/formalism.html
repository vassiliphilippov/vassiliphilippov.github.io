formalism

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrməˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməlɪzəm / (BrE)
(Philosophy) The theory that formal statements, such as those in logic or mathematics, lack intrinsic meaning, and their symbols serve mainly to demonstrate useful forms or structures.
theory that formal statements lack intrinsic meaning

Examples

Some philosophers adhere to formalism, arguing that mathematical statements are just manipulations of symbols without meaning.
In discussions of logic, formalism opposes views that ascribe inherent meaning to mathematical language.
The debate between intuitionism and formalism shaped much of twentieth-century mathematical philosophy.

Etymology

From Latin 'formalis' (of form) + '-ism' (denoting a system, principle, or ideological movement).

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrməˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməlɪzəm / (BrE)
The belief or doctrine that it is the formal structure, rather than the content, that should be emphasized or represented, especially in art, literature, or philosophy.
emphasis on formal structure over content

Examples

The school of Russian formalism argued that the structure of language is more important than its content.
In art criticism, formalism values composition and technique above the message of the work.
Some mathematicians are drawn to formalism, focusing strictly on systematic processes and logical structure.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: content - the subject matter or substance, as opposed to form

Etymology

From French formalisme, from Latin forma (form) + -ism.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrməˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈfɔːməˌlɪzəm / (BrE)
strict or excessive adherence to prescribed forms, rules, or conventions, especially in art, religion, or conduct.
strict adherence to prescribed forms or rules

Examples

His approach to teaching was marked by a rigid formalism that left little room for creativity.
Some critics argue that legal formalism can hinder justice by prioritizing process over fairness.
The ceremony was conducted with such formalism that it felt disconnected from its original purpose.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rigorism - the strict application of principles or rules
  • Antonym: spontaneity - behavior or actions arising from natural impulse, not rigid adherence to form

Etymology

From French formalisme and Medieval Latin formalis (pertaining to form).
