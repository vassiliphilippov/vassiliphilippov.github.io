nullification

1. NOUN

/ ˌnʌlɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnʌlɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of making something legally null, void, or without effect; the action of counteracting or invalidating something
the act of making null or void

Examples

The court's decision resulted in the nullification of the restrictive law.
Some states attempted to assert their power through the nullification of federal legislation.
The treaty called for the nullification of previous agreements between the countries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: invalidation - the act of making something invalid
  • Synonym: cancellation - the act of cancelling or making void
  • Antonym: enactment - the act of making something into law (contrasting action)

Etymology

From Late Latin nullificatio, from nullificare ('to make nothing').

2. NOUN

/ ˌnʌlɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌnʌlɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
(in U.S. history) the doctrine that a state has the right to nullify, or invalidate, any federal law which that state has deemed unconstitutional with respect to the United States Constitution.
states' rights doctrine to invalidate federal law
legal american

Examples

The doctrine of nullification was championed by some Southern states in the early nineteenth century.
The crisis surrounding nullification nearly led to armed conflict between South Carolina and the federal government.
Modern legal scholars generally reject nullification as incompatible with the Constitution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interposition - a related doctrine where a state intervenes between its citizens and the federal government
  • Antonym: supremacy clause - the constitutional principle giving federal law precedence over state law

Etymology

From Latin 'nullificare' (to make nothing) + English '-tion'.
