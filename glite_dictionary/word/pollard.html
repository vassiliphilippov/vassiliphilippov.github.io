pollard

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːlɚd / (AmE), / ˈpɒləd / (BrE)
an animal, typically one with horns, that has had its horns removed or has shed them
animal with removed or shed horns

Examples

The farmer separated the pollard from the rest of the cattle for health reasons.
In the herd, one pollard stood out, lacking the horns of its peers.
Removing horns turns the animal into a pollard, which can reduce injuries among livestock.

Etymology

From Middle English *polle* (head), referring to the cutting or removal of parts of the head.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːlərd / (AmE), / ˈpɒlɑːd / (BrE)
a tree with its upper branches cut back regularly to promote a denser, bushy growth of foliage
tree with upper branches cut back

Examples

The ancient pollard by the river provides a home for many birds.
After years of regular cutting, the oak became a dense pollard.
Gardeners decided to make the willow a pollard to encourage more foliage.

Etymology

From Middle English *pol*, poll (head) + -ard, referring to a tree with its 'head' or top lopped off.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-13129082-n

3. VERB

/ ˈpɑːlərd / (AmE), / ˈpɒləd / (BrE)
to cut the upper branches of (a tree) so as to encourage new growth and produce a dense head of foliage; to convert (a tree) into a pollard
cut upper branches of a tree

Examples

The gardeners pollarded the willow trees to encourage new growth.
Some trees along the avenue are regularly pollarded to maintain their shape.
Learning how to pollard properly is important for tree health.

Synset Examples

pollard trees

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prune - to trim a tree, shrub, or bush by cutting away dead or overgrown branches
  • Synonym: to lop - cut off the branches or twigs of a tree or bush

Etymology

From Middle English 'polle' (the head) + -ard, originally referring to an animal that had its horns cut off, later applied to trees.
