strait

1. NOUN

/ streɪt / (AmE), / streɪt / (BrE)
A narrow passage of water connecting two larger bodies of water.
narrow sea channel between two areas

Examples

The ships navigated through the dangerous strait between the islands.
The Bering Strait separates Russia and Alaska.
They sailed across the strait at dawn, watching the sun rise over the water.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: isthmus - a narrow strip of land connecting two larger land areas, usually with water on either side

Etymology

From Old French *estreit* (narrow), from Latin *strictus* (drawn tight).

2. ADJ

/ streɪt / (AmE), / streɪt / (BrE)
narrow; of limited width or space, especially used in older or formal contexts.
narrow; of limited width
archaic

Examples

The strait passage was difficult for even the slimmest person to squeeze through.
In that ancient city, many streets are strait and winding, barely wide enough for a cart.
'Strait is the gate, and narrow is the way, which leadeth unto life,' the preacher quoted.

Synset Examples

strait is the gate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: narrow - small in width
  • Antonym: broad - having a great width

Etymology

From Old French *estreit* (narrow, tight), from Latin *strictus* (drawn tight).

3. NOUN

/ streɪt / (AmE), / streɪt / (BrE)
a difficult or distressing situation, especially one involving hardship or trouble
a difficult or troubled situation

Examples

After losing his job, he found himself in financial straits for several months.
Their company is in desperate straits after a series of bad investments.
The country was in dire straits following years of drought.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: predicament - a difficult, unpleasant, or embarrassing situation
  • Synonym: difficulty - a state or condition of having problems
  • Synonym: trouble - a situation in which you experience problems

Etymology

From Old French *estreit* (narrow), from Latin *strictus* (drawn tight, narrow).
