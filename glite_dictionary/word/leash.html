leash

1. NOUN

/ liːʃ / (AmE), / liːʃ / (BrE)
control or restraint imposed on someone or something, often used figuratively to describe the extent of freedom allowed.
figurative restraint or control

Examples

The project manager kept a tight leash on the team's spending to avoid overruns.
As the children grew older, their parents gave them a longer leash to make their own decisions.
The new regulations put a leash on risky financial practices in the industry.

Synset Examples

asked for a collar on program trading in the stock market
kept a tight leash on his emotions
he's always gotten a long leash

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restraint - the action of keeping someone or something under control
  • Antonym: freedom - the power or right to act, speak, or think as one wants

Etymology

From Old French *lais* (strap, thong), from *laisser* (to let).

2. NOUN

/ liːʃ / (AmE), / liːʃ / (BrE)
A long, thin piece of rope, chain, or cord used for holding and controlling an animal.
rope or cord to restrain an animal

Examples

She held the dog tightly by its leash as they crossed the busy street.
Please put your puppy on a leash before taking him outside.
The park requires all pets to be kept on a leash at all times.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lead - a strap or cord for leading an animal by the collar (chiefly British)

Etymology

From Old French *lesse* (a leash or snare), from Latin *laqueus* (noose, snare).
