augur

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈɔːɡə / (BrE)
In ancient Rome, a religious official who interpreted omens to guide public policy or decision-making.
Roman religious official interpreting omens

Examples

In ancient Rome, an augur would study the flight of birds to predict the outcome of important events.
The senator consulted an augur before making his final decision on the new law.
Historical records describe the vital role played by the augur in Roman ceremonies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soothsayer - a person supposed to be able to foresee the future
  • Synonym: oracle - a priest or priestess through whom advice or prophecy was sought in ancient times

Etymology

From Latin augur (an official diviner in ancient Rome).

2. VERB

/ ˈɔɡər / (AmE), / ˈɔːɡə / (BrE)
to be a sign that something will happen in the future, often as an omen or prediction
to predict or be a sign of the future

Examples

Heavy clouds augur a storm is on the way.
The early success did not augur well for the rest of the project.
Such kindness may augur better relations between the two countries.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bode - to be an omen of a particular outcome
  • Synonym: to portend - to be a sign or warning of something to come

Etymology

From Latin 'augurare' (to foretell, to be an omen).

3. VERB

/ ˈɔɡər / (AmE), / ˈɔːɡə / (BrE)
to suggest or indicate a future outcome, often through signs or omens; to predict or foretell, especially based on omens
to predict or foretell (esp. from omens)

Examples

The dark clouds augur a coming storm.
Strong sales augur well for the company's future.
Some believe that dreams augur important events in one's life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to predict - to say what will happen in the future
  • Synonym: to foretell - to predict something before it happens

Etymology

From Latin 'augurare' (to predict, to foretell by augury).
