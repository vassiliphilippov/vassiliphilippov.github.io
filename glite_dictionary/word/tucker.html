tucker

1. VERB

/ ˈtʌkɚ / (AmE), / ˈtʌkə / (BrE)
(informal) To make someone very tired; to exhaust someone completely.
to tire out completely
informal

Examples

That long hike really tuckered me out.
By the end of the day, the children were completely tuckered.
This heat will tucker you if you’re not careful.

Synset Examples

This kind of work exhausts me
I'm beat
He was all washed up after the exam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exhaust - to make someone very tired
  • Antonym: to refresh - to make someone feel less tired or more energetic

Etymology

Probably from British dialect 'tucker' meaning 'to torment', of uncertain origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtʌkər / (AmE), / ˈtʌkə / (BrE)
a detachable yoke of linen or lace worn over the breast of a low-cut dress, especially in the 17th to 19th centuries
detachable garment worn over breast
archaic

Examples

The museum displayed a delicate tucker from the Victorian era, trimmed with fine lace.
She wore a silk tucker to add modesty to her evening gown.
Fashion in the 18th century often required a tucker as part of women’s formal attire.

Etymology

From the verb 'to tuck,' originally referring to something tucked or pleated.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: n
