to move in on

1. VERB

/ muːv ɪn ɑn / (AmE), / muːv ɪn ɒn / (BrE)
to become involved in a situation, often in order to take control of it, usually without being invited
to intervene in order to control or dominate

Examples

The rival gang tried to move in on their territory after the leader was arrested.
Big corporations often move in on small markets once they've proven successful.
After the founder retired, new management quickly moved in on the company.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to take over - to assume control of something, especially by force or without invitation
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove oneself or pull back from involvement or control
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02445779-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ muːv ɪn ɑn / (AmE), / muːv ɪn ɒn / (BrE)
to make unwelcome or intrusive advances towards someone, often in a romantic or competitive way
make intrusive advances towards

Examples

He tried to move in on her at the party, but she wasn't interested.
After I expressed my idea, my colleague began to move in on the project.
She was annoyed when he attempted to move in on her conversation with the director.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to hit on - to make sexual advances towards someone
  • Antonym: to back off - to withdraw or stop making advances
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02023027-v
Synset POS: v
