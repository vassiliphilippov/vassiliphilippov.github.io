to kick in

1. VERB

/ kɪk ɪn / (AmE), / kɪk ɪn / (BrE)
to force something open with a vigorous kick, especially a door or barrier
force open by kicking, especially a door

Examples

The police had to kick in the door to rescue the trapped family.
He managed to kick in the window when he lost his keys.
Protesters attempted to kick in the locked gate during the demonstration.

Synset Examples

kick in the doors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to break down - to forcibly open or destroy something, especially a door or barrier

2. VERB

/ kɪk ɪn / (AmE), / kɪk ɪn / (BrE)
to begin to have an effect or to take effect, especially after a delay
begin to take effect

Examples

After a few minutes, the pain medication finally kicked in and she began to feel better.
When the effects of caffeine kick in, he usually feels more alert and focused.
You might not notice a difference at first, but the new policy will kick in next month.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to set in - to begin and seem likely to continue (e.g. a period or feeling)
  • Antonym: to wear off - to diminish gradually so as to have less effect

/ kɪk ɪn / (AmE), / kɪk ɪn / (BrE)
to contribute money or resources to a cause, project, or shared effort
contribute money or resources
informal

Examples

If everyone kicks in a few dollars, we can buy a nice gift.
We all kicked in to help cover the cost of the new equipment.
She promised to kick in some money for the fundraiser.

4. VERB

/ kɪk ɪn / (AmE), / kɪk ɪn / (BrE)
to begin to have an effect or start working, often suddenly or at a specific time
begin to take effect; start working

Examples

The painkillers usually kick in about twenty minutes after you take them.
When the alarm system kicked in, the lights started flashing and a siren sounded.
It took a moment for her confidence to kick in before she began her speech.
