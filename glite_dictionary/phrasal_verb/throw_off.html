to throw off

1. VERB

/ θroʊ ˈɔf / (AmE), / θrəʊ ˈɒf / (BrE)
to remove something quickly or carelessly, especially an article of clothing or a quality/feature
remove quickly (esp. clothing or quality)

Examples

She rushed in and threw off her wet jacket by the door.
He struggled to throw off his old reputation as unreliable.
As soon as the race was over, the athlete threw off his running shoes.

Synset Examples

he shed his image as a pushy boss
shed your clothes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to take off - to remove an article of clothing
  • Antonym: to put on - to place an article of clothing on one's body
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01516062-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ θroʊ ˈɔf / (AmE), / θrəʊ ˈɒf / (BrE)
to get rid of someone or something that is pursuing, restricting, or affecting you
get rid of a pursuer or inconvenience

Examples

The suspect tried to throw off the police by taking several turns through back alleys.
Despite her efforts, she couldn't throw off the flu for weeks.
He accelerated suddenly to throw off the cyclists who were following closely behind.

Synset Examples

I couldn't shake the car that was following me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to shake off - to get rid of someone or something, especially a pursuer or annoyance
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02077473-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ θroʊ ɔf / (AmE), / θrəʊ ɒf / (BrE)
to cause confusion or disruption in someone’s plans or schedule
disrupt or confuse plans or routine

Examples

The sudden noise threw off the speaker’s concentration during his presentation.
Her travel plans were thrown off by a delayed flight.
Changing the meeting time threw off my entire afternoon schedule.

Synset Examples

The unexpected rain threw off our picnic plans.
She tends to throw off her schedule with last-minute plans.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to disrupt - to interrupt the normal progress of something
  • Synonym: to confuse - to make someone unable to think clearly or understand
