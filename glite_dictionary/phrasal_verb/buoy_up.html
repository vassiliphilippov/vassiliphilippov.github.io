to buoy up

1. VERB

/ ˈbu.i ʌp / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪ ʌp / (BrE)
to make someone feel more cheerful or confident
make someone more cheerful or confident

Examples

Her kind words helped to buoy up my spirits during a tough time.
The team's enthusiasm really buoyed up the atmosphere in the office.
A little praise can buoy up even the most discouraged student.

Synset Examples

the conversation lightened me up a bit

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cheer up - to make happier or more cheerful
Source: WordNet

2. VERB

/ ˈbɔɪ ʌp / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪ ʌp / (BrE)
to keep something or someone afloat on the surface of a liquid; to support so that it does not sink
to keep afloat; support on a liquid

Examples

The life vest buoyed up the child in the water.
Special foam inserts buoy up the boat when it's heavily loaded.
Waves buoyed up the debris for several days before it washed ashore.

Synset Examples

The life vest buoyed him up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: float - to rest or move on the surface of a liquid without sinking
  • Antonym: sink - to go down below the surface of a liquid

Etymology

From the noun 'buoy', meaning a floating object, and the adverb 'up'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01221076-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈbɔɪ ʌp / (AmE), / ˈbɔɪ ʌp / (BrE)
to become or make someone become more cheerful or confident
become more cheerful

Examples

The good news helped to buoy up her spirits after a difficult week.
A sense of optimism began to buoy up the whole team before the final match.
He was feeling low, but the laughter of his friends started to buoy up his mood.

Synset Examples

after a glass of wine, he lightened up a bit

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cheer up - become happier or make someone happier

Etymology

From 'buoy', meaning to keep afloat, originally from Old French boue (float).
