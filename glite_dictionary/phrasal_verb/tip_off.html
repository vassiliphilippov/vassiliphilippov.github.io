to tip off

1. VERB

/ tɪp ɔf / (AmE), / tɪp ɒf / (BrE)
to give secret or confidential information, especially as a warning or alert, often to the authorities.
give a secret warning or information

Examples

Someone tipped off the police before the robbery took place.
She tipped off her friend about the surprise inspection at work.
Authorities were tipped off to the illegal activity by an anonymous caller.

Synset Examples

He tipped off the police about the terrorist plot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to warn - to inform someone in advance about possible danger or trouble
  • Antonym: to betray - to reveal information that was meant to be secret or confidential

2. VERB

/ tɪp ɔf / (AmE), / tɪp ɒf / (BrE)
to give someone a secret or confidential warning or piece of information, especially to alert them of possible danger or an opportunity
secretly warn or inform someone

Examples

The police were able to act quickly because someone tipped off the authorities about the plan.
She tipped off her friend that the surprise inspection would happen the next day.
An anonymous caller tipped off the reporter about the scandal.

Synset Examples

I can't tip you off about the surprise party.
She received a tip off about the stock market changes.
She acted on a tip-off regarding the stock market.
The police received a tip-off about the robbery.
I got a tip-off about the new cafe opening soon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to warn - to tell someone about possible danger or problems
  • Synonym: alert - to notify or warn of potential trouble or opportunity

3. VERB

/ tɪp ɔf / (AmE), / tɪp ɒf / (BrE)
to give someone a secret warning or advance information about something, especially so they can take action or avoid trouble
to give secret advance warning

Examples

The police were tipped off about the robbery ahead of time.
Someone tipped off the manager that there would be an inspection.
He was tipped off about the upcoming sale.

Synset Examples

He was tipped off about the upcoming sale.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to warn - to inform someone in advance of possible danger or problem
  • Antonym: to betray - to reveal information about someone to their disadvantage

Etymology

From the verb 'tip' (to give information) and 'off' (indicating separation or alert), possibly originating in underworld slang in the late 19th century.
