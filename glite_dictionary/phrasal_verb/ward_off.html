to ward off

1. VERB

/ wɔrd ɔf / (AmE), / wɔːd ɒf / (BrE)
to prevent something unpleasant or harmful from affecting you by taking action; to repel or avert
prevent or repel something unpleasant

Examples

She wore a charm to ward off evil spirits.
Doctors recommend getting vaccinated to ward off the flu.
The villagers lit fires to ward off wild animals.

Synset Examples

Ward off danger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fend off - to defend oneself against (an attack or attacker)
  • Synonym: to deflect - to cause something to change direction; to prevent something from being directed at you

Etymology

From 'ward' meaning to guard or protect, plus 'off' indicating distance or repulsion.

2. VERB

/ wɔrd ɔf / (AmE), / wɔːd ɒf / (BrE)
to prevent something unpleasant or dangerous from affecting you; to keep something away
prevent or keep away (something undesirable)

Examples

Some believe garlic can ward off evil spirits.

3. VERB

/ wɔrd ɑf / (AmE), / wɔːd ɒf / (BrE)
to prevent something or someone from approaching, attacking, or affecting you
prevent from approaching or affecting

Examples

She wore a bracelet of garlic to ward off evil spirits.
Extra security cameras were installed to ward off potential thieves.
Vitamin C can help ward off colds during the winter.

Synset Examples

We used bug spray to ward off the midgies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to fend off - to defend oneself against or repel something
  • Antonym: invite - to welcome or encourage something to come or happen

Etymology

From Middle English 'ward' (to guard or protect), combined with 'off' (away).
