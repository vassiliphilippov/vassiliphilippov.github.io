to suck in

1. VERB

/ sʌk ɪn / (AmE), / sʌk ɪn / (BrE)
to draw something inwards, especially by creating a partial vacuum so that air, liquid, or another substance moves into a space
draw in by suction

Examples

She told me to suck in my stomach before taking the photo.
The vacuum cleaner easily sucks in dirt and dust from the carpet.
He sucked in a deep breath before jumping into the cold water.

Synset Examples

suck in your cheeks and stomach

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to inhale - to breathe in; to draw air into the lungs
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01284745-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ sʌk ɪn / (AmE), / sʌk ɪn / (BrE)
to take in or absorb (something) as if by suction; to draw in completely
take in or absorb something completely

Examples

The sponge can suck in a surprising amount of water.
The air purifier was designed to suck in dust particles from the room.
Her mind seemed to suck in every detail of the new subject.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to absorb - to take in a liquid, gas, or other substance from the environment
  • Synonym: to soak up - to take in or absorb liquid or information
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01200618-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ sʌk ɪn / (AmE), / sʌk ɪn / (BrE)
(of a force or situation) to attract someone or something powerfully, often against their initial intention.
attract or draw in by force

Examples

Many young people are sucked in by the excitement of city life.
She was sucked in by a group of friends and soon felt at home.
Foreign investors were sucked in by the promise of rapid economic growth.

Synset Examples

The current boom in the economy sucked many workers in from abroad

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to draw in - to attract or pull toward oneself
  • Antonym: to repel - to drive or force away
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02771650-v
Synset POS: v
