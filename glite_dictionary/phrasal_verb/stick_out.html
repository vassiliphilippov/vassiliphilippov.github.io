to stick out

1. VERB

/ stɪk aʊt / (AmE), / stɪk aʊt / (BrE)
to be easily seen or attract attention because of being different or noticeable
be easily noticeable; attract attention

Examples

Her bright red coat made her stick out in the crowd.
The error in the report really stuck out to me.
His unconventional ideas always stick out during meetings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to stand out - to be very noticeable or distinct

2. VERB

/ stɪk aʊt / (AmE), / stɪk aʊt / (BrE)
to continue to endure an unpleasant situation or person; to tolerate something difficult until the end
endure or tolerate an unpleasant situation

Examples

Even though the job was tough, she decided to stick out the remaining few months.
He could hardly stick out another hour in such a noisy environment.
Despite all the problems, they managed to stick out the entire training program.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to endure - to suffer patiently; to tolerate something difficult
  • Synonym: to tolerate - to allow or accept something unpleasant or difficult
  • Antonym: to give up - to stop doing something before it is finished, especially because it is difficult

3. VERB

/ stɪk aʊt / (AmE), / stɪk aʊt / (BrE)
to extend outward or project beyond a surface or boundary
extend or project outward

Examples

A piece of wire began to stick out from the fence.
Her elbows stuck out awkwardly as she squeezed through the gap.
A broken branch was sticking out from the trunk of the tree.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to protrude - to extend beyond or above a surface
  • Antonym: to recede - to move back or withdraw

/ stɪk aʊt / (AmE), / stɪk aʊt / (BrE)
to continue or endure a difficult or unpleasant situation until the end; to persist despite adversity
endure a situation, persist

Examples

Even though the job was tough, she managed to stick out the entire year.
He found the training exhausting, but he decided to stick out until the end.
Many students quit early, but Sarah chose to stick out the whole course.
