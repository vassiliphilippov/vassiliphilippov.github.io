to soft-pedal

1. VERB

/ ˈsɔft ˌpɛdəl / (AmE), / ˈsɒft ˌpɛdəl / (BrE)
to play down the importance or severity of something; to minimize or make something seem less significant than it is
play down; minimize

Examples

Politicians often soft-pedal problems during election campaigns to appeal to voters.
The company tried to soft-pedal the negative effects of the merger in their press release.
Rather than confront the issue directly, she chose to soft-pedal her concerns at the meeting.

Synset Examples

His advisers soft-pedaled the president's blunder

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to downplay - to make something seem less important or serious
  • Antonym: to emphasize - to give special importance to something

Etymology

From the 'soft pedal' on a piano, which reduces the instrument's volume.

2. VERB

/ ˌsɔftˈpɛdəl / (AmE), / ˌsɒftˈpɛdəl / (BrE)
to make something seem less important or less severe than it really is; to downplay or minimize
downplay or minimize something

Examples

The company tried to soft-pedal the negative effects of the policy change.
Politicians often soft-pedal bad news to maintain public support.
Instead of facing the issue directly, she chose to soft-pedal her concerns.

Synset Examples

She decided to soft-pedal her criticism to avoid conflict.
He tried to soft-pedal his mistakes during the meeting.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to downplay - to make something seem less important
  • Antonym: to emphasize - to give special importance to something

Etymology

From 'soft pedal', a piano pedal that softens the sound, used figuratively for downplaying.
