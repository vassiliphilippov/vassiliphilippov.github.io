to drag on

1. VERB

/ dræɡ ɑn / (AmE), / dræɡ ɒn / (BrE)
to continue for longer than is necessary or expected, often becoming tedious
continue for too long

Examples

The meeting seemed to drag on for hours without any resolution.
Negotiations can drag on when neither side wants to compromise.
Her speech started well, but it began to drag on and lose the audience's attention.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to prolong - to make something last longer
  • Antonym: end quickly - to finish rapidly or within expected time

2. VERB

/ dræɡ ɑn / (AmE), / dræɡ ɒn / (BrE)
to continue for longer than expected or desired, often becoming boring or tiresome
continue for a long, tiresome period

Examples

The meeting seemed to drag on forever, leaving everyone exhausted by the end.
As the trial continued to drag on, the witnesses grew increasingly frustrated.
Winter tends to drag on in northern regions, with snow lasting late into April.

Synset Examples

The speech dragged on for two hours

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to go on - to continue happening or doing something
  • Synonym: to draw out - to cause something to last longer than usual
  • Antonym: end - to come to a stop or finish

3. VERB

/ dræɡ ɑn / (AmE), / dræɡ ɒn / (BrE)
(of an event or process) to continue for longer than is necessary or expected, often becoming tedious or unproductive.
continue tediously or unnecessarily long

Examples

The meeting seemed to drag on forever, leaving everyone exhausted by the end.
Their negotiations dragged on for months without any significant agreement.
We hope this legal case won’t drag on for years.

Synset Examples

Don't let this project drag on too long.
Their argument began to drag on without resolution.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to draw out - to cause something to last longer than necessary
  • Antonym: end - to come to a conclusion, stop continuing
