to shore up

1. VERB

/ ʃɔr ʌp / (AmE), / ʃɔː ʌp / (BrE)
to support or strengthen something, especially by placing something solid underneath or against it
to support or reinforce something

Examples

The engineers had to shore up the old bridge to prevent it from collapsing.
Extra funding was used to shore up the struggling company during the crisis.
They placed wooden beams to shore up the sagging wall until repairs could be made.

Synset Examples

shore and buttress an old building

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reinforce - to make something stronger
  • Antonym: to undermine - to weaken the support of something

Etymology

From the noun 'shore' (a prop or support), likely from Middle English 'schore'.

2. VERB

/ ʃɔr ʌp / (AmE), / ʃɔːr ʌp / (BrE)
to support or strengthen something that is weak or likely to fail, either physically or figuratively
support or strengthen (something)

Examples

The company took measures to shore up its financial position after the crisis.
Engineers were called in to shore up the collapsing wall.
She worked extra hours to shore up her chances of getting a promotion.

Synset Examples

The government plans to shore up the economy with new policies.
They needed to shore up the foundation before winter.
To shore up his confidence, he practiced daily.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to reinforce - to make something stronger, especially by adding material
  • Antonym: to undermine - to weaken something gradually

Etymology

From the noun "shore" (a prop or support), plus the particle "up".
