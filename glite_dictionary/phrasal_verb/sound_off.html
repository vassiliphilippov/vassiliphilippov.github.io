to sound off

1. VERB

/ saʊnd ɔf / (AmE), / saʊnd ɒf / (BrE)
to express complaints or opinions loudly and forcefully, often repeatedly or at length
complain or voice opinions loudly

Examples

The workers sounded off about unsafe conditions in the factory meeting.
He tends to sound off whenever something doesn't go his way.
People were sounding off on social media about the sudden change in policy.

Synset Examples

My mother complains all day
She has a lot to kick about

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to complain - to express dissatisfaction or annoyance
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00909100-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ saʊnd ɔf / (AmE), / saʊnd ɒf / (BrE)
to express one's opinion openly and without hesitation, often loudly or forcefully
speak one's opinion openly
informal

Examples

During the meeting, she didn't hesitate to sound off about the proposed changes.
He tends to sound off whenever someone mentions politics.
Several employees sounded off about their frustrations with the new schedule.

Synset Examples

John spoke up at the meeting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to speak up - to speak more loudly or boldly, especially to express an opinion
  • Antonym: to keep quiet - to not express one's opinion; to remain silent
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01029677-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ saʊnd ɔf / (AmE), / saʊnd ɒf / (BrE)
(of musicians or signals) to begin to play or make a sound, especially to mark a start.
to begin playing or making a sound

Examples

At precisely noon, the band sounded off with a lively march.
As the ceremony began, the trumpets sounded off in perfect unison.
When the gates opened, the orchestra sounded off to signal the start of the event.

Synset Examples

The musicians struck up a tune

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to strike up - to begin playing music; to start a tune
Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
