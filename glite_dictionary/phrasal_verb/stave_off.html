to stave off

1. VERB

/ steɪv ɔf / (AmE), / steɪv ɒf / (BrE)
to prevent something bad or unwanted from happening, at least for a period of time; to ward off
to prevent or delay something bad

Examples

Regular exercise can help stave off illness as you get older.
The government introduced new measures to stave off economic collapse.
She ate a snack to stave off her hunger until dinner.

Synset Examples

Let's avoid a confrontation
head off a confrontation
avert a strike

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ward off - to prevent something unpleasant from affecting you
  • Synonym: to avert - to prevent something harmful from happening

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'to stave off', meaning to drive away with a staff or stave; 'stave' from Old English 'stæf' (staff).

2. VERB

/ steɪv ɑf / (AmE), / steɪv ɒf / (BrE)
to prevent something bad or unwanted from happening or to delay it temporarily
prevent or delay something undesirable

Examples

She takes supplements to stave off illness during the winter months.
The quick action of firefighters helped stave off disaster in the neighborhood.
Extra investment is needed to stave off a major economic downturn.

Synset Examples

The team worked extra hours to stave off project delays.
He took vitamins to stave off the flu this winter.
She exercised regularly to stave off health issues.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to ward off - to prevent something negative from affecting you
  • Synonym: to avert - to prevent something bad from happening

Etymology

From the verb "stave" meaning to break a hole in, later developing the sense of fending off; related to stave (noun: staff or stick).
