to put in little-ease

1. VERB

/ pʊt ɪn ˈlɪt̬l iːz / (AmE), / pʊt ɪn ˈlɪtl iːz / (BrE)
to place (someone) in a state of discomfort or physical restriction, especially as a form of punishment; historically, to confine in a cramped prison cell known as the 'Little Ease'.
confine uncomfortably (historical punishment)
archaic

Examples

Prisoners were often put in little-ease as a warning to others.
He threatened to have the thief put in little-ease if he stole again.
The rebel was put in little-ease for defying the king's orders.

Synset Examples

He was put in little-ease for his misbehavior.

Etymology

Refers to the 'Little Ease'—a small, uncomfortable prison cell in the Tower of London used from the Middle Ages.
