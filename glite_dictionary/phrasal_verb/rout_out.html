to rout out

1. VERB

/ raʊt aʊt / (AmE), / raʊt aʊt / (BrE)
to force someone or something out of a place, especially by searching or with authority
force or drive out

Examples

The police routed out the squatters from the abandoned building.
She routed out her old notebooks from the attic when she moved house.
The officer routed out the soldiers from their tents before dawn.

Synset Examples

The police routed them out of bed at 2 A.M.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evict - to force someone to leave a place, especially their home
  • Synonym: to drive out - to make someone or something leave a place
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02060390-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ raʊt aʊt / (AmE), / raʊt aʊt / (BrE)
to search for and find something, often after a thorough search
search for and find

Examples

The old documents were finally routed out from the attic after hours of searching.
She managed to rout out the recipe her grandmother used to make.
He routed out the missing files from the back of the drawer just in time.

Synset Examples

What did you rout out in the library?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to look for - to attempt to locate something by searching
  • Synonym: seek - to try to find something

Etymology

From the verb 'rout', meaning to dig out, of Middle English origin, possibly from Old French 'rooter'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02297226-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ raʊt aʊt / (AmE), / raʊt aʊt / (BrE)
to force someone or something to leave a hiding place, especially by searching or attacking
force out of hiding

Examples

The soldiers managed to rout out the rebels hiding in the mountains.
It took weeks to rout out the last of the invaders from the city.
Investigators tried to rout out any evidence the suspect had concealed.

Synset Examples

rout out the fighters from their caves

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to drive out - to force someone or something to leave a place
  • Synonym: to flush out - to force someone or something out of hiding

Etymology

From 'rout' (to drive out) + 'out'; 'rout' from Middle English, possibly from Old French 'route' (a defeat, a breaking up).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01111362-v
Synset POS: v
