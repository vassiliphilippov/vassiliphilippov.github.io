to cop out

1. VERB

/ kɑp aʊt / (AmE), / kɒp aʊt / (BrE)
to avoid doing something that one is expected to do, often because of fear or lack of courage
avoid responsibility (due to fear)
informal

Examples

He was supposed to make the speech, but he copped out at the last minute.
Don't cop out now—everyone is counting on you.
She always finds a way to cop out of doing the difficult tasks.

Synset Examples

She copped out when she was supposed to get into the hang glider

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to chicken out - to decide not to do something because of fear

2. VERB

/ kɑp aʊt / (AmE), / kɒp aʊt / (BrE)
to avoid doing something that you should do or accepting responsibility, especially by giving excuses
avoid responsibility or commitment
informal

Examples

He didn't want to finish the project, so he decided to cop out at the last minute.
It's easy to cop out when things get tough, but real leaders don't quit.
You shouldn't just cop out of your responsibilities whenever you feel overwhelmed.

Synset Examples

Don't cop out; just give it your best shot.
He decided to cop out of the meeting at the last minute.
Being a slacktivist can sometimes feel like a cop-out.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evade - avoid doing something or dealing with something
  • Antonym: face up to - accept or confront a responsibility or difficult situation

Etymology

Originally US slang; 'cop' meaning 'to grab or take', + 'out'.

3. VERB

/ kɑp aʊt / (AmE), / kɒp aʊt / (BrE)
to avoid fulfilling a duty, responsibility, or commitment, typically by making excuses or withdrawing
avoid responsibility or commitment
informal american

Examples

He tends to cop out of difficult conversations when emotions run high.
When asked to take the lead, she copped out by saying she was too busy.
Don't cop out now—your team is counting on you.

Synset Examples

She always cops out when things get tough.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to evade - to escape or avoid, especially by cleverness or trickery
  • Antonym: to confront - to face up to and deal with (a problem or difficult situation)

Etymology

Originally American slang, possibly from 'cop' in the sense of 'seize or catch', meaning to evade being caught by obligation.
