to rev up

1. VERB

/ rɛv ʌp / (AmE), / rɛv ʌp / (BrE)
to increase speed or activity; to make something go faster or become more energetic, often by increasing effort or intensity
to speed up or intensify activity

Examples

We need to rev up production to meet the deadline.
The coach tried to rev up the team before the big match.
As the holiday season approached, retailers began to rev up their advertising campaigns.

Synset Examples

let's rev up production

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accelerate - to begin to move more quickly; to increase speed
  • Antonym: to decelerate - to reduce speed; to slow down

Etymology

From 'rev,' short for 'revolution' (of an engine), plus the adverb 'up.'

2. VERB

/ rɛv ʌp / (AmE), / rɛv ʌp / (BrE)
to increase the speed at which an engine operates by accelerating its revolutions per minute
increase engine speed (RPM)

Examples

He likes to rev up his car engine before driving off.
The mechanic instructed me to rev up the motorcycle to check the sound.
You could hear them revving up their engines at the starting line.

Synset Examples

rev up an engine

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to accelerate - to speed up; to increase in speed

Etymology

From 'rev', a shortening of 'revolution' (in the sense of revolution per minute), plus 'up'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00440653-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rɛv ʌp / (AmE), / rɛv ʌp / (BrE)
to increase the speed, intensity, or activity of something; to make something more energetic or active
make more intense or energetic

Examples

The company plans to rev up production to meet high demand this season.
She tried to rev up the audience with an inspiring speech.
Marketing efforts were revved up ahead of the product launch.

Synset Examples

The team needs to rev up their efforts before the deadline.
He likes to rev up the crowd before the concert.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to intensify - to become or make more intense

Etymology

Originally from the use of 'rev' as an abbreviation of 'revolution' (of an engine).
