to bring on

1. VERB

/ brɪŋ ɑn / (AmE), / brɪŋ ɒn / (BrE)
to introduce or release (a product, book, play, etc.) to the public
release or launch something

Examples

The company plans to bring on a new smartphone model next year.
After months of work, the author was ready to bring on her debut novel.
The theater will bring on a fresh production to attract new audiences.

Synset Examples

produce a movie
bring out a book
produce a new play

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to bring out - to produce and launch something (e.g., a book, product)
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02161530-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ brɪŋ ɑn / (AmE), / brɪŋ ɒn / (BrE)
to cause something or someone to appear or become active; to introduce or make something happen.
cause to appear or happen

Examples

It's time to bring on the next performer for the show.
Allergies can bring on a headache if you're not careful.
They will bring on dessert after everyone finishes their main course.

Synset Examples

bring on the birthday cake

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cause - make something happen
  • Antonym: to send away - to make something or someone leave
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01647780-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ brɪŋ ɑn / (AmE), / brɪŋ ɒn / (BrE)
to cause something, especially something unpleasant or significant, to begin or happen
cause something to happen

Examples

The new management policies brought on several unexpected problems for the team.
Exposure to cold weather can bring on a sore throat or even a cold.
Her comments in the meeting brought on a heated debate among the staff.

Synset Examples

induce a crisis

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cause - to make something happen
  • Synonym: trigger - to cause something to start
  • Antonym: to prevent - to stop something from happening
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01647592-v
Synset POS: v
