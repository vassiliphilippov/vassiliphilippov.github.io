to trot out

1. VERB

/ trɑt aʊt / (AmE), / trɒt aʊt / (BrE)
to produce or display something for inspection, attention, or admiration, often repeatedly or in a routine way
bring out for display or effect

Examples

Every year, the company trots out its latest technology at the trade show.
She always trotted out the same story whenever guests visited.
Politicians often trot out familiar promises during election campaigns.

Synset Examples

His novel trots out a rich heiress
always able to trot out some new excuse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to show off - to display something conspicuously or proudly
  • Antonym: hide away - to keep something out of sight or secret

2. VERB

/ trɑt aʊt / (AmE), / trɒt aʊt / (BrE)
to present or display something, especially repeatedly or for effect, often to impress or persuade others
present or show off something, often for effect

Examples

Whenever guests visit, he likes to trot out his collection of rare coins.
The senator trotted out the same arguments during every debate.
Marketing teams often trot out impressive statistics to win over new clients.

Synset Examples

They will trot out the new product at the conference.
She plans to trot out her best recipes at the dinner.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to show off - to display something with the intention of impressing others

3. VERB

/ trɑt aʊt / (AmE), / trɒt aʊt / (BrE)
to present or use something repeatedly or routinely, especially in a way that is not new or interesting
present or use repeatedly, often unoriginally

Examples

Every time he's questioned, he trots out the same tired excuse for his behavior.
The company spokesperson trotted out their standard response during the press conference.
Whenever asked about the delay, she trots out a list of reasons.

Synset Examples

He tends to trot out excuses when he’s late.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repeat - to say or do something again
  • Synonym: to bring up - to mention or introduce a topic, especially repeatedly
  • Antonym: to conceal - to hide or keep something secret

Etymology

Originally from the literal sense of 'trot' (to move at a moderate speed) combined with 'out'; figurative use developed in the 20th century.
