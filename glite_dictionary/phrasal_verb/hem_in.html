to hem in

1. VERB

/ hɛm ɪn / (AmE), / hɛm ɪn / (BrE)
to surround someone or something closely, often so that escape or movement is difficult or impossible
surround closely to restrict movement

Examples

The enemy soldiers managed to hem in the last of the defenders.
I feel like the high walls of the city hem in its residents.
Protesters were hemmed in by police on all sides during the march.

Synset Examples

The Turks besieged Vienna

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to besiege - to surround a place with armed forces to capture it or force surrender
  • Synonym: to encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Antonym: release - to allow to go free; to let go

Etymology

From 'hem' meaning to enclose or border, with 'in' emphasizing enclosure.

2. VERB

/ hɛm ɪn / (AmE), / hɛm ɪn / (BrE)
to surround someone or something so closely as to restrict movement or freedom
surround restrictively

Examples

The tiny house was hemmed in by tall apartment buildings on all sides.
She felt hemmed in by her family's constant demands and expectations.
The sheep were hemmed in by a circle of fencing to keep them from wandering.

Synset Examples

The building was hemmed in by flowers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to enclose - to surround on all sides; to close in
  • Antonym: free - to release from confinement or restriction

Etymology

From the verb "hem" meaning to enclose or confine, possibly influenced by the noun "hem" (edge of cloth), plus the particle "in" indicating enclosure.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: v
