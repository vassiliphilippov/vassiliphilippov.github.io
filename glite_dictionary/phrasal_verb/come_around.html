to come around

1. VERB

/ kʌm əˈraʊnd / (AmE), / kʌm əˈraʊnd / (BrE)
to change your opinion or position, especially after initially disagreeing
change opinion or position

Examples

After much discussion, she finally came around and agreed to the proposal.
It took him months to come around to the idea of moving abroad.
They didn’t agree at first, but they came around in the end.

Synset Examples

He came around to our point of view

Related Terms

  • Synonym: change one's mind - to adopt a different opinion or decision
  • Antonym: to stand firm - to refuse to change one's opinion or decision
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00656117-v

2. VERB

/ kʌm əˈraʊnd / (AmE), / kʌm əˈraʊnd / (BrE)
(of a regular event or time) to occur or happen again, especially as part of a regular cycle.
to occur or happen again regularly

Examples

My birthday always seems to come around faster each year.
The town festival comes around every summer in July.
Once tax season comes around, everyone becomes more concerned about their finances.

Synset Examples

Christmas rolled around again

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to recur - to happen again, especially at intervals
Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ kʌm əˈraʊnd / (AmE), / kʌm əˈraʊnd / (BrE)
to change one's opinion or attitude after initial reluctance; or to return, appear, or make a visit, especially after an absence.
change of opinion; return or visit

Examples

After much debate, John finally came around to our proposal and agreed to the plan.
They came around to visit us last weekend after being away for months.
It took some time, but she eventually came around and apologized.

Synset Examples

Trust me, she'll come around in her own good time.
They didn’t come around until after the meeting.
She will come around to our way of thinking.
He takes time, but he usually comes around.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to come round - Alternative British spelling/variant of 'come around'
