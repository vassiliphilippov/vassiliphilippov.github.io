to root out

1. VERB

/ rut aʊt / (AmE), / ruːt aʊt / (BrE)
to pull up (a plant) by the roots or as if by the roots; to remove completely
pull up by the roots; remove completely

Examples

The gardener had to root out all the weeds before planting new flowers.
After years of neglect, we rooted out the old shrubs from the backyard.
It's important to root out invasive plants to protect native species.

Synset Examples

uproot the vine that has spread all over the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to uproot - remove a plant from the ground by pulling up the roots
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01569697-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ruːt aʊt / (AmE), / ruːt aʊt / (BrE)
to find and remove or destroy something or someone completely, especially something harmful or unwanted
remove or eliminate completely

Examples

The new administration promised to root out corruption in government offices.
Investigators worked tirelessly to root out the source of the leaks.
She is determined to root out any form of discrimination in the workplace.

Synset Examples

the vestiges of political democracy were soon uprooted
root out corruption

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eradicate - to completely destroy or get rid of something harmful
  • Synonym: to uproot - to pull something out by the roots; remove completely
  • Antonym: foster - to encourage the development or growth of something
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01666012-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ rut aʊt / (AmE), / ruːt aʊt / (BrE)
to find and eliminate or get rid of something undesired, especially something hidden or difficult to detect
eliminate or get rid of completely

Examples

The new manager is determined to root out corruption within the organization.
It can take years to root out deeply ingrained prejudices from a society.
She worked tirelessly to root out unfair practices in the workplace.

Synset Examples

He wanted to root out the problem before it escalated.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eradicate - to destroy completely, to eliminate
  • Synonym: to wipe out - to destroy or remove completely
  • Degree Lower: to discover - to find something hidden or lost

Etymology

From the image of removing a plant by its roots to ensure complete elimination.
