to lead off

1. VERB

/ lid ɔf / (AmE), / liːd ɒf / (BrE)
to begin something, especially an event, series, or activity; to make a start
to begin or start (an event, activity, etc.)

Examples

She will lead off the conference with a welcome speech.
The parade led off at exactly noon, as planned.
Who wants to lead off the discussion today?

Synset Examples

The U.S. started a war in the Middle East
The Iraqis began hostilities
begin a new chapter in your life

Related Terms

  • Synonym: start - to begin doing or experiencing something
  • Antonym: finish - to bring an activity or event to an end
  • Antonym: to conclude - to bring something to an end

WordNet ID: oewn-00349400-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ liːd ɔf / (AmE), / liːd ɒf / (BrE)
to cause someone to adopt immoral or improper behavior; to corrupt the morals of someone, especially a younger or inexperienced person.
to corrupt or teach immoral behavior

Examples

The older boys tried to lead off their younger siblings by encouraging them to skip school.
She worried that bad company might lead off her son into a life of crime.
It is wrong to lead off innocent children by exposing them to harmful habits.

Synset Examples

It was common practice to lead off the young ones, and teach them bad habits

Related Terms

  • Synonym: corrupt - cause someone to become immoral or dishonest
  • Antonym: reform - to improve someone's behavior or morals
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02586477-v
Synset POS: v
