to break even

1. VERB

/ breɪk ˈivən / (AmE), / breɪk ˈiːvən / (BrE)
to neither make a profit nor incur a loss; to have expenses equal to income or revenue
make neither profit nor loss

Examples

It may take the business two years to break even after launching.
If ticket sales reach a thousand, the event will finally break even.
They just managed to break even last quarter despite higher expenses.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: profit - to gain financial benefit; the money made after costs
  • Antonym: lose money - to spend or lose more money than is earned

2. VERB

/ breɪk ˈivən / (AmE), / breɪk ˈiːvən / (BrE)
to reach a point at which no profit or loss is made, especially in business, gambling, or competition
make neither profit nor loss

Examples

After several tough years, the company finally managed to break even last quarter.
If you want to break even at the casino, you should know when to walk away.
The event was a success, but we barely broke even after covering all the costs.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: profit - a financial gain from business or investment
  • Antonym: to lose - to be deprived of money or resources in a transaction or operation
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02011251-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ breɪk ˈivən / (AmE), / breɪk ˈiːvən / (BrE)
to have no net loss or profit; to cover costs without making a gain or loss
have no net profit or loss

Examples

After paying all the expenses, the charity event managed to break even.
If the company can break even this year, the owners will be satisfied.
We didn’t make a profit, but at least we broke even on the project.

Synset Examples

They managed to break even after a tough quarter.
The business aims to break even by the end of the year.
If we sell 100 tickets, we’ll break even on costs.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: profit - a financial gain, especially the difference between revenue and cost
  • Antonym: lose money - to spend more money than is earned, resulting in a net loss
