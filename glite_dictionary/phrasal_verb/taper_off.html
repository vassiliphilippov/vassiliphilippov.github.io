to taper off

1. VERB

/ ˈteɪpər ɔf / (AmE), / ˈteɪpə ɒf / (BrE)
to gradually become smaller, weaker, or less active until stopping completely
gradually decrease or diminish

Examples

After the holidays, demand for the product began to taper off significantly.
The doctor advised her to taper off the medication slowly rather than stopping it abruptly.
Attendance at the evening lectures tends to taper off toward the end of the semester.

Synset Examples

Business tapered off

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to diminish - to become or make something smaller or less
  • Synonym: wane - to decrease in size, extent, or degree

2. VERB

/ ˈteɪpər ɔf / (AmE), / ˈteɪpə ɒf / (BrE)
to gradually diminish in strength, intensity, or quantity, especially so as to end weakly or fade away
to gradually become weaker or end

Examples

The applause began to taper off as the speaker left the stage.
Sales usually taper off after the holiday season.
Her interest in the project seemed to taper off over time.

Synset Examples

The music just petered out — there was no proper ending

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to peter out - to gradually diminish and come to an end

Etymology

From 'taper', meaning to become thinner towards one end, and 'off', indicating cessation or reduction.

3. VERB

/ ˈteɪpɚ ɑf / (AmE), / ˈteɪpə ɒf / (BrE)
to become gradually smaller, fewer, or less; to decrease slowly over time
gradually decrease or lessen

Examples

The doctor's instructions said to taper off the medication over two weeks.
Attendance at the weekly meetings began to taper off as the year progressed.
If you suddenly stop exercising, it's better to taper off rather than quit abruptly.

Synset Examples

You need to taper off the workout intensity slowly.
The medication should taper off gradually over a week.
Sales began to taper off after the holiday season.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to diminish - to become or make less
  • Antonym: increase - to become greater in size, amount, or degree

Etymology

From 'taper' (to become gradually narrower), likely from Middle English, influenced by shape of a taper (candle), plus 'off'.
