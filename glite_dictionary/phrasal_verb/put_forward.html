to put forward

1. VERB

/ pʊt ˈfɔrwɚd / (AmE), / pʊt ˈfɔːwəd / (BrE)
to insist on having one’s opinions or rights recognized; to assert or express oneself confidently
insist on having one’s opinions recognized

Examples

During the meeting, she put forward her ideas despite initial resistance from the team.
It is important for students to put forward their views in classroom discussions.
He finally decided to put forward his concerns about workplace safety.

Synset Examples

Women should assert themselves more!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to assert oneself - to behave in a confident way and insist on one’s rights or opinions

2. VERB

/ pʊt ˈfɔrwərd / (AmE), / pʊt ˈfɔːwəd / (BrE)
to suggest someone or something as a candidate for an honor, award, or position
propose as a candidate

Examples

She was put forward for the award by her colleagues due to her outstanding work.
Three candidates were put forward for the vacant position at the meeting.
The committee decided to put forward his name as a nominee for the prize.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to nominate - to formally propose as a candidate
  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove from consideration as a candidate
Source: WordNet

3. VERB

/ pʊt ˈfɔrwərd / (AmE), / pʊt ˈfɔːwəd / (BrE)
to suggest, propose, or present an idea, plan, or argument for consideration or discussion.
suggest, propose, or present (an idea, etc.)

Examples

She decided to put forward her own proposal at the meeting.
Several new theories were put forward during the seminar.
He is always willing to put forward fresh ideas to solve the problem.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: to withdraw - to remove or take back a suggestion or proposal
