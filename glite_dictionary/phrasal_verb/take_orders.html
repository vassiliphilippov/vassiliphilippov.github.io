to take orders

1. VERB

/ teɪk ˈɔrdɚz / (AmE), / teɪk ˈɔːdəz / (BrE)
to be ordained as a priest or minister in the Christian Church
become ordained as a priest or minister

Examples

After many years of study, he finally took orders and became a parish priest.
She decided to take orders following a spiritual awakening in her youth.
Many scholars in the Middle Ages would also take orders to join the clergy.

Synset Examples

She took orders last month

WordNet ID: oewn-02391408-v

2. VERB

/ teɪk ˈɔrdɚz / (AmE), / teɪk ˈɔːdəz / (BrE)
to receive and be expected to follow directions or commands from someone in authority
obey commands from authority

Examples

She refuses to take orders from anyone she doesn't respect.
As a new recruit, he had to take orders without question.
It's not easy for her to take orders after years of being her own boss.

Synset Examples

I don't take orders from you!

Related Terms

  • Antonym: give orders - to issue commands to others
  • Synonym: to obey - to do what one is told or commanded
