to weed out

1. VERB

/ wid aʊt / (AmE), / wiːd aʊt / (BrE)
to remove people or things that are not wanted or are less good from a group or collection
remove unwanted parts or people

Examples

The recruitment process helps weed out unsuitable candidates early on.
Strict quality control ensures that defective products are weeded out before they reach customers.
As part of the reorganization, the team is weeding out old or unnecessary policies.

Synset Examples

The company weeded out the incompetent people
The new law weeds out the old inequities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eliminate - remove or get rid of something
  • Antonym: to retain - keep or continue to have something

Etymology

Metaphorically from the act of removing weeds from a garden.

2. VERB

/ wid aʊt / (AmE), / wiːd aʊt / (BrE)
to remove or eliminate people or things that are unwanted or less desirable from a group or collection
remove unwanted people or things

Examples

The company plans to weed out unproductive employees during the reorganization.
Regular audits help to weed out fraudulent transactions in the system.
It's important to weed out unnecessary features before launching the product.

Synset Examples

The teacher decided to weed out the students who were not participating.
We need to weed out the bad ideas from the list.
It’s time to weed out unnecessary expenses from the budget.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to eliminate - remove or get rid of something completely
  • Antonym: to retain - keep, continue to have

Etymology

From the literal action of removing weeds from soil, metaphorically extended to removing something undesirable.
