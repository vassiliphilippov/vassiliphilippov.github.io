to cotton to

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑtn̩ tu / (AmE), / ˈkɒtn̩ tuː / (BrE)
to begin to like or feel friendly toward someone or something; to become attracted to or comfortable with someone or something
become fond of or friendly to
informal american

Examples

It took a while, but the team soon cottoned to their new coach.
She immediately cottoned to the idea of traveling abroad next summer.
He didn't cotton to the new software at first, but now he uses it every day.

Synset Examples

cotton to something

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to warm to - begin to like or feel more positive about someone or something
  • Synonym: take a shine to - develop a liking for someone or something
  • Synonym: to take to - begin to like someone or something
  • Antonym: dislike - feel aversion or distaste for someone or something

Etymology

Probably from the idea of adapting comfortably, as cotton fabric does, in American English usage.

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑtn̩ tu / (AmE), / ˈkɒtn̩ tə / (BrE)
(informal) to begin to like or accept (someone or something)
begin to like or accept
informal american

Examples

It took the new employee a while to cotton to the company's culture.
Most children quickly cotton to the idea of recess.
She didn't cotton to spinach when she first tried it.

Synset Examples

She didn't cotton to his rude remarks.
They didn't cotton to the new rules at first.
He seems to cotton to her ideas quickly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to take to - to develop a liking for someone or something

Etymology

Originally American English, dating to the early 19th century; the origin is uncertain, possibly related to the idea of forming a close attachment (as fibers stick together).
