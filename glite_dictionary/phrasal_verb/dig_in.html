to dig in

1. VERB

/ dɪɡ ɪn / (AmE), / dɪɡ ɪn / (BrE)
(of soldiers or other groups) to prepare a defensive position by occupying a trench or fortified area, often for protection during combat or threat.
prepare a defensive position (military)

Examples

The soldiers dug in on the hillside to wait for reinforcements.
Knowing an attack was coming, the unit prepared to dig in for the night.
The army dug in along the riverbank, reinforcing their defensive line.

Synset Examples

The troops dug in for the night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to entrench - to establish a defensive military position in a trench
Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-01533912-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ dɪɡ ɪn / (AmE), / dɪɡ ɪn / (BrE)
to begin eating food eagerly and with enthusiasm
eat heartily; start eating enthusiastically

Examples

The children were hungry and couldn't wait to dig in as soon as dinner was served.
As soon as the barbecue was ready, everyone gathered around to dig in.
Don't be shy—please dig in and help yourself to the food.

Synset Examples

The food was placed on the table and the children pitched in

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to tuck in - to eat food enthusiastically (mainly BrE)
  • Antonym: to pick at - to eat slowly or with little appetite
