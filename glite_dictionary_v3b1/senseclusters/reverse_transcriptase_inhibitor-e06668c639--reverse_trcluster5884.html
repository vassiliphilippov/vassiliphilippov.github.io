reverse transcriptase inhibitor

noun
drug inhibiting viral reverse transcriptase
5.7
Pronunciation: /rɪ.ˈvɜːrs træn.skrɪp.ˈteɪs ɪn.ˈhɪb.ɪ.tər/ /; / /riː.ˈvɜːrs træn.skrɪp.ˈteɪs ɪn.ˈhɪb.ɪ.tər/ (AmE), /rɪ.ˈvɜːs træn.skrɪp.ˈteɪs ɪn.ˈhɪb.ɪ.tə/ /; / /riː.ˈvɜːs træn.skrɪp.ˈteɪs ɪn.ˈhɪb.ɪ.tə/ (BrE)
a drug that blocks the activity of reverse transcriptase, an enzyme used by retroviruses such as HIV to replicate their genetic material

Examples

Reverse transcriptase inhibitors for HIV
Doctors often prescribe reverse transcriptase inhibitors to patients with HIV to slow the progression of the virus.
Many modern HIV treatment regimens include at least one reverse transcriptase inhibitor as a core component.
The development of the first reverse transcriptase inhibitor revolutionized the treatment of AIDS in the 1980s.
