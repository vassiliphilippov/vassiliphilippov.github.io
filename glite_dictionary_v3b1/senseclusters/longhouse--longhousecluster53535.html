longhouse

noun
communal dwelling
5.8
Pronunciation: /ˈlɔːŋ.haʊs/ (AmE), /ˈlɒŋ.haʊs/ (BrE)
a long, communal building used as a dwelling by groups of people, especially in traditional societies

Examples

A longhouse dwelling
The entire family lived together in a spacious longhouse near the river.
Ceremonies and important meetings were often held inside the village longhouse.
Visitors were welcomed into the longhouse to share food and stories with the community.
The ancient villagers built a large longhouse to share warmth and resources during the harsh winters.
Inside the longhouse, families gathered around the central fire to cook and tell stories.
Archaeologists uncovered the remains of a Viking longhouse, its hearths and postholes visible in the earth.
The Iroquois people traditionally lived in a longhouse with several families sharing the space.
Visitors to the museum can walk through a reconstructed longhouse to learn about Indigenous culture.
Each longhouse was built from wood and bark, providing shelter for the entire community.
