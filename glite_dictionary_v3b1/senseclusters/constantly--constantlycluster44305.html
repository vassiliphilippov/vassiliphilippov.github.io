constantly

adverb
all the time; without interruption
1.0 50/m
Pronunciation: / Ààk…ëÀên.st…ônt.li /; / Ààk…ënst…ôntli / (AmE), / Ààk…ín.st…ônt.li /; / Ààk…ínst…ôntli / (BrE)
in a way that happens all the time or very frequently, without stopping or interruption

Examples

To constantly check
She is constantly checking her phone for messages.
The baby cried constantly throughout the night, keeping everyone awake.
Water is constantly flowing from the mountain spring.
City traffic is constantly noisy, especially during rush hour.
She was constantly checking her phone for new messages during the meeting.
The weather here is constantly changing, so it's wise to carry an umbrella.

Synonyms

  • continuously
    2.3 10/m
    continuously is without stopping in a steady way, while constantly is more general and can mean very often or always.
  • nonstop
    1.3 3/m
    nonstop is used for events that run without pauses, while constantly describes things that happen very often or all the time.
  • all the time
    1.0 70/m
    all the time is a common spoken phrase meaning always, while constantly can be slightly more formal or describe frequent actions.
  • incessantly
    6.3 2/m
    incessantly describes something annoying that keeps going without pause, while constantly is more neutral and can be positive.
  • ceaselessly
    5.3 0.7/m
    ceaselessly means without any end or break, often formal, while constantly is more common and widely used in everyday speech.
  • unceasingly
    6.6 0.3/m
    unceasingly is a formal word for something that does not stop, while constantly is common and fits many daily situations.
  • perpetually
    4.8 3/m
    perpetually suggests a long unchanging state or habit, while constantly often means frequent actions over time rather than fixed state.
  • around the clock
    3.0 2/m
    around the clock means all day and night without stopping, while constantly can mean often but not necessarily every hour.
  • every second of the day
    2.5 0.03/m
    every second of the day is a strong, informal way to say literally all moments, while constantly is more neutral and fits many contexts.
