complete

adjective
absolute; total (often pejorative intensifier)
2.8 13/m
Pronunciation: / k…ômÀàplit / (AmE), / k…ômÀàpliÀêt / (BrE)
used to emphasize that someone or something is a total or absolute example of a particular quality, often in a negative or pejorative sense

Examples

A complete fool
He's a complete idiot if he believes that story.
She felt like a complete failure after losing the match.
That was a complete disaster from start to finish.

Synonyms

  • perfect
    5.1 15/m
    perfect is an informal, often pejorative label used to point to a total example, while complete is a general intensifier.
  • utter
    3.7 9.67/m
    utter is used to show something is absolute and strong, while complete is a more common word for total or whole.
  • out-and-out
    6.3 0.39/m
    out-and-out is an idiom that stresses a total, often negative case, while complete is a plain intensifier for totality.
  • downright
    4.9 0.79/m
    downright is used to stress something clearly bad or strong, while complete is more neutral for total extent or quality.
  • absolute
    3.9 11/m
    absolute means total or perfect in degree and often sounds more formal, while complete is a common intensifier for totalness.
  • pure
    3.9 14/m
    pure often points to an unmixed, clear example and can feel formal, while complete is a simple intensifier for total quality.
  • unadulterated
    6.9 0.40/m
    unadulterated describes something undiluted or pure and sounds formal, while complete is a general word for total or absolute.
  • sodding
    9.9 0.00/m
    sodding is a British informal, mildly vulgar intensifier used for annoyance, while complete is neutral and not vulgar.
