English-Gothic architecture

noun
Gothic architectural style in medieval England
4.2 7.1
Pronunciation: /ˈɪŋ.glɪʃ ˈgɑː.θɪk ˈɑːr.kə.tek.tʃər/ /; / /ˈɪŋ.lɪʃ ˈgɑː.θɪk ˈɑːr.kə.tek.tʃər/ (AmE), /ˈɪŋ.glɪʃ ˈgɒ.θɪk ˈɑː.kɪ.tek.tʃə/ /; / /ˈɪŋ.lɪʃ ˈgɒ.θɪk ˈɑː.kɪ.tek.tʃə/ (BrE)
A style of Gothic architecture that developed in 14th and 15th century England, noted for its vertical emphasis, four-centered (Tudor) arches, and fan vaulting.

Examples

English-Gothic architecture style
Many famous churches in England were built in the English-Gothic architecture style during the Middle Ages.
The cathedral is a masterpiece of English-Gothic architecture with its impressive fan vaults.
Tourists admire the vertical lines and elaborate stonework typical of English-Gothic architecture.

Related Terms

  • degree higher: Renaissance architecture - the succeeding architectural style characterized by classical forms and symmetry
  • degree lower: Romanesque architecture - the preceding architectural style characterized by semi-circular arches and heavy structure
