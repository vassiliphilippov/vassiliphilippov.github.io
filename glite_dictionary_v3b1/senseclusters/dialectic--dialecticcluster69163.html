dialectic

noun
contradiction or interplay of opposing ideas
9.3 7.1
Pronunciation: /daɪ.ə.ˈlek.tɪk/ (AmE), /daɪ.ə.ˈlek.tɪk/ (BrE)
a method or process in philosophy where opposing ideas or arguments are discussed to reach a new understanding or truth

Examples

Hegelian dialectic
In Hegelian philosophy, progress is achieved through the dialectic of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis.
The debate between the two philosophers was driven by a dialectic of reason and emotion.
The historian analyzed the dialectic between individual freedom and collective responsibility in society.
Hegel's dialectic shaped much of modern philosophy with its focus on thesis, antithesis, and synthesis.
In academic debates, dialectic helps participants refine their arguments and approach the truth.
The philosopher explained the concept of dialectic as a way to uncover deeper truths through discussion.
