bicameralism

noun
dual-mind theory
9.9
Pronunciation: /bɪ.ˈkæm.ə.rəl.ɪz/ /; / /baɪ.ˈkæm.ə.rəl.ɪz/ (AmE), /bɪ.ˈkæm.ə.rəl.ɪz/ /; / /baɪ.ˈkæm.ə.rəl.ɪz/ (BrE)
the psychological theory that early human minds functioned as two separate parts, one issuing commands and the other obeying them.

Examples

Bicameralism in the mind
Julian Jaynes's book introduced the concept of bicameralism to explain ancient human consciousness.
Some researchers debate whether bicameralism accurately describes the mental processes of early societies.
The theory of bicameralism suggests that ancient people heard voices as commands from gods.

Related Terms

  • synonym: consciousness - the state of being aware of and able to think and perceive
  • antonym: unconscious - the part of the mind not within conscious awareness
