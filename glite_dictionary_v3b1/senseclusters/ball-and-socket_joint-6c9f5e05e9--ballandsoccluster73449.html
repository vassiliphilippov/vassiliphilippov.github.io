ball-and-socket joint

noun
type of joint with spherical movement
3.0 3.4
Pronunciation: / bɔl ənd ˈsɑkɪt dʒɔɪnt /; / ˌbɔl ən ˈsɑkɪt dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / bɔːl ənd ˈsɒkɪt dʒɔɪnt /; / ˌbɔːl ən ˈsɒkɪt dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
a joint in the body where a rounded end of one bone fits into a cup-like cavity of another, allowing movement in almost any direction (such as the hip or shoulder)

Examples

The ball-and-socket joint
Damage to the hip's ball-and-socket joint can severely restrict movement and cause pain.
Orthopedic surgeons often replace a ball-and-socket joint with an artificial implant during hip replacement surgery.
The shoulder is a classic example of a ball-and-socket joint, allowing a wide range of motion.
Damage to the ball-and-socket joint in the hip can make walking painful.
Engineers sometimes use a ball-and-socket joint in robotics to mimic natural motion.
The human shoulder is a classic example of a ball-and-socket joint, allowing a wide range of movement.

Related Terms

  • antonym: hinge joint - a joint allowing movement in one plane, like the elbow or knee
