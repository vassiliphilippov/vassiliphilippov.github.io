Pascal's law

noun
principle of pressure transmission in fluids
Pronunciation: / pæsˈkɑlz lɔ /; / pæˈskɑlz lɔ / (AmE), / pæsˈkɑːlz lɔː / (BrE)
the scientific principle that when pressure is applied to a confined fluid, it is transmitted equally in all directions and acts at right angles to surfaces in contact with the fluid

Examples

Pascal's law of pressure
According to Pascal's law, pressure in a fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.
Engineers rely on Pascal's law when designing equipment that uses fluid power.
Hydraulic systems such as car brakes operate based on Pascal's law.
Engineers often use Pascal's law to design hydraulic systems in machinery and vehicles.
The hydraulic press operates according to Pascal's law, allowing a small input force to generate a large output force.
When you squeeze a tube of toothpaste, Pascal's law explains why the paste emerges evenly from the nozzle.

Related Terms

  • degree lower: Archimedes' principle - the principle stating that an object submerged in a fluid experiences a buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced
  • synonym: hydrostatics - the branch of physics that deals with fluids at rest
