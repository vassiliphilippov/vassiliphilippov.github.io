subsidization

noun
the act of giving a subsidy
Pronunciation: / ˌsʌb.sɪ.dəˈzeɪ.ʃən /; / ˌsʌbsɪdəˈzeɪʃən /; / ˌsʌbsɪdaɪˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.sɪ.daɪˈzeɪ.ʃən /; / ˌsʌbsɪdaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of providing financial assistance or support, especially by a government, to help an industry, activity, or group

Examples

International subsidization
International subsidization of developing countries helps support education and healthcare initiatives.
Many critics argue that heavy subsidization of agriculture can distort market prices.
The government's subsidization of renewable energy has encouraged more companies to invest in solar power.
Farmers rely heavily on subsidization to keep their businesses afloat.
International subsidization practices are often debated in trade negotiations.
The government's subsidization of the arts helped many theaters avoid closure during tough times.
Critics argue that excessive subsidisation can lead to inefficiencies and dependency.
The government's subsidisation of the renewable energy sector has made solar panels more affordable.
Without significant subsidisation, small farmers would not be able to compete in the global market.
