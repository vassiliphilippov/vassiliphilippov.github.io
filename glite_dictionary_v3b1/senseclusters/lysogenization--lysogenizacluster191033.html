lysogenization

noun
integration of phage DNA into a bacterium
9.9
Pronunciation: / laɪˌsɑdʒənəˈzeɪʃən /; / ˌlaɪsəˌdʒɛnəˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / laɪˌsɒdʒənaɪˈzeɪʃən /; / ˌlaɪsɒdʒɪnaɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process by which a bacterium incorporates a phage's genetic material into its genome, resulting in the formation of a lysogen.

Examples

Lysogenization of bacteria
After lysogenization, the bacterial cell can replicate the viral DNA along with its own.
Scientists study lysogenization to understand how phages affect bacterial evolution.
The efficiency of lysogenization varies depending on the bacteriophage and the host strain.
Following lysogenisation, the bacterium can reproduce normally while carrying the integrated viral genome.
Researchers measured the rate of lysogenisation among different strains of E. coli in the experiment.
The process of lysogenisation allows certain viruses to persist harmlessly within bacterial cells.

  • antonym: lytic cycle - destructive phage cycle resulting in host cell lysis
