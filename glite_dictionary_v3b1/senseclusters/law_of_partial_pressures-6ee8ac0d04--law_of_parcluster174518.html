law of partial pressures

noun
law stating total pressure of gas mixture equals sum of individual pressures
7.4 4.4
Pronunciation: /lɔː əv ˈpɑːr.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.ərz/ /; / /lɑː əv ˈpɑːr.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.ərz/ (AmE), /lɔː əv ˈpɑː.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.əz/ (BrE)
A scientific law stating that in a mixture of gases, the total pressure is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas, with each partial pressure being what the gas would exert if it were alone in the same volume and temperature.

Examples

The law of partial pressures
According to the law of partial pressures, you can calculate the total pressure by adding the individual gas pressures.
Dalton formulated the law of partial pressures after researching gas mixtures in the early 19th century.
The anesthesiologist explained how the law of partial pressures affects the delivery of gases during surgery.
