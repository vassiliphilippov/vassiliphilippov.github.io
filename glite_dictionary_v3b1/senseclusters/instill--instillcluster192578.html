instill

verb
impart gradually
4.8 5.56/m
Pronunciation: / ÉªnËˆstÉªl / (AmE), / ÉªnËˆstÉªl / (BrE)
to gradually cause someone to have a feeling, idea, value, or quality

Examples

To instill good values
Parents try to instill good values in their children from an early age.
Teachers can instill a love of reading through engaging stories and discussions.
The coach worked hard to instill confidence in his team.
Her teacher hoped to instill a love of reading in all her students.
Parents often try to instill good manners in their children from an early age.
The coach works hard to instill confidence and discipline in the team.
Her parents hoped to instill a sense of responsibility in her from a young age.
Leaders must instill trust and integrity within their teams.
The teacher worked hard to instill confidence in his students through constant encouragement.

Synonyms

  • inculcate
    8.1 1.77/m
    inculcate teaches an idea by repeated instruction, while instill means to cause someone gradually to accept a feeling or value.
  • ingrain
    6.1 2.59/m
    ingrain makes a habit or belief become a deep, lasting part of someone, while instill focuses on gradually creating that feeling.
  • implant
    5.7 1.04/m
    implant sets an idea firmly and often quickly into someone's mind, while instill suggests a slower, gradual building of belief.
  • imbue
    6.9 2.69/m
    imbue fills someone or something with a strong quality or feeling, while instill describes slowly causing that feeling to grow.
  • infuse
    4.6 5.03/m
    infuse fills someone or something with a quality or mood, while instill focuses on slowly forming a person's idea or value.
  • impart
    4.8 2.41/m
    impart gives information, knowledge, or a quality to someone, while instill implies gradually causing beliefs or feelings to develop.
