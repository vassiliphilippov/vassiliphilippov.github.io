sensationalism

noun
use of shocking or exaggerated content
5.6 5.6
Pronunciation: /sens.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ /; / /səns.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ (AmE), /sens.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ /; / /səns.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ (BrE)
the use of shocking, exciting, or exaggerated content to attract attention, especially in journalism or media

Examples

Media sensationalism
Many critics argue that media sensationalism distracts from more serious news reporting.
The film was criticized for its sensationalism and graphic content rather than its artistic merit.
The newspaper was accused of sensationalism after publishing shocking headlines about the celebrity scandal.
Critics argue that sensationalism in the media can distort public perception of important issues.
Some TV shows rely on sensationalism to boost their ratings rather than presenting factual information.
The newspaper was accused of sensationalism for its exaggerated coverage of the scandal.
