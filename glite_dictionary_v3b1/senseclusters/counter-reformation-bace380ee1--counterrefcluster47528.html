Counter-Reformation

noun
Catholic reform movement opposing the Reformation
8.7
Pronunciation: / ˈkaʊntər ˌrɛfəˈrmeɪʃən /; / ˌkaʊntərˌrɛfəˈrmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˈkaʊntə ˌrɛfəˈmeɪʃən /; / ˌkaʊntərəˌrɛfəˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
the period and movement of reform in the Roman Catholic Church in the 16th and 17th centuries, in response to the Protestant Reformation, aimed at reviving and strengthening Catholic doctrine and practices

Examples

The Counter-Reformation period
Art and architecture flourished in Catholic countries during the Counter-Reformation as part of efforts to inspire religious devotion.
During the Counter-Reformation, the Catholic Church implemented sweeping changes to address abuses and clarify doctrine.
The Jesuit order played a significant role in the spread of the Counter-Reformation across Europe.
Art and architecture from the Counter-Reformation period are known for their dramatic and emotional style.
The Counter-Reformation implemented many changes within the Catholic Church to combat Protestant influence.
The Jesuit order played a key role during the Counter-Reformation, focusing on education and missionary work.
