supersaturation

noun
excess dissolved solute
8.5
Pronunciation: / ˌsuːpərˌsætʃəˈreɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsuːpəsætʃəˈreɪʃən / (BrE)
a state where a solution or air contains more of a dissolved substance than it can normally hold at a given temperature or pressure, often leading to crystallization or condensation

Examples

A supersaturation state

Crystals began to form as soon as supersaturation was achieved in the mixture.
In metallurgy, supersaturation can make metals harder but also more brittle.
When the temperature dropped, the supersaturation of the solution caused crystals to appear.
Scientists carefully monitored the supersaturation level to prevent unwanted reactions.
The experiment demonstrated how supersaturation can lead to rapid crystal growth.
When the air reaches supersaturation, clouds or fog are likely to form.
The scientist observed supersaturation in the solution before crystals began to appear.
Rapid cooling of humid air can result in supersaturation and the formation of ice.

Related Terms

  • antonym: precipitation - the process where dissolved material comes out of solution as a solid
  • degree lower: saturation - the state when no more solute can dissolve in a solvent at a given temperature
