chemokinesis

noun
cell movement response
9.9
Pronunciation: /kiː.moʊ.kɪ.ˈniː.sɪs/ /; / /kə.moʊ.kɪ.ˈniː.sɪs/ (AmE), /kiː.məʊ.kɪ.ˈniː.sɪs/ /; / /kə.məʊ.kɪ.ˈniː.sɪs/ (BrE)
movement or increased activity of cells or organisms in response to a chemical stimulus, without moving in a specific direction

Examples

Chemokinesis in cells
The scientist observed chemokinesis in bacteria exposed to the new chemical compound.
Unlike chemotaxis, chemokinesis does not involve movement toward or away from the chemical source.
Researchers measured the increased chemokinesis of immune cells after treatment with the stimulant.
Scientists observed increased chemokinesis in white blood cells exposed to the new compound.
The study compared chemokinesis and chemotaxis in different types of bacteria.
Certain chemicals can trigger chemokinesis without causing the cells to move in a specific direction.
