indicative mood

noun
mood expressing factual statements or questions
Pronunciation: / ɪnˈdɪk.ə.tɪv mud / (AmE), / ɪnˈdɪk.ətɪv muːd / (BrE)
a grammatical mood used to express statements of fact or questions, as opposed to commands or hypothetical situations

Examples

The indicative mood form
In English, most statements and questions are in the indicative mood.
Teachers often ask students to identify verbs in the indicative mood during grammar lessons.
The sentence 'She walks to school' is an example of the indicative mood.

Related Terms

  • antonym: imperative mood - mood used to express commands or requests
  • antonym: subjunctive mood - mood used to express wishes, hypotheticals, or non-real actions
