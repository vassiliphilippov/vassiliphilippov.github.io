contango

noun
upward futures curve
Pronunciation: / kənˈtæŋɡoʊ / (AmE), / kənˈtæŋɡəʊ / (BrE)
a situation in financial markets where the price for a future delivery is higher than the current price or a nearer delivery price

Examples

Gold contango price
Investors noticed a clear contango in the gold futures market this month.
When the oil market is in contango, storage becomes more profitable for traders.
A persistent contango can signal expectations of rising supply or falling demand.
The oil market entered contango when traders expected prices to rise in the coming months.
Investors monitor contango to decide whether to store commodities or sell them immediately.
During periods of contango, holding futures contracts can be more expensive than buying the commodity now.
Oil markets were in contango, with future prices much higher than the current spot price.
Investors watch for contango to assess potential profits from storing commodities.
A persistent contango can indicate high storage costs or expectations of rising prices.
