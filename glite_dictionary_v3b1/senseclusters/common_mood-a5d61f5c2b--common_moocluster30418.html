common mood

noun
unmarked grammatical mood for objective facts
7.1
Pronunciation: / ˈkɑmən muːd / (AmE), / ˈkɒmən muːd / (BrE)
A grammatical mood that is unmarked and used to state an act or condition as an objective fact.

Examples

The common mood
In many languages, the common mood is used for simple statements of fact.
Most English sentences are in the common mood, such as 'He goes to school.'
Unlike the subjunctive, the common mood does not express wishes or hypotheticals.

Related Terms

  • synonym: indicative mood - the grammatical mood used for factual statements
  • antonym: subjunctive mood - the grammatical mood expressing doubt, wishes, or hypothetical situations
