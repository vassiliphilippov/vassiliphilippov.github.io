sensationalize

verb
to exaggerate for effect
6.8
Pronunciation: /sɛn.ˈseɪ.ʃən.əl.aɪz/ /; / /sɛn.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nə.laɪz/ (AmE), /sɛn.ˈseɪ.ʃən.əl.aɪz/ /; / /sɛn.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nə.laɪz/ (BrE)
to present information in a way that makes it seem more exciting, shocking, or dramatic than it really is

Examples

To sensationalize a story
The newspaper was accused of sensationalizing the story to boost sales.
Some documentaries avoid sensationalizing sensitive topics and focus on facts instead.
Critics claim that the film sensationalizes real-life events for dramatic effect.
The media often sensationalizes celebrity scandals to boost ratings.
Critics accused the documentary of sensationalizing the facts to make the story more dramatic.
Some websites sensationalize headlines to attract more clicks from readers.
Some newspapers tend to sensationalize even minor news stories.
The documentary was criticized for trying to sensationalize the facts to attract more viewers.
It is important not to sensationalize events when reporting on sensitive topics.

Related Terms

  • antonym: downplay - to make something seem less important or serious than it really is
  • synonym: exaggerate - to make something seem larger, better, or worse than it really is
  • antonym: understate - to describe something as less important or serious than it really is
