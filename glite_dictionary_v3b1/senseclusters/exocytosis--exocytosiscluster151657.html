exocytosis

noun
cellular substance release
7.0
Pronunciation: / ˌɛksoʊsaɪˈtoʊsɪs / (AmE), / ˌɛksəʊsaɪˈtəʊsɪs / (BrE)
the process by which a cell releases substances to the outside by fusing a vesicle with its membrane

Examples

Exocytosis process
The hormone is secreted from the cell through exocytosis.
During exocytosis, waste materials are expelled from the cell into the surrounding environment.
Neurotransmitters are released into the synapse by exocytosis in nerve cells.
Neurons use exocytosis to release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft during communication.
The hormone insulin leaves pancreatic cells through exocytosis to enter the bloodstream.
Many waste products are expelled from cells by exocytosis as part of normal cellular function.
During exocytosis, cells expel waste materials by merging vesicles with the outer membrane.
Hormones are often released from gland cells through exocytosis into the bloodstream.
The process of exocytosis is essential for nerve cells to send signals to each other.

Related Terms

  • antonym: endocytosis - a process in which a cell takes in substances by engulfing them with its membrane
  • synonym: secretion - the process of producing and releasing substances from a cell or gland
