Newton's second law

noun
law of physics relating force, mass, and acceleration
3.4
Pronunciation: / ˈnuːtənz ˈsɛkənd lɔ / (AmE), / ˈnjuːtənz ˈsɛkənd lɔː / (BrE)
the law of physics stating that the rate of change of momentum of an object is proportional to the applied force and occurs in the direction of the force; often expressed as Force = mass × acceleration.

Examples

Newton's second law of motion
According to Newton's second law, a greater force results in a greater acceleration for the same mass.
Engineers often use Newton's second law when designing vehicles to ensure proper acceleration and safety.
The physics teacher explained Newton's second law using the example of pushing a shopping cart.

Related Terms

  • degree lower: Newton's first law - law stating that an object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a force
  • degree higher: Newton's third law - law stating that every action has an equal and opposite reaction
