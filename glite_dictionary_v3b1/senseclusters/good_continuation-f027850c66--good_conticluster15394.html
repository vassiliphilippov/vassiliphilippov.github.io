good continuation

noun
Gestalt psychology principle of visual continuity
3.3 6.4
Pronunciation: /gʊd kən.tɪ.nju.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /gʊd kən.tɪ.njuː.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /gɪd kən.tɪ.nju.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /gʊd kənt.ɪ.nju.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /gʊd kənt.ɪ.njʊ.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
a principle in Gestalt psychology that suggests our perception tends to follow the smoothest path, seeing lines or patterns as continuing in their established direction.

Examples

The good continuation principle
According to the principle of good continuation, we naturally perceive intersecting lines as two continuous paths rather than abrupt angles.
Artists often use good continuation to guide the viewer’s eye smoothly across a painting.
The concept of good continuation helps explain why we easily follow the trajectory of a curved road.

Related Terms

  • synonym: Gestalt principle - any of several psychological principles about perception based on Gestalt theory
