put on

verb
add or attach to something existing
1.7 1.30/m
Pronunciation: / pÊŠt É‘n / (AmE), / pÊŠt É’n / (BrE)
to add or attach something to something that already exists, often to extend or enlarge it

Examples

To put on an extension
The couple plans to put on a balcony overlooking the garden.
They decided to put on a new extension to their house last summer.
We may put on another wing to the school to accommodate more students.

Synonyms

  • add on
    3.2 0.44/m
    add on means including something extra to what exists, while put on focuses on attaching or extending something.
  • add
    1.0 211/m
    add means to make something larger by joining extra parts or qualities, while put on often means attaching or extending something.
  • append
    4.6 1.40/m
    append means to attach something to the end of a list or document, while put on means to add or extend something.
  • tack on
    4.1 1.17/m
    tack on means to add something extra as an afterthought, while put on is a neutral term for attaching or extending something.
  • tag on
    3.4 0.06/m
    tag on means to attach or add an extra item often as an afterthought, while put on simply means attach or extend.
  • annex
    6.9 0.43/m
    annex means to add a large part, often territory, to a larger whole, while put on means attach or extend something smaller.
  • affix
    5.0 2.32/m
    affix means to fasten or stick something firmly in place, while put on can mean adding or attaching, not always so formal.
  • stick on
    2.7 0.95/m
    stick on means to attach something to a surface with adhesive so it stays, while put on more generally means to add or extend.
