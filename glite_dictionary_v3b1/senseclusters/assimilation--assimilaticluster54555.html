assimilation

noun
incorporating new ideas into existing knowledge
5.7 4.8
Pronunciation: /ə.sɪ.mə.ˈleɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ə.sɪ.mɪ.ˈleɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /ə.sɪ.mə.ˈleɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ə.sɪ.mɪ.ˈleɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
the process by which new ideas or information are incorporated into an existing mental framework or structure

Examples

Idea assimilation
Children use assimilation when they fit new experiences into their existing ideas about the world.
Good teachers encourage assimilation by connecting new material to what students already know.
Jean Piaget described assimilation as a key process of cognitive development.
According to Piaget, assimilation involves fitting new experiences into pre-existing mental frameworks.
In childhood learning, assimilation helps children interpret unfamiliar situations using what they already know.
The theory suggests that assimilation is complemented by accommodation, leading to cognitive development.

Related Terms

  • antonym: accommodation - the cognitive process of modifying existing mental patterns to incorporate new information; often contrasted with assimilation
