preempt

verb
take the place of; have precedence over
6.8 2.61/m
Pronunciation: / priÀà…õmpt / (AmE), / priÀêÀà…õmpt /; / priÀà…õmpt / (BrE)
to act before someone else in order to prevent something from happening or to take control of a situation

Examples

To preempt a problem
A sudden change in schedule preempted the team's regular practice session.
The breaking news report will preempt the movie scheduled for tonight.
The prime minister's statement preempted questions from the press by addressing their main concerns.
The government acted quickly to preempt a possible crisis in the region.
She spoke first to preempt any objections from the committee members.
The company launched its product early to preempt competitors from entering the market.
The government acted quickly to preempt a possible crisis in the financial markets.
She tried to preempt any complaints by addressing the issues in her presentation.
To preempt confusion, the teacher explained the new rules before the test began.

Synonyms

  • forestall
    7.1 2.19/m
    forestall means acting ahead to stop or delay something, while preempt means acting before others to prevent or take control.

Related Terms

  • antonym: allow - to let something happen without interference
  • synonym: anticipate - to act in expectation of something
  • synonym: forestall - to prevent something by acting ahead of time
  • synonym: supersede - take the place of (a person or thing previously in authority or use)
