Gresham's Law

noun
economic law: bad money drives out good
Pronunciation: / ˈgrɛʃəmz lɑː / (AmE), / ˈgrɛʃəmz lɔː / (BrE)
The economic principle that when two forms of money of equal face value are in circulation, the one with less intrinsic value tends to circulate more, while the more valuable one is hoarded and disappears from circulation; bad money drives out good.

Examples

Gresham's Law principle
According to Gresham's Law, people will spend coins composed of cheaper metals and keep those made of precious metals.
Historians often cite Gresham's Law to explain the disappearance of silver coins from circulation.
When governments debase their currency, Gresham's Law can help predict which coins will vanish first.
