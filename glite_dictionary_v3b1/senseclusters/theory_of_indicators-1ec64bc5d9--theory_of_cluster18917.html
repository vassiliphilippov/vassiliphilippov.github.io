theory of indicators

noun

Pronunciation: /ˈθiː.ə.ri ə ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.tərz/ /; / /ˈθɪə.ri ə ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.tərz/ (AmE), /ˈθɪə.ri ə ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.təz/ /; / /ˈθiː.ə.ri ə ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.təz/ (BrE)
(Chemistry) The theory that all indicators are either weak acids or weak bases whose ionized and non-ionized forms have different colors, enabling them to indicate pH changes.

Examples

Theory of indicators in chemistry
A detailed understanding of the theory of indicators is essential for analytical chemistry experiments.
According to the theory of indicators, the visible color change depends on the different forms of the indicator in solution.
The theory of indicators explains how dyes like phenolphthalein work in titrations.
