Kepler's second law

noun
law describing variation in a planet’s orbital speed
7.9
Pronunciation: / ˈkɛplɚz ˈsɛkənd lɑ / (AmE), / ˈkɛpləz ˈsɛkənd lɔː / (BrE)
the law stating that a line joining a planet and the sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time, describing how a planet's speed varies along its orbit

Examples

Kepler's second law of motion
According to Kepler's second law, planets move faster when they are closer to the sun in their orbit.
Astronomers use Kepler's second law to calculate how long it takes a planet to travel certain sections of its orbit.
The changing speed of a planet as it travels around the Sun can be explained by Kepler's second law.

Related Terms

  • synonym: Kepler's first law - the law stating that planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus
  • synonym: Kepler's third law - the law relating the square of a planet's orbital period to the cube of its average distance from the Sun
