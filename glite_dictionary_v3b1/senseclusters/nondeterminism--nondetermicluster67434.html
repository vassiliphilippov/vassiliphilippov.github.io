nondeterminism

noun
multiple possible outcomes
Pronunciation: / ˌnɑndɪˈtɜrmɪˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌnɒndɪˈtɜːmɪnɪzəm / (BrE)
the property or quality of a system or process where the same starting point can lead to different possible outcomes, often used in computing or mathematics

Examples

Nondeterminism in computing
In theoretical computer science, nondeterminism allows a machine to follow several possible computation paths.
The concept of nondeterminism is important when analyzing the complexity of algorithms.
Some problems can be solved more efficiently if nondeterminism is permitted in the computational model.
The presence of nondeterminism in the system made it difficult to reproduce certain bugs.
Researchers studied how nondeterminism affected the results of parallel computing experiments.
Unexpected nondeterminism can occur when multiple processes access shared resources at the same time.
In computer science, nondeterminism can make it difficult to predict how a program will behave.
The presence of nondeterminism in a system means the same input may lead to different results.
Researchers study nondeterminism to understand how randomness affects complex processes.

Related Terms

  • antonym: determinism - the property of having only one possible outcome from a given starting point
  • synonym: randomness - the quality of lacking a predictable pattern or plan
