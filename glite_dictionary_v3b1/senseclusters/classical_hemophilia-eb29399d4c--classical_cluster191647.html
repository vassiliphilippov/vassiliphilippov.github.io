classical hemophilia

noun
hereditary bleeding disorder caused by lack of factor VIII
6.9 4.2
Pronunciation: /ˈklæs.ɪ.kəl hiː.mə.ˈfɪl.iː.ə/ (AmE), /ˈklæs.ɪ.kəl hiː.mə.ˈfɪl.iː.ə/ (BrE)
A hereditary bleeding disorder caused by a congenital deficiency of factor VIII, resulting in prolonged bleeding; also known as hemophilia A, occurring primarily in males.

Examples

Classical hemophilia bleeding disorder
Because classical hemophilia is an X-linked disorder, it almost always affects males.
The patient was diagnosed with classical hemophilia after several unexplained episodes of severe bleeding.
Treatment for classical hemophilia often involves infusions of factor VIII concentrate.
Because classical haemophilia is X-linked, it rarely affects women but is more common in men.
Genetic testing can confirm a diagnosis of classical haemophilia in families with a history of the disorder.
Patients with classical haemophilia require regular infusions of clotting factor VIII to prevent bleeding episodes.

Related Terms

  • synonym: hemophilia - genetic disorder impairing blood's ability to clot
