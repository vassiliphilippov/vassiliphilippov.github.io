pleasure principle

noun
psychoanalytic concept of instant gratification
2.2 6.9
Pronunciation: /ˈpleʒ.ər ˈprɪn.sə.pəl/ /; / /ˈpleʒ.ər ˈprɪn.sɪ.pəl/ (AmE), /ˈple.ʒə ˈprɪn.sɪ.pəl/ /; / /ˈple.ʒə ˈprɪn.sə.pəl/ (BrE)
(In psychoanalysis) the instinctual demand of the id to seek immediate gratification of desires, regardless of reality or consequences.

Examples

The pleasure principle in psychology
According to Freud, the pleasure principle drives the id to pursue immediate satisfaction of needs and desires.
Children often operate under the pleasure principle, acting on impulse rather than reason.
The conflict between the pleasure principle and the reality principle is central to psychoanalytic theory.

Related Terms

  • antonym: reality principle - (in psychoanalysis) the ability of the mind to assess the external world and act upon it accordingly, often in opposition to the pleasure principle
