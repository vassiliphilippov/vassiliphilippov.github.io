Dalton's law of partial pressures

noun
law on total pressure of gas mixtures
7.0
Pronunciation: /ˈdɔːl.tənz lɔː əv ˈpɑːr.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.ərz/ /; / /ˈdɔːl.tənz lɑː əv ˈpɑːr.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.ərz/ /; / /ˈdæl.tənz lɔː əv ˈpɑːr.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.ərz/ (AmE), /ˈdɔːl.tənz lɔː əv ˈpɑː.ʃəl ˈpreʃ.əz/ (BrE)
A law stating that the total pressure exerted by a mixture of gases equals the sum of the partial pressures each gas would exert if it alone occupied the entire volume at the same temperature.

Examples

Dalton's law of partial pressures states
According to Dalton's law of partial pressures, the total pressure in the vessel was the sum of all the gases present.
Chemists often rely on Dalton's law of partial pressures when analyzing the behavior of gas mixtures.
When mixing oxygen and nitrogen, Dalton's law of partial pressures helps predict the resulting pressure.

Related Terms

  • synonym: partial pressure - the pressure a single gas in a mixture would exert if it alone occupied the volume
