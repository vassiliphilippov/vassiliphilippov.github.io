retake

verb
take back by force (esp. in battle)

to take back possession or control of something, especially after it has been lost or taken

Examples

To retake the city
It took several days for the forces to retake the border post from the enemy.
Rebels tried to retake the region that had been occupied for months.
The army was able to retake the city after a fierce battle.
Police worked quickly to retake control of the building from the intruders.
The army managed to retake the city after days of fierce fighting.
They planned a mission to retake the hostages from their captors.

Synonyms

  • regain
    3.3 17/m

  • take back
    3.0 2.00/m
    take back means to recover possession you once gave or lent, while retake often describes taking control again quickly.
  • recapture
    5.6 2.26/m
    recapture means to take back control, often after escape or loss, while retake is a general act of taking back.
  • reclaim
    4.1 4.77/m
    reclaim means to claim or recover something as yours again, while retake focuses on physically taking back control.
  • get back
    1.6 40/m
    get back means to recover something you lost in casual speech, while retake sounds more formal or planned when seizing control.
  • win back
    3.4 1.30/m
    win back means to recover something through effort or persuasion, while retake usually means taking back direct physical control.
  • reacquire
    5.5 0.24/m
    reacquire means to obtain something again, often by buying or formal means, while retake implies taking back possession directly.
  • retrieve
    3.7 18/m
    retrieve means to go fetch and bring something back, often calmly, while retake implies seizing control from someone or somewhere.
