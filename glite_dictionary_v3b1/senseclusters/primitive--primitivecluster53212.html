primitive

noun
function from which another is derived (math)
7.0
Pronunciation: / ˈprɪm.ə.tɪv /; / ˈprɪm.ɪ.t̬ɪv / (AmE), / ˈprɪm.ɪ.tɪv / (BrE)
a basic or simple element, idea, or form that is the foundation for something more complex (such as in math, language, or technology)

Examples

A primitive in math
In calculus, finding a primitive is the inverse process of differentiation.
Physicists often use the primitive of a velocity function to determine displacement.
The primitive of a continuous function can be found using integration.
In language studies, linguists often identify the primitive of a word family to trace its origins.
The dictionary lists 'act' as the primitive for words like 'action', 'active', and 'actor'.
The verb 'pick' is considered the primitive from which the noun 'picket' is derived.
In computer graphics, lines and polygons are considered primitives for building images.
The artist started with a few primitives before adding details to the painting.
Mathematics often uses primitives like points and lines as the basis for more complex concepts.

Related Terms

  • synonym: antiderivative - a function whose derivative is a given function
  • antonym: complex - something made up of many interconnected parts
  • synonym: component - a part or element of a larger whole
  • antonym: derivative - a word formed from another by derivation, such as by adding a prefix or suffix
  • synonym: fundamental - a central or essential part or principle
  • synonym: root - the basic part of a word, to which affixes can be added in derivation
