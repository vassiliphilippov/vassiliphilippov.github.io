Tripitaka

noun
Buddhist scripture collection
Pronunciation: / trɪˈpɪtəkə / (AmE), / trɪˈpɪtəkə / (BrE)
the main collection of Buddhist scriptures, traditionally divided into three parts (Vinaya, Sutra, and Abhidharma), also known as the Pali Canon

Examples

The Tripitaka scriptures
The ancient Tripitaka contains teachings that are central to Buddhist philosophy.
Scholars spent years translating the Tripitaka into modern languages for wider study.
A digital version of the Tripitaka is now available for researchers around the world.
Buddhist monks dedicate years to studying the Tripitaka in order to understand the teachings of the Buddha.
The Tripitaka is divided into three sections, each focusing on a different aspect of Buddhist doctrine.
Scholars often compare the Tripitaka to other major religious texts in terms of influence and structure.
Monks spend years studying the Tripitaka to deepen their understanding of Buddhist teachings.
The Tripitaka is considered the most authoritative scripture in Theravada Buddhism.
Translations of the Tripitaka have made Buddhist philosophy accessible to people around the world.

  • synonym: Pali Canon - the authoritative scriptures of Theravada Buddhism, also known as the Tipitaka
