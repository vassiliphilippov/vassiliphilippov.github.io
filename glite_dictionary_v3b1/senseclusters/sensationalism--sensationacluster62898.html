sensationalism

noun
philosophical doctrine: feeling as sole moral criterion
5.1 6.6
Pronunciation: /sens.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ /; / /səns.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ (AmE), /sens.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ /; / /səns.ˈseɪ.ʃə.nəl.ɪ.zəm/ (BrE)
(Philosophy) the doctrine that sensation or sensory experience is the basis of knowledge or ethics

Examples

[Philosophy] sensationalism theory
Critics of sensationalism claim it fails to consider rational or objective standards of right and wrong.
His theory of ethics is rooted in sensationalism, prioritizing sensation above reason or tradition.
Some philosophers advocate sensationalism as a basis for morality, arguing that only feelings can determine good.
Critics of sensationalism argue that reason also plays a vital role in gaining knowledge.
In the lecture, she explained sensationalism as the view that all ideas arise from physical sensations.
The philosophy department offered a course on sensationalism and other theories of perception.

Related Terms

  • synonym: empiricism - the philosophical belief that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience
  • synonym: hedonism - the doctrine that pleasure or happiness is the highest good
  • antonym: rationalism - the philosophical doctrine that knowledge comes primarily from reason
  • antonym: utilitarianism - the doctrine that actions are right if they benefit the majority
