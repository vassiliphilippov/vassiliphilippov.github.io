humor

noun
one of four bodily fluids in medieval physiology
3.7
Pronunciation: / ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
(historical) any of the four bodily fluids (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile) once believed to affect health and temperament

Examples

Medieval humor theory
According to medieval medicine, each humor was associated with a particular temperament and element.
Doctors once believed that an imbalance of humors could cause illness or changes in personality.
In Shakespeare's plays, references to the four humors reflect popular medical beliefs of his era.
Early physicians believed that an imbalance of the humors could cause disease.
It was once thought that personality traits depended on the proportions of different humors in the body.
Medieval medicine was based on the theory of the four humors.
According to medieval medicine, an excess of any humour could cause disease.
The ancient Greeks believed that the balance of bodily humours affected a person's health and temperament.
The eye contains two main humours: the aqueous humour and the vitreous humour.
