King's shilling

noun
token of enlistment in British army
4.6 9.9
Pronunciation: /kɪŋz ˈʃɪl.ɪŋ/ (AmE), /kɪŋz ˈʃɪl.ɪŋ/ (BrE)
a historical term referring to the money given to a recruit as a token of their enlistment in the British army, often used metaphorically to symbolize acceptance of military service and loyalty to the Crown

Examples

Taking the King's shilling
In those days, taking the King's shilling meant a binding commitment to serve.
Many young men found themselves in the army after accepting the King's shilling in a local tavern.
Once you had the King's shilling in your pocket, turning back was no longer an option.
