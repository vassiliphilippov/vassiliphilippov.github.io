Doppler effect

noun
change in wave frequency due to relative motion
8.3 5.4
Pronunciation: / ˈdɑplɚ ɪˌfɛkt / (AmE), / ˈdɒplə ɪˌfɛkt / (BrE)
the change in the observed frequency or wavelength of a wave when the source and observer are moving relative to each other

Examples

The Doppler effect in waves
Astronomers use the Doppler effect to measure the speed at which stars move away from Earth.
The Doppler effect explains why an ambulance siren changes pitch as it passes by.
Weather radars rely on the Doppler effect to detect the speed and movement of precipitation.

Related Terms

  • degree lower: blueshift - decrease in wavelength of electromagnetic waves due to source approaching
  • degree higher: redshift - increase in wavelength of electromagnetic waves due to source moving away
