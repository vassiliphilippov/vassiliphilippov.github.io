hyperbolic geometry

noun
branch of non-Euclidean geometry
5.9 8.4
Pronunciation: /haɪ.pər.ˈbɑː.lɪk dʒiː.ˈɑː.mə.triː/ /; / /haɪ.pər.ˈbɔː.lɪk dʒiː.ˈɑː.mə.triː/ (AmE), /haɪ.pə.ˈbɒ.lɪk dʒiː.ˈɒ.mɪ.trɪ/ /; / /haɪ.pə.ˈbɒ.lɪk ˈdʒɒ.mɪ.trɪ/ (BrE)
a branch of non-Euclidean geometry in which, given a line and a point not on it, there exist at least two distinct parallel lines through the point that do not intersect the given line

Examples

Hyperbolic geometry branch
In hyperbolic geometry, triangles have angle sums of less than 180 degrees.
Karl Gauss pioneered hyperbolic geometry in the nineteenth century.
Most undergraduate mathematics programs introduce students to hyperbolic geometry and its differences from classical geometry.

Related Terms

  • antonym: Euclidean geometry - the geometry based on Euclid's axioms, especially the parallel postulate
  • degree lower: elliptic geometry - a non-Euclidean geometry in which no parallel lines exist
