structuralism

noun
anthropological theory of underlying structures
Pronunciation: / ˈstrʌktʃɚəˌlɪzəm /; / ˈstrʌktʃərəˌlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈstrʌktʃərəlɪzəm /; / ˈstrʌktʃərəlɪz(ə)m / (BrE)
a theory or approach in fields like anthropology, linguistics, or sociology that analyzes things by focusing on underlying structures rather than individual elements

Examples

Structuralism in anthropology
Claude Lévi-Strauss is widely credited with developing structuralism in anthropology.
Some critics argue that structuralism downplays the role of individual agency in society.
The influence of structuralism can be seen in modern literary and cultural theory.
Although structuralism has declined in influence, it remains foundational in linguistic theory.
Many literary critics adopted structuralism to analyze recurring patterns and systems in texts.
The rise of structuralism transformed the study of language in the twentieth century.
Structuralism argues that the relationships among parts of society shape individual behavior.
Many 20th-century thinkers adopted structuralism to analyze the underlying patterns of culture.
The anthropologist’s research was influenced by structuralism, focusing on systems rather than individuals.

Related Terms

  • antonym: functionalism - anthropological theory emphasizing social functions
  • antonym: individualism - the belief that individuals are more important than societal structures
  • antonym: poststructuralism - a movement reacting against structuralism, emphasizing instability of meaning
