voluntarism

noun
theological doctrine of will
Pronunciation: / ˈvɑːlənˌtɛrɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈvɒlənˌtɪərɪzəm /; / ˈvɒlənˌtɛərɪzəm / (BrE)
the philosophical or religious belief that will (God's or human) is the main force, more important than reason or intellect

Examples

Philosophical voluntarism
In medieval philosophy, voluntarism emphasized the supremacy of God's will over human understanding.
The debate between voluntarism and intellectualism shaped much of early theological thought.
Some scholars argue that voluntarism influenced later discussions about free will and morality.
Some philosophers argue that voluntarism places too much emphasis on personal will over rational thought.
The theory of voluntarism influenced debates about free will and moral responsibility in the nineteenth century.
In his writings, he defends voluntarism as the foundation of human creativity and decision-making.

Related Terms

  • antonym: determinism - the doctrine that all events are determined by causes external to the will
  • antonym: intellectualism - the doctrine that reason is the primary source of knowledge or moral law
  • antonym: rationalism - the belief that reason is the chief source of knowledge
