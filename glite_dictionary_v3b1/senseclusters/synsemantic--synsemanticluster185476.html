synsemantic

adjective
needs other words to have meaning (linguistics)
6.4 9.4
Pronunciation: /sɪn.sɪ.ˈmæn.tɪk/ /; / /sɪn.sə.ˈmæn.tɪk/ (AmE), /sɪn.sɪ.ˈmæn.tɪk/ /; / /sɪn.sə.ˈmæn.tɪk/ (BrE)
(of a word or phrase) meaningful only when it appears together with other words; not having independent meaning.

Examples

Synsemantic words
Articles such as 'the' or 'an' are classic examples of synsemantic words in many languages.
Auxiliary verbs like 'can' and 'will' are often classified as synsemantic rather than autosemantic.
In linguistics, prepositions are typically considered synsemantic because their full meaning depends on accompanying words.

Related Terms

  • antonym: autosemantic - having meaning independently; can stand alone
