Coriolis effect

noun
deflection of moving objects due to Earth's rotation
8.4 6.7
Pronunciation: /kɔːr.iː.ˈoʊ.lɪs ɪ.ˈfekt/ /; / /kɔːr.iː.ˈoʊ.lɪs ə.ˈfekt/ (AmE), /kɒ.riː.ˈəʊ.lɪs ɪ.ˈfekt/ /; / /kɒ.riː.ˈəʊ.lɪs ə.ˈfekt/ (BrE)
(physics) the apparent deflection of moving objects when viewed in a rotating reference frame, such as the deflection of winds and currents on Earth caused by its rotation.

Examples

The Coriolis effect on winds
Ocean currents are influenced by the Coriolis effect, leading them to curve as they flow across the globe.
Pilots must account for the Coriolis effect when plotting long-distance flight paths.
The Coriolis effect causes hurricanes in the Northern Hemisphere to spin counterclockwise.

Related Terms

  • synonym: Coriolis force - the force responsible for the Coriolis effect in a rotating system
