major premise

noun
main premise in a syllogism
Pronunciation: / ˈmeɪdʒər ˈprɛmɪs / (AmE), / ˈmeɪdʒə ˈprɛmɪs / (BrE)
The premise in a syllogism that contains the major term, which is the predicate of the conclusion.

Examples

A major premise in logic
A valid syllogism must have both a major premise and a minor premise.
In the syllogism 'All humans are mortal; Socrates is human; therefore Socrates is mortal,' the major premise is 'All humans are mortal.'
The major premise always contains the predicate term of the conclusion.

Related Terms

  • antonym: minor premise - the premise of a syllogism containing the minor term (subject of the conclusion)
