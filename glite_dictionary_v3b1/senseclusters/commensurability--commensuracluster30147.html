commensurability

noun
having a common measure
9.9
Pronunciation: /kə.men.sə.rə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ /; / /kə.men.sjə.rə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ (AmE), /kə.men.sə.rə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ /; / /kə.men.sjə.rə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ (BrE)
the property of two quantities having a common measure, so their ratio is a rational number

Examples

Commensurability of lengths
In ancient Greek mathematics, the commensurability of two line segments was an important concept.
The commensurability of the time intervals allowed the scientists to compare their results easily.
If two lengths lack commensurability, their ratio cannot be expressed as a simple fraction.
