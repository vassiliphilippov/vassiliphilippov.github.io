fight off

verb
repel or resist an attack or threat
3.7 2.42/m
Pronunciation: / faÉªt É‘f / (AmE), / faÉªt É’f / (BrE)
to succeed in repelling or resisting something, especially an attack, threat, or unwanted feeling.

Examples

To fight off illness
Despite feeling sleepy, he struggled to fight off his drowsiness during the long meeting.
She tried to fight off a bad cold by getting plenty of rest and drinking water.
The soldiers managed to fight off the enemy forces until reinforcements arrived.

Synonyms

  • fend off
    3.9 2.89/m
    fend off is an everyday verb for pushing away an attacker or problem, while fight off stresses succeeding in resisting it.
  • ward off
    4.4 3.50/m
    ward off is used to keep harm or illness away before it starts, while fight off describes actively repelling something that attacks you.
  • repel
    4.2 1.54/m
    repel is a direct, often formal verb for forcing something away, while fight off is more casual and often used for feelings or sickness.
  • repulse
    7.0 0.48/m
    repulse often shows strong rejection or disgust toward people or attacks, while fight off focuses on resisting or surviving an attack.
  • drive back
    4.1 1.08/m
    drive back means force someone or something to retreat from a place, while fight off means repel or resist the threat itself.
  • hold off
    4.0 1.43/m
    hold off means delay or keep an opponent from advancing for a time, while fight off means fully repel or overcome it.
  • rebuff
    7.9 0.30/m
    rebuff usually means reject an approach or offer bluntly in speech or action, while fight off refers to physically or mentally resisting attack.
  • combat
    4.1 9.48/m
    combat means actively fight against a long-term problem or danger, while fight off suggests a short, successful act of repelling something.
