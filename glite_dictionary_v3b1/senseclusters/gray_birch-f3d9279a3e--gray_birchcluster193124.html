gray birch

noun
North American birch tree with pale bark
6.9
Pronunciation: / ɡreɪ bɝtʃ /; / ɡreɪ bɝːtʃ / (AmE), / ɡreɪ bɜːtʃ / (BrE)
A medium-sized birch tree native to eastern North America, with white or pale grey bark, typically found in second-growth or disturbed areas, and with wood considered of little value.

Examples

The gray birch tree
The gray birch is often found in abandoned fields and burned areas across the Northeast.
The wood of the gray birch is not used commercially due to its poor quality.
Unlike the white birch, gray birch has triangular leaves and a more delicate appearance.
After the fire, grey birch quickly colonized the area, forming a young, dense forest.
The grey birch is commonly found in abandoned fields and open woodland in the northeastern United States.
You can recognize grey birch by its smooth, papery pale bark and triangular leaves.

Related Terms

  • synonym: paper birch - a birch species with white bark used for making paper, native to North America
  • synonym: white birch - A birch tree (Betula papyrifera) with white, papery bark often confused with gray birch
