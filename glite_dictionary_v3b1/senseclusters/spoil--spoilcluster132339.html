spoil

verb
make something less good or unusable
2.4 2.4
Pronunciation: /spɔɪl/ (AmE), /spɔɪl/ (BrE)
to damage, ruin, or make something less good, useful, or enjoyable

Examples

To spoil a surprise
If you leave milk out of the fridge, it will spoil quickly.
She didn't want to spoil the surprise by telling him the secret.
Too much sunlight can spoil old photographs, causing them to fade.
Careless handling can easily spoil delicate equipment.
One mistake could spoil an otherwise perfect performance.
The loud noise threatened to spoil the peaceful atmosphere of the library.
A sudden rainstorm could spoil our plans for a picnic in the park.
If you overcook the vegetables, it will spoil the entire meal.
She didn’t mean to spoil the surprise, but she accidentally let the secret slip.

Related Terms

  • antonym: fix - to repair or restore something to a good condition
  • synonym: foil - to prevent something considered wrong from succeeding
  • synonym: frustrate - to prevent someone from achieving something
  • antonym: improve - to make something better or enhance its quality
  • synonym: mar - to damage or spoil the appearance or quality of something
  • antonym: repair - to restore something damaged to a good condition
  • synonym: ruin - to damage something so badly that it loses its value or function
  • synonym: thwart - to prevent someone from carrying out their plans
  • synonym: wreck - to cause serious damage to something
