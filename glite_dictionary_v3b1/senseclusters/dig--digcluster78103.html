dig

verb
remove inner part or core by excavation
Pronunciation: / dɪɡ / (AmE), / dɪɡ / (BrE)
to break up, remove, or move earth or other material, usually with a tool or machine; to make a hole or excavation

Examples

To dig a hole
Archaeologists dig into ancient mounds to reveal hidden chambers and artifacts.
The workers had to dig out the center of the old stump to remove it.
They plan to dig the hillside to create room for the new tunnel.
Farmers dig the soil every spring to prepare for planting.
The children love to dig holes in the garden with their small shovels.
We had to dig a trench for the new water pipe.
He dug the shovel into the hard, frozen ground.
She dug her heels into the sand for extra stability.
The surgeon dug his fingers into the wound to locate the object.

Related Terms

  • antonym: bury - to put or hide under ground
  • synonym: excavate - to make a hole or channel by digging; to remove earth carefully and systematically
  • synonym: jab - to push something quickly and forcefully into a surface
  • synonym: mine - to extract (coal, minerals, etc.) from the earth
