low-information

adjective
describing people lacking factual political knowledge

Used especially in political contexts to describe a person or group perceived as having little factual knowledge about an issue and as relying on superficial cues (slogans, personalities) rather than substantive evidence.

Examples

Low-information voters
Critics accused low-information voters of being swayed by slogans rather than careful consideration.
During the debate, the campaign targeted low-information districts with simple messages and ads.
Journalists warned that relying on polls of a low-information public can mislead political predictions.

Related Terms

  • antonym: well-informed - having or showing a lot of knowledge about a subject; the opposite of being poorly informed.
