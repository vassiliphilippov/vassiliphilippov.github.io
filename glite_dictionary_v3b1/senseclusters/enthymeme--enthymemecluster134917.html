enthymeme

noun
incomplete syllogism
9.9
Pronunciation: / ˈɛnθɪˌmiːm / (AmE), / ˈɛnθɪmiːm / (BrE)
an argument in which one premise is left unstated or implied, relying on the audience to supply it

Examples

An enthymeme argument
The speaker used an enthymeme to persuade the audience without stating every assumption.
In classical rhetoric, an enthymeme is often more effective than a formal syllogism.
Her argument was an enthymeme, expecting listeners to fill in the missing premise themselves.
The philosopher explained how an enthymeme relies on the audience to fill in missing premises.
In persuasive writing, an enthymeme can make arguments seem more natural and convincing.
She recognized the enthymeme in his speech because not every assumption was stated outright.
