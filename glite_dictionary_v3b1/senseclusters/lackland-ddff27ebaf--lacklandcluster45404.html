Lackland

proper noun
historical epithet
Pronunciation: / ˈlækˌlænd / (AmE), / ˈlækˌlænd / (BrE)
a historical epithet meaning 'one without land', notably used for King John of England and in some place names

Examples

King John Lackland
King John was often called Lackland because he inherited little property from his father.
The title Lackland appears in several medieval documents describing English royalty.
Some places in the United States are named Lackland after historical figures.
