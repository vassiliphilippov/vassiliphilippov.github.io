mercantilism

noun
economic policy favoring regulated national wealth
9.9 8.1
Pronunciation: /mər.ˈkæn.təl.ɪ.zəm/ (AmE), /mə.ˈkæn.təl.ɪ.zəm/ /; / /mɜː.ˈkæn.təl.ɪ.zəm/ (BrE)
an economic theory and system, popular in 16th-18th century Europe, that promoted government regulation of trade to increase a nation's wealth and power

Examples

Mercantilism economic theory
Historians debate the long-term impacts of mercantilism on colonial expansion.
Many European countries adopted mercantilism to compete for global influence and wealth.
The decline of mercantilism led to the rise of free-market economic theories.
British government policies during the 18th century were shaped largely by principles of mercantilism.
Many historians debate the long-term effects of mercantilism on global trade.
The rise of mercantilism in Europe influenced the colonization of the Americas.

Related Terms

  • antonym: free trade - international trade left to its natural course without tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions
  • synonym: protectionism - the policy of protecting domestic industries by restricting imports
