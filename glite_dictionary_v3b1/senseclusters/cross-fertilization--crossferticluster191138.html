cross-fertilization

noun
fertilization by gametes from different individuals
4.4
Pronunciation: / ˌkrɔsˌfɜrt̬əlɪˈzeɪʃən /; / krɔs fɚtɪləˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkrɒsˌfɜːtɪlaɪˈzeɪʃən /; / krɒs fəːtɪlʌɪˈzeɪʃən / (BrE)
The process in which an egg from one individual is fertilized by sperm from a different individual of the same species.

Examples

Plant cross-fertilization
Cross-fertilization in plants increases genetic diversity, leading to stronger and more adaptable offspring.
Some animals depend on cross-fertilization to maintain healthy populations and avoid inbreeding.
The botanist explained how cross-fertilization happens naturally when insects carry pollen between flowers.
Biologists observed that cross-fertilisation led to healthier populations in controlled experiments.
Many plants rely on cross-fertilisation to increase genetic diversity and resilience to disease.
The absence of pollinators can greatly reduce the chance of cross-fertilisation in flowering plants.
