exclusion principle

noun
quantum physics principle about identical particles
7.0 7.8
Pronunciation: /ɪk.ˈskluː.ʒən ˈprɪn.sə.pəl/ /; / /ɪks.ˈkluː.ʒən ˈprɪn.sə.pəl/ (AmE), /ɪk.ˈskluː.ʒən ˈprɪn.sɪ.pəl/ /; / /ɪks.ˈkluː.ʒən ˈprɪn.sɪ.pəl/ (BrE)
(in physics) the principle that no two identical fermions (such as electrons, protons, or neutrons) in a given system may occupy quantum states with the same set of quantum numbers.

Examples

The exclusion principle in physics
Because of the exclusion principle, no two fermions can be in the same quantum state simultaneously.
Quantum chemistry relies on the exclusion principle to predict the structure of molecules.
The exclusion principle explains why electrons in an atom occupy different energy levels.

Related Terms

  • synonym: Pauli exclusion principle - the specific principle as formulated by Wolfgang Pauli, applied to electrons
