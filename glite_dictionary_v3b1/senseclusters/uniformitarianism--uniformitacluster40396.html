uniformitarianism

noun
scientific principle of consistency
Pronunciation: / ˌjuːnəˌfɔrmɪˈtɛriəˌnɪzəm /; / ˌjuːnəˌfɔrmɪˈtɛriənɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌjuːnɪˌfɔːmɪˈteəriəˌnɪzəm /; / ˌjuːnɪˌfɔːmɪˈteəriənɪzəm / (BrE)
the scientific principle that the same natural laws and processes we see today have always operated in the past and apply everywhere (especially in geology)

Examples

The uniformitarianism theory
The theory of uniformitarianism helped scientists understand how mountains and valleys formed over millions of years.
Geologists rely on uniformitarianism to interpret rock layers and ancient landscapes.
Without uniformitarianism, it would be difficult to explain the gradual changes seen in Earth's surface.
The principle of uniformitarianism is fundamental to modern geology.
Charles Lyell promoted uniformitarianism in his influential book on Earth’s history.
According to uniformitarianism, the forces shaping the planet today have always been at work.

Related Terms

  • synonym: actualism - the idea that current processes explain past events
  • antonym: catastrophism - the theory that Earth's features formed by sudden, short-lived events
