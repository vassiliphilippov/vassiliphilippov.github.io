drag on

verb
continue for too long
Pronunciation: / dræɡ ɑn / (AmE), / dræɡ ɒn / (BrE)
to continue for longer than is necessary or expected, often becoming boring or tedious

Examples

To drag on too long
Her speech started well, but it began to drag on and lose the audience's attention.
Negotiations can drag on when neither side wants to compromise.
The meeting seemed to drag on for hours without any resolution.
As the trial continued to drag on, the witnesses grew increasingly frustrated.
The meeting seemed to drag on forever, leaving everyone exhausted by the end.
Winter tends to drag on in northern regions, with snow lasting late into April.

Their negotiations dragged on for months without any significant agreement.
We hope this legal case won’t drag on for years.
