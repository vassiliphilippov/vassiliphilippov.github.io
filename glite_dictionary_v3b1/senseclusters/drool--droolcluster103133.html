drool

verb
show strong desire or eagerness for something
6.3 1.47/m
Pronunciation: / druÀêl / (AmE), / druÀêl / (BrE)
(informal) To show strong desire or eagerness for something, often in a way that is obvious or exaggerated.

Examples

To drool over food
He was practically drooling with envy when his friend showed off the new car.
I can't help but drool over the latest gadgets in the store.
The children drooled at the sight of the enormous chocolate cake.

Synonyms

  • drool over
    6.8 0.40/m
    drool over is used when someone stares at or praises something with obvious longing, while drool is general eager desire.
  • salivate
    6.1 0.62/m
    salivate describes eager, almost physical anticipation often in formal or written language, while drool is an informal, obvious show of desire.
  • slobber
    4.8 0.88/m
    slobber usually means let saliva drip or shows messy, uncontrolled desire, while drool is a more general informal eagerness.

Related Terms

  • synonym: covet - to long for something that belongs to someone else
  • synonym: salivate - to show eager anticipation for something, often used figuratively
