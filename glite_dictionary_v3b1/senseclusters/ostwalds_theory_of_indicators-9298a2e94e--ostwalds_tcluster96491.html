Ostwald's theory of indicators

noun
chemical theory on acid/base indicators
9.9
Pronunciation: /ˈɑːst.wɑːldz ˈθiː.ər.iː ˈɪn.də.keɪ.tərz/ /; / /ˈɑːst.wɑːldz ˈθɪə.riː ˈɪn.də.keɪ.tərz/ (AmE), /ˈɒst.wɔːldz ˈθɪə.ri ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.təz/ /; / /ˈɒst.wɒldz ˈθɪə.ri ˈɪn.dɪ.keɪ.təz/ (BrE)
the chemical theory stating that all indicators are either weak acids or weak bases whose ionized and non-ionized forms have different colors, explaining the color changes during titration

Examples

Ostwald's theory of indicators in chemistry
According to Ostwald's theory of indicators, the color change is due to the different colors of the ionized and unionized forms.
In chemistry class, we studied Ostwald's theory of indicators to understand the behavior of litmus and phenolphthalein.
The Ostwald's theory of indicators explains why some substances change color when an acid or base is added.
