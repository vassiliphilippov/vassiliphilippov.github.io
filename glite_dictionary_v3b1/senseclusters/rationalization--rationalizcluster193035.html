rationalization

noun
attempt to justify or explain with reasons
Pronunciation: / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən /; / ˌræʃənələˈzeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃən /; / ˌræʃənəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
the act of explaining or justifying actions or feelings with logical reasons, even if these are not true or appropriate (including in psychology)

Examples

Personal rationalization
He used elaborate rationalization to defend his questionable decisions at work.
Her rationalization for missing the meeting was that she needed more time to prepare.
Sometimes, people resort to rationalization when they feel guilty about their actions.
His constant rationalization of bad decisions prevents him from realizing his true motivations.
In therapy, he learned to recognize when he was using rationalization to avoid uncomfortable truths.
She offered a rationalization for missing the meeting, but her colleagues sensed there was another reason.
He offered a detailed rationalisation for his decision, though it seemed forced.
Her rationalisation of her lateness was that traffic had been unusually heavy.
Sometimes, people use rationalisation to defend actions they secretly regret.

Related Terms

  • antonym: denial - a defense mechanism where unpleasant realities are ignored or refused to be accepted
  • synonym: justification - the action of showing something to be right or reasonable
  • synonym: rationalization - American spelling of rationalisation
