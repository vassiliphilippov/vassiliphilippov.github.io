direct discourse

noun
exact speech quoted in reported language
6.4 5.1
Pronunciation: /də.ˈrekt ˈdɪs.kɔːrs/ /; / /daɪ.ˈrekt ˈdɪs.kɔːrs/ (AmE), /dɪ.ˈrekt ˈdɪs.kɔːs/ /; / /daɪ.ˈrekt ˈdɪs.kɔːs/ (BrE)
the reporting of speech or thought by repeating the exact words used by a speaker or writer, usually within quotation marks

Examples

Direct discourse in writing
He preferred direct discourse in his journalism to capture the subject’s own voice.
In English classes, students are taught to use direct discourse when quoting an author’s words.
Whenever dialogue appears in novels, it is presented as direct discourse within quotation marks.

Related Terms

  • antonym: indirect discourse - reporting what someone said using different words, not a direct quotation
