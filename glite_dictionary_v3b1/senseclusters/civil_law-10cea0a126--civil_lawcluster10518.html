civil law

noun
Roman-derived legal system; codified law
4.0
Pronunciation: / ˈsɪv.əl lɔ /; / ˈsɪvəl lɔ / (AmE), / ˈsɪv.əl lɔː /; / ˈsɪvəl lɔː / (BrE)
the system of law that deals with private rights and matters between individuals, especially as used in many European countries and based on Roman law, as opposed to criminal or religious law

Examples

Civil law system
Many European countries base their legal systems on civil law rather than common law.
Students of international law often compare civil law with common law traditions.
The origins of civil law can be traced back to the Roman Empire.
Many European nations developed distinct codes of civil law in the nineteenth century.
Property disputes are typically resolved through civil law courts.
The country's legal system is based primarily on civil law rather than common law.

Related Terms

  • antonym: common law - legal system based on judicial precedents rather than codified statutes
  • antonym: criminal law - system of law concerned with the punishment of offenders
