unsaturation

noun
double or triple bonds
7.1
Pronunciation: /ʌn.sætʃ.ə.ˈreɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ən.sætʃ.ə.ˈreɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /ʌn.sætʃ.ə.ˈreɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ən.sætʃ.ə.ˈreɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
the presence of double or triple bonds in a molecule, especially in chemistry, meaning it is not fully saturated with hydrogen

Examples

Chemical unsaturation
The unsaturation of fatty acids affects their melting point and nutritional properties.
Chemists analyze the degree of unsaturation to determine the structure of organic compounds.
Oils with higher unsaturation are generally considered healthier for cooking.
The degree of unsaturation in a fat determines whether it is considered healthy or not.
Chemists measure unsaturation to predict how a molecule will react with other substances.
The unsaturation of the compound affects its chemical reactivity.

Related Terms

  • degree lower: monounsaturated - containing only one double bond in a molecule
  • degree higher: polyunsaturated - containing more than one double or triple bond in a molecule
  • antonym: saturation - the state of having no double or triple bonds in a molecule
