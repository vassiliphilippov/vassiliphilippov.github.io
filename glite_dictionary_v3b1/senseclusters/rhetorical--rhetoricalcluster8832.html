rhetorical

adjective
relating to style over substance in speech or writing
7.3 8.06/m
Pronunciation: / r…ôÀàt…îÀêr…™k…ôl /; / …π…ôÀàt…î…π…™k…ôl / (AmE), / r…™Ààt…ír…™k…ôl / (BrE)
relating to the art or style of using language to persuade or impress, often focusing on effect rather than substance

Examples

Rhetorical question
Her argument was criticized as being merely rhetorical rather than substantive.
Many politicians give rhetorical speeches that lack concrete plans or details.
The essay was full of rhetorical flourishes but said very little of substance.
He asked a rhetorical question to emphasize his point.
Her speech was full of rhetorical flourishes that impressed the audience.
The editor made only minor rhetorical changes to the original text.

Synonyms

  • grandiloquent
    9.9 0.09/m
    grandiloquent uses very elaborate, showy language to impress listeners, while rhetorical focuses on persuasive style and effect.
  • magniloquent
    9.9 0.00/m
    magniloquent uses lofty, pompous language to sound grand, while rhetorical describes language that aims to persuade or impress.
  • bombastic
    8.5 0.81/m
    bombastic uses loud, inflated words that often lack real meaning, while rhetorical describes language shaped to persuade or create effect.
  • oratorical
    7.7 0.33/m
    oratorical relates to public speaking and strong delivery, while rhetorical focuses on the style of language used to persuade or impress.

  • antonym: substantive - focused on important or meaningful content
