Weber's law

noun
principle in psychophysics about noticeable differences
9.3
Pronunciation: / ˈweɪbɚz lɔ / (AmE), / ˈveɪbəz lɔː / (BrE)
the principle in psychophysics stating that the just-noticeable difference between two stimuli is proportional to the magnitude of the original stimulus

Examples

Weber's law in psychophysics
According to Weber's law, the ability to detect a change in weight depends on the relative, not absolute, difference.
Scientists use Weber's law to explain why humans notice differences in loudness more easily at lower volumes.
The experiment demonstrated Weber's law by showing that small changes in bright light were harder to notice than in dim light.

Related Terms

  • synonym: just-noticeable difference - the smallest change in a stimulus that can be detected
