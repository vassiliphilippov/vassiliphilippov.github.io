stare decisis

noun
legal precedent principle
Pronunciation: / ˌstɑːreɪ dɪˈsaɪsɪs /; / ˌstɛəri dɪˈsaɪsɪs / (AmE), / ˌstɑːreɪ dɪˈsaɪsɪs / (BrE)
the legal principle that courts should follow previous decisions (precedent) when making rulings in similar cases

Examples

Stare decisis principle
The Supreme Court followed stare decisis and upheld the earlier decision.
Lawyers often argue that stare decisis should guide the outcome of similar cases.
Some judges believe that stare decisis ensures stability and predictability in the law.
The judge cited stare decisis as the reason for following the earlier court's ruling.
Law students learn that stare decisis helps ensure consistency and predictability in the legal system.
Some cases challenge stare decisis by arguing that previous decisions were wrongly decided.
The court relied on stare decisis to uphold the previous ruling in the case.
Many organizations use stare decisis to maintain consistency in their policies over time.
Debates about whether to follow stare decisis often arise when new evidence challenges old decisions.

Related Terms

  • antonym: innovation - the act of introducing new ideas or methods
  • antonym: overrule - to reject or set aside a previous legal decision
  • synonym: precedent - a previous court decision used as a rule for future cases
