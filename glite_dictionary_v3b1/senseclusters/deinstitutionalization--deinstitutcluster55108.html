deinstitutionalization

noun
moving from institutions
9.8
Pronunciation: /diː.ɪn.stɪ.tuː.ʃən.əl.aɪ.ˈzeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /diː.ɪn.stɪ.tjuː.ʃən.əl.aɪ.ˈzeɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /diː.ɪn.stɪ.tjuː.ʃən.əl.aɪ.ˈzeɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
the process or policy of moving people from long-term institutions, especially psychiatric hospitals, to community-based care or services

Examples

Deinstitutionalization of patients
Deinstitutionalization led to many patients receiving treatment in their own communities instead of hospitals.
The policy of deinstitutionalization began in the mid-20th century in several countries.
Advocates argue that deinstitutionalization improves quality of life for people with mental health conditions.
The deinstitutionalization movement aimed to help patients live more independently in their communities.
After deinstitutionalization, many former patients needed new forms of support and housing.
Critics argue that rapid deinstitutionalization left some people without adequate mental health care.
The deinstitutionalization of psychiatric patients changed the way mental health care was delivered in many countries.
Advocates argue that deinstitutionalization allows people to live more independently within their communities.
After deinstitutionalization, many former patients struggled to find adequate support outside of hospitals.
