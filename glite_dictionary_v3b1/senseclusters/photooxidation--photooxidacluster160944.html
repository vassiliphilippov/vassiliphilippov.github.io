photooxidation

noun
light-induced oxidation
Pronunciation: / ˌfoʊtoʊˌɑksɪˈdeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌfəʊtəʊˌɒksɪˈdeɪʃən / (BrE)
a chemical reaction where light (especially sunlight) causes a substance to be oxidized, often leading to damage or changes in materials or living things

Examples

Photooxidation of skin
Excessive sunlight can lead to photooxidation of skin cells, increasing the risk of premature aging.
The fading of colors in old photographs is often due to photooxidation caused by exposure to light.
Researchers study photooxidation to understand how light damages biological tissues at the molecular level.
Prolonged sunlight can cause photooxidation of plastics, making them brittle.
The scientist studied how photooxidation affects the stability of certain medications.
To prevent photooxidation, the samples were stored in dark containers.
Summer sunlight accelerates photooxidation of nitrogen oxides, producing ground-level ozone that worsens smog.
The process of photooxidation can damage plastics and other materials exposed to sunlight for long periods.
Scientists study photooxidation to understand how air pollution forms in urban environments.

Related Terms

  • antonym: reduction - a chemical reaction involving the gain of electrons or loss of oxygen
