mass-energy equivalence

noun
interchangeability of mass and energy in physics
Pronunciation: / mæs ˈɛnɚdʒi ɪˈkwɪvələns / (AmE), / mæs ˈɛnədʒi ɪˈkwɪvələns / (BrE)
the principle in physics that mass and energy are interchangeable and that a given mass corresponds to a specific amount of energy, as described by the equation E = mc²

Examples

Mass-energy equivalence principle
Einstein's theory of relativity introduced the concept of mass-energy equivalence to modern physics.
The famous equation E = mc² mathematically expresses mass-energy equivalence.
The principle of mass-energy equivalence explains how nuclear energy is released from atomic reactions.
