generalization

noun
transfer of a learned response to similar stimuli
3.8 5.1
Pronunciation: / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rələˈzeɪʃ(ə)n /; / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlaɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (AmE), / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rəlʌɪˈzeɪʃ(ə)n /; / ˌdʒɛn(ə)rələˈzeɪʃ(ə)n / (BrE)
(Psychology) The process by which a learned response to one stimulus is elicited by similar, but different, stimuli.

Examples

Generalization of a response
In classical conditioning, generalization can cause a dog to salivate at the sound of similar tones.
The child's fear showed generalization when she became anxious around all dogs, not just the one that bit her.
Therapists monitor generalization to ensure positive behaviors apply to new settings or stimuli.
Her dog’s fear of thunder extended by generalisation to loud noises in general.
In psychological studies, generalisation can lead subjects to react to stimuli that only resemble the original trigger.
The experiment measured how generalisation affected the animal’s behavior in different environments.

Related Terms

  • antonym: discrimination - the ability to distinguish between different stimuli in psychological conditioning
