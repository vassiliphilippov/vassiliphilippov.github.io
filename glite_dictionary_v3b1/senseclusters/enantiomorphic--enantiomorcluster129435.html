enantiomorphic

adjective
mirror-image structure
9.9
Pronunciation: / ɪˌnæntiəˈmɔːrkɪk / (AmE), / ɪˌnæntiəˈmɔːfɪk / (BrE)
having a structure that is a mirror image of another but cannot be placed exactly on top of it (often used for crystals or molecules that exist in left- and right-handed forms)

Examples

Enantiomorphic crystal structure
The two molecules are enantiomorphic, making them important in pharmaceutical research.
Some crystals display enantiomorphic forms that cannot be matched by simple rotation.
The left and right hands are classic examples of enantiomorphic structures in biology.
The scientist observed enantiomorphic crystals forming in both left- and right-handed versions.
Some minerals display enantiomorphic properties, making them important in crystallography studies.
The enantiomorphic nature of the structure means it cannot be mapped onto its mirror image.

Related Terms

  • antonym: achiral - having a property where an object is superimposable on its mirror image
  • synonym: chiral - having a property where an object is not superimposable on its mirror image
