classical conditioning

noun
learning by pairing a stimulus with a reflex
7.4 3.2
Pronunciation: / ˌklæsɪkəl kənˈdɪʃənɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌklæsɪkəl kənˈdɪʃənɪŋ / (BrE)
a learning process in which a neutral stimulus becomes associated with a reflex response by being repeatedly paired with a stimulus that naturally evokes that response.

Examples

Classical conditioning theory
In psychology class, we learned how classical conditioning can explain certain phobias.
Pavlov discovered classical conditioning through experiments with dogs and the sound of a bell.
The child's fear of dogs was a result of classical conditioning after a bite from a neighbor's pet.

Related Terms

  • antonym: operant conditioning - a learning process where behavior is shaped by consequences, such as rewards or punishments
