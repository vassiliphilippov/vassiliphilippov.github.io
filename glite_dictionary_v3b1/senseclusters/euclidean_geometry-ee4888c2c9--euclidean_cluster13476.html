Euclidean geometry

noun
geometry based on Euclid's axioms
8.2 5.3
Pronunciation: / juːˈklɪdiən dʒiˈɑːmətri / (AmE), / juːˈklɪdiən dʒiˈɒmɪtri / (BrE)
The system of geometry based on the axioms and postulates established by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid, dealing primarily with flat (plane) space.

Examples

Euclidean geometry axioms
In Euclidean geometry, the angles of a triangle always add up to 180 degrees.
Many concepts in Euclidean geometry still form the basis for high school mathematics.
The parallel postulate distinguishes Euclidean geometry from other types of geometry.

Related Terms

  • antonym: non-Euclidean geometry - any system of geometry based on postulates different from those of Euclid, such as hyperbolic or elliptic geometry
