severability

noun
legal contract principle
8.1
Pronunciation: /sə.vɜːr.ə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ /; / /se.vɜːr.ə.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ (AmE), /sə.və.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ /; / /se.və.ˈbɪl.ə.tɪ/ (BrE)
the legal principle that if part of a contract is invalid, the rest can still remain effective

Examples

The severability clause
The severability clause protects the rest of the agreement if one part is declared invalid.
Lawyers often include a severability provision to ensure contracts remain enforceable.
Without severability, a single illegal term could void the entire contract.
The severability of the contract means that if one part is invalid, the rest remains effective.
Engineers tested the severability of the modules before shipping the product to customers.
The judge considered the severability clause when making her decision about the disputed section.

Related Terms

  • synonym: divisibility - the quality of being able to be separated into parts
  • antonym: inseparability - the quality of not being able to be separated
  • antonym: invalidity - the state of being not legally acceptable or enforceable
