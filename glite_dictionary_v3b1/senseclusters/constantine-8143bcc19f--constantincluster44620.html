Constantine

proper noun
Roman emperor (circa 280–337); adopted Christianity and founded Constantinople
Pronunciation: / ˈkɑːnstənˌtaɪn / (AmE), / ˈkɒnstəntiːn / (BrE)
The Roman emperor (circa 280–337) who ended the persecution of Christians, adopted Christianity as the Empire's official religion, and established Constantinople as the new capital.

Examples

Emperor Constantine the Great
Constantine is often credited with making Christianity the dominant religion in the Roman Empire.
Many historians consider Constantine's reign a turning point in both Roman and Christian history.
The city of Constantinople, named after Constantine, became a major center of culture and trade.
