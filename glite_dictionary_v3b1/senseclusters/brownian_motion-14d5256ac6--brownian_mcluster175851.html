Brownian motion

noun
random motion of particles in a fluid
Pronunciation: / ˈbroʊniən ˈmoʊʃən /; / ˈbraʊniən ˈmoʊʃən / (AmE), / ˈbraʊniən ˈməʊʃən / (BrE)
the random movement of tiny particles suspended in a fluid, caused by collisions with the fast-moving molecules in the fluid

Examples

Brownian motion of particles
Albert Einstein studied Brownian motion to provide evidence for the existence of atoms.
The pollen grains displayed Brownian motion under the microscope.
Tiny dust particles in water exhibit Brownian motion due to molecular collisions.
A basic understanding of Brownian motion is fundamental in the study of statistical physics.
The scientist observed Brownian motion when examining pollen grains in water under the microscope.
Tiny dust particles suspended in the air exhibit Brownian motion due to collisions with gas molecules.

Related Terms

  • synonym: diffusion - the spreading of particles from an area of higher concentration to one of lower concentration
