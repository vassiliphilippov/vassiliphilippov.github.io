railroad

verb
force or pressure someone into action
7.2 5.6
Pronunciation: / ˈreɪlˌroʊd / (AmE), / ˈreɪlrəʊd /; / ˈreɪlˌrəʊd /; / ˈreɪl.rəʊd / (BrE)
to force or pressure someone to do something or to make something happen quickly or unfairly

Examples

To railroad a decision
He felt they tried to railroad him into signing the agreement without proper review.
She refused to let them railroad her into making a quick decision.
The committee attempted to railroad the proposal through without discussion.
Don't let them railroad you into making a hasty choice.
She felt railroaded into signing the contract before reading it.
The committee tried to railroad the new policy through without proper discussion.
Legislators warned that the government could not railroad controversial measures through parliament.
She accused management of attempting to railroad the changes without proper debate.
The board tried to railroad the new policy despite objections from several members.

Related Terms

  • antonym: block - to prevent progress or approval, opposite to forcing through
  • synonym: bulldoze - to push something through forcefully or ruthlessly
  • synonym: bully - to pressure or intimidate someone into doing something
  • synonym: coerce - to force someone to do something by using threats or pressure
  • antonym: encourage - to give someone support or confidence to do something
  • antonym: hinder - to make it difficult for something to happen, opposite on the spectrum
  • degree lower: persuade - to cause someone to do something by reasoning or arguing
  • synonym: pressurize - to apply pressure in order to make someone do something
