Newton's theory of gravitation

noun
Newton's law of universal gravitation
4.9
Pronunciation: /ˈnuː.tənz ˈθiː.ə.ri əv græv.ɪ.ˈteɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ˈnjuː.tənz ˈθiː.ə.ri əv græv.ɪ.ˈteɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /ˈnjuː.tənz ˈθɪə.ri əv græv.ɪ.ˈteɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ˈnjuː.tənz ˈθiː.ə.ri əv græv.ɪ.ˈteɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
the scientific theory stating that any two particles of matter attract each other with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them

Examples

Newton's theory of gravitation explains gravity
Before Einstein's work, most scientists explained gravity using Newton's theory of gravitation.
High school physics students often learn about Newton's theory of gravitation early in their courses.
The orbits of the planets can be predicted accurately using Newton's theory of gravitation.
