cordon off

verb
close off an area with barriers or tape
6.3 0.9/m
Pronunciation: / Ààk…îrd…ôn …îf / (AmE), / Ààk…îÀêd…ôn …íf / (BrE)
to prevent people from entering or leaving an area by placing a line of police, barriers, or tape around it

Examples

To cordon off an area
Firefighters cordoned off the building to keep bystanders at a safe distance.
Officials cordoned off the park during the investigation.
The police quickly cordoned off the street after the accident.

Synonyms

  • cordon
    5.8 1/m
    cordon is a line of people or barriers marking an area, while cordon off is the action of closing it with that line.
  • seal off
    3.9 1/m
    seal off uses official or strong barriers to close an area completely, while cordon off often uses a visible police line or tape.
  • barricade
    4.8 2/m
    barricade blocks access by piling strong barriers or objects, while cordon off uses a line or tape and is usually less solid.
  • block off
    3.8 0.8/m
    block off closes a road, path, or entrance to stop people passing through, while cordon off usually seals with a police line.
  • close off
    3.9 1/m
    close off makes something inaccessible or separated, often permanently, while cordon off often means a temporary police or tape barrier.
  • rope off
    4.8 0.3/m
    rope off separates an area by putting ropes around it for light control, while cordon off involves police or stronger barriers for security.
  • bar off
    5.6 0.01/m
    bar off blocks or restricts access with a physical barrier, while cordon off usually blocks using a visible line of police or tape.
  • fence off
    3.7 0.4/m
    fence off encloses an area by putting up a fence, while cordon off usually uses temporary tape or police lines for short-term control.
  • rail off
    7.0 0.01/m
    rail off separates an area by putting up a railing, while cordon off uses a marked line or tape set by police for safety.
