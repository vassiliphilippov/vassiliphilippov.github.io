infer

verb
deduce or conclude from evidence
5.5 7.59/m
Pronunciation: / …™nÀàf…úr / (AmE), / …™nÀàf…ôÀê /; / …™nÀàf…úÀê / (BrE)
to reach a conclusion or form an opinion based on evidence or reasoning, rather than from direct statements

Examples

To infer a meaning
From her silence, I inferred that she was not pleased with the decision.
The researcher inferred a connection between the two phenomena based on the data.
We can infer from the evidence that the suspect left before midnight.
Based on the evidence presented, the jury could infer that the defendant was present at the scene.
From her tone of voice, I inferred that she was upset about the decision.
Scientists often infer the existence of planets by observing changes in starlight.
Based on the data, scientists infer that the species migrated due to climate change.
From the evidence presented, the detective was able to infer who committed the crime.
You can often infer a person's feelings by their body language.

Synonyms

  • deduce
    4.6 4.45/m
    deduce means reaching a conclusion by clear logical steps from facts, while infer means forming an opinion from available evidence.
  • deduct
    4.4 0.18/m
    deduct means reaching a conclusion by reasoning from known facts, while infer means forming an opinion from evidence.
  • derive
    4.3 2.96/m
    derive means obtain a result by reasoning or deduction, while infer means form an opinion from given evidence.
  • gather
    3.1 13/m
    gather means understanding or concluding something from facts or hints, while infer means forming a reasoned opinion from evidence.
  • extrapolate
    8.5 3.78/m
    extrapolate means estimate unknown information by extending known trends, while infer means form a conclusion from available evidence.

Related Terms

  • synonym: deduce - to arrive at a fact or conclusion by reasoning; draw as a logical conclusion
  • synonym: extrapolate - to extend or project known information to unknown areas
  • antonym: imply - to suggest or indicate something without stating it directly (the counterpart of inference)
