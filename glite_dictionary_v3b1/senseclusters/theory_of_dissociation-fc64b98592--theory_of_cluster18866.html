theory of dissociation

noun
chemical theory of ionization of acids and bases
8.7 5.5
Pronunciation: /ˈθiː.ər.iː əv dɪ.soʊ.si.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ˈθɪ.ər.iː əv dɪ.soʊ.si.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ (AmE), /ˈθɪə.riː əv dɪ.səʊ.si.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ /; / /ˈθɪə.riː əv dɪ.səʊ.ʃi.ˈeɪ.ʃən/ (BrE)
In chemistry, a theory that explains how acids and bases dissociate into ions in aqueous solution, with acids producing hydrogen ions and bases producing hydroxyl ions; their reaction yields salt and water.

Examples

The theory of dissociation in chemistry
According to the theory of dissociation, hydrochloric acid produces hydrogen ions when dissolved in water.
Many foundation-level chemistry textbooks introduce students to the theory of dissociation early on.
The theory of dissociation revolutionized our understanding of how acids and bases behave in water.
