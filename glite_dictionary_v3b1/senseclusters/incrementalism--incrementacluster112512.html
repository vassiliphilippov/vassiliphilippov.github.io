incrementalism

noun
gradual change method
Pronunciation: / ˌɪnkrəˈmɛntəlɪzəm /; / ˌɪŋkrəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (AmE), / ˌɪŋkrɪˈmɛntəlɪzəm /; / ˌɪŋkrəˈmɛntəlɪzəm / (BrE)
a method or policy of making changes or improvements gradually in small steps rather than through sudden or major shifts

Examples

Policy of incrementalism
Many governments use incrementalism to introduce new policies without causing public unrest.
The company adopted incrementalism to improve its processes step by step over several years.
Critics argue that incrementalism can be too slow when urgent action is needed.
The company used incrementalism to improve its software, adding new features one at a time.
Many political reforms happen through incrementalism rather than sudden, sweeping changes.
Critics argue that incrementalism can be too slow when urgent action is needed.
The government adopted incrementalism to reform the healthcare system, making small changes each year.
Critics argue that incrementalism is too slow to address urgent social problems.
Many organizations prefer incrementalism over drastic reforms to minimize disruption.

Related Terms

  • synonym: gradualism - the policy of advancing toward a goal by gradual steps
  • antonym: radicalism - the belief in making major or fundamental changes quickly
  • antonym: revolution - a sudden and major change
