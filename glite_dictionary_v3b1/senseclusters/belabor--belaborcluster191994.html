belabor

verb
discuss at excessive length
Pronunciation: / bəˈleɪbɚ /; / bɪˈleɪbɚ /; / bɪˈleɪbər / (AmE), / bɪˈleɪbə / (BrE)
to talk about or criticize something at unnecessary length or repeatedly

Examples

To belabor a point
Please don't belabor every minor detail during the meeting.
The article tends to belabor the obvious, making it tedious to read.
There's no need to belabor the point; everyone understands your argument.
It's unnecessary to belabor the mistake; we've already acknowledged it and learned from it.
She felt unfairly belabored by the harsh comments in the review.
The senator continued to belabor his opponent's record during the debate, repeating the same criticisms several times.
Let's not belabour the issue; everyone already understands the main argument.
She often belabours her explanations, repeating the same details over and over.
The professor tended to belabour minor points during his lectures, making them longer than necessary.

Related Terms

  • synonym: criticize - to express disapproval of someone or something by pointing out faults
  • synonym: dwell on - to think, speak, or write at length about a particular subject
  • synonym: overdo - to do something to an excessive degree
  • synonym: overemphasize - to give too much emphasis or stress to something
  • antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration for someone or something
  • antonym: skim - to deal with or read superficially or quickly
