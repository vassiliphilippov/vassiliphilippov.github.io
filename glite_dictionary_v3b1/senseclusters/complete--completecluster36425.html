complete

adjective
having every necessary part; whole
1.0 39/m
Pronunciation: / k…ômÀàplit / (AmE), / k…ômÀàpliÀêt / (BrE)
having all the necessary or appropriate parts, elements, or steps; whole or entire; perfect and not lacking anything

Examples

A complete set
She finally had a complete set of the antique dishes.
The report was declared complete after all sections were submitted.
Without your signature, the application is not complete.
His knowledge of the subject is complete and impressive.
She is a complete professional in every aspect of her work.
The restoration made the building a complete replica of the original.

Synonyms

  • entire
    1.0 183/m
    entire means whole with nothing missing, while complete can also mean having every needed part and being finished.
  • whole
    1.0 324/m
    whole refers to something undivided and intact, while complete stresses that every necessary part or step is present.
  • comprehensive
    4.6 38/m
    comprehensive covers all parts or aspects in detail, while complete simply says nothing is missing or that it is finished.
  • full
    1.0 251/m
    full means containing as much as possible or no empty space, while complete focuses on having every required part.
  • total
    1.0 136/m
    total expresses a whole amount or absolute degree, while complete refers to having all parts or being finished.

Related Terms

  • antonym: incomplete - not having all necessary parts or elements; unfinished
  • synonym: perfect - being entirely without fault or defect; flawless
  • synonym: whole - entire; full; with no part missing
