traceability

noun
ability to track origins
Pronunciation: / ˌtreɪsəˈbɪlɪti / (AmE), / ˌtreɪsəˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
the ability to track or verify the history, location, or use of something, especially in a process or supply chain

Examples

Supply chain traceability
The company improved traceability in its supply chain to ensure product quality and safety.
Strict traceability requirements help track food from farm to table.
Software engineers use tools to maintain traceability between requirements and test cases.
Traceability is essential in the food industry to ensure products are safe and authentic.
The company improved traceability by using barcodes to track each item through the supply chain.
Government regulations now require full traceability of medical devices from production to delivery.
Food producers must ensure the traceability of their products from farm to table.
The traceability of each part is crucial in the aerospace industry for safety reasons.
Modern supply chains use barcodes to improve traceability and prevent counterfeit goods.
