barotropic

adjective
pressure depends on density
9.9
Pronunciation: / ˌbæroʊˈtrɑːpɪk /; / ˌbæroʊˈtrɑpɪk / (AmE), / ˌbærəˈtrɒpɪk / (BrE)
describing a fluid (like air or water) where pressure depends only on density, so properties or motion are the same at all depths

Examples

barotropic fluid
A barotropic atmosphere has no temperature gradients at constant pressure.
In meteorology, a barotropic fluid simplifies the analysis of weather systems.
The ocean can be considered barotropic when its pressure depends only on water density.
A barotropic current moves the entire water column in the same direction regardless of depth.
Meteorologists studied the barotropic structure of the atmosphere to predict large-scale weather patterns.
The model assumes a barotropic ocean, where temperature and salinity do not change with depth.

Related Terms

  • antonym: baroclinic - describing a fluid where pressure depends on both density and temperature
