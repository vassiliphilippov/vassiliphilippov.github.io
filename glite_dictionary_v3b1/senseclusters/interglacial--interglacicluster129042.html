interglacial

noun
warm period between ice ages
7.9
Pronunciation: /ɪn.tər.ˈɡleɪ.ʃəl/ (AmE), /ɪn.tə.ˈɡleɪ.ʃəl/ (BrE)
a warm period of time between two ice ages when glaciers retreat and temperatures rise

Examples

An interglacial period
Scientists study interglacial periods to understand how Earth's climate has changed over time.
The fossils found in the interglacial layer suggest a much warmer environment than today.
During an interglacial, forests expanded and many animal species migrated northward.
Many species flourished during the last interglacial.
Scientists study ancient sediments to learn about past interglacials and climate change.
The current warm period is considered an interglacial in Earth's climate history.
Scientists study interglacial periods to understand changes in Earth's climate.
During the last interglacial, sea levels were much higher than they are today.
Fossil evidence from an interglacial shows forests once grew where there is now tundra.

Related Terms

  • antonym: glacial - a period of colder climate when glaciers advance
  • antonym: glacial period - a cold period during an ice age when glaciers advance
  • antonym: ice age - a long period of reduced temperatures when glaciers covered large parts of the earth
