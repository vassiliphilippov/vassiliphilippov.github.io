beggar-my-neighbor policy

noun
policy benefiting self by harming others
9.9 9.9
Pronunciation: / ˈbɛɡər maɪ ˈneɪbər ˈpɑləsi /; / ˈbɛɡər maɪ ˈneɪbər ˈpɑːləsi / (AmE), / ˈbɛɡə maɪ ˈneɪbə ˈpɒləsi / (BrE)
an economic or political strategy that seeks to advance one's own interests by damaging or disadvantaging others, especially other countries, often by means such as tariffs or competitive devaluation.

Examples

Beggar-my-neighbor policy in trade
During the 1930s, several nations implemented beggar-my-neighbor policies to try to escape the Great Depression.
Many economists warn that a beggar-my-neighbor policy can lead to trade wars and global instability.
The country adopted a beggar-my-neighbor policy by imposing tariffs that harmed its trading partners.
A beggar-my-neighbour policy can lead to trade wars and increased tensions between nations.
During the Great Depression, many countries adopted a beggar-my-neighbour policy to protect their own economies.
Experts argue that a global recession is worsened by each country’s reliance on a beggar-my-neighbour policy.

Related Terms

  • antonym: free trade - international trade left to its natural course without tariffs or other restrictions
  • synonym: protectionism - an economic policy of restricting imports to protect domestic industries
