strangle

noun
options strategy (call + put at different strikes)
Pronunciation: / ˈstræŋɡəl / (AmE), / ˈstræŋɡ(ə)l / (BrE)
(in finance) An options strategy in which an investor holds both a call and a put option with the same expiration but different strike prices; when bought (a long strangle) it is used to profit from large price moves regardless of direction.

Examples

A strangle strategy
Investors often implement a strangle when they expect high volatility but are unsure of direction.
Traders bought a long strangle before the earnings report to profit from a big price swing.
The analyst warned that a short strangle carries large risk if implied volatility rises unexpectedly.

Related Terms

  • synonym: straddle - a similar options strategy using a call and a put at the same strike price; often compared with a strangle
