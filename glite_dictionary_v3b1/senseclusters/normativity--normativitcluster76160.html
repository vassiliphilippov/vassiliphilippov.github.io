normativity

noun
binding force of norms
9.0
Pronunciation: / ˌnɔrməˈtɪvəti /; / ˌnɔːrməˈtɪvəti / (AmE), / ˌnɔːməˈtɪvəti /; / ˌnɔːməˈtɪvɪti / (BrE)
the quality or state of establishing, expressing, or relating to standards, rules, or norms, especially about what is right, acceptable, or required

Examples

The normativity of rules
The normativity of international law depends on whether countries agree to follow its rules.
Debates about the normativity of social customs often arise in multicultural societies.
Some philosophers question the normativity of moral principles in a changing world.
The concept of normativity is central to many debates in ethics and philosophy.
Social normativity shapes our understanding of acceptable behavior in different communities.
Discussions about normativity often address who has the power to set societal standards.
The philosopher discussed the normativity of moral rules in her lecture.
Debates about normativity often focus on whether ethical statements can guide action.
Some argue that law derives its authority from its normativity rather than from power alone.

Related Terms

  • synonym: authority - the power or right to give orders or make decisions
  • antonym: descriptiveness - the quality of describing rather than prescribing norms
  • antonym: descriptivity - the quality of describing what is, rather than what ought to be
  • antonym: illegitimacy - the state of not being authorized or accepted as proper
  • synonym: legitimacy - the quality of being accepted as right or proper
  • synonym: normative - relating to what is considered right or acceptable
  • synonym: prescriptiveness - the quality of laying down rules or directions
