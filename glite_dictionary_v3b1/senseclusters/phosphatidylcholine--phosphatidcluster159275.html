phosphatidylcholine

noun
membrane lipid
9.4
Pronunciation: / ˌfɑsfəˌtaɪdəlˈkoʊlin / (AmE), / ˌfɒsfətɪdɪlˈkəʊliːn / (BrE)
a type of phospholipid (fat molecule) found in cell membranes, often used in foods, cosmetics, industry, and as a dietary supplement

Examples

Phosphatidylcholine molecule
Scientists used phosphatidylcholine to create stable liposomes for the new vaccine.
The cell membrane contains a high concentration of phosphatidylcholine molecules.
Many supplements advertise phosphatidylcholine for its potential benefits to liver health.
Many processed foods contain phosphatidylcholine to help blend oil and water ingredients.
Cosmetic creams often use phosphatidylcholine for its ability to stabilize mixtures.
Researchers studied the effects of phosphatidylcholine supplements on liver health.
Lecithin is rich in phosphatidylcholine, which is important for healthy cell membranes.
Some dietary supplements contain phosphatidylcholine to support liver function.
Scientists study phosphatidylcholine to understand its role in brain health and memory.

Related Terms

  • synonym: lecithin - a mixture of phospholipids, including phosphatidylcholine, found in plant and animal tissues
  • degree lower: phosphatidylethanolamine - a phospholipid similar to phosphatidylcholine, found in biological membranes
