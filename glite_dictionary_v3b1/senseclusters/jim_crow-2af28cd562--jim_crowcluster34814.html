Jim Crow

noun
system of racial segregation in the US
Pronunciation: / dʒɪm kroʊ / (AmE), / dʒɪm krəʊ / (BrE)
the former laws and social system in the United States that enforced racial segregation, especially in the Southern states, between the late 19th and mid-20th centuries

Examples

Jim Crow laws
Historians often discuss the impact of Jim Crow on African American life during the early twentieth century.
Many civil rights activists fought to overturn Jim Crow practices throughout the United States.
The Jim Crow laws enforced segregation in schools, transportation, and public facilities.
After the Civil War, Jim Crow laws institutionalized discrimination throughout the South.
Many civil rights leaders dedicated their lives to ending Jim Crow segregation.
The effects of Jim Crow can still be seen in modern American society.

Related Terms

  • antonym: civil rights - the rights of citizens to political and social freedom and equality
  • antonym: integration - the act of combining or mixing different groups, especially different races in society
  • synonym: segregation - the action or state of setting someone or something apart from others; specifically, racial separation
