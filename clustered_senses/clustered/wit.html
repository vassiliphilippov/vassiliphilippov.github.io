1. NOUN

/ wɪt / (AmE), / wɪt / (BrE)
the ability to think quickly and inventively; keen intelligence or mental sharpness
keen intelligence; mental sharpness

Examples

Her wit allowed her to solve the problem in seconds.
His quick wit made him popular among his classmates.
You need wit as well as courage to survive in the city.

Synset Examples

he's got plenty of brains but no common sense

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intelligence - the ability to acquire and apply knowledge and skills
  • Antonym: stupidity - lack of intelligence or common sense

Etymology

From Old English 'witt' (understanding, intellect), related to Old High German 'wizzi', from Proto-Germanic roots.

1. NOUN

/ wɪt / (AmE), / wɪt / (BrE)
a person who is clever and amusing and known for making witty remarks or jokes
a clever and amusing person

Examples

At dinner, John proved himself a real wit with his quick and clever comments.
Oscar Wilde was known as a famous wit in Victorian society.
She always enjoys parties because there's usually at least one wit to keep everyone laughing.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: joker - a person who makes jokes, often to amuse others

Etymology

From Old English 'wit' (understanding, intellect), related to Old High German 'wizzi' (knowledge).

2. NOUN

/ wɪt / (AmE), / wɪt / (BrE)
the ability to express amusing and clever ideas, or a remark that shows this ability
clever and amusing verbal expression

Examples

He was admired for his quick wit and clever jokes.

The comedian’s sharp wit kept the audience laughing throughout the show.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: humor - the quality of being amusing or entertaining
  • Antonym: dullness - the lack of sharpness or liveliness in conversation; dull humor

Etymology

From Old English 'witan' (to know), related to knowledge and perception.
