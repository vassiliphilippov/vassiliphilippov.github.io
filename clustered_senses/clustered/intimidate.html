To frighten or overawe someone, especially to make them do what you want, often by threats.

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtɪməˌdeɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɪmɪdeɪt / (BrE)
to frighten or overawe someone, especially in order to make them do what one wants, often by threats
to frighten or threaten into action

Examples

She refused to be intimidated by their threats and stood her ground.
The manager tried to intimidate the new employee with harsh criticism.
They used loud voices in an attempt to intimidate us into leaving.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: threaten - to state one's intention to harm or punish
  • Antonym: reassure - to say or do something to remove someone's doubts or fears

Etymology

From Latin intimidare, from in- (in, into) + timidus (fearful).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtɪməˌdeɪt / (AmE), / ɪnˈtɪmɪdeɪt / (BrE)
to frighten or overawe someone, especially in order to make them do what one wants
to frighten or overawe

Examples

He was intimidated by the judge's stern manner in the courtroom.
Some politicians attempt to intimidate their opponents with harsh language.
The large dog tried to intimidate the mail carrier, but she stood her ground.

Synset Examples

Her boss intimidates her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: threaten - state one's intention to cause harm or trouble to someone
  • Synonym: bully - seek to harm, intimidate, or coerce someone perceived as vulnerable
  • Antonym: encourage - give support, confidence, or hope to someone

Etymology

From Latin intimidare, from in- (into) + timidus (fearful).
