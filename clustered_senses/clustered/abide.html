1. VERB

/ əˈbaɪd / (AmE), / əˈbaɪd / (BrE)
(archaic or literary) To live or dwell in a place.
live or dwell (archaic)
archaic

Examples

In the quiet village, the poet chose to abide for several peaceful years.
She abides where the river meets the forest, far from the city.
They will abide in the old manor until the repairs are finished.

Synset Examples

You can stay with me while you are in town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reside - to live in a place

Etymology

1. VERB

/ əˈbaɪd / (AmE), / əˈbaɪd / (BrE)
to tolerate or endure someone or something unpleasant, especially in negative contexts
tolerate or endure something unpleasant

Examples

I cannot abide his rudeness any longer.
Many employees say they won't abide such unfair treatment.
She simply could not abide the noise in her new apartment.

Synset Examples

I cannot bear his constant criticism
The new secretary had to endure a lot of unprofessional remarks
he learned to tolerate the heat
She stuck out two years in a miserable marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tolerate - allow or endure without interference or strong reaction
  • Synonym: to endure - suffer patiently or withstand difficulty
  • Synonym: bear - accept or endure something unpleasant

Etymology

Old English 'abīdan' (to wait, remain), from 'a-' (on, at) + 'bīdan' (to wait).
