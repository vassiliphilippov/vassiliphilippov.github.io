to compete or struggle with others in order to achieve, win, or gain something

1. VERB

/ kənˈtɛnd / (AmE), / kənˈtɛnd / (BrE)
to compete with others in order to achieve or win something
compete for something

Examples

Only the most talented athletes can contend at the highest level.
Several teams will contend for the championship this season.
The two companies contended fiercely for market dominance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compete - to strive to win or gain something in rivalry with others
  • Antonym: yield - to give way or submit, often an opposite of contending

Etymology

From Latin contendere (to stretch, strive, fight).

2. VERB

/ kənˈtɛnd / (AmE), / kənˈtɛnd / (BrE)
to engage in a struggle or competition, especially to gain power or control
to struggle or compete for something

Examples

Local communities contend with each other for access to limited water resources.
Several political parties contend for influence during the election season.
The two teams will contend fiercely for the championship title this year.

Synset Examples

the tribesmen fought each other
Siblings are always fighting
Militant groups are contending for control of the country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compete - to strive to gain or win something by defeating others
  • Synonym: fight - to take part in a violent struggle involving the exchange of physical blows or weapons
  • Antonym: yield - to give way to arguments, demands, or pressure

Etymology

From Latin 'contendere' meaning 'to stretch, strive, fight'.

to argue, challenge, or assert something, especially in a discussion or formal situation

1. VERB

/ kənˈtɛnd / (AmE), / kənˈtɛnd / (BrE)
to dispute or challenge something, especially in a formal argument or legal case
dispute; challenge (formally, legally)

Examples

Environmental groups contended the development permit, citing potential harm to local wildlife.
She plans to contend the charges in court, believing they are unfounded.
The company decided to contend the results of the government audit.

Synset Examples

They contested the outcome of the race

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contest - to dispute or challenge something, often legally
  • Antonym: accept - to agree to or not dispute something, the opposite of challenge

Etymology

From Latin contendere (to strive, to contend).

2. VERB

/ kənˈtɛnd / (AmE), / kənˈtɛnd / (BrE)
to state something as a position in an argument; to assert or claim forcefully
assert; claim someone's position

Examples

He contended that the policy would only benefit the wealthy.
Many people contend that education should be free for everyone.
The scientist contends that his theory can explain these unusual results.

Synset Examples

He contended that Communism had no future

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assert - state a fact or belief confidently and forcefully
  • Antonym: deny - to state something is not true

Etymology

From Latin contendere, meaning 'to strive, contend'.

3. VERB

/ kənˈtɛnd / (AmE), / kənˈtɛnd / (BrE)
to argue or assert in a discussion, especially when expressing opposing views
argue or assert in a discussion

Examples

Some historians contend that the event never occurred as described.
The lawyers contended over the true meaning of the contract.
They contend about the best approach to solve the problem.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: argue - to give reasons for or against something, often in disagreement
  • Antonym: agree - to have the same opinion as someone else

Etymology

From Latin contendere (to stretch, strive, contend).
