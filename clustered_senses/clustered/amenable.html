amenable

Summary

  • Willing to be influenced or controlled; open and responsive to suggestions or authority.
    • ADJ: open to influence or persuasion
    • ADJ: open or willing to be influenced
  • (of a thing) capable of being acted upon in a particular way; responsive or susceptible to a proposed action or treatment.
    • ADJ: capable of being acted upon
  • liable or accountable to a higher authority or jurisdiction; answerable.
    • ADJ: answerable to authority
Willing to be influenced or controlled; open and responsive to suggestions or authority.

1. ADJ

/ əˈmiːnəbəl / (AmE), / əˈmiːnəbəl / (BrE)
willing to be influenced or controlled; easily persuaded or responsive to suggestions or advice.
open to influence or persuasion

Examples

Her children are usually amenable to instructions if you explain the reasons behind them.
The board is not amenable to making exceptions, even for special circumstances.
The manager was amenable to our new proposal, so changes were quickly approved.

Synset Examples

a tractable student
an amenable child

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tractable - easy to control or influence
  • Antonym: intractable - hard to control or deal with

Etymology

From Old French *amener* (to lead) and ultimately from Latin *minare* (to drive, lead).

1. ADJ

/ əˈmiːnəbl̩ / (AmE), / əˈmiːnəbl̩ / (BrE)
(of a thing) capable of being acted upon in a particular way; responsive or susceptible to a proposed action or treatment.
capable of being acted upon

Examples

Only patients whose conditions are amenable to this therapy should be enrolled in the trial.
Some stains are not amenable to standard cleaning methods and require special solvents.
The new treatment proved amenable to the disease and improved the patient's condition.

Synset Examples

an amenable hospitalization should not result in untimely death
the tumor was not amenable to surgical treatment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: susceptible - likely or liable to be influenced or harmed by a particular thing
  • Antonym: resistant - opposing the effect or action of something

Etymology

From Old French *amener* (to bring, lead), from Latin *minare* (to drive).
