1. NOUN

/ kloʊv / (AmE), / kləʊv / (BrE)
one of the small segments into which a garlic bulb is divided, each resembling a small, separate bulb
a segment of a garlic bulb

Examples

Each clove can be easily separated from the garlic bulb by hand.
Peel a clove of garlic and chop it finely for the sauce.
She added three cloves of garlic to the stew for extra flavor.

Etymology

From Old English 'clufu', related to the idea of 'cleaving' or splitting, referring to something that is split or divided.

2. NOUN

/ kloʊv / (AmE), / kləʊv / (BrE)
A spice made from the dried, unopened flower bud of the clove tree, used whole or ground to flavor foods.
spice from dried clove flower bud

Examples

He studded the orange with clove to make a fragrant centerpiece.
She added a single clove to the simmering apple cider for extra warmth.
The recipe calls for ground clove to enhance the cake's flavor.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allspice - a spice similar to cloves and used similarly in cooking
  • Synonym: nutmeg - an aromatic spice, often paired with cloves in recipes

Etymology

From Old French 'clou de girofle', meaning 'nail of clove', because of its shape; ultimately from Latin 'clavus' (nail).

1. NOUN

/ kloʊv / (AmE), / kləʊv / (BrE)
A tropical evergreen tree with symmetrical red flowers, widely grown for its aromatic flower buds used as the spice cloves.
tropical tree producing aromatic flower buds

Examples

In the plantations of Indonesia, many farmers grow the clove for local and export markets.
The clove is valued for its strongly scented flower buds, which are harvested and dried for use as a spice.
The clove tree prefers a warm, humid climate and deep, rich soil.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: cloves - the aromatic dried flower buds of the clove tree, used as a spice

Etymology

From Old French *clou de girofle*, from Latin *clavus* (nail), in reference to the shape of the bud.
