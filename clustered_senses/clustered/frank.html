To officially mark mail or grant exemption from payment or examination, especially in a postal or official context.

1. VERB

/ fræŋk / (AmE), / fræŋk / (BrE)
to stamp a letter or package with an official mark indicating the date and time of mailing
stamp mail with a postmark

Examples

All outgoing mail must be franked to ensure proper delivery.
She spent the afternoon franking hundreds of envelopes in the office.
The clerk will frank your letter at the counter before posting it.

Etymology

From French *franc*, meaning 'free', originally used for mail sent free of charge with a frank mark.

2. VERB

/ fræŋk / (AmE), / fræŋk / (BrE)
to exempt (a person or item) from payment or examination by means of an official pass, privilege, or written authorization; especially to allow passage through customs or similar checks without normal scrutiny or duty
to exempt by official pass or privilege

Examples

Diplomats are often franked through customs without their baggage being searched.
His package was franked at the border, so it arrived more quickly than usual.
The senator's letters were franked, allowing them to bypass postal fees and some inspections.

Etymology

Originally from Old French *franc* (free), used in the sense of being free from payment or duty.

1. NOUN

/ fræŋk / (AmE), / fræŋk / (BrE)
a device, mark, or authorized label on mail indicating that postage has been paid or the mail may be sent free of charge
postage mark or device indicating paid or free mail

Examples

Countries use distinctive marks as a frank to show that postage has been paid.
The collector displayed a rare nineteenth-century frank from the British postal system.

Synset Examples

Prior to the establishment of the UPU in 1874, international mails sometimes bore mixed franking before the world's postal services universally agreed to deliver international mails bearing only the franking of the country of origin.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: franking - the act or system of stamping mail to indicate postage has been paid

Etymology

From Middle French 'franc' (free of charge); originally from Medieval Latin 'francus' (free).
