NOUN
Pronunciation: / ˌkætəˈɡɔrɪkəl ɪmˈpɛrətɪv / (AmE), / ˌkatɪˈɡɒrɪkəl ɪmˈpɛrətɪv / (BrE)
(in Kantian ethics) The moral principle that commands actions which are universally applicable and dictated by a sense of duty, independent of personal desires or consequences.
Kantian principle of moral duty

Examples

Kant’s categorical imperative requires us to act only according to maxims we could will as universal laws.
Many philosophers have debated the relevance of the categorical imperative in contemporary ethical issues.
The notion of the categorical imperative has shaped much of modern moral philosophy.
