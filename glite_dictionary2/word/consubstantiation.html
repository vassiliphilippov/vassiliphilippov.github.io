1. ✝️ Christian doctrine ("consubstantiation in the Eucharist")

Pronunciation: / ˌkɑːnsʌbˌstænʃiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌkɒnsʌbˌstænʃɪˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
(in Christian theology) the doctrine that, after consecration in the Eucharist, the substance of Christ's body and blood coexist with the substance of the bread and wine.
Christian doctrine: Eucharist coexistence

Examples

Lutheran churches traditionally reject consubstantiation in favor of their own teachings about the Eucharist.
Some theologians have debated the concept of consubstantiation for centuries.
The doctrine of consubstantiation distinguishes certain Anglican beliefs from Roman Catholic ones.

Related Terms

  • antonym – transubstantiation: the doctrine that bread and wine become the actual body and blood of Christ in the Eucharist
