crazy

1. ADJ

/ ˈkreɪzi / (AmE), / ˈkreɪzi / (BrE)
Very strange, surprising, or fantastic in a way that attracts attention or seems out of the ordinary.
bizarre; fantastic; very strange

Examples

He wore a crazy costume to the party that made everyone laugh.
Her apartment is decorated in a crazy mix of colors and styles.
We had a crazy idea to drive across the country in two days.

Synset Examples

had a crazy dream
wore a crazy hat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bizarre - very strange or unusual; odd
  • Synonym: fantastic - imaginative or fanciful; extraordinary

Synset POS: s

2. ADJ

/ ˈkreɪzi / (AmE), / ˈkreɪzi / (BrE)
Extremely enthusiastic, excited, or wildly irrational about something, often to an excessive or unreasonable degree.
wildly enthusiastic or excited

Examples

People thought he was crazy to quit his job and start a new business.
She was crazy about the idea of traveling the world.
The fans went crazy when the band appeared on stage.

Synset Examples

the crowd went crazy
was crazy to try his new bicycle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enthusiastic - having or showing intense and eager enjoyment or interest
  • Antonym: calm - not feeling or showing strong emotions

Etymology

From English 'craze' (to crack), influenced by Middle English 'crasen'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00923212-s
Synset POS: s

3. ADJ

/ ˈkreɪzi / (AmE), / ˈkreɪzi / (BrE)
(of an idea, plan, or action) extremely foolish or not sensible; lacking sound judgment.
foolish or unsound (of plans or ideas)

Examples

Her friends thought it was a crazy idea to start a business during a recession.
It would be crazy to drive through the blizzard just to buy ice cream.
They invested in a crazy scheme that everyone else said would never work.

Synset Examples

a crazy scheme
half-baked ideas
a screwball proposal without a prayer of working

Related Terms

  • Antonym: sensible - showing good sense or judgment
  • Antonym: rational - based on or in accordance with reason or logic
  • Synonym: absurd - wildly unreasonable or illogical

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English 'craze' (to shatter, break), possibly of Scandinavian origin.

Source: WordNet

Synset POS: s
