to borrow

1. VERB

/ ˈbɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɒrəʊ / (BrE)
to take and use something belonging to someone else with the intention of returning it
take something temporarily with intent to return

Examples

Can I borrow your notes for the lecture this afternoon?
He borrowed my umbrella and promised to return it tomorrow.
She often borrows books from the local library.

Synset Examples

May I borrow your lawn mower?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lend - to give something to someone for a period of time, expecting it to be returned

Etymology

From Old English borgian (to lend, borrow), related to Old High German borgan (to guarantee), of Germanic origin.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-02329022-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈbɑːroʊ / (AmE), / ˈbɒrəʊ / (BrE)
to take and use (an idea, method, or custom) from another source as if it were one's own
adopt and use from another source

Examples

Many modern technologies borrow concepts from nature to solve engineering problems.

Related Terms

4. VERB NEW

/ c8b51d0ro8a / (AmE), / c8b52r598a / (BrE)
(in computing) to obtain a temporary reference to a value without transferring ownership, allowing code to read or (if permitted) modify the value while the original owner keeps ownership.
take a temporary reference (programming)

Examples

In Rust, you can borrow a value immutably so multiple functions can read it safely.
She borrowed a mutable reference to the array to update its elements directly.
The library design avoids borrowing ownership; instead it hands out short-lived references for access.

Synset Examples
