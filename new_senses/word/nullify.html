to nullify

1. VERB

/ ˈnʌlɪˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈnʌlɪfʌɪ / (BrE)
to show or declare that something has no legal force or effect; to make something invalid
make legally invalid

Examples

A single mistake can nullify months of careful preparation.
If any part of the contract is found to be illegal, it could nullify the entire agreement.
The court's decision will nullify the previous ruling if it finds the evidence insufficient.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to invalidate - to make something not valid
  • Synonym: cancel - to make something no longer valid or effective
  • Antonym: to validate - to make something valid or confirm its validity

Etymology

From Latin 'nullificare' (to make nothing), from 'nullus' (none) + 'facere' (to make).

3. VERB

/ ˈnʌləˌfaɪ / (AmE), / ˈnʌlɪfaɪ / (BrE)
to make something lose its effect or value; to counteract or cancel out the effectiveness of something
to make ineffective; to cancel out

Examples

Careful planning can nullify the risks involved in starting a new business.
He tried to nullify her influence with his own arguments.
The referee decided to nullify the goal due to an offside violation.

Synset Examples

Her optimism neutralizes his gloom
This action will negate the effect of my efforts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: negate - to make ineffective or invalidate
  • Synonym: counteract - to act against in order to reduce its force or neutralize
  • Antonym: enhance - to increase or improve the effect of something

Etymology

From Medieval Latin nullificare, from Latin nullus (none) + facere (to make).
