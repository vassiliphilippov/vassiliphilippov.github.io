adenosine

1. NOUN

/ əˈdɛnəˌsin / (AmE), / əˈdiːnəˌsiːn / (BrE)
a nucleoside formed by the combination of adenine and ribose, found in all living cells as a component of nucleic acids and important biomolecules such as ATP and ADP
nucleoside component of nucleic acids
medical

Examples

Adenosine is an essential nucleoside in many cellular processes, especially those involving energy transfer.
During strenuous exercise, levels of adenosine in the blood may increase, causing fatigue.
Researchers are studying how adenosine regulates sleep and wakefulness in the human brain.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ATP - adenosine triphosphate, an energy-carrying molecule
  • Synonym: adenine - a purine base found in DNA and RNA

Etymology

From adenine + -ose + -ine; named for its adenine and sugar components.

(medicine) a prescription intravenous drug used to rapidly terminate certain types of fast heart rhythm by briefly slowing conduction through the atrioventricular (AV) node.
intravenous antiarrhythmic drug
medical

Examples

In the emergency room the cardiologist quickly gave adenosine to stop the patient's rapid heart rhythm.
During the code, paramedics administered a bolus of adenosine and monitored the heart for rhythm change.
Before the procedure, the team explained that adenosine may briefly slow the heart to restore normal rhythm.
