vitrification

1. NOUN

/ ˌvɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
A glass-like substance formed by the process of being turned into glass, typically by heating.
a glassy substance formed by heating

Examples

The vitrification of certain minerals leads to a shiny, glass-like coating.
The nuclear waste underwent vitrification to make it stable for long-term storage.
When heated to a high temperature, clay can experience vitrification and become waterproof.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glass - a hard, brittle, transparent solid substance made by fusing sand with soda, lime, and sometimes other ingredients

Etymology

From Latin vitrum (glass) + -fication (making or becoming).

2. NOUN

/ ˌvɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌvɪtrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən / (BrE)
the process of becoming glass-like or being converted into a glassy substance by heat or fusion
conversion into glass or glass-like substance

Examples

Nuclear waste can be stabilized through vitrification, turning it into solid glass for long-term storage.
Scientists observed vitrification in the lava as it cooled and hardened into a shiny, brittle crust.
The vitrification of the pottery created a smooth, glassy finish on its surface.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glassification - the process of making something glass-like

Etymology

From Latin vitreus (glass) + -fication (making or becoming).

The process of preserving biological materials (for example eggs, embryos, sperm, or tissues) by rapid cooling into an amorphous, glass-like solid to prevent ice crystal formation.
rapid freezing preservation of biological material
medical

Examples

The clinic recommended vitrification of embryos to improve survival rates after thawing for future transfers.
Studies show that oocyte vitrification preserves fertility better than some traditional slow-freezing methods in certain patients.
After the lab procedure, the surgeon noted that tissue samples survived vitrification without damaging ice crystals forming.
