commons

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmənz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmənz / (BrE)
land, usually in a rural area, that is owned or used collectively, especially as a pasture available for use by all members of a community.
collectively shared pasture land

Examples

Disputes often arose over who had the right to graze animals on the commons.
Many traditional English villages still have a commons at their center.
The villagers drove their sheep onto the commons each spring.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: common land - land belonging to or used by the people of a community collectively

Etymology

From Old French 'comun', from Latin 'communis' (common, shared).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmənz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmənz / (BrE)
a public area of open land in a city, available for recreational use by the community
public land for recreation in a city

Examples

City residents gathered for a summer concert on the commons.
Joggers and dog walkers filled the commons early in the morning.
The children played soccer on the commons every Saturday afternoon.

Synset Examples

they went for a walk in the park

Related Terms

  • Synonym: park - an area of public land, often landscaped, for recreation
  • Synonym: green - a grassy, open public space in a town or city

Etymology

From Middle English 'communes', meaning land held in common for public use.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmənz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmənz / (BrE)
the social class consisting of people who are not members of the nobility or clergy, especially as represented in legislative assemblies
social class below nobility and clergy

Examples

During medieval times, the commons had little political power compared to the nobility.
He came from the commons, but rose to become an influential politician.
The House of Commons was established to give representation to ordinary citizens.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nobility - the class of people having hereditary or honorary title; aristocracy
  • Antonym: clergy - the body of all people ordained for religious duties

Etymology

From Latin 'communis' (common, general); used historically to refer to non-nobles/non-clergy in society.

Examples

The university library curated a new digital commons where students share open textbooks and course materials.
Community volunteers maintain the software and metadata in the commons for everyone's benefit.
