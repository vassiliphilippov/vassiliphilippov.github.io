completeness

1. NOUN

/ kəmˈplit.nəs / (AmE), / kəmˈpliːt.nɪs / (BrE)
The state of being complete; having all necessary parts or elements.
state of being complete or whole

Examples

Many people seek completeness in their work to ensure nothing is left out.
The completeness of his research impressed the entire review committee.
The report has a sense of completeness that makes it easy to understand.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: incompleteness - the state of being incomplete; lacking parts
  • Synonym: wholeness - the quality of being whole or entire

Etymology

From complete + -ness, after Latin *complētitūdō*.

2. NOUN

/ kəmˈplitnəs / (AmE), / kəmˈpliːtnɪs / (BrE)
(Logic) The quality of a logical system in which every proposition that cannot be derived from its axioms would lead to a contradiction if added; a system where all true statements can be derived from its axioms.
property of a logical system having all derivable truths

Examples

Debates about completeness are fundamental in the philosophy of mathematics.
Kurt Gödel famously proved the completeness of first-order logic.
The completeness of a system ensures that if a statement is true, it can be proved within the system.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soundness - the property of a logical system where all provable statements are true
  • Synonym: consistency - the property of a logical system where no contradictions can be derived

Etymology

From complete + -ness; 'complete' ultimately from Latin 'completus' (filled up).
