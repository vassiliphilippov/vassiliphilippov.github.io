turnkey

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɝnˌki / (AmE), / ˈtɜːnˌkiː / (BrE)
an officer in charge of locking and guarding prisoners in a jail; a prison guard (historical).
prison guard (historical term)
archaic

Examples

Dickens often described the harsh manner of a turnkey in his novels.
In Victorian England, the turnkey was responsible for all the cell doors in the old prison.
The turnkey handed the prisoner his food through a small hatch every morning.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jailer - a person in charge of a jail or prisoners
  • Synonym: guard - someone who watches over others for security

Etymology

From 'turn' + 'key', referring to the person who turns the key to lock or unlock cells.

2. ADJ NEW

/ ˈtɝnki / (AmE), / ˈtɜːnki / (BrE)
Ready to use or operate immediately because all necessary components, installation, and configuration have been provided.
ready-to-use; fully installed

Examples

The company sold a turnkey solar installation that included panels, wiring, and permits for the homeowner.
We hired a contractor to deliver a turnkey office so employees could start work immediately.
Clients prefer turnkey software that handles setup, training, and support without additional purchases.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plug-and-play - designed to work immediately when connected, with no complex setup
  • Antonym: custom-built - made or arranged to meet particular specifications rather than provided complete and ready

Etymology

From the earlier compound phrase 'turn-key' (literally 'turn' + 'key'), originally referring to operation by turning a key; later extended to mean ready to operate.
