flipper

1. NOUN

/ ˈflɪpər / (AmE), / ˈflɪpə / (BrE)
A flat, paddle-like shoe worn on the foot to aid swimming, especially underwater.
swimming aid worn on the foot

Examples

He packed his snorkel, mask, and flippers for the trip.
She put on her flippers before diving into the ocean.
The instructor recommended using flippers to swim more efficiently.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fin - an external appendage on aquatic animals or swimming equipment used for movement through water

Etymology

From 'flip' (to move with a light, quick motion) + '-er' (agent noun suffix), by analogy with the limbs of aquatic animals.

2. NOUN

/ ˈflɪpɚ / (AmE), / ˈflɪpə / (BrE)
A broad, flat limb or appendage of an aquatic animal, such as a whale, seal, or turtle, adapted for swimming.
flat swimming limb of aquatic animals

Examples

A turtle propels itself through the water with strong flippers.
The dolphin injured its left flipper on a piece of sharp coral.
The seal used its flipper to wave at the crowd during the show.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fin - a thin, flat body part or attachment that helps fish and some animals to swim

Etymology

From English 'flip' (move suddenly) + '-er'.

3. NOUN NEW

/ ˈflɪpər / (AmE), / ˈflɪpə / (BrE)
An informal term for a person who buys property (often a house), renovates it quickly, and then resells it to make a profit.
person who renovates and resells property
informal

Examples

The real estate flipper bought the run-down bungalow and renovated it within three months.
Investors warned that every inexperienced flipper risks losing money when the market cools.
Local television followed a pair of flippers transforming an abandoned house into a stylish home.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: house flipper - a person who flips houses (buys, renovates, and resells them quickly for profit)
  • Synonym: property investor - someone who buys property to make money from rent or resale; similar but may invest for longer term

Etymology

From verb 'flip' (to buy and resell quickly for profit) + agent suffix '-er'; attested from late 20th century in U.S. real-estate usage.
