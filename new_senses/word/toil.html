toil

1. NOUN

/ tɔɪl / (AmE), / tɔɪl / (BrE)
Hard and continuous work, especially physically demanding or laborious work.
hard, continuous physical work

Examples

After years of toil in the fields, he finally retired.
Her hands were roughened by a lifetime of manual toil.
The project was completed after months of relentless toil.

Synset Examples

his labor did not require a great deal of skill

Related Terms

  • Synonym: labor - work, especially physical work for wages
  • Antonym: leisure - free time when not working

Etymology

From Old French *toiler* (struggle, wrestle), from Latin *tudiculare* (to crush).

2. VERB

/ tɔɪl / (AmE), / tɔɪl / (BrE)
to work extremely hard or with great effort, especially over a long period
work hard; labor intensely

Examples

After hours of toiling in the hot sun, the workers finally completed the road.
Farmers often toil from dawn till dusk during harvest season.
She toiled late into the night to finish her research paper.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

3. NOUN NEW

/ tɔɪl / (AmE), / tɔɪl / (BrE)
(in IT, DevOps, and Site Reliability Engineering) tedious, repetitive operational work required to keep software systems running, typically manual, automatable, and providing little lasting value.
repetitive operational work (IT)

Examples

Without automation, the daily toil of managing the platform consumes engineers' mornings.
Reducing repetitive toil through scripts allowed the team to focus on product improvements.
The SRE handbook advised measuring toil to justify investments in automation and tooling.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drudgery - tedious, menial, or repetitive work
  • Antonym: automation - the use of tools or software to perform tasks with minimal human intervention

Etymology

Modern technical sense extended from the general word 'toil' (hard work). From Old French *toiler* (struggle), from Latin *tudiculare* (to crush).
