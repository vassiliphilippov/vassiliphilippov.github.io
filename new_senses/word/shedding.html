shedding

1. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (BrE)
the process of losing or discarding something naturally, such as hair, leaves, or skin
the process of discarding or losing something naturally

Examples

During autumn, the shedding of leaves covers the entire yard.
Excessive shedding in cats can sometimes indicate a health problem.
The dog’s shedding gets worse every summer as the weather gets hotter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: molting - the process of shedding feathers, skin, or hair

Etymology

From the verb 'shed', of Old English origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (BrE)
the loss of bits of outer skin or similar material by peeling, coming off in scales, or being cast off
loss of skin or similar material

Examples

He noticed the shedding of dead skin cells after his sunburn.
Regular shedding of fur is common in many dog breeds.
The shedding of their old skin allows snakes to grow larger.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: molting - periodic shedding of the outer layer, as feathers or skin

Etymology

From the verb 'shed' (to cast off), Old English 'sceadan' (to separate).

3. NOUN NEW

/ ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈʃɛdɪŋ / (BrE)
(in medicine, especially virology and epidemiology) the release of virus particles from an infected person, animal, or environment, which can allow infection to spread to others.
release of virus particles
medical

Examples

Public health officials monitor shedding to determine when patients are most contagious during outbreaks.
High levels of nasal shedding were detected in volunteers after they received the experimental vaccine.
Researchers measured viral shedding rates to assess how long infected individuals may spread the disease.

Synset Examples

Etymology

From the verb 'shed' (Old English sceadan, 'to separate'); extended in modern medicine to mean release of infectious particles.
