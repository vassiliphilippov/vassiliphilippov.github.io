concavity

1. NOUN

/ kɑnˈkæv.ə.ti / (AmE), / kɒŋˈkæv.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
the quality or state of being curved or bent inward, or a surface or shape that curves inward
inwardly curved shape or surface

Examples

Dentists check each tooth for any concavity that could indicate a cavity or decay.
The concavity of the bowl allowed it to hold more soup than a flat plate.
The archway's concavity created a cool, shaded area beneath the stone structure.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: convexity - the state of being curved outward

Etymology

From Late Latin concavitas, from Latin concavus (hollow, arched inward).

2. NOUN

/ kɑnˈkævəti / (AmE), / kɒnˈkævəti / (BrE)
the quality or state of being concave, or a surface or space that curves inward
inwardly curved surface or property

Examples

If a lens has a concavity on one side, it will bend light differently than a convex lens.
The architect designed a dramatic concavity in the building’s facade to channel light.
There was a noticeable concavity in the center of the old wooden table.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: convexity - the quality or state of being convex (curving outward)
  • Synonym: concave - curved inward like the inner surface of a sphere

Etymology

From Late Latin concavitas, from Latin concavus ‘hollow, arched, concave’.

3. NOUN NEW

/ kɑnˈkæv.ə.ti / (AmE), / kɒnˈkæv.ə.ti / (BrE)
(mathematics) The property of a real-valued function whose graph lies on or above every chord: the line segment between any two points on the graph lies on or below the graph; often used in optimization to indicate a function has a unique global maximum.
mathematical property of concave functions

Examples

The professor explained that the concavity of the function ensures any local maximum is global on the interval.
Their optimization model relied on the concavity of the objective to simplify the search for optimal solutions.
Checking the concavity of a cost curve helped the students predict diminishing returns.
