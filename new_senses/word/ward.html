ward

1. NOUN

/ wɔrd / (AmE), / wɔːd / (BrE)
a person, especially a child, who is placed under the protection or care of a guardian or the court
person under care or guardianship

Examples

After losing her parents, Emily became a ward of the state.
As her ward, he was responsible for her education and well-being.
The billionaire supported several wards through his charitable foundation.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: guardian - a person who is legally appointed to care for another

Etymology

From Old English weard (guard, protection), influenced by Old French warde.

2. NOUN

/ wɔrd / (AmE), / wɔːd / (BrE)
a district into which a city or town is divided for administrative or electoral purposes
district for administration or elections

Examples

Each city council member represents a ward in the municipal government.
New boundaries for the third ward were approved by the committee.
Voters in the southern ward turned out in record numbers for the election.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precinct - a district of a city or town as defined for police or electoral purposes

Etymology

From Old English 'weard' (guard, protection), later extended to administrative districts.

3. NOUN

/ wɔrd / (AmE), / wɔːd / (BrE)
a section of a prison containing several cells and inmates; a division of a prison facility
prison division or section

Examples

Each ward was locked down after the incident.
Prisoners in the high-security ward are under constant supervision.
The riot started in the east ward and quickly spread to the rest of the prison.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: cell - a small room in a prison where a prisoner is kept
  • Degree Higher: block - a larger section of a prison containing several wards or units

Etymology

From Old English 'weard' (guard, protection); usage extended to subdivisions under protection or security.
