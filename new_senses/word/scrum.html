scrum

1. NOUN

/ skrʌm / (AmE), / skrʌm / (BrE)
In rugby, a method of restarting play in which the forwards of each team bind together and push against each other while the ball is thrown in between them to contest possession.
rugby method of restarting play with forwards pushing

Examples

A powerful scrum can give a rugby team a big advantage in regaining possession.
England's forwards dominated every scrum during the match.
The referee called for a scrum after the ball was knocked on.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scrummage - alternative term for scrum in rugby

Etymology

Originally from English dialect 'scroom' or 'scrum', meaning a confused throng, probably a shortening of 'scrummage', an alteration of 'scrimmage'.

2. NOUN NEW

/ skrʌm / (AmE), / skrʌm / (BrE)
(in software development and product management) an Agile framework for organizing teams to deliver work in short, iterative cycles (sprints) with defined roles and ceremonies.
Agile framework for iterative team development

Examples

The team adopted scrum to organize two-week sprints and daily stand-up meetings during the project.
During the audit, the manager explained how scrum increases transparency and speeds up product delivery.
She trained as a scrum master and then coached two teams to adopt Agile practices successfully.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Agile - an approach to software development emphasizing iterative delivery and collaboration; Scrum is a specific Agile framework.
  • Antonym: waterfall - a traditional, sequential approach to development that contrasts with iterative frameworks like Scrum.

Etymology

Named metaphorically after the rugby term 'scrum' to evoke teamwork and close collaboration; popularized as the framework name in the 1990s.
