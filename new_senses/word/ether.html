ether

1. NOUN

/ ˈiːθər / (AmE), / ˈiːθə / (BrE)
a class of organic compounds in which two hydrocarbon groups are joined by an oxygen atom
type of organic compound
medical

Examples

Certain types of ether can be hazardous if inhaled in large quantities.
Some solvents, such as ether, are used in pharmaceutical manufacturing.
The laboratory ordered several kinds of ether for organic synthesis.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ester - organic compound derived from an acid, similar in naming and function

Etymology

From Latin 'aether', from Greek 'aithēr' (upper air, ether).

3. NOUN

/ ˈiːðər / (AmE), / ˈiːθə / (BrE)
(historical) In ancient and medieval science, the fifth and highest element, believed to fill the universe beyond the terrestrial sphere and compose the heavenly bodies.
the fifth element in ancient and medieval science
archaic

Examples

In Aristotle’s cosmology, ether was thought to make up the stars and planets.
Medieval scholars debated the properties of ether as the purest substance in the universe.
The concept of ether as a celestial element is now considered obsolete by modern science.

Etymology

From Latin 'aether', from Greek 'aithēr' (upper air, bright sky).

4. NOUN

/ ˈiːθɚ / (AmE), / ˈiːθə / (BrE)
In physics (historically), a hypothetical substance once believed to fill all space and to transmit electromagnetic waves.
historical physics medium

Examples

In the late nineteenth century, scientists believed that light traveled through the ether.
Many early physicists wrote papers discussing the properties of the ether.
The concept of the ether was eventually disproved by experiments such as the Michelson-Morley experiment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: luminiferous ether - the specific term for the ether as the medium for light waves
  • Antonym: vacuum - empty space without matter, now known to not require a medium for electromagnetic propagation

Etymology

From Latin aether, from Greek aithēr (upper air).
