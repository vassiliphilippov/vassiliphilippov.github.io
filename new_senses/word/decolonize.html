to decolonize

1. VERB

/ dɪˈkɑlɪˌnaɪz / (AmE), / diˈkɒlɪnaɪz / (BrE)
To grant independence to a country or region that was a colony.
grant independence to a former colony

Examples

Movements to decolonize territories gained momentum after World War II.
Several African nations were decolonized in the twentieth century.
The government worked with international organizations to decolonize the island and transfer power to local leaders.

Synset Examples

West Africa was decolonized in the early 1960's

Related Terms

  • Antonym: colonize - to establish control over a foreign territory and settle it with people from the colonizing country

Etymology

From de- (removal) + colonize, ultimately from Latin 'colonia' (settlement).

2. VERB NEW

/ dɪˈkɑlɪˌnaɪz / (AmE), / diˈkɒlənaɪz / (BrE)
To remove or challenge colonial assumptions, biases, or influences in knowledge, curricula, institutions, or cultural practices.
remove colonial bias from knowledge or institutions

Examples

Universities have begun to decolonize their curricula by including indigenous authors and perspectives.
She argued that museums should be decolonized so exhibits reflect colonized peoples' voices and histories.
The committee is decolonizing research methods to remove eurocentric assumptions from study design.

Synset Examples

The committee is decolonizing research methods to remove eurocentric assumptions from study design.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: colonize - to establish control over a territory or impose cultural dominance; opposite of decolonize in many contexts
  • Synonym: decolonization - the process of removing colonial influence; the noun form referring to reforms in politics, culture, or knowledge

Etymology

From de- (removal) + colonize; ultimately from Latin 'colonia' (settlement).
