own

1. ADJ

/ oʊn / (AmE), / əʊn / (BrE)
used after a possessive to emphasize that something belongs to a particular person or thing and not to anyone else.
emphasizing possession (adj.)

Examples

Every child needs their own room.
He always brings his own lunch to work.
She wanted a place to call her own.

Synset Examples

for your own use

‘ain’ is Scottish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: personal - referring or relating to a particular person

Etymology

From Old English *āgen*, from Proto-Germanic *aiganą* (to possess).

2. VERB

/ oʊn / (AmE), / əʊn / (BrE)
to have legal possession or control of something; to possess.
to possess; to have legal ownership

Examples

He owns several rare books that are very valuable.
She is hoping to own her own home one day.
They owned the company for over twenty years before retiring.

Synset Examples

He owns three houses in Florida
How many cars does she have?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: possess - to have as property; own
  • Antonym: lease - to rent something from another, not own

Etymology

From Old English āgan (to possess, to have), related to German 'eigen' (own).

3. VERB NEW

/ oʊn / (AmE), / əʊn / (BrE)
(slang, informal) to defeat, humiliate, or dominate someone decisively, especially in games, sports, or competitive online interactions.
defeat decisively (slang)
slang informal

Examples

Skilled players can own opponents with quick strategy in ranked matches.
She owned the tournament after winning three matches without losing a set.
During the final round, he enjoyed owning rivals with unexpected, clever plays.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defeat - to win against someone in a contest or fight
  • Synonym: dominate - to have commanding influence over or control of someone or something
  • Antonym: lose to - to be defeated by someone

Etymology

A slang extension of the verb 'own' (to possess). The sense 'to dominate or humiliate' developed in gaming and hacker communities in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.
