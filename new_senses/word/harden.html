to harden

1. VERB

/ ˈhɑrdən / (AmE), / ˈhɑːdən / (BrE)
to make or become hard or harder
make or become hard

Examples

Exposure to air caused the paint to harden quickly.
Repeated freezing and thawing can harden the surface of the soil.
The cement will harden as it dries overnight.

Synset Examples

The cold hardened the butter

Related Terms

  • Antonym: soften - to make or become soft or softer
  • Synonym: solidify - to become solid or make solid

Etymology

From Old English heardian, from heard (hard).

3. VERB

/ ˈhɑrdn̩ / (AmE), / ˈhɑːdn̩ / (BrE)
to make or become less sensitive or more used to something, especially something unpleasant, through repeated exposure
become less sensitive, get used to

Examples

His experiences in the military hardened him to the harsh realities of life.
Repeated losses gradually hardened the team to disappointment.
Years of working in the emergency room had hardened her to the sight of injury.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inure - to accustom someone to something unpleasant
  • Antonym: soften - to make or become less severe or less hard

Etymology

From Old English *heardian*, from *heard* (hard).

4. VERB NEW

/ ˈhɑrdən / (AmE), / ˈhɑːdən / (BrE)
(computing) to make a computer system, application, or network more secure by applying configuration changes, removing or disabling vulnerable services, and enforcing stricter access controls.
make a system more secure (computing)

Examples

System administrators harden servers by disabling unnecessary services and applying security patches regularly.
Before deployment, the team hardened the application configuration to reduce attack surface and permissions.
After the breach, engineers focused on hardening endpoints and updating default passwords across devices.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secure - to protect a system or data from attack, loss, or unauthorized access
  • Antonym: vulnerable - open to attack, damage, or unauthorized access

Etymology

Metaphorical extension of 'make hard'; ultimately from Old English heardian (to make hard).
