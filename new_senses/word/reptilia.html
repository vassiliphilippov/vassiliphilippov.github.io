Reptilia

1. NOUN

/ rɛpˈtɪliə / (AmE), / rɛpˈtɪlɪə / (BrE)
A class of cold-blooded, air-breathing vertebrates, typically having a dry skin covered with scales or horny plates; includes snakes, lizards, turtles, and crocodiles.
class of reptiles

Examples

During the Mesozoic era, Reptilia included some of the largest land animals ever to exist.
Scientists study the evolutionary history of Reptilia to understand the development of modern reptiles.
The class Reptilia is characterized by their scaly skin and laying of shelled eggs.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reptile - a cold-blooded vertebrate animal of the class Reptilia

Etymology

From Latin 'reptilis' (creeping), via New Latin Reptilia (the creeping animals).

2. NOUN

/ rɛpˈtɪliə / (AmE), / rɛpˈtɪlɪə / (BrE)
a class of cold-blooded, air-breathing vertebrates with a fully ossified skeleton and typically covered with scales or horny plates; includes snakes, lizards, turtles, and crocodiles
class of cold-blooded vertebrates (reptiles)

Examples

Scientists study fossils from the Reptilia to understand how these animals dominated prehistory.
The class Reptilia includes snakes, lizards, turtles, and crocodiles.
Unlike mammals, members of Reptilia are cold-blooded and breathe air.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reptile - a cold-blooded vertebrate of the class Reptilia
  • Antonym: Amphibia - the class of cold-blooded vertebrates including frogs and salamanders

Etymology

From Latin 'reptilis' (creeping) + scientific suffix '-ia' forming taxonomic classes.
