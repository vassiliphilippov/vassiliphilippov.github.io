to cross-fertilise

1. VERB

/ krɔsˈfɜrtɪlaɪz / (AmE), / krɒsˈfəːtɪlaɪz / (BrE)
(of a plant or animal) to undergo cross-fertilization; to become fertile through the union of gametes from different individuals.
undergo cross-fertilization

Examples

Certain insects help flowers to cross-fertilise, leading to increased genetic diversity.
The biologist observed the species as they began to cross-fertilise in the controlled environment.
When two different plant varieties are grown together, they may cross-fertilise naturally in the wild.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-fertilise - to fertilize itself, without gametes from another individual

Etymology

From 'cross' + 'fertilise', following the pattern of 'cross-fertilization' (originally from Latin 'fertilis', meaning 'fertile').

2. VERB

/ krɑs ˈfɜrdlˌaɪz / (AmE), / krɒs ˈfɜːtɪlaɪz / (BrE)
to cause (a plant or animal) to undergo cross-fertilization, typically by transferring pollen or reproductive cells from one individual to another of a different variety or species
to cause cross-fertilization in plants or animals

Examples

Farmers sometimes cross-fertilise their crops to develop plants with greater resistance to disease.
She cross-fertilised two types of tomatoes to produce a new hybrid with improved flavor.
To increase genetic diversity, scientists often cross-fertilise different strains of plants in the laboratory.

Synset Examples

Mendel cross-fertilized different kinds of beans

Related Terms

  • Antonym: self-fertilise - to fertilize with gametes from the same individual

Etymology

Formed from 'cross' + 'fertilise', based on the biological term 'cross-fertilization'.

3. VERB NEW

/ krɔsˈfɜrtəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / krɒsˈfɜːtəˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to combine or exchange ideas, methods, or approaches between different disciplines, groups, or fields so that new ideas or innovations develop.
mix ideas across fields

Examples

The conference aimed to cross-fertilise ideas between biology and data science to boost innovation.
They cross-fertilised insights from design and engineering to develop a more user-friendly product.
By cross-fertilising methods from sociology and economics, researchers found new solutions to complex problems.
