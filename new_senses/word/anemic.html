anemic

1. ADJ

/ əˈnimɪk / (AmE), / əˈniːmɪk / (BrE)
Lacking in vigor, energy, or spirit; weak or feeble.
lacking energy or vigor

Examples

Her anemic response failed to convince the committee of her interest.
The company's sales growth was anemic compared to last year.
The team's anemic performance disappointed the fans.

Synset Examples

an anemic attempt to hit the baseball

Related Terms

  • Antonym: vigorous - strong, healthy, and full of energy

Etymology

From medical Latin 'anaemia', from Greek 'anaimia' (lack of blood), originally describing a blood condition, later used figuratively.

2. ADJ

/ əˈniː.mɪk / (AmE), / əˈniː.mɪk / (BrE)
Suffering from anemia or relating to anemia; also, lacking in color, strength, or vitality.
suffering from or relating to anemia
medical

Examples

After months of fatigue, the doctor diagnosed her as anemic.
Critics described the team's performance as anemic in the final game.
The patient's skin appeared pale and anemic during the examination.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: robust - strong and healthy; vigorous
  • Synonym: pallid - pale, typically because of poor health

Etymology

From French 'anémique', from Greek 'anēmia' (lack of blood), from 'an-' (without) + 'haima' (blood).

3. ADJ NEW

/ əˈniːmɪk / (AmE), / əˈniːmɪk / (BrE)
(in software engineering) describing a domain model or object design that contains data but little or no behavior or business logic; regarded as an anti-pattern.
lacking business logic (software)

Examples

The team criticized the anemic domain model for lacking business logic and encapsulated behavior.
After refactoring, the developers replaced the anemic entities with rich objects that include validation and methods.
Many believe an anemic model is an anti-pattern because it separates data from important domain behavior.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: domain model - a representation of the important concepts and data in a software system's problem domain
  • Synonym: anti-pattern - a commonly used but counterproductive solution or design in software engineering

Etymology

Metaphorical extension from medical 'anemia' (Greek anaimia, 'lack of blood'); popularized in software-engineering writing (early 2000s) to describe a design anti-pattern.
