elasticity

1. NOUN

/ ɪˌlæˈstɪsəti / (AmE), / ɪˌlæˈstɪsəti / (BrE)
the ability of a material to return to its original shape after being stretched, compressed, or deformed
tendency to return to original shape

Examples

As fabrics age, they can lose their elasticity and become saggy or misshapen.
Engineers must consider the elasticity of materials when designing bridges and buildings.
Thanks to its elasticity, the rubber band snapped back into place after being stretched.

Synset Examples

the waistband had lost its snap

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flexibility - the quality of bending easily without breaking
  • Antonym: rigidity - the quality of being stiff and not bending

Etymology

From French 'élasticité', from New Latin 'elasticitas', from 'elasticus' (elastic).

2. NOUN NEW

/ ɪˌlæˈstɪsəti / (AmE), / ɪˌlæˈstɪsəti / (BrE)
(in computing, especially cloud computing) the ability of a system or platform to automatically increase or decrease allocated resources (such as CPU, memory, or instances) in response to changing demand.
automatic scaling of computing resources

Examples

The platform's elasticity enables seamless automatic scaling of resources during traffic spikes.
Cloud providers advertise elasticity so customers only pay for capacity they actually use.
System architects design elasticity into services to handle sudden increases or decreases in load.

Synset Examples

The platform's elasticity enables seamless automatic scaling of resources during traffic spikes.
Cloud providers advertise elasticity so customers only pay for capacity they actually use.
System architects design elasticity into services to handle sudden increases or decreases in load.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scalability - the capacity of a system to handle increased load by adding resources
  • Synonym: autoscaling - automatic adjustment of computing resources based on predefined rules or metrics

Etymology

Metaphorical extension from the physical property 'elasticity' (from French 'élasticité', from New Latin 'elasticitas').
