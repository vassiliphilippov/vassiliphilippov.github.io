induction

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌk.ʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌk.ʃən / (BrE)
the process of initiating or bringing about a particular state or condition, especially as an initial stage in a process.
the act of initiating a process or state

Examples

Rapid induction of labor was necessary for the health of the mother and child.
The induction of chemical reactions can be triggered by light or heat.
The induction of sleep using medication is common before surgery.

Synset Examples

the induction of an anesthetic state

Related Terms

  • Synonym: initiation - the action of beginning something
  • Antonym: termination - the act of bringing something to an end

Etymology

From Latin inductio (a leading in, introduction), from inducere (to lead in).

5. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the production of an electric current or electromotive force in a circuit by a changing magnetic field or by a varying current in a neighboring circuit
production of electric current by magnetic field

Examples

Faraday's law explains how induction produces electricity in a coil of wire.
The transformer relies on induction to transfer energy between its windings.
When the magnetic field changed, induction created a current in the secondary circuit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: electromagnetic induction - production of electricity by a varying magnetic field

Etymology

From Latin 'inductio' (the act of leading in), adopted in early scientific use for the ‘leading in’ of an electric current.
