nudge

1. NOUN

/ nʌdʒ / (AmE), / nʌdʒ / (BrE)
a slight push, usually with the elbow or hand, to attract attention or prompt action
a slight push to attract attention

Examples

She gave him a gentle nudge to wake him up.
The cat demanded attention with a small nudge against my leg.
With a nudge of his elbow, he pointed out the mistake.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poke - a quick push with a finger or a sharp object

Etymology

Perhaps from a dialectal variant of 'nick' or 'notch' (origin uncertain).

WordNet ID: oewn-00113817-n
Synset POS: n

2. VERB

/ nʌdʒ / (AmE), / nʌdʒ / (BrE)
to encourage or prompt someone to take action, especially by gently pestering or persuading them
gently encourage or prompt

Examples

He kept nudging me about finishing the project until I finally did it.
She tried to nudge him into applying for the new job.
Sometimes you need to nudge your friends to get them to try something new.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: encourage - give support, confidence, or hope to someone
  • Synonym: prod - to poke or urge someone into action
  • Antonym: discourage - to cause someone not to want to do something

Etymology

Perhaps from a dialectal English word meaning 'to jog' or 'prod'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00768334-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ nʌdʒ / (AmE), / nʌdʒ / (BrE)
to push someone or something gently, often with an elbow, to get their attention or prompt an action
push gently, usually to get attention

Examples

During the meeting, Sarah gave Tom a gentle nudge to let him know it was his turn to speak.
He tried to nudge her awake without startling her.
The dog nudged my hand, wanting me to pet him.

Synset Examples

She nudged my elbow when she saw her friend enter the restaurant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: poke - to jab or prod someone or something with a finger or object
  • Antonym: shove - to push someone or something roughly

Etymology

Origin unknown; first recorded in the late 17th century.

(in behavioural economics and public policy) a policy, design feature, or default arrangement intended to influence people's choices in a predictable way without forbidding options or significantly changing economic incentives.
policy/design tool to influence choices

Examples

The government introduced a small nudge by auto-enrolling employees to boost retirement savings.
Designers used subtle nudges like clearer labels and default options to increase healthy choices.
A well-timed nudge on the checkout page increased donations while leaving every choice unchanged.
