moat

1. NOUN

/ moʊt / (AmE), / məʊt / (BrE)
A deep, wide ditch surrounding a castle, fort, or town, typically filled with water and intended as a defense against attack.
defensive ditch, often water-filled

Examples

During the renovation, archaeologists discovered remnants of the original moat.
Some zoos use a moat instead of fences to separate animals from visitors.
The ancient castle was surrounded by a wide moat to keep out invaders.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ditch - a long narrow excavation in the ground

Etymology

From Old French *mote* (mound, embankment, moat).

2. NOUN NEW

/ moʊt / (AmE), / məʊt / (BrE)
In business, a durable competitive advantage that protects a company's market share and profits from rivals.
durable competitive advantage (business)

Examples

Investors praised the startup's strong moat, which protected its market share against new competitors.
The company's patented technology created a wide moat that rivals found difficult to cross.
Analysts looked for businesses with recurring revenue, pricing power, and a durable moat.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: competitive advantage - a condition or set of factors that makes a company more successful than its rivals
  • Synonym: barrier to entry - an obstacle that makes it difficult for new firms to enter an industry

Etymology

Metaphorical extension of 'moat' (defensive ditch) from Old French *mote*; popularized in business and investment writing (notably by investor Warren Buffett) to refer to a company's lasting protection from competitors.
