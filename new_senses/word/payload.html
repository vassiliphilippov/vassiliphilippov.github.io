payload

1. NOUN

/ ˈpeɪˌloʊd / (AmE), / ˈpeɪ.ləʊd / (BrE)
the amount of goods or cargo carried by a vehicle, especially by an aircraft, truck, or spacecraft
amount of goods carried by a vehicle

Examples

The airplane reached its maximum payload before taking off from the runway.
The truck was designed to transport a heavy payload across long distances.
This rocket can carry a scientific payload into orbit around the Earth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cargo - goods carried by a ship, aircraft, or vehicle
  • Synonym: freight - goods transported in bulk by truck, train, ship, or aircraft

Etymology

Formed in English from 'pay' + 'load', originally referring to the load that pays for a trip.

2. NOUN

/ ˈpeɪˌloʊd / (AmE), / ˈpeɪ.ləʊd / (BrE)
the part of a missile, rocket, or similar vehicle that carries its explosive, chemical, biological, or other charge
explosive or cargo-carrying part of a missile or rocket

Examples

Scientists designed a special payload to deliver chemical agents with precision.
The missile's payload can consist of either conventional explosives or nuclear warheads.
The rocket failed before releasing its payload over the intended target.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warhead - the explosive head of a missile, bomb, or torpedo

Etymology

From 'pay' (to compensate) + 'load', originally meaning the revenue-producing cargo of a vehicle.

the part of a transmitted packet, frame, or message that contains the actual data being sent, excluding headers, metadata, or protocol overhead.
data portion of a packet

Examples

The network packet's payload contained the encrypted user data securely.
During troubleshooting, the engineer inspected the payload to identify the corrupted file transfer.
IoT devices send small payload segments frequently to report sensor readings to the server.
