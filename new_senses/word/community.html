community

1. NOUN

/ kəˈmjunəti / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːnəti / (BrE)
(Ecology) A group of interdependent organisms living together in the same area and interacting with each other.
group of interacting organisms in a habitat (ecology)

Examples

A forest community includes various species of trees, plants, animals, and microorganisms.
Ecologists study how energy flows through a community of organisms.
The decline of one species can disrupt the balance of the entire community.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ecosystem - a biological community of interacting organisms and their physical environment

Etymology

From Latin 'communitas' (fellowship, community), from 'communis' (common, shared).

8. NOUN NEW

/ kəˈmjunəti / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːnəti / (BrE)
(in network science and graph analysis) a subgroup of nodes within a larger network that has denser connections internally than with the rest of the network; often identified by clustering or community-detection algorithms.
densely connected subgroup in a network

Examples

Network scientists detected a dense community of users who frequently retweeted each other's posts during the election.
The algorithm clustered the graph into three main communities, uncovering groups with shared interests.
By examining edges, she identified a tight community around the influencer with strong mutual connections.

Synset Examples

Network scientists detected a dense community of users who frequently retweeted each other's posts during the election.
The algorithm clustered the graph into three main communities, uncovering groups with shared interests.
By examining edges, she identified a tight community around the influencer with strong mutual connections.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cluster - a group of nodes in a network that are more strongly connected to each other than to other nodes
  • Synonym: module - a functionally or structurally distinct subgroup within a network, often overlapping with the idea of a community

Etymology

Extended metaphorical use from Latin communitas (fellowship, community); adopted in 21st-century network science to name densely connected subgroups of nodes.
