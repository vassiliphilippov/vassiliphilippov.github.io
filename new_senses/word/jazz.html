jazz

1. VERB

/ dʒæz / (AmE), / dʒaz / (BrE)
to play or perform music in a style characteristic of jazz
play music in jazz style

Examples

Experienced pianists can jazz a simple tune to make it sound lively and vibrant.
She decided to jazz the melody, adding some improvisational flair.
The band loves to jazz up old classics during their concerts.

Etymology

From the noun 'jazz', originally American English, referring to a type of music developed by African-Americans in the early 20th century.

3. NOUN

/ dʒæz / (AmE), / dʒaz / (BrE)
A style of dance music that originated in the early 20th century, characterized by improvisation, syncopated rhythms, and often played by large bands, especially popular in the 1920s.
20th-century dance music with improvisation

Examples

Many musicians were inspired by the energetic rhythms of classic jazz.
She enjoys listening to jazz from the 1920s and 30s.
The band played lively jazz tunes that filled the dance floor.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: swing - a style of jazz music with a lively rhythm popular in the 1930s and 40s
  • Degree Lower: ragtime - a style of early jazz music characterized by syncopated rhythms
  • Synonym: blues - a music genre that influenced jazz, known for its melancholy sound

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Creole or African sources, popularized in the early 20th century in the United States.

4. NOUN

/ dʒæz / (AmE), / dʒæz / (BrE)
A style of popular music that originated in New Orleans around 1900, characterized by improvisation, syncopated rhythms, and strong swing.
a style of popular music with improvisation

Examples

Many musicians admire the creativity and complexity of jazz.
She's been a jazz singer for over twenty years.
The festival features live jazz performances every evening.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blues - a related genre of music with roots in African-American communities
  • Antonym: classical - a contrasting music genre with structured forms and traditions

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Creole patois 'jass' meaning 'strenuous activity, excitement.'
