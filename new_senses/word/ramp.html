ramp

1. NOUN

/ ræmp / (AmE), / ræmp / (BrE)
a movable staircase that allows passengers to board or leave an aircraft
movable staircase for boarding aircraft

Examples

Passengers waited at the bottom of the ramp until the cabin crew gave them permission to board.
She walked down the ramp and stepped onto the tarmac, glad to be back on the ground.
The airport staff quickly positioned the ramp next to the plane's door.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gangway - a movable bridge or platform used for boarding or leaving ships or aircraft

2. NOUN

/ ræmp / (AmE), / ræmp / (BrE)
A sloping surface joining two different levels, used for moving people or vehicles between them.
sloping surface joining two levels

Examples

A temporary ramp was built to help vehicles cross the construction site.
The wheelchair access ramp made the entrance easy for everyone.
Workers carried heavy boxes up the loading ramp at the warehouse.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: staircase - a set of steps for going from one level to another

4. NOUN

/ ræmp / (AmE), / ræmp / (BrE)
A North American wild onion (Allium tricoccum) with broad leaves, a slender bulb, and a strong garlic-like odor and flavor, often used as a vegetable.
North American wild onion

Examples

Because of their short season, ramps are highly prized by chefs and home cooks alike.
In spring, foragers search the woods for ramps to add to seasonal dishes.
The restaurant featured a special salad made with fresh ramps and roasted potatoes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wild leek - an alternative name for the same plant, Allium tricoccum

Etymology

Probably of British origin, related to 'ramson,' an English name for wild garlic.

6. VERB

/ ræmp / (AmE), / ræmp / (BrE)
(of an animal, especially in heraldry or zoology) to stand with the forelegs or arms raised, as if in a threatening or menacing posture.
stand with raised forelegs/arms menacingly

Examples

Many startled bears ramp on their hind legs if threatened.
The horse appeared to ramp, rearing up on its hind legs in alarm.
The lion on the coat of arms is shown to ramp above the shield.

Etymology

From Old French 'ramper' (to climb, crawl), ultimately of Germanic origin.
