to unload

1. VERB

/ ʌnˈloʊd / (AmE), / ʌnˈləʊd / (BrE)
to remove goods or cargo from a vehicle, container, or similar receptacle
remove cargo from a vehicle or container

Examples

It took nearly an hour to unload the cargo ship at the port.
Please unload your suitcases from the car so we can take them inside.
The workers began to unload the boxes from the truck at sunrise.

Synset Examples

unload the truck
offload the van

Related Terms

  • Antonym: load - to put goods or cargo onto a vehicle or into a container

Etymology

From un- (expressing reversal) + load; first recorded in the early 17th century.

/ ʌnˈloʊd / (AmE), / ʌnˈləʊd / (BrE)
to remove cargo, passengers, or goods from a vehicle or container

Examples

The workers began to unload the boxes from the truck.

3. VERB NEW

/ ʌnˈloʊd / (AmE), / ʌnˈləʊd / (BrE)
to express strong negative feelings, complaints, or anger to someone; to vent or pour out one's frustrations onto another person (often used with on).
vent feelings to someone
informal

Examples

After the meeting she began to unload her frustrations on anyone who would listen.
He unloaded on his friend about the unfair treatment at work last night.
Rather than keep quiet, she started unloading her anger at the support group meeting.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: vent - to express strong emotions or complaints in order to get relief
  • Antonym: bottle up - to keep emotions inside rather than expressing them

Etymology

From un- + load; figurative extension from the literal idea of removing a burden to expressing or transferring an emotional burden onto someone.
