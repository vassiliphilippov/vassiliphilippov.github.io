prejudice

1. VERB

/ ˈprɛdʒ.ə.dɪs / (AmE), / ˈprɛdʒ.ɪ.dɪs / (BrE)
to influence someone so that they form an opinion about something before having enough information or without considering it fairly
to influence opinion in advance, unfairly

Examples

His earlier statements may have prejudiced many people against the new proposal.
Try not to prejudice the jury with your comments before the trial begins.
We must be careful not to prejudice the outcome by making assumptions too soon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bias - to influence someone so they support a particular point of view unfairly

Etymology

From Old French 'prejudicier' (do harm), from Latin 'praeiudicium' (prior judgment).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɛdʒədɪs / (AmE), / ˈprɛdʒʊdɪs / (BrE)
An unreasonable or unfair opinion or feeling, especially when formed without enough thought or knowledge.
unreasonable or unfair bias

Examples

His prejudice against new technology prevented him from embracing innovation at work.
Laws exist to protect people from racial prejudice in the workplace.
She tried to overcome her own prejudice and see the situation more objectively.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bias - inclination or prejudice for or against one person or group
  • Antonym: impartiality - lack of bias or prejudice; fairness

Etymology

From Latin 'praejudicium' (prior judgment, damage) via Old French 'préjudice'.
