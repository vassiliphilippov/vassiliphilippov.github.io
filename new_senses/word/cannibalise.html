to cannibalise

1. VERB

/ ˈkæn.ɪ.bəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkan.ɪ.bəˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to eat the flesh of members of one's own species, especially referring to humans eating human flesh
to eat the flesh of one's own kind

Examples

In some ancient cultures, rituals required warriors to cannibalise their enemies.
Researchers condemn any attempt to cannibalise as morally unacceptable.
The stranded survivors were forced to cannibalise to stay alive.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: to cannibalize - Alternative spelling, especially in American English
  • Degree Lower: devour - To eat hungrily or quickly

Etymology

From Spanish 'caníbal', from the name of the Carib people; sense developed in English in the 16th century.

2. VERB

/ ˈkæn.ə.bəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkæn.ɪ.bə.laɪz / (BrE)
to take parts from one item, especially a machine or vehicle, to use in repairing or creating another
take parts from to repair another

Examples

The mechanic had to cannibalise an old car to fix the engine in my truck.
They cannibalised one jet to find spare parts for the rest of the fleet.
To keep our computers running, we often cannibalise memory from outdated machines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: salvage - to save parts from damaged items for reuse
  • Synonym: repair - to restore something that is broken to good condition

Etymology

From 'cannibal', by analogy with consuming one's own kind; first used for disassembling equipment for parts in the mid-20th century.

3. VERB NEW

/ ˈkæn.ɪ.bəˌlaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkæn.ɪ.bəˌlaɪz / (BrE)
to reduce or take sales, demand, or market share away from one of a company's existing products by introducing another product or variant from the same company.
reduce one's own product sales

Examples

The launch of the budget model was meant to cannibalise sales of the premium version within months.
When the cheaper model arrived, it quickly cannibalised several of the company's midrange product sales.
Market research warned against cannibalising starter products, because internal competition could harm brand growth.
