homestead

1. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊmˌstɛd / (AmE), / ˈhəʊm.stɛd / (BrE)
A house and the surrounding land occupied by a family, especially in a rural area.
family home with adjoining land

Examples

After years in the city, she decided to return to the rural homestead where she grew up.
The old homestead has been in our family for generations.
They built their homestead on the edge of the forest, surrounded by acres of farmland.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dwelling - a place where someone lives

Etymology

From Old English 'hāmstede' ('home' + 'place, site').

2. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊmˌstɛd / (AmE), / ˈhəʊmstɛd / (BrE)
A piece of land, especially in the United States, acquired from public lands by filing a record and living on and cultivating it as required by the homestead law.
land acquired under US homestead law

Examples

After years of cultivation, their homestead finally became productive farmland.
The settlers claimed a homestead in the Midwest, hoping to build a new life.
Under the Homestead Act, millions of acres were distributed as homestead land.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: homesteader - a person who acquires and lives on a homestead under the homestead law

Etymology

From Old English hāmstede (home site, homestead), 'hām' (home) + 'stede' (place, site).

Source: WordNet

3. NOUN

/ ˈhoʊmˌstɛd / (AmE), / ˈhəʊmstɛd / (BrE)
A house, especially a farmhouse, and the land surrounding it, often used as a family residence and for farming.
farmhouse with adjoining land

Examples

The homestead consisted of a wooden farmhouse and several barns.
They bought a small homestead in the countryside to start farming.
This old homestead has been in our family for generations.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: farmhouse - a house on a farm, especially where the farmer lives
  • Degree Higher: estate - an area of land with a large house owned by one person, family, or organization

Etymology

From Old English 'hāmstede', from 'hām' (home) + 'stede' (place).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-03534809-n
Synset POS: n

4. VERB

/ ˈhoʊmˌstɛd / (AmE), / ˈhəʊmˌstɛd / (BrE)
to settle on land granted by the government and establish a home there, especially as under homesteading laws
settle government-granted land

Examples

During the land rush, many people hoped to homestead and build a new life.
His grandparents were among the first to homestead in that part of Nebraska.
In the late 1800s, thousands of families moved west to homestead vast tracts of prairie.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: settle - to move to a new area and establish a residence there

Etymology

From the noun 'homestead', originally Old English 'hāmstede' (home + place). The verb derives from the U.S. Homestead Act era.

5. NOUN NEW

/ ˈhoʊməstɛd / (AmE), / ˈhəʊmstɛd / (BrE)
(law) the legal status of a primary residence that qualifies for protection from certain creditors and may receive property-tax or other statutory exemptions.
legal protection for a primary residence
legal

Examples

The couple declared their homestead to qualify for state tax exemptions on their primary residence.
Because of the state homestead protection, the bank could not seize their house to pay the debt.
After the accident, she registered the property as her legal homestead to gain creditor protection.
