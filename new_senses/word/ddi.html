DDI

1. NOUN

/ ˌdiː ˌdiː ˈaɪ / (AmE), / ˌdiː ˌdiː ˈaɪ / (BrE)
An antiviral drug (didanosine) used to treat HIV infection by inhibiting reverse transcriptase.
antiviral drug for HIV (didanosine)
medical

Examples

Doctors may prescribe DDI in combination with other antiretroviral medications.
Side effects of DDI can include nausea and peripheral neuropathy.
The introduction of DDI greatly improved treatment options for HIV patients.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: didanosine - an antiviral drug used to treat HIV infection
  • Synonym: reverse transcriptase inhibitor - a class of drugs that inhibit the reverse transcriptase enzyme in HIV

Etymology

Acronym from the chemical name didanosine.

2. NOUN NEW

/ diː diː aɪ / (AmE), / diː diː aɪ / (BrE)
A drug–drug interaction: an interaction between two or more medications that changes their effects or increases the risk of harm.
drug–drug interaction
medical

Examples

Before starting the new antibiotic, the pharmacist checked for any possible DDI with her current medications.
Clinical guidelines recommend reviewing patients' prescriptions to avoid harmful DDI that increase bleeding risk.
Electronic prescribing systems warn doctors automatically when a potential DDI could reduce drug effectiveness.
