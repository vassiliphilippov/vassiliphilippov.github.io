opsin

1. NOUN

/ ˈɑpsɪn / (AmE), / ˈɒpsɪn / (BrE)
a protein in the retina that, when combined with retinal, forms the light-sensitive pigment rhodopsin
retinal protein in visual pigments
medical

Examples

Mutations in the gene coding for opsin can lead to color blindness or other vision disorders.
Researchers discovered that opsin plays a key role in the conversion of light into electrical signals in the eye.
The breakdown of rhodopsin separates retinal from opsin, which is necessary for visual adaptation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rhodopsin - a light-sensitive pigment in the retina formed by opsin and retinal

Etymology

From Greek 'opsis' (sight, vision) + '-in' (protein suffix).

2. NOUN NEW

/ \u02c8\u0251ps\u026an / (AmE), / \u02c8\u0252ps\u026an / (BrE)
(in neuroscience and optogenetics) a light-sensitive protein (often from microbes or other non-visual sources) used to control the activity of cells by illumination.
light-sensitive protein used to control cells

Examples

The neuroscientist inserted a gene for a microbial opsin to control neurons with blue light.
Using different opsins allows researchers to activate or inhibit specific brain cells.
After viral delivery, the team recorded neuronal responses to light in tissue expressing the opsin.
