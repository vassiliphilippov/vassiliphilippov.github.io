unperson

1. NOUN

/ ˈʌnˌpɜrsən / (AmE), / ˈʌnˌpɜːsən / (BrE)
a person who has been officially ignored or erased from recognition, particularly for ideological or political reasons
person erased or ignored for political reasons

Examples

After falling out of favor with the regime, he became an unperson overnight.
She feared speaking out, knowing dissenters could be turned into unpersons by the authorities.
The novel describes a world where critics of the government are systematically declared unpersons.

Synset Examples

the former senator is treated as a nonperson by this administration
George Orwell predicted that political dissidents would be treated as unpersons

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nonperson - a person regarded as not existing or without rights or status

Etymology

First popularized by George Orwell in his novel Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949), formed from 'un-' (not) and 'person'.

2. VERB NEW

/ ˈʌnˌpɜrsən / (AmE), / ˈʌnˌpɜːsən / (BrE)
to remove or erase someone from public recognition, official records, or public platforms, typically by censoring, suppressing, or deplatforming them.
erase from public recognition

Examples

The regime tried to unperson the dissident by removing their name from official records.
After the scandal, the broadcaster had effectively unpersoned the presenter by erasing archived videos.
Fear of being unpersoned stopped many activists from speaking publicly about the abuses.

Synset Examples

The regime tried to unperson the dissident by removing their name from official records.
After the scandal, the broadcaster had effectively unpersoned the presenter by erasing archived videos.
Fear of being unpersoned stopped many activists from speaking publicly about the abuses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erase - to remove or obliterate something written or recorded; to remove from existence or memory
  • Synonym: deplatform - to remove someone's access to public platforms or media, especially online, so their views are no longer heard there

Etymology

Back-formation from the noun 'unperson', a term popularized by George Orwell in Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949).
