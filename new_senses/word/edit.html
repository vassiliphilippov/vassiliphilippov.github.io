to edit

1. VERB

/ ˈɛdɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛdɪt / (BrE)
to prepare written material for publication or presentation by correcting, revising, or adapting its content
revise written work for publication

Examples

Journalists often edit their articles several times to ensure accuracy and clarity.
She was hired to edit the manuscript before it went to print.
The publisher asked him to edit the novel for length and style.

Synset Examples

Edit a book on lexical semantics
she edited the letters of the politician so as to omit the most personal passages

Related Terms

  • Synonym: revise - to reconsider and alter text in order to improve it
  • Synonym: proofread - to read text in order to detect and correct errors

Etymology

From Latin 'editus', past participle of 'edere' (to give out, publish).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00200761-v
Synset POS: v

2. VERB

/ ˈɛdɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛdɪt / (BrE)
to supervise the preparation and publication of a newspaper, magazine, book, or other periodical, deciding on the content and making revisions as necessary
supervise publication of a publication or periodical

Examples

For over a decade, Mr. Robinson has edited the prestigious science magazine.
He was hired to edit the national daily newspaper, bringing a fresh perspective to its coverage.
She will edit the new anthology of short stories compiled from emerging writers.

Synset Examples

The same family has been editing the influential newspaper for almost 100 years

Related Terms

  • Synonym: publish - to prepare and issue printed or digital material for public distribution

Etymology

From Latin 'edere' (to bring forth, publish), via French 'éditer'.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00970070-v
Synset POS: v

3. VERB

/ ˈɛdɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛdɪt / (BrE)
to remove or eliminate material from a text, film, or other work, often to improve it or adjust its length
remove or cut parts from a work

Examples

She had to edit out unnecessary paragraphs to meet the word limit.
The director decided to edit several scenes to make the movie shorter.
They plan to edit the interview before it airs.

Synset Examples

she edited the juiciest scenes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to remove or delete parts of a text or work
  • Synonym: delete - to remove or erase written or recorded material
  • Antonym: add - to include or insert material into a text or work

Etymology

From Latin editus, past participle of edere (‘to bring forth, publish’).

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-00201227-v
Synset POS: v

4. VERB

/ ˈɛdɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛdɪt / (BrE)
to arrange, cut, and assemble the parts of recorded material, such as film or audio, to create a final version
arrange and assemble parts of recorded material

Examples

Professional sound engineers edit the recordings to ensure the best possible audio quality.
She spent hours learning how to edit her short film before the final screening.
They edited together clips from different interviews to create a compelling documentary.

Synset Examples

edit film
cut recording tape

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to remove parts of a recording or text
  • Synonym: assemble - to put together separate parts to form a whole

Etymology

From Latin 'editus', past participle of 'edere', meaning 'to put forth'.

5. VERB NEW

/ ˈɛdɪt / (AmE), / ˈɛdɪt / (BrE)
(in genetics/biotechnology) to change or alter the DNA of an organism deliberately using molecular techniques (for example CRISPR) in order to add, remove, or modify genetic information.
alter an organism's DNA

Examples

Scientists can edit the genome of crops to improve drought tolerance.
Using CRISPR, researchers edited the faulty gene to study its role in disease.
Ethicists debate whether it is acceptable to edit human embryos for nonmedical traits.

Synset Examples

Scientists can edit the genome of crops to improve drought tolerance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: genetic modification - the deliberate alteration of an organism's genetic material
  • Synonym: genetic engineering - the direct manipulation of an organism's genes using biotechnology

Etymology

From Latin 'editus', past participle of 'edere' (to bring forth, publish); a metaphorical extension of 'edit' (revise) to deliberate alteration of genetic code.
