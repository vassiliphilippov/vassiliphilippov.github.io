snapshot

1. NOUN

/ ˈsnæpˌʃɑt / (AmE), / ˈsnæpʃɒt / (BrE)
an informal photograph taken quickly, especially with a hand-held camera
an informal photograph

Examples

He kept a snapshot of his dog in his wallet.
My favorite snapshot from vacation shows us laughing on the beach.
She took a snapshot of her friends at the party.

Synset Examples

my snapshots haven't been developed yet
he tried to get unposed shots of his friends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: photograph - a picture made with a camera
  • Antonym: portrait - a painted or photographed representation of a person, usually posed

Etymology

From 'snap' + 'shot', first used in the late 19th century to describe a quick photograph.

2. NOUN NEW

/ ˈsnæpˌʃɑt / (AmE), / ˈsnæpʃɒt / (BrE)
A point-in-time copy of a digital system's state (for example, a virtual machine, filesystem, or database) used to preserve data for backup, rollback, or replication.
point-in-time digital copy

Examples

The database administrator created a snapshot of the production database before applying the patch.
Before updating the server, they stored several snapshots so they could roll back quickly if needed.
Using filesystem snapshots reduced downtime during the migration by allowing fast recoveries.

Synset Examples

The database administrator created a snapshot of the production database before applying the patch.
Before updating the server, they stored several snapshots so they could roll back quickly if needed.
Using filesystem snapshots reduced downtime during the migration by allowing fast recoveries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: backup - a copy of data kept to restore the original after loss or corruption
  • Synonym: checkpoint - a saved state used to resume or restore a process or system

Etymology

From photographic 'snapshot' (snap + shot); extended in computing as a metaphor for a quick, fixed copy of system state.
