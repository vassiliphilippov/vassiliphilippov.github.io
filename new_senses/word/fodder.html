fodder

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɑː.dɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɒd.ə / (BrE)
coarse food for livestock, typically made from whole plants, leaves, or stalks of cereal crops
coarse animal feed; livestock food

Examples

Corn stalks are commonly used as fodder on many farms.
Drought led to a shortage of fodder for the cattle this year.
The farmer stored bales of fodder in the barn for the winter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: forage - coarse food for grazing animals; feed

Etymology

From Old English 'fodor' (food, fodder), related to German 'Futter'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɑːdər / (AmE), / ˈfɒdə / (BrE)
(often figurative) soldiers or people who are regarded as expendable, especially in the face of danger such as artillery fire.
expendable soldiers or people

Examples

Commanders sometimes treated inexperienced recruits as fodder during the most dangerous battles.
Many feel that young volunteers became mere fodder in the long and bloody conflict.
The rebels refused to be used as political fodder in someone else’s fight.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cannon fodder - soldiers regarded as expendable in war

Etymology

From Old English 'fodor', from a Germanic root related to feeding cattle.

Source: WordNet
WordNet ID: oewn-09910752-n
Synset POS: n

3. VERB

/ ˈfɑːdər / (AmE), / ˈfɒdə / (BrE)
to give food such as hay or straw to domesticated animals.
to feed animals (with fodder)

Examples

Each morning, the farmer would fodder the cattle before sunrise.
The task of foddering the animals was shared among the children.
They carefully foddered the horses with a mix of hay and oats.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: feed - to give food to animals or people

Etymology

From Old English *fodor* (food, fodder), ultimately from Proto-Germanic.
