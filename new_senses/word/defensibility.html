defensibility

1. NOUN

/ dɪˌfɛnsəˈbɪləti / (AmE), / dɪˌfɛnsəˈbɪləti / (BrE)
The quality or state of being able to be defended against attack, criticism, or challenge.
quality of being defensible

Examples

Architects considered the defensibility of the site before choosing a location for the fortress.
The defensibility of the company's position was seriously questioned during the trial.
The lawyer argued for the defensibility of his client's actions in court.

Synset Examples

they built their castles with an eye to their defensibility
client complaints create a felt need for the defensibility of individual actions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defensibleness - the quality of being defensible
  • Antonym: indefensibility - the quality of being impossible to defend

Etymology

From Latin 'defendere' (to defend) + English '-ibility' (ability to be).

2. NOUN NEW

/ dɪˌfɛnsəˈbɪlɪti / (AmE), / dɪˌfɛnsəˈbɪlɪti / (BrE)
In business and strategy, the quality of a company, product, or plan that allows it to keep a sustainable competitive advantage and resist rivals, often through barriers such as patents, network effects, brand strength, or scale.
business competitive advantage (moat)

Examples

Investors assess defensibility to determine whether a startup can fend off competitors.
The product's network effects gave it strong defensibility in an already crowded market.
Without patents, the company's defensibility depended on brand recognition and rapid innovation.

Synset Examples

Investors assess defensibility to determine whether a startup can fend off competitors.
The product's network effects gave it strong defensibility in an already crowded market.
Without patents, the company's defensibility depended on brand recognition and rapid innovation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moat - a sustainable competitive barrier that protects a business from rivals
  • Synonym: competitive advantage - a feature or asset that lets a company perform better than competitors
  • Antonym: vulnerability - the quality of being open to attack or competition; lack of defensibility

Etymology

From Latin 'defendere' (to defend) + English suffix '-ibility' (ability to be).
