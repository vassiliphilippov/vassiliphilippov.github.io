to convolve

1. VERB

/ kənˈvɑlv / (AmE), / kənˈvɒlv / (BrE)
to twist or coil together; to wind into a spiral or curved shape
twist or coil together

Examples

Her hair seemed to convolve into intricate curls with little effort.
The two strands of rope convolve around each other to make a sturdy cord.
Vines convolve elegantly around the old fence, creating a natural lattice.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coil - to wind into loops or rings
  • Antonym: unwind - to straighten from a coiled or twisted form

Etymology

From Latin 'convolvere', from 'con-' (together) + 'volvere' (to roll).

2. VERB NEW

/ kənˈvɑlv / (AmE), / kənˈvɒlv / (BrE)
(mathematics, signal processing, computing) to combine two functions or signals by the mathematical operation called convolution, producing a third function that expresses how the shape of one is modified by the other.
combine functions by convolution

Examples

The neural network layer convolves the input with a small kernel to detect edges in images.
In signal processing, engineers often convolve a signal with a filter to remove unwanted noise.
To compute the system response, the students convolved the input and impulse response by numerical integration.
