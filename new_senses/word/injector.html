injector

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛktər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
A device that uses a motive fluid (such as steam or air) to drive another fluid into a system, often used for pumping or mixing.
device that uses fluid to move another fluid

Examples

A vacuum injector can be used to remove air from laboratory setups.
Modern jet engines use a fuel injector to deliver fuel to the combustion chamber.
The steam injector was invented to efficiently feed water into boilers.

Synset Examples

Depending on the specific application, an injector can take the form of an eductor-jet pump, a water eductor, a vacuum ejector, a steam-jet ejector, or an aspirator.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ejector - device that expels a substance using a jet of fluid
  • Synonym: aspirator - device for creating suction, often by jet action

Etymology

From Latin 'injectus', past participle of 'inicere' (to throw in, inject), plus '-or' denoting an agent or device.

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛktɚ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
a device used for forcing a fluid or particles into another substance or space, such as injecting water into a boiler or particles into an accelerator
device for injecting substances

Examples

A malfunctioning fuel injector can cause a car engine to run poorly.
Scientists calibrated the injector to introduce particles into the accelerator at precise speeds.
The injector on the steam engine needed to be replaced after years of heavy use.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: syringe - a device used for injecting fluids, especially in medical contexts
  • Synonym: pump - a machine for moving fluids, similar in function to some injectors

Etymology

From Latin 'injectus', past participle of 'inicere' (to throw in), plus English '-or' (agent noun suffix).

3. NOUN NEW

/ ɪnˈdʒɛktər / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛktə / (BrE)
(in computing) a component or framework that provides or supplies the required objects or services to other parts of a program by performing dependency injection, often at startup or runtime.
software dependency-injection component

Examples

The application's startup uses an injector to assemble services and wire dependencies.
Developers configured the injector so each controller received the correct service instances automatically.
Several frameworks include built-in injectors to simplify building large, modular applications.
