sublimation

1. NOUN

/ ˌsʌbləˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsʌblɪˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
(psychology) the process of modifying a natural impulse or instinct, especially a sexual one, into a socially acceptable activity or expression.
redirecting an impulse into acceptable action (psychology)
medical

Examples

Her success in business was seen by some as a form of sublimation of personal desires.
Some psychologists argue that sublimation allows individuals to channel sexual energy into productive, socially valued activities.
Through sublimation, he redirected his aggressive urges into creating powerful works of art.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repression - the act of suppressing unacceptable impulses (psychology)

Etymology

From Latin *sublimare* (raise), influenced by use in psychoanalytic theory.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsʌbləˈmeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌsʌblɪˈmeɪʃən / (BrE)
(Chemistry) The process in which a substance changes directly from a solid to a gas without passing through the liquid stage.
solid to gas change (chemistry)
medical

Examples

Dry ice undergoes sublimation at room temperature, turning directly from solid to carbon dioxide gas.
In chemistry labs, sublimation is used to purify certain compounds by turning them into vapor and then re-condensing them.
The sublimation of snow on a sunny day can make it disappear without melting.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deposition - the change of state from gas directly to solid

Etymology

From Medieval Latin sublimationem (a raising or uplifting), from Latin sublimare (to raise), relating to the process of a substance rising into vapor.
