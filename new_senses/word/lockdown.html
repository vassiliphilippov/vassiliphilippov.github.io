lockdown

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɑkˌdaʊn / (AmE), / ˈlɒkˌdaʊn / (BrE)
a situation in which people, especially prisoners, are confined to their rooms or cells as a security measure, often to regain control during an incident or threat
prisoners confined to cells as a security measure

Examples

After the riot, the warden initiated a full lockdown to restore order.
During the lockdown, all prisoners were kept in their cells for 24 hours a day.
The prison was placed under lockdown after the fight broke out between inmates.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curfew - a regulation requiring people to remain indoors during designated hours

Etymology

From 'lock' + 'down', first used in the context of prison security in the 1970s US.

2. NOUN NEW

/ ˈlɑkˌdaʊn / (AmE), / ˈlɒkˌdaʊn / (BrE)
a state of a computer, network, device, or software in which access and many functions are severely restricted to prevent tampering, intrusion, or data loss.
severely restricted system access

Examples

When the breach was detected, the server entered lockdown, halting external connections and data access.
The company's security team put the employee's laptop into lockdown until they could investigate the intrusion.
After the malware alert, the firewall triggered lockdown and blocked all unusual outbound traffic.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secure mode - a state in which a system restricts operations and access to protect itself from threats
  • Synonym: isolation - separation or restriction to prevent access or spread; in computing, running processes or data separately for safety

Etymology

From 'lock' + 'down'; originally used for prison security in the 1970s and later extended metaphorically to technology and other contexts.
