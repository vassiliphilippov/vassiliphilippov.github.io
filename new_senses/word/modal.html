modal

1. NOUN

/ ˈmoʊ.dəl / (AmE), / ˈməʊ.dəl / (BrE)
an auxiliary verb (such as 'can', 'may', or 'will') used to express possibility, necessity, obligation, or other modalities
auxiliary verb expressing modality

Examples

In English grammar, modals like 'should' and 'might' help express different levels of certainty.
Students often confuse when to use a modal with the base form of the verb.
The modal 'could' is often used to suggest possibilities or requests.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: auxiliary verb - a verb used in forming tenses, moods, voices, etc.
  • Antonym: main verb - the verb that shows the main action in a clause

Etymology

From Latin modalis, from modus (measure, manner).

/ ˈmoʊ.dl̩ / (AmE), / ˈməʊ.dl̩ / (BrE)
relating to a musical mode, especially as used in early church (ecclesiastical) music.
relating to a musical mode

Examples

Many Gregorian chants are written in a modal style rather than following modern major or minor keys.
The composer experimented with modal harmony to create an ancient, mystical atmosphere.
This piece exemplifies the use of modal melodies in Renaissance choral music.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tonal - relating to a musical key or tonality rather than a mode

Etymology
