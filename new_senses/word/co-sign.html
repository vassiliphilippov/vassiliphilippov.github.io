to co-sign

1. VERB

/ ˌkoʊˈsaɪn / (AmE), / ˌkəʊˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to sign a document jointly with another person, especially as a guarantee for a loan or financial agreement
sign jointly (esp. for a loan)

Examples

He regretted having co-signed his cousin’s loan when the payments were missed.
If you co-sign the apartment lease, you will be responsible for the rent if your friend defaults.
She agreed to co-sign the loan so her brother could buy a car.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guarantee - to formally assure the performance or reliability of something, often financially

Etymology

From Latin prefix 'co-' (together) + English 'sign'.

2. VERB

/ koʊˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kəʊˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to sign a document jointly with another person, especially to accept shared responsibility for an obligation
sign jointly with another person

Examples

Both employees were required to co-sign the expense report before submission.
Her father agreed to co-sign the loan so she could buy a car.
If you co-sign a credit application, you are legally responsible for repayment.

Synset Examples

Husband and wife co-signed the lease

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sign - to write one's signature on a document

Etymology

From Latin 'co-' (together) and 'signare' (to sign).

3. VERB NEW

/ koʊˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kəʊˈsaɪn / (BrE)
informal: to publicly express support for or agree with a statement, idea, proposal, or person.
publicly endorse; agree with
informal

Examples

Many celebrities decided to co-sign the petition to publicly endorse the charity's reforms.
I completely co-sign her view that the new policy will improve work–life balance.
After the speech, three senior researchers co-signed a joint statement supporting the study.
