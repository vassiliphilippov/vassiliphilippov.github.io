to hibernate

1. VERB

/ ˈhaɪbɚˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈhʌɪbəneɪt / (BrE)
to spend a period of time in an inactive or dormant state, especially during cold weather
be in a dormant or inactive state

Examples

Bears hibernate through the coldest months to conserve energy.
During the economic downturn, the company chose to hibernate rather than close permanently.
Many reptiles hibernate when temperatures drop in the winter.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dormant - in a state of rest or inactivity
  • Antonym: active - engaged in action or activity

Etymology

From Latin hibernare (to pass the winter), from hiberna (winter quarters).

2. VERB

/ ˈhaɪbərˌneɪt / (AmE), / ˈhaɪbəneɪt / (BrE)
(of some animals) to spend the winter in a state like deep sleep in order to survive cold and food scarcity.
spend the winter in a dormant state

Examples

Bears often hibernate in dens or caves until spring arrives.
If food is scarce, some reptiles will hibernate for several months.
Many animals, such as bats and groundhogs, hibernate to survive the cold winter months.

Synset Examples

Bears must eat a lot of food before they hibernate in their caves

Related Terms

  • Antonym: aestivate - spend the summer in a dormant state (of animals)

Etymology

From Latin hibernare (to spend the winter), from hiberna (winter quarters).

(computing) to save the current contents of a computer's memory to non-volatile storage and power the device down so the session can be restored later.

Examples

Before the flight, she set her laptop to hibernate so it would save work and turn off.

Closing all apps and hibernating the tablet helped him conserve battery during travel.
