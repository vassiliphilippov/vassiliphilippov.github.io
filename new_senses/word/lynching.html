lynching

1. NOUN

/ ˈlɪntʃɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈlɪntʃɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of killing someone, usually by hanging, carried out by a mob without legal authority or trial
extrajudicial killing by a mob

Examples

Historians have documented many lynchings in the early twentieth century in the southern United States.
The film depicted the horrors and injustices of lynching in a small town.
The tragic lynching of the activist shocked the entire nation.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: execution - the carrying out of a sentence of death on a condemned person
  • Degree Lower: murder - the unlawful premeditated killing of one human being by another

Etymology

From the surname Lynch, referencing 'Lynch law,' attributed to Captain William Lynch (1742–1820) of Virginia.

a figurative use meaning a very public, often aggressive condemnation, shaming, or 'cancellation' of a person by a group (especially online), carried out without fair process.
figurative public shaming or cancellation

Examples

The online lynching of the celebrity prompted a public apology and platform bans.
After one angry tweet, the politician faced a virtual lynching that destroyed his reputation overnight.
Editors debated whether the harsh column amounted to a journalistic lynching rather than fair criticism.
