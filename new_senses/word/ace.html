ACE

1. NOUN

/ ˌeɪ si ˈi / (AmE), / ˌeɪ si ˈi / (BrE)
a proteolytic enzyme that converts angiotensin I into angiotensin II, playing a crucial role in blood pressure regulation
enzyme controlling blood pressure
medical

Examples

Doctors may prescribe ACE inhibitors to treat high blood pressure.
Research on ACE has led to new treatments for heart failure.
The level of ACE in the blood can be important for diagnosing certain conditions.

Etymology

Acronym from angiotensin-converting enzyme.

2. NOUN NEW

/ \u02cce\u026a si \u02c8i / (AmE), / \u02cce\u026a si \u02c8i / (BrE)
(in public health and psychology) an event or experience in childhood that is traumatic or highly stressful and is linked to increased risk of poor health or social outcomes later in life.
adverse childhood experience (trauma)
medical

Examples

A high ACE score is associated with greater health risks in adulthood.
After the study, clinicians screened children for multiple ACEs to identify early support needs.
Communities that reduce poverty and violence can lower young people's overall ACE burdens.
