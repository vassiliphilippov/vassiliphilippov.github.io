D-day

1. NOUN

/ ˈdiˌdeɪ / (AmE), / ˈdiː ˌdeɪ / (BrE)
The day on which the Allied forces began their invasion of France during World War II, specifically June 6, 1944.
Allied invasion of France, 1944

Examples

Every year, veterans commemorate the anniversary of D-day in remembrance ceremonies.
He gave a detailed account of the events that unfolded on D-day.
The Normandy landings on D-day changed the course of World War II.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: VE Day - Victory in Europe Day, marking the end of WWII in Europe

Etymology

Military term for the designated day of a planned operation; widely adopted to refer specifically to June 6, 1944.

A decisive or critical day when an important action or decision will take place; a make-or-break date for an event or project.
decisive, make-or-break day

Examples

The product launch next month is the company's D-day to prove the new strategy works.
After years of planning, the team's playoff match felt like their D-day for championship glory.
She marked the final review as her personal D-day, when she must decide whether to accept the offer.
