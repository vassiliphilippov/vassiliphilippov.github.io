similarity

1. NOUN

/ ˌsɪm.əˈlɛr.ə.ti / (AmE), / ˌsɪm.ɪˈlær.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
In psychology, especially Gestalt theory, the principle that elements in a visual field that are similar in appearance are perceived as belonging together or forming a group.
psychological principle of grouping by likeness

Examples

Designers use similarity to organize visual information and help users interpret interfaces.
The Gestalt principle of similarity explains why we perceive these shapes as a single group.
When analyzing a logo, notice how the similarity of colors can create a sense of unity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: proximity - Gestalt principle: elements close to each other are perceived as a group
  • Antonym: dissimilarity - the state of being different or unalike

Etymology

From Latin 'similitudo' meaning likeness or resemblance.

2. NOUN

/ ˌsɪməˈlɛrɪti / (AmE), / ˌsɪməˈlarəti / (BrE)
The state or quality of being similar; a likeness or resemblance between two or more things.
the state of being similar; resemblance

Examples

Despite their similarity, the two concepts are fundamentally different.
Genetics can explain the similarity between family members.
There is a remarkable similarity between their painting styles.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: difference - the quality that makes one thing unlike another
  • Synonym: resemblance - a similarity to someone or something

Etymology

From Latin 'similitudo', from 'similis' meaning 'like, resembling'.

(in computing and data science) a quantitative measure or score that indicates how alike two data objects are, often computed by algorithms such as cosine similarity, Jaccard index, or Euclidean-based measures.
quantitative likeness measure (data science)

Examples

The recommender system computes similarity scores between user profiles and items to suggest products.
Researchers measured cosine similarity between document vectors to cluster related news articles.
A higher similarity value indicates the algorithm considers two images more alike.
