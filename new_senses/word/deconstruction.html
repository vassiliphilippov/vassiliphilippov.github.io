deconstruction

1. NOUN

/ ˌdiːkənˈstrʌkʃən / (AmE), / ˌdiːkənˈstrʌkʃən / (BrE)
a method of critical analysis that seeks to reveal hidden contradictions and layers of meaning in texts, often by questioning traditional assumptions about language and interpretation
philosophical theory of textual analysis

Examples

Her essay employed deconstruction to challenge traditional interpretations of the novel.
Many scholars use deconstruction to analyze common themes in literature and film.
The professor explained how deconstruction can reveal contradictions in seemingly straightforward texts.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: criticism - the analysis and evaluation of works of art or literature

Etymology

From French 'déconstruction', coined by philosopher Jacques Derrida in the 1960s.

(in cooking) a technique in which a traditional dish is broken into its component parts and presented separately so diners can experience and combine textures and flavours themselves.
culinary technique separating a dish into components

Examples

The restaurant's deconstruction of the classic apple pie separated crust, fruit, and cream into elegant components.
For the menu, the chef offered a deconstruction of the burger, serving patty, bun, and sauce separately.
Her review praised the deconstruction that highlighted textures while keeping the dish's original flavors balanced.
