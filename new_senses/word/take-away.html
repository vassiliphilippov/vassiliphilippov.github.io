take-away

1. ADJ

/ ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (BrE)
(of food) prepared to be eaten off the premises or taken out for consumption elsewhere.
food to be eaten off the premises
british

Examples

She placed an order at the take-away counter before heading home.
The café offers both dine-in and take-away options for customers.
We decided to get some take-away fish and chips for dinner.

Synset Examples

takeout pizza
the takeout counter
‘take-away’ is chiefly British

Related Terms

  • Synonym: takeout - food prepared to be eaten off the premises (chiefly American)

Etymology

Formed from the verb ‘take’ and preposition ‘away’, chiefly British; see also American ‘takeout’.

2. NOUN NEW

/ ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (AmE), / ˈteɪkəˌweɪ / (BrE)
a main point, lesson, or important piece of information that someone learns from a talk, meeting, experience, or text.
main point or lesson

Examples

My main take-away from the seminar was to keep user feedback central to design decisions.
The consultant summarized three practical take-aways that the team could implement next quarter.
If there's one take-away from our conversation, it's that communication must improve across departments.

Synset Examples

My main take-away from the seminar was to keep user feedback central to design decisions.
The consultant summarized three practical take-aways that the team could implement next quarter.
If there's one take-away from our conversation, it's that communication must improve across departments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: main point - the most important idea or conclusion from something said or written
  • Synonym: lesson - an important thing that someone learns as a result of experience or study

Etymology

From the verb 'take' + adverb 'away'; attested as a figurative noun in recent English (see also American 'takeaway').
