vaccinia

1. NOUN

/ vækˈsɪniə / (AmE), / vækˈsɪniə / (BrE)
a viral disease of cattle that causes mild skin lesions, and the virus used in the vaccine for smallpox in humans
virus used in smallpox vaccination
medical

Examples

Vaccinia virus was historically instrumental in the global eradication of smallpox.
A mild skin reaction can sometimes occur at the site of vaccinia inoculation.
Researchers are investigating new uses for vaccinia virus in developing other vaccines.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: smallpox - a serious, contagious disease caused by the variola virus

Etymology

From Latin 'vaccinus' (of or from cows), related to 'vacca' (cow).

2. NOUN

/ vækˈsɪniə / (AmE), / vækˈsɪniə / (BrE)
a local infection caused by inoculation with the cowpox virus, especially as used in vaccination to confer immunity to smallpox
infection from smallpox vaccination
medical

Examples

He developed a mild case of vaccinia after receiving the smallpox vaccine.
Researchers still study vaccinia to understand immune responses to viral infections.
The vaccinia virus was crucial in the global eradication of smallpox.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cowpox - an infectious disease caused by the cowpox virus, related to smallpox and used in vaccination
  • Antonym: smallpox - a serious, contagious, and sometimes fatal infectious disease caused by the variola virus

Etymology

From New Latin, from Latin 'vacca' (cow), reflecting its origin in the cowpox virus.

3. NOUN NEW

/ vækˈsɪniə / (AmE), / vækˈsɪniə / (BrE)
(in biotechnology and medical research) an engineered form of the vaccinia virus used as a viral vector to deliver antigens or therapeutic genes in vaccines and oncolytic therapies.
vaccinia viral vector for vaccines/therapies
medical

Examples

The research team used a recombinant vaccinia vector to deliver the tumor antigen in mice.
In the clinical trial, patients received a dose of modified vaccinia designed to attack cancer cells.
Scientists often choose vaccinia because its large genome allows insertion of multiple therapeutic genes.
