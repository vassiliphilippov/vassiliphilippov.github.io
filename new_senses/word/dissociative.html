dissociative

1. ADJ

/ dɪˈsoʊʃiˌeɪtɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈsəʊsiətɪv / (BrE)
(especially in psychology or medicine) tending to cause or relating to dissociation, particularly of mental processes or identity.
causing or relating to dissociation
medical

Examples

Certain drugs can have dissociative effects on perception and consciousness.
Researchers are studying the dissociative nature of some mental health conditions.
The patient exhibited symptoms of a dissociative disorder after the traumatic event.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: associative - tending to associate or connect; the opposite of dissociative

Etymology

From Latin 'dissociare' (to separate) + '-ive'.

2. NOUN NEW

/ dɪˈsoʊʃiˌeɪtɪv / (AmE), / dɪˈsəʊsiətɪv / (BrE)
a psychoactive drug (or class of drugs) that produces dissociation — a sense of detachment from reality, self, or surroundings (for example, ketamine or PCP).
drug causing dissociation
medical

Examples

At the festival, several attendees admitted trying a dissociative that left them feeling unreal and detached from their bodies.
Paramedics recognized signs of overdose after the patient had taken a dissociative and lost motor control.
Researchers studied how chronic use of dissociatives affects memory and perception in long-term users.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dissociative anesthetic - a type of drug that produces dissociation and is used medically as an anaesthetic (often cited as a subtype of dissociatives).

Etymology

From Latin 'dissociare' (to separate) + English suffix '-ive'; the adjective later came to be used as a noun for substances that cause dissociation.
