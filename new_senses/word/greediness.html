greediness

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡriːdi.nəs / (AmE), / ˈɡriːdɪnɪs / (BrE)
An excessive or insatiable desire for food.
excessive desire for food

Examples

His greediness at the dinner table was astonishing to everyone present.
She was chastised for her greediness, having eaten three servings before anyone else started.
The child's greediness for sweets led to an upset stomach.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gluttony - habitual greed or excess in eating

Etymology

From 'greedy' + '-ness'; ultimately from Old English 'grǣdig'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡriːdinəs / (AmE), / ˈɡriːdinɪs / (BrE)
An excessive or insatiable desire, especially for wealth or possessions.
excessive desire for wealth or possessions

Examples

His greediness drove him to manipulate the stock market for personal gain.
Many people criticize the greediness of large corporations.
The lawyer's greediness became apparent when he demanded an outrageous fee.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: greed - intense and selfish desire for something, especially wealth or power
  • Antonym: generosity - quality of being kind and generous; willingness to give

3. NOUN NEW

/ ˈɡriːdinəs / (AmE), / ˈɡriːdinəs / (BrE)
(in computer science) the tendency of an algorithm or heuristic to choose the locally best option at each step, possibly causing it to miss a globally optimal solution.
preference for local choices in algorithms

Examples

The algorithm's greediness caused it to accept a quick solution instead of searching for a better one.
Researchers measured the greediness of several heuristics to compare their performance on large graphs.
Reducing the algorithm's greediness by adding lookahead improved results on many benchmark problems.

Synset Examples

Reducing the algorithm's greediness by adding lookahead improved results on many benchmark problems.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: greedy algorithm - an algorithmic approach that makes a locally optimal choice at each step
  • Antonym: exhaustive search - a search that examines all possible solutions (contrasts with a greedy approach)

Etymology

From 'greedy' + '-ness'; ultimately from Old English 'grædig'.
