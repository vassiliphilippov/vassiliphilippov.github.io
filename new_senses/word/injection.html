injection

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʒɛkʃən / (BrE)
the act of forcefully inserting a substance into something, especially by using pressure
forceful insertion of a substance

Examples

Fuel injection allows the engine to run more efficiently in modern cars.
The injection of contrast dye made the scan results much clearer.
The nurse prepared the injection and asked the patient to roll up his sleeve.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: infusion - the process of delivering a liquid, usually medication, into the body, often slowly

Etymology

From Latin 'injectio', from 'injicere' (to throw in).

4. NOUN NEW

/ \u026an\u02c8d\u0292\u025bk\u0283\u0259n / (AmE), / \u026an\u02c8d\u0292\u025bk\u0283\u0259n / (BrE)
(in software engineering) a design pattern in which an object's required components or services (its dependencies) are supplied from the outside rather than created by the object itself.
software design pattern (dependency injection)

Examples

The framework uses injection to supply services to components, promoting loose coupling.
Unit tests were easier because the developers relied on injection to provide mock objects.
After refactoring, the codebase adopted injection, allowing implementations to be swapped at runtime.
