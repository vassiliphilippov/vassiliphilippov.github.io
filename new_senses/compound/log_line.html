log line

1. NOUN

/ lɑɡ laɪn / (AmE), / lɒɡ laɪn / (BrE)
A length of knotted cord wound on a reel and attached to a piece of wood, used in navigation to measure a ship's speed through water.
knotted cord used to measure ship speed

Examples

After retrieving the log line, the navigator recorded the number of knots let out.
Before modern instruments, a log line was essential for every long sea voyage.
The sailors threw the log line overboard to determine their ship's speed.

Etymology

From 'log' (referring to the wood used for measuring) and 'line' (the cord). Nautical in origin.

2. NOUN NEW

/ lɑɡ laɪn / (AmE), / lɒɡ laɪn / (BrE)
A single line in a computer or system log file that records an event, message, or timestamp.
single line in a log file

Examples

The developer checked the log line to find when the error first occurred during the outage.
All log lines included a timestamp, the process ID, and a short message about the event.
A single log line showed that the service restarted unexpectedly at midnight.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: log entry - a recorded item in a log file, often equivalent to a log line
  • Synonym: log message - the textual message portion of a log line describing an event or status

Etymology

From 'log' (short for logbook, a record of events) + 'line' (a single line of text).
