high-water mark

1. NOUN

/ ˌhaɪ ˈwɔtɚ ˌmɑrk / (AmE), / ˌhaɪ ˈwɔːtə ˌmɑːk / (BrE)
a line or mark indicating the highest level reached by a body of water, or figuratively, the highest level or point achieved by something
the highest point reached (by water or figuratively)

Examples

That album was considered the high-water mark of her career.
The high-water mark on the wall indicates how severe last year's storm was.
The debris on the riverbank clearly showed the high-water mark after the flood.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peak - the highest point or level, especially of achievement
  • Antonym: low-water mark - the lowest point or level reached by a body of water or by something figuratively

2. NOUN

/ ˌhaɪ ˈwɔtɚ mɑrk / (AmE), / ˌhaɪ ˈwɔːtə mɑːk / (BrE)
the highest level of success or achievement that has been reached
highest point of achievement

Examples

Her performance this year set a new high-water mark.
The company’s profits reached their high-water mark last year.
Winning the championship was the high-water mark of his sports career.

Synset Examples

Her performance this year set a new high-water mark.
The high-water mark of his career was winning the award.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peak - the highest point or greatest success
  • Antonym: nadir - the lowest point; opposite of a high point

Etymology

Originally referred to the highest point reached by rising water, now used figuratively for the peak of achievement.

(in computing) the maximum level of a resource (for example, memory or disk) reached by a process or system, often recorded to trigger cleanup, growth, or scaling decisions.
peak resource usage (computing)

Examples

The allocator uses the high-water mark to decide when to allocate more memory.
During stress tests the server's high-water mark reached 95% of available disk capacity.
Developers monitor the application's high-water mark to schedule garbage collection and scaling events.

Etymology

Metaphorical extension from the literal 'high-water mark' (a line left by rising water); applied in computing to mean the highest observed resource level.
