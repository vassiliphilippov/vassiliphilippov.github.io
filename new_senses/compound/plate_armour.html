plate armour

1. NOUN

/ pleɪt ˈɑrmər / (AmE), / pleɪt ˈɑːmə / (BrE)
A type of personal armor made from large iron or steel plates, used historically to protect the body, especially in the late Middle Ages.
historical body armor made from metal plates

Examples

By the fifteenth century, plate armour had largely replaced mail among European cavalry.
Craftsmen spent months forging plate armour to fit each individual warrior perfectly.
The museum’s medieval collection features a stunning suit of plate armour once worn by a famous knight.

Synset Examples

The increasing power and availability of firearms and the nature of large, state-supported infantry led to more portions of plate armour being cast off in favour of cheaper, more mobile troops.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mail - a type of armor consisting of small metal rings linked together in a pattern to form a mesh

2. NOUN

/ pleɪt ˈɑrmər / (AmE), / pleɪt ˈɑːmə / (BrE)
specially hardened steel plates used to protect fortifications or vehicles from enemy fire
hardened steel protective plating

Examples

Advancements in plate armour made military vehicles much harder to penetrate during the war.
Engineers reinforced the bunker with additional plate armour for greater protection against explosions.
The tank's plate armour was designed to withstand heavy artillery fire.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: body armour - protective clothing to shield the wearer from physical harm

Etymology

From 'plate' (flat sheet of metal) and 'armour' (protective covering), dating to Middle English and Old French origins.

/ pleɪt ˈɑrmər / (AmE), / pleɪt ˈɑːmə / (BrE)
A hard ballistic insert (typically ceramic, composite, or steel) carried in a vest or carrier to stop or reduce bullets and shrapnel.
ballistic plate for body armour

Examples

The officer's plate armour included ceramic plates in his tactical vest.
She ordered new plate armour rated to stop rifle rounds before her deployment overseas.
Modern plate armour is often ceramic or composite and fits into the front and back pockets.
