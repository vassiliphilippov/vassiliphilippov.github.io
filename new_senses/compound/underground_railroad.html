Underground Railroad

1. NOUN

/ ˌʌndərˌɡraʊnd ˈreɪlroʊd / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˌɡraʊnd ˈreɪlrəʊd / (BrE)
A secret network organized by abolitionists to help enslaved African Americans escape to free states and Canada before the American Civil War.
secret network aiding escaped slaves (U.S. history)

Examples

Harriet Tubman is one of the most famous conductors of the Underground Railroad.
The Underground Railroad played a crucial role in helping many slaves reach freedom before the Civil War.
The history textbook included a chapter on the Underground Railroad and its impact on American society.

Etymology

Named for the metaphor of a railway, with 'underground' indicating secrecy; first used in the early 19th century in the U.S.

Examples

Humanitarian groups set up an informal Underground Railroad to help asylum seekers reach safety across the border.
Journalists described the network of safe houses as a modern Underground Railroad for migrants fleeing violence.
Volunteers warned they risk prosecution by running an illegal Underground Railroad across several countries.
