double dipping

1. NOUN

/ ˈdʌbəl ˌdɪpɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈdʌbəl ˌdɪpɪŋ / (BrE)
The practice of receiving two incomes from the same source, such as holding a government job while also drawing a government pension.
receiving two incomes from the same source

Examples

Critics argue that double dipping unfairly benefits public employees at taxpayer expense.
Investigations revealed widespread double dipping in the agency over the last decade.
The city introduced new rules to prevent double dipping among retired officials who return to government jobs.

2. NOUN NEW

/ ˈdʌbəl ˈdɪpɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈdʌbəl ˈdɪpɪŋ / (BrE)
(in data science and statistics) the practice of using the same data (or information derived from it) both to train a model and to evaluate it, producing biased or overly optimistic performance estimates.
using the same data for training and evaluation

Examples

Reviewers noted the paper suffered from double dipping, using the same dataset for training and testing.
Because of double dipping, their model reported unrealistically high accuracy on the evaluation set.
Our lab fixed the issue by avoiding double dipping and adopting proper cross-validation procedures.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: data leakage - the unintended transfer of information from test data into training, which can inflate model performance estimates

Etymology

From literal 'double dip' (to dip twice) used metaphorically to mean taking twice; applied to datasets in modern statistics and machine learning.
