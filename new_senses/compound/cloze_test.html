cloze test

1. NOUN

/ kloʊz tɛst / (AmE), / kləʊz tɛst / (BrE)
a test used to assess reading ability in which words are removed from a passage and the reader is required to fill in the blanks
passage with blanks to test reading skill

Examples

Creating a cloze test is a popular technique in English language teaching.
Students found the cloze test challenging because some of the missing words were difficult to guess.
The teacher used a cloze test to evaluate the students' comprehension skills.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gap-fill - a similar test requiring completion of missing words in a text

Etymology

From 'closure,' coined in the 1950s by psychologist W. L. Taylor.

2. NOUN NEW

/ kloʊz tɛst / (AmE), / kləʊz tɛst / (BrE)
(in computational linguistics and machine learning) a training or evaluation task in which words or tokens are masked in a text and a model must predict the missing items; also called a masked-language modeling objective.
NLP masked-word prediction task

Examples

During pretraining, the model learns through a cloze test objective by predicting masked tokens.
The tutorial asked students to implement a simple cloze test to evaluate their language model's accuracy.
Researchers compared models using a challenging cloze test containing idioms and rare vocabulary items.

Synset Examples

During pretraining, the model learns through a cloze test objective by predicting masked tokens.
The tutorial asked students to implement a simple cloze test to evaluate their language model's accuracy.
Researchers compared models using a challenging cloze test containing idioms and rare vocabulary items.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: masked language modeling - a training objective where some tokens are hidden and the model predicts them
  • Synonym: fill-in-the-blank - a task where missing words in a sentence or passage must be supplied

Etymology

From 'cloze' (from 'closure'), coined in the 1950s by psychologist W. L. Taylor; later adopted in computational linguistics to name masked-word prediction objectives.
