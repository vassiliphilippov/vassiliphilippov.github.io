Constitutional Convention

1. NOUN

/ kənˌstɪtuˈʃənl kənˈvɛnʃən / (AmE), / kənˌstɪtjuˈʃənl kənˈvɛnʃən / (BrE)
the assembly of delegates in 1787 that drafted the Constitution of the United States
U.S. assembly that drafted the Constitution

Examples

Debates at the Constitutional Convention addressed issues such as representation and federal power.
Many historians study the Constitutional Convention to understand the founding principles of the U.S. government.
The Constitutional Convention of 1787 resulted in the creation of the current United States Constitution.

2. NOUN NEW

/ k\u0259n\u02ccst\u026atu\u02c8\u0283\u0259nl k\u0259n\u02c8v\u025bn\u0283\u0259n / (AmE), / k\u0259n\u02ccst\u026atju\u02c8\u0283\u0259nl k\u0259n\u02c8v\u025bn\u0283\u0259n / (BrE)
(in politics) a formal meeting or assembly convened to draft or propose amendments to a constitution, often used of the process for proposing national or state constitutional changes.
meeting to propose constitutional amendments
legal

Examples

Several states called a Constitutional Convention to discuss proposed changes to their state constitution.
Under Article V, some activists urged Congress to permit a Constitutional Convention to propose national amendments.
The commission debated whether a Constitutional Convention would better address systemic reform than piecemeal legislation.
