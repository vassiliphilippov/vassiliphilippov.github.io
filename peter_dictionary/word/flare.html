flare

1. NOUN

/ flɛr / (AmE), / fleə / (BrE)
a shape that spreads outward, becoming wider at one end or edge, especially in clothing or design

Examples

This gives a wide flare to the pecs, causing them to flow dramatically upward into deltoids and dramatically downward into the serratus and lats.

Synset Examples

the skirt had a wide flare

Related Terms

  • Antonym: taper - a gradual narrowing toward one end

Etymology

Probably from Old Norse *flara* (to spread out), related to 'flare' (to blaze up).

2. NOUN

/ flɛr / (AmE), / fleə / (BrE)
a device producing a bright burst of light, used for signaling, warning, or illumination

Examples

We knew the enemy was subdued, because a flare was fired as the signal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: signal - an act, gesture, or device that gives information or instructions
  • Synonym: beacon - a fire or light used as a signal, especially on the coast

Etymology

Probably from 17th-century Scandinavian (e.g., Norwegian 'flara', to blaze up).

3. VERB

/ flɛr / (AmE), / fleə / (BrE)
(of an object or part) to become wider or spread outward, especially at one end.

Examples

The horse shied at the dreadful thing and flared its nostrils.

Synset Examples

The bellbottom pants flare out

Related Terms

  • Antonym: narrow - to become or make less wide

Etymology

Probably altered from obsolete 'flair' (to spread), of Scandinavian origin.

4. VERB

/ flɛɹ / (AmE), / fleə / (BrE)
to burn with a sudden, brief, or intense brightness or flame

Examples

A match flared, and he reached above his head to light a lantern which hung from a wire loop.

Synset Examples

Every star seemed to flare with new intensity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blaze - to burn with a strong, bright flame
  • Degree Lower: glow - to shine with a steady, soft light

Etymology

Probably alteration of 'flare' meaning to spread or flutter, of Scandinavian origin.
