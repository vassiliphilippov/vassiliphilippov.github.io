farther

1. ADV

/ ˈfɑrðɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
To or at a greater extent, degree, or a more advanced stage.

Examples

Indeed we are forced to go much farther.
But the farther out he moves, the thinner will be his hold on conclusive evidence, and the larger the speculative component in his inferences.
They sit alone in their rooms and try to paint, and only succeed in isolating themselves still farther from life.

Synset Examples

further complicated by uncertainty about the future
let's not discuss it further
nothing could be further from the truth
they are further along in their research than we expected
the application of the law was extended farther
he is going no farther in his studies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: further - to or at a greater extent or degree or a more advanced stage; used in both physical and abstract senses
  • Antonym: closer - at a smaller distance or extent

Etymology

Comparative form of 'far', influenced by 'further'.

2. ADV

/ ˈfɑrðɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
At a greater distance in space or degree; more remote or advanced.

Examples

In spreading the factories even farther, the automobile may not have changed to any great extent the growth pattern of the cities.

Synset Examples

nothing could be further from the truth
further from our expectations
farther from the truth
farther from our expectations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: further - at a greater distance or to a greater extent; synonym of farther
  • Antonym: closer - at a shorter distance; antonym of farther

Etymology

Comparative of 'far'; influenced by analogy with 'further.'

3. ADV

/ ˈfɑrðər / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
To or at a greater distance in space or time; used especially for physical distance.

Examples

With the universal list as his weapon, Swadesh has extended his march of conquest farther and farther into the past, eight, ten, twelve millennia back.

Synset Examples

went only three miles further
further in the future

Related Terms

  • Synonym: further - to or at a greater distance in space or time (alternative form, esp. nonphysical)

Etymology

Comparative of 'far', from Old English 'feor', further influenced by Middle English 'further' and 'farther'.
