choke

1. VERB

/ tʃoʊk / (AmE), / tʃəʊk / (BrE)
to make breathing difficult by being too tight, or by pressing or blocking the throat, airways, or passage

Examples

Now, Adam, in the gray light of afternoon, stared across at the hut opposite his tent, and thought of Simms Purdew lying in there in the gloom, snoring on his bunk, with the fumes of whisky choking the air.
Molten glare singed their eyelids an angry crimson; suffocating air sapped their strength and strained their nerves to snapping; dust choked their throats and lay like acid in their lungs.

Synset Examples

This neckband is choking the cat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strangle - to squeeze or constrict the throat so as to cut off breathing
  • Synonym: suffocate - to die or cause to die from lack of air or inability to breathe
  • Antonym: release - to free from tightness or pressure

Etymology

From Old English 'cēocian', related to 'cheek' and 'jaw'.

2. VERB

/ tʃoʊk / (AmE), / tʃəʊk / (BrE)
To be unable to breathe properly because of overwhelming emotion, such as sadness or excitement.

Examples

Synset Examples

She choked with emotion when she spoke about her deceased husband

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sob - to cry noisily, making loud, convulsive gasps
  • Synonym: gasp - catch one's breath with an open mouth, due to pain or strong emotion

3. VERB

/ tʃoʊk / (AmE), / tʃəʊk / (BrE)
To squeeze tightly around someone's neck in order to strangle them, often using one's hands.

Examples

In another attack, Samuel Verstandig, 41, proprietor of a food store in the 2100 block Aiken street, told police two Negroes assaulted him in his store and stole $ 150 from the cash register after choking and beating him.

Synset Examples

The man choked his opponent

Related Terms

  • Synonym: strangle - to kill by squeezing the throat to cut off the air supply

Etymology

From Old English *ceocian* (to strangle), related to *ceoce* (cheek, jaw).
