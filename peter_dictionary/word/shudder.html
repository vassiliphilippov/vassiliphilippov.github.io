shudder

1. VERB

/ ˈʃʌdɚ / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
(of a person) to tremble or shake suddenly and uncontrollably, often from fear, disgust, or cold.

Examples

Both shudder at the thought of proceeding too far beyond the sewage system and the electric light lines.
The boy shuddered at the deathly pale countenance with its wrinkles and gray hair.
The thought made Pamela shudder.
I shuddered and backed out of the room.

Synset Examples

The children are shivering--turn on the heat!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shiver - to shake slightly and quickly, especially from cold, fear, or excitement

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin; first recorded in Middle English (late 14th century).

2. VERB

/ ˈʃʌdɚ / (AmE), / ˈʃʌdə / (BrE)
to tremble violently or convulsively, often from fear, cold, or strong emotion

Examples

A single kick made it spring open, shuddering.
Greg tightened his turn until the plane shuddered.
I shuddered to think that someone so dear to me could even associate with such a sinister milieu.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tremble - to shake involuntarily, typically as a result of anxiety or excitement
  • Synonym: quiver - to shake with slight, rapid movements
  • Synonym: shake - to move quickly up and down or from side to side

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English *shoderen*, related to *shudder* (noun), of imitative origin.
