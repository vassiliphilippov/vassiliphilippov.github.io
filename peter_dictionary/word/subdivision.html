subdivision

1. NOUN

/ ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (BrE)
A part or section formed by dividing something larger, especially a distinct division within an organization or system.

Examples

There's Gaetan d'Amours who is our newest Mr. Canada; Jean-Paul Senesac, whose story appeared here two issues ago; Jack Boissoneault, who was with us last month; Charles Harve, who recently won the "Most Muscular Man "subdivision award in the Mr. Canada event; and a host of others.

Synset Examples

a branch of Congress
botany is a branch of biology
the Germanic branch of Indo-European languages

Related Terms

  • Synonym: branch - a division of a larger organization or system

Etymology

From Late Latin 'subdivisio', from Latin 'sub-' (under) + 'dividere' (to divide).

2. NOUN

/ ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (BrE)
a residential area created by dividing a larger tract of land into individual lots for houses, often with planned streets and amenities.

Examples

The major contributor was a shopping center with houses being added to the system as the subdivision developed.
The BOD of the raw sewage was typical of domestic sewage from a subdivision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: housing development - an area where new homes are built as a planned community

Etymology

From Latin 'subdividere' (to divide further), from 'sub-' (under) + 'dividere' (to divide).

3. NOUN

/ ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (AmE), / ˌsʌb.dɪˈvɪʒ.ən / (BrE)
The act of dividing something that has already been divided; a further division or partition.

Examples

Land within commuting distance of a growing city is usually high in price, higher if it has subdivision possibilities.
The possibilities of selling the property later on for suburban subdivision.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: division - the act or process of dividing

Etymology

From Latin 'subdividere', meaning 'to divide further'.
