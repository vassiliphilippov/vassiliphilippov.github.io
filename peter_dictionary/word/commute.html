2. VERB

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
to travel regularly between one's home and place of work or study

Examples

Land within commuting distance of a growing city is usually high in price, higher if it has subdivision possibilities.
When he added to his Pittsburgh commitments the directorship of the London Philharmonic Orchestra in 1958, he conducted one hundred fifty concerts within nine months, "commuting "between the two cities.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: commuter - a person who travels regularly between home and work

Etymology

From Latin 'commutare', meaning 'to change altogether'; originally referred to exchange or substitution, and later came to denote regular travel between two fixed points in the 19th century.

3. VERB

/ kəˈmjut / (AmE), / kəˈmjuːt / (BrE)
(of an authority) to change a punishment to a less severe one.

Examples

She had been sentenced to 180 years in prison, but former Gov. Stratton commuted her term to 75 years, making her eligible for parole, as one of his last acts in office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reduce - to make something smaller or less in amount, degree, or size

Etymology

From Latin 'commutare' (to interchange, change altogether).
