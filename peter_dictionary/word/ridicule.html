ridicule

1. VERB

/ ˈrɪdɪˌkjuːl / (AmE), / ˈrɪdɪˌkjuːl / (BrE)
to mock or make fun of someone or something in a way that causes laughter or scorn

Examples

Nothing in English has been ridiculed as much as the ambiguous use of words, unless it be the ambiguous use of sentences.
While it is easy enough to ridicule Hawkins' pronouncement in Pleas of the Crown from a metaphysical point of view, the concept of the "oneness "of a married couple may reflect an abiding belief that the communion between husband and wife is such that their actions are not always to be regarded by the criminal law as if there were no marriage.
Many of them, moreover, are beginning to complain about the scarcity of Western amusements and to ridicule the old life of the bazaar merchant, the mullah, and the peasant.

Synset Examples

The satirists ridiculed the plans for a new opera house
The students poked fun at the inexperienced teacher
His former students roasted the professor at his 60th birthday

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mock - to make fun of someone or something, especially by imitating them
  • Synonym: deride - to express contempt for; to ridicule
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From French *ridiculer*, from Latin *ridiculus* (laughable), from *ridere* (to laugh).

2. NOUN

/ ˈrɪdɪˌkjuːl / (AmE), / ˈrɪdɪkjuːl / (BrE)
The act of making someone or something the subject of mockery or scorn; words or actions intended to make fun of or humiliate.

Examples

He is a target of ridicule to his wife, and often - since private affairs rarely remain private - to the outside world as well.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mockery - the action of mocking or making fun of someone or something
  • Synonym: scorn - open dislike and disrespect or derision often mixed with indignation
  • Antonym: praise - expression of approval or admiration

Etymology

From Latin 'ridiculum' (a joke), from 'ridere' (to laugh).
