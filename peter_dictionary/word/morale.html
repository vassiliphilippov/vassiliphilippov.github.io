morale

1. NOUN

/ məˈræl / (AmE), / məˈrɑːl / (BrE)
the level of confidence, enthusiasm, and discipline of a person or group at a particular time, especially as it affects their willingness to work or succeed

Examples

West Berlin morale is low and, in age distribution, the situation is unfavorable.
Turnover rates of personnel went up, production dropped, and morale was visibly reduced.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spirit - the prevailing mood or attitude within a group
  • Degree Lower: confidence - belief in oneself and one's powers or abilities

Etymology

From French *morale* (moral state), from Latin *moralis* (moral).

2. NOUN

/ məˈræl / (AmE), / məˈrɑːl / (BrE)
The level of confidence, enthusiasm, and discipline of a person or group at a particular time, especially as it affects motivation and performance.

Examples

Have you permitted it to become a giveaway program rather than one that has the goal of improved employee morale and, consequently, increased productivity?
However, among the girls, there are some morale enhancing compensations for not going to college.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spirit - a person's mood or attitude in a group context, especially in adversity
  • Synonym: enthusiasm - eager enjoyment or interest, especially as a motivator
  • Synonym: confidence - belief in oneself and one's powers or abilities

Etymology

From French *moral* (moral), ultimately from Latin *morālis* (moral).
