scrub

1. VERB

/ skrʌb / (AmE), / skrʌb / (BrE)
To clean something by rubbing it hard, often with a brush and water.

Examples

Argiento had been trained so rigorously by the Jesuits that Michelangelo was unable to change his habits: up before dawn to scrub the floors, whether they were dirty or not; water boiling on the fire for washing laundry every day, the pots scoured with river sand after each meal.
Scrub the studio once a week.
He scrubbed absent-mindedly at the pans and reflected on how things had turned out.
Would he have to clean up after her every day, clean the kitchen, the bathroom, and get down on his knees and scrub the kitchen floor, then hang up her dresses, pick up her stockings, make the bed while she lay around?

Synset Examples

She scrubbed his back

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: wipe - to clean or dry a surface by rubbing, usually less vigorously
  • Degree Higher: scour - to clean or brighten a surface by rubbing it hard, often with an abrasive

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *scrubben*, of Scandinavian origin; compare Danish *skrubbe* (to scrub).

2. VERB

/ skrʌb / (AmE), / skrʌb / (BrE)
to clean something thoroughly by rubbing it, usually with a brush, soap, or water

Examples

They love to dust, scrub, polish, wax floors, move the furniture around from place to place, take down the curtains, put up new ones and have themselves a real ball.

Synset Examples

surgeons must scrub prior to an operation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wash - to clean with water or another liquid, usually without intense rubbing
  • Degree Higher: polish - to make something smooth and shiny by rubbing it

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English *scrubben* (to rub hard), of Scandinavian origin; related to Swedish *skrubba*.
