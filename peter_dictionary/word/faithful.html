faithful

1. ADJ

/ ˈfeɪθfəl / (AmE), / ˈfeɪθfəl / (BrE)
remaining loyal and steadfast in affection, support, or allegiance to someone or something.

Examples

Escalation is their first love and their last; they will be faithful unto death.
And Paul Lipson, as Morris, the faithful one who never gets home to his Shirley's dinner, was fine, too.
Expressed differently: if the price for becoming a faithful follower of Jesus Christ is some form of self-destruction, whether of the body or of the mind - sacrificium corporis, sacrificium intellectus - then there is no alternative but that the price remain unpaid.
They have insisted, rather, on living fully and completely within modern culture and, so far from considering this treason to God, have looked upon it as the only way they could be faithful to him.
A useful comment on his relation to his region may be made, I think, by noting briefly how in handling Southern materials and Southern problems he has deviated from the pattern set by other Southern authors while remaining faithful to the essential character of the region.

Synset Examples

years of faithful service
faithful employees
we do not doubt that England has a faithful patriot in the Lord Chancellor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loyal - showing firm support or allegiance
  • Antonym: disloyal - not loyal; unfaithful
  • Synonym: devoted - very loving or loyal

Etymology

From Old French *feitful*, from *feit* (faith), from Latin *fides* (faith, trust).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfeɪθfəl / (AmE), / ˈfeɪθfəl / (BrE)
resembling or reproducing the original very closely; accurate or true to the original.

Examples

And although Schnabel's pianism bristles with excitement, it is meticulously faithful to Schubert's dynamic markings and phrase indications.

Synset Examples

a close translation
a faithful copy of the portrait
a faithful rendering of the observed facts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: accurate - free from errors, exact
  • Synonym: exact - precisely correct or true in every detail
  • Antonym: inaccurate - not correct or faithful to the original

Etymology

From Old French 'feithful', from 'feid' (faith), from Latin 'fides'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfeɪθfəl / (AmE), / ˈfeɪθfəl / (BrE)
(plural noun) People who are loyal and devoted followers, especially of a religion, leader, or cause.

Examples

When Kate hurried in alarm to tell him to put it out, she saw other dots of flames among the western Virginia hills from the few scattered fires of the faithful.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: believer - a person who accepts a particular faith or set of beliefs
  • Synonym: devotee - a person who is very interested in and enthusiastic about someone or something
  • Synonym: follower - a person who supports or admires a leader or cause
  • Antonym: apostate - a person who renounces a religious or political belief or principle

Etymology

From Old French *feithful*, from *feid* (faith).
