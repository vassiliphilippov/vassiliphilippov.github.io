abstract

1. ADJ

/ ˈæbstrækt / (AmE), / ˈæbstrækt / (BrE)
existing in thought or as an idea but not having a physical or concrete existence

Examples

Synset Examples

Related Terms

2. ADJ

/ ˈæbstrækt / (AmE), / ˈæbstrækt / (BrE)
(of art) not representing external reality or objects of nature, often using shapes, colors, and forms for effect.

Examples

We have heard that after seeing Mr. Quasimodo's work it will be virtually impossible to deny the artistic validity and importance of the whole abstract movement.

Synset Examples

a large abstract painting

Related Terms

  • Antonym: figurative - (of art) representing forms that are recognizably derived from real objects
  • Synonym: nonrepresentational - not intended to represent or depict a person, place, or thing in the natural world (in art)

Etymology

From Latin abstractus (drawn away), from abstrahere (to draw away).

3. VERB

/ æbˈstrækt / (AmE), / əbˈstrɑːkt / (BrE)
(formal) To take something away, especially dishonestly or without permission; to steal or pilfer.

Examples

From the curio cabinet on its south wall and the bureaus beneath, you abstracted seventeen ivory, metal, wood, and stone sculptures of Oriental and African origin, two snuffboxes, and a jade handled magnifying glass.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pilfer - to steal things of little value
  • Synonym: steal - to take another person's property without permission

Etymology

From Latin 'abstrahere' (to draw away).

4. NOUN

/ ˈæbˌstrækt / (AmE), / ˈæb.strækt / (BrE)
A general idea, quality, or principle that is not connected with any specific instance, object, or person.

Examples

The submission of detailed plans would place the issues before the court more readily than would discussion of divestiture or disenfranchisement in the abstract.

Synset Examples

he loved her only in the abstract--not in person

Related Terms

  • Antonym: concrete - existing in a material or physical form; not abstract

Etymology

From Latin abstractum (something withdrawn), from abstrahere (to draw away).

5. VERB

/ əbˈstrækt / (AmE), / əbˈstrækt / (BrE)
(formal) To consider something independently of its specific instances, qualities, or details; to generalize from particular examples.

Examples

(We abstract here from technological progress and assume that prices of all other products change proportionately.)

Synset Examples

Let's abstract away from this particular example

Related Terms

  • Synonym: generalize - to make broad statements from specific cases

Etymology

From Latin abstrahere (to draw away), from ab- (away) + trahere (to draw).
