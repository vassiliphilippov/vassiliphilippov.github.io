swing

1. VERB

/ swɪŋ / (AmE), / swɪŋ / (BrE)
to make a wide, sweeping movement, especially with the arms or an object

Examples

"It's kind of like golf - if you do n't swing a club very often, your timing gets off ".
The unfortunate queen mounted the scaffold; the headsman swung his axe; the head dropped off; end of film.
"Well ", he explained, "s'posin you was a nester swingin' the long rope?
Mike swung the pistol in a savage backlash.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brandish - to wave or swing something, especially a weapon, in a threatening or excited way
  • Synonym: flail - to wave or swing something or arms about wildly

Etymology

Old English 'swingan' (to beat, rush, fling oneself) related to Old Norse 'swinga'.

2. VERB

/ swɪŋ / (AmE), / swɪŋ / (BrE)
to move, walk, or cause to move with a swaying, oscillating, or curving motion

Examples

They are definitely in the same room with you, but your head starts to swing as though you were sitting on the very edge of a tennis court watching a spirited volley.
He stared at the far morning, expecting a pendulum to swing across the horizon.
He thought of Simms Purdew, who once had risen at the edge of a cornfield, a maniacal scream on his lips, and swung a clubbed musket like a flail to beat down the swirl of Rebel bayonets about him.
The flat-bottomed boat swung slowly to the pull of the current.
One lithe leg straddled the railing and swung loosely before the creaking, torn pales.

Synset Examples

He swung back

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sway - move or cause to move slowly or rhythmically backward and forward or from side to side

Etymology

Old English *swingan* (to beat, whip, rush), of Germanic origin.

4. NOUN

/ swɪŋ / (AmE), / swɪŋ / (BrE)
A seat, often suspended by ropes or chains, for children to sit on and move back and forth as a form of play.

Examples

It swayed with the wicker swings and screeched with the rusted hinges of screen doors.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seesaw - a long board balanced on a central support, used as a children's playground toy

Etymology

From Old English 'swingan' meaning 'to beat, dangle, or oscillate'.
