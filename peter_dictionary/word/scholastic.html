scholastic

1. ADJ

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlæstɪk / (BrE)
relating to schools, education, or academic matters

Examples

The bill is designed to provide special schooling for more deaf students in the scholastic age at a reduced cost to the state.
Certainly there would be less anxiety, fewer accidents (it is the clumsy child who sustains the worst injuries), and higher scholastic averages, since alert children work better.

Synset Examples

scholastic year

Related Terms

  • Synonym: academic - relating to education and scholarship

Etymology

From Old French scholastique, from Latin scholasticus (of a school), from Greek scholastikos (studious).

2. NOUN

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlæstɪk / (BrE)
A person who is excessively concerned with formal rules and academic learning, often at the expense of practical matters.

Examples

The TEA estimated there would be 182 scholastics to attend the day school in Dallas County, saving them from coming to Austin to live in the state deaf school.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pedant - person overly concerned with formal rules and academic learning

Etymology

From Latin 'scholasticus', from Greek 'skholastikos' (devoting one's leisure to learning).

3. ADJ

/ skəˈlæstɪk / (AmE), / skəˈlæstɪk / (BrE)
of or relating to scholasticism, the system of philosophy and theology taught in medieval European universities based on Aristotelian logic and the writings of the early Church Fathers.

Examples

The actual function of patristic civilian canonist scholastic communism was adequately set forth by St. Gregory almost a millennium before More wrote Utopia.

Synset Examples

scholastic philosophy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scholasticism - medieval system of philosophy and theology based on classical logic and Church Fathers

Etymology

From Late Latin scholasticus (scholar), from Greek scholastikos (devoted to learning).
