redundancy

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌndənsi / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌndənsi / (BrE)
The state or fact of being unnecessary or no longer needed; superfluity or excess.

Examples

While it must be said that these same Protestants have built some new churches during this period, and that religious population shifts have emptied churches, a principal reason for this phenomenon of redundancy is that fewer Protestants are going to church.

Synset Examples

the use of industrial robots created redundancy among workers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: superfluity - an excessive amount or something not needed
  • Antonym: necessity - the state of being needed or essential

Etymology

From Latin redundare, meaning 'to overflow'.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈdʌndənsi / (AmE), / rɪˈdʌndənsi / (BrE)
The inclusion of extra information or repetition in a message or system to reduce the chance of errors in transmission or operation.

Examples

This is done for simplicity of commands and to bring the hidden redundancy up to where misunderstanding has almost zero possibility.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: duplication - the act of making an exact copy or double of something, often for reliability or backup

Etymology

From Latin 'redundantia' (overflow, superabundance), from 'redundare' (to overflow).
