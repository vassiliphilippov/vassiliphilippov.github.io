fond

1. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
Having or displaying affection, tenderness, or warmth towards someone or something.

Examples

Our inability to explain why certain people are fond of us frequently induces the same kind of ritual and malaise.
We are already committed to establishing man's supremacy over nature and everywhere on earth, not merely in the limited social political economical context we are fond of today.
He made a point of frowning, of acting out the part of the fond father confessor.

Synset Examples

affectionate children
a fond embrace
fond of his nephew
a tender glance
a warm embrace

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affectionate - showing fondness or tenderness
  • Synonym: tender - showing gentleness or affection
  • Antonym: indifferent - having no particular interest or sympathy

Etymology

From Middle English fonde (foolish, loving), from fon (to fool, be foolish), of uncertain origin.

2. ADJ

/ fɑnd / (AmE), / fɒnd / (BrE)
Showing excessive or foolish affection or indulgence toward someone or something.

Examples

The audience was fond of Harry Hawk, he was a dear, in or out of character, but he was not particularly funny.

Synset Examples

adoring grandparents
deceiving her preoccupied and doting husband with a young captain
hopelessly spoiled by a fond mother

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adoring - showing deep affection and admiration
  • Synonym: doting - foolishly or excessively fond

Etymology

From Middle English fond (foolish, silly), of uncertain origin; later developed sense of 'affectionate'.
