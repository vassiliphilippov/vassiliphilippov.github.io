anchor

1. NOUN

/ ˈæŋkɚ / (AmE), / ˈaŋkə / (BrE)
a person or thing that provides stability, support, or confidence in an uncertain situation

Examples

As such it acts as an anchor for the people.

Synset Examples

faith is his anchor
the keystone of campaign reform was the ban on soft money
he is the linchpin of this firm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mainstay - a chief support or main source of strength
  • Synonym: keystone - the central, most important part of something
  • Synonym: linchpin - the most important person or thing in a system or organization

Etymology

From Latin 'ancora', from Greek 'ankura' (anchor).

2. VERB

/ ˈæŋkər / (AmE), / ˈaŋkə / (BrE)
to secure a vessel in place by dropping an anchor

Examples

"There's a big boat anchored near the Place de la Concorde, with a swimming pool in it - did n't you notice it?
The morning hawk, hungry for any eatable, killable, digestible item, kept his eyes on the ring of anchored ships that lay off the shores in the bay, sheltered by the Jersey inlets.
Hardly anyone ashore marked her as she anchored stern-to off Berth 29 on the mole.

Synset Examples

We anchored at Baltimore

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moor - to secure a boat by attaching it to a fixed object or the shore

Etymology

From Latin *ancora*, via Old English *ancor* and Old French *ancre* (anchor).

3. NOUN

/ ˈæŋkər / (AmE), / ˈæŋkə / (BrE)
A heavy object attached to a vessel by a cable or chain and dropped into the water to prevent the vessel from moving.

Examples

The British ships rolled at anchor, sent out picket boats and waited for orders from London.
Once, after the Discovery lay for a week in rough weather, Hudson ordered the anchor raised before the sea had calmed.
The anchor cable would have been lost overboard, but Philip Staffe was on hand to sever it with his axe.

Etymology

From Latin 'ancora', from Greek 'ánkȳra' (anchor).

4. VERB

/ ˈæŋkɚ / (AmE), / ˈæŋkə / (BrE)
to fix or secure something firmly in position so that it does not move

Examples

In the vulnerable areas of the Pacific there should be restrictions against building homes on exposed coasts, or at least a requirement that they be either raised off the ground or anchored strongly against waves.
Only the heavy bones of the oxen kept them anchored.
Orthodontic work is possible because teeth are held firmly but not rigidly, by a system of peridontal membrane with an involved nerve network, to the bone in the jaw; they are not anchored directly to the bone.

Synset Examples

anchor the lamppost in concrete

Related Terms

  • Synonym: secure - make something firmly fixed or safe
  • Antonym: detach - remove or unfasten from whatever it is attached to

Etymology

From Latin 'anchora', from Greek 'ankura' (anchor).
