hawk

1. NOUN

/ hɑk / (AmE), / hɔːk / (BrE)
A diurnal bird of prey with short rounded wings and a long tail, known for its keen eyesight and hunting ability.

Examples

On the east side of the island of Manhattan the indifferent hawk knew the East River that connected New York Bay with Long Island Sound.
The morning hawk, hungry for any eatable, killable, digestible item, kept his eyes on the ring of anchored ships that lay off the shores in the bay, sheltered by the Jersey inlets.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eagle - large bird of prey with a massive hooked bill and long broad wings
  • Synonym: falcon - bird of prey with long pointed wings and a notched beak
  • Antonym: dove - gentle bird, typically smaller and seed-eating, symbolic opposite of 'hawk' in politics

Etymology

Old English *hafoc*; related to Dutch *havik* and German *Habicht*.

2. VERB

/ hɑk / (AmE), / hɔːk / (BrE)
to sell or offer goods for sale by calling out in public places, often while traveling from place to place

Examples

Cardboard noisemakers, substitutes for the unavailable tin models, were being hawked and bought at makeshift stands every few yards along Broadway, and one's ears were continually serenaded by the horns' rasps and bleats.
In a raucous take-off on radio commercials, Singer Ray Stevens hawks a cure-all for neuritis, neuralgia, head-cold distress, beriberi, overweight, fungus, mungus and water on the knee.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: peddle - to sell goods by going from place to place

Etymology

Probably from Middle Low German *hoken* (to peddle, squat), related to 'huckster'.
