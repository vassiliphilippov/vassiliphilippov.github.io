riddle

1. NOUN

/ ˈrɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈrɪdəl / (BrE)
a person or thing that is difficult to understand or explain; a mysterious or puzzling problem

Examples

It has come to mean myths, legends, tales, songs, proverbs, riddles, superstitions, rhymes and such literary forms of expression.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: puzzle - something that is difficult to understand or solve
  • Synonym: enigma - a person or thing that is mysterious or difficult to understand
  • Synonym: mystery - something that is difficult or impossible to understand or explain

Etymology

From Old English *rǣdels* (interpretation, riddle) related to *rǣdan* (to advise, interpret).

2. VERB

/ ˈrɪdəl / (AmE), / ˈrɪdəl / (BrE)
to pose a riddle or a difficult problem to someone

Examples

It is worth dwelling in some detail on the crisis of this story, because it brings together a number of characteristic elements and makes of them a curious, riddling compound obscurely but centrally significant for Mann's work.

Synset Examples

riddle me a riddle

Etymology

From Old English *rǣdels* (riddle, counsel, opinion, guess), related to *rǣdan* (to interpret, advise).

3. VERB

/ ˈrɪd.əl / (AmE), / ˈrɪd.əl / (BrE)
to pierce or fill (something) with many holes, especially by shooting or attacking repeatedly

Examples

In time, these minor manifestations will multiply and effloresce, riddling you with frambesia, the king's evil, sheep rot, and clonic spasm, until your very existence becomes a burden and you cry out for release.
The U. N. - chartered plane which was flying from the conference city of Ndola in Northern Rhodesia had been riddled with machinegun bullets last weekend and was newly repaired.
Well, where I get hazy is where the cleaning woman shows up the following morning and finds your wife in bed with her lover, riddled with .38 caliber bullets.

Synset Examples

The bullets riddled his body

Related Terms

  • Synonym: perforate - to pierce and make a hole or holes in something

Etymology

From Middle English *ridlen* (to sift), related to the noun 'riddle' (sieve), originally meaning 'to pass through a sieve', later extended to 'pierce with many holes'.
