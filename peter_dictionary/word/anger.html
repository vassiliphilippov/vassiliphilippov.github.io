anger

1. NOUN

/ ˈæŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈaŋɡə / (BrE)
A strong feeling of displeasure, hostility, or annoyance resulting from a real or perceived grievance or provocation.

Examples

Not terror, but anger and resentment have been the general reaction outside the Soviet sphere.
Something of this can be learned from "The Way to the Churchyard " (1901), an anecdote about an old failure whose fit of anger at a passing cyclist causes him to die of a stroke or seizure.
They marched with bayonets fixed, and as fixed on their faces was anger, fear, and torment.
John stiffened in anger.
Anger and fear fused in John.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: rage - intense, uncontrolled anger
  • Degree Lower: annoyance - slight feeling of irritation or displeasure
  • Antonym: calm - absence of strong emotion or agitation

Etymology

From Old Norse 'angr' meaning 'grief, sorrow'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈæŋɡər / (AmE), / ˈæŋɡə / (BrE)
A strong feeling of displeasure, annoyance, or hostility.

Examples

Clumps of brush that they passed were so many enchained demons straining in anger to tear and gnaw on his bones.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: rage - violent, uncontrollable anger
  • Degree Lower: annoyance - a mild feeling of irritation or displeasure
  • Antonym: calm - the absence of anger; peacefulness

Etymology

From Old Norse 'angr' (grief, sorrow, affliction).

3. VERB

/ ˈæŋɡɚ / (AmE), / ˈæŋɡə / (BrE)
to make (someone) feel angry; to cause anger in someone

Examples

The men were at first puzzled, then angered by the aimless tacking.

Synset Examples

The news angered him

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: enrage - to make extremely angry
  • Degree Lower: annoy - to mildly disturb or irritate someone

Etymology

From Old Norse *angr* (grief, sorrow), from Proto-Germanic *anguz* (narrow, painful).
