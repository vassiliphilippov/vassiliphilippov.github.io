pulse

1. NOUN

/ pʌls / (AmE), / pʌls / (BrE)
(electronics) A sharp, transient wave of electrical current or voltage, often occurring as part of a series or as a signal, characterized by a sudden change from the normal electrical state.

Examples

From the technical standpoint, records differ from live music to the degree that they fail to convey the true color, texture, complexity, range, intensity, pulse, and pitch of the original.

Synset Examples

the pulsations seemed to be coming from a star

2. NOUN

/ pʌls / (AmE), / pʌls / (BrE)
the regular throbbing of the arteries as blood is propelled through them, usually as felt at the wrist or neck; the heartbeat as felt through arterial walls.

Examples

A single measurement of the spin spin relaxation time f was obtained at 10 Mc / sec by pulse** methods.

Synset Examples

he could feel the beat of her heart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: heartbeat - a single complete pulsation of the heart

Etymology

From Latin 'pulsus' (a beating or striking), past participle of 'pellere' (to push, drive).

3. VERB

/ pʌls / (AmE), / pʌls / (BrE)
to produce or modulate (energy, especially electromagnetic or sound waves) in the form of short, regular bursts or pulses

Examples

Various methods of pulsing, scanning, and displaying these sound waves are used to detect submarines, map ocean floors, and even communicate under water.

Synset Examples

pulse waves
a transmitter pulsed by an electronic tube

4. VERB

/ pʌls / (AmE), / pʌls / (BrE)
to expand and contract in a regular, rhythmic way; to beat or throb steadily

Examples

The pulsing glow of a cigarette.

Synset Examples

The baby's heart was pulsating again after the surgeon massaged it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: throb - beat with a strong, regular rhythm
  • Degree Lower: flutter - move or beat rapidly and lightly

Etymology

From Latin 'pulsare' (to beat, strike), frequentative of 'pellere' (to drive, strike).
