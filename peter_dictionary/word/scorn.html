scorn

1. NOUN

/ skɔrn / (AmE), / skɔːn / (BrE)
A strong feeling that someone or something is worthless and deserving only of contempt; intense lack of respect or disdain.

Examples

Rudyard Kipling's scorn for the "jargon "of psychical research was altered somewhat when he wondered "how, or why, had I been shown an unreleased roll of my life film "?
But when he was finally through, their scorn was made apparent.

Synset Examples

he was held in contempt
the despite in which outsiders were held is legendary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: contempt - the feeling that someone or something is beneath consideration, worthless
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Old French 'escarn', of Germanic origin; related to 'scorn' (verb).

2. VERB

/ skɔrn / (AmE), / skɔːn / (BrE)
to treat or regard (someone or something) with contempt or disdain; to show that you think someone or something is not worthy of respect

Examples

It is no coincidence that the hebephrenic patient, the most severely dedifferentiated of all schizophrenic patients, shows, as one of his characteristic symptoms, laughter - laughter which now makes one feel scorned or hated, which now makes one feel like weeping, or which now gives one a glimpse of the bleak and empty expanse of man's despair; and which, more often than all these, conveys a welter of feelings which could in no way be conveyed by any number of words, words which are so unlike this welter in being formed and discrete from one another.

Synset Examples

He despises the people he has to work for
The professor scorns the students who don't catch on immediately

Related Terms

  • Synonym: despise - to feel contempt or a deep repugnance for
  • Synonym: contemn - to treat or regard with contempt
  • Antonym: admire - to regard with respect or warm approval

Etymology

From Old French *escorner* (to mock, to scorn), from Vulgar Latin *excoronare* (to remove one's crown; to treat with contempt).
