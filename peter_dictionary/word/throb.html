throb

1. VERB

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
(of a part of the body or a wound) to beat, pulsate, or pound with a strong, regular rhythm, often causing pain or discomfort.

Examples

At these times he felt a kind of pain in his upper chest, but it was an objective pain, in no way different from others in intensity and not different in kind; it was like the bandaged wound on the back of his head which occasionally throbbed; it was merely another part of his weakness.
The room filled with smoke, and Maggie's head throbbed with excitement and fatigue, but Stuart had such a happy, earnest look of proud possession on his face that Maggie could n't bear to do anything to quench it.

Synset Examples

my head is throbbing
Her heart was throbbing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - to beat, as with regular or rhythmic movement
  • Degree Lower: ache - to experience a continuous dull pain

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, reflecting the sound or feeling of a repeated beat.

2. VERB

/ θrɑb / (AmE), / θrɒb / (BrE)
(of a heart, engine, or similar) to beat or pulsate with a strong, regular rhythm; to vibrate or move with a steady repeated sound or movement.

Examples

And the valley stretched endlessly out ahead, scorched and baked and writhing in its heat, until it vanished into the throbbing wall of fiery orange brown haze.
The engine throbbed into life.

Synset Examples

The baby's heart was pulsating again after the surgeon massaged it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulsate - to expand and contract with a steady, regular rhythm
  • Synonym: beat - to strike or hit repeatedly; to pulsate regularly, as in the heart

Etymology

Of imitative origin, possibly echoing the sound of a beating heart or engine.
