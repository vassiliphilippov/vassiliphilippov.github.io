stake

1. VERB

/ steɪk / (AmE), / steɪk / (BrE)
to risk money or something valuable on the outcome of an uncertain event; to place a bet on

Examples

I'm willing to stake my political career on it ".
"Stake me ", she said, "and let me at those dice.

Synset Examples

Which horse are you backing?
I'm betting on the new horse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bet - to risk money on a game or event
  • Synonym: wager - to risk money or something else on the result of an event

Etymology

From Old English *staca* (post, stake), sense extended to 'that which is risked' in gambling.

2. NOUN

/ steɪk / (AmE), / steɪk / (BrE)
A strong, pointed post that is driven into the ground to mark a position, boundary, or location.

Examples

The chevaux de frise, those sharp stakes and barriers around the fort at the Battery, pointed to a conflict between the town and sea power rolling in glassy swells as the tide came in.

Synset Examples

a pair of posts marked the goal
the corner of the lot was indicated by a stake

Related Terms

  • Synonym: post - an upright timber or metal support

Etymology

From Old English staca (post, stake), of Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ steɪk / (AmE), / steɪk / (BrE)
a right or share, especially a financial or legal involvement, in something such as a business or enterprise

Examples

Ideally speaking, it should be allowed to operate only where the public has a great stake in the continuity of supply or services, and where the actions of a single proprietor are secondary to the needs of society.
He must think of others who have a stake in the question and in its answer.

Synset Examples

they have interests all over the world
a stake in the company's future

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interest - a legal or financial share or involvement in something
  • Degree Higher: ownership - the state of owning something, often as a higher degree of involvement than stake

Etymology

From Old Norse 'stak', meaning 'stake, post'. The sense of 'share in a venture' developed from the earlier meaning of 'a sum deposited as a pledge'.

4. VERB

/ steɪk / (AmE), / steɪk / (BrE)
to mark out or indicate (an area or boundary) by driving stakes into the ground

Examples

This bodes heated contests in several districts where claims have already been staked out by Democratic hopefuls who do n't see eye-to-eye with the CDC.

Synset Examples

stake out the path

Related Terms

  • Synonym: demarcate - to set the boundaries or limits of something
  • Synonym: claim - to assert ownership of a piece of land or territory

Etymology

From Old English *staca* (stake, post), of Germanic origin.

5. VERB

/ steɪk / (AmE), / steɪk / (BrE)
to risk something valuable, such as money, reputation, or a future prospect, on the outcome of an uncertain event

Examples

The "belaboring "is of course jocular, yet James was not lacking in fundamental seriousness - unless we measure him by that ultimate seriousness of the great religious leader or thinker who stakes all on his vision of God.
Public-spirited backers staked him to a brand-new airplane, aimed at putting their city and state on the flying map.

Synset Examples

I will stake my good reputation for this

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wager - to risk money or something valuable on a bet or uncertain outcome
  • Synonym: bet - to risk money or valuables on the result of something uncertain

Etymology

From Middle English stake (wager), from Old English staca (stake, post), figurative sense of 'risk at a wager' developed later.
