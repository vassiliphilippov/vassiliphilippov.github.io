reckon

1. VERB

/ ˈrɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈrɛk.ən / (BrE)
to suppose or think; to judge or consider something to be probable

Examples

Ritter proceeded, "You now emerge into a new epoch in which late in the year 1819 or 1820, you will have to reckon.
"Not a danged thing but rattlesnakes, so I reckon I 'll get the boss rattler to help me ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: suppose - to assume something to be true or probable
  • Synonym: guess - to estimate or form an opinion without certain knowledge

Etymology

From Old English 'gerecenian' (to relate, recount), later influenced by Dutch 'rekenen' (to count) and Middle English 'rekenen'.

To suppose, believe, or expect something to be true.

From Old English *recenian* (to explain, relate), related to Old Norse *rekna* (to count, reckon).

3. VERB

/ ˈrɛkən / (AmE), / ˈrɛkən / (BrE)
to calculate or estimate a figure or amount; to compute mathematically

Examples

While a wind wave never travels at more than 60 miles per hour, the velocity of a tsunami in the open sea must be reckoned in hundreds of miles per hour.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: calculate - to determine a result by mathematical or logical methods
  • Synonym: estimate - to roughly calculate or judge the value or number of something

Etymology

From Old English *gerecenian* (to explain, relate), influenced by Old French *reconter* (to count, tell, relate) and ultimately from Latin *computare* (to calculate).

4. VERB

/ ˈrɛkən / (AmE), / ˈrɛkən / (BrE)
to consider or regard (someone or something) as being a particular kind or having a particular quality

Examples

The separate vocational or technical high schools in the large cities must be reckoned as permanent institutions.

Synset Examples

She views this quite differently from me
I consider her to be shallow
I don't see the situation quite as negatively as you do

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consider - to think about something carefully, especially in making decisions
  • Synonym: deem - to judge, consider, or regard in a specified way

Etymology

From Old English recenian (to explain, relate), later influenced by Middle English rekenen (to count), from Old Dutch rekenen.
