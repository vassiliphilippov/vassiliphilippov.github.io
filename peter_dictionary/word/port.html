port

1. NOUN

/ pɔrt / (AmE), / pɔːt / (BrE)
A small opening in the side of a ship, vehicle, or fortress, especially one through which guns are fired.

Examples

The gilt sterns of the men-of-war becoming clearer to him, the sides of the wooden sea walls alternately painted yellow and black, the bronze cannon at the ports.

Etymology

From Middle French 'porte' (door, gate), from Latin 'porta' (gate).

2. NOUN

/ pɔrt / (AmE), / pɔːt / (BrE)
a town or city with a harbour where ships load or unload, or a place where people and goods enter or leave a country, such as a seaport or airport

Examples

It was responsible and sometimes dangerous work because the thieving is awful in the port of New York.
When she reached port, she was found to have on board only eight men, all near starvation.
Military experts say a tight naval blockade off Cuban ports and at the approaches to Cuban waters would require two naval task forces, each built around an aircraft carrier with a complement of about 100 planes and several destroyers.
They could be sent to U. S. ports for rulings whether cargo should be confiscated.
At times, three ships a day from the Soviet bloc are unloading in Cuban ports.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harbor - a sheltered place along a coast where vessels may find shelter

Etymology

From Latin portus (harbour, haven), via Old French port.

3. NOUN

/ pɔrt / (AmE), / pɔːt / (BrE)
A sweet, dark red fortified wine originally made in Portugal, typically served as a dessert wine.

Examples

A cold supper was ordered and a bottle of port.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sherry - pale or dark fortified wine from Spain, also a dessert wine

Etymology

From Portuguese 'porto', referring to the city of Oporto, where the wine was traditionally exported from.

4. NOUN

/ pɔrt / (AmE), / pɔːt / (BrE)
the left side of a ship or aircraft when facing forward (towards the bow or nose)

Examples

To port was a point 200 feet high rising behind to a precipice of 2000 feet.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: starboard - the right side of a ship or aircraft when facing forward

Etymology

Adopted in the 16th century, possibly from the practice of steering at the right (starboard) which required loading and unloading from the left (port) side.
