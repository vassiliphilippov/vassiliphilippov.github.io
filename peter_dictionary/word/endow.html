endow

1. VERB

/ ɛnˈdaʊ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdaʊ / (BrE)
to provide someone or something with a particular quality, ability, or characteristic

Examples

Linking the smoothly changing images together, the eye itself endows them with the illusion of movement.
They recognized that slavery was a moral issue and not merely an economic interest, and that to recognize it explicitly in their Constitution would be in explosive contradiction to the concept of sovereignty they had set forth in the Declaration of 1776 that "all men are created equal, that they are endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights, that among them are life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bestow - to give (especially a gift, honor, or right)
  • Antonym: deprive - to take something away from someone, denying a quality or possession

Etymology

From Old French 'endouer', from Latin 'dotare' (to provide with a dowry), from 'dos' (dowry).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈdaʊ / (AmE), / ɪnˈdaʊ / (BrE)
to provide a person, institution, or project with a permanent source of income or financial support, especially as a gift or bequest

Examples

Longwood Gardens, near Kennett Square, Pa. (about 12 miles from Wilmington, Del.), was developed and heavily endowed by the late Pierre S. du Pont.

Synset Examples

When she got married, she got dowered

Related Terms

  • Synonym: finance - to provide funding for something

Etymology

From Old French *endouer* (to provide with a dowry), from Latin *dotare* (to endow, provide a dowry), from *dos* (dowry).
