impulse

1. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
A sudden, brief electrical signal or wave, especially one that is transient and sharp, as used in electronics.

Examples

We know that the number of radio and television impulses, sound waves, ultra-violet rays, etc., that may occupy the very same space, each solitary upon its own frequency, is infinite.

Synset Examples

the pulsations seemed to be coming from a star

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pulse - an abrupt surge or series of waves, especially in electronics

Etymology

From Latin impulsus (a push against), from impellere (to push against).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
a sudden electrical signal that travels along a nerve fiber, transmitting information throughout the nervous system

Examples

Hereby, the external object viewed by the eyes remains the thing that is seen, not the retinal image, the purpose of which would be to achieve perceptive cooperation by stirring sympathetic impulses in the other sensory centers, motor tensions, associated word symbols, and consciousness.
In experiments with topical application of strychnine on the cerebral cortex, the transmission of impulses from the cortex to the hypothalamus was demonstrated.

Synset Examples

they demonstrated the transmission of impulses from the cortex to the hypothalamus

Etymology

From Latin impulsus (a pushing against), from impellere (to drive on).

3. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
a sudden force or motion applied for a short time, especially to produce a change in movement; in physics, the product of force and the time over which it acts

Examples

The Russian experimenters claim that only a small fraction of the impulse from the sensors is caused by the incident momentum with the remainder being momentum of ejected material from the sensor.

Synset Examples

the impulse knocked him over

Related Terms

  • Synonym: momentum - the quantity of motion of a moving body

Etymology

From Latin impulsum, past participle of impellere (to push against, to strike).

4. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
a sudden strong urge or desire to act, often without deliberate thought or planning; an instinctive motive.

Examples

Nevertheless, impulses still exist among the ruling elite to rationalize and thus to perpetuate the need for centralized and authoritarian practices.

Synset Examples

profound religious impulses

Related Terms

  • Synonym: urge - a strong desire or impulse
  • Synonym: compulsion - an irresistible urge to act

Etymology

From Latin 'impulsus', past participle of 'impellere' meaning 'to push against'.

5. NOUN

/ ˈɪm.pʌls / (AmE), / ˈɪm.pʌls / (BrE)
a sudden strong urge or desire to act, often without thinking carefully

Examples

They had had to work on very simple foundations and had not dared to give rein to impulses.
In B. M. Spinley's portrayal of the underprivileged and undereducated youth of London, a salient finding was the inability to postpone gratification, a need to satisfy impulses immediately without the pleasure of anticipation or of savoring the experience.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whim - a sudden wish or change of mind, especially one that is unusual or unexplained
  • Synonym: caprice - a sudden and unaccountable change of mood or behavior
  • Synonym: urge - a strong desire or impulse
  • Antonym: deliberation - careful consideration or thought, especially before making a decision

Etymology

From Latin impulsus (a push forward, impulse), from impellere (to push against, drive).
