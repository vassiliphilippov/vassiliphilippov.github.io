baseball

1. NOUN

/ ˈbeɪsˌbɔl / (AmE), / ˈbeɪsbɔːl / (BrE)
a hard ball with a core of cork and rubber, wound in yarn and covered with stitched leather, used in the sport of baseball

Examples

Not satisfied with various unofficial checks on the liveliness of baseballs currently in use, the major leagues have ordered their own tests, which are in progress at Massachusetts Institute of Technology.
The only area in which American boys hold their own is the baseball throw.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: softball - a softer and slightly larger ball used in the game of softball

Etymology

From base + ball; named after the game of baseball.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbeɪsˌbɔl / (AmE), / ˈbeɪsbɔːl / (BrE)
A game played between two teams of nine players who take turns batting and fielding, using a bat and ball; the aim is to score runs by hitting the ball and running around four bases.

Examples

Boston Red Sox Outfielder Jackie Jensen said Monday night he was through playing baseball.
Liston is Bill Liston, baseball writer for the Boston Traveler, who quoted Jensen as saying:
Just when it seems baseball might be losing its grip on the masses up pops heroics to start millions of tongues to wagging.
Both, of course, were remarkable feats and further embossed the fact that baseball rightfully is the national pastime.
It will forever be a baseball mystery how a team will suddenly start hitting after a distressing slump.

Synset Examples

he played baseball in high school
there was a baseball game on every empty lot
there was a desire for National League ball in the area
play ball!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: softball - a similar game with a larger ball and smaller field
  • Synonym: cricket - an English bat-and-ball game with different rules
  • Antonym: football - team sport with a ball, known as soccer in the US

Etymology

From base + ball, modeled after earlier bat-and-ball games; popularized in the United States in the 19th century.
