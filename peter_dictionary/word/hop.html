hop

1. VERB

/ hɑp / (AmE), / hɒp / (BrE)
to jump or move a short distance with light, quick movements, often on one leg or with both feet together

Examples

But it also looked like a toad, hopping away from the light.
The women said they had seen him wave an exhausted farewell; but he might have been shooing away the fleas that hopped from his yellow dog onto him.
So, for that matter, are the newer dances - the "Kalmuk Dance "with its animal movements, that genial juggling act by Sergei Tsvetkov called "The Platter ", the rousing and beautiful betrothal celebration called "Summer ", "The Three Shepherds "of Azerbaijan hopping up on their staffs, and, of course, the trenchant "Rock' n' Roll ".
She hesitated, she hopped, she roll and rocked, skipped and jumped, but in some two weeks she started to pace, From that time to this she has shown steady improvement and now looks like one of the classiest things on the grounds.
They lay, with the birds hopping from branch to branch above them and the bright sky peeping down at them.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: leap - jump or spring a long way, to a great height, or with great force
  • Synonym: skip - move along lightly, stepping from one foot to the other with a hop or bounce

Etymology

Old English hoppian, of Germanic origin, related to Dutch hoppen and German hopsen.

2. VERB

/ hɑp / (AmE), / hɒp / (BrE)
(informal) To travel by quickly getting onto a vehicle, such as a train, bus, or aircraft, especially without prior arrangement or sometimes without paying.

Examples

Ludie hopped rides on freight cars, and was chased by Mr. Yankton, the railroad guard.

Synset Examples

She hopped a train to Chicago
He hopped rides all over the country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: catch - to board a vehicle such as a train or bus

3. NOUN

/ hɑp / (AmE), / hɒp / (BrE)
a quick jump, usually on one foot, or a sudden upward or forward movement

Examples

The new Riverside pitcher turned out to have an overhand fast ball that took a hop.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jump - an act of jumping from one place to another

Etymology

From Middle English hoppen, from Old English hoppian (to leap, jump).

4. VERB

/ hɑp / (AmE), / hɒp / (BrE)
to move quickly, often by making short jumps or leaps from one place to another

Examples

Alacrity, the Podger cat, came by the hammock, rubbed her back briefly against it, and then, sure of a welcome, hopped up.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jump - to push oneself off a surface and into the air by using the muscles in one's legs and feet
  • Synonym: skip - to move with light steps or leaps, often in a playful way
  • Synonym: leap - to jump a long way, to a great height, or with great force

Etymology

Old English *hoppian* (to spring, leap).
