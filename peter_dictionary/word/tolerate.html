tolerate

1. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləˌreɪt / (BrE)
To allow or endure the existence, presence, or behaviour of something or someone unpleasant or undesired without interference.

Examples

The professor in turn dares not tolerate the influence in his classes of an organization in the policies and standards of which he has no voice.
Drunkenness was no longer tolerated.
For three years, since the liquor territorial conference, Torrio had, with his elastic patience, and because he knew that retaliation could cause only violent warfare and disaster to business, tolerated O' Banion's impudent double-crossing.
We simply can n't tolerate further Russian weapons, including the possibility of long-range nuclear missiles, being located in Cuba.
But he did recommend that President Kennedy state clearly that if Communist countries shipped any further arms to Cuba that it would not be tolerated.

Synset Examples

I cannot bear his constant criticism
The new secretary had to endure a lot of unprofessional remarks
he learned to tolerate the heat
She stuck out two years in a miserable marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: endure - to suffer patiently; to tolerate
  • Synonym: bear - to accept or deal with something unpleasant
  • Synonym: accept - to agree to receive or tolerate
  • Antonym: forbid - to not allow or tolerate; to prohibit

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to bear, endure).

2. VERB

/ ˈtɑːləˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈtɒləreɪt / (BrE)
To allow the existence, presence, or practice of something that one does not necessarily like or agree with, without interference or prohibition.

Examples

We will tolerate no guerrillas in the casinos or the swimming pools.

Synset Examples

We don't allow dogs here
Children are not permitted beyond this point
We cannot tolerate smoking in the hospital

Related Terms

  • Synonym: permit - to allow or authorize
  • Antonym: forbid - to not allow; to prohibit

Etymology

From Latin tolerare (to bear, endure).
