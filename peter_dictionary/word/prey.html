prey

1. VERB

/ preɪ / (AmE), / preɪ / (BrE)
to exploit or take advantage of someone, especially in a way that is morally wrong or harmful

Examples

By preying on the sick, by playing callously on the hopes of the desperate, by causing the sufferer to delay proper medical care, these medical ghouls create pain and misery by their very activity.

Synset Examples

He feeds on her insecurity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exploit - to use someone or something unfairly for one's own advantage

Etymology

From Latin praedari (to plunder), from praeda (booty, prey).

2. NOUN

/ preɪ / (AmE), / preɪ / (BrE)
a person who is the victim of an attack, exploitation, or harm, especially by another person or by adverse circumstances

Examples

Even some of the queens will die before the winter is over, falling prey to enemies or disease.
He would have had ample time to go into a blind somewhere and wait his prey.

Synset Examples

he fell prey to muggers
everyone was fair game
the target of a manhunt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: victim - a person harmed, injured, or killed as a result of a crime, accident, or other event or action
  • Antonym: predator - a person or animal who preys on others

Etymology

From Old French 'preie', from Latin 'praeda' (booty, plunder).

3. NOUN

/ preɪ / (AmE), / preɪ / (BrE)
An animal that is hunted and killed by another for food.

Examples

Even Rector himself was prey to this spirit of competition and he knew it, not for a more exalted office in the hierarchy of the church - his ambitions for the bishopry had died very early in his career - but for the one clear victory he had talked about to the colonel.
I always ask that of all my prey.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: predator - an animal that hunts and kills other animals for food

Etymology

From Old French *preie* (booty), from Latin *praeda* (booty, plunder).
