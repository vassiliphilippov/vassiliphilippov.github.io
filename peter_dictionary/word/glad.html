glad

1. ADJ

/ ɡlæd / (AmE), / ɡlæd / (BrE)
feeling or showing pleasure or happiness; delighted about something

Examples

I'm glad to say there's none of that distraction in this "Fiorello! "
My definition of this much abused adjective is that a reconstructed rebel is one who is glad that the North won the War.
"Fanny and Mrs. Godwin will probably be glad to hear that Mary has safely recovered from a very favorable confinement, and that her child is well ".
"How many times have I told you "- he began, and was almost glad when she cut him off - "Too many times "!
Macneff smiled and said, "I am glad that your scriptural lessons have left such an impression ".

Synset Examples

glad you are here
glad that they succeeded
gave a glad shout
a glad smile
heard the glad news
a glad occasion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: happy - feeling or showing pleasure or contentment
  • Antonym: sad - feeling or showing sorrow or unhappiness
  • Degree Higher: delighted - feeling or showing great pleasure
  • Degree Lower: pleased - feeling or showing satisfaction or pleasure

Etymology

From Old English glæd (bright, shining, cheerful), related to Old Norse glædr (bright, glad).

2. ADJ

/ ɡlæd / (AmE), / ɡlæd / (BrE)
Feeling pleased, happy, or appreciative about something; showing or expressing joy or satisfaction.

Examples

He was glad to have it there.
I'’m glad we could spend this time together.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: happy - feeling or showing pleasure or contentment
  • Synonym: pleased - feeling or showing satisfaction or pleasure
  • Antonym: sad - feeling sorrow or unhappiness

Etymology

Old English glæd (bright, cheerful, glad); related to Old Norse glaðr (bright, glad).

3. ADJ

/ ɡlæd / (AmE), / ɡlæd / (BrE)
Willing and eager to do something or to help; ready to be of service.

Examples

It's too bad I did n't call you, and it's too bad I let Schaeffer use Accacia when he could have had a boy who 'd be glad to learn something of Homicide procedure.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: willing - ready or prepared to do something
  • Antonym: reluctant - unwilling and hesitant

Etymology

From Old English glæd (bright, cheerful, kind), related to Old Norse glaðr (bright, glad).
