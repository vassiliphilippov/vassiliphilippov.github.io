scandal

1. NOUN

/ ˈskændl̩ / (AmE), / ˈskændl̩ / (BrE)
Rumors or gossip about the private lives or conduct of people that cause public disgrace or shock.

Examples

He imagined they were the kind whose tax returns were never examined (if they were, they were never penalized), whose children had no unhappy romances, whose names never knew scandal.
But if Joyce got involved in murder or salacious scandal, the role would probably go to the sponsor's wife, Mrs. Oatnut Grits.
She had some amusing scandal about the Farneses in the old days.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gossip - casual conversation or reports about others, often involving details not confirmed as true
  • Synonym: rumor - circulating story or report of uncertain truth
  • Synonym: innuendo - an indirect, often derogatory implication

Etymology

From Old French 'escandle', from Late Latin 'scandalum' (cause of offence, stumbling block), from Greek 'skandalon'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈskændl̩ / (AmE), / ˈskændl̩ / (BrE)
An event or action that causes public outrage or shock due to being morally or legally wrong.

Examples

The previous scandals, gaily diverting as they were, had only served to increase his popularity.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - loss of respect or honor caused by a shameful act
  • Synonym: outrage - an action that causes shock or anger

Etymology

From Latin 'scandalum' (cause of offense, stumbling block), via Old French 'scandale'.
