clutch

1. NOUN

/ klʌtʃ / (AmE), / klʌtʃ / (BrE)
a critical or high-pressure situation in which success or failure is determined, especially in sports or competitions

Examples

Kieffer, the only junior in the group, was commended for his ability to hit in the clutch, as well as his all-round excellent play.

Synset Examples

he is a good man in the clutch

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crisis - a time of intense difficulty or danger
  • Synonym: pinch - a difficult or critical situation

2. VERB

/ klʌtʃ / (AmE), / klʌtʃ / (BrE)
to grasp or hold something tightly, often suddenly or with force

Examples

He was frightened; his fists clutched so tightly that his knuckles hurt.
He stood stiffly erect, clutching the staff, his body half hidden by the limp cloth.
He clutched the staff, and his dark eyes blinked apologetically.
She stood clutching her shawl around her shoulders until he had swung the car onto the road.
You have your paper horn clutched in your big, craggy fist, and for your entrance you have planned a noisy, colorful and exuberant greeting to your friends and tenants.

Synset Examples

The sales clerk quickly seized the money on the counter
She clutched her purse
The mother seized her child by the arm
Birds of prey often seize small mammals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grab - to take hold of something suddenly or roughly
  • Synonym: seize - to take hold of something quickly and firmly
  • Antonym: release - to let go of something held

Etymology

From Middle English 'clucchen', related to Old English 'clyccan' (to clutch or clench).

3. NOUN

/ klʌtʃ / (AmE), / klʌtʃ / (BrE)
the act of grasping or holding something tightly

Examples

Beneath his clutch, a flat strip of muscle surged on the bone.

Synset Examples

he released his clasp on my arm
he has a strong grip for an old man
she kept a firm hold on the railing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grip - the act of grasping something tightly
  • Antonym: release - the act of letting go of something held

Etymology

From Middle English clucchen, of Germanic origin; related to Old English clyccan (to clutch).

4. VERB

/ klʌtʃ / (AmE), / klʌtʃ / (BrE)
to hold something tightly, especially with your hands, often because you are afraid, anxious, or in pain

Examples

The two little bangs meant that he was getting impatient to have a crowd of customers waited on and that if he had to he would jerk open the door and drag out, by the opposite door handle which she would be clutching, whichever-the-hell clerk it was who thought she could waste so much store time on the pot.

Synset Examples

She clutched my arm when she got scared

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grip - hold something very firmly
  • Antonym: release - let go of something held

Etymology

Probably from Old English *clyccan* (to bring together, bend).
