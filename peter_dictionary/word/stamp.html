stamp

1. NOUN

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
a mark or symbol impressed, printed, or engraved, especially as official evidence of approval, identity, or payment

Examples

In any given period piece the costumes, bric-a-brac, vehicles, and decor, bear the stamp of unimpeachable authenticity.

Synset Examples

he put his stamp on the envelope

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seal - an official mark or stamp; a device to make such a mark
  • Synonym: imprint - a mark or impression made by pressure

Etymology

From Middle English *stampen* (to stamp), from Old English *stampian* (to stamp, stamp upon).

2. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
To categorize or regard someone or something in a fixed, often stereotypical way, typically by attaching a label or expectation.

Examples

The dialogue is sharp, witty and candid - typical "do n't eat the daisies "material - which has stamped the author throughout her books and plays, and it was obvious that the Theatre-by-the-Sea audience liked it.

Synset Examples

I was stereotyped as a lazy Southern European

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stereotype - to label or judge according to a fixed idea
  • Synonym: typecast - to assign repeatedly to the same type of role or category

Etymology

From Old English 'stampe' (to strike with the foot), later extended figuratively to mean marking or impressing a characteristic.

3. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
to reveal or mark clearly as having a particular quality, character, or style

Examples

Schnabel's emphasis on structural clarity, his innate rhythmic vibrancy, and impetuous intensity all tend to stamp his reading as a symphonic one.

Synset Examples

His playing stamps him as a Romantic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brand - to mark as having a particular quality or reputation
  • Synonym: label - to assign a category or quality to someone or something

Etymology

From Middle English 'stampen', from Old English 'stempan' (to stamp, tread), ultimately of Germanic origin.

4. NOUN

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
a particular kind, type, or class of person or thing

Examples

This seems like an attitude favoring a sort of totalitarian bureaucracy which, under a President of the same stamp, would try to coerce an uncooperative Congress or Supreme Court.

Synset Examples

more men of his stamp are needed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kind - a group of things or people with similar characteristics
  • Synonym: sort - a category or type
  • Synonym: type - a category or class; a group with common characteristics

Etymology

Metaphorical use from the literal sense of 'stamp' (mark made by a stamp), originating from Middle English, from Old French 'estampe', from 'estamper' (to stamp).

5. NOUN

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
A distinctive form, style, or character in which something is made or exists.

Examples

While Protogeometric vases usually turn up, especially outside Greece proper, together with as many or more examples of local stamp, these "non Greek "patterns had mostly vanished by the later ninth century.

Synset Examples

pottery of this cast was found throughout the region

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cast - the form or style of something
  • Synonym: mold - a distinctive type or style
  • Synonym: pattern - a repeated decorative design or style

Etymology

From Middle English stampe, from Old English stampian (to stamp, strike); extended figurative use for 'distinctive form' attested since the 17th century.

6. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
to bring one's foot down heavily, especially as a way of showing emotion, or to walk with heavy, forceful steps

Examples

"I could scratch her eyes out ", Eileen cried and stamped her foot when I came back from the phone booth.
The battle of the drib-drool continues, but most of New York's knowing sophisticates of Abstract Expressionism are stamping their feet impatiently in expectation of V (for Vindication) Day, September first, when Augustus Quasimodo's first one-man show opens at the Guggenheim.
For the first few months of their marriage she had tried to be nice about Gunny, going out with him to watch this pearl without price stamp imperiously around in her stall.

Synset Examples

The men stomped through the snow in their heavy boots

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stomp - to tread or walk heavily and noisily

Etymology

From Middle English 'stompen' or 'stampen', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'stampen' and German 'stampfen'.

7. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
to affix a postage stamp to (an envelope, package, etc.)

Examples

She signed the letters quickly, stamped them, and placed them on the hall table for Raphael to mail in town.

Synset Examples

Are the letters properly stamped?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unstamp - to remove or not affix a stamp

8. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
To destroy, suppress, or extinguish forcefully, as if by treading heavily.

Examples

The world exploded when Fascism challenged all concepts of peace and liberty, and the outraged, freedom loving peoples of the Capitalist and Socialist worlds combined forces to stamp Fascist tyranny into cringing submission.

Synset Examples

Stamp fascism into submission
stamp out tyranny

Related Terms

  • Synonym: eradicate - to completely remove or eliminate
  • Synonym: quash - to suppress or put an end to
  • Antonym: encourage - to give support or confidence to (opposite sense)

Etymology

From Old English stampian, of Germanic origin.

9. VERB

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
to mark or make an impression in or on something, often using an object, design, or official device

Examples

Second, they believed it important to determine the fate of the captain - a man whose name is permanently stamped on our maps, on American towns and counties, on a great American river, and on half a million square miles of Arctic seas.
They are included in all types of mathematical handbooks and they are stamped on some types of precision measuring instruments.
So you go on and stamp your form, sonny, and stop wasting my time.

Synset Examples

a man whose name is permanently stamped on our maps

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imprint - to mark or impress a pattern, design, or lettering
  • Synonym: engrave - to cut or carve a design or words into a surface

Etymology

From Old English 'stampian', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'stampen' and German 'stampfen', meaning to tread or make an impression.

10. NOUN

/ stæmp / (AmE), / stæmp / (BrE)
a small piece of paper with an adhesive back, stuck to a letter or package to show that postage fees have been paid

Examples

The safe is a repository for three dead flashlight batteries, a hundred and fifty unused left-hand fingerprint cards, a stack of unsold Policemen's Ball tickets from last year, and thirty-seven cents in coins and stamps.
Some fellas collect stamps.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: postage stamp - adhesive label indicating postage paid

Etymology

From Middle English 'stampe' (to strike, impress), related to the older custom of impressing a mark on paper or metal coins.
