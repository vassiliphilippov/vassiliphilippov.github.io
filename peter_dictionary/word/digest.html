digest

1. VERB

/ daɪˈdʒɛst / (AmE), / dɪˈdʒɛst / (BrE)
to think about and absorb information so that you can understand and remember it

Examples

If the investigation committee spent months digesting the thoughtful food served them at the Laboratory School, they left Helva with a morsel as well.

Synset Examples

I cannot digest all this information

Related Terms

  • Synonym: absorb - to take in information and understand it fully
  • Synonym: process - to deal with and think about information to understand it

Etymology

From Latin 'digerere' (to separate, distribute, arrange), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'gerere' (to carry).

2. VERB

/ daɪˈdʒɛst / (AmE), / daɪˈdʒɛst / (BrE)
(of a person or animal) to break down food in the body so that it can be absorbed and used.

Examples

She stopped flying into rages and started digesting her food; she developed a peaches and cream complexion and a sunny disposition, and she asked for nothing more of life than that she be kept dry and comfortable and fed huge amounts of food at stated intervals and be carried to where she could watch activity going on around her.

Synset Examples

I cannot digest milk products

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: absorb - to take in or soak up (used of nutrients after digestion)

Etymology

From Latin 'digestus', past participle of 'digerere' meaning 'to separate, distribute, arrange'.

3. NOUN

/ daɪˈdʒɛst / (AmE), / daɪˈdʒɛst / (BrE)
A periodical publication that presents summaries of news or other material.

Examples

Reader's Digest condensed books.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: summary - a brief statement or account of the main points of something
  • Synonym: abstract - a summary of the contents of a book, article, or speech

Etymology

From Latin 'digestum', meaning 'a collection' or 'summary'.
