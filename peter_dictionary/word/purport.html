purport

1. VERB

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to claim or appear to do or be something, especially falsely or without proof

Examples

From the beginning of commercial recording, new discs purported to be indistinguishable from The Real Thing have regularly been put in circulation.
Many of the suggested systems seem to have only the most tenuous relationship to the language structures that they purport to represent.
Do its spokesmen seem more interested in the amount of money they collect than in the principles they purport to advocate?

Synset Examples

The letter purports to express people's opinion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: claim - to assert that something is the case
  • Synonym: profess - to state something, sometimes falsely or insincerely
  • Synonym: pretend - to claim falsely to have a specific quality or position

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman *purporter*, from Old French *purporter* (to convey, carry).

2. VERB

/ pərˈpɔrt / (AmE), / pəˈpɔːt / (BrE)
to appear or claim to do something, often falsely; to profess or intend

Examples

It purported to be a reasonably serious attempt at a treatment of jazz musicians, their aims, their problems - the tug-of-war between the "pure "and the "commercial "- and seemed a promising vehicle, for the two men shared a common interest in jazz.

Synset Examples

I aim to arrive at noon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: profess - to claim, often falsely, to have a quality or feeling
  • Synonym: pretend - to claim or appear to do something falsely
  • Synonym: mean - to have as an intention; to intend

Etymology

From Old French *purporter* 'to convey, to carry,' from Latin *portare* 'to carry'.
