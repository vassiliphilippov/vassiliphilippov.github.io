oath

1. NOUN

/ oʊθ / (AmE), / əʊθ / (BrE)
A profane or obscene word or phrase used to express strong emotion such as anger or surprise; a curse or expletive.

Examples

He had had the need to understand what life lurked behind the mask of flesh, behind the oath, the banter, the sadness.
What the hell did you do to set him off, anyway?

Synset Examples

expletives were deleted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - an offensive word or phrase expressing anger or annoyance
  • Synonym: expletive - a word or phrase, especially an obscene one, used to express emotion
  • Synonym: swear word - an offensive or obscene word

Etymology

From Old English āþ, of Germanic origin, related to Old Norse eiðr, Gothic aiþs; more commonly meaning a solemn promise, but also used for profane expressions since the 15th century.

2. NOUN

/ oʊθ / (AmE), / əʊθ / (BrE)
a formal promise or declaration, especially to tell the truth in a court of law or to fulfill a particular duty or commitment

Examples

The Federals is making everybody take the oath of loyalty around these parts too ", he crowed.
After he had gone, Kate asked Uncle Randolph proudly, "Would you take their oath "?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affirmation - a formal declaration, often as an alternative to an oath, especially in legal contexts
  • Antonym: perjury - the criminal offense of lying under oath

Etymology

From Old English āth, related to Old High German eid, meaning 'oath'.

3. NOUN

/ oʊθ / (AmE), / əʊθ / (BrE)
A solemn promise, often invoking a divine witness, about one's future action or behavior.

Examples

I ask you again, sir, here and now under oath.

Synset Examples

they took an oath of allegiance

Related Terms

  • Antonym: affirmation - a formal declaration without a religious element

Etymology

From Old English āth, related to Old Norse eiðr and German Eid.
