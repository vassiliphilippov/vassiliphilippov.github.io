breed

1. VERB

/ brid / (AmE), / briːd / (BrE)
to cause something to happen, develop, or exist; to give rise to or engender

Examples

Show her the new swimming pool and she would say, slapping her ankle, "I suppose this must be where you breed your gigantic mosquitoes ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: engender - to cause or give rise to (a feeling, situation, or condition)
  • Synonym: foster - to encourage or promote the development of something
  • Antonym: prevent - to stop something from happening

Etymology

From Old English *bredan*, related to 'brood'.

2. NOUN

/ brid / (AmE), / briːd / (BrE)
A group of animals within a species having distinctive characteristics, often developed and maintained by humans through selective breeding.

Examples

Cows receiving drug may not be officially tested under breed registry testing programs.

Synset Examples

he experimented on a particular breed of white rats
he created a new strain of sheep

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: species - the basic category for classifying living things, broader than 'breed'
  • Degree Lower: strain - a genetic variant or subgroup within a breed, often used for cultivated plants or animals

Etymology

Old English *brēdan* (to breed, produce), related to *brood*.

3. VERB

/ brid / (AmE), / briːd / (BrE)
(of animals, especially horses) to copulate, usually with the intention of producing offspring.

Examples

In addition to urging greater restrictions on aerial spraying, Buchheister called for support of the Wilderness bill, creation of national seashore parks, including Point Reyes; preservation of the wetlands where birds breed; a pesticides co-ordination act; stronger water pollution control programs, and Federal ratification of an international convention to halt pollution of the sea by oil.

Synset Examples

The horse covers the mare

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mate - (of animals) to come together for breeding; to copulate
  • Synonym: reproduce - to produce offspring; to procreate

Etymology

Old English *brēdan* (to breed, nourish), from *brōd* (brood, offspring).
