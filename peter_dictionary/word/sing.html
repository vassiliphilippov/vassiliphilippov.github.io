sing

1. VERB

/ sɪŋ / (AmE), / sɪŋ / (BrE)
to make musical sounds with the voice, especially words with a set tune

Examples

No casts are listed, but Lotte Lehmann sent word that the Negro soprano, Grace Bumbry, will sing Venus in "Tannhaeuser ".
In the fullness of her vocal splendor, however, she could sing the famous scene magnificently.
Richard Tucker sang Edgardo in glorious voice.
Miss Sutherland first sang Lucia at Covent Garden in 1959.
She will sing "La Sonambula "with it here next week.

Synset Examples

Sing Christmas carols

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chant - to sing or recite with a repetitive, rhythmic pattern
  • Degree Lower: hum - to make a wordless tune with closed lips

Etymology

Old English 'singan', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'zingen' and German 'singen'.

2. VERB

/ sɪŋ / (AmE), / sɪŋ / (BrE)
to produce musical tones with the voice, especially by using the vocal cords in a controlled way to create a melody or song

Examples

Her entrance in Scene 2, Act 1, brought some disconcerting applause even before she had sung a note.
Charlotte Fairchild was excellent as the loyal Marie, who became the second Mrs. LaGuardia, singing and acting with remarkable conviction.
And little Zeme North, a Dora with real spirit and verve, was fascinating whether she was singing of her love for Floyd, the cop who becomes sewer commissioner and then is promoted into garbage, or just dancing to display her exuberant feelings.
On the basis of what they give us we can know how the young Caruso sang, appreciate the distinctive qualities of Parsifal under Karl Muck's baton, or sense the type of ensemble Toscanini created in his years with the New York Philharmonic.
Thus, on the highroad, a troop of soldiers "marched in their own dust and sang ", while on the footpath one man walks alone.

Synset Examples

She was singing while she was cooking
My brother sings very well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chant - to repeat a phrase or melody in a rhythmic singsong manner
  • Degree Lower: hum - to make a sound with closed lips, often tunefully
  • Synonym: croon - to sing or hum in a soft, low voice
  • Antonym: shout - to say something very loudly; opposite to singing melodically

Etymology

Old English *singan*, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch *zingen*, German *singen*.

3. VERB

/ sɪŋ / (AmE), / sɪŋ / (BrE)
(informal) To divulge confidential information or secrets, especially under pressure.

Examples

And I would further note that they all - with one exception again - sang in one key or another the same song.

Synset Examples

Be careful--his secretary talks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spill the beans - to reveal secret information unintentionally or indiscreetly
  • Synonym: snitch - to inform on someone; to reveal secret or incriminating information about someone
  • Antonym: clam up - to refuse to speak or divulge information

4. VERB

/ sɪŋ / (AmE), / sɪŋ / (BrE)
to produce musical tones with the voice, especially in a tuneful or melodious way

Examples

I have no idea to this day what those two Italian ladies were singing about.

Synset Examples

The nightingale was singing

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chant - to sing or say something repeatedly using a special tone of voice
  • Synonym: croon - to sing softly and smoothly

Etymology

Old English *singan*; related to German *singen* and Dutch *zingen*.
