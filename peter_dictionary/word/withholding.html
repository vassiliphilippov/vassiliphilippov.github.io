withholding

1. NOUN

/ wɪðˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of holding something back or keeping it in one's possession or control

Examples

While there are now allegations of the withholding of "favorable evidence developed at the hearing "and a denial of a "full and fair hearing ", no such claim was made by petitioner at any stage of the administrative process.

Synset Examples

I resented his withholding permission
there were allegations of the withholding of evidence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: retention - the act of keeping something rather than losing it
  • Antonym: release - the act of allowing something to go or set free

Etymology

From the verb 'withhold' (from Old English with-, against + healdan, to hold).

2. NOUN

/ wɪθˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪθˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
A tax deducted from an employee's wages and paid directly to the government by the employer.

Examples

Generally, the refund may be obtained by filing Form 1040 A accompanied by the withholding statement (Form W-2).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: withholding tax - income tax withheld from wages and paid by the employer

Etymology

From the verb 'withhold,' meaning 'to hold back,' + '-ing.'

3. NOUN

/ wɪðˈhoʊldɪŋ / (AmE), / wɪðˈhəʊldɪŋ / (BrE)
the act of deducting tax or other amounts from an employee's wages or salary before payment

Examples

Nonresident aliens living in Canada or Mexico who earn wages in the United States may be subject to withholding of tax on their wages, the same as if they were citizens of the United States.
However, if their United States income is not subject to the withholding of tax on wages, their returns are due June 15, 1962, if they use a calendar year, or the 15 th day of the 6 th month after the close of their fiscal year.
If he had income other than wages subject to withholding, he may be required to file Form 1040.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deduction - an amount that is or may be subtracted from something, especially from taxable income or tax to be paid
  • Antonym: reimbursement - repayment for money that has already been spent or lost

Etymology

From the verb 'withhold' + -ing; 'withhold' derives from Old English 'wiðholdan', meaning to keep back.
