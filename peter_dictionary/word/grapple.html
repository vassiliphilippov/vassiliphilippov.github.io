grapple

1. VERB

/ ˈɡræpəl / (AmE), / ˈɡrapəl / (BrE)
To seize or hold someone firmly, especially in a struggle or wrestling match.

Examples

The first series of Sherlock Holmes adventures ends with Holmes and Moriarty grappling together on the edge of a cliff.

Synset Examples

the two men grappled with each other for several minutes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wrestle - to struggle hand-to-hand, as in wrestling
  • Antonym: release - to let go of or free something held or seized

Etymology

From Middle English *grapellen*, from Old French *grappiller* (to seize), from *grappe* (hook).

2. VERB

/ ˈɡræpəl / (AmE), / ˈɡræpəl / (BrE)
to try hard to deal with or overcome a problem or difficulty

Examples

He considered them with brooding eyes, brows bunched as his brain grappled with the problem and tried to find some solution.
Yet he presents a realm of source material which may well serve other writers if not himself: the problems with which a New South must grapple in groping through a blind adolescence into the maturity of urbanization.
Having persisted too long in deliberate ignorance and denial of the forces that threatened her, Pamela was relieved now to admit their potency and to be taking definite steps toward grappling with them.

Synset Examples

We got by on just a gallon of gas
They made do on half a loaf of bread every day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cope - to deal effectively with something difficult
  • Synonym: confront - to face up to and deal with a problem or challenge
  • Antonym: evade - to avoid dealing with or facing something

Etymology

From Middle English *grapil*, from Old French *grappil* (a hook), diminutive of *grape* (hook, forked instrument); figurative sense developed later.
