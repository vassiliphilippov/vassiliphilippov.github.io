sour

1. ADJ

/ ˈsaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊə / (BrE)
(of a smell) having a sharp, acidic, or unpleasant odor like that of something fermented or stale.

Examples

Yet if he were not there, they would have missed him, as they would have missed the sounds of bees buzzing against the screen door in early June; or the smell of thick tomato paste - the ripe smell that was both sweet and sour - rising up from aluminum trays wrapped in fly dotted cheesecloth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rancid - having the unpleasant smell or taste of decomposing oils or fats

Etymology

Old English sūr, from Proto-Germanic *sūraz.

2. VERB

/ ˈsaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊə / (BrE)
(of milk, cream, or other foodstuff) to become sour or spoiled; to go bad so that it tastes or smells unpleasant.

Examples

Such ambiguous exercises compound confusion by making it worse compounded, and they are sometimes expanded until the cream of the jest sours.
I eat too much eggs and poark it sowered [ on ] my stomack and turn loose on me ".

Synset Examples

The milk has soured
The wine worked
The cream has turned--we have to throw it out

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spoil - (of food) to become unfit for eating
  • Degree Higher: ferment - (of food or drink) to undergo fermentation

Etymology

From Old English sūr (sour), related to Old Norse sūrr and German sauer.

3. VERB

/ ˈsaʊɚ / (AmE), / ˈsaʊə / (BrE)
to make something taste sour or become more sour

Examples

It was spoiled now for seed, and it would sour and mold in three days if they failed to find a place and fuel to dry it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acidify - to make or become acidic
  • Antonym: sweeten - to make or become sweet

Etymology

From Old English *sūr* (sour, acidic), related to German *sauer*.
