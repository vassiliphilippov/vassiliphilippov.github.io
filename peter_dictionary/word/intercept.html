intercept

1. VERB

/ ɪn.tərˈsɛpt / (AmE), / ɪn.təˈsɛpt / (BrE)
to stop, catch, or seize something or someone before they reach their destination

Examples

The Air Force's, and the game's, final play, was a long pass by quarterback Bob McNaughton which Gannon intercepted on his own 44 and returned 22 yards.
Someone on his staff - he suspected it was Ed Thornburg - intercepted them and for this Andy was grateful.
Finally, avoiding hummocks and seeking low ground, they intercepted the rain squall.
Shayne strolled across to intercept the reporter in front of the two-story house where Felice Perrin lived, and asked casually, "Get the police okay "?
First I 've got to intercept my brothers before they get any deeper into trouble.

Synset Examples

The fighter plane was ordered to intercept an aircraft that had entered the country's airspace

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to stop movement by placing something in the way
  • Synonym: catch - to seize or capture something that is moving
  • Antonym: allow - to permit something to proceed without prevention

Etymology

From Latin 'intercipere', from 'inter-' (between) + 'capere' (to seize).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɪntərˌsɛpt / (AmE), / ˈɪntəsɛpt / (BrE)
the point at which a straight line or curve crosses a coordinate axis, especially in a graph or equation.

Examples

Hence, the electron paramagnetic effects (slope) can be separated from the nuclear effects (intercept).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intersection - the point or set of points where two lines, curves, or surfaces meet

Etymology

From Latin intercipere (to seize, cut off), but technical sense arose in mathematical terminology in the 19th century.
