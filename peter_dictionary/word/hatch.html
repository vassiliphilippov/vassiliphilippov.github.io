hatch

1. VERB

/ hætʃ / (AmE), / hætʃ / (BrE)
(of a young animal) to emerge from an egg.

Examples

When the larvae hatch, they feed on the beebread, although they also receive extra honey meals from their mother.
Also, aids in the control of horn flies by preventing them from hatching in the droppings.

Synset Examples

young birds, fish, and reptiles hatch

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emerge - to come out or appear from somewhere
  • Antonym: incubate - to keep eggs at the right temperature for hatching

Etymology

Probably from Middle English hacchen, of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ hætʃ / (AmE), / hætʃ / (BrE)
to devise or invent (a plan, scheme, or plot), typically in secret

Examples

In Missouri (which we are including in our general Midwest region) you can glance into Mark Twain's birthplace at Hannibal, see the landmarks of his life and writings and visualize where Huck Finn hatched his boyish mischief.

Synset Examples

He thought up a plan to get rich quickly
no-one had ever thought of such a clever piece of software

Related Terms

  • Synonym: concoct - to create or devise (a story or plan)
  • Synonym: devise - to plan or invent by careful thought
  • Synonym: plot - to plan secretly, especially something illegal or harmful

Etymology

From Old English *hæccan* (to produce young from eggs), later extended figuratively to plans and schemes.
