boundary

1. NOUN

/ ˈbaʊndɛri / (AmE), / ˈbaʊndəri / (BrE)
A line or imaginary limit that marks the edge or limit of an area, subject, or activity.

Examples

State Department officials refusing to show their passes at the boundary, and driving two blocks into East Berlin under military escort, will not avail.
The freeway with narrowly spaced interchanges concentrates and mitigates the access problem, but it also acts inevitably as an artificial, isolating boundary.
City planners do not always use this boundary as effectively as they might.
Andrei stopped at Litowski Place and looked around quickly at the boundary of civil buildings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: border - the edge or dividing line between two places, often countries or regions
  • Synonym: limit - a point or line beyond which something does not or may not extend
  • Synonym: frontier - a border between two countries, or the edge of settled or known territory

Etymology

From Middle English 'boundarie', from 'bound' + '-ary', influenced by Anglo-Latin 'boundarium' (boundary line).

2. NOUN

/ ˈbaʊnd.ɚ.i / (AmE), / ˈbaʊn.d(ə)r.i / (BrE)
A line or imaginary division marking the limits or edges of an area, such as land, territory, or a field.

Examples

After this holocaust, a changing world occupied the minds of men; a world beset with new boundaries, new treaties and governments, new goals and methods, and the age-old fears of aggression and subjugation - hunger and exposure.
They never troubled themselves about us while we were playing, because the fence formed such a definite boundary and "Do n't go outside the gate "was a command so impossible of misinterpretation.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: border - the edge or boundary of something, especially a country or area
  • Synonym: limit - a point or level beyond which something does not or may not extend or pass
  • Synonym: frontier - a line or border separating two countries

Etymology

From bound (limit) + -ary (denoting relating to), dating to late 16th century.
