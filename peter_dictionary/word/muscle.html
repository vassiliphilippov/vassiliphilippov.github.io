muscle

1. NOUN

/ ˈmʌsəl / (AmE), / ˈmʌsl̩ / (BrE)
A contractile tissue in the body that produces movement by contracting and relaxing, enabling bodily movements and maintaining posture.

Etymology

From Latin 'musculus' (little mouse, muscle), diminutive of 'mus' (mouse), due to the resemblance of some muscles to mice moving under the skin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈmʌsəl / (AmE), / ˈmʌsəl / (BrE)
Animal tissue composed mainly of cells that can contract, producing movement in the body.

Examples

Because the body contains so much liquid, transmission of ultrasonic signals proceeds fairly well in muscles and blood vessels.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tendon - A flexible cord of strong tissue attaching muscle to bone

Etymology

From Latin musculus, meaning 'little mouse', referring to the appearance of some muscles.

3. NOUN

/ ˈmʌsəl / (AmE), / ˈmʌsl̩ / (BrE)
Power or influence, especially that which is used to coerce or control others; force.

Examples

Let'’s get 'em all now, while we got the muscle.

Synset Examples

the senators used their muscle to get the party leader to resign

Related Terms

  • Synonym: might - great power or strength (especially physical)
  • Synonym: clout - influence or power, especially in politics or business

Etymology

From Latin musculus (a little mouse, muscle), originally referring to the body tissue and later used figuratively for strength or power.
