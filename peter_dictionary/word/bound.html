bound

1. NOUN

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
a real or imaginary line that marks the limits or edges of something; a boundary

Examples

But He set a bound to his [ state of ] sin, by interposing death, and thus causing sin to cease, putting an end to it by the dissolution of the flesh, which should take place in the earth, so that man, ceasing at length to live in sin, and dying to it, might live to God ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boundary - a dividing line or limit between areas

Etymology

From Old French *bonde* (limit), from Medieval Latin *bonda* (limit), of Germanic origin.

2. ADJ

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
Confined or restrained by physical bonds, such as ropes or chains.

Examples

As the robbers leave the looted train, the film suddenly cuts back to the station, where the telegrapher's little daughter arrives with her father's dinner pail only to find him bound on the floor.
We seem to be bound by love; Sigmen himself must have foreseen it.
Some psychologists, in fact, suggest that career bound husbands often are more to blame for topsy-turvy marriages than their wives.
Once, then - for how many years or how few does not matter - my world was bound round by fences, when I was too small to reach the apple tree bough, to twist my knee over it and pull myself up.

Synset Examples

bound and gagged hostages

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tied - fastened or held with a rope, string, or cord
  • Antonym: unbound - not tied up or confined by bonds

Etymology

Past participle of Middle English 'binden' (to bind), from Old English 'bindan'.

3. ADJ

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
(of an element or substance) held in chemical or physical union with another element, substance, or material.

Examples

However, the conversion from mass to size is unreliable, since many photographic meteors give evidence of a fluffy, loosely bound meteorite structure with densities as low as * * f.
The protein bound iodine was 6.6 | mg. %, and the radioactive iodine uptake over the thyroid gland was 46 % in 24 hours, with a conversion ratio of 12 %.
Part of the time saved was spent on a preliminary estimate for a long-distance plan to free bound oxygen in the sands of Mars to make the planet more friendly to future human generations.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: free - (of an element or substance) not held in chemical or physical union; uncombined

4. ADJ

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
(of a book or object) secured with a cover or binding. Often used as a combining form (e.g. 'leather-bound').

Examples

He pulled with all his strength at the heavy, brass bound door, and shuffled along the wainscoted wall.
These narratives of coarse action and crude language appeared first in local newspapers, as a rule, and later found their way between book covers, though rarely into the planters' libraries beside the morocco bound volumes of Horace, Mr. Addison, Mr. Pope, and Sir Walter Scott.

Synset Examples

bound volumes
leather-bound volumes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unbound - not secured with a cover or binding (of books or papers)

Etymology

Past participle of 'bind', from Old English 'bindan'.

5. VERB

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
(of a place or area) to form the boundary or edge of; to border or enclose.

Examples

Husbandry was bounded by snake-rail fences, and there were grazing cattle.
The tract is bounded by Island ave., Dicks ave., 61 st st., and Eastwick ave..

Related Terms

  • Synonym: border - to be adjacent to or form an edge of something
  • Synonym: enclose - to surround and close off on all sides

Etymology

From Middle English 'bounden', past participle of 'binden' (to bind), influenced by Middle French 'bonde' (boundary).

6. VERB

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
to spring back or spring away from an impact; to move with a sudden leap or jump, often energetically

Examples

As Rector was walking back toward the residential hall, Johnson came out of the basement and bounded up to him.

Synset Examples

These particles do not resile but they unite after they collide

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leap - to jump or spring a long way, to a great height, or with great force
  • Synonym: jump - to push oneself off a surface and into the air by using the muscles in one's legs and feet

7. VERB

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
to move forward by leaping or jumping energetically

Examples

He came bounding up the stairs and joined the dance.
Having volunteered that he was a man of about sixty, he bounded up the stairs and with each leap rendered the number less credible.
Chandler, looking to right and left to see how his men were faring, suddenly saw another figure bounding up the hill, hurling grenades and hollering the battle cry as he ran.

Synset Examples

The horse bounded across the meadow
The child leapt across the puddle
Can you jump over the fence?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leap - to jump a long way, high, or with great force
  • Synonym: jump - to push oneself off a surface and into the air by using the muscles in one's legs and feet

Etymology

From Middle English 'bounden', from Old French 'bondir' (to leap), from Latin 'bondire', of imitative origin.

8. ADJ

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
certain or destined to do or experience something; (usually followed by 'to') sure or obliged by circumstances or fate.

Examples

The evidence suggests that foreign peoples believe the United States is weaker than the Soviet Union, and is bound to fall still further behind in the years ahead.
A smart, shrewd and ambitious young man, well connected, and with a knack for getting in the good graces of important people, he was bound to go far.

Synset Examples

an old house destined to be demolished
he is destined to be famous

Related Terms

  • Synonym: destined - certain to be or do something in the future
  • Degree Lower: doomed - likely to have an unfortunate outcome

9. ADJ

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
Intending or heading to go in a particular direction or to a specified destination; often used as a combining form (e.g., 'college-bound')

Examples

Today many college bound students try to take a course in personal typing, as they feel a certain degree of mastery of this skill is almost essential for one who proposes to do academic work in college and a professional school.

Synset Examples

children bound for school
a flight destined for New York

Related Terms

  • Synonym: destined - certain to go or be in a particular place or situation

Etymology

Past participle of 'bind,' meaning to tie or direct, with sense of 'destined for' dating to the late 16th century.

10. VERB

/ baʊnd / (AmE), / baʊnd / (BrE)
to place limits or restrictions on something; to confine within a particular range or set of conditions

Examples

Even if we were not bound by Nugent, petitioner here would not be entitled to the report.
Yet they were not so bound by past experience and constriction as to deny their immediate perceptions and to be dominated by their knowledge of what the experience should be.

Synset Examples

restrict the use of this parking lot
limit the time you can spend with your friends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: restrict - to limit the range or extent of something
  • Synonym: limit - to set or serve as a limit to

Etymology

From Middle English bounden, from Old French bondir (to leap, originally 'to bind'), from Latin *bundāre*, from *bunda* (“a bound, a limit”).
