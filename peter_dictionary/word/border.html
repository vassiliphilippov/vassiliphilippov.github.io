border

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdər / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
The official line that separates two countries, regions, or areas; a boundary line.

Examples

Tanks lined up at the border will be no more helpful.
Annisberg was about seventy-five miles west of Birmingham, near the Georgia border and on the Tallahoosa River, a small and dirty stream.
So, for all practical purposes, it was an independent nation and by now had its own organized government (unrecognized outside its own borders).
Boun My - the name means one who has a boun, a celebration, and is therefore lucky - was born in Savannakhet, the Border of Paradise.
In a book review of "The Soviet Cultural Offensive ", he says, "Long before the State Department organized its bureaucracy into an East-West Contacts Staff in order to wage a cultural counter-offensive within Soviet borders, the sharp cutting-edge of American culture had carved its mark across the Russian steppes, as when the enterprising promoters of' Porgy and Bess' overrode the State Department to carry the contemporary' cultural warfare' behind the enemy lines.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boundary - a real or imaginary line that marks the edge or limit of something
  • Synonym: frontier - the border or edge of a country or settled region

Etymology

From Old French 'bordure' (edge, border), from 'border' (to border), from Frankish or Latin origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdɚ / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
a strip or boundary around the edge of something, especially as a decorative or defining feature

Examples

That cold frame was my morale builder; its mass of bright bloom set in a border of snow made my spirits rise every time I looked at it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: edge - the outside limit of an object, area, or surface
  • Synonym: margin - the border or edge of something, especially a page, area, or surface

Etymology

From Old French 'bordure' (seam, edge), from 'border' (to border), ultimately from Old Frankish 'bord' (board, plank, side).

3. NOUN

/ ˈbɔrdər / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
A line separating two countries, regions, or areas; the boundary or the area immediately inside the boundary.

Examples

My intention, therefore, is not to say that Faulkner's awareness has been confined within the borders of the South, but rather that he has looked at his world as a Southerner and that presumably his outlook is Southern.
I hope I can make it across the border.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boundary - a line marking the limits of an area; a dividing line
  • Synonym: frontier - a border separating two countries or regions
  • Antonym: center - the middle point or part of something, opposite of border

Etymology

From Old French *bordure* (seam, edge, border), from *border* (to border), of Germanic origin.

4. VERB

/ ˈbɔrdər / (AmE), / ˈbɔːdə / (BrE)
to enclose, edge, or surround with a boundary; to form a border around something as if framing it

Examples

She looked out at the corn field, the great green deep acres of it rolled out like the sea in the field beyond the whitewashed fence bordering the grounds.

Synset Examples

frame a picture

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frame - to enclose in a frame
  • Synonym: surround - to be all around (something or someone)
  • Antonym: borderless - without a border

Etymology

From Old French 'bordure' (edge, border), from 'border' (to border), from 'bord' (edge).
