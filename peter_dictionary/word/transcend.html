transcend

1. VERB

/ trænˈsɛnd / (AmE), / trænˈsɛnd / (BrE)
to go beyond or surpass the limits of something; to be superior to or excel above a standard

Examples

A broader concept of imitation is needed, one which acknowledges that true invention is important, that the artist's creativity in part transcends the non artistic causal factors out of which it arises.

Synset Examples

She exceeded our expectations
She topped her performance of last year

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exceed - to be greater than a specified quantity or standard
  • Synonym: surpass - to do or be better than; to excel
  • Antonym: fall short - to fail to reach a required or expected level or standard

Etymology

From Latin 'transcendere' (to climb over, surpass), from 'trans-' (across) + 'scandere' (to climb).

2. VERB

/ trænˈsɛnd / (AmE), / trænˈsɛnd / (BrE)
to go beyond the limits of something; to be or become greater, better, or more significant than something

Examples

First, and most obvious, was the growing nationalism and the tendency to regard the state, and the individual's identification with the state, as transcending other ties of social solidarity.
Although he pointed out that mandatory legislation impinging on home rule is basically distasteful, he added that the vital interest in election results transcended county lines.
The creative urge, for example, transcends the body and the self.

Synset Examples

Their loyalty exceeds their national bonds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exceed - to be greater than a particular number or amount; to surpass
  • Synonym: surpass - to do or be better than someone or something
  • Antonym: fall short - to fail to reach a desired level or standard

Etymology

From Latin transcendere, from trans- 'across' + scandere 'to climb'.
