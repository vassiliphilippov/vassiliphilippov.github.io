subordinate

1. ADJ

/ səˈbɔrdənət / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
(of a person or thing) lower in rank or importance; having less power or authority than someone else.

Examples

Furthermore, religion tends to integrate the whole range of values from the highest or ultimate values of God to the intermediary and subordinate values; for example, those regarding material objects and pragmatic ends.
From the enlisted men he pistol-whipped to the subordinate officer whose wife he tried to rape, a lot of men had plenty of reason heartily to dislike Marcus Reno.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superior - higher in rank or importance

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinatus, past participle of subordinare (‘to place in a lower order’).

2. NOUN

/ səˈbɔːrdɪnət / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪnət / (BrE)
a person who has a lower rank or position and is subject to the authority or control of another; an assistant or employee under someone's supervision

Examples

The assumptions upon which the example shown in Figure 3 is based are: (a) One man can direct about six subordinates if the subordinates are chosen carefully so that they do not need too much personal coaching, indoctrinating, etc..

Related Terms

  • Antonym: superior - person with a higher rank or position
  • Synonym: assistant - a person who helps or supports someone, especially in a job

Etymology

From Medieval Latin subordinator, from Latin sub- (under) + ordinare (to arrange).

3. VERB

/ səˈbɔrdɪˌneɪt / (AmE), / səˈbɔːdɪneɪt / (BrE)
to treat or consider as less important or of lower rank

Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: promote - to raise to a more important position
