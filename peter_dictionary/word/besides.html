besides

1. ADV

/ bɪˈsaɪdz / (AmE), / bɪˈsaɪdz / (BrE)
used to introduce an additional point or reason; in addition; furthermore.

Examples

Besides, he can hardly avoid musing on the instability of death which, what with exhumations and rehabilitations, seems to match that of life.
Besides, the records were dusty.
There was an air of blindness in her gray eyes, the startled horse look that ultimately comes to some women who are born at the end of an ancestral line long since divorced from money-making and which, besides, has kept its estate intact.
Besides, they were not the only impressions.
Besides, Miss Henrietta - as she was generally known since she had put up her hair with a chignon in the back - had little time to spare them from her teaching and writing; so Cyrus Adler became interested in her friend Racie Friedenwald, and Joe Jastrow - the only young man who when he wrote had the temerity to address her as Henrietta, and signed himself Joe - fell in love with pretty sister Rachel.

Synset Examples

I don't want to go to a restaurant; besides, we can't afford it
she couldn't shelter behind him all the time and in any case he wasn't always with her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moreover - used to add information that supports or continues a previous statement
  • Synonym: furthermore - used to introduce a further point or statement
  • Antonym: however - used to introduce a contrast or opposing point

Etymology

From Middle English, a combination of 'by' and 'side(s)',' meaning 'by the side of.'

2. ADV

/ bɪˈsaɪdz / (AmE), / bɪˈsaɪdz / (BrE)
Used to add another point, fact, or argument to what has already been said; in addition; also.

Examples

Each of us had his own specialty besides.
Besides, the new tenants pay more rent.

Synset Examples

he has a Mercedes, too

Related Terms

  • Synonym: also - in addition; too
  • Synonym: moreover - furthermore; in addition
  • Antonym: however - but; yet

Etymology

From Middle English 'beside' (at the side of) plus adverbial -s ending.
