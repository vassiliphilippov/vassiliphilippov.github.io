drift

1. NOUN

/ drɪft / (AmE), / drɪft / (BrE)
a slow movement or force that causes something to be carried along by currents of air, water, or other mediums, especially in a passive or gradual manner

Examples

The ratio of the measured antenna temperature change during a drift scan across the moon to the average brightness temperature of the moon over the antenna beam (assuming that the brightness temperature of the sky is negligible) was found, by graphical integration of the antenna directivity diagram, to be 0.85.
Air drifts from one area to another are, therefore, conceivable.
Also, interlobular air drifts may be all but nonexistent in the cow; probably occur in the horse much as in the human being; and, in contrast are present to a relatively immense degree on a segmental basis in the dog where lobules are absent (Van Allen and Lindskog,' 31).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: current - a flow of air, water, or electricity in a particular direction

Etymology

From Middle English 'drifte', related to Old English 'drīfan' (to drive).

2. VERB

/ drɪft / (AmE), / drɪft / (BrE)
to move slowly and aimlessly away from a set course, path, or direction, either physically or metaphorically

Examples

He stopped pacing, leaned against the dank, timbered wall and let his mind drift.
They kept drifting apart and merging again in his mind like some minute form of life on a microscope slide.
I saw you driftin away - but I tried.

Synset Examples

don't drift from the set course

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stray - to move away from the correct place or path
  • Synonym: veer - to change direction suddenly or slightly

Etymology

Probably from Middle Dutch *driften* (to drive, drift).

3. NOUN

/ drɪft / (AmE), / drɪft / (BrE)
a gradual shift or movement away from an intended course, typically as a result of external influences such as wind or current (used especially for ships or aircraft)

Examples

The riotous onrush of industrialism after the War for Southern Independence and the general secular drift to the Religion of Humanity, however, prepared the way for a reception of the French Revolution's socialistic offspring of one sort of another.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deviation - the action of departing from an established course or accepted standard
  • Antonym: course - the intended route or direction of movement

4. VERB

/ drɪft / (AmE), / drɪft / (BrE)
to move slowly and gradually, especially as a result of air or water currents, without direct control

Examples

But because teeth sometimes may drift back to their original position, a retaining appliance is used to lock them in place.
Many of them have drifted into the cities and towns and seaports.
The big fans were going, drawing from the large room the remnants of stale smoke which drifted about in pale strata underneath the ceiling.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: float - to rest or move on or near the surface of a liquid without sinking
  • Synonym: blow - to move in a current of air
  • Antonym: sink - to go down below the surface of a liquid

Etymology

From Middle English 'driften', from Old Norse 'drífa' (to drive, drift).
