forecast

1. VERB

/ ˈfɔrkæst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːkɑːst / (BrE)
to predict or estimate a future event or trend, especially based on data or analysis

Examples

However, they do demonstrate the presence of large normal pressures in the presence of flat shear fields which were forecast by the theory in the first part of the paper.
Accordingly, if epiphyseal diaphyseal fusion occurs in this phalanx near menarche, early and late menarches might have been forecast rather precisely at the time of Onset of ossification for the 18 girls with "same schedule ".
A similar analysis of Figure 6 for the 34 boys would necessitate quite a different conclusion about the predictive value of onset age in forecasting their attainment of the pubescent stage.
Has the probable price situation in your field been forecast as a basis for future planning?
Total farm output for this year is officially forecast at 129 % of the 1947 - 49 average, three points higher than the July 1 estimate and exactly equal to the final figure for 1960.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: predict - say or estimate that a specified thing will happen in the future or will be a consequence of something
  • Synonym: project - estimate or forecast (something) on the basis of present trends
  • Antonym: retrospect - to look back or review past events (opposite in time direction)

Etymology

From fore- (before) + cast (Middle English 'casten', to throw, reckon).

2. VERB

/ ˈfɔrˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːkɑːst / (BrE)
to predict or indicate a future event or trend, especially on the basis of available signs or evidence

Examples

All this, though, is simply a prelude, a curtain-raiser, for what ensues, and I doubt whether any Occidental could accurately forecast it.

Synset Examples

These signs bode bad news

Related Terms

  • Synonym: predict - to say that something will happen in the future
  • Synonym: prophesy - to state that something will happen in the future, often with authority or divine inspiration
  • Antonym: retrospect - a review or consideration of past events

Etymology

From fore- (before) + cast (to throw, project), originating in Middle English.

3. VERB

/ ˈfɔrˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːkɑːst / (BrE)
to estimate or predict that something will happen in the future based on available information

Examples

Have you examined these trends, forecast the effects, and planned your marketing strategy to compete effectively under changing circumstances?
the aspect of learning the internal organization of obvious natural genetic groups of languages as well as their more remote and elusive external links; of classification first, with elapsed age merely a by-product; of acquiring evidential knowledge of what happened in Athabascan, in Yokuts, in Uto-Aztecan in the last few thousand years as well as forecasting what more anciently may have happened between them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: predict - to say that an event or action will happen in the future
  • Degree Lower: guess - to estimate or suppose something without sufficient information

Etymology

From fore- (before) + cast (to throw, to calculate); originally meant to calculate or plan beforehand.

4. NOUN

/ ˈfɔrˌkæst / (AmE), / ˈfɔːkɑːst / (BrE)
A statement about what is likely to happen in the future, based on information available at the time, especially about weather, business, or social trends.

Examples

Practically all forecasts mention new and exciting products on the horizon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prognosis - a prediction about the likely outcome of something, especially in medicine
  • Synonym: prediction - a statement about what will happen in the future, based on knowledge or experience

Etymology

From Middle English 'forecaste', from 'fore-' (before) + 'cast' (to throw, calculate).
