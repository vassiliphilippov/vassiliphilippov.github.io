pretence

1. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛns / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛns / (BrE)
a claim, feeling, or quality that is false or not genuine; the act of pretending

Examples

The pretence was that he was delivering a prescription from Dr. Verdi.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pretense - US spelling of 'pretence', a false claim or act of pretending
  • Synonym: deception - the act of deceiving or misleading
  • Antonym: truth - that which is true or in accordance with fact or reality

Etymology

From Old French 'pretense', from Medieval Latin 'praetensus', from Latin 'praetendere' (to pretend).

2. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛns / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛns / (BrE)
An act of giving a false appearance or excuse in order to conceal the real purpose; a guise or pretext.

Examples

Those named in the Greek paper were manufacturing reasons to steal aft under pretence of some call of duty, so as to be near Spencer, watching an opportunity to communicate with him.

Synset Examples

under the guise of friendship he betrayed them

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guise - an external form or appearance concealing the true nature of something
  • Synonym: pretext - a reason given in justification of a course of action that is not the real reason

Etymology

From Old French *pretensse*, from Medieval Latin *pretensio* (claim, pretext), from Latin *praetendere* (to stretch forth, allege as an excuse).

3. NOUN

/ prɪˈtɛns / (AmE), / prɪˈtɛns / (BrE)
An attempt to make something that is not the case appear true; a false show or claim.

Examples

That little farce you played with my sister.

Synset Examples

his conformity was only pretending

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pretense - the act of giving a false appearance (American spelling)
  • Synonym: deception - the act of deceiving or misleading
  • Antonym: honesty - the quality of being honest, absence of deceit

Etymology

From Old French 'pretense', from Medieval Latin 'praetensus', past participle of Latin 'praetendere' (to pretend). Spelling 'pretence' is chiefly British; 'pretense' is American.
