spoil

1. VERB

/ spɔɪl / (AmE), / spɔɪl / (BrE)
to ruin or damage the quality, value, or enjoyment of something

Examples

While observing the operations of our Government in various parts of the world, I have felt that in many situations where our policies were good we have tended to ignore minor problems which spoiled our main effort.

Synset Examples

I botched the dinner and we had to eat out
the pianist screwed up the difficult passage in the second movement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ruin - to destroy or severely harm something so it cannot recover
  • Antonym: repair - to restore something that is broken or damaged

Etymology

From Old French 'espoillier', from Latin 'spoliare' (to strip, plunder).

2. VERB

/ spɔɪl / (AmE), / spɔɪl / (BrE)
(of food or other perishable items) to become unfit for use or consumption, typically as a result of decay or decomposition.

Examples

It was spoiled now for seed, and it would sour and mold in three days if they failed to find a place and fuel to dry it.

Synset Examples

the meat must be eaten before it spoils

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rot - to undergo natural decomposition; to decay
  • Synonym: decay - to decompose or rot, as with organic matter
  • Antonym: preserve - to keep something in its original state; to prevent from spoiling

Etymology

From Old French 'espojler' and Latin 'spoliare' (to strip, plunder).

3. VERB

/ spɔɪl / (AmE), / spɔɪl / (BrE)
to treat someone, especially a child, with too much kindness or indulgence, often resulting in undesirable behavior or attitudes

Examples

I have a sentimental weakness for my children, and I spoil them.

Synset Examples

grandparents often pamper the children
Let's not mollycoddle our students!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pamper - treat with too much care or indulgence
  • Synonym: mollycoddle - treat someone with excessive indulgence or protectiveness

Etymology

From Old French 'espoillier', from Latin 'spoliare' (to strip, to rob).
