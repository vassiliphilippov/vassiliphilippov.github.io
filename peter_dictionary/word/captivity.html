captivity

1. NOUN

/ kæpˈtɪvəti / (AmE), / kæpˈtɪvəti / (BrE)
the condition of being kept in confinement or under restraint, especially against one's will

Examples

It seems likely that the Indian python comes out ahead because records of its growth have been made more carefully and frequently; it responds exceptionally well to captivity and does not reach proportions that make it hard to keep.

Synset Examples

he was held in captivity until he died
the imprisonment of captured soldiers
his ignominious incarceration in the local jail
he practiced the immurement of his enemies in the castle dungeon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imprisonment - the state of being imprisoned; confinement
  • Antonym: freedom - the state of being free; absence of captivity or restraint

Etymology

From Latin captivitas, from captivus meaning 'captive'.

2. NOUN

/ kæpˈtɪvəti / (AmE), / kæpˈtɪvɪti / (BrE)
The state of being kept as a prisoner or slave and not being free.

Examples

An understanding of the new role of residential association in an industrial society serves to illuminate the forces which have fashioned the iron cage of conformity which imprisons the churches in their suburban captivity.

Synset Examples

So every bondman in his own hand bears the power to cancel his captivity

Related Terms

  • Antonym: freedom - the power or right to act, speak, or think as one wants; the state of not being imprisoned or enslaved

Etymology

From Latin captivitas, from captivus (captive).
