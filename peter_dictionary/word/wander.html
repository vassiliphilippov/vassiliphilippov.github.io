wander

1. VERB

/ ˈwɑndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
to walk or move about without a fixed course, purpose, or destination, often in a leisurely or aimless way

Examples

In France he had puzzled the meaning of the great stone monuments men had thrown up to the sky, and always as he wandered, he felt a stranger to their exultation.
At home, he would n't even wash his hands for supper, and he wandered around the yard in a pair of sweaty old corduroys.
Those souls like to wander off, and must be called back.
You can n't wander along in the dark, can you?
Once out in the street, the traffic was a gadfly maze in which he wandered stricken.

Synset Examples

The gypsies roamed the woods
roving vagabonds
the wandering Jew
The cattle roam across the prairie
the laborers drift from one town to the next
They rolled from town to town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roam - to move about or travel aimlessly or unsystematically, especially over a wide area
  • Synonym: meander - to proceed by or take a winding or indirect course
  • Synonym: stray - to move away aimlessly from a group or from the right course or place
  • Antonym: stay - to remain in the same place; the opposite of moving about aimlessly

Etymology

From Old English 'wandrian' (to wander, stray) related to Old High German 'wantarōn' (to wander), possibly from a Germanic root meaning 'to turn, wind'.

2. VERB

/ ˈwɑndər / (AmE), / ˈwɒndə / (BrE)
(of a person's romantic or sexual feelings) to be unfaithful to one's partner in marriage or a romantic relationship.

Examples

Motive - her husband wandering?

Synset Examples

She cheats on her husband
Might her husband be wandering?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cheat - to be sexually unfaithful to one's partner
  • Antonym: be faithful - to remain loyal to one's partner in a romantic or sexual relationship
