wail

1. VERB

/ weɪl / (AmE), / weɪl / (BrE)
to make a long, loud, high-pitched cry, usually of pain, grief, or sorrow

Examples

Then the pixies and the zombies took over while the banshees wailed in the distance.
Mike remained in trance; there was much to grok, loose ends to puzzle over and fit into his growing - all that he had seen and heard and been at the Archangel Foster Tabernacle (not just cusp when he and Digby had come face to face alone) - why Bishop Senator Boone made him warily uneasy, how Miss Dawn Ardent tasted like a water brother when she was not, the smell of goodness he had incompletely grokked in the jumping up and down and wailing -
Shadow looked surprised, wailed, and trotted after her.
The wailing, guitar strumming minstrels of the cattle kingdom made up songs about him.
[MAN ON MEGAPHONE SPEAKING SPANISH] [EXPLOSION] [PEOPLE SHOUTING] [CAR HONKING] [SIREN WAILING] MICHAEL: Fredo!

Synset Examples

wail in self-pity
howl with sorrow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: howl - make a long, loud, doleful cry; especially of animals or of people in pain or distress
  • Degree Lower: sob - cry noisily, making loud, convulsive gasps
  • Degree Higher: scream - give a long, loud, piercing cry, usually from intense emotion

Etymology

From Old Norse *væla* (to lament, cry), related to Old English *wǣlan* (to bewail).

2. NOUN

/ weɪl / (AmE), / weɪl / (BrE)
A long, loud cry expressing sorrow, pain, or grief.

Examples

The events of the last quarter of an hour, mysterious to any bird accustomed only to the predictable life of coop and barnyard, had overcome the doctor's hen and she gave out a series of cackly wails, perhaps mourning her nest, but briefly enjoyed.
The doctor's wits had not left him, however, for all his sixty-eight years, and the wails were almost immediately lost in the sound of water rushing out from the showerhead.

Synset Examples

their pitiful laments could be heard throughout the ward

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lament - an expression of sorrow or grief
  • Synonym: cry - a loud vocal sound expressing emotion

Etymology

Probably from Old Norse *væla* (to lament).

3. VERB

/ weɪl / (AmE), / weɪl / (BrE)
To cry weakly or softly, often expressing pain or sorrow.

Examples

"Four years "! she wailed.

Synset Examples

she wailed with pain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whimper - cry with low, feeble sounds
  • Degree Higher: sob - cry noisily, making loud, convulsive gasps

Etymology

From Middle English 'waylen', probably from Old Norse 'væla' (to lament, cry).
