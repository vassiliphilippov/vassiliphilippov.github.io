divert

1. VERB

/ daɪˈvɝt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to entertain or amuse someone, especially by providing an agreeable distraction

Examples

The previous scandals, gaily diverting as they were, had only served to increase his popularity.

Synset Examples

The play amused the ladies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amuse - to entertain or make someone laugh or smile
  • Synonym: entertain - to provide enjoyment or amusement

Etymology

From Latin divertere, meaning 'to turn aside'.

2. VERB

/ daɪˈvɜrt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to cause something or someone to change direction from its intended course; to turn aside.

Examples

Usually, this was done when attention was diverted by someone else's long, boring story.
Her extendibles were diverted, connected or augmented and the final, delicate-beyond-description brain taps were completed while Helva remained anesthetically unaware of the proceedings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deviate - to depart from an established course

Etymology

From Latin 'divertere', from 'di-' (aside) + 'vertere' (to turn).

3. VERB

/ dəˈvɝt / (AmE), / daɪˈvɜːt / (BrE)
to cause someone or something to change direction or be sent along a different course from the intended or expected one

Examples

This may both divert the attention of the uninitiate and cause confusion for the more knowledgeable.
She lived by the rules, never compromising, never blinded or diverted by circumstance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: redirect - to send to a different place or in a different direction

Etymology

From Latin 'divertere' (to turn aside), from 'dis-' (aside) + 'vertere' (to turn).
