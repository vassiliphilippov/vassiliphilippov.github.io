induction

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the production of an electromotive force across a conductor in a changing magnetic field; an electrical phenomenon by which a voltage is generated in a circuit by a change in the flow of current

Examples

This measurement was obtained by W. Blumberg of the University of California,Berkeley, by observing the breadth of the free induction decay signal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: electromagnetic induction - the process by which a changing magnetic field induces a current in a conductor
  • Antonym: conduction - the process by which electricity is directly transmitted through a substance

Etymology

From Latin inductio (a leading in), from inducere (to lead in).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈdʌkʃən / (BrE)
the process of reasoning from particular facts or cases to general principles or conclusions; inductive reasoning

Examples

It is very easy to establish by induction on r that if f is in * * f then * * f that is, * * f, etc..

Related Terms

  • Antonym: deduction - reasoning from general principles to specific cases

Etymology

From Latin 'inductio' (a leading in), from 'inducere' (to lead in).

From Latin 'inductio', from 'inducere' (to lead in).
