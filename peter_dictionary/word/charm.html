charm

1. NOUN

/ tʃɑrm / (AmE), / tʃɑːm / (BrE)
a quality that makes someone or something attractive and pleasing; attractiveness that delights or fascinates

Examples

As for the ladies, they were full of charm, and sincerity, and deep and abiding affection for this hurrying driving, honest, little man.
The already faded pastel charms of the naive music itself vanish entirely in Fistoulari's melodramatic contrasts between ultra vehement brute power and chilly, if suave, sentimentality.
Yet nationalism has lost few of its charms for the historian, writer or man in the street.
He had the same bullet head of curly reddish hair but he did n't have Jim's poker-faced humor or his brains or his charm.
Charming and peaceful - but what were charm and peace compared to high adventure?

Synset Examples

his smile was part of his appeal to her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: allure - an attractive or tempting quality
  • Synonym: appeal - attractive or interesting quality
  • Antonym: repulsion - a feeling of intense distaste or disgust

Etymology

From Old French *charme*, from Latin *carmen* (song, verse, incantation).

2. NOUN

/ tʃɑrm / (AmE), / tʃɑːm / (BrE)
a series of words or a phrase that is believed to have magical power when spoken or written

Examples

Inscribed around its base is a charm in Balinese, a dialect I take it you do n't comprehend.

Synset Examples

he whispered a spell as he moved his hands
inscribed around its base is a charm in Balinese

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spell - a set of words with magical power
  • Synonym: incantation - ritual recitation of words with supposed magical power

Etymology

From Old French *charme*, from Latin *carmen* (song, verse, incantation).

3. NOUN

/ tʃɑrm / (AmE), / tʃɑːm / (BrE)
an object or piece of jewelry believed to bring good luck or protect against bad luck

Examples

More potent a charm to bring back that time of life than this record of a few pictures and a few remembered facts would be a catalogue of the minutiae which are of the very stuff of the mind, intrinsic, because they were known in the beginning not by the eye alone but by the hand that held them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: talisman - object believed to have magical powers of protection or luck
  • Synonym: amulet - small object worn to ward off evil or bring good fortune

Etymology

From Old French *charme*, from Latin *carmen* (song, incantation).

4. VERB

/ tʃɑrm / (AmE), / tʃɑːm / (BrE)
to attract or delight someone greatly, often by being pleasing, interesting, or enchanting

Examples

Famed cellist Pablo Casals took his instrument to the East Room of the White House yesterday and charmed the staff with a two-hour rehearsal.
But, even if Mr. Sansom labors too hard to extract more refinements of meaning and feeling from his travel experiences than the limits of language allow, he still can charm and astound.

Synset Examples

She captured all the men's hearts

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enchant - to fill someone with great delight or charm
  • Synonym: captivate - to attract and hold the interest and attention of someone
  • Antonym: bore - to make someone feel uninterested or dull

Etymology

From Old French charmer, from Latin carminare (to chant, cast a spell), from carmen (song, verse).
