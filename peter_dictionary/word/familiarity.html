familiarity

1. NOUN

/ fəˌmɪlˈjæɹəti / (AmE), / fəˌmɪlɪˈærɪti / (BrE)
The state of being well known or easily recognized, often due to frequent exposure or long acquaintance.

Examples

Mr. McKinley, for all his sprawling and his easy familiarity, was completely alert to his son, eyes always on the still face, jumping to anticipate Scotty's desires.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: novelty - the quality of being new, original, or unfamiliar

Etymology

From Old French 'familiarite', from Latin 'familiaritas' (close acquaintance, intimacy).

2. NOUN

/ fəˌmɪlˈjɛrəti / (AmE), / fəˌmɪlˈjærəti / (BrE)
Personal knowledge or close understanding of someone or something; the state of being familiar with a subject or person.

Examples

In much the same way, we recognize the importance of Shakespeare's familarity with Plutarch and Montaigne, of Shelley's study of Plato's dialogues, and of Coleridge's enthusiastic plundering of the writings of many philosophers and theologians from Plato to Schelling and William Godwin, through which so many abstract ideas were brought to the attention of English men of letters.
This limited familiarity with the possible phenomena has severely hampered work with tone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: acquaintance - a person's knowledge or experience of something
  • Antonym: ignorance - lack of knowledge or information

Etymology

From Old French 'familiarité', from Latin 'familiaritas' (intimacy, friendship), from 'familiaris' (familiar).

3. NOUN

/ fəˌmɪlˈjæɾɪti / (AmE), / fəˌmɪlɪˈærɪti / (BrE)
A close, warm friendship or intimate acquaintance.

Examples

He had left her inviolate, thinking familiarity would gentle her in time.

Synset Examples

the absence of fences created a mysterious intimacy in which no one knew privacy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intimacy - close familiarity or friendship

Etymology

From Old French 'familiarite', from Latin 'familiaritas' (intimacy, friendship).
