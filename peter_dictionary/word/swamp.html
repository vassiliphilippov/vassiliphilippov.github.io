swamp

1. NOUN

/ swɑmp / (AmE), / swɒmp / (BrE)
An area of low-lying land that is frequently flooded and typically supports woody vegetation, having better drainage than a bog and more trees and shrubs than a marsh.

Examples

Tolek Alterman had returned from the colonies in Palestine and, before the national leadership, exalted the miracles of drying up swamps and irrigating the desert.
He loathed the idea of drying up swamps or the chills of malaria or of leaving his natural birthright.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: marsh - wetland with grasses and reeds, fewer trees than a swamp
  • Degree Lower: bog - wetland with poor drainage and acidic, spongy ground

Etymology

Probably of Low German origin, related to Middle Low German *swamp* (sponge, swamp).

2. VERB

/ swɑmp / (AmE), / swɒmp / (BrE)
(of water or another substance) to fill or flood something quickly beyond its capacity, often causing it to sink or become submerged; to overwhelm completely.

Examples

He stalked into the water and fell heavily over the side of the flat-bottomed barge, his weight nearly swamping the craft.

Synset Examples

the basement was inundated after the storm
The images flooded his mind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deluge - to flood or overwhelm with a great amount of something
  • Synonym: inundate - to flood, overflow or overwhelm

Etymology

From late 17th-century, derived from the noun 'swamp' (marshy land).

3. VERB

/ swɑmp / (AmE), / swɒmp / (BrE)
to drench or submerge, especially with water; to overwhelm by excess or flooding

Examples

In 1707 an earthquake in Japan generated waves so huge that they piled into the Inland Sea; one wave swamped more than 1000 ships and boats in Osaka Bay.

Synset Examples

The tsunami swamped every boat in the harbor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flood - to inundate with water
  • Synonym: overwhelm - to overpower or engulf completely
