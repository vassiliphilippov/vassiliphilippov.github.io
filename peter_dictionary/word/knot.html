knot

1. NOUN

/ nɑt / (AmE), / nɒt / (BrE)
A small group or tightly clustered gathering of people or things.

Examples

A knot of old worshippers, chiefly women, listened weekly to a sermon.

Synset Examples

a small knot of women listened to his sermon
the bird had a knot of feathers forming a crest

Etymology

From Old English cnotta, of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ nɑt / (AmE), / nɒt / (BrE)
a fastening made by looping and tying a rope, cord, or similar material upon itself or to another object

Examples

His hand went to her shoulder and pushed aside the knotted scarf that surmounted the striped poplin gown; then, to better purpose, he took hold of the knot and with dextrous fingers, untied it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bow - a type of knot formed by tying two loops together, commonly used for shoelaces or ribbons
  • Synonym: noose - a loop with a slipknot that tightens under load
  • Antonym: untie - to undo a knot or something tied

Etymology

Old English *cnotta*, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch *knoop* and German *Knoten*.

3. VERB

/ nɑt / (AmE), / nɒt / (BrE)
to tie or fasten into a knot or knots; to make something into a knot

Examples

Memory flooded him the instant he opened his eyes and the sick feeling knotted his stomach.

Synset Examples

She knotted her fingers

Related Terms

  • Antonym: untie - to undo a knot or something tied

Etymology

From Old English 'cnotta', of Germanic origin.

4. NOUN

/ nɑt / (AmE), / nɒt / (BrE)
A hard, rounded area in a piece of wood where a branch grew, often making the grain cross-grained and harder than the surrounding wood.

Examples

A Michigan soldier stationed in Georgia wrote in 1864: "I expect to be tough as a knott as soon as I get over the Georgia Shitts ".

Synset Examples

the saw buckled when it hit a knot

Etymology

From Old English cnotta, related to Old Norse knutr (knot).
