avant-garde

1. ADJ

/ ˌævɑ̃ ˈɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˌævɒ̃ ˈɡɑːd / (BrE)
Favoring or introducing radically new and experimental ideas and methods, especially in the arts.

Examples

Nevertheless, Prokofieff was much influenced by Paris during the Twenties: the Paris which was the artistic center of the Western World - the social Paris to which Russian aristocracy migrated - the chic Paris which attracted the tourist dollars of rich America - the avant-garde Paris of Diaghileff, Stravinsky, Koussevitzky, Cocteau, Picasso - the laissez-faire Paris of Dadaism and ultramodern art - the Paris sympathique which took young composers to her bosom with such quick and easy enthusiasms.

Synset Examples

an avant-garde theater piece

Related Terms

  • Synonym: innovative - introducing new ideas; original and creative in thinking
  • Antonym: conventional - based on or in accordance with what is generally done or believed

Etymology

From French avant-garde, literally 'advance guard' (avant 'before' + garde 'guard').

2. NOUN

/ ˌævɑ̃ˈɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˌavɒ̃ˈɡɑːd / (BrE)
A group of people who develop new and experimental ideas and methods, especially in art, music, or literature.

Examples

You will join me in the avant-garde of the new aesthetic.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: mainstream - the dominant trend or majority opinion in a particular field

Etymology

From French 'avant-garde' meaning 'vanguard' or 'advance guard'.
