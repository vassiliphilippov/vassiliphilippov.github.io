vent

1. NOUN

/ vɛnt / (AmE), / vɛnt / (BrE)
the external opening of the urinary or genital system, especially in lower vertebrates such as fish, amphibians, and reptiles

Examples

One female, collected on an island off the coast of Nicaragua, was gravid and measured 4 feet 8 inches from snout to vent (her tail should be between 6 and 7 inches long).

Etymology

From Old French 'vent' (opening), from Latin 'ventus' (wind); applied to animal anatomy by analogy to an opening or outlet.

2. VERB

/ vɛnt / (AmE), / vɛnt / (BrE)
to express or release strong feelings, especially anger or frustration, often by speaking or acting forcefully

Examples

Above all, disabuse yourselves of any thought that I propose to vent moral indignation at your rifling my residence, to whimper over the loss of a few objets d'art, or to shame you into rectitude.
He straightened up, ready to vent his exasperation, then grew afraid.

Synset Examples

She vented her anger
The graduates gave vent to cheers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: express - to put (feeling, thought) into words or actions
  • Antonym: bottle up - to deliberately not express emotions

Etymology

From Latin 'ventus' (wind), via Old French 'venter', sense influenced by the idea of letting out air or gas.

3. NOUN

/ vɛnt / (AmE), / vɛnt / (BrE)
an opening that allows air, gas, or smoke to pass out of or into a confined space

Examples

Ventilation is provided in a concrete block basement shelter by vents in the wall and by the open entrance.
Vent pipes also are necessary (as shown in figs. 9, 10, and 11), but filters are not.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grille - a metal or plastic cover with openings for air, often over a vent
  • Synonym: duct - a passage or tube for air, gas, or liquid
  • Antonym: seal - to close tightly so that air or gas cannot escape

Etymology

From Old French *vent* (wind), from Latin *ventus* (wind).
