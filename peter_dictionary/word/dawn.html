dawn

1. NOUN

/ dɔn / (AmE), / dɔːn / (BrE)
The time each day when light first appears in the sky, just before the sun rises.

Examples

The landscape of Greek history broadens widely, and rather abruptly, in the eighth century B.C., the age of Homer's "rosy fingered Dawn ".
Argiento had been trained so rigorously by the Jesuits that Michelangelo was unable to change his habits: up before dawn to scrub the floors, whether they were dirty or not; water boiling on the fire for washing laundry every day, the pots scoured with river sand after each meal.
Before dawn.
At dawn Argiento went to Jacopo Galli.
Kate had no idea what they were talking of, although she had seen the blue lights and strange fires burning and winking on the ridges at night, had heard horsemen on the River Road and hill trails through the nights till dawn.

Synset Examples

we got up before dawn
they talked until morning

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: sunrise - the time when the sun appears above the horizon
  • Antonym: dusk - the time when darkness begins at the end of the day

Etymology

From Old English 'dagian' (to become day), related to 'day'.

2. NOUN

/ dɔn / (AmE), / dɔːn / (BrE)
The beginning or earliest stage of a period, development, or event.

Examples

But Greg's area remained as placid as a Florida dawn.

Synset Examples

the dawn of civilization
the morning of the world

Related Terms

  • Synonym: beginning - the point in time or space at which something starts
  • Antonym: end - the final stage or termination of something

Etymology

From Old English 'dagian' meaning 'to become day'.

3. VERB

/ dɔn / (AmE), / dɔːn / (BrE)
(of a fact or realization) to become clear or enter someone's consciousness; to begin to be understood.

Examples

It dawns on you that instead of a lump to fill the seat across the bridge table from you, he was a man, and that because Gratt Shafer was making you miserable, you were passing it down to him, to Gratt Shafer's substitute, that other guy.

Synset Examples

It dawned on him that she had betrayed him
she was penetrated with sorrow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: occur - (of an idea or realization) to come into the mind

Etymology

Originally from Old English 'dagian', meaning 'to become day'. Figurative sense referring to mental clarity developed later.
