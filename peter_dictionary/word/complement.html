complement

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑm.pləˌmɛnt / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.plɪˌmɛnt / (BrE)
to add to something in a way that enhances or completes it; to supply what is lacking so as to make whole or perfect

Examples

Immediately, the film improved and it improved because in narrative it found a content based on time to complement its own unbreakable connection with time.
Complementing the political principle of nationalism is the legal principle of sovereignty.
She lived in an ultra-modern house whose decoration, appointments, paint, and even pets were chosen to complement her coloring; the pets were a couple of Siamese cats.

Synset Examples

I need some pepper to complement the sweet touch in the soup

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supplement - to add to something to make up for a deficiency or to complete it

Etymology

From Latin complementum (that which fills up or completes), from complēre (to fill up).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmpləmənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmplɪmənt / (BrE)
a thing that contributes extra features to something else in such a way as to improve or complete it

Examples

Heydrich, in opening the Conference, followed the reasoning and even the phraseology of the order issued earlier by Goering which authorized the Final Solution as "a complement to "previous "solutions "for eliminating the Jews from German living space through violence, economic strangulation, forced emigration, and evacuation.

Synset Examples

a fine wine is a perfect complement to the dinner
wild rice was served as an accompaniment to the main dish

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supplement - an addition designed to complete, strengthen, or make up for a lack
  • Synonym: accompaniment - something that goes well or is served with something else
  • Antonym: contrast - something strikingly different, providing an opposing feature

Etymology

From Latin complementum (that which fills up or completes), from complere (to fill up).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkɑmpləmənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmplɪmənt / (BrE)
the complete number or quantity needed to make something full or complete

Examples

Its entire complement of non-commissioned officers on the platoon level had departed as cadre for another unit, and its vehicles were still those used in the drive across Luzon in World War 2,.

Synset Examples

a full complement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: quota - a fixed, limited amount or number that is officially allowed

Etymology

From Latin complementum (that which fills up), from complēre (to fill up).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmpləmənt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmplɪmənt / (BrE)
the number or quantity of people or things required to make a group complete, especially a full crew or staff

Examples

Military experts say a tight naval blockade off Cuban ports and at the approaches to Cuban waters would require two naval task forces, each built around an aircraft carrier with a complement of about 100 planes and several destroyers.

Synset Examples

a full complement of workers

Etymology

From Latin complementum (that which fills up or completes), from complere (to fill up).

5. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmpləˌmɛnt / (AmE), / ˈkɒmplɪmənt / (BrE)
A word or phrase, especially in grammar, that is necessary to complete the meaning of a particular expression or construction.

Examples

is Bill's broken a chair, dominant stress will usually be on the complement a chair.
In I know one thing dominant stress will usually be on the complement one thing; in one thing I know it will usually be on the predicator know.
In small-town people are very friendly dominant stress will generally be on the complement very friendly; in the double sentence the smaller the town, the friendlier the people it will generally be on the subjects the town and the people.
it will generally be on the complement a linguist.
Dominant stress is on her luggage both in that's her luggage, where her luggage is the complement, and in there's her luggage, where it is the subject.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: object - a noun or noun phrase governed by a verb or preposition
  • Degree Lower: subject complement - a complement that follows a linking verb and describes the subject
  • Antonym: adjunct - a word or phrase that adds extra information but is not a complement

Etymology

From Latin complementum (that which fills up or completes), from complēre (to fill up).
