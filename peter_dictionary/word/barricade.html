barricade

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɛrɪˌkeɪd / (AmE), / ˈbarɪkeɪd / (BrE)
A barrier, especially one set up by police to stop or control traffic on a street or road, often for the purpose of catching a fugitive or inspecting vehicles.

Examples

Closer still, regular barricades of barbed wire hung on timber supports.
At 7: 25 two hotel doormen came thumping down the steps, carrying a saw-horse to be set up as a barricade in front of the haberdashery store window next to the entranceway, and as I watched them in their gaudy red coats that nearly scraped the ground, their golden, fringed epaulets and spic, red visored caps, I suddenly saw just over their shoulders Jessica gracefully making her way through the crowd.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roadblock - Barrier to stop or divert road traffic

Etymology

From French 'barricade', from 'barrique' (barrel), as barrels were often used to form barricades.

2. VERB

/ ˈbær.ɪ.keɪd / (AmE), / ˈbær.ɪ.keɪd / (BrE)
To block or obstruct a place by putting up barriers to prevent movement or passage.

Examples

He added, "If this does n't work out, the three of you barricade yourself in the house and talk terms with them ".

Synset Examples

block the way
barricade the streets
stop the busy road

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to obstruct or impede movement
  • Antonym: unblock - to remove obstacles so that passage is possible

Etymology

From French 'barricader', from 'barrique' meaning 'barrel', originally referring to using barrels as barriers.
