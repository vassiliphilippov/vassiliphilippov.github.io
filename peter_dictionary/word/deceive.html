deceive

1. VERB

/ dɪˈsiv / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːv / (BrE)
to make someone believe something that is not true, typically in order to gain some personal advantage

Examples

Rather than being deceived, the eye is puzzled; instead of seeing objects in space, it sees nothing more than - a picture.
He did not wish to deceive Kayabashi exactly, just to display the mission activities in a graphic and impressive manner.
The man began to creep in the wrong direction, deceived by a slight rise in the ground!
And he took repeated care to let his colleagues know that he intended them: "Even the Unitarian churches have caught the malaria, and are worse than those who deceived them "- which implied that they were very bad indeed.
The aborigine is not deceived; he knows that the land is hard and pitiless.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mislead - cause someone to have a wrong idea or impression
  • Synonym: trick - deceive or cheat by a trick
  • Antonym: betray - to be disloyal or unfaithful to someone
  • Antonym: be honest - to tell the truth or act truthfully

Etymology

From Old French *deceivre*, from Latin *decipere* meaning 'to ensnare, cheat'.

2. VERB

/ dɪˈsiv / (AmE), / dɪˈsiːv / (BrE)
to make someone believe something that is not true; to mislead or trick someone

Examples

Could it be that my own eyes and ears had deceived me?
Unless the Administration and the Defense Department have been deceiving us, the facts do not support the assertion that we are "compelled "to resume atmospheric testing.

Synset Examples

The insurance company deceived me when they told me they were covering my house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mislead - to give someone the wrong idea or impression; to lead into error
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive or cheat someone by cunning or skill
  • Antonym: honest - free of deceit; truthful and sincere
  • Antonym: truthful - telling or expressing the truth; honest

Etymology

From Old French *deceivre*, from Latin *decipere* (to ensnare, cheat).
