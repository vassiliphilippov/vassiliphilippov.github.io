flounder

1. VERB

/ ˈflaʊndɚ / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
to struggle to move or behave awkwardly, especially with difficulty or confusion

Examples

He flounders and lets music sprawl.
Doubts thus inculcated left me floundering for a while and, like some higher critical friends, trying to continue to use the Bible as the Word of God while at the same time holding it to have been subjected to a vast number of redactions and interpolations:

Synset Examples

She is floundering in college

Related Terms

  • Synonym: struggle - to make forceful or violent efforts to get free or achieve something
  • Synonym: falter - to lose strength or momentum; to hesitate

Etymology

Origin uncertain; perhaps related to Dutch *flodderen* (to flop about) or influenced by the fish name 'flounder' due to its awkward movement.

2. VERB

/ ˈflaʊndɚ / (AmE), / ˈflaʊndə / (BrE)
to move or walk with great difficulty, often struggling awkwardly to keep one's balance, especially in water, mud, or another difficult environment

Examples

The man had come floundering aboard the flat-bottomed barge at the last instant, brandishing the flag of truce.
Watson watched two of them flounder into the shallow water and listened to their voices beg shrilly.
The three of them floundered through the door into the interior and fell in a heap.

Synset Examples

He staggered along in the heavy snow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stagger - to walk or move unsteadily, as if about to fall
  • Synonym: struggle - to make forceful or violent efforts to get free of restraint or resist attack

Etymology

Probably from earlier meaning 'to stumble, struggle,' perhaps a blend of 'blunder' and 'founder'.
