shell

1. NOUN

/ ʃɛl / (AmE), / ʃɛl / (BrE)
the hard, protective outer covering of certain animals, such as mollusks, crustaceans, and eggs

Examples

By the end of the third act, the artist is dead but the body lingers on, a shell among other shells.

They possessed no outer fortifications, no hard shells of confidence; they had enough difficulty getting from day to day, let alone having an awful crime thrust upon them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carapace - the hard upper shell of a turtle, crustacean, or arachnid
  • Synonym: exoskeleton - external skeleton that supports and protects an animal's body

Etymology

Old English *scell*, *scyll*; related to Dutch *schel* and German *Schale*.

2. NOUN

/ ʃɛl / (AmE), / ʃɛl / (BrE)
The hard outer covering or case of certain animals, such as arthropods, turtles, and some mollusks, that protects their bodies.

Examples

And the cast shell of a locust, straw colored and transparent, weighing nothing, fragile but entire, with eyes like bubbles and a gaping slit down its back.
Every morning early, in the summer, we searched the trunks of the trees as high as we could reach for the locust shells, carefully detached their hooked claws from the bark where they hung, and stabled them, a weird faery herd, in an angle between the high roots of the tulip tree, where no grass grew in the dense shade.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: carapace - the hard upper shell of a turtle, crustacean, or arachnid

Etymology

Old English *scell*, from Proto-Germanic *skaljo*.

3. NOUN

/ ʃɛl / (AmE), / ʃɛl / (BrE)
an explosive projectile consisting of a cylindrical metal casing filled with an explosive charge, fired from a large gun or cannon

Examples

He put the shells on the table, as though he could no longer bear to hold them.

Etymology

From Old English *scealu* (shell, husk, pod), later extended to the meaning of an explosive projectile in the 19th century.
