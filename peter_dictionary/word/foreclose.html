foreclose

1. VERB

/ fɔrˈkloʊz / (AmE), / fɔːˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to take legal action to take possession of a mortgaged property when the borrower fails to keep up payments; to deprive someone of the right to redeem a mortgage.

Examples

For less than a dozen miles from the unplowed land of the dead man lived another settler who had ignored the warnings that his existence might be foreclosed on - a blatant and defiant rustler named Fred Powell.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repossess - to take back possession of property, especially for failure to pay money owed

Etymology

From Old French 'forclos', past participle of 'forclore' (to exclude), from Latin 'foris' (outside) + 'claudere' (to close).

2. VERB

/ fɔrˈkloʊz / (AmE), / fɔːˈkləʊz / (BrE)
To prevent something from happening; to make impossible.

Examples

The shadows of the trees engulfed her, foreclosing every possible exit from the grove.

Synset Examples

My sense of tact forbids an honest answer
Your role in the projects precludes your involvement in the competitive project

Related Terms

  • Synonym: preclude - to prevent something from happening; to make impossible
  • Synonym: prevent - to stop something from happening or existing

Etymology

From Old French *forclos*, from *forclore* (to exclude), from Latin *foris* (outside) + *claudere* (to close).
