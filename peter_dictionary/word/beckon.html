beckon

1. VERB

/ ˈbɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈbɛk.ən / (BrE)
to make a gesture with the hand, head, or arm to signal someone to come closer or follow

Examples

Religion at its best is out in front, ever beckoning and leading on, and, as Lippman put it, "mobilizing all man's scattered energies in one triumphant sense of his own infinite importance ".
He beckoned to her from the door and she slipped quietly outside.
Now you go outside and beckon me when it's safe ".
The hall was empty and Alex beckoned; they climbed the stairs which creaked, very loudly to their sensitive ears, and reached the next floor.
"Take hands, this urgent visage beckons us ".

Synset Examples

She waved to her friends
He waved his hand hospitably

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wave - move one's hand to and fro as a signal or greeting
  • Synonym: gesture - make a movement, especially of the hand or head, to express an idea or meaning

Etymology

From Old English 'beceon' (to beckon, make a sign), related to 'beck' (a gesture) and Middle Dutch 'bekenen'.

2. VERB

/ ˈbɛk.ən / (AmE), / ˈbɛk.ən / (BrE)
(of a place, opportunity, or thing) to appear inviting or attractive; to seem to call someone toward it.

Examples

The great world beyond beckons.

Synset Examples

The shop window decorations beckoned

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lure - to attract or tempt by offering pleasure or advantage
  • Synonym: invite - to encourage someone to come or participate
  • Antonym: repel - to drive or force back; to be unattractive

Etymology

From Old English *beċnian* (to beckon, signal).
