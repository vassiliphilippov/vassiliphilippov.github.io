recruit

1. NOUN

/ rɪˈkrut / (AmE), / rɪˈkruːt / (BrE)
A person who has recently joined the armed forces and is undergoing initial training.

Examples

It is they, of course, who keep it alive and preserve it so the same spirit will continue to be present in the Corps for future recruits to find as they come into it.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: veteran - a person with long experience in the armed forces

Etymology

From French *recrute*, from Old French *recru* (exhausted, worn out), past participle of *recruire*, from Latin *recrescere* (to grow again).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈkrut / (AmE), / rɪˈkruːt / (BrE)
to enlist or assemble people, especially for military service or similar organizations

Examples

Maj. Anderson of Fort Sumter is home and recruiting volunteers for the U.S. Army.
In the first place there is the obvious problem of recruiting high caliber personnel.
They recruit spics, they **recruit** niggers.

Synset Examples

raise an army
recruit new soldiers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: enlist - to enroll or be enrolled in the armed services

Etymology

From French *recruter*, from obsolete French *recrue* (fresh supply of troops).

3. VERB

/ rɪˈkrut / (AmE), / rɪˈkruːt / (BrE)
to find and persuade people to join a company, organization, or group, especially to fill a job or role

Examples

He had obtained and provisioned a veteran ship called the Discovery and had recruited a crew of twenty-one, the largest he had ever commanded.
It is from this unpromising background that the fictional private detective was recruited.
But he has shown a sixth-sense ability to spot, recruit and excite able researchers, and has developed unexpected talents in fund raising and speech-making.

Synset Examples

The lab director recruited an able crew of assistants

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hire - to employ someone for a job
  • Synonym: enlist - to enroll or be enrolled in an organization or group, especially the armed forces

Etymology

From French *recruter*, from Middle French, from Old French *recroistre* (to grow again), from Latin *recrescere* (to grow again).
