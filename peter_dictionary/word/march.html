march

1. VERB

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
to walk in a regular, measured, and stately manner, typically as part of a procession or parade, especially by soldiers

Examples

This features the marching songs of several nations, recorded as though the various national bands were marching by your reviewing stand.
Thus, on the highroad, a troop of soldiers "marched in their own dust and sang ", while on the footpath one man walks alone.
They marched with bayonets fixed, and as fixed on their faces was anger, fear, and torment.
One moment, the road was filled with disciplined troops, marching four by four with a purpose as implacable as death; the next, a cloud of gun smoke covered a screaming fury of sound, out of which the redcoat soldiers emerged with their bayonets and their cursing fury.
Their only hope of survival was to hold to the road and keep marching.

Synset Examples

They processed into the dining room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parade - to move or march in a formal procession
  • Antonym: straggle - to move along slowly and without order, opposite of march in ranks

Etymology

From Middle French *marcher* (to walk, march), possibly from Old French *marcher* (to trample), of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
to make someone walk or proceed intentionally and in a noticeable or public way, often for display, inspection, or punishment

Examples

A week later the sentence of the Council was carried out: Jake Camaret and the woman were marched naked through the streets past a mocking populace.

Synset Examples

She parades her new husband around town

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parade - to walk or march in an ostentatious way

Etymology

From Middle French marcher (to walk, to march), from Old French marchier.

3. VERB

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
to walk with a regular, measured, and deliberate step, often in a procession or as part of a group, especially as a demonstration of discipline or purpose

Examples

That other Jew, a young man too, had left that greatcoat behind, in a rich house, and marched away.
He was marching up and down the locker room of the Grassy Brae Golf Club shouting, "Bomb Cuba!
Otherwise, UN will march blindly on to certain defeat.
They march you in, naked as the day you were born, skin burning and half-blind from that delousing shit they throw on you.

Synset Examples

He marched into the classroom and announced the exam
The soldiers marched across the border

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stride - to walk with long, decisive steps in a specified direction
  • Degree Lower: trudge - to walk slowly and with heavy steps, typically due to exhaustion or difficult conditions

Etymology

From Middle French *marcher* (to walk, tread) and Old French *marchier* (to walk, to trample), probably of Frankish origin.

4. NOUN

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
an organized procession in which a group of people walk together, often for a specific purpose such as a protest, celebration, or military display

Examples

Dangling his gaudy trinkets before them, he told of the Rebel losses in the mountains, at Cheat and Rich mountains both, and the Federal march on Beverly.

Synset Examples

the march went up Fifth Avenue

Related Terms

  • Synonym: parade - an organized public procession, often celebratory
  • Synonym: procession - a group of people moving forward in an orderly way

Etymology

From Middle French *marcher* (to walk, to march).

5. VERB

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
to force someone to walk or proceed somewhere, especially in a deliberate or controlled way

Examples

Now Pathet Lao propagandists were reported marching him barefoot from village to village, as evidence of evil American intervention.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: herd - to make people or animals move together as a group, usually by force

6. NOUN

/ mɑrtʃ / (AmE), / mɑːtʃ / (BrE)
A steady, continuous advance or progress, especially in a metaphorical sense such as time or events moving forward.

Examples

The person of the artist becomes a final bastion of meaning in a world rendered meaningless by the march of events and the decay of classical religious and philosophical systems.
With the universal list as his weapon, Swadesh has extended his march of conquest farther and farther into the past, eight, ten, twelve millennia back.

Synset Examples

the march of science
the march of time

Related Terms

  • Synonym: progress - forward or onward movement toward a destination, often in a metaphorical sense

Etymology

From Middle English marche, from Old French marchier (to walk, to march), of Germanic origin.
