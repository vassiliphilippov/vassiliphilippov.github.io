slip

1. NOUN

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
a small or unintentional mistake made, especially in speech, writing, or action

Examples

I am usually filled with an uneasiness that through some unwitting slip all hell may break loose.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mistake - an action or judgment that is wrong or incorrect
  • Degree Higher: blunder - a careless or serious mistake

2. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
(of standards, performance, or condition) to deteriorate or decline; to become worse or less effective.

Examples

According to his own testimony, he never actually saw things as shaped but only as generally amorphous "blots "of color of a more or less indefinite size; at their edges they slipped pretty much out of focus altogether.
On balance, prices of those bonds have slipped a bit since then.
Chancellor Adenauer's Christian Democratic Party slipped only a little in the voting but it was enough to lose the absolute Bundestag majority it has enjoyed since 1957.
Without a great acceleration in the metropolitan area's economy, there will not be sufficient jobs for the growing numbers of youngsters, and St. Louis will slip into second-class status.
Let's face it, with all due respect, the Don - rest in peace - was slipping.

Synset Examples

My grades are slipping

Related Terms

  • Antonym: improve - to make or become better
  • Synonym: decline - to become worse or decrease in quality

3. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
to move smoothly, quietly, or effortlessly, especially in or out of a place or position

Examples

Noticing Russell's horse in front of the long log building, he assumed his friend had slipped inside and would be able to put up a good fight, so he began working his way down the ditch to join him.
Keep the belt just tight enough so the pulleys won n't slip when pulled by hand; excess tension will only cause undue wear on the motor and spindle bearings.

Synset Examples

the bolt slipped into place
water slipped from the polished marble

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slide - move smoothly along a surface while maintaining contact with it
  • Synonym: glide - move smoothly and continuously with little noise or resistance

Etymology

From Middle English slippen, of uncertain origin; perhaps from Old English *slipan.

4. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
to move quietly, quickly, and often secretly, so that people do not notice

Examples

Stevie was constantly slipping into the church.
Then he slipped and went down on his hands and knees in the melting snow.
He beckoned to her from the door and she slipped quietly outside.
And once Bake slipped in, pale and drawn, last night's liquor still on his breath with some of today's added to it.
A woman with a small boy slipped in between them.

Synset Examples

The ship slipped away in the darkness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sneak - to move in a quiet, secret way
  • Synonym: creep - to move slowly and carefully to avoid being heard
  • Antonym: march - to walk quickly and with purpose, often noisily

Etymology

From Old English *slipan* (to slip, glide).

5. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
to put something somewhere quietly, quickly, or inconspicuously, often so that others do not notice

Examples

No one told on Ludie, not even when he slipped live grasshoppers into the mite-box.
The light supper over, Claire went to him and, slipping an arm about his shoulder, sat on his knee.
Rumor had it he slipped two small rocks under each victim's head as a sort of trademark.
When a dancer does well, she provokes a quiet bombardment of dollar bills - although the Manhattan clubs prohibit the more cosmopolitan practice of slipping the tips into the dancers' costumes.
Gavin slipped his arms around his chest and hugged him fiercely.

Synset Examples

He slipped some money into the waiter's hand

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sneak - to move, give, or put something secretly or without being noticed
  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else
  • Antonym: remove - to take something away or out

Etymology

From Old English *slīpan* (to slip, glide), related to Old Norse *slīpa* (to slip).

6. NOUN

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
a small mistake or error, especially one made in speech, writing, or behavior, often causing embarrassment or awkwardness

Examples

Journalism supplies us with an endless run of such slips.
Readers of the Reader's Digest are familiar with such items which often appear in its lists of verbal slips, like the ad in a California paper that advertised "House for rent.
This slip is so-called because its semi ambiguous English always seems to refer to a person's anatomy but never quite means what it seems to say.
It wit is a misnomer because it covers slips as well as wit.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gaffe - an unintentional act or remark causing embarrassment
  • Synonym: blunder - a careless or stupid mistake
  • Synonym: faux pas - a social blunder or slip

7. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
to move sideways or obliquely, especially in an uncontrolled or accidental way

Examples

The first one or two roof boards (marked "E "in fig. 6) are slipped into place across the roof beams, from outside the shelter.
Killpath leaned forward; his foot slipped off the chair and he put it back again, frowning now.
Others, badly wounded, gripped hands in manes, knees in bellies, held on as long as possible and then, weak from ghastly wounds, slipped sideways, slowly, almost thoughtfully, to be broken under the slashing hoofs.
hung on a hook on the wall, and underneath it I could see his tie, knotted, ready to be slipped over his head, a black badge of frayed respectability that ought never to have left his neck.
The mountainside grew steeper and she slipped once or twice on the smooth pine needles.

Synset Examples

the wheels skidded against the sidewalk

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skid - to slide, typically sideways or obliquely, in an uncontrolled way
  • Synonym: slide - to move smoothly along a surface while maintaining contact with it

Etymology

From Old English 'slīpan' (to slip, glide), related to Middle Low German and Middle Dutch 'slīpen'.

8. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
to make a small mistake, especially due to carelessness or a momentary lapse

Examples

But it was not easy for him and he often slipped.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blunder - to make a careless or serious mistake
  • Synonym: err - to make an error; be incorrect
  • Antonym: correct - to remove mistakes or make right

9. VERB

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
(of an object or body part) to move out of place or position, often accidentally or unintentionally.

Examples

Let your hand drop to your side and let the gun slip out.

Synset Examples

dislocate joints
the artificial hip joint luxated and had to be put back surgically

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dislocate - to move (a bone or joint) out of its normal position

10. NOUN

/ slɪp / (AmE), / slɪp / (BrE)
An accidental or careless misstep, especially one that may cause or threatens to cause a fall.

Examples

These two slipped on a banana peel.

Synset Examples

he blamed his slip on the ice
the jolt caused many slips and a few spills

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: fall - a sudden drop, often as a result of losing balance
  • Synonym: trip - an accidental, usually minor misstep causing imbalance

Etymology

From Old English slipian (to glide, slip).
