decay

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈkeɪ / (AmE), / dɪˈkeɪ / (BrE)
a gradual decrease in the magnitude of something, especially a stored charge, current, or signal, over time

Examples

This measurement was obtained by W. Blumberg of the University of California,Berkeley, by observing the breadth of the free induction decay signal.
Soon they will fight their way into the lower middle-class suburbs, and the churches will experience the same decay and rebuilding cycle which has characterized their history for a century.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deterioration - the process of becoming progressively worse
  • Synonym: decline - a gradual and continuous loss of strength, numbers, quality, or value

Etymology

From Old French *decair*, from Latin *decadere* (to fall down, decline).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈkeɪ / (AmE), / dɪˈkeɪ / (BrE)
the process of rotting or decomposition, particularly of organic matter

Examples

Dead fledgling birds, their squashed looking nakedness and the odor of decay that clung to the hand when they had been buried in our graveyard in front of the purple flags.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rot - the process of decomposition of organic matter
  • Synonym: putrefaction - the process of decay or rotting in a body or other organic matter
  • Antonym: preservation - the act of keeping something from decaying

Etymology

From Old French decair, from Latin decadere (to fall down, decay), from de- (down) + cadere (to fall).

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈkeɪ / (AmE), / dɪˈkeɪ / (BrE)
The process of gradually declining in quality, strength, or condition.

Examples

The person of the artist becomes a final bastion of meaning in a world rendered meaningless by the march of events and the decay of classical religious and philosophical systems.
Without the decay of a sense of objective reference (except as the imitation of mystery), the stress on subjective invention would never have been stimulated into being.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deterioration - the process of becoming worse
  • Antonym: improvement - the process of becoming better

Etymology

From Old French *decai* (a decline), from the verb *dechaoir*, from Latin *decadere* (to fall off).

4. VERB

/ dɪˈkeɪ / (AmE), / dɪˈkeɪ / (BrE)
(of a stored charge, magnetic flux, or current) to decrease gradually and eventually disappear; to lose strength or intensity over time, especially in a physical or technical context.

Examples

The radioactivity of fallout decays rapidly at first.
There will come a time in a basement shelter when the radiation has decayed enough to allow use of the whole basement.
She had the feeling that, under the mouldering leaves, there would be the bodies of dead animals, quietly decaying and giving their soil back to the mountain.

Synset Examples

the particles disintegrated during the nuclear fission process

Related Terms

  • Synonym: discharge - to release or lose stored electrical energy

Etymology

From Old French 'decair', from Latin 'decadere' (to fall, sink, deteriorate).

5. VERB

/ dɪˈkeɪ / (AmE), / dɪˈkeɪ / (BrE)
to gradually be destroyed by a natural process; to fall into ruin or deteriorate over time

Examples

Parents are often concerned that orthodontic appliances may cause teeth to decay.
In turn the other teeth are likely to decay because food particles may become impacted in them.

Synset Examples

The unoccupied house started to decay

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rot - to decompose or break down due to natural processes
  • Synonym: disintegrate - to break up into small parts as a result of decay or decomposition

Etymology

From Old French decair, from Latin decadere, meaning 'to fall down, sink'.
