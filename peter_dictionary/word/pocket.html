pocket

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɒkɪt / (BrE)
a supply of money available for spending, often used in reference to a person's financial resources

Examples

Rep. Charles E. Hughes of Sherman, sponsor of the bill, said a failure to enact it would "amount to making a gift out of the taxpayers' pockets to banks, insurance and pipeline companies ".
Because they prepare their own meals they also keep in their pockets a good portion of that $ 10.50 food bill along with most of the tip money.
And at the end of the year, you figure the tax wrong, you pay them out of your own pocket.

Synset Examples

they dipped into the taxpayers' pockets

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wallet - a flat case for carrying money and cards

Etymology

From Old Northern French *poket*, diminutive of *poke* (bag, sack).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɒkɪt / (BrE)
a small enclosed or isolated area, especially one that is different from its surroundings

Examples

He does not know whether to look up or look aside, to put his hands in his pockets or to clench them at his side, to cross the street, or to continue on the same side.
It was the end of the afternoon when he took the huge key out of his pocket and inserted it into the keyhole.

Synset Examples

the trapped miners found a pocket of air

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cavity - a hollow space within a solid object
  • Synonym: pouch - a small bag or sack; small enclosed area
  • Synonym: chamber - an enclosed space or compartment

Etymology

From Old French *poque* (bag, pouch), from Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ ˈpɑːkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɒkɪt / (BrE)
a small pouch sewn into or on clothing, used for carrying small items

Examples

He, too, cocked his cap at a jaunty angle, jingled marbles in his pocket, and swaggered down Main Street.
He always seemed to have money in his pocket.
He worked standing, with his left hand in his pocket as though he were merely stopping for a moment, sketching with the surprised stare of one who was watching another person's hand.
"Right in my pocket ".
"I reached into that funny little pocket that is high up on my dress.

Etymology

From Middle English poket, a diminutive of 'poke' meaning bag, from Old French poque.

4. VERB

/ ˈpɑːkɪt / (AmE), / ˈpɒkɪt / (BrE)
to put (something) into one's pocket, especially quickly or discreetly

Examples

When these had been pocketed, we could still spend a morning cracking open other pebbles for our delight in seeing how much prettier they were inside than their dull exteriors indicated.

Synset Examples

He pocketed the change

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slip - to put quickly or quietly somewhere

Etymology

From Middle English poket, diminutive of pok (bag, pouch), of Germanic origin.
