discredit

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (AmE), / dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (BrE)
Loss or lack of respect or reputation; the state of being held in low esteem.

Examples

And his belief is not to his discredit.

Synset Examples

your actions will bring discredit to your name
because of the scandal the school has fallen into disrepute

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disrepute - the state of being in low esteem by the public
  • Antonym: honor - high respect; esteem
  • Antonym: credit - public acknowledgment or praise for an achievement

Etymology

From Latin 'discredere', from 'dis-' (opposite of) + 'credere' (to believe).

2. VERB

/ dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (AmE), / dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (BrE)
to cause people to stop believing or trusting someone or something; to harm the reputation or credibility of someone or something

Examples

We are left helpless to cope with it because we do not dare speak of it as anything real for fear that to do so would imply a commitment to that which has already been discredited and proved false.

Synset Examples

The paper discredited the politician with its nasty commentary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disprove - to show that something is not true or valid
  • Antonym: vindicate - to clear someone or something of blame or suspicion

Etymology

From Latin 'discredere', from 'dis-' (not) + 'credere' (to believe).

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (AmE), / dɪsˈkrɛdɪt / (BrE)
to harm the reputation or credibility of someone or something; to cause people to stop respecting or trusting them

Examples

It would be a shame, Mr. Chairman, if we allowed a few rotten apples to give a bad name to the whole barrel.

Synset Examples

This newspaper story discredits the politicians

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defame - to damage the good reputation of someone
  • Synonym: slander - to make false spoken statements that damage someone's reputation
  • Antonym: vindicate - to clear someone of blame or suspicion

Etymology

From Latin 'discredere', from 'dis-' (expressing reversal) + 'credere' (to believe).
