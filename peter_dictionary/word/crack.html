crack

1. VERB

/ kræk / (AmE), / kræk / (BrE)
to hit something or someone forcefully, typically producing a sharp cracking noise

Examples

Dick McAuliffe cracked the first of his two doubles against Lefty Don Rudolph to open the Bear's attack.
She had taken him out of the schoolhouse and closed the school for the summer, after she saw Miss Snow crack Joel across the face with a ruler for letting a snake loose in the schoolroom.

Synset Examples

The teacher cracked him across the face with a ruler

Related Terms

  • Synonym: whack - to strike forcefully with a sharp blow
  • Synonym: slap - to hit quickly with the flat part of the hand

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English 'cracken', from Old English 'cracian' (to resound).

2. VERB

/ kræk / (AmE), / kræk / (BrE)
to make a sudden sharp or snapping sound

Examples

From above me and somewhere behind me, a rifle cracked.
They answered him in monosyllables, nods, occasionally muttering in Greek to one another, awaiting the word from Papa, who restlessly cracked his knuckles, anxious to stuff himself into his white Cadillac and burst off to the freeway.

Synset Examples

his fingers snapped

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snap - to make a sudden, sharp cracking sound

Etymology

From Middle English crakken, from Old English cracian (make a sharp noise, resound), of imitative origin.

3. NOUN

/ kræk / (AmE), / kræk / (BrE)
A narrow opening, typically one appearing between two surfaces or through something broken.

Examples

Sure you can, if you know how the system works, where the cracks are.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gap - an opening or space between two things
  • Synonym: fissure - a long, narrow opening or line of breakage
  • Synonym: crevice - a narrow opening, especially in rock or a wall

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'crakken', related to Old English 'cracian' (to resound, crack).
