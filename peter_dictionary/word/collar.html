collar

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑlər / (AmE), / ˈkɒlə / (BrE)
A band of material around the neck of a shirt, dress, coat, or similar garment, usually folded over.

Examples

In a lacy open weave shoes have a luster finish, braided collar and bow highlight on the squared throat.
A tall lady, with a ruffled collar very low on her bosom, turned insolent green eyes upon him.
He gave Mr. Jack a real going over; he brushed his shoulders and his back and his collar with long, firm strokes.
He was puffing on a cigar, and he was turning up his coat collar against the rain.
Antoine got a little hot under the collar.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cuff - the end part of a sleeve, where the material of the sleeve is turned back or a separate band is sewn on

Etymology

From Old French *coler*, from Latin *collāre* (neck band), from *collum* (neck).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑːlɚ / (AmE), / ˈkɒlə / (BrE)
(informal) To arrest or capture someone, especially a suspect or criminal.

Examples

He fought down the impulse to rush in and collar the vicious puncher on the spot.

Synset Examples

the police nabbed the suspected criminals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nab - to catch or seize quickly, especially by surprise
  • Synonym: arrest - to take someone into custody by legal authority
  • Antonym: release - to set someone free from custody

Etymology

Originally from the noun 'collar,' referring to grabbing someone by the collar when apprehending them.
