cradle

1. NOUN

/ ˈkreɪ.dl̩ / (AmE), / ˈkreɪ.dl̩ / (BrE)
A place where something begins, develops, or is nurtured in its early stages.

Examples

But barring a miracle, and do n't hold your breath for it, Chicago will not see the Leningrad-Kirov Ballet, which stems from the ballet cradle of the Maryinsky and is one of the great companies of the world.

Synset Examples

the birthplace of civilization

Related Terms

  • Synonym: birthplace - the place where something originates

Etymology

From Old English *cradol* (little bed or cot), of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkreɪ.dəl / (AmE), / ˈkreɪ.dl̩ / (BrE)
A small bed for an infant, typically on rockers or with curved legs so that it can be rocked gently.

Examples

In the room next to theirs was a huge cradle, of mahogany, ornately carved and decorated with gold leaf.
It was the most important-looking cradle he had ever seen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crib - a small bed with high sides for a baby or young child

Etymology

From Old English 'cradol' (cradle, manger), related to Proto-Germanic *kradulō.

3. VERB

/ ˈkreɪ.dəl / (AmE), / ˈkreɪ.dl̩ / (BrE)
to hold something or someone gently and protectively, often in the arms

Examples

Alex entered first and was followed by the doctor who, for all his care, manifested a perceptible bulge on his left side where the hen was cradled.
We all know what the dummies will do "- he gestured at the hummocks in which the decorticated men were cradled - "and we all know what the new people will ask.
Oso slept unconcernedly, his rifle cradled in his arms; I did n't catch a wink.

Synset Examples

He cradles the child in his arms

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cuddle - hold (someone or something) close in one's arms as a way of showing love or affection

Etymology

From Old English 'cradol' (little bed, cradle), of Germanic origin.
