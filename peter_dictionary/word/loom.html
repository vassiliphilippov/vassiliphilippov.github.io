loom

1. VERB

/ lum / (AmE), / luːm / (BrE)
(of something bad or threatening) to seem likely to happen soon and cause worry; to appear large and frightening, often in a vague or indistinct way.

Examples

Lastly, governmental and private planners will at this stage begin to see large capital requirements looming ahead.
But in the prospect of winning the battle loomed the specter of losing a costlier war.
"President Kennedy once again interpreted the Soviet proposals, to sign a peace treaty with Germany as a threat, as part of the world menace allegedly looming over the countries of capitalism.

Synset Examples

The terrible vision brooded over her all day long

Related Terms

  • Synonym: threaten - to state an intention to cause harm or to be imminent in a menacing way
  • Degree Lower: approach - to come near in time or space (neutral connotation)

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Middle English 'lome' (tool), but application to threatening appearance is first recorded in the 16th century.

2. VERB

/ lum / (AmE), / luːm / (BrE)
To appear as a large, often indistinct or threatening shape, especially in a way that dominates or overshadows surroundings.

Examples

Looming over all, Papa-san leered down at him, threatening a hundred hidden malevolencies.
Before Payne loomed the Old Clubhouse, Seward's home, where Key had once been killed.
The Gap looming before him - the place where had confronted Jack English on that day so many years ago - was his exit from all that had meaning to him.

Synset Examples

The huge sculpture predominates over the fountain
Large shadows loomed on the canyon wall

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tower - rise to a great height above surroundings
  • Antonym: diminish - become less in size, importance, or intensity

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse *lúma* (to lurk) and dialectal Swedish *loma* (to move slowly).

3. NOUN

/ lum / (AmE), / luːm / (BrE)
a machine used for weaving yarn or thread into fabric.

Examples

Shy, slender-waisted girls at the loom in harmony with the frangipani by the wayside.

Etymology

Old English *geloma* (tool), later shortened to 'loom'.

4. VERB

/ lum / (AmE), / luːm / (BrE)
to appear as a large, indistinct, and often threatening shape, especially coming into view gradually or appearing to threaten

Examples

Fantastic colors are to be seen in the fanciful formations of eroded rock which loom out of the semiarid country in both parks.
Compulsory retirement at sixty-five looms on our horizon.
Now, looming on the horizon are such things as estimated totals, calculated risks and I. B. M. machines.
In my sights I watched him looming bigger and bigger.

Synset Examples

Another air plane loomed into the sky

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emerge - to come out or appear from somewhere
  • Antonym: vanish - to disappear suddenly or completely

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Middle Dutch *lomen* (to move slowly).
