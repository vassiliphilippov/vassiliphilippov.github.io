dramatize

1. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to present or represent something in a vivid, theatrical, or exaggerated manner; to make something appear more dramatic than it really is

Examples

These incidents, typical of many others, dramatize the distressing fact that no controversy during the last several decades has caused more tension, rancor and strife among religious groups in this country than the birth-control issue.

Synset Examples

These events dramatize the lack of social responsibility among today's youth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exaggerate - to make something seem larger or more important than it is
  • Antonym: understate - to describe or represent something as being smaller or less important than it actually is

Etymology

From Greek drama (action, play) via French dramatiser.

2. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
to adapt (a story, event, or situation) into a dramatic form, such as a play, screenplay, or performance

Examples

Without saying or seeming to say that in portraying the Sartoris and the Compson families Faulkner's chief concern is social criticism, we can say nevertheless that through those families he dramatizes his comment on the planter dynasties as they have existed since the decades before the Civil War.

Synset Examples

adopt a book for a screenplay

Related Terms

  • Synonym: adapt - to make something suitable for a new use or purpose

Etymology

From Greek *dramatizein*, from *drama* (action, play).

3. VERB

/ ˈdræməˌtaɪz / (AmE), / ˈdræmətaɪz / (BrE)
To make something more vivid, interesting, or exciting by adding noticeable or theatrical details, often to attract attention or stir emotions.

Examples

It may be true that pool lighting dramatizes an evening scene, but lights also attract all the insect life for miles around.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embellish - to make (something) more attractive by the addition of decorative details or features
  • Synonym: exaggerate - to represent (something) as being larger, better, or worse than it really is
  • Antonym: understate - to present or describe (something) as being smaller or less important than it actually is

Etymology

From Greek drama (action, play) via French dramatiser.
