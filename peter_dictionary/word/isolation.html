isolation

1. NOUN

/ ˌaɪ.səˈleɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌaɪ.səˈleɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
The state of being separated from others, either physically or socially.

Examples

This man's isolation is not merely momentary, it is permanent.
A variety of techniques have been directed toward the isolation and study of blood group antibodies.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seclusion - the state of being private and away from other people
  • Antonym: integration - the action of combining and mixing with others

Etymology

From French *isolation*, from Italian *isolare* (to isolate), from Latin *insula* (island).

2. NOUN

/ ˌaɪsəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌaɪsəˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of separating something or someone from others; the state of being set apart

Examples

The Kremlin's goal is the isolation and capture, not of Ghana, but of the United States - and this purpose may be served very well by countries that masquerade under a "neutralist "mask, yet in fact are dependable auxiliaries of the Soviet Foreign Office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: separation - the action or state of moving or being moved apart
  • Antonym: integration - the action of combining or adding parts to make a unified whole

Etymology

From French 'isolation', from 'isoler' (to isolate), from Italian 'isolare', from 'isola' (island), from Latin 'insula'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌaɪsəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌaɪsəˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
the feeling of being alone and unwanted by others

Examples

More profound and more disturbing, however, is the moral isolation of Raymond Chandler's Philip Marlowe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: loneliness - the state of feeling sad because one has no friends or company

Etymology

From French 'isolation', from 'isoler' (to isolate), from Italian 'isolare', from 'isola' (island), from Latin 'insula'.
