regret

1. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
to feel sorry or sad about something that has happened or that you have done; to experience remorse or contrition

Examples

I, for one, rather regret that Schnabel did n't collaborate with the Budapest Quartet, whose rugged, athletic playing was a good deal closer to this pianist's interpretative outlook than the style of the Belgian group.
And no doubt many people in states like the Carolinas and Georgia, which were among the most Tory in sentiment in the eighteenth century, bitterly regretted the revolt against the Crown.
Madden regretted not being able to find fault with so true a statement.
Jones said he regretted Hughes had made a personal attack on a past president.
I regret that Bertha Madeira [ today's "Voice "] obtained incorrect information.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: repent - to feel or express sincere regret or remorse about one's wrongdoing or sin
  • Synonym: remorse - deep regret or guilt for a wrong committed
  • Antonym: rejoice - to feel or show great joy or delight

Etymology

From Old French *regreter* (to bewail the dead), from re- (expressing intensive force) + *greter* (to weep, cry).

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
A feeling of sadness or disappointment about something that has happened or been done, especially a loss or a mistake.

Examples

Like Mrs. Dalloway, with her regrets about Peter Walsh, he had his moments of melancholy over a youth too well spent.
There would still be plenty of moments of regret and sadness and guilty relief.
There's not a day goes by I don't feel regret.

Synset Examples

he drank to drown his sorrows
he wrote a note expressing his regret
to his rue, the error cost him the game

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remorse - deep regret or guilt for a wrong committed
  • Synonym: sorrow - a feeling of deep distress caused by loss, disappointment, or other misfortune
  • Antonym: rejoicing - great joy; the opposite feeling of sadness or regret

Etymology

From Old French *regret* (remorse, sorrow), from *regreter* (to bewail), of Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
To express something with regret, especially in formal statements, often used when delivering bad news.

Examples

"Dearly beloved ", he preached, "unless you repent of your sins in a measure, and become converted to a degree, you will, I regret to say, be damned to a more or less extent ".

Synset Examples

I regret to say that you did not gain admission to Harvard

Related Terms

  • Synonym: apologize - to express regret for an action or statement

Etymology

From Old French 'regreter' (to bewail the dead), from 're-' (again) + 'greter' (to weep, lament).

4. VERB

/ rɪˈɡrɛt / (AmE), / rɪˈɡrɛt / (BrE)
To feel sad or disappointed because something is lost or absent.

Examples

In recent days there have been extensive lamentations over the absence of original drama on television, but not for years have many regretted the passing of new plays on radio.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: miss - feel the absence of someone or something
  • Synonym: mourn - feel or show sorrow for the loss of something or someone

Etymology

From Old French *regreter* (to bewail the dead), from *re-* (expressing intensive force) + *greter* (to weep, lament).
