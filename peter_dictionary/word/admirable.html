admirable

1. ADJ

/ ˈæd.mə.rə.bəl / (AmE), / ˈæd.mɪ.rə.bəl / (BrE)
deserving respect or approval; inspiring admiration

Examples

Life is further characterized, in antithesis to Piepsam, as animal: the image of a dog, which appears at several places, is first given as the criterion of amiable, irrelevant interest aroused by life considered simply as a spectacle: a dog in a wagon is "admirable ", "a pleasure to contemplate "; another wagon has no dog, and therefore is "devoid of interest ".

Synset Examples

among her many admirable qualities are generosity and graciousness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: praiseworthy - deserving praise or commendation
  • Synonym: commendable - deserving praise
  • Antonym: deplorable - deserving strong condemnation; shockingly bad

Etymology

From Latin 'admirabilis' (wonderful), from 'admirari' (to admire).

2. ADJ

/ ˈæd.mə.rə.bəl / (AmE), / ˈæd.mɪ.rə.bəl / (BrE)
deserving respect or approval; worthy of admiration

Examples

But they deny the values of the city - the crowded, competitive, tolerant city, the "melting pot "which gave off so many of the most admirable American qualities.
This was delivered in a forthright way, without coyness and over pretended interest - an admirable way with children.
William R. Stillwell, an admirable Georgian whose delightful correspondence is preserved in the Georgia Department of Archives and History, liked to tease his wife in his letters.

Synset Examples

an estimable young professor
trains ran with admirable precision
his taste was impeccable, his health admirable

Related Terms

  • Synonym: estimable - worthy of respect; deserving esteem
  • Synonym: praiseworthy - deserving praise or approval
  • Antonym: deplorable - deserving strong condemnation; very bad

Etymology

From Latin 'admirabilis' (wonderful, admirable), from 'admirari' (to admire).
