bleed

1. VERB

/ bliːd / (AmE), / bliːd / (BrE)
to lose blood from the body as a result of injury or illness

Examples

Rathbone said he was bleeding to death.
Before him, mutilated, bleeding to death, they lay.
As might be expected, girls in this situation bleed very little and perhaps not at all in the process of losing their virginity.
The hotel staff, as well as residents of the Excelsior, told us they saw that both ladies were bleeding from scratches as they were seen fleeing down the hall.
It was pitiful to see the thin ranks of warriors, old and young, wheeling and twisting their ponies frantically from side to side only to be tumbled bleeding from their saddles by the relentless slam, slam of the cruelly efficient Hawkinses.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hemorrhage - to lose blood profusely, especially internally or as a medical condition

Etymology

Old English 'blēdan', from 'blōd' (blood).

2. VERB

/ bliːd / (AmE), / bliːd / (BrE)
to draw blood from a person, especially as a medical treatment; to cause (someone) to lose blood, typically by incision or other means

Examples

Tibet may bleed China as Algeria is bleeding France.
Tibet may bleed China as Algeria is bleeding France.

Synset Examples

In the old days, doctors routinely bled patients as part of the treatment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: phlebotomize - to draw blood from (medically)

Etymology

Old English *blēdan*, from *blōd* (blood).
