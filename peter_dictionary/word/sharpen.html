sharpen

1. VERB

/ ˈʃɑːr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to make (an image, sound, or idea) clearer or more focused

Examples

Perhaps one way to sharpen our sense of the modernity of Utopian communism is to contrast it with the principal earlier types of communistic theory.

Synset Examples

Please focus the image; we cannot enjoy the movie

Related Terms

  • Antonym: blur - to make unclear or less distinct

Etymology

From Old English scearpian, from 'scearp' (sharp).

2. VERB

/ ˈʃɑr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to make (something such as an argument, impression, or sense) more crisp, clear, or precise

Examples

My curiosity was sharpened a day or two before the interview by a conversation I had with a well-informed teacher of literature, a Jesuit father, at a conference on religious drama near Paris.

Synset Examples

We had to sharpen our arguments

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clarify - to make something more clear or easier to understand
  • Antonym: blunt - to make less sharp or less effective

Etymology

From sharp + -en, originally meaning to make sharp (in physical sense), later extended to abstract senses.

3. VERB

/ ˈʃɑːr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to become sharp or sharper, either literally or figuratively

Examples

Her chin sharpened.

Synset Examples

The debate sharpened

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dull - to become less sharp

Etymology

From sharp + -en (verb-forming suffix).

4. VERB

/ ˈʃɑːr.pən / (AmE), / ˈʃɑː.pən / (BrE)
to make or become sharp or sharper

Examples

An African knife and battle-ax are at this moment being sharpened by McKinley and Green.

Synset Examples

sharpen the knives

Related Terms

  • Antonym: dull - make or become less sharp

Etymology

From sharp + -en (Old English scearpian, to make sharp).
