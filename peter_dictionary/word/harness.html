harness

1. NOUN

/ ˈhɑrnɪs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːnɪs / (BrE)
A set of leather straps and fittings placed on a draft animal, such as a horse, to enable it to pull a cart or other load.

Examples

He stood on the porch and watched him struggling with the heavy harness, and finally went over to help him.

Etymology

From Old French 'harneis' (equipment), of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈhɑrnɪs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːnɪs / (BrE)
to control and make use of natural resources, energy, or other forces for a practical purpose

Examples

Particularly was this true when the norms previously applied were no longer satisfactory to many, when customs were rapidly changing as the forces of the new productivity were harnessed.

Synset Examples

harness natural forces and resources

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exploit - make full use of and derive benefit from (a resource)
  • Synonym: utilize - make practical and effective use of

Etymology

From Old French *harnois* (equipment), of Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ ˈhɑrnəs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːnɪs / (BrE)
to put a harness on (an animal, especially a horse) in order to attach it to a vehicle or implement.

Examples

Harnessing a team to a buckboard, they drove out to a willow lined creek about a half-mile off, then climbed down and began chopping.
The first time he was harnessed he stood like a gentle old mare; the crupper under his tail seemed to be old stuff.

Synset Examples

harness the horse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yoke - to put a yoke on; to couple (animals)

Etymology

From Old French 'harneis', of Germanic origin.

4. NOUN

/ ˈhɑrnəs / (AmE), / ˈhɑːnɪs / (BrE)
A set of straps or fittings by which a person or object is secured to something, often worn around the body to attach equipment or provide safety.

Examples

"I 've got her as neat as I can ", Donovan said, as he dropped the straps of the Seton harness over Greg's shoulders.
There I got my Colt Special and shoulder harness, slipped my coat on, and went back into the front room.

Etymology

From Old French *harneis* (equipment), of Germanic origin.
