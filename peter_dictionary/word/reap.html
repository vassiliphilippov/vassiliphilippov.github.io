reap

1. VERB

/ rip / (AmE), / riːp / (BrE)
to cut and gather a crop or harvest; to obtain or receive as a result of one's own or others' actions

Examples

As it is, they consider that the North is now reaping the fruits of excess egalitarianism, that in spite of its high standard of living the "American way "has been proved inferior to the English and Scandinavian ways, although they disapprove of the socialistic features of the latter.
Amy had to be safe, had to come back to them - if only to reap that share of life's experiences that were her due, if only to give her parents another chance to do better by her.
Present conservation practices regard wildlife, not as an expendable natural resource, but as an annual harvest to be sown and also reaped.

Synset Examples

harvest the grapes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harvest - gather a crop; bring in a harvest

Etymology

Old English *reopan* (to reap, harvest), related to Old High German *rioban* (to rob, strip).

2. VERB

/ riːp / (AmE), / riːp / (BrE)
to receive or obtain (something), especially as a result of one’s own actions or efforts

Examples

Being a teacher of American literature, I remembered Whittier's "Massachusetts to Virginia ", where he said: "But that one dark loathsome burden ye must stagger with alone, And reap the bitter harvest which ye yourselves have sown ".

Synset Examples

He drew great benefits from his membership in the association

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obtain - to get or acquire something
  • Synonym: derive - to receive or obtain from a source
  • Antonym: suffer - to experience or undergo something bad as a result of actions

Etymology

Old English *rīpan* (to reap, gather a harvest).
