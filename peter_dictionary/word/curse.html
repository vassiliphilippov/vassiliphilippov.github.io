curse

1. NOUN

/ kɝs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
A solemn utterance intended to invoke a supernatural power to inflict harm or punishment on someone or something.

Examples

On the street outside, Hieronymus envisions a holocaust of the vanities of this world, such a burning of artistic and erotic productions as his namesake actually brought to pass in Florence, and prophetically he issues his curse: "Gladius Dei super terram cito et velociter ".

Related Terms

  • Antonym: blessing - a prayer or invocation for divine favor and protection

Etymology

From Old English curs, of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ kɜrs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
to use offensive or profane language; to utter obscenities or profanities

Examples

He was promised that no harm would befall him if he would come out, but he cursed and replied that he would shoot any man coming near the door.
John would curse.
Bake cursed.
Then they let out a bellow of anguish and rage and, cursing, screaming and hollering "Garryowen "! they charged into the enemy like wild men.
Jess cursed again, and entered the barn.

Synset Examples

The drunken men were cursing loudly in the street

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swear - to use offensive or profane language
  • Synonym: blaspheme - to speak irreverently about religious things

Etymology

From Old English 'curs' (a prayer that harm may befall someone), of uncertain origin.

3. VERB

/ kɝs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
to utter offensive, profane, or obscene words, especially in anger

Examples

When they stood about his tent, chaffing each other, exchanging their obscenities, cursing command or weather, he had studied their faces.
I heard Pa cursing all the way downstairs.
When he was going to town, nothing was good enough - he had cursed at Winston once for leaving a fleck of polish on his shoelace.
He had cursed at them and threatened them.
Cursing himself for having ridden out the last few days without a rifle in his saddle boot, Russ drew his Colt and examined it briefly.

Synset Examples

The taxi driver who felt he didn't get a high enough tip cursed the passenger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swear - to use offensive or profane language

Etymology

From Old English cursian, related to curse (noun) of obscure origin.

4. VERB

/ kɝs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
to wish harm or misfortune upon someone; to invoke evil or supernatural powers to bring harm

Examples

In blind panic of grief she accepted Jonathan's dictum, and believed in her desperation that she had been cursed by God.
Grabski sat in a sweat saturated undershirt, cursing the excessive heat which clamped an uneasy stillness before sundown.

Synset Examples

The bad witch cursed the child

Related Terms

  • Antonym: bless - to invoke or wish good upon someone
  • Synonym: damn - to condemn to misfortune or punishment

Etymology

From Old English curs (a prayer that misfortune fall upon), origin uncertain.

5. NOUN

/ kɝs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
An evil spell or supernatural power believed to cause harm or misfortune.

Examples

After a minute he went on, "People must think the curse is on me, seeing you fresh as an apple and me old and gray ".

Synset Examples

a witch put a curse on his whole family
he put the whammy on me

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hex - an evil spell or curse, especially in folklore
  • Antonym: blessing - a prayer or act that brings good fortune; opposite of a curse

Etymology

From Old English curs, of uncertain origin; possibly related to Old French curuz (anger).

6. NOUN

/ kɝs / (AmE), / kɜːs / (BrE)
A profane or obscene word or phrase used to express anger or surprise.

Examples

Presently he heard footsteps crossing the yard, and Jess's smothered curses.

Synset Examples

expletives were deleted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expletive - an obscene or profane word or phrase
  • Synonym: swear word - word or phrase considered rude or offensive
  • Antonym: blessing - a prayer asking for divine favor (antonym in the sense of speaking positive words)

Etymology

From Old English 'curs', of uncertain origin.
