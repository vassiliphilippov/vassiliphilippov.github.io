slide

1. NOUN

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
(Music) A rapid, continuous movement up or down in pitch along the musical scale, often produced on string or brass instruments.

Examples

The violinist, in particular, is very indulgent with swoops and slides, and his tone is pinched and edgy.

Synset Examples

the violinist was indulgent with his swoops and slides

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glissando - a continuous slide upward or downward between two notes
  • Synonym: portamento - a smooth, continuous movement from one note to another

2. NOUN

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
a small, flat rectangular piece of glass used to mount specimens for examination under a microscope

Examples

The sections were mounted on cold slides smeared with Haupts' adhesive (Johansen, 1940) in earlier experiments, and in later experiments with a different mixture of the same components reported by Schramm and Rottger (1959).
The sections were then thawed by placing a finger under the slide and dried under a fan for 30 minutes; until used they were stored for as long as 2 weeks.

3. NOUN

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
A piece of playground equipment consisting of a smooth sloping surface that children climb up and slide down for fun.

Examples

The mouth was thin lipped and wide, the long cleft in the upper lip like a slide.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chute - a sloping channel or slide for conveying things to a lower level

4. VERB

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
to move smoothly, quietly, or unobtrusively along a surface; to pass or progress in a continuous, gliding motion

Examples

He had crossed the river which now, beyond the woods yonder, was sliding darkly under the mist.
The covers slid down his skinny neck so I saw his head, fuzzed like a dandelion gone to seed, but his face was turned to the wall - there was the pale shadow of his nose on the plaster - and I thought, Well you do n't look much like a pig-drunk bully now.
Above him slid the evasive shadow of a storm cloud.
Even Hudson, experienced in Arctic sailing and determined as he was, must have had qualms as he slid down the Thames.
He slid in at her side, tucked a cigar into his mouth, and politely proffered one to her.

Synset Examples

They slid through the wicket in the big gate

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slip - to move smoothly, easily, and quietly
  • Synonym: glide - to move smoothly and continuously along, as if without effort
  • Antonym: clatter - to move with a loud, rattling sound, opposite of smooth, unobtrusive motion

Etymology

From Old English *slīdan* (to slip, glide), related to Old High German *slītan* (to glide) and Old Norse *slīða* (to slide).

5. VERB

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
to move smoothly along a surface while maintaining continuous contact, often sideways or in an uncontrolled way

Examples

A news item described the launching of a ship: "Completing the ceremony, the beautiful movie star smashed a bottle of champagne over her stern as she slid gracefully down the ways into the sea ".
The doors of the D train slid shut, and as I dropped into a seat and, exhaling, looked up across the aisle, the whole aviary in my head burst into song.
Mike caught the ball just as the catcher slid into the bag.
All Deegan had to do was slide, fall away, but instead, he rammed into the catcher.
A young man was killed and two others injured at midnight Friday when the car they were riding slid into a utility pole on Lake Avenue near Waddell Street, NE, police said.

Synset Examples

the wheels skidded against the sidewalk

Related Terms

  • Synonym: skid - to slide, typically sideways or out of control
  • Synonym: glide - to move smoothly and continuously along a surface
  • Synonym: slip - to lose one's footing and slide suddenly

Etymology

From Old English *slīdan* meaning 'to slip, glide'.

6. NOUN

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
A sudden downward movement of a mass of earth, rocks, snow, or similar material down a slope, often as a result of gravity.

Examples

A tsunami may be started by a sea bottom slide, an earthquake or a volcanic eruption.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: landslide - a collapse of a mass of earth or rock from a mountain or cliff

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
to move smoothly, quickly, and quietly along a surface

Examples

He turned away and returned in a moment with a pint of brandy in a small paper sack which he slid over the counter to Shayne.
As the detective slid it into his pocket, Rourke asked sadly, "What in hell are you going to do, Mike "?

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glide - to move smoothly and continuously along, as if without effort
  • Synonym: slip - to slide suddenly or involuntarily; to lose footing or grip

Etymology

From Old English *slīdan* (to slip, glide), related to Dutch *slijden* and German *schleiten*.

8. NOUN

/ slaɪd / (AmE), / slaɪd / (BrE)
an act of moving smoothly along a surface while staying in contact with it

Examples

But the Anniston boy had begun his slide too late.

Synset Examples

his slide didn't stop until the bottom of the hill
the children lined up for a coast down the snowy slope

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glide - to move smoothly and continuously along, as if without effort
  • Synonym: skid - to slide, typically sideways or obliquely, on slippery ground or as a result of stopping or turning too quickly

Etymology

From Middle English slid(en) (to move smoothly), from Old English slid- (stem of slīdan 'to glide, slide').
