loot

1. NOUN

/ lut / (AmE), / luːt / (BrE)
(informal) Money, especially when referring to it casually or as the proceeds of questionable activity.

Examples

Authorities hesitate to quote exact figures, however, believing that any sum they come up with is only a surface manifestation - turned up by their inevitably limited policing - of the real loot of the medical racketeer.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boodle - slang term for money
  • Synonym: dough - (informal) money
  • Synonym: cash - money in the form of notes and coins
  • Synonym: bread - (slang) money

Etymology

From Hindi *lūṭ* (goods taken in plundering), popularized in English during British colonial rule in India.

2. NOUN

/ lut / (AmE), / luːt / (BrE)
goods or money stolen or taken illegally, especially during a war or riot

Examples

Furthermore - and this, to me, strikes an especially warming note - it shall avail the vandals naught to throw away or dispose of their loot.
Carmer's ingenious cache for his loot had been found.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: booty - valuables taken by force or theft
  • Synonym: swag - stolen goods, especially in slang
  • Synonym: plunder - goods taken by force, especially in war

Etymology

From Hindi lūṭ (robbery, booty), from Sanskrit luṇṭh- (to rob, plunder).

3. VERB

/ lut / (AmE), / luːt / (BrE)
to steal or take something illegally, especially during a riot or war, or to steal intellectual property

Examples

In his teens O' Banion was enrolled in the vicious Market Street gang and he became a singing waiter in McGovern's Cafe, a notoriously low and rowdy dive in North Clark Street, where befuddled customers were methodically looted of their money by the singing waiters before being thrown out.

Synset Examples

This writer plundered from famous authors

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plunder - to steal goods, typically using force, especially in time of war or civil disorder
  • Degree Lower: pilfer - to steal things of little value
  • Synonym: rob - to take property unlawfully from a person or place by force or threat of force

Etymology

From Hindi *lūṭ* (plunder, booty), via Urdu and Sanskrit *luṇṭhati* (he plunders).
