swear

1. VERB

/ swɛr / (AmE), / swɛə / (BrE)
to make a solemn promise or statement, often invoking a higher authority or taking an oath

Examples

Even while suffering the trip to his home, Cook swore to Moore and Lane that he would kill the Indian.
He knew who was riding after him - the men he had known all his life, the men who had worked for him, sworn their loyalty to him.
I swear I'’ll make it up to you.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: promise - to assure someone that one will do something
  • Synonym: vow - to solemnly promise or pledge

Etymology

Old English swerian, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch zweren and German schwören.

2. VERB

/ swɛr / (AmE), / swɛə / (BrE)
to make a formal statement or give evidence, especially in a court of law, promising that what you say is true

Examples

Hands on Bible, seaman Lodley and carpenter Staffe swore that Juet had tried to persuade them to keep muskets and swords in their cabins.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: testify - give evidence as a witness in a law court

Etymology

From Old English 'swerian', related to Old High German 'swerien', meaning to take an oath.

3. VERB

/ swɛr / (AmE), / swɛə / (BrE)
to use offensive or profane language, especially by uttering obscenities or blasphemies

Examples

He swore, and said, "All right.
Vince swore.
Vince swore again.
Grosse swore now.

Synset Examples

The drunken men were cursing loudly in the street

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curse - to say rude or offensive words about someone because you are angry

Etymology

Old English *swerian* (to take an oath), meaning broadened to include use of profane language.
