mislead

1. VERB

/ mɪsˈlid / (AmE), / mɪsˈliːd / (BrE)
to cause someone to believe something that is not true; to give someone wrong or inaccurate information or guidance

Examples

If the Cardinals heed Manager Gene Mauch of the Phillies, they won't be misled by the Pirates' slower start this season.
Instead of her old confidence in the simplest, purest, most moving musical expression, Miss Schwarzkopf is letting herself be tempted by the classic sin of artistic pride - that subtle vanity that sometimes misleads a great artist into thinking that he or she can somehow better the music by bringing to it something extra, some personal dramatic touch imposed from the outside.

Synset Examples

The pedestrian misdirected the out-of-town driver

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true, especially in order to gain an advantage
  • Synonym: misguide - to guide someone in the wrong direction or give incorrect advice
  • Antonym: enlighten - to give someone greater knowledge and understanding about a subject or situation
  • Antonym: inform - to give information to someone; to tell

Etymology

From Middle English 'misleden', from 'mis-' (wrongly) + 'lead'.

2. VERB

/ mɪsˈliːd / (AmE), / mɪsˈliːd / (BrE)
to cause someone to have a wrong idea or belief; to give false or misleading information to someone, often intentionally

Examples

Those modern scholars who urge that we must keep in mind the fundamental continuity of Aegean development from earliest times - granted occasional irruptions of peoples and ideas from outside - are correct; but all too many observers have been misled by this fact into minimizing the degree of change which took place in the early first millennium.
The charges by the late junior Senator from Wisconsin not only destroyed innocent people but misled the nation.
Tell him I know Roth misled him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Antonym: inform - to give information to someone, especially facts or news

Etymology

From Old English 'mis-' (wrongly) + 'lēdan' (to lead).
