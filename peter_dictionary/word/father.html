Father

1. NOUN

/ ˈfɑðər / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
(in Christianity) one of the influential theologians of the early Christian Church (2nd to 7th centuries) whose writings helped establish and confirm church doctrine.

Examples

Because they have kept the faith of their medieval fathers, English Catholics have always strongly resented the charge of being "English ".
The grounds for the Church's position are Scriptural (Old Testament), the teachings of the fathers and doctors of the early Church, the unbroken tradition of nineteen centuries, the decisions of the highest ecclesiastical authority and the natural law.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Doctor of the Church - saint recognized for significant contribution to theology or doctrine

Etymology

From Old English fæder, ultimately from Proto-Indo-European *pəter- (father). Here used as a title of respect for early Church leaders.

2. NOUN

/ ˈfɑðɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
(in some Christian Churches) a title or form of address for a priest, especially in the Roman Catholic or Orthodox tradition.

Examples

My curiosity was sharpened a day or two before the interview by a conversation I had with a well-informed teacher of literature, a Jesuit father, at a conference on religious drama near Paris.
This is Father Carmelo.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Padre - Spanish/Italian-influenced term for a military chaplain or Catholic priest

Etymology

From Old English fæder, ultimately from Latin pater (father). Used as a title for clergy from medieval times.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfɑðɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɑːðə / (BrE)
(in Christianity) God regarded as the first person of the Trinity; the Creator and sustainer of the universe.

Examples

Michael, do you believe in God, the Father, Creator of Heaven and Earth?

Synset Examples

hear our prayers, Heavenly Father

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: Son - Jesus Christ, the second person of the Trinity
  • Degree Lower: Holy Spirit - The third person of the Christian Trinity

Etymology

From Old English fæder, ultimately from Proto-Indo-European *ph₂tḗr (father).
