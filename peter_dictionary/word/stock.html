stock

1. NOUN

/ stɑk / (AmE), / stɒk / (BrE)
the capital raised by a company or corporation by issuing shares, giving shareholders ownership interests

Examples

It permits the state to take over bank accounts, stocks and other personal property of persons missing for seven years or more.
The other bill, by Sen. A. M. Aikin Jr. of Paris, would relieve real estate brokers, who pay their own annual licensing fee, from the $ 12 annual occupation license on brokers in such as stocks and bonds.
The Manchester Depot Sewer Company issued 214 shares of stock at $ 10 each for construction of a sewer in that locality, and assessments were made for its maintenance.
At about the time the Marsden enterprise was getting under way, the Vail Light and Lumber Company started construction of a chair stock factory on the site of the present Bennington Co-operative Creamery, intending to use its surplus power for generating electricity.
He had sold oil stock to Bob Hope and Bing Crosby in person; he had helped fight an oil-well fire that raged six days and nights.

Synset Examples

he owns a controlling share of the company's stock

Related Terms

  • Synonym: share - a unit of ownership in a company or financial asset
  • Antonym: bond - a fixed income instrument that represents a loan made by an investor to a borrower

Etymology

From Old English stocc (tree trunk, log), later used in the sense of 'fund' or 'supply,' leading to the financial usage.

2. NOUN

/ stɑk / (AmE), / stɒk / (BrE)
A supply of goods or materials kept for future use or sale.

Examples

He seems to have at least a few 30 - and 50 - megaton bombs on hand, since we cannot assume that he has exploded his entire stock.

Synset Examples

he brought back a large store of Cuban cigars

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inventory - a complete list of items such as goods in stock

Etymology

From Old English *stocc* (tree trunk, post, stake, log).

3. NOUN

/ stɑk / (AmE), / stɒk / (BrE)
the part of a firearm, such as a rifle or shotgun, to which the barrel and firing mechanism are attached, held against the shooter's shoulder when firing

Examples

Certain this menace was only imaginary, he yet stared in fascinated horror, his hand sticky against the stock of his weapon.

Synset Examples

the rifle had been fitted with a special stock

Etymology

From Old English stocc (tree trunk, log, post), related to the wooden structure of original stocks.

4. NOUN

/ stɑk / (AmE), / stɒk / (BrE)
The goods or merchandise kept on the premises of a shop or warehouse and available for sale or distribution.

Examples

When he had stored his stock in the great oak chest, locked the two big hasps and secured the additional chain, tied the fly of the tent, and picked up the cash box, he moved up the darkening street.
It was professedly worth three thousand dollars in stock and good will, and the name was written in gold in foot high letters across each of the two display windows.
"My reputation's my stock in trade ", Tom mentioned more than once.

Synset Examples

they carried a vast inventory of hardware
they stopped selling in exact sizes in order to reduce inventory

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inventory - a complete list of items such as property, goods in stock, or contents of a building
  • Synonym: supply - the amount of something available for use or sale
  • Antonym: out of stock - not available for sale due to none remaining in inventory

Etymology

From Old English 'stocc' meaning 'stump, post, stock', later used in the sense of goods kept for sale or distribution.

6. ADJ

/ stɑk / (AmE), / stɒk / (BrE)
(of a phrase, answer, or idea) so regularly used as to be automatic, unoriginal, or clichéd; lacking freshness because of overuse.

Examples

Another stock vaudeville gag ran: "Mother is home sick in bed with the doctor ".

Synset Examples

hackneyed phrases

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trite - lacking originality or freshness; dull on account of overuse
  • Synonym: hackneyed - (of a phrase or idea) lacking significance through having been overused
  • Antonym: original - not derived from something else; fresh and new

Etymology

Originally from Old English *stocc* (tree trunk, post), influencing the figurative meaning of 'standard, common' in expressions.
