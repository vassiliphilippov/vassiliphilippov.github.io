dam

1. NOUN

/ dæm / (AmE), / dæm / (BrE)
A barrier built across a river or stream to hold back water or prevent flooding.

Examples

An old shipwreck, a high dam, an old covered bridge, a place to find agates or other semi-precious stones or a place to pan gold, etcetera may be of interest.
Examples are in public utilities, making military aircraft and accessories, or where the investment and risk for a proprietorship would be too great for a much needed project impossible to achieve by any means other than the corporate form, e. g. constructing major airports or dams.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weir - a low dam built across a river to raise or regulate the water level
  • Synonym: levee - an embankment built to prevent the overflow of a river

Etymology

From Middle English *dam*, from Old Norse *dammr* (pond, dam).

2. VERB

/ dæm / (AmE), / dam / (BrE)
To obstruct the flow of water in a river or stream by building a dam, or to restrict the flow as if by a dam.

Examples

No matter how determined or wealthy boating lovers of the Southwest had been, for example, they could never have created anything approaching the fifty square-mile Lake Texoma, located between Texas and Oklahoma, which resulted when the Corp of Army Engineers dammed the Red River.

Synset Examples

dam the gorges of the Yangtse River

Related Terms

  • Synonym: block - to obstruct or stop something from moving through a passage or opening
  • Antonym: unblock - to remove an obstruction and allow free flow again

Etymology

From Middle English 'dam', probably from Middle Dutch 'dam', meaning 'barrier'.
