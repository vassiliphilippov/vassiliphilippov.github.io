lick

1. VERB

/ lɪk / (AmE), / lɪk / (BrE)
to pass the tongue over the surface of something, typically in order to taste, moisten, or clean it

Examples

"Chicken ", Mose said, and theatrically licked his lips.
He licked his lips again.
And the forked tongue of gossip licked its sinister way from back porch to back porch.
Killpath licked his lips.
Emerson evaded the problem by shoving it aside, or rather by leaving it behind him: he walked out of the Unitarian communion, so that it could lick the wound of his departure, preserve its self-respect and eventually accord him pious veneration.

Synset Examples

the dog licked her hand

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slurp - to make a loud sucking sound while eating or drinking, often with the tongue

Etymology

Old English liccian, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch likken and German lecken.

2. VERB

/ lɪk / (AmE), / lɪk / (BrE)
to solve or overcome (a problem or challenge), especially after difficulty

Examples

"Just when you think you have it licked, this golf course can get up and bite you ", Player had said one afternoon midway through the tournament.

Synset Examples

did you solve the problem?
Work out your problems with the boss
this unpleasant situation isn't going to work itself out
did you get it?
Did you get my meaning?
He could not work the math problem

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solve - find an answer or solution to a problem
  • Synonym: overcome - succeed in dealing with a problem or difficulty

Etymology

Sense derives from the idea of 'licking' as 'defeating'; first recorded in 19th-century American English.
