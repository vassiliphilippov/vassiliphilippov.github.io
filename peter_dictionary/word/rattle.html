rattle

1. VERB

/ ˈrætl̩ / (AmE), / ˈrætl̩ / (BrE)
to make a series of short, sharp noises as a result of being shaken or hitting against something repeatedly

Examples

The walls bulged, the floor trembled, the windowpanes rattled.
"The tires are rattling on the wheels now.
The tables were all spinning, the dice rattling, the bar crowded.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clatter - to make a loud, rattling sound
  • Degree Higher: bang - to make a sudden loud noise

Etymology

From Middle English 'ratelen', from Old English 'hrætele', of imitative origin.

2. NOUN

/ ˈrætəl / (AmE), / ˈrætəl / (BrE)
A rapid series of short, loud sounds, such as the noise made by a rattling object or heard in certain medical conditions.

Examples

Was I supposed to buy a funny hat and a rattle for Jessica?
The first rattle of the machine guns, at 7: 10 in the evening, roused around me the varied voices and faces of fear.

Synset Examples

the death rattle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clatter - a continuous rattling sound as of hard objects falling or striking each other

Etymology

From Middle English 'ratelen', probably of imitative origin.

3. VERB

/ ˈrætl̩ / (AmE), / ˈræt.əl / (BrE)
to shake or cause something to shake and make a rapid series of short, sharp knocking or tapping sounds.

Examples

He slapped the buckskin again and it kicked wildly, its hoofs rattling the side of the stall.
Jess painfully got to his feet as someone rattled the door.
Chairman Khrushchev (Kennedy) rattles his rockets (sabre) in order to cure his internal ills and to strengthen his negotiating position.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clatter - to make a loud rattling sound, especially by hard objects striking together
  • Synonym: jangle - to make a discordant ringing or rattling sound

Etymology

From Middle English ratelen, related to Middle Dutch ratelen (to rattle).
