dip

1. VERB

/ dɪp / (AmE), / dɪp / (BrE)
to stain or dye something by immersing it in a liquid for a short time

Examples

Spectators in white crush textures dip toe and heel in smooth black, navy and taffy tan.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dye - to color or stain something, especially fabric, with a dye

Etymology

Old English *dyppan* (to immerse, dip), related to deep.

2. VERB

/ dɪp / (AmE), / dɪp / (BrE)
to put something quickly into a liquid and take it out again

Examples

Argiento made him lie down on the bed, brought a pan of hot water, dipped some clean white linen cloth and applied it to extract the splinter.
The fun of toasting their own sausages over the small Oriental charcoal burners and dipping them in tasty sauces will keep your group busy - try it and see!

Synset Examples

dip the garment into the cleaning solution
dip the brush into the paint

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dunk - to dip quickly into liquid, especially food

Etymology

Probably from Middle English dippen, from Old English dyppan (to immerse, plunge).

3. VERB

/ dɪp / (AmE), / dɪp / (BrE)
to decrease or go down slightly and for a short time, especially in price or value

Examples

Volume dipped to 3.28 million shares from 3.98 million Monday.

Synset Examples

Prices dipped

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drop - to fall or decrease, especially suddenly or significantly
  • Antonym: rise - to increase or go up

Etymology

From Old English *dyppan* (to immerse), related to *deep*.

4. VERB

/ dɪp / (AmE), / dɪp / (BrE)
to put food quickly into a liquid, especially as part of the process of eating or cooking

Examples

Dip in melted butter and roll in flour.

Synset Examples

She dunked the piece of bread in the sauce

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dunk - to dip something quickly into liquid, especially food

Etymology

From Old English 'dyppan' (to immerse, submerge).
