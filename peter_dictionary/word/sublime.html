sublime

1. VERB

/ səˈblaɪm / (AmE), / səˈblaɪm / (BrE)
(of a substance) to change directly from a solid to a vapor and then condense back to solid without passing through the liquid state, especially as a process in chemistry.

Examples

The silver chloride was fused under vacuum in the presence of aluminum chips with the resultant product of * * f which was sublimed into a flask on the vacuum line.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evaporate - to turn from liquid into vapor
  • Synonym: condense - to change from vapor to liquid or solid
  • Antonym: deposit - to change from vapor to solid without becoming liquid; reverse of sublimation

Etymology

From Latin *sublimare* (to raise, elevate), from *sublimis* (uplifted, high).

2. ADJ

/ səˈblaɪm / (AmE), / səˈblaɪm / (BrE)
of such excellence, grandeur, or beauty as to inspire great admiration or awe.

Examples

Presupposed in Plato's system is a doctrine of levels of insight, in which a certain kind of detached understanding is alone capable of penetrating to the most sublime wisdom.

Synset Examples

well-meaning ineptitude that rises to empyreal absurdity
empyrean aplomb
the sublime beauty of the night

Related Terms

  • Synonym: majestic - having or showing impressive beauty or dignity
  • Synonym: grand - magnificent and imposing in appearance or style
  • Antonym: ordinary - with no special or distinctive features; normal
  • Antonym: mediocre - of only average quality; not very good

Etymology

From Latin 'sublimis' (uplifted, high, lofty).
