mechanics

1. NOUN

/ məˈkænɪks / (AmE), / mɪˈkænɪks / (BrE)
The branch of physics that deals with the motion of bodies and the forces acting on them.

Examples

This has an interesting analogy with the assumption stated by Philippoff that "the deformational mechanics of elastic solids can be applied to flowing solutions ".
There is one exception to the above statement as has been pointed out, and that is that fluids can relax by flowing into fields of lower rates of shear, so the statement should be modified by stating that the mechanics are similar.
If the mechanics are similar, we can also infer that the thermodynamics will also be similar.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dynamics - branch of mechanics concerned with forces and motion
  • Degree Lower: statics - branch of mechanics dealing with bodies at rest or in equilibrium
  • Degree Higher: physics - natural science concerning matter, motion, and energy

Etymology

From mechanic + -s, originally referring to the science of machines.

2. NOUN

/ məˈkænɪks / (AmE), / mɪˈkænɪks / (BrE)
the practical or technical aspects of doing or operating something, especially considered in detail or as a process

Examples

Then I plan my attack: the parts I will finish first, the range of values, the accenting of minor details - all in all, mechanics of producing the finished job with a maximum of crispness.
It is she who says aye or nay to the intimate questions of sexual technique and mechanics - not the husband.

Synset Examples

a mechanism of social control
mechanisms of communication
the mechanics of prose style

Related Terms

  • Synonym: technique - a way of carrying out a particular task, especially the technical skill involved

Etymology

From mechanic, from Latin mechanicus, from Ancient Greek mēkhanikos (engineer).
