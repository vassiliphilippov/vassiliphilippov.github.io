sail

1. NOUN

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
a large piece of strong fabric attached to a boat or ship so that the wind can push it along the water

Examples

Both of them had known the feeling of the small life in her waiting, ready, for the two of them to run up her sails.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canvas - strong, heavy cloth used for sails

Etymology

From Old English 'seg(e)l', related to Old Norse 'segl', German 'Segel', meaning sail.

2. VERB

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
to travel across or on a body of water in a boat or ship, especially using sails or as a method of navigation

Examples

Nearly twenty-five years before, Captain John Davis had noted, as he sailed near the Arctic Circle, "a very great gulf, the water whirling and roaring, as it were the meeting of tides ".
In 1602, George Waymouth, in the same little Discovery that Hudson now commanded, had sailed 300 miles up the strait before his frightened men turned the ship back.
Worse, his present crew included five men who had sailed with him before.
But men willing to sail at all into waters where wooden ships could be crushed like eggs were hard to find.
With difficulty, Hudson persuaded him to rejoin the ship, and they sailed from Iceland.

Synset Examples

We sailed the Atlantic
He sailed the Pacific all alone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: navigate - to direct the course of a ship or boat

Etymology

From Old English 'seġlan', related to 'segel' (sail) and Germanic roots.

3. NOUN

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
A trip in a boat or ship, especially taken for pleasure or recreation.

Examples

In fact, Hudson's sail up the Great North River had disposed of one of the last hopes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: voyage - a long journey by sea or in space
  • Synonym: cruise - a journey on a ship taken for pleasure

Etymology

From Old English 'seġl' (sail, sailing-canvas, sail-ship); related to Dutch 'zeil', German 'Segel'.

4. VERB

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
to travel on water in a ship or boat propelled by wind, especially using sails

Examples

One did one's best and if fortune frowned, an eighteen year old boy with murder in his heart sailed aboard one's ship.

Synset Examples

I love sailing, especially on the open sea
the ship sails on

Related Terms

  • Synonym: navigate - to plan and direct the course of a ship
  • Antonym: row - to propel a boat forward using oars (not wind)

Etymology

From Old English 'seġlian', related to 'seġl' (sail, cloth for sailing).

5. VERB

/ seɪl / (AmE), / seɪl / (BrE)
to move smoothly and effortlessly through the air or over a surface, often with a gliding or sweeping motion

Examples

The economy seems to be sailing along on an even keel and the 1961 hurricane season and auto strikes are at an end so they can n't be blamed in November.

Synset Examples

The diva swept into the room
Shreds of paper sailed through the air
The searchlights swept across the sky

Related Terms

  • Synonym: glide - move smoothly and quietly, as if without effort
  • Synonym: sweep - move swiftly and smoothly, often in a wide curve

Etymology

From Old English *seġl*, relating to the canvas sheet used to catch wind; extended to metaphorical uses of smooth movement.
