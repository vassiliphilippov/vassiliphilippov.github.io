wield

1. VERB

/ wild / (AmE), / wiːld / (BrE)
to hold and use (a weapon, tool, or instrument) with skill or control

Examples

The truth is, however, that when Mel Chandler first reported to the regiment the only steed he had ever ridden was a swivel chair and the only weapon he had ever wielded was a pencil.

Synset Examples

The burglar wielded an axe
The young violinist didn't manage her bow very well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: brandish - to wave or flourish a weapon as a threat
  • Synonym: manipulate - to handle skillfully, especially in managing or using tools

Etymology

From Old English *wieldan* (to govern, control, possess, have power over).

2. VERB

/ wild / (AmE), / wiːld / (BrE)
to have and use power, authority, or influence; to exercise control or command

Examples

It was not opposed to either justice or morality; it merely wished to minimize subjective views of officials who wielded public authority.
The Communists wield a power unknown to Hitler.
Mahayana had gods, and magic, a pantheon, heavens and hells, and gorgeously appareled priests, monks, and nuns, all of whom wielded power over souls in the other world.

Synset Examples

wield power and authority

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exercise - to use or apply (a power or right)

Etymology

From Old English *wealdan* (to rule, control), related to Old High German *waltan* (to have power).
