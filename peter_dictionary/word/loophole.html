loophole

1. NOUN

/ ˈluːˌpoʊl / (AmE), / ˈluːˌpəʊl / (BrE)
An ambiguity or inadequacy in a law, regulation, or contract that allows someone to avoid its intended effect.

Examples

If there had been, he would have found a loophole, because Arnold is one golfer who knows the code as thoroughly as the man who wrote the book.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: technicality - a minor detail in a law or contract exploited to avoid an obligation

Etymology

Originally a narrow opening in a wall for observation or firing, later figuratively for means of escape; from Middle English loop (slit, opening) + hole.

2. NOUN

/ ˈluːpˌhoʊl / (AmE), / ˈluːphəʊl / (BrE)
A narrow opening in a wall, especially of a fortification, through which defenders can observe or fire weapons.

Examples

The ten or more dangerous parties singled out for prosecution were still at large, and Pels realized that if these men entrenched themselves in their adobe houses, defending themselves through loopholes, it would be most difficult to capture them.

Etymology

From Middle Dutch 'lupen' (to look) + 'hole'.
