repeal

1. VERB

/ rɪˈpil / (AmE), / rɪˈpiːl / (BrE)
to officially cancel a law, rule, or decision so that it is no longer valid

Examples

Pelham said Sunday night there was research being done on whether the "quickie "vote on the increase can be repealed outright or whether notice would have to first be given that reconsideration of the action would be sought.
and was repealed.
Accordingly, if it is not repealed by the Congress at its present session, I shall have no alternative thereafter but to direct the Secretary of Defense to disregard the section unless a court of competent jurisdiction determines otherwise.

Synset Examples

He revoked the ban on smoking
lift an embargo
vacate a death sentence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: revoke - to withdraw or cancel officially
  • Synonym: rescind - to cancel or repeal a law, order, or agreement
  • Antonym: enact - to make a law or rule officially valid

Etymology

From Anglo-Norman and Old French *rapeler*, from Latin *recallāre*, meaning 'to recall'.

2. NOUN

/ rɪˈpil / (AmE), / rɪˈpiːl / (BrE)
the official or legal act of revoking, annulling, or canceling a law, order, or regulation

Examples

In the budget message for 1959, and again for 1960, I recommended immediate repeal of section 601 of the Act of September 28, 1951 (65 Stat. 365).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abrogation - the act of formally revoking a law or agreement
  • Antonym: enactment - the process of making a law or regulation active

Etymology

From Old French 'repeal', from Latin 'repellere' (to drive back).
