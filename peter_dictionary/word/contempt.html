contempt

1. NOUN

/ kənˈtɛmpt / (AmE), / kənˈtɛmpt / (BrE)
A manner or attitude showing disrespect and a lack of reverence for someone or something.

Examples

I have heard people talk with contempt about the British regulars, but that only proves that a lot of people talk about things of which they are deplorably ignorant.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorn - open or unqualified contempt or disdain; derision

Etymology

From Latin 'contemptus' (scorn, despising), from 'contemnere' (to despise, to scorn).

2. NOUN

/ kənˈtɛmpt / (AmE), / kənˈtɛmpt / (BrE)
A feeling that someone or something is not worthy of respect, often accompanied by intense dislike.

Examples

Although he is perfectly willing to cooperate with Scotland Yard, Holmes has nothing but contempt for the intelligence and mentality of the police.
He turned his head to the source of the disturbance and instantly back to the window and his rifle sight, dismissing Hoag for the moment with the same contempt he had shown in their encounter at Hoag's apartment.

Synset Examples

he was held in contempt
the despite in which outsiders were held is legendary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disdain - the feeling that someone or something is unworthy of one's consideration or respect
  • Antonym: respect - a feeling of deep admiration for someone or something

Etymology

From Latin contemptus (scorn, despising), from contemnere (to scorn).
