inaugural

1. ADJ

/ ɪˈnɔɡjərəl / (AmE), / ɪˈnɔːɡjʊərəl / (BrE)
(of an event or action) marking the beginning of an institution, activity, or period of office; serving to set in motion.

Examples

However, this inaugural feast did its sponsors no good whatever.

Synset Examples

the magazine's inaugural issue
the initiative phase in the negotiations
an initiatory step toward a treaty
his first (or maiden) speech in Congress
the liner's maiden voyage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: initial - existing or occurring at the beginning
  • Synonym: maiden - being the first of its kind

Etymology

From Latin *inaugurālis*, from *inaugurare* (to consecrate, install).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈnɔːɡjərəl / (AmE), / ɪˈnɔːɡjʊərəl / (BrE)
A formal speech given at the beginning of a special event or ceremony, especially the address delivered by a president at an inauguration.

Examples

Lincoln understood this better than most when he said in his "Second Inaugural "that God "gives to both North and South this terrible war, as the woe due to those by whom the offense came ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inaugural address - formal speech delivered at a ceremony marking the start of something, especially a presidency
  • Antonym: valedictory - a speech or address given as a farewell

Etymology

From Latin *inauguralis*, from *inaugurare* (to install under good omens, inaugurate).

3. ADJ

/ ɪˈnɔɡjərəl / (AmE), / ɪˈnɔːɡjərəl / (BrE)
Relating to or marking the beginning of an official position, event, or period, especially a formal investiture or induction.

Examples

He opens his discourse, however, with a review of the Eisenhower inaugural festivities at which a sympathetic press had assembled its massive talents, all primed to catch some revelation of the emerging new age.

Synset Examples

the President's inaugural address
an inaugural ball

Etymology

From Latin 'inauguralis', from 'inaugurare' (to consecrate by augury).
