inflection

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈflɛkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈflɛkʃən / (BrE)
the way in which a speaker's voice rises and falls, or becomes louder and softer; variation in pitch or tone, especially as a pattern characteristic of a language

Examples

The Irish accent is, as one would expect, combined with slight inflections from the French.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: intonation - the rise and fall of the voice in speaking, especially as a feature of language
  • Synonym: prosody - the patterns of rhythm and sound used in poetry and language

Etymology

From Latin *inflectio* (a bending, curve), from *inflectere* (to bend or change)

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈflɛkʃən / (AmE), / ɪnˈflɛkʃən / (BrE)
the change in the form of a word, typically by adding endings, to express different grammatical functions such as tense, case, voice, or number

Examples

For example, the inflected forms of a word can be represented, insofar as regular inflection allows, by a stem and a set of endings to be attached.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: conjugation - variation of the form of a verb for tense, mood, and other grammatical categories
  • Synonym: declension - variation of the form of a noun, pronoun, or adjective to mark case, number, and gender

Etymology

From Latin *inflectere* (to bend in, change).
