tragedy

1. NOUN

/ ˈtrædʒədi / (AmE), / ˈtrædʒədi / (BrE)
A very sad event resulting in great loss or misfortune.

Examples

Resolved, that we find in this fearful tragedy at Harper's Ferry a reason for more earnest effort to remove the evil of slavery from the whole land as speedily as possible.
The Skopas people seemed to him of that breed of human beings whose insularity frees them from tragedy.
Few of the neighbors came, but Mrs. Tussle came, called by tragedy.
Andy's co-workers kept their distance, awed by the tragedy.
Everyone with a personal or group tragedy to relate had to be given his day in court as in some vast collective dirge.

Synset Examples

the whole city was affected by the irremediable calamity
the earthquake was a disaster

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disaster - a sudden event, such as an accident or a natural catastrophe, that causes great damage or loss of life
  • Synonym: catastrophe - a sudden and widespread disaster
  • Antonym: blessing - something good that brings happiness or relief

Etymology

From Greek tragōidia (goat song, tragic play), via Latin tragoedia.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtrædʒədi / (AmE), / ˈtrædʒədi / (BrE)
A form of drama in which the main character is brought to ruin or suffers extreme sorrow, especially as a consequence of a tragic flaw, moral weakness, or inability to cope with unfavorable circumstances.

Examples

"The great Greek tragedies are concerned with man against Fate, not man against man for the prize of a woman's body.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: comedy - a dramatic work that is light and often humorous or satirical

Etymology

From Greek *tragōidia*, from *tragos* (goat) + *ōidē* (song), referring to ancient Greek dramatic performances.
