swerve

1. VERB

/ swɝv / (AmE), / swɜːv / (BrE)
to change direction suddenly and sharply, especially to avoid something

Examples

Eyes swerved in the patronne's head, Alex coughed loudly, and the doctor, with a sforzando of chicken noises floating behind him, took to the stairs in long shanked leaps.
Today, Africa is swerving violently away from the West and plunging, it would seem, into the Soviet orbit.
If of the founders of glottochronology Swadesh has escaped our steady plodding, and Lees has repudiated his own share in the founding, that is no reason why we should swerve.
When it was followed by a second, whining even closer, Cobb swerved sharply aside into a depression.

Synset Examples

The car cut to the left at the intersection
The motorbike veered to the right

Related Terms

  • Synonym: veer - to change direction suddenly
  • Synonym: turn - to change direction

Etymology

Old English sweorfan (to rub, scour), later influenced by Middle Low German swerven (to swirl, turn).

2. NOUN

/ swɝv / (AmE), / swɜːv / (BrE)
A sudden, sharp turn or deviation from a straight course.

Examples

Johnson was trying to grab the wheel, though the swerve of the truck was throwing him away from it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: veer - to change direction suddenly; to turn aside

Etymology

Probably from Middle English swerven, from Old English sweorfan (to rub, scour, wipe off, file away).
