fuse

1. VERB

/ fjuz / (AmE), / fjuːz / (BrE)
to combine or blend two or more things together to form a single entity

Examples

Nor have we remembered that in the melting pot of America the hundreds of isolated and semi isolated ethnic, regional and occupational groups did not fuse into a homogeneous national unit until long after education and industrialization had caused them to cast oral tradition aside as a means of carrying culturally significant material.
The process of flattening seemed inexorable, and it became necessary to emphasize the surface still further in order to prevent it from fusing with the illusion.
Anger and fear fused in John.

Synset Examples

The colors blend well

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blend - to mix things together thoroughly so that they combine into a single substance
  • Synonym: merge - to combine or cause to combine to form a single entity
  • Antonym: separate - to move or be moved apart; to stop being joined or mixed

Etymology

From Latin 'fundere' meaning 'to pour, melt'.

2. VERB

/ fjuːz / (AmE), / fjuːz / (BrE)
(of a substance) to become plastic or liquefied through the application of heat.

Examples

The silver chloride was fused under vacuum in the presence of aluminum chips with the resultant product of * * f which was sublimed into a flask on the vacuum line.

Synset Examples

The substances fused at a very high temperature

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melt - to change from a solid to a liquid state through heat
  • Antonym: solidify - to make or become hard or solid

Etymology

From Latin 'fundere' (to pour, melt), via French 'fuser'.

3. NOUN

/ fjuz / (AmE), / fjuːz / (BrE)
A safety device consisting of a strip of wire that melts and breaks an electric circuit if the current exceeds a safe level.

Examples

Before you use' em the light company's got to run in a heavy line and you 'll need a new fuse box for the extra circuits.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: circuit breaker - a device that automatically interrupts an electrical circuit to prevent damage from overload or short circuit

Etymology

From Latin 'fusus' (spindle), by analogy with metal filaments; technical adoption in the 19th century.
