poem

1. NOUN

/ ˈpoʊ.əm / (AmE), / ˈpəʊ.ɪm / (BrE)
A piece of writing that expresses emotions, experiences, and ideas in a style more concentrated, imaginative, and rhythmical than that of ordinary speech or prose; often arranged in lines and stanzas and sometimes using rhyme and meter.

Examples

Robert Frost, for instance, writes about rural life in New England, but he does not include any significant amount of folklore in his poems.
They were poems in a strange language, of which he could barely touch a meaning - enough to make his being ache with the desire for the fullness he sensed there.
She was the pursuer as clearly as was Venus in Shakespeare's poem.
"The hero of his next poem is Napoleon Bonaparte ", said Claire, with slightly overdone carelessness.
At the same time another child - this one of Shelley's brain - was given to the world: Alastor, a poem of pervading beauty in which the reader may gaze into the still depths of a fine mind's musings.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: verse - writing arranged with metrical rhythm, typically having rhyme
  • Antonym: prose - written or spoken language in its ordinary form, without metrical structure

Etymology

From Latin 'poema', from Greek 'poiēma' (thing made, poem), from 'poiein' (to make).
