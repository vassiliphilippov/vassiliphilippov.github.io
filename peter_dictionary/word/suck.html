suck

1. NOUN

/ sʌk / (AmE), / sʌk / (BrE)
an act of drawing in liquid or air through the mouth by creating a partial vacuum

Examples

This is followed by a tremendous suck of water away from the shore as the first great trough arrives.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: sip - to drink in small quantities by sucking gently

Etymology

From Middle English suken, from Old English sūcan (to suck), from Proto-Germanic *sūkaną.

2. VERB

/ sʌk / (AmE), / sʌk / (BrE)
to draw something in by creating a vacuum or by force, as with air or liquid

Examples

And men also used vacuum cleaners in both rooms, sucking dust up once more.
He splashed on, mud sucking at his feet with each step, until he reached the end of the drainage ditch and the beginning of the fence that enclosed the farm.

Synset Examples

Mud was sucking at her feet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inhale - to draw in air; to breathe in
  • Synonym: absorb - to take in or soak up, typically gradually

Etymology

From Old English *sūcan*, of Germanic origin, related to Dutch *zuigen* and German *saugen*.

3. VERB

/ sʌk / (AmE), / sʌk / (BrE)
to draw something into the mouth by creating suction, often by closing the lips around it and inhaling

Examples

It's perfectly normal for babies to suck their thumbs, and no mother need worry if a child continues this habit until he is two or three years old.
In an instant he had sucked it dry.
Not really startled, but careful to appear so, Grosse sucked noisily on his pipe.

Synset Examples

suck the poison from the place where the snake bit
suck on a straw
the baby sucked on the mother's breast

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: sip - to drink a liquid by taking small mouthfuls
  • Degree Higher: gulp - to swallow something quickly or in large mouthfuls

Etymology

From Old English sucan, from Proto-Germanic *sukanan, and Proto-Indo-European *sūg- (to suck).
