tolerance

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɑː.lə.rəns / (AmE), / ˈtɒl.ər.əns / (BrE)
The willingness to accept or allow attitudes, beliefs, or behaviors that are different from one's own.

Examples

Now, under the impact of his wife's disclosures, he was brought suddenly to the realization that there was a limit to tolerance, however brilliant, however far-famed the offender might be.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intolerance - unwillingness to accept views, beliefs, or behavior different from one's own

Etymology

From Latin tolerantia (a bearing, enduring), from tolerare (to bear, endure).

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɑlɚəns / (AmE), / ˈtɒl(ə)rəns / (BrE)
The ability of an organism to endure or survive unfavorable environmental conditions.

Examples

As for the night being warm, he would have been comfortable naked on Mount Everest but his water brothers had little tolerance for changes in temperature and pressure; he was considerate of their weakness, once he learned of it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: resistance - ability to withstand or repel a negative force (e.g., disease, chemicals)

Etymology

From Latin tolerantia (endurance, forbearance).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɑl.ə.rəns / (AmE), / ˈtɒl.ər.əns / (BrE)
The willingness or ability to accept or allow the existence of opinions, behaviors, or practices that one does not necessarily agree with.

Examples

Since the magnitude of the plan made secrecy impossible, once the wheels had began to turn, persons controlling German industries, social institutions, and armed forces became, through their anti-Semitism or their tolerance of it, conscious accomplices of Hitler's crimes; whether in the last degree or a lesser one was a matter to be determined individually.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intolerance - unwillingness to accept views, beliefs, or behavior different from one's own

Etymology

From Latin tolerantia (a bearing, endurance), from tolerare (to bear, to endure).
