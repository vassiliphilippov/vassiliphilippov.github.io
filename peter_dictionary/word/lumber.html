lumber

1. NOUN

/ ˈlʌmbɚ / (AmE), / ˈlʌmbə / (BrE)
Wood that has been cut and prepared for use as building material.

Examples

Telegraphers at the Depot at this time were Aaron C. Burr and Mark Manley of "Burr and Manley ", dealers in lumber and dry goods.
Telephone wires from Louis Dufresne's house in East Manchester to the Dufresne lumber job near Bourn Pond were up about 1895.
Michelangelo bought some scrap lumber.
The National Lumber Manufacturers Association, Washington, D. C., is developing plans to utilize specially treated lumber for underground shelter construction.
The old woman had a nephew from North Italy, a poor boy from a lumber mill who had got tired of the seasonal unemployment, and who had migrated to Canada to work on the railway.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: timber - wood prepared for building (chiefly British usage)

Etymology

From Middle English 'lomber', possibly from Lombard, the name of Italian timber merchants.

2. VERB

/ ˈlʌmbər / (AmE), / ˈlʌmbə / (BrE)
to move in a slow, heavy, and awkward way, often making noise or appearing clumsy

Examples

He brushed by the idiotic boy and lumbered heavily up the stairs.

Synset Examples

The heavy man lumbered across the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trudge - to walk slowly and with heavy steps, typically due to exhaustion or harsh conditions

Etymology

Probably from earlier sense 'to move in a slow or awkward way'; related to lumber (noun), from Middle English lumber, possibly from Lombard (as in Lombard furniture).
