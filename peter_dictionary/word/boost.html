boost

1. NOUN

/ bust / (AmE), / buːst / (BrE)
an act that encourages or gives support to someone or something; a lift or help

Examples

And that's meant as a boost, not a knock.
It is, I am reliably given to understand, the technical argot for those who engage in your particular branch of the boost; i.e., burglars who rob while the tenants are absent, in contrast to hot slough prowlers, those who work while the occupants are home.
In the calm which follows the reading of a poem, for example, is the effect produced by the enforced quiet, by the musical quality of words and rhythm, by the sentiments or sense of the poem, by the associations with earlier readings, if it is familiar, by the boost to the self-esteem for the semi-literate, by the diversion of attention, by the sense of security in a legitimized withdrawal, by a kind license for some variety of fantasy life regarded as forbidden, or by half conscious ideas about the magical power of words?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lift - an act of raising or boosting support, morale, etc.
  • Synonym: encouragement - the act of giving someone support or confidence
  • Antonym: setback - an event that hinders progress or lowers morale

Etymology

Originally US slang (early 19th century), perhaps from obsolete 'boose' (to push, to pull), of Scandinavian origin.

2. VERB

/ bust / (AmE), / buːst / (BrE)
to help or encourage something to increase or improve; to contribute to the progress or growth of something

Examples

This boosted local telegraph business and Manchester basked in all the free advertising.

Synset Examples

I am promoting the use of computers in the classroom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: promote - to help something develop or increase (encourage, advance)
  • Antonym: hinder - to make it difficult for something to develop or succeed

Etymology

Originally American English; of uncertain origin, perhaps imitative.

3. VERB

/ bust / (AmE), / buːst / (BrE)
to improve or increase something; to help something to grow, develop, or become more successful

Examples

Subways improved land values without taking away land; freeways boost valuation less (because the garages they require are not prime buildings by a long shot), and reduce the acreage that can be taxed.
In six months O' Banion had boosted the profits to $ 100000 a month - mainly by bringing pressure to bear on fifty Chicago speak-easy proprietors to shift out to the suburb.

Synset Examples

The tax cut will boost the economy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: promote - encourage or support the growth or development of something
  • Synonym: encourage - give support or confidence to; foster growth or development
  • Antonym: hinder - make it difficult for something to happen or progress
  • Antonym: impede - to slow down or block the progress of something

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'bosten', of uncertain origin.

4. NOUN

/ bust / (AmE), / buːst / (BrE)
An increase, especially in cost or amount, such as a rise in prices or rates.

Examples

The simple passage of an additional eleven months' time makes the second 3 % boost more acceptable.

Synset Examples

they asked for a 10% rise in rates

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rise - an increase in cost or amount
  • Synonym: jump - a sudden increase, especially in amount or number
  • Antonym: drop - a reduction or decrease in amount or cost

Etymology

First recorded in the early 19th century; of uncertain origin, possibly from dialectal or obsolete forms meaning 'to push upward.'

5. VERB

/ bust / (AmE), / buːst / (BrE)
To increase or raise (something), especially by a significant or sudden amount.

Examples

It would be financed by boosting the social security payroll tax by as much as $ 37 a year for each of the workers now paying such taxes.
The Kennedy plan alone would boost the base to $ 5000 a year and the payroll tax to 6.5 per cent - 3.25 per cent each.
Higher toll rates also are helping boost revenues.

Synset Examples

The landlord hiked up the rents

Related Terms

  • Synonym: raise - to cause to increase in amount or level
  • Antonym: lower - to reduce the amount, value, or level of something

Etymology

Originally American English, early 19th century, of uncertain origin, possibly from Dutch 'boosten' (to push, to shove).

Examples

The March gain plus this optimism has been encouraging enough to prompt auto makers to boost production schedules for the next quarter.

Synset Examples

boost the voltage in an electrical circuit

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lower - to reduce something in amount, degree, or position

Etymology

Originated as American slang in the early 19th century, possibly from obsolete Dutch 'boosten' (to push).
