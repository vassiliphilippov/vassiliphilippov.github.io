bay

1. NOUN

/ beɪ / (AmE), / beɪ / (BrE)
A wide inlet of the sea or a lake that curves inward towards the land, larger than a cove but smaller than a gulf.

Examples

Mynheer, Sir Francis, the valley society, the very smell of the river on his right purling along to the bay past fish weirs and rocks, and ahead the sleepy ribbon of moon drenched road.
The morning hawk, hungry for any eatable, killable, digestible item, kept his eyes on the ring of anchored ships that lay off the shores in the bay, sheltered by the Jersey inlets.
Across the bay the Palisades were heavy in green timber; their rock paths led down to the Hudson.
Below in the open bay facing Manhattan was Staten Island, gritty with clam shells and mud flats behind which nested farms, cattle barns, and berry thickets.
The Starbird had been long at the bottom of the bay.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: gulf - a large area of sea partly surrounded by land, bigger than a bay
  • Degree Lower: cove - a small, sheltered bay

Etymology

From Old French *baee* (opening, hole) and earlier from Old Provençal *bada* (to gape, yawn).

2. NOUN

/ beɪ / (AmE), / beɪ / (BrE)
the deep, prolonged barking or howling of a dog, especially when on the scent during hunting

Examples

Or the bay of female dogs in heat.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: howl - a long, loud, mournful cry or wail, as of a dog or wolf

Etymology

From Old French 'abaiier' (to bark, howl), from 'abayer'.

3. VERB

/ beɪ / (AmE), / beɪ / (BrE)
to utter a loud, deep, prolonged sound or cry, often in anger or excitement

Examples

"Fools ", he bayed, "what do you think you are doing "?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: howl - to make a long, loud, mournful cry or wail

Etymology

From Old French *abaiier* (to bark) and related to Latin *baubari* (to bark).
