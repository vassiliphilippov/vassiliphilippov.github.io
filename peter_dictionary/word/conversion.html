conversion

1. NOUN

/ kənˈvɝʒən / (AmE), / kənˈvɜːʃən / (BrE)
(in sports) a successful attempt to score extra points immediately following a touchdown or try, such as by a kick or run in football or rugby.

Examples

He ran his string of successful conversions this season to 13 straight before one went astray last Saturday night in the 41 - 8 slaughter of Washington State.
Moritz was bothered during the first two games this year by a pulled muscle in the thigh of his right (kicking) leg and, as a result, several of his successful conversions have gone barely far enough.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extra point - an additional point scored after a touchdown in American football

Etymology

From Latin conversio (a turning around), applied metaphorically to 'turning' a try or touchdown into extra points.

2. NOUN

/ kənˈvɝːʒən / (AmE), / kənˈvɜːʃən / (BrE)
a profound change in a person's beliefs, especially the adoption of a new religious faith or a turning point in spiritual life

Examples

In spite of the increase in numbers and prestige brought about by the conversions of Newman and other Tractarians of the 1840's and 1850's, the Catholic segment of England one hundred years ago was a very small one (four per cent, or 800000) which did not enjoy a gracious hearing from the general public.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: apostasy - renunciation or abandonment of a religious faith

Etymology

From Latin conversio (a turning around), from convertere (to turn around).
