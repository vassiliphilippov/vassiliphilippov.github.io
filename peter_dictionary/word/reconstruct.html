reconstruct

1. VERB

/ ˌriːkənˈstrʌkt / (AmE), / ˌriːkənˈstrʌkt / (BrE)
to cause someone or a group to adapt or reform, especially in a social or political context

Examples

There are of course many Souths; but for this discussion the most important division is between those who have been reconstructed and those who have n't.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reform - to make changes in order to improve socially or politically
  • Synonym: rehabilitate - to restore to a former status, especially after damage or disrepute

Etymology

From Latin reconstructus, past participle of reconstruere, from re- (again) + construere (to construct).

2. VERB

/ ˌriːkənˈstrʌkt / (AmE), / ˌriːkənˈstrʌkt / (BrE)
to form an impression or understanding of a past event by piecing together available information or evidence

Examples

It is difficult to reconstruct the primeval fears of man.
Jubal could n't reconstruct the crime from the way the girls behaved because patterns kept shifting - ABC vs D, then BCD vs A & & & or AB vs CD, or AD vs CB, through all ways that four women can gang up on each other.
The historian's business is to penetrate to the inside of the actions with which he is dealing and reconstruct or rather rethink the thoughts which constituted them.

Synset Examples

reconstruct the events of 20 years ago

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reimagine - to form a new conception or mental picture of something based on partial information

Etymology

From Latin re- (again) + construere (to build).

3. VERB

/ ˌriː.kənˈstrʌkt / (AmE), / ˌriː.kənˈstrʌkt / (BrE)
to build or put something together again after it has been damaged or destroyed

Examples

I reconstruct his sentences from notes made immediately after our conversation.

Synset Examples

The house was rebuild after it was hit by a bomb

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rebuild - to build something again after it has been damaged or destroyed

Etymology

From Latin 're-' (again) + 'construere' (to build).
