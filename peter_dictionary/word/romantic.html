romantic

1. ADJ

/ roʊˈmæntɪk / (AmE), / rəʊˈmæntɪk / (BrE)
Relating to or characteristic of Romanticism or the Romantic movement in the arts and literature, marked by emphasis on emotion, imagination, and individualism.

Examples

The concert opened with another big romantic score, Schumann's Overture to Manfred, which suffered fate, this time with orchestral thrusts to the Byronic point to keep it afloat.
Those accustomed to broader, more romantic statements of the symphony can be expected to react strongly when they hear this one.
Thorpe came to Louisiana from the East as a young man prepared to find in the new country the setting of romantic adventure and idealized beauty.
And like Jo March, who saw her sisters Meg and Amy involved in "lovering "before herself, Henrietta saw her sisters Rachel and Sadie drawn outside their family circle by the attraction of suitors, Rachel by Joe Jastrow, and Sadie by Max Lobl, a young businessman who would write her romantic descriptions of his trips by steamboat down the Mississippi.
After all, Shelley is no "orthodox "or Hellenic Platonist, and even his "romantic "Platonism can be distinguished from that of his contemporaries.

Synset Examples

romantic poetry

Related Terms

  • Antonym: classical - relating to Classical period or style, especially in the arts

Etymology

From French 'romantique', from Medieval Latin 'romanticus', originally referring to the romance languages.

2. NOUN

/ roʊˈmæntɪk / (AmE), / rəʊˈmantɪk / (BrE)
A person who is guided by idealism, emotion, or a love of romance rather than practicality or realism.

Examples

Mickie had a pleasant glow as he said, "You see, both of them, I mean the President and Jeff Lawrence, are romantics.
A romantic is one who thinks the world is divinely inspired and all he has to do is find the right key, and then divine justice and altruism will appear.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: realist - a person who accepts the world as it is and deals with it accordingly

Etymology

From French romantique, from Medieval Latin romanticus, from Latin Romanicus (Roman).

3. NOUN

/ roʊˈmæntɪk / (AmE), / rəʊˈmæntɪk / (BrE)
an artist, writer, or thinker associated with or influenced by the Romantic Movement of the late 18th and early 19th centuries.

Examples

English philosopher Samuel Alexander's debt to Wordsworth and Meredith is a recent interesting example, as also A. N. Whitehead's understanding of the English romantics, chiefly Shelley and Wordsworth.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Romanticism - the artistic and intellectual movement emphasizing emotion and individualism

Etymology

From French romantique, ultimately from medieval Latin romanticus ('in the Roman manner'), referring to the qualities of medieval romance literature.

4. ADJ

/ rəˈmæntɪk / (AmE), / rəˈmæntɪk / (BrE)
expressive of or exciting sexual love or romance; demonstrating affection or passion in a manner characteristic of romance

Examples

I would have foregone my romantic chances rather than leave a friend sweltering and dusty and - Well, at least I would n't have shouted back a taunt.
He had picked out this pathless trail, instead of the common one, in a moment of romantic fancy, to give them privacy on their honeymoon.

Synset Examples

her amatory affairs
amorous glances
a romantic adventure
a romantic moonlight ride

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amorous - showing, feeling, or relating to sexual desire
  • Synonym: passionate - showing or caused by strong feelings or a strong belief, especially of love
  • Antonym: platonic - (of love or friendship) intimate and affectionate but not sexual

Etymology

From French *romantique*, from medieval Latin *romanticus*, from Latin *romanice* (in the Roman manner).
