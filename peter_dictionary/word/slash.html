slash

1. VERB

/ slæʃ / (AmE), / slæʃ / (BrE)
to cut with a sweeping, forceful stroke, especially with something like a knife, sword, or axe

Examples

In the fifth, Wally Post slashed a 2 - run homer off Bud Daley, but by that time the score was 11 - 5 and it really did n't matter.
There was a little blood on the hem of her dress, for the assassin had slashed Miss Harris's companion, Major Rathbone, with a knife.
She brought the quirt down, slashing it across his cheek, and he tried to step back.
Mike took the bayonet from Dean's hand and slashed the picket line.
Mike ran down the line, slashing picket ropes with the bayonet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: slice - to cut through something smoothly, especially with a sharp instrument

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *slaschen*, of imitative origin.

2. VERB

/ slæʃ / (AmE), / slæʃ / (BrE)
to cut or wound something with a violent, sweeping stroke, often making a long, deep opening

Examples

Off in the distance a searchlight flashed on, its beam slashing the sky.
He saw only dimly moving figures, but when he slashed them they yelled and fled.
It slashed the sloping manure scented lawns with concrete steps which climbed upward to white wooden porches.

Synset Examples

she slashed her wrists

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to make an opening or wound with a sharp-edged tool

Etymology

From Middle English *slashen*, of imitative origin or influenced by Old French *esclachier* (to break, smash).

3. VERB

/ slæʃ / (AmE), / slæʃ / (BrE)
to strike forcefully with a sharp object, such as a knife, blade, or whip

Examples

He drew back his arm to slash the gunbarrel across Jess's face, but did n't finish the motion.
He hunched his left shoulder into it and slashed at Roberts' forearm with his own, felt the blade slide off his sleeve.
Roberts sagged and slashed at him wildly.

Synset Examples

The teacher often flogged the students
The children were severely trounced

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to divide or incise with a sharp instrument

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English slaschen, probably imitative or related to Old French esclachier (to break, burst).

4. VERB

/ slæʃ / (AmE), / slæʃ / (BrE)
to reduce something by a large amount, especially in a sudden or forceful way

Examples

Paradoxically, the sales rise is due in large measure to Government efforts to slash farm output.
Within a year, without reducing wages, Underwood's production costs were cut one third, prices were slashed.

Synset Examples

Prices were slashed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to reduce the amount of something
  • Antonym: increase - to make larger in amount or degree

Etymology

Originally meaning ‘to cut with a sweeping stroke’, extended in 20th century to mean ‘reduce drastically’.
