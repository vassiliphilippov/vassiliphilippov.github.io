disperse

1. VERB

/ dɪˈspɝs / (AmE), / dɪˈspɜːs / (BrE)
to cause to break up and spread in different directions; to scatter

Examples

Freed soil must be dispersed and protected against flocculation.

Synset Examples

break up kidney stones
disperse particles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or spread things over an area
  • Antonym: collect - to gather things together

Etymology

From Latin dispergere (to scatter, spread about), from dis- (apart) + spargere (to scatter).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈspɝs / (AmE), / dɪˈspɜːs / (BrE)
to cause to separate and move in different directions, especially over a wide area

Examples

Andy had no desire to linger himself but Hub reported that the mob outside was still large despite the efforts of the police to disperse them.

Synset Examples

She waved her hand and scattered the crowds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or spread thinly or irregularly in all directions

Etymology

From Latin 'dispersus', past participle of 'dispergere' (to scatter, spread abroad).

3. VERB

/ dɪsˈpɝs / (AmE), / dɪsˈpɜːs / (BrE)
to distribute or spread something or people over a wide area; to scatter in different directions

Examples

With troops dispersed on fields of battle rather than on the parade ground, it may seem that a certain amount of monitoring is automatically enforced by the lines of communication.
If we could use all the small airfields we have in this country, we could disperse our strategic aircraft by a factor of 10 or more.

Synset Examples

He scattered gun powder under the wagon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or distribute things so that they cover an area randomly; very similar meaning
  • Antonym: gather - to bring things or people together in one place; opposite of scatter or disperse

Etymology

From Latin dispersus, past participle of dispergere 'to scatter'.
