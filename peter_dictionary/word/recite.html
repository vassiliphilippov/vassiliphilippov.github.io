recite

1. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to say aloud from memory, especially in a formal or public setting; to deliver something such as a poem or passage in front of an audience, often using expressive speech

Examples

It has been my experience to find as many men as women in church, and to hear almost everyone in church congregations reciting the Latin prayers and responses at Mass.
Sadie studied piano; played Chopin in the "Soiree Musicale of Mr. Guthrie's Pupils "; and she recited "Hector's Farewell to Andromache "most movingly, to the special delight of Rabbi Jastrow at his home in Germantown near Philadelphia, where the Szold girls took turns visiting between the visits of the Jastrow boys at the Szolds' in Baltimore.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: declaim - to speak rhetorically, often in a loud or formal manner
  • Degree Lower: read - to look at and comprehend the meaning of written matter; to say aloud written or printed words

Etymology

From Latin recitare (to read out, repeat); from re- (again) + citare (to call, summon).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈsaɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈsaɪt / (BrE)
to say aloud something learned or memorized, especially before an audience or as part of a formal exercise

Examples

They hope, of course, to reclaim the non-Catholic population to the Catholic faith, and at every Sunday Benediction they recite by heart the "Prayer for England ":
To recite the particulars of recent Soviet successes is hardly reassuring.
[DOOR SLAMMING] [GULLS CAWING] [SINGING IN ITALIAN] [CHURCH ORGAN PLAYING] [PRIEST RECITING IN LATIN] [SPEAKING LATIN] [UPBEAT MUSIC PLAYING] MAN: Did you bring the car keys?

Synset Examples

she recited a poem
The pupil recited his lesson for the day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: read - to look at and comprehend the meaning of written or printed matter
  • Synonym: recitation - the act of reciting from memory, especially publicly
  • Antonym: improvise - to create or perform spontaneously, without preparation

Etymology

From Latin recitāre (to read out, recite).
