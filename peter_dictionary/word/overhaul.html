overhaul

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈhɔl / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈhɔːl / (BrE)
to catch up with and pass while traveling in the same direction

Examples

I let up on the accelerator, only to gradually reach again the 60 m. p. h. which would, I hoped, overhaul Herry and the blonde, and as there were cars whose drivers apparently had something more important to catch than had I, Mrs. Major Roebuck settled down to practicing on Corporal Johnson the kittenish wiles she would need when making her duty call on Colonel and Mrs. Somebody in Sante Fe.

Synset Examples

The sports car passed all the trucks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overtake - to catch up with and pass; especially used in British English

Etymology

From over- (above, beyond) + haul (to pull or drag), early 17th century.

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvɚˈhɔl / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈhɔːl / (BrE)
to examine thoroughly and make necessary repairs, renovations, or revisions to something

Examples

After drastically overhauling NATO, Western leadership should turn to reducing the suspicions that tear apart the East and West.

Synset Examples

You should overhaul your car engine
overhaul the health care system

Related Terms

  • Synonym: renovate - to restore to a good state by repair or remodeling
  • Synonym: repair - to fix or mend something damaged or broken
  • Antonym: neglect - to fail to care for or repair something

Etymology

From 'overhaul,' originally nautical, meaning to slacken (a rope) by pulling it over, later generalized to thorough examination and repair.
