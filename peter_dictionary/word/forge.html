forge

1. VERB

/ fɔrdʒ / (AmE), / fɔːdʒ / (BrE)
to invent or devise (an idea, plan, or explanation) with considerable effort or ingenuity

Examples

When Prokofieff forged his new clarity of "lucid, straightforward music, so difficult to compose ", he shaped his talents to his purpose.

Synset Examples

excogitate a way to measure the speed of light

Related Terms

  • Synonym: devise - to plan or invent by careful thought
  • Synonym: conceive - to form an idea or plan in the mind
  • Synonym: fabricate - to invent or concoct, typically with deceitful intent

Etymology

From Old French *forgier*, from Latin *fabricare* (to shape, build)

2. VERB

/ fɔrdʒ / (AmE), / fɔːdʒ / (BrE)
to make a fake copy of something, especially a document, signature, or work of art, with the intention of deceiving others

Examples

On Election Day - November 4 - he energetically marshalled his force of bludgeon men, bribers, and experts in forging repeat votes.

Synset Examples

he faked the signature
they counterfeited dollar bills
She forged a Green Card

Related Terms

  • Synonym: counterfeit - to make an exact imitation of something valuable with the intention to deceive or defraud
  • Synonym: fake - to make something that is not genuine, intended to deceive
  • Antonym: authenticate - to prove that something is genuine or real

Etymology

From Old French *forgier* (to forge, create), from Latin *fabricare* (to make, build).

3. VERB

/ fɔrdʒ / (AmE), / fɔːdʒ / (BrE)
to shape metal by heating and hammering; to make or form something with concentrated effort, especially using metalworking techniques

Examples

This tragic lad had forged his own shackles.

Synset Examples

hammer the silver into a bowl
forge a pair of tongues

Related Terms

  • Synonym: smith - to shape metal by heating and hammering, as a blacksmith does
  • Antonym: cast - to shape metal by pouring it into a mold

Etymology

From Latin *fabrica* (workshop), via Old French *forgier* (to forge, make).
