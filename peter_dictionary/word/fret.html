fret

1. VERB

/ frɛt / (AmE), / frɛt / (BrE)
to worry or be anxious, especially about unimportant matters

Examples

He would wake up in the middle of the night and fret about it.

Synset Examples

don't fuss too much over the grandchildren--they are quite big now

Related Terms

  • Synonym: worry - to feel anxious or uneasy
  • Synonym: fuss - to show unnecessary concern or activity about something

Etymology

From Old English 'fretan', meaning 'to devour', later developing the sense of being consumed by worry.

2. VERB

/ frɛt / (AmE), / frɛt / (BrE)
to be visibly worried, anxious, or irritated, especially about something unimportant

Examples

A year ago today, when the Democrats were fretting and frolicking in Los Angeles and John F. Kennedy was still only an able and ambitious Senator who yearned for the power and responsibility of the Presidency, Theodore H. White had already compiled masses of notes about the Presidential campaign of 1960.

Synset Examples

don't fret over these small details

Related Terms

  • Synonym: worry - to feel anxious or troubled about actual or potential problems
  • Degree Higher: agonize - to experience great mental distress about a decision or event
  • Antonym: relax - to make or become less anxious or tense

Etymology

From Old English 'fretan' (to devour, eat up), later influenced by Middle English 'freten' (to gnaw).
