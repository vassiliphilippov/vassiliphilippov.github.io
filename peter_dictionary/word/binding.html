binding

1. NOUN

/ ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (BrE)
The capacity of a substance to attract and hold another substance, often used in scientific contexts such as chemistry or biochemistry.

Examples

one of extremely low anionic binding capacity, one of low anionic binding capacity and one of high anionic binding capacity.
It has been shown that thyroglobulin binds thyroxine, but the binding does not appear to be particularly strong.
It has been suggested that the plasma thyroxine binding proteins, which have an extremely high affinity for thyroxine, compete with thyroglobulin for thyroxine (Ingbar and Freinkel, 1957).
This project was carried out under sponsorship of the Ballistic Missile Division of the Air Research and Development Command, U. S. Air Force, and had as its goal the investigation of the transport by diffusion of the heat energy of chemical binding.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: affinity - the degree to which a substance tends to combine with another

Etymology

From bind + -ing, denoting the process or capacity of binding.

2. ADJ

/ ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (BrE)
Having legal or formal force; involving an obligation that must be carried out.

Examples

Among primitive peoples the sanctions and dictates of religion were more binding than any of the other controls exercised by the group; and in modern societies such influence is still great.

Synset Examples

a binding contract

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nonbinding - not having legal or formal force; not obligatory
  • Synonym: enforceable - able to be imposed or carried out by law
  • Synonym: mandatory - required by law or rules; compulsory

Etymology

Present participle of 'bind', from Old English 'bindan' (to tie, fasten).

3. NOUN

/ ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (AmE), / ˈbaɪndɪŋ / (BrE)
A strip of material sewn over or along the edge of something, especially a piece of fabric, for reinforcement, protection, or decoration.

Examples

Miss Ada was looking fine; she had on her Easter suit, blue, with lavender binding.

Etymology

From bind + -ing, formed from the verb 'bind' (Old English bindan).
