moderate

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɑːdərət / (AmE), / ˈmɒdərət / (BrE)
average in amount, intensity, quality, or degree; not excessive or extreme

Examples

English Catholics reacted to this event with moderate but real hope.
Pansies do n't have to be coddled; they 'd rather have things rugged, with only moderate protection on the coldest days.
Three seconds flat is the usual time, and the space is crossed by moderate mileage, while the overwhelming immensity of such journeys must be conceived as a static pulsation through an enormous number of coexistent frequencies which perpetuate all events.
There was a slight lapse condition, a moderate fog, and 100 per cent relative humidity.
It is convenient to classify a child's onset ages and completion ages as "advanced ", "moderate " (modal), or "delayed "according to whether the child's age equivalent "dots "appeared to the left of, upon, or to the right of the appropriate short transverse line.

Synset Examples

moderate prices
a moderate income
a moderate fine
moderate demands
a moderate estimate
a moderate eater
moderate success
a kitchen of moderate size
the X-ray showed moderate enlargement of the heart

Related Terms

  • Synonym: average - of a level typical or normal; not extreme
  • Antonym: excessive - greater than what is necessary, normal, or desirable
  • Degree Lower: mild - not severe, strongly felt, or extreme
  • Degree Higher: severe - very great; intense or extreme

Etymology

From Latin moderatus (restrained, within bounds), past participle of moderari (to moderate, restrain).

2. ADJ

/ ˈmɑdərət / (AmE), / ˈmɒdərət / (BrE)
Not extreme; within reasonable or average limits.

Examples

But apart from racial problems, the old unreconstructed South - to use the moderate words favored by Mr. Thomas Griffith - finds itself unsympathetic to most of what is different about the civilization of the North.
However, they were hostile to Jewish Orthodoxy, professing to believe in Judaism "but in a moderate way ".

Synset Examples

a moderate penalty
temperate in his response to criticism

Related Terms

  • Antonym: extreme - very great in degree, exceeding the ordinary
  • Synonym: temperate - showing moderation or self-restraint
  • Degree Lower: mild - not severe or strong; gentle

Etymology

From Latin moderatus, past participle of moderari (to control, regulate).

3. NOUN

/ ˈmɑːdərət / (AmE), / ˈmɒdərət / (BrE)
a person whose political views are not extreme and who favors moderate or centrist positions

Examples

A perceptive journalist, Sam Lubell, has phrased it in the title of one of his books as The Revolt Of The Moderates.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: centrist - a person who holds moderate political views
  • Antonym: extremist - a person with extreme political views
  • Antonym: radical - a person with far-reaching political views

Etymology

From Latin moderatus (kept within measure), past participle of moderari (to moderate, control).

4. VERB

/ ˈmɑd.əˌreɪt / (AmE), / ˈmɒd.ər.eɪt / (BrE)
to preside over a discussion, meeting, or debate to ensure it proceeds in an organized, fair, and effective manner

Examples

Tonight Atlantic Monthly editor Edward Weeks moderates a round table of four Russian writers in a discussion of Soviet literature.

Synset Examples

John moderated the discussion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chair - to preside over a meeting or discussion

Etymology

From Latin moderari (to manage, regulate, moderate).
