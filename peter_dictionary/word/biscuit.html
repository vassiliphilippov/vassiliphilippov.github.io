biscuit

1. NOUN

/ ˈbɪskɪt / (AmE), / ˈbɪskɪt / (BrE)
A small, soft bread made with baking powder or baking soda, typically round, often eaten at breakfast or with meals in American English.

Examples

Virginia treated him with attention and tried to tempt his appetite with special food: biscuits, cookies, candies - the result of devoted hours in the tiled kitchen.
Young Mrs. Arthur had opened the oven and there was a drifting odor of hot biscuits.
He was readying a batch of sourdough biscuits for the Dutch oven.
He poured the water off the sourdough and off the flour, salvaging the chunky, watery messes for biscuits of a sort.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scone - a small, lightly sweetened cake similar to American biscuit (British usage)
  • Antonym: biscuit - a sweet, crisp baked good (British usage, 'cookie' in American English)

Etymology

From Old French *bescuit* ('twice-cooked'), from Latin *bis* ('twice') + *coquere* ('to cook'), though American meaning evolved in the 19th century.

2. NOUN

/ ˈbɪs.kɪt / (AmE), / ˈbɪs.kɪt / (BrE)
(British English) A small flat sweet baked cake, typically crisp and eaten as a snack; known as a 'cookie' in American English.

Examples

The Ferraros offered them biscuits with the coffee.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cookie - (American English) sweet baked treat similar to a British biscuit

Etymology

From Old French *bescuit* (‘twice cooked’), from Latin *bis* (‘twice’) + *coquere* (‘to cook’).
