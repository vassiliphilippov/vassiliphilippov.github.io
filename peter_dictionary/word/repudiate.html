repudiate

1. VERB

/ rɪˈpjuːdiˌeɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈpjuːdieɪt / (BrE)
To refuse to accept, support, or be associated with; to reject or disown formally.

Examples

Those in power tend to procrastinate and even to repudiate the electoral process.
If of the founders of glottochronology Swadesh has escaped our steady plodding, and Lees has repudiated his own share in the founding, that is no reason why we should swerve.
Such a decision should have placed a powerful weapon in the hands of the entire housing industry, but there is little evidence that realtors, or at least their associations, have repudiated the principle in such clauses.

Synset Examples

She renounced her husband
The parents repudiated their son

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disown - to refuse to acknowledge or accept as one's own
  • Antonym: accept - to receive or agree to willingly

Etymology

From Latin repudiatus, past participle of repudiare (to cast off, divorce), from repudium (divorce, rejection).

2. VERB

/ rɪˈpjuːdieɪt / (AmE), / rɪˈpjuːdieɪt / (BrE)
To refuse to acknowledge, accept, or recognize as valid; to reject the truth, authority, or legality of something.

Examples

In the light of these circumstances, as well as the fact that the issue at trial in this respect centered entirely on the Department's recommendation, which petitioner repudiated but which both the appeal board and the courts below found supported by the record, we find no relevancy in the hearing officer's report and notes.

Synset Examples

The woman repudiated the divorce settlement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reject - to refuse to accept, believe in, or agree with something
  • Antonym: accept - to consent to receive or recognize something as valid

Etymology

From Latin repudiatus, past participle of repudiare (to cast off, reject), from repudium (divorce, rejection).
