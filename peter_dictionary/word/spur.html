spur

1. NOUN

/ spɝ / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
something that encourages or stimulates action or effort; a motivating influence.

Examples

In the field of entertainment there is no spur to financial daring so effective as audience boredom, and the first decade of the new device was not over before audiences began staying away in large numbers from the simple-minded, one minute shows.
By her eighteenth birthday her bent for writing was so evident that Papa and Mamma gave her a Life of Dickens as a spur to her aspiration.
The Title 8, program of the National Defense Education Act of 1958 was a great spur to this trend toward area schools.

Synset Examples

the ceaseless prodding got on his nerves

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incentive - a thing that motivates or encourages someone to do something
  • Synonym: motivation - the reason or reasons for acting or behaving in a particular way
  • Antonym: deterrent - a thing that discourages or is intended to discourage someone from doing something

Etymology

From Old English 'spura', 'spura' (“spur, spurn”), related to the verb 'spurnan' (“to kick, spur”), originally referring to the metal tool but later used figuratively.

2. NOUN

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
a short railway line that branches off from a main line, typically to serve a particular industrial or commercial site

Examples

But anybody who promises a substantial volume of business can get a railroad to run a short spur to his plant these days, and many businesses can live without the railroad.

Etymology

From Old English *spura* (metal point worn on the heel), figuratively applied to a projection or branch.

3. VERB

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
to urge a horse to move faster by pressing spurs into its sides

Examples

He unhitched his horse, walked it away, mounted, and spurred it on.

Synset Examples

the rider spurred his horse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: goad - to provoke or urge into action

Etymology

From Old English *spura* (spur), related to Middle Low German *spōr* and Old Norse *spori*.

4. VERB

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
To strike a horse with a spur in order to urge it forward or increase its speed.

Examples

He came spurring and whooping down the road, his horse kicking up clouds of dust, shouting:

Related Terms

  • Synonym: urge - to encourage or force someone or something to move or act more quickly

Etymology

From Old English spura, spora (spur), related to the action of prodding with a spur.

5. NOUN

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
a sharp, pointed projection from a surface or structure, as found on animals, plants, buildings, or geographical formations

Examples

And all this too shall pass away: it came to him out of some dim corner of memory from a church service when he was a boy - yes, in a white church with a thin spur steeple in the patriarchal Hudson Valley, where a feeling of plenitude was normal in those English Dutch manors with their well-fed squires.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: projection - something that juts out from a surface
  • Synonym: protuberance - something that sticks out from a surface

Etymology

From Old English spura, spura ‘spike, spur’, related to spurnan ‘to kick’.

6. NOUN

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
a tubular or slender pointed outgrowth at the base of the corolla or petal in some flowers, often containing nectar

Examples

Pale yellow snapdragons that by pinching could be made to bite; seed-pods of the balsams that snapped like fire-crackers at a touch; red and yellow columbines whose round tipped spurs were picked off and eaten for the honey in them; morning-glory buds which could be so grasped and squeezed that they burst like a blown-up paper bag; bright flowers from the trumpet vine that made "gloves "on the ends of ten waggling fingers.

7. NOUN

/ spɝ / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
a small, pointed device attached to the heel of a rider's boot, used to urge a horse forward

Examples

Clapping spurs to the bronc he set off at a sharp canter, with growing alarm.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rowel - the small, rotating wheel at the end of some spurs

Etymology

From Old English *spura*, related to Old Norse *spori* and German *Sporen* (spur).

8. VERB

/ spɝː / (AmE), / spɜː / (BrE)
to encourage or incite someone to take action or put in more effort

Examples

But now, for the first time since Lou Gehrig (with 47 home runs) spurred Ruth on in 1927, two men playing for the same team have zeroed in on 60.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motivate - stimulate interest or enthusiasm for doing something
  • Antonym: discourage - cause someone to lose confidence or enthusiasm

Etymology

From Old English spura (prick, goad), related to Middle Low German spore (spur).

Examples

This includes foreseeing balance-of-payments crises, with adequate attention to reducing dependence on imports and adopting realistic exchange rates to encourage infant industries and spur exports.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stimulate - to raise levels of physiological or nervous activity in; to encourage interest or activity
  • Antonym: deter - to discourage someone from taking action

Etymology

From Old English spura (metal point for urging a horse); figurative sense developed later.
