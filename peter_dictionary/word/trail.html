trail

1. VERB

/ treɪl / (AmE), / treɪl / (BrE)
to fall behind or move more slowly than others, especially in a competition, race, or pursuit

Examples

Momentarily the Reds were back in the ball game, trailing only 6 - 3, but the drive fizzled when John Edwards fouled out with men on second and third and two out.
He trailed Ludie to the baseball game in the lot on Kingston Street near the Dutch Reformed.

Except for a few months in late 1960 and early 1961, retail farm equipment sales have trailed year-earlier levels since the latter part of 1959.

Synset Examples

But in so many other areas we still are dragging

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lead - to be ahead in a competition, race, or pursuit

Etymology

From Middle English 'traylen,' likely from Old French 'trailler' (to tow, drag).

2. NOUN

/ treɪl / (AmE), / treɪl / (BrE)
A mark or line left on a surface by something that has passed, such as a physical track, mark, or evidence of passage.

Examples

It grows terribly and unavoidably in power and leaves in its wake a trail of misery, moral disintegration, and destruction.
The mass scale used in Table 5 - 1 was derived on the assumption that the motion of the glowing trail is related to the momentum transfer to the trail by the meteorite, permitting the calculation of the mass if the velocity is known (Cook and Whipple, 1958).

Related Terms

4. NOUN

/ treɪl / (AmE), / treɪl / (BrE)
a series of signs or evidence left behind that can be followed to discover or solve something, especially in the context of an investigation

Examples

Before daylight Sunday morning, a posse of twenty-three men under the leadership of Deputy Sheriff Frank MacPherson of Catskill followed the trail to the house of Francisco Chaves, where 100 to 150 Mexicans had gathered.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lead - a piece of information or evidence that may help solve a problem or crime
  • Synonym: clue - a fact or object that helps to solve mysteries or problems

5. VERB

/ treɪl / (AmE), / treɪl / (BrE)
to follow someone or something in order to catch or find them, often by tracking signs left behind

Examples

Finding him dead, Cook caught Russell's horse and rode to the cattle foreman's house to report the incident and request bloodhounds to trail the assassins.
No ringers, either - even if they are trailing legitimate invitees.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chase - to pursue in order to catch or catch up with
  • Synonym: pursue - to follow with the intention of catching or achieving
  • Antonym: lead - to be ahead of or guide (antonym of follow/trail)

Etymology

From Old French 'trailer' (to tow, pull) and medieval Latin 'trahiculare', from Latin 'trahere' (to pull, drag).
