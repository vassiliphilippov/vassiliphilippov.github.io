tunnel

1. NOUN

/ ˈtʌn.əl / (AmE), / ˈtʌn.əl / (BrE)
An underground or enclosed passageway, typically one for vehicles, trains, or pedestrians, passing through or under an obstacle such as a hill or building.

Examples

The bottom part of a burrow has a number of side tunnels or "cells ", each of which is provided with an egg plus a store of beebread.

Synset Examples

the tunnel reduced congestion at that intersection

Related Terms

  • Synonym: subway - an underground railway system or its tunnel
  • Antonym: bridge - a structure carrying a pathway or road over an obstacle (opposite in function to tunnel)

Etymology

From Old French 'tonnel' (tube, pipe), from Medieval Latin 'tonnellum', diminutive of Latin 'tonna' (cask, barrel).

2. VERB

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
to force or make a way through or under something, often by digging as if making a tunnel

Examples

Only paved alleyways tunneled through the walls of those fortresses into the mysterious core of intimacy behind the houses where backyards owned no fences, where one man's property blended with the next to form courtyards in which no one knew privacy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bore - to make a hole by drilling or digging
  • Synonym: penetrate - to force or make a way into or through something

Etymology

From Middle English 'tonnel', from Old French 'tonnel' (diminutive of 'ton', meaning cask, barrel).

3. VERB

/ ˈtʌnəl / (AmE), / ˈtʌnəl / (BrE)
to make or force a way through something, especially by digging a tunnel

Examples

Below, twenty vaults tunnel through the understructure of the bridge.
I remember thinking it would take a man 600 years to tunnel through the wall with it.

Synset Examples

burrow through the forest

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burrow - to make a hole or passage through by digging, especially for shelter; to excavate

Etymology

From Old French tonnelle (subterranean passage), ultimately from Latin tunna (barrel).
