desert

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɛzɚt / (AmE), / ˈdezət / (BrE)
A large area of land, typically covered with sand or rocks, that has very little rainfall and few or no plants.

Examples

A site may be a rundown slum or a desolate piece of desert in appearance today but have excellent potentials for the future with a little development or water.
Other natural features which can be of high interest are the forests, canyons, mountains, deserts, seacoast, beaches, sand dunes, waterfalls, springs, etcetera with which the area is blessed.
Tolek Alterman had returned from the colonies in Palestine and, before the national leadership, exalted the miracles of drying up swamps and irrigating the desert.
But I suspect that the old Roman was referring to change made under military occupation - the sort of change which Tacitus was talking about when he said, "They make a desert, and call it peace " ("Solitudinem faciunt, pacem appellant ".)
Later on he had an idea to build a city out of a desert stop-over for G.I.s on the way to the West Coast.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: oasis - a fertile area in a desert, where water is available
  • Synonym: wasteland - land that is barren or neglected, often similar to a desert

Etymology

From Old French 'desert', from Late Latin 'desertum' (something left waste), from Latin 'deserere' (to abandon).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈzɝt / (AmE), / dɪˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to leave someone who needs or depends on you; to abandon, especially when they are in need

Examples

When consciousness deserts the sleeping body and the wakeful world, it continues in the myriad progressions of the ever-present past and future, in a life as vibrant and real as the one left when the body tired and required sleep.
Kate felt she had deserted the boy in her own loss.
Occasionally she deserts the simple and elegant for a fun piece simple because "It's unlike me ".
The thousands of city migrants who desert the farms yearly must readjust with even greater stress and tension: the sacred wilderness is gradually surrendering to suburbs and research parks and industrial areas.

Synset Examples

The mother deserted her children

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abandon - to leave someone or something behind, especially permanently or in a difficult situation

Etymology

From Old French 'deserter', from Late Latin 'desertare' (to abandon).

3. VERB

/ dɪˈzɝt / (AmE), / dɪˈzɜːt / (BrE)
to abandon a person, cause, group, or place, especially in a way considered disloyal or treacherous, often in order to join an opposing side

Examples

The mate, Robert Juet, who had kept the journal on the half Moon, was experienced - but he was a bitter old man, ready to complain or desert at any opportunity.

Synset Examples

If soldiers deserted Hitler's army, they were shot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abandon - to leave behind or give up completely
  • Synonym: betray - to be disloyal or treacherous to a person or group

Etymology

From Old French *deserter*, from Late Latin *desertare*, frequentative of *deserere* (to abandon).
