clap

1. VERB

/ klæp / (AmE), / klæp / (BrE)
to strike the palms of one's hands together, typically to show appreciation or in time with music

Examples

Someone clapped tentatively then quickly stopped.

Synset Examples

The children were clapping to the music

Related Terms

  • Synonym: applaud - to show approval by clapping the hands

Etymology

From Middle English clappen, from Old English clappan (to throb, clap), of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ klæp / (AmE), / klæp / (BrE)
To strike the palms of one's hands together repeatedly to show approval or enjoyment, especially after a performance.

Examples

But they kept on clapping for a long, long time.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: applaud - to express approval by clapping hands
  • Antonym: boo - to express disapproval, often vocally in a performance

Etymology

From Middle English *clappen*, from Old English *clappan* (to clap, slap, make a noise).

3. NOUN

/ klæp / (AmE), / klæp / (BrE)
A sudden, very loud noise, especially one caused by thunder, explosions, or a sharp striking action.

Examples

He hardly noticed the blue green flashes of lightning and the hard claps of thunder.
Still a quarter-mile away, the fresh clap of guns only served to increase his speed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bang - a sudden loud noise, especially a sharp or explosive sound
  • Synonym: crash - a sudden loud noise of something breaking or heavy impact

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, echoing the sound of a sudden strike or noise.

4. VERB

/ klæp / (AmE), / klæp / (BrE)
to strike or bring together quickly and forcefully, as in clapping wings or spurs, often producing a sharp sound or motion

Examples

Clapping spurs to the bronc he set off at a sharp canter, with growing alarm.

Synset Examples

The big bird clapped its wings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flap - to move quickly up and down, especially wings

Etymology

From Old English *clappian* (to throb, beat), of imitative origin.

5. VERB

/ klæp / (AmE), / klæp / (BrE)
To put or place something quickly or forcibly, often in a sudden or decisive manner.

Examples

Silence came into the forest - a solid being that clapped its hand over the murmuring mouths of the birds and the whispered comfort of the trees.

Synset Examples

The judge clapped him in jail

Etymology

From Middle English 'clappen', from Old English 'clæppan', meaning 'to throb or strike'.
