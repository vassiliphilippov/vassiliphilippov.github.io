roar

1. VERB

/ ɹɔɹ / (AmE), / ɹɔː / (BrE)
to make a long, loud, deep sound or cry, as an animal or a person might when angry, in pain, or full of emotion

Examples

I do n't know whether he was after our rider, who had gone by a minute before, or whether he was simply scouting conditions; but when he passed us by, a musket roared, and he reared his horse, swung it around, and began to whip it back in the direction from which he had come.
Cousin Simmons roared.
"Arianist "! a rowdy with a big blob of a nose roared.
And in the role of Will Danaher, Philip Bosco roars and sneers sufficiently to intimidate not only one American but the whole British army, if he chose.
They went along the pass talking with each other for a short while until they came to a river roaring with torrent.

Synset Examples

wail in self-pity
howl with sorrow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: howl - emit a long, loud, mournful cry
  • Synonym: bellow - to shout loudly or make a deep loud sound, like an animal
  • Antonym: whisper - to speak or say something very quietly

Etymology

From Old English 'rarian', of Germanic origin; related to Dutch 'razen' and German 'rasen'.

2. VERB

/ rɔr / (AmE), / rɔː / (BrE)
(of wind, water, vehicles, etc.) to make a very loud, deep, continuing noise.

Examples

What did it matter to him that the park at the foot of Ash Road stretched beneath elevated trains that roared from the stucco station into the city's center at half-hour intervals?
Nearly twenty-five years before, Captain John Davis had noted, as he sailed near the Arctic Circle, "a very great gulf, the water whirling and roaring, as it were the meeting of tides ".
Many of them paid for their curiosity with their lives, for some minutes later the first giant wave roared over the shore.
Even as the conviction of truth roared through him, shattering his last hope of safety, he was reaching to release the hand brake, to head up the road for home, doing her bidding.
Unsinkable slowed and stopped, hundreds of brilliant white flares swayed eerily down from the black, the air raid sirens ashore rose in a keening shriek, the anti-aircraft guns coughed and chattered - and above it all motors roared and the bombs came whispering and wailing and crashing down among the ships at anchor at Bari.

Synset Examples

The wind was howling in the trees
The water roared down the chute

Related Terms

  • Synonym: howl - make a long, loud, wailing noise (of wind or animals)
  • Synonym: rumble - make a low, continuous, heavy sound
  • Antonym: whisper - speak very softly or make a soft sound (like wind)

Etymology

From Old English *rārian*; related to Dutch *razen* (to rave), German *rohren* (to roar).

3. NOUN

/ rɔr / (AmE), / rɔː / (BrE)
a very loud, deep noise like that made by a wild animal

Examples

With a roar of pain and fury Jess made his attack.

Synset Examples

his bellow filled the hallway

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bellow - a deep loud roar, typically in pain or anger
  • Synonym: growl - a low, guttural sound made by an animal or person

Etymology

From Middle English roren, from Old English rārian (to bellow, cry loudly).

4. NOUN

/ rɔr / (AmE), / rɔː / (BrE)
a deep, prolonged, and loud noise, often made by an animal, a crowd, or natural forces such as wind or water

Examples

The control tower gave him immediate take-off permission, and the clean roar of the engine that took him off the rough strip spoke well of the skill of Donovan.
The way his red rubber lips were stretched across his pearly little teeth I though he was only having a little joke, but, no, he wanted me to bend down from the roar of wind so he could roar something into my ear.
Through the splash of the rising waters, they could hear the roar of the river as it raged through its canyon, gnashing big chunks out of the banks.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: boom - a loud, deep, resonant sound
  • Synonym: rumble - a continuous deep, resonant sound
  • Antonym: whisper - a soft or very quiet sound

Etymology

From Middle English roren, from Old English rārian (to cry, make a loud noise).

5. VERB

/ rɔr / (AmE), / rɔː / (BrE)
to utter words loudly and forcefully, often in a commanding or angry manner

Examples

The way his red rubber lips were stretched across his pearly little teeth I though he was only having a little joke, but, no, he wanted me to bend down from the roar of wind so he could roar something into my ear.
Captain Musmanno roared at his men to lash three of the casks of olive oil together for a raft.

Synset Examples

`Get out of here,' he roared

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shout - to speak or call out loudly
  • Synonym: yell - to shout loudly, typically in anger or excitement

Etymology

From Old Norse *raura*, related to Old English *rārian* (to cry out).

6. VERB

/ rɔr / (AmE), / rɔː / (BrE)
To make a loud, deep, and prolonged sound, especially like that of a wild animal; to shout or speak with a loud voice.

Examples

"Hell, yes ", I roared back between dusty lips.

Synset Examples

The bull bellowed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bellow - emit a loud, deep noise, typically as an animal or a person shouting
  • Degree Lower: shout - utter a loud call or cry, typically as a person
  • Degree Higher: scream - utter a loud, high-pitched, and intense cry

Etymology

From Old English *rárian*, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch *raeren* and German *röhren* (to bellow, roar).
