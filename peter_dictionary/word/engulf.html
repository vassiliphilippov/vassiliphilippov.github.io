engulf

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡʌlf / (BrE)
(of a natural force or emotion) to sweep over or surround completely; to overwhelm someone or something entirely.

Examples

As the play unfolds, however, the audience is subtly brought into the grip of an awful evil which grows with ominously gathering power and soon engulfs the community.
Terror engulfed the thousands of Belgian civilians who had remained in the country.
Top scientists have warned that an area hit by an atomic missile of massive power would be engulfed in a suffocating fire storm which would persist for a long time.

Synset Examples

He immersed himself into his studies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overwhelm - to overpower or completely overcome

Etymology

From Old French en- (in) + golfe (gulf), meaning to swallow up.

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈɡʌlf / (AmE), / ɪnˈɡʌlf / (BrE)
(of a liquid, darkness, or an overwhelming force) to cover or surround someone or something completely; to sweep over and enclose.

Examples

They were engulfed by the weird silence, broken only by the low, angry murmur of the river.
The shadows of the trees engulfed her, foreclosing every possible exit from the grove.

Synset Examples

The bright light engulfed him completely

Related Terms

  • Synonym: swallow - to take in and cause to disappear
  • Synonym: submerge - to go or make something go under water or another surface
  • Antonym: uncover - to remove a covering; to reveal

Etymology

From Old French *engolfier*, from Late Latin *ingulfare* (to swallow up), from Latin *in* (in) + *gulfus* (whirlpool).
