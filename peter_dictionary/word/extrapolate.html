extrapolate

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
to estimate or infer an unknown value or trend by extending or projecting known information, especially beyond the original observation range

Examples

In addition, the size distribution obtained from visual and radar observations of meteors may be extrapolated to the micrometeorite domain.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: interpolate - estimate or insert values within a known range of data

Etymology

From Latin 'extra' (outside) + 'polare' (to polish, but here by analogy with 'interpolate').

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
to estimate or infer unknown information by extending or projecting known data or experience into an unobserved area

Examples

Many workers believe that the response is proportional to the incident momentum of the particles, a relation deduced from laboratory results linearly extrapolated to meteoritic velocities.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: interpolate - to insert or estimate values within a known range of data

Etymology

From Latin extra (outside) + English interpolate, influenced by the pattern of 'interpolate'.

3. VERB

/ ɪkˈstræpəˌleɪt / (AmE), / ɪkˈstræpəleɪt / (BrE)
To infer or estimate something by extending or projecting known information into an area not known or observed, often based on trends or data.

Examples

These keys are the working principles of physics, mathematics and astronomy, principles which are then extrapolated, or projected, to explain phenomena of which we have little or no direct knowledge.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: interpolate - to insert something (usually data) between fixed points

Etymology

From Latin extra (outside) + English 'interpolate', modeled after 'interpolate'.
