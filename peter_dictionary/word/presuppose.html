presuppose

1. VERB

/ ˌpriːsəˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌpriːsəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to require or assume something as a necessary condition or precondition for something else to be true or possible

Examples

It could be argued that only such neighborhoods can sustain religious activity, since worship presupposes some local stabilities.
These desires presuppose a sense of causally efficacious powers in which one is involved, some working for one's good, others threatening ill.

Synset Examples

This step presupposes two prior ones

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assume - to accept something as true without proof
  • Synonym: imply - to strongly suggest as a logical consequence

Etymology

From Latin praesupponere, from prae- 'before' + supponere 'to suppose'.

2. VERB

/ ˌprɛsəˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌpriːsəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
To suppose or assume something as a prerequisite or necessary prior condition; to take for granted in advance.

Examples

Presupposed in Plato's system is a doctrine of levels of insight, in which a certain kind of detached understanding is alone capable of penetrating to the most sublime wisdom.
The third choice, negotiation, presupposes, as Russian behavior demonstrates, a great deal of wishful thinking to make it appear reasonable.

Synset Examples

I presuppose that you have done your work

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assume - to suppose something to be true without proof
  • Synonym: postulate - to suggest or accept something as true for reasoning or discussion

Etymology

From Latin praesupponere: prae- (before) + supponere (to put under).
