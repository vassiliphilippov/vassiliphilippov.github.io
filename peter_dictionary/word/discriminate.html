discriminate

1. VERB

/ dɪˈskrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to recognize or perceive a difference between things; to distinguish one thing from another

Examples

The limits are suggested by an imaginary experiment: contrast the perceptual skill of English professors with that of their colleagues in discriminating among motor cars, political candidates, or female beauty.
We cannot consider ourselves educated if we do not read; if we are not discriminating in our reading; if we do not know how to use what we do read.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distinguish - to recognize or treat as different

Etymology

From Latin discriminatus, past participle of discriminare (to divide, separate), from discrimen (distinction).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈskrɪməˌneɪt / (AmE), / dɪˈskrɪmɪneɪt / (BrE)
to treat a person or group differently and usually unfairly, especially because of their sex, race, or other personal characteristics

Examples

The University of Georgia has long claimed that it does not discriminate against any applicant on the basis of race or color.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: segregate - to separate people or things, often by race, sex, etc.
  • Antonym: favor - to show preference towards one person or group over others
  • Synonym: prejudice - preconceived opinion not based on reason or experience; bias

Etymology

From Latin discriminare (to divide, separate), from discrimen (distinction).
