soften

1. VERB

/ ˈsɔfən / (AmE), / ˈsɒfən / (BrE)
to make (images or sounds) less harsh, sharp, or loud; to render gentler or subtler in effect

Examples

It bulks under a veil of thin, new grass, like some embarrassing fact of physicalness, and I think Mrs. Pastern set out the statuary to soften its meaning.
"You own this place "? she said, and her tone had softened until it was almost friendly.
"In addition to the usual tools, I make constant use of cleansing tissue, not only to wipe my brushes, but to mop up certain areas, to soften edges, and to open up lights in dark washes.
It is softened by the saltbush and the bluebush, has a peaceful quality, the hills roll softly.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mellow - make or become softer or more pleasant in tone or appearance
  • Antonym: harden - to make or become harder or more severe

Etymology

From Old English 'softian', from 'soft' (gentle, easy) + '-en' (verb-forming suffix).

2. VERB

/ ˈsɔfən / (AmE), / ˈsɒfən / (BrE)
To make something less strong, severe, or extreme; to reduce the force or impact of something.

Examples

A measure passed by Congress just before adjourning softened the ruling's impact, on prior-year returns still under review, for clay-mining companies that make brick and tile products.

Synset Examples

soften a shock
break a fall

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mitigate - make less severe, serious, or painful
  • Antonym: intensify - make more intense or stronger

Etymology

From soft + -en; 'soft' from Old English 'softe', '-en' is a verb-forming suffix.

3. VERB

/ ˈsɔfən / (AmE), / ˈsɒfən / (BrE)
to become soft or softer

Examples

Your brain is going soft from playing with that girl.

Synset Examples

The bread will soften if you pour some liquid on it

Related Terms

  • Antonym: harden - to become hard or harder

Etymology

From soft + -en (verb-forming suffix).
