fancy

1. VERB

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
to imagine or conceive of something in one's mind; to picture or believe, often without evidence

Examples

So do n't see yourself as a heroine or fancy this little adventure is an event of major importance ".
He liked to fancy himself as a chieftain and to dress for the part.
For it seems that Barco, fancying himself a ladies' man (and why not, after seven marriages?)

Synset Examples

I can't see him on horseback!
I can see what will happen
I can see a risk in this strategy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imagine - to form a mental image or concept
  • Synonym: visualize - to form a mental image, especially of something not present or visible

Etymology

From Middle English 'fauncy', a contraction of 'fantasy'; from Old French 'fantaisie', from Latin 'phantasia'.

2. ADJ

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
Elaborately decorated or ornamented; not plain or simple.

Examples

She gave herself fancy airs!
Hastening to the attic, the temperature of which was easily hotter than the Gold Coast, you proceeded to mask the windows with a fancy wool coverlet, some khaki pants, and the like, and to ransack the innumerable boxes and barrels stored there.
Is it larger or fancier than you really need?

Synset Examples

fancy handwriting
fancy clothes

Related Terms

  • Antonym: plain - simple, not decorated or ornamented
  • Synonym: ornate - elaborately or highly decorated

Etymology

From Middle English 'fauncy', contraction of 'fantasy'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
Imagination, especially of a whimsical or superficial kind; a mental image or idea formed by the mind, often used in literary or philosophical contexts.

Examples

In May, 1803, Ritter, in another flight of fancy, wrote to Oersted a letter that contained a remarkable prophecy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: imagination - the faculty or action of forming new ideas or images not present to the senses
  • Synonym: fantasy - imagination, especially when extravagant or unrestrained

Etymology

From Middle English 'fantasie', from Old French, from Latin 'phantasia', from Greek 'phantasia' (appearance, imagination).

4. NOUN

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
a belief or idea that is imagined and not based on reality; an illusion or delusion.

Examples

He had picked out this pathless trail, instead of the common one, in a moment of romantic fancy, to give them privacy on their honeymoon.

Synset Examples

they have the illusion that I am very wealthy

Related Terms

  • Synonym: illusion - something that is not what it seems; a deceptive appearance or impression
  • Synonym: delusion - a belief that is not true; a false idea
  • Antonym: reality - the true situation that exists; the real facts

Etymology

From Middle English 'fantasie', via Old French from Latin 'phantasia', from Greek 'phantasia' (appearance, imagination).

5. VERB

/ ˈfænsi / (AmE), / ˈfænsi / (BrE)
to feel a liking or desire for something

Examples

Vernon, however, held out for plain oyster roast, and plenty of it, unadorned by herbs or any seasoning but salt, though he did fancy a bit of lemon.

Synset Examples

She fancied a necklace that she had seen in the jeweler's window

Related Terms

  • Synonym: like - to find agreeable, enjoyable, or satisfactory
  • Synonym: desire - to want or wish for something
  • Antonym: dislike - to not like or be pleased with something

Etymology

From Middle English 'fantasie', from Old French, from Latin 'phantasia' (imagination).
