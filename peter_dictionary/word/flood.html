flood

1. NOUN

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
A very large quantity or flow of something that appears or arrives suddenly and overwhelmingly.

Examples

The flood is not as great - only 700 a week according to one apparently conservative account - but it is symptomatic.
Political theoretical understanding, although almost at a standstill during this century, did develop during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and resulted in a flood of inventions which increased the possibility for man to coexist with man.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deluge - an overwhelming rush or abundant outpouring

Etymology

Old English flōd (a flowing of water, a flood), related to flow.

2. VERB

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
to cover or submerge (an area or object) with a large amount of water or other liquid

Examples

Other factors, such as water temperature, depth of water, the fish life it supports, wave action, flooding, etcetera, will affect its recreation value.
They hurled a cement barge against a freighter in Honolulu Harbor, knocked down telephone lines, marooned automobiles, flooded lawns, killed six cows.
His eyes were threaded by little filaments of red as if tiny veins had burst and flooded blood into them.

Synset Examples

The swollen river flooded the village
The broken vein had flooded blood in her eyes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inundate - to flood or overwhelm, especially with water

Etymology

Old English flōd (mass of water, flood), related to flow.

3. NOUN

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
An overflow of a large amount of water beyond its normal confines, especially over what is normally dry land.

Examples

Solar activities could presumably bring long periods of flood or drought.
If there was a fire or a flood he would save Mama first and Violet next.

Synset Examples

plains fertilized by annual inundations

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inundation - the overflowing of water onto normally dry land
  • Antonym: drought - a prolonged period of abnormally low rainfall

Etymology

Old English flōd (a flowing, water, deluge), related to Dutch vloed and German Flut; from Proto-Germanic *flōduz.

4. NOUN

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
A great quantity or outpouring of something, or the act of overflowing or filling beyond capacity.

Examples

He felt the sweetness of pity flood through him, veining his very flesh.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overflow - the act of spilling over or exceeding capacity
  • Degree Lower: trickle - a small, slow flow, as of liquid or people

Etymology

From Old English *flōd* (a flowing, stream, flood), related to *flow*.

5. VERB

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
(of emotions, memories, thoughts, etc.) to fill or overwhelm someone suddenly and in large amounts; to inundate.

Examples

The wraith-like events of the last few days flooded David's mind and he rubbed his unshaved chin and felt again the ache in his kidneys caused by his saddle odyssey from Boston.
The terrible vision of the ghetto streets flooded his mind.
Memory flooded him the instant he opened his eyes and the sick feeling knotted his stomach.
Was it supposed, perchance, that A + M (vocational training, that is) was quite sufficient for the immigrant class which flooded that part of the New England world in the post Civil War period, the immigrants having been brought in from Southern Europe, to work in the mills, to make up for the labor shortage caused by migration to the West?
In the 1930's, cures for the depression literally flooded Washington.

Synset Examples

the basement was inundated after the storm
The images flooded his mind

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overwhelm - to overpower in thought, feeling, or numbers
  • Synonym: deluge - to inundate with a great quantity of something

7. NOUN

/ flʌd / (AmE), / flʌd / (BrE)
A very large quantity of something that arrives or happens at the same time; an overwhelming number or amount.

Examples

After a flood of protests, they were reinstated at the beginning of 1953.
It could halt a flood of Communist arms and strategic supplies now reaching Castro.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: torrent - a sudden, large amount of something (such as words or feelings) released at once

Etymology

From Old English *flōd* (a flow, a flood, a deluge), related to *flow*.
