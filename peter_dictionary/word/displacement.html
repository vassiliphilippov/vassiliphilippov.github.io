displacement

1. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
the movement of something from its usual place or position, especially the distance moved by a body in a particular direction; in physics, the uniform motion of an object from one position to another

Examples

The average sludge age based on displacement of solids was calculated to be 14.5 days.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: motion - the action or process of moving or being moved
  • Synonym: translation - the process of moving something from one place to another without rotation

Etymology

From French déplacement, from déplacer (to displace)

2. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
the act of taking the place of someone or something, especially by using indirect, deceptive, or underhanded means

Examples

Success may mean merely the displacement of Western influence.
The displacement (at least to a considerable extent) of the ethical jurisprudence of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries by positivism reshaped both international law theory and doctrine.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: supplanting - the act of taking the place of another, often by force or strategy
  • Synonym: replacement - the act of replacing or being replaced

Etymology

From French déplacement, from déplacer (to displace), from dé- (dis-, away) + placer (to place).

3. NOUN

/ dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (AmE), / dɪsˈpleɪsmənt / (BrE)
The action or process of moving something from its usual or original position, typically without rotation.

Examples

The actual mean of 1.07 being about halfway between 0 of complete correlation and 2.0 of no correlation, it is evident that there is a pretty fair degree of similarity in the behavior even of particular individual items of meaning as regards long-term stem displacement.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shift - a movement or change in position

Etymology

From French déplacement, from déplacer (to displace), from Latin dis- (apart) + placēre (to place).
