understanding

1. NOUN

/ ˌʌndərˈstændɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈstændɪŋ / (BrE)
The ability to comprehend or grasp the meaning, significance, or nature of something.

Examples

When the words are used, we are never sure which of the traditional meanings the user may have in mind, or to what extent his revisions and rejections of former understandings correspond to ours.
When we say that a mythological mode of thought must be completely abandoned, we mean it must be abandoned as the sole or proper means for presenting the Christian understanding of existence.
Because Christianity teaches not only salvation in history, but salvation by the history of Christ, such an interpretation of death would require a drastic revision of the Christian understanding of the work of Christ.
Freud probably contributed more than anyone else to the understanding of dreams, enabling us to recognize their equivalents in our wakeful thoughts.
Political theoretical understanding, although almost at a standstill during this century, did develop during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and resulted in a flood of inventions which increased the possibility for man to coexist with man.

Synset Examples

he has virtually no understanding of social cause and effect

Related Terms

  • Synonym: comprehension - the ability to understand something
  • Antonym: misunderstanding - failure to understand correctly

Etymology

From Old English understandan (to comprehend), from under (among, between) + standan (to stand).

2. NOUN

/ ˌʌndərˈstændɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈstændɪŋ / (BrE)
an informal or tacit agreement, often not written, between people or groups about what will be done

Examples

If modernization programs are imposed from above, without the understanding and cooperation of the people, they will encounter grave difficulties.
The Company maintained a store at which products of England could be purchased and brought in goods for the new merchants on the understanding that they refrain from trading in furs.

Synset Examples

they had an agreement that they would not interfere in each other's business
there was an understanding between management and the workers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agreement - a formal arrangement between parties

Etymology

From Old English 'understandan' (to comprehend) and later extended to 'mutual agreement'.

3. NOUN

/ ˌʌndɚˈstændɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈstændɪŋ / (BrE)
Sympathetic support or approval for another person's feelings, situation, or opinions.

Examples

What had he thought of, to go to John, grovel and beg understanding?

Synset Examples

his sympathies were always with the underdog
I knew I could count on his understanding

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sympathy - feelings of pity and sorrow for someone else's misfortune; support or approval

Etymology

From Old English 'understandan' (to comprehend) + -ing, originally meaning 'standing under' in support.

4. ADJ

/ ˌʌndərˈstændɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈstændɪŋ / (BrE)
showing sympathy, compassion, or empathy toward others; able to recognize and share another person's feelings or situation

Examples

He must become more expressive of his own desires, more demanding and less' understanding "'.

Synset Examples

an understanding friend

Related Terms

  • Synonym: compassionate - feeling or showing sympathy for others
  • Synonym: sympathetic - showing understanding and care for someone else's suffering
  • Antonym: callous - showing or having an insensitive and cruel disregard for others

Etymology

From Old English 'understandan' meaning 'comprehend.' The adjective use derives from the noun.

5. NOUN

/ ˌʌndərˈstændɪŋ / (AmE), / ˌʌndəˈstændɪŋ / (BrE)
The ability to think, reason, or form judgements; the power of rational thought and discernment.

Examples

Presupposed in Plato's system is a doctrine of levels of insight, in which a certain kind of detached understanding is alone capable of penetrating to the most sublime wisdom.

Synset Examples

we are told that man is endowed with reason and capable of distinguishing good from evil

Related Terms

  • Synonym: reason - the ability to think logically or to draw sensible conclusions

Etymology

From Old English 'understandan' ('to comprehend, grasp the meaning of'), formed from 'under' (among, between, in the presence of) + 'standan' (to stand).
