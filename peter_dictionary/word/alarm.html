alarm

1. NOUN

/ əˈlɑrm / (AmE), / əˈlɑːm / (BrE)
A sudden feeling of fear or anxiety caused by the awareness of danger.

Examples

She dashes around in alarm.
When Kate hurried in alarm to tell him to put it out, she saw other dots of flames among the western Virginia hills from the few scattered fires of the faithful.
Although a look of alarm passed over his face, he did not arrest his movements but disappeared into the shower room just as the chambermaid emerged from number nine.
Still, it would be surprising if what one reads did not contribute to one's ideas of right and wrong; certainly the awakened alarm over the comic books and the continuous concern over prurient literature indicate some peripheral aspects of this influence.
Clapping spurs to the bronc he set off at a sharp canter, with growing alarm.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fear - an unpleasant emotion caused by threat or danger
  • Antonym: calm - a state of being free from fear or anxiety

Etymology

From Old French *alarme*, from Italian *all'arme!* ('to arms!'), a call to arms.

2. VERB

/ əˈlɑrm / (AmE), / əˈlɑːm / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel frightened, disturbed, or filled with sudden worry

Examples

He stared at it, amazed, alarmed.
We have no answer as to why we are not alarmed.

Synset Examples

I was horrified at the thought of being late for my interview
The news of the executions horrified us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dismay - to cause someone to feel worry, disappointment, or distress
  • Antonym: reassure - to remove someone’s doubts or fears

Etymology

From Italian 'allarme', from 'all' arme' (to arms).

3. VERB

/ əˈlɑrm / (AmE), / əˈlɑːm / (BrE)
to make someone feel frightened, worried, or aware of possible danger

Examples

Generally the first salvo of a tsunami is a rather sharp swell, not different enough from an ordinary wave to alarm casual observers.

Synset Examples

The empty house alarmed him
We alerted the new neighbors to the high rate of burglaries

Related Terms

  • Antonym: calm - to make someone feel relaxed or less worried

Etymology

From Italian *all'arme!* (to arms!), from Latin *arma* (arms, weapons).

4. NOUN

/ əˈlɑrm / (AmE), / əˈlɑːm / (BrE)
a device used to warn of danger or an undesirable event, often by making a loud noise or signal

Examples

This in turn sets off an alarm, notifying the observers at the station that a tsunami is in progress.
The moment the alarm goes off, information is immediately forwarded to Honolulu, which is the center of the warning system.
At 17: 07 that afternoon (Greenwich time) the shock was recorded by the seismograph alarm in Honolulu.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: alarm clock - a clock that sounds an alarm at a set time
  • Synonym: warning system - system to signal danger or problems

Etymology

From Italian *allarme* (to arms!), originally from Old French phrase 'à l'arme' meaning 'to the arms' or 'to arms'.

5. NOUN

/ əˈlɑrm / (AmE), / əˈlɑːm / (BrE)
A device or system that automatically gives a sound or signal to warn of danger.

Examples

[ALARM RINGING] MAN: Stop! Stop!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alert - an automatic warning signal or notification

Etymology

From Italian all'arme meaning 'to arms' (a call to arms).
