warmth

1. NOUN

/ wɔrmpθ / (AmE), / wɔːmθ / (BrE)
The comfortable or pleasant sensation caused by moderate heat.

Examples

Social invention did not have to await social theory any more than use of the warmth of a fire had to await Lavoisier or the buoyant protection of a boat the formulations of Archimedes.
One soft evening - that marvelous sea blessed time when the sun's departing warmth lingers and a smell of spume and wrack haunts everything - Amy had picked herself off the floor and begun to walk.
His head undulated gradually, covering space, to come straining beneath the taut belly within the warmth of those teats.
The girl crawled out into the renewing warmth of the sunshine, hugging her shoulders and still trembling.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: heat - high temperature; sensation of being hot
  • Antonym: coolness - the sensation of moderate coldness

Etymology

From Middle English warmthe, from warm + -th (noun suffix).

2. NOUN

/ wɔːrmθ / (AmE), / wɔːmθ / (BrE)
The quality or state of having a moderate or comfortable degree of heat.

Examples

The air took on a special strength now that they 'd left the fecund warmth of the farmland behind.

Synset Examples

an agreeable warmth in the house

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: heat - high temperature; the quality of being hot
  • Antonym: coolness - the quality of being moderately cold; lack of heat

Etymology

From Old English wearmth, from warm (adj.) + -th (noun suffix).

3. NOUN

/ wɔrmθ / (AmE), / wɔːmθ / (BrE)
a friendly or affectionate quality in a person's manner or behavior; kindness and sincerity

Examples

But Carla; eyes were on Agnese whose glowing face and softening eyes gave her a look of warmth and happiness.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: coldness - lack of friendliness or affection; emotional distance

Etymology

From Middle English 'warmthe', from Old English 'wearmth', related to 'warm'.
