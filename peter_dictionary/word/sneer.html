sneer

1. NOUN

/ snɪr / (AmE), / snɪə / (BrE)
A facial expression showing contempt or scorn, characterized by a slight raising or curling of the upper lip.

Examples

With a sneer, the man spread his legs and, a third time, confronted them.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: smirk - a smug, conceited, or silly smile

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *snieren* (to scorn), related to Middle Low German *snieren* (to snarl).

2. VERB

/ snɪr / (AmE), / snɪə / (BrE)
to smile or speak in a contemptuous or mocking manner, often with a slight lifting of the upper lip

Examples

And in the role of Will Danaher, Philip Bosco roars and sneers sufficiently to intimidate not only one American but the whole British army, if he chose.
The necessity is not clear to me, and, in any case, to present a case-hardened race-driver as saying he has left his car, which, or whom, he calls "Giuseppe ", parked "on the Place Vendome sneering at a dozen Bentleys and Rolls-Royces parked around him "is not a liberty; it is an absurdity.

Synset Examples

she sneered her contempt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scorn - to feel or show contempt or disdain
  • Synonym: mock - to ridicule or make fun of, often contemptuously

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *sneren*, of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse *snara* (to snarl).

3. VERB

/ snɪr / (AmE), / snɪə / (BrE)
to smile or speak in a way that shows contempt or scorn for someone or something

Examples

Rankin sneered at him.

Synset Examples

she sneered at her little sister's efforts to play the song on the piano

Related Terms

  • Synonym: smirk - to smile in an irritatingly smug, conceited, or silly way

Etymology

Probably from Middle English snar, perhaps a variant of sneer, akin to Old Norse snara ('to snarl').
