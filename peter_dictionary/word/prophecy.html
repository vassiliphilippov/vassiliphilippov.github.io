prophecy

1. NOUN

/ ˈprɑːfəsi / (AmE), / ˈprɒfəsi / (BrE)
A statement that predicts what will happen in the future, especially one made under divine inspiration.

Examples

La^utner, for his part, "belonged to the present-day race of small artists, who do not demand the utmost of themselves ", and the bitter description of the type includes such epithets as "wretched little poseurs ", the devastating indictment "they do not know how to be wretched decently and in order ", and the somewhat extreme prophecy, so far not fulfilled: "They will be destroyed ".

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prediction - the act of predicting what will happen
  • Synonym: oracle - a person or utterance giving wise or authoritative decisions or predictions, especially ones believed to be inspired by gods

Etymology

From Old French prophecie, from Latin prophetia, from Greek prophēteia (gift of interpreting the will of the gods).

2. NOUN

/ ˈprɑː.fə.si / (AmE), / ˈprɒf.ə.si / (BrE)
A statement that predicts future events and is believed to be revealed by a divine source.

Examples

In May, 1803, Ritter, in another flight of fancy, wrote to Oersted a letter that contained a remarkable prophecy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: prediction - a statement about what will happen in the future, not necessarily divinely inspired

Etymology

From Old French 'profecie', from Late Latin 'prophētīa', from Greek 'prophēteia' (gift of interpreting the will of the gods).
