falter

1. VERB

/ ˈfɔltɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to speak in a hesitant or unsteady manner, often due to uncertainty or emotion

Examples

His speech faltered.

Synset Examples

The speaker faltered when he saw his opponent enter the room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stammer - to speak with involuntary pauses or repetitions
  • Synonym: stutter - to speak with repeated or prolonged sounds, typically involuntarily

Etymology

From Old French 'falter' (to stagger, stammer); likely of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ ˈfɔltɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to become weak, unsteady, or less confident; to show uncertainty or hesitation

Examples

Vocational training which holds no hope that the skill developed will be in fact a marketable skill becomes just another school "chore "for those whose interest in their studies has begun to falter.

Synset Examples

Their enthusiasm is faltering

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hesitate - to pause before saying or doing something, usually because of uncertainty
  • Synonym: waver - to become weak or unsteady; to be undecided
  • Antonym: persevere - to continue steadily despite difficulty

Etymology

From Middle English 'falteren', of uncertain origin, perhaps imitative of unsteady movement or related to Old Norse 'faltrask' (to stagger).

3. VERB

/ ˈfɔltɚ / (AmE), / ˈfɔːltə / (BrE)
to move unsteadily or hesitantly, as if about to lose strength or give way

Examples

Any organism that falters or misperceives the signals or weakens is done.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stumble - to trip or momentarily lose balance while moving
  • Synonym: waver - to move unsteadily back and forth
  • Antonym: steady - to make or become steady, not faltering

Etymology

From Middle English falteren, probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse faltra (to stagger).
