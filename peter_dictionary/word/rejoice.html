rejoice

1. VERB

/ rɪˈdʒɔɪs / (AmE), / rɪˈdʒɔɪs / (BrE)
to feel or show great joy or delight

Examples

In the next few days they had cause to rejoice.
Already many an American knows his count, and rejoices or worries depending on whether it is nearer 180 (safe) or 250 (dangerous).
When it was my turn, I, too, printed the truth as I knew it about Batista, and rejoiced to see his regime topple.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: celebrate - to acknowledge a significant or happy day or event with a social gathering or enjoyable activity
  • Antonym: grieve - to feel intense sorrow, especially due to loss

Etymology

From Old French *rejoir* (to rejoice), from Latin *gaudere* (to be glad), with the prefix *re-* expressing intensive force.

2. VERB

/ rɪˈdʒɔɪs / (AmE), / rɪˈdʒɔɪs / (BrE)
To feel or show great joy or delight.

Examples

And when they fly away, the part of you that knows it was a sin to lock them up does rejoice.

Synset Examples

Who cannot exult in Spring?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exult - to show triumphant elation or jubilation; to rejoice greatly
  • Antonym: lament - to express sorrow, regret, or unhappiness about something

Etymology

From Old French 'rejoir', from Late Latin 'rejoicare' (to make glad again), from Latin 're-' (again) + 'gaudere' (to rejoice).
