wind

1. NOUN

/ wɪnd / (AmE), / wɪnd / (BrE)
The natural movement of air, especially a strong or noticeable current, from an area of high pressure to an area of low pressure.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: breeze - a gentle wind
  • Degree Higher: gale - a very strong wind
  • Degree Higher: storm - violent disturbance of the atmosphere
  • Synonym: air current - a flow of air, often used synonymously

Etymology

Old English 'wind', from Proto-Germanic *windaz, from Proto-Indo-European *wē- (to blow).

2. VERB

/ waɪnd / (AmE), / waɪnd / (BrE)
(of a road, path, river, etc.) to follow a course that bends repeatedly in different directions rather than being straight.

Examples

There were three houses in Salyer's Canyon just at the foot of a low bluff, the road winding along the top, entering above, and then passing down in front of the houses, thence to the Vermejo.
As they were riding along this winding road on the bench of land between the two bluffs, a volley of rifle fire suddenly crashed around the two officers.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: meander - to follow a winding or intricate course, especially of a river or road
  • Antonym: straighten - to make or become straight

Etymology

Old English 'windan' (to turn, twist, go), from Proto-Germanic *windan.

3. NOUN

/ wɪnd / (AmE), / wɪnd / (BrE)
Air taken into and expelled from the lungs; breath, especially as affected by exertion or a blow to the body.

Examples
