cable

1. NOUN

/ ˈkeɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈkeɪ.bəl / (BrE)
a thick wire or bundle of wires for carrying electricity or signals

Examples

Used in several sets of high reps once or twice each week it will not be long before your entire upper leg takes on a razor-sharp definition in which the muscles look like wire cables writhing and twisting under the skin!

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: wire - a thin, flexible strand of metal used to conduct electricity or signals
  • Degree Lower: rope - a strong, thick line of fibers twisted together

Etymology

From Middle French *cable*, from Latin *capulum* (halter, cable).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkeɪ.bəl / (AmE), / ˈkeɪ.bəl / (BrE)
(historical) A message sent by telegraph, especially overseas; a telegram transmitted over a submarine cable.

Examples

Then epistolatory me was a foreign correspondent dispatching exciting cables and communiques, full of dash and wit and glamor, quoting from the books I read, imitating the grand styles of the authors recommended by a teacher in whose special, after school class I was enrolled.
The American newspaperman worried about getting to the cable office.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegram - a message sent by telegraph

Etymology

Originally referring to the submarine cables used for telegraphic communication; later the term was extended to the messages sent via these cables.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkeɪ.bəl / (AmE), / ˈkeɪ.bəl / (BrE)
a very strong, thick rope made of twisted strands of hemp or steel wire, often used for support, towing, or mooring

Examples

The anchor cable would have been lost overboard, but Philip Staffe was on hand to sever it with his axe.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: rope - a length of strong cord made by twisting together strands of hemp, nylon, or similar material
  • Degree Higher: hawser - a thick rope or cable used in mooring or towing a ship

Etymology

From Middle English, from Old French 'cable', from Latin 'capulum' (halter, rope).

4. VERB

/ ˈkeɪbəl / (AmE), / ˈkeɪbəl / (BrE)
to send a telegram or message by cable; to communicate by telegraph

Examples

Our staff man cabled from Turin as follows -

Related Terms

  • Synonym: telegraph - to send a message by telegraph
  • Degree Higher: email - to send a message electronically

Etymology

From French 'câble', from Latin 'capulum' (halter, rope, sling).
