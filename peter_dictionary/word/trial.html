trial

1. NOUN

/ ˈtraɪ.əl / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.əl / (BrE)
An act or process of testing or trying something to discover its effectiveness, quality, or properties, especially in scientific or experimental contexts.

Examples

In the first trial an inert substance was disseminated from a boat travelling some ten miles off shore under appropriately selected meteorological conditions.
Figure 1 describes the results obtained in this trial.
Some experiments are composed of repetitions of independent trials, each with two possible outcomes.
The binomial probability distribution may describe the variation that occurs from one set of trials of such a binomial experiment to another.
More generally, suppose that an experiment consists of a number of independent trials, that each trial results in either a "success "or a "non success " ("failure "), and that the probability of success remains constant from trial to trial.

Synset Examples

in the experimental trials the amount of carbon was measured separately
he called each flip of the coin a new trial

Related Terms

  • Synonym: experiment - a scientific procedure to test a hypothesis

Etymology

From Old French *trier* (to try), from Late Latin *tritare* (to grind), related to 'try'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtraɪ.əl / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.əl / (BrE)
A test or experiment to assess, discover, or demonstrate something's qualities, effectiveness, or suitability.

Examples

These were located at the homes of Government employees, in Government Offices, buildings and reservations within the trial area.
In spite of normal thyroid function tests, a trial of propylthiouracil, 400 mg. daily for one week, was given but served only to intensify muscle weakness.
One who invites such trials of character is either foolhardy, overconfident or too simple and childlike in faith in mankind to see the danger.

Synset Examples

a sample for ten days free trial
a trial of progesterone failed to relieve the pain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: test - procedure to assess quality or performance

Etymology

From Old French *trial* (test), from *trier* (to try), related to English 'try'.

3. NOUN

/ ˈtraɪ.əl / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.əl / (BrE)
A formal examination of evidence before a judge, and typically a jury, in order to decide guilt or innocence in a court of law.

Examples

The police credited him with twenty-five murders but he was never brought to trial for one of them.
These two boys were brought to trial.

Synset Examples

he had a fair trial and the jury found him guilty
most of these complaints are settled before they go to trial

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hearing - a proceeding in which arguments, witnesses, or evidence are heard before an official
  • Antonym: appeal - a legal process to review and possibly reverse a court's decision

Etymology

From Old French *trier* (to try), from Late Latin *tritare* (to wear out), influenced by the legal meaning of 'try.'
