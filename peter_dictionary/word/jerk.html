jerk

1. VERB

/ dʒɝk / (AmE), / dʒɜːk / (BrE)
To pull or move something suddenly and sharply.

Examples

Several slugs fired into the bed jerked aside the blanket to reveal an apparently lifeless hand.
There was a brief interruption while one of O' Banion's men jerked out both his guns and threatened to shoot a waiter who was pestering him for a tip.
She jerked the coat back on and squeezed it around her again, but not soon enough.
"Small violently jerked the weather royal brace with full intention to carry away the mast.
The two little bangs meant that he was getting impatient to have a crowd of customers waited on and that if he had to he would jerk open the door and drag out, by the opposite door handle which she would be clutching, whichever-the-hell clerk it was who thought she could waste so much store time on the pot.

Synset Examples

He turned the handle and jerked the door open

Related Terms

  • Synonym: yank - pull or move with a sudden, sharp movement
  • Synonym: tug - pull something hard or suddenly

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin, suggesting a sudden movement or motion.

3. VERB

/ dʒɝk / (AmE), / dʒɜːk / (BrE)
to make a sudden, sharp, and often involuntary movement

Examples

There had also been a man who must have had St. Vitus or something, because he kept jerking his head.
Nearing home, he jerked to attention at the distant crack of a gun.

Synset Examples

his face is twitching

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twitch - to make a sudden quick movement, often involuntarily

Etymology

Origin uncertain; possibly from Middle English *yerk* (to strike, lash), possibly of Scandinavian origin.

4. VERB

/ dʒɝk / (AmE), / dʒɜːk / (BrE)
to move or make something move with a sudden, sharp, or abrupt motion

Examples

Morgan jerked his head toward the front door.
The boy jerked away.
He jerked once in the grass and lay still.

Synset Examples

The patient's legs were jerkings

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tug - to pull something suddenly or sharply
  • Degree Lower: pull - to draw or haul someone or something with force
  • Degree Higher: yank - to pull something with a sudden, strong movement

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'jerken' or 'chirken', of imitative origin.
