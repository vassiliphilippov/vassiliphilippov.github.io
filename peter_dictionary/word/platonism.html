Platonism

1. NOUN

/ ˈpleɪtəˌnɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈpleɪtənɪzəm / (BrE)
The philosophical doctrine that abstract concepts, such as numbers, forms, or universals, exist independently of their names and of the physical world, originating from the ideas of the Greek philosopher Plato.

Examples

Accordingly we may speak of the Platonism peculiar to Shelley's poems or the type of Stoicism present in Henley's "Invictus ", and we may find that describing such Platonism or such Stoicism and contrasting each with other expressions of the same attitude or mode of thought is a difficult and challenging enterprise.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: nominalism - the doctrine that universals or abstract concepts do not exist independently but only as names

Etymology

From Plato, the Greek philosopher (427–347 BCE), combined with the suffix -ism.
