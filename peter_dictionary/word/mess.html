mess

1. NOUN

/ mɛs / (AmE), / mɛs / (BrE)
A situation or place that is dirty, disorganized, or in a state of confusion or disorder.

Examples

Who would clean up the mess when the war was over?
His talk turns to what he calls "the mess ", or sometimes "this buzzing confusion ".
The only chance of renovation is to open our eyes and see the mess.
It is not a mess you can make sense of ".
One cannot speak anymore of being, one must speak only of the mess.

Synset Examples

the house was a mess
she smoothed the mussiness of the bed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disorder - lack of order or organization
  • Synonym: chaos - complete disorder and confusion
  • Antonym: order - a state in which everything is arranged or organized properly

Etymology

Possibly from Middle English 'mes' meaning portion of food, which later came to refer to a disordered state.

2. NOUN

/ mɛs / (AmE), / mɛs / (BrE)
a room or building in a military base where service personnel eat and socialize

Examples

They caught Trig stealing liquor from the officers' mess, and he got a couple of girls in trouble.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: canteen - a place in a factory, school, or military base where food and drinks are sold and eaten
  • Degree Higher: officers' mess - military dining area reserved for officers
  • Degree Lower: sergeants' mess - military dining area reserved for sergeants

Etymology

From Old French *mes* (portion of food, dish), from Latin *missus* (a sending, course at table).

3. NOUN

/ mɛs / (AmE), / mɛs / (BrE)
A meal provided to members of the armed forces or other service personnel, typically eaten together in a mess hall.

Examples

Even the mess sergeant, Bill Brown, a dapper, cocky transfer from an airborne division, went out on the range.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mess hall - a building or room where members of the armed forces eat meals

Etymology

From Old French 'mes' (portion of food, dish), from Latin 'missus' (a course at table, originally 'a sending'), past participle of 'mittere' (to send).

4. NOUN

/ mes / (AmE), / mes / (BrE)
a dish or portion of soft, semiliquid food, such as porridge, usually somewhat unappetizing in appearance

Examples

He poured the water off the sourdough and off the flour, salvaging the chunky, watery messes for biscuits of a sort.

Synset Examples

a mess of porridge

Etymology

From Old French 'mes' (dish, meal), from Late Latin 'missus' (course at table).
