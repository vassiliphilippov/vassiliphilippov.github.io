embody

1. VERB

/ ɪmˈbɑdi / (AmE), / ɪmˈbɒdi / (BrE)
to represent or express an idea, quality, or concept in a tangible or visible form; to give a concrete form to something abstract

Examples

He suggests that Gore Court embodies all the glories of Tudor splendor.
When the power of the latter was made both limited and explicit - when norms were clarified and made more precise and the creation of new norms was placed exclusively in parliamentary hands - two purposes were served: Government was made subservient to an institutionalized popular will, and law became a rational system for implementing that will, for serving conscious goals, for embodying the "public policy ".
Nonetheless, although few in number they are a stubborn crew, as tenacious of life as the Hardshell Baptists, which suggests that there is some kind of vital principle embodied in their faith.

Synset Examples

Derek Jacobi was Hamlet

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incarnate - to embody or personify in bodily form
  • Synonym: epitomize - to be a perfect example of
  • Synonym: symbolize - to be a symbol of; to stand for

Etymology

From en- (to make) + body, originally meaning 'to give a body to'.

2. VERB

/ ɛmˈbɑdi / (AmE), / ɪmˈbɒdi / (BrE)
to give a tangible or visible form to an idea, quality, or feeling; to represent or express something in a physical or concrete form

Examples

The insuperable reward systems that most religions embody have great motivating effects.
In their vases were embodied the basic aesthetic and logical characteristics of Greek civilization, at first hesitantly in Protogeometric work, and then more confidently in the initial stages of the Geometric style.
the relative contribution to safety of inspection programs embodying -

Synset Examples

He embodies all that is evil wrong with the system

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incarnate - to embody or give physical form to something, especially in human form
  • Synonym: personify - to represent a quality or idea by a person or as if by a person

Etymology

From en- (a variant of 'in-', meaning 'in') + body; first used in late Middle English.

3. VERB

/ ɛmˈbɑdi / (AmE), / ɪmˈbɒdi / (BrE)
To represent or express an idea, quality, or feeling in a tangible or visible form.

Examples

Understanding a work of art involves recognition of the ideas that it reflects or embodies.

Synset Examples

This painting embodies the feelings of the Romantic period

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incarnate - to embody or represent in human form
  • Synonym: symbolize - to serve as a symbol of, to represent

Etymology

From en- (cause to) + body, modeled on Middle French *embodier* or directly from 'body'.
