bite

1. VERB

/ baɪt / (AmE), / baɪt / (BrE)
To use the teeth to cut, grip, or tear something, often causing injury or separation.

Examples

If he bites a playmate she says, "Danny won't like you ".
His unsuccessful strivings to give up drink are represented as religious strivings; he keeps a bottle in a wardrobe at home, and "before this wardrobe Praisegod Piepsam had before now gone literally on his knees, and in his wrestlings had bitten his tongue - and still in the end capitulated ".
"While Henry Morgan was escorting Miss Vera Green from the church social last Saturday night, a savage dog attacked them and bit Mr. Morgan on the public square ".
She was biting into a small red radish; and that action always caused her to lift her lip from the sting of the thing.
She would try to see over the bulge of her cheeks and somewhat under her teeth to the place where she was biting.

Synset Examples

Gunny invariably tried to bite her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chew - to crush food with the teeth before swallowing
  • Synonym: gnaw - to bite or nibble something persistently

Etymology

From Old English *bītan*, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch *bijten* and German *beißen*.

2. NOUN

/ baɪt / (AmE), / baɪt / (BrE)
A wound or puncture caused by the teeth of an animal or a person when biting.

Examples

He found tepid water in a pitcher and a last bit of soap, and he lathered his face and stood stropping the razor on his broad leather belt, its buckle held firm by a knob of the bedpost.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: scratch - a wound caused by scraping or scratching, especially with nails or claws

Etymology

From Old English *bītan* (to bite); related to Old High German *bīzan* and Gothic *beitan*.

3. NOUN

/ baɪt / (AmE), / baɪt / (BrE)
A small piece or mouthful of food.

Examples

She took another bite of toast.

Synset Examples

all they had left was a bit of bread

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mouthful - an amount of food or drink taken into the mouth at one time
  • Degree Lower: nibble - a small bite or small amount of food
  • Degree Higher: portion - a part or share of the whole; a serving of food

Etymology

From Old English *bītan* (to bite, cut with the teeth).

4. VERB

/ baɪt / (AmE), / baɪt / (BrE)
(of an object or tool) to penetrate or cut into something, as a knife or a sharp edge does.

Examples

Curt twisted to one side, and the tines of the fork bit into the floor.

Synset Examples

The fork bit into the surface

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pierce - to penetrate or puncture, especially with a sharp object
  • Synonym: cut - to penetrate or divide with a sharp-edged instrument

Etymology

Old English *bītan*, of Germanic origin, related to Dutch *bijten* and German *beißen*.

5. NOUN

/ baɪt / (AmE), / baɪt / (BrE)
the act of using the teeth to grip, cut, or chew something

Examples

In fact, I hear the bite reflex is so strong, they have to pry the victim's jaws open... with a crowbar.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chew - to crush or grind with the teeth
  • Degree Lower: nibble - to take small bites

Etymology

From Old English *bītan* (to bite), from Proto-Germanic *bītaną*.
