bless

1. VERB

/ blɛs / (AmE), / blɛs / (BrE)
To make the sign of the cross over someone, or to call upon God for protection and favor; to consecrate.

Examples

Other natural features which can be of high interest are the forests, canyons, mountains, deserts, seacoast, beaches, sand dunes, waterfalls, springs, etcetera with which the area is blessed.
Bless you!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consecrate - to declare or make sacred or holy
  • Antonym: curse - to invoke harm or evil upon; the opposite of bless

Etymology

From Old English *blētsian*, meaning ‘to make holy with blood’ (later ‘with blessing’), influenced by *blōd* (‘blood’), later associated with Latin *benedicere* (‘to speak well of, bless’).

2. VERB

/ blɛs / (AmE), / blɛs / (BrE)
to confer prosperity, happiness, or good fortune on someone

Examples

May He bless you all.
When a man comes to this point in his life, he wants to turn over the things he'’s been blessed with.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: curse - to invoke or wish misfortune or harm upon

Etymology

From Old English blētsian (to consecrate, bless).

3. VERB

/ blɛs / (AmE), / blɛs / (BrE)
to ask for or confer divine favor or protection upon; to give a benediction to

Examples

Here was what was called the American dream, namely, the effort to build a structure which would be something new in history and to do so in such fashion that God could bless it.
So, I say, Mr. Speaker, God bless you and keep you for many years not only for this body but for the United States of America and the free world.
May the Divine Speaker in Heaven bless this country with Sam Rayburn's continued service here for years to come.
He called her "the Mare "much as Mrs. Whipsnade spoke of "the Queen, God bless her ".
Bless you!

Synset Examples

The dying man blessed his son

Related Terms

  • Antonym: curse - to invoke harm or evil upon; antonym of 'bless'
  • Synonym: benediction - the act of blessing or asking for divine favor

Etymology

From Old English blētsian (to make sacred, hallow, consecrate), related to blōd (blood), originally meaning to mark with blood as a religious rite.
