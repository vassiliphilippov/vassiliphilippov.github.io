spark

1. NOUN

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
A brief, bright flash caused by electrical conduction through a gas, often as a result of an electric field.

Examples

That is, when Mr. Milstein thrust straight to the core of the music, sparks flying, bow shredding, violin singing, glittering and sometimes spitting, Mr. Hendl could go along.
But it strikes sparks on occasion and their light causes all else to be forgotten.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: arc - an electrical discharge that bridges a gap between two electrodes

Etymology

From Middle English 'spark' or 'sperke', from Old English 'spearca' (spark, particle, atom).

2. NOUN

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
a small, momentary flash of light produced by electricity, friction, or striking two objects together

Examples

No matter how devoted a man was, no matter how fully he gave his life to the Lord, he could never extinguish that one spark of pride that gave him definition as an individual.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: glimmer - a faint or wavering light
  • Degree Higher: flare - a sudden brief burst of bright light

Etymology

From Old English *spearca* (spark, glowing or fiery particle).

3. NOUN

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
a quality of brightness or liveliness in a person's expression, especially conveying animation or merriment

Examples

Shakespeare did not usually invent the incidents in his plays, but borrowed them from old stories, ballads, and plays, wove them together, and then breathed into them his spark of life.

Synset Examples

he had a sparkle in his eye
there's a perpetual twinkle in his eyes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twinkle - a brief gleam or flash, especially in the eyes suggesting amusement or liveliness
  • Synonym: glimmer - a faint or wavering light, or hint of emotion in expression

Etymology

From Old English *spearca* (spark, small particle of fire), of Germanic origin.

4. VERB

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
to emit or produce sparks; to give off small flashes of light or fiery particles, often as a result of electricity or friction

Examples

Her temper sparked like charcoal when it first lights up.

Synset Examples

A high tension wire, brought down by a storm, can continue to spark

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flare - to burn with a sudden intensity or burst of light
  • Degree Lower: smolder - to burn slowly with smoke but no flame

Etymology

From Middle English 'sparke', from Old English 'spearca' (spark, glowing particle).

5. VERB

/ spɑrk / (AmE), / spɑːk / (BrE)
to cause something to start or happen, especially suddenly; to trigger or incite an action, feeling, or event

Examples

The pro-Western government, which the United States had helped in a revolt against the Souvanna Phouma "neutralist "government, never did appear to spark much fighting spirit in the Royal Lao Army.
The net result is a uniquely satisfying sense of comradeship, the kind of comradeship which sparks enthusiasm and blunts the cutting edge of sacrifice and hardship.
The issue was sufficiently potent in 1935 to spark secession from the American Federation of Labor of its industrial union members.
This is one of the most constructive suggestions made in this critical field in years, and I certainly hope it sparks some action.
As America on wheels was responsible for an industry of motor courts, motels, and drive-in establishments where you can dine, see a movie, shop, or make a bank deposit, the ever-increasing number of boating enthusiasts have sparked industries designed especially to accommodate them.

Synset Examples

trigger a reaction
actuate the circuits

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trigger - to cause (an event or situation) to happen or exist
  • Synonym: initiate - to cause (a process or action) to begin

Etymology

From Middle English 'sparke', from Old English 'spearca' (spark).
