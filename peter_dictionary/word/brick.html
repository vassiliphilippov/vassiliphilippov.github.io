brick

1. NOUN

/ brɪk / (AmE), / brɪk / (BrE)
A rectangular block of clay, baked either in the sun or in a kiln, used as a building or paving material.

Examples

And wherever the new thruways go up their banks are lined by neat glass and metal and colored brick light industry.
There was the slate roof of the church; ivy climbed the red brick walls like a green scaled monster.
What had been the ambassador's suite was now jagged walls of blackened brick.
Bricks are an alternative.
The Structural Clay Products Institute, Washington, D. C., is working to develop brick and clay products suitable for shelter construction.

Etymology

From Old French *briche* or *bric*, possibly from Middle Dutch *bricke* (a tile or broken piece).

2. NOUN

/ brɪk / (AmE), / brɪk / (BrE)
(informal, dated) A good, reliable, and trustworthy person.

Examples

He was a square brick of a man with a moon round face and sunken Polish features.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pillar of society - a respected and dependable member of the community

Etymology

Metaphorical use of 'brick' (building block) to describe someone as a reliable 'building block' in society, first attested in the 19th century.
