darkness

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɑːrknəs / (AmE), / ˈdɑːknəs / (BrE)
The state or condition of being without light; the absence of illumination.

Examples

The frequently postulated antique worry that the daylight hours might dwindle to complete darkness apparently gave rise to a ritual and celebration which we still recognize.
There was a fog, which increased the darkness of the night.
In the half darkness the banisters gleamed, and the hall seemed enormous.
He smashed it in and tumbled into darkness.
In the darkness, she saw him stirring.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: light - the natural agent that makes things visible
  • Degree Lower: gloom - partial or relative darkness
  • Degree Higher: blackness - the quality of being intensely dark or black

Etymology

From Old English *deorcnes* (darkness), from *deorc* (dark) + *-ness* (state, quality).

2. NOUN

/ ˈdɑːrknəs / (AmE), / ˈdɑːknəs / (BrE)
A lack of moral or spiritual values; evil or wickedness.

Examples

If there were only darkness, all would be clear.
It is because there is not only darkness but also light that our situation becomes inexplicable.

Synset Examples

the powers of darkness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: evil - profound immorality or wickedness
  • Synonym: wickedness - the quality of being morally wrong
  • Antonym: goodness - the quality of being morally good

Etymology

From Old English *deorcnes*, from *deorc* (dark) + *-ness*.

3. NOUN

/ ˈdɑrknəs / (AmE), / ˈdɑːknəs / (BrE)
A place or area where there is little or no light.

Examples

Again he stood in the darkness listening, but there was only the scrape of a shod hoof on a plank floor.

Synset Examples

he moved off into the darkness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gloom - partial or total darkness
  • Antonym: light - illumination, the absence of darkness

Etymology

From Old English 'deorcnes', from 'deorc' (dark) + '-nes' (-ness).
