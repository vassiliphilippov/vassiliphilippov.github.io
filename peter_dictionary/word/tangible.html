tangible

1. ADJ

/ ˈtændʒəbəl / (AmE), / ˈtændʒɪb(ə)l / (BrE)
capable of being touched or perceived physically; able to be felt or sensed, especially by touch.

Examples

He treats this deep-eyed, bearded, bony crackpot with tangible affection and respect.
I need something tangible.

Synset Examples

skin with a tangible roughness

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intangible - not able to be touched or physically sensed
  • Synonym: palpable - able to be touched or easily perceived

Etymology

From Late Latin tangibilis (that can be touched), from Latin tangere (to touch).

2. ADJ

/ ˈtændʒəbəl / (AmE), / ˈtændʒɪbəl / (BrE)
Clear and definite; real or concrete enough to be regarded as actual or factual.

Examples

While the U. S. Department of Labor has a program of projecting industry and occupational employment trends and publishing current outlook statements, there is little tangible evidence that these projections have been used extensively in local curriculum planning.

Synset Examples

tangible evidence
his brief time as Prime Minister brought few real benefits to the poor

Related Terms

  • Antonym: intangible - not able to be touched or grasped; not having physical presence or clear proof

Etymology

From Late Latin tangibilis (that may be touched), from tangere (to touch).
