dart

1. VERB

/ dɑrt / (AmE), / dɑːt / (BrE)
to move suddenly and quickly in a particular direction

Examples

And when he came to examine the scene, there was a certain staginess to it, it had the smell of planning, and a swift suspicion darted into his mind.
His small black eyes darted inside the olive oval of his face.
They were covered with tiny white blossoms, their scant roots clawing at the stony ground, and wild birds darted in and about and through them so they were nearly alive with the rustle and cry.

Synset Examples

The hummingbird flitted among the branches

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - to run or travel somewhere in a great hurry
  • Synonym: flit - to move swiftly and lightly

Etymology

From Old French *dart*, from Frankish or another Germanic source meaning 'spear' or 'missile'; used figuratively for quick movement.

2. VERB

/ dɑrt / (AmE), / dɑːt / (BrE)
to move or cause to move suddenly and quickly in a particular direction

Examples

Their conversations were, almost invariably, accompanied by the same gestures - arms and pointed forefingers darting toward each other in arclike semicircular motions.

Synset Examples

His forefinger darted in all directions as he spoke

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - to move quickly and suddenly
  • Synonym: bolt - to move suddenly and quickly, especially in escape
  • Antonym: creep - to move slowly and carefully

Etymology

From Middle English *darten*, from Old French *darder* (to throw a dart), from *dard* (dart).

3. VERB

/ dɑrt / (AmE), / dɑːt / (BrE)
to move or run suddenly and quickly in a particular direction

Examples

He darted inside the stockade and freed the horses there.

Synset Examples

She dashed into the yard

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - to move suddenly and quickly
  • Synonym: rush - to move with urgent haste
  • Antonym: linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary, opposite of moving quickly

Etymology

From Middle English 'darten', from Old French 'darter', from 'dart' (a thrown weapon).
