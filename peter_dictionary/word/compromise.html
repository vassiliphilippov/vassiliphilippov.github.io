compromise

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmprəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmprəˌmaɪz / (BrE)
An agreement or settlement of a dispute that is reached by each side making concessions; a middle way between two extremes.

Examples

The only hope for West Berlin lies in a compromise which will bring down the wall and reunite the city.
The materials for compromise are at hand: The Nation, Walter Lippmann and other sober commentators (see Alan Clark on p. 367) have spelled them out again and again.
A compromise will leave both sides without the glow of triumph, but it will save Berlin.
Whether the compromises - on both sides - that made possible the interim appointment can then be repeated remains to be seen.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: concession - something given up to reach agreement

Etymology

From Medieval Latin compromissum (a mutual promise), from Latin compromittere (to make a mutual promise).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmprəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmprəmaɪz / (BrE)
an agreement reached by each side making concessions

Examples

"There can be no compromise when souls are in jeopardy ".

Synset Examples

the newly elected congressmen rejected a compromise because they considered it `business as usual'

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agreement - a negotiated and typically legally binding arrangement
  • Synonym: deal - an arrangement or agreement, especially in business or politics
  • Antonym: stalemate - a situation in which neither side can make progress
  • Antonym: impasse - a situation in which no progress is possible; deadlock

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *compromissum* (a mutual promise to abide by an arbiter’s decision).

3. VERB

/ ˈkɑmprəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒmprəmaɪz / (BrE)
to reach an agreement in which each side gives up some of what it wants; settle a dispute by mutual concession

Examples

She lived by the rules, never compromising, never blinded or diverted by circumstance.
The fear of war can make us either too weak to stand and too willing to compromise, or too reckless and too nervous to negotiate for peace as long as there is any chance to negotiate.
Incurably optimistic, dogmatic, and utterly fearless, in his youth a devout Baptist, in spite of his friendship for the Quaker poet John Greenleaf Whittier (1807 - 1892) he eventually attacked the orthodox churches for what he deemed their cowardly compromising on the slavery issue and in his invariably ardent manner was emphatically unorthodox and denied the plenary inspiration of the Bible.

Synset Examples

nobody will get everything he wants; we all must compromise

Related Terms

  • Synonym: agree - to have the same opinion or come to an arrangement
  • Synonym: concede - to admit or yield, often reluctantly
  • Antonym: disagree - to have a different opinion or not come to an arrangement
  • Synonym: yield - to give way or submit to pressure

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *compromissum* (a mutual promise), from Latin *compromittere* (to make a mutual promise).

4. VERB

/ ˈkɑːm.prəˌmaɪz / (AmE), / ˈkɒm.prə.maɪz / (BrE)
to settle a dispute by each side making concessions; to reach agreement by mutual adjustment of conflicting claims or principles

Examples

I 've tried to compromise by letting them take the little car now and then.
Now that Israel enjoys relative prosperity and a reduction of tensions, the secularists are less disposed to compromise.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: negotiate - try to reach an agreement by discussion
  • Degree Lower: yield - give way as part of making an agreement

Etymology

From Medieval Latin compromissum (a mutual promise), from Latin compromittere (to make a mutual promise).
