rear

1. NOUN

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
the part or side of something that is opposite to the front; the back part

Examples

After you have taken a breather, reverse the position of your legs so that the front thigh of the previous exercise is now to the rear, and the rear thigh now to the front, and perform the same movement in the same manner.
He was like a mean horse to come at from the rear.

Synset Examples

his room was toward the rear of the hotel

Related Terms

  • Antonym: front - the part or side of something that faces forward or is most prominent

Etymology

From Old French *rere* (back, behind), from Latin *retro* (back, backwards).

2. NOUN

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
the part of something that is furthest from the observer or entry point; the back part

Examples

At the rear of the auditorium, upstairs, some men tried to push open the door to the box corridor.
Looking the setup over, Stevens started coolly for the rear of the place.

Synset Examples

he stood at the back of the stage
it was hidden in the rear of the store

Related Terms

  • Antonym: front - the part or side that is seen first or is most forward
  • Synonym: back - the part of something opposite the front

Etymology

From Middle English rere, from Anglo-Norman rere, from Old French riere (behind), from Latin retro (backwards).

3. VERB

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
(of a horse or similar animal) to rise up onto its hind legs, usually in fear or excitement.

Examples

Once more, Katie reared, and whinnied in fear.
Katie reared a third time, then, trembling, descended.
Again Katie reared, and now, wickedly, he compelled her to bring her hooves down again and again upon the sprawled figure of the stranger.
I do n't know whether he was after our rider, who had gone by a minute before, or whether he was simply scouting conditions; but when he passed us by, a musket roared, and he reared his horse, swung it around, and began to whip it back in the direction from which he had come.
The redcoat officer collapsed like a punctured bolster, and the horse reared and threw him from the saddle, except that one booted foot caught in the stirrup.

Synset Examples

The horse reared in terror

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buck - (of a horse) to leap or spring while arching the back

4. VERB

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
(literary) To rise upright or high, especially in a striking or impressive way.

Examples

On August 3, two massive headlands reared out of the mists - great gateways never before, so far as Hudson knew, seen by Europeans.

Synset Examples

The building rose before them

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tower - to rise high above the surrounding area
  • Antonym: sink - to go down below the surface or to a lower position

Etymology

Old English *hrēran* (to raise, stir up), related to Old Norse *hréra*.

5. NOUN

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
the back part of a military formation, procession, or similar grouping

Examples

Another force flanked the company and took up a position on a hill to the rear.

Synset Examples

infantrymen were in the rear

Related Terms

  • Antonym: front - the foremost part of a military formation or procession

Etymology

From Middle English rere, from Old French rere (back, behind), from Latin retro (backwards).

6. VERB

/ rɪr / (AmE), / rɪə / (BrE)
to care for and educate (a child or young animal) until fully grown; to bring up or raise

Examples

Home is where a man was born, reared, went to school and, most particularly, where grandma is.
Reared in England, she studied to be a teacher, earned several scholarships and was graduated with honors from the University of London.

Synset Examples

raise a family
bring up children

Related Terms

  • Synonym: raise - to bring up or care for children or young animals
  • Synonym: foster - to care for and encourage the development of someone, especially a child

Etymology

From Old English *rǣran* (to raise, rear), related to raise.
