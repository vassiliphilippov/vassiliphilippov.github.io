debt

1. NOUN

/ dɛt / (AmE), / dɛt / (BrE)
A sum of money that is owed or the state of owing money or a favor to someone or something.

Examples

Dominated by the vicious circle of the university promotion system, the analyst looks down on and gets along poorly with the other three groups, although he cannot deny his debt to the collector.
We find it in Plato's republic, and in Utopia More acknowledges his debt to that book.
English philosopher Samuel Alexander's debt to Wordsworth and Meredith is a recent interesting example, as also A. N. Whitehead's understanding of the English romantics, chiefly Shelley and Wordsworth.
It's $250,000 in debt and we haven't seen one balloon.

Synset Examples

he is badly in debt

Related Terms

  • Antonym: credit - the ability to obtain goods or services before payment, based on trust

Etymology

From Old French 'dette', from Latin 'debitum' (something owed). The 'b' is silent and reflects the Latin spelling.

2. NOUN

/ dɛt / (AmE), / dɛt / (BrE)
A sum of money, goods, or services owed by one person, organization, or country to another.

Examples

Part-time farming gives a measure of security if the regular job is lost, provided the farm is owned free of debt and furnishes enough income to meet fixed expenses and minimum living costs.
He died before he could completely pay off his debts.
Some years later the bank handling the Mercer liquidation received a check for $ 300000, enough to clear up the debt.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: credit - money or resources available to be borrowed or received, as the opposite of debt
  • Synonym: liability - a financial obligation or amount owed

Etymology

From Old French 'dette', from Latin 'debitum' (something owed).
