corroborate

1. VERB

/ kəˈrɑbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / kəˈrɒbəreɪt / (BrE)
to confirm or support a statement, theory, or finding with additional evidence or information

Examples

Some time later the missing part of the relic was found and the complete inscription, together with other new evidence, fully corroborated the ancient priest's information.
The patient himself denied that he had any visual imagery at all; and there was ample evidence of the following sort to corroborate him.
Then I realized that she had been deliberately showing me, this time, what Granny was like; and when I replied in this spirit, she corroborated my hunch.

Synset Examples

his story confirmed my doubts
The evidence supports the defendant

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confirm - to establish the truth or correctness of something
  • Antonym: refute - to prove that something is false or incorrect

Etymology

From Latin corroborare (to strengthen), from com- (together) + robur (strength).

2. VERB

/ kəˈrɑbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / kəˈrɒbəˌreɪt / (BrE)
to provide evidence or information that supports a statement, theory, or finding

Examples

Corroborating Mr. Hodges' figures was the Federal Reserve Board's report of the large sales increase in the nation's department stores for the week ending March 4.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confirm - to show that something is definitely true by providing more proof
  • Antonym: refute - to prove that a statement or idea is false

Etymology

From Latin corroborare (to strengthen), from com- (together) + roborare (to make strong), from robur (strength).

3. VERB

/ kəˈrɑbəˌreɪt / (AmE), / kəˈrɒb.ə.reɪt / (BrE)
to provide evidence or information that supports a statement, theory, or finding; to confirm or make more certain

Examples

He can corroborate our charges on enough counts for this committee to recommend a charge of perjury against Michael Corleone.

Synset Examples

The stories and claims were born out by the evidence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: confirm - to establish the truth or correctness of something

Etymology

From Latin corroborare (to strengthen), from 'cor-' (together) + 'robur' (strength).
