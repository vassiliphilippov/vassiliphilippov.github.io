intrude

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈtruːd / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter a place or situation where you are not wanted or invited, often causing inconvenience or disruption

Examples

The music is always allowed the living space needed to attain its full sonority; yet the hall never intrudes as a quasi performer.
They are relentlessly rebuffed on all sides by a waitress, the police, and an intruding government tutor.

Synset Examples

They intruded on our dinner party
She irrupted into our sitting room

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interrupt - to stop the continuous progress of an activity by saying or doing something
  • Synonym: invade - to enter a place or situation forcibly or without permission; to intrude aggressively

Etymology

From Latin 'intrudere', from 'in-' (in) + 'trudere' (to thrust).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈtrud / (AmE), / ɪnˈtruːd / (BrE)
to enter a place or situation unlawfully or without permission, especially on someone else's property or privacy

Examples

But that year was different, for just as the city, in the form of my street clothes, had intruded upon my mountain nights, so an essential part of the summer gave promise of continuing into the fall: Jessica and I, about to be separated not by a mere footbridge or mess-hall kitchen but by the immense obstacle of residing in cruelly distant boroughs, had agreed to correspond.

Synset Examples

Don't trespass on my land!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trespass - to enter someone's land or property without permission

Etymology

From Latin intrudere, from in- (in) + trudere (to thrust).
