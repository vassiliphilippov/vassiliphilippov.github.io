patch

1. NOUN

/ pætʃ / (AmE), / pætʃ / (BrE)
a small area on a surface that is different in color, texture, or form from its surroundings

Examples

The firm red spleen weighed 410 gm., and its surface was mottled by discrete, small patches of white material.
At the same time, the wallpaper strips themselves seem to be pushed into depth by the lines and patches of shading charcoaled upon them, and by their placing in relation to the block capitals; and these capitals seem in turn to be pushed back by their placing, and by contrast with the corporeality of the woodgraining.
Fourteen of the sculptures you took posses properties of a most curious and terrifying nature, as you will observe when your limbs begin to wither and your hair falls out in patches.
At the pool's far end was the little cabana Joyce had mentioned, and on the water's surface floated scattered lavender patches of limp looking lather.
The burning air dried his sweat soaked clothes in salt edged patches.

Synset Examples

a bald spot
a leopard's spots
a patch of clouds
patches of thin ice
a fleck of red

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spot - a small round mark or area that is different in color or texture
  • Synonym: fleck - a very small patch or mark of color or light

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English pacche, of uncertain origin, possibly related to Old French piece (piece, bit).

2. VERB

/ pætʃ / (AmE), / pætʃ / (BrE)
to repair by joining pieces of material together, often by sewing or affixing a new piece over a damaged area

Examples

The form was swathed in an army blanket, much patched, fastened at the neck with a cord.
The act, cut to shreds and hastily patched together during the afternoon, had not been rehearsed sufficiently by anyone.

Synset Examples

patch the skirt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mend - to repair something that is broken or damaged
  • Synonym: fix - to repair something that is not working or is damaged
  • Antonym: tear - to damage by pulling apart or cutting

Etymology

From Middle English pacche, probably from an unattested Old French word of Germanic origin.

3. NOUN

/ pætʃ / (AmE), / pætʃ / (BrE)
a small area of land covered by particular plants or vegetation

Examples

A strange wood creature came floating up from a patch of berry bushes.
A few yards beyond the group of men, a man's nude body lay face down on a patch of thick green dichondra.
Too late, he realized that in turning, he had wheeled them onto a patch of sandy ground, instead of atop a grade or ridge.

Synset Examples

a bean plot
a cabbage patch
a briar patch

Related Terms

  • Synonym: plot - a small piece of ground marked out for a purpose such as gardening

4. VERB

/ pætʃ / (AmE), / pætʃ / (BrE)
to mark or cover something with patches of a different material or color, sometimes used figuratively

Examples

To starboard was a cape a thousand feet high, patched with ice and snow, populated by thousands of screaming sea birds.

Synset Examples

The field was patched with snow

Related Terms

  • Synonym: spot - to mark with spots or specks

5. NOUN

/ pætʃ / (AmE), / pætʃ / (BrE)
a piece of cloth or material used to mend, cover, or decorate a hole or worn area in a garment or other item

Examples

Lolotte held a patch of leather, Rev steadied something, a tiny brad, waiting for George's poised hammer.
Everything in the office, the spreading circle of lamplight, the patch of leather in Lolotte's hands.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mend - to repair something that is broken or damaged
  • Synonym: patch up - to repair or mend, especially quickly or temporarily

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle English pacche of uncertain origin.

6. VERB

No more advice on how to patch things up.
