sock

1. VERB

/ sɑk / (AmE), / sɒk / (BrE)
to hit someone or something very hard

Examples

Instead of wanting to sock the poor bastard I found myself having a fellow-feeling for him.
When driving rain or mist socked in one valley, Fogg would chandelle up and over to reverse course and try another one, ranging from the Ottauquechee up to Danville in search of safe passage through the mountain passes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: punch - to strike with the fist
  • Synonym: strike - to hit with force
  • Degree Lower: tap - to hit lightly
  • Degree Higher: wallop - to hit very hard

Etymology

Probably variant of 'sock' (noun), originally meaning 'a light shoe' and later associated with a sharp blow; exact origin uncertain.

2. NOUN

/ sɑk / (AmE), / sɒk / (BrE)
A piece of clothing made of soft material that covers the foot and reaches to between the ankle and knee, worn inside shoes.

Examples

The first thing every morning when she washed her face she could see the scar on her chin where he 'd cut her with a boot cleat, and maybe she saw him heaving it again, the dirty sock popping out as it flew.
I couldn't find my socks without him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stocking - close-fitting, often longer piece of clothing for the foot and leg
  • Degree Higher: shoe - outer covering for the foot
  • Antonym: barefoot - without any covering on the feet

Etymology

From Middle English ‘socke’, from Old English ‘socc’, from Latin ‘soccus’ (a light, low-heeled shoe).
