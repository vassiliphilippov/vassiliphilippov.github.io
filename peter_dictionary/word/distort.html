distort

1. VERB

/ dɪˈstɔrt / (AmE), / dɪˈstɔːt / (BrE)
to twist or form something into a spiral shape, often causing it to lose its original form

Examples

These droplets are distorted by the normal forces just as a balloon would be pulled or pressed out of shape in one's hands.

Synset Examples

The cord is all twisted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twist - to bend or turn something into a spiral shape

Etymology

From Latin distortus, past participle of distortere (to twist apart).

2. VERB

/ dɪˈstɔrt / (AmE), / dɪˈstɔːt / (BrE)
to change the facts, meaning, or appearance of something so that it is no longer accurate or true

Examples

The positivist theory would here distort our meaning beyond recognition.
Telephone orders distort the picture:
Here he would have free expression because the material could be moved to distort forms.
(b) A message runs too great a risk of being distorted if it is to be relayed more than about six consecutive times.
The editorial, by omitting the words anti-trust enforcement, totally distorted Mr. Kennedy's views.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: falsify - to alter information or evidence so as to mislead
  • Synonym: misrepresent - to give a false or misleading account of
  • Antonym: clarify - to make something less confused or more clearly comprehensible
  • Antonym: straighten - to make or become straight (by extension: to make clear or accurate)

Etymology

From Latin 'distortus', past participle of 'distorquere' (to twist apart).
