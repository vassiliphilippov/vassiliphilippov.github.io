compulsion

1. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
a strong, usually irrational urge to perform an act, especially one that is repetitive or against one's conscious wishes

Examples

We talk about national character in the same way that Copernicus talked of the compulsions of celestial bodies to move in circles.

Synset Examples

her compulsion to wash her hands repeatedly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obsession - persistent and unwanted thought that dominates a person's mind

Etymology

From Latin compulsio (a driving, compelling), from compellere (to drive together, force).

2. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
A strong, often irresistible urge to do or say something, especially when it is against one's conscious wishes or better judgment.

Examples

Also, since the man questioned feels a strong compulsion to answer (and thereby avoid the consequences of being thought queer) the question has assumed some measurable properties of a command.
It might be that certain people were born with a compulsion to complicate their lives, while others could live blissfully motionless almost indefinitely, like lizards in the sun, too indolent to blink their eyes.
There was no compulsion behind them.

Synset Examples

he felt a compulsion to babble on about the accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: impulse - a sudden strong urge or desire to act
  • Synonym: urge - a strong desire or impulse
  • Antonym: inhibition - a mental process that restrains or suppresses an impulse

Etymology

From Latin 'compellere' (to drive together), past participle 'compulsus'.

3. NOUN

/ kəmˈpʌlʃən / (AmE), / kəmˈpʌlʃən / (BrE)
the act of using force or coercion to make someone do something

Examples

Out of compulsion to say something cheery, Ben Prime blurted, "Well, we were lucky to be on soft ground when the first floodheads hit.

Synset Examples

though pressed into rugby under compulsion I began to enjoy the game
they didn't have to use coercion

Related Terms

  • Synonym: coercion - the act of persuading or forcing someone to do something by using force or threats

Etymology

From Latin compulsio (compulsion, pressure), from compellere (to force, drive together).
