frantic

1. ADJ

/ ˈfræn.tɪk / (AmE), / ˈfræn.tɪk / (BrE)
done in a wild and hurried way because of fear, anxiety, or excitement; marked by uncontrolled excitement or emotion.

Examples

And in its engineers' frantic attempts to achieve maximum dynamic impact and earsplitting brilliance, the recording sounds as though it had been "doctored for super high fidelity ".

Synset Examples

a crowd of delirious baseball fans
something frantic in their gaiety
a mad whirl of pleasure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: delirious - in a state of uncontrolled excitement or emotion
  • Antonym: calm - not showing or feeling nervousness, anger, or other strong emotions

Etymology

From Middle English *frantike*, from Old French *frantique*, from Latin *phreneticus* (delirious).

2. ADJ

/ ˈfræn.tɪk / (AmE), / ˈfræn.tɪk / (BrE)
Extremely worried, upset, or agitated, usually because of fear or other intense emotion.

Examples

The scant flesh grew cool beneath her frantic hands.
Stravinsky, nearing the age of eighty, is like a lost and frantic bird, flitting from one abandoned nest to another, searching for a home.
Blood gushed from his nose, and he backed off as rapidly as he could, stumbling over his own feet in his frantic haste to get away from Curt's fists.

Synset Examples

frantic with anger and frustration
frenetic screams followed the accident
a frenzied look in his eye

Related Terms

  • Synonym: frenzied - wildly excited or uncontrolled
  • Antonym: calm - not showing or feeling nervousness, anger, or other strong emotions

Etymology

From Middle English 'frantik', from Old French 'frantique', from Medieval Latin 'phreneticus' (delirious), ultimately from Greek 'phrenitikos' (madness of the mind).
