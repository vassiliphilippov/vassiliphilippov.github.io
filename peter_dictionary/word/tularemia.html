tularemia

1. NOUN

/ ˌtuːləˈriːmiə / (AmE), / ˌtjuːləˈriːmiə / (BrE)
A serious infectious disease, chiefly of rodents and rabbits, sometimes transmitted to humans by bites of ticks, flies, or through handling infected animals, causing fever and other symptoms.

Examples

In tularemia produced by aerosol exposure, one would not expect to find the classical ulcer of "rabbit fever "on a finger.
Agents that are known to cause frequent infections among laboratory workers such as those causing Q fever, tularemia, brucellosis, glanders, coccidioidomycosis, etc., belong in this category.

Etymology

From New Latin, named after Tulare County, California, where the disease was first identified.
