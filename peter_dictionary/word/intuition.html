intuition

1. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.tuˈɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.tjuˈɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
The ability to know or understand something immediately, without the need for conscious reasoning.

Examples

David Cortlandt had certain psychic intuitions that this rebellion was not wholly what it appeared on the surface.
Leaving the theatre after the performance, I had a flash of intuition that life, after all (as Rilke said), is just a search for the nonexistent cup of hot coffee, and that this unpretentious, moving, clever, bitter slice of life was the greatest thing to happen to the American theatre since Brooks Atkinson retired.
Henrietta was discovering in the process of writing, as the born writer does, not merely a channel for the discharge of accumulated information but a stimulus to the development of the creative powers of observation, insight and intuition.
Such an understanding, although it must seek to be sympathetic, is not a matter of intuition.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: instinct - an innate, typically fixed pattern of behavior or response
  • Antonym: reasoning - the process of thinking about something in a logical way

Etymology

From Late Latin intuitionem (a looking at, consideration), from Latin intueri (to look at, contemplate).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɪn.tuˈɪ.ʃən / (AmE), / ˌɪn.tjuːˈɪ.ʃən / (BrE)
a feeling or impression that something is true or likely, without conscious reasoning

Examples

Feverishly, he tried to brush away this intuition.

Synset Examples

he had an intuition that something had gone wrong

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hunch - a feeling or guess based on intuition rather than known facts
  • Synonym: premonition - a strong feeling that something is about to happen, especially something unpleasant
  • Antonym: reasoning - the action of thinking about something in a logical way; using logic instead of intuition

Etymology

From Latin 'intuitio' (a looking at, consideration), from 'intueri' (to look at, contemplate).
