dare

1. VERB

/ dɛr / (AmE), / deə / (BrE)
To have the courage or boldness to do something that is risky, difficult, or goes beyond usual limits, often without permission or in defiance.

Examples

We have staved off a war and, since our behavior has involved all these elements, we can only keep adding to our ritual without daring to abandon any part of it, since we have not the slightest notion which parts are effective.
I wanted to wipe my flint, but I did n't dare to, the state my hands were in, just as I did n't dare to do anything about the priming.

Synset Examples

How dare you call my lawyer?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: venture - to risk going somewhere or doing something dangerous or uncertain
  • Synonym: challenge - to invite someone to take part in a contest or to dare someone to do something
  • Antonym: hesitate - to pause before doing something, often due to uncertainty or lack of courage

Etymology

Old English *dearr* (present tense of *durran*, to dare, venture), Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ dɛr / (AmE), / deə / (BrE)
to challenge someone to do something dangerous, difficult, or out of the ordinary, often as a test of courage

Examples

I did book jackets and covers for paperback reprints: naked girls huddling in corners of dingy furnished rooms while at the doorway, daring the cops to take him, is the guy in shirt sleeves clutching a revolver.
Parker insisted upon not resigning, even when the majority wanted him to depart, upon daring the Fellowship to throw him out.
She remembered, suddenly, a night of savage moonlight and scudding clouds when she and Adelia, having dared each other, had stolen out of their great safe house and come here, hand in hand, hoping and fearing ghosts.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: encourage - to give support or confidence to; to urge someone to do something
