enclose

1. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to surround or close off on all sides; to contain within something

Examples

But they are still enclosed in their larval cells and remain there throughout the summer, fall, and winter.
The trompe-l'oeil illusion here is no longer enclosed between parallel flatnesses, but seems to thrust through the surface of the drawing paper and establish depth on top of it.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surround - to be all around (someone or something)
  • Synonym: encircle - to form a circle around; to surround
  • Antonym: free - to let go or release from confinement or restriction

Etymology

From Old French 'enclos', past participle of 'enclore', from Latin 'includere' (to shut in).

2. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
to surround or cover (something) completely; to put within something so as to be surrounded or shut in

Examples

A helpful leaflet may be enclosed in the letter.
The roof timbers are precut and the panels standard so that the house can be completely enclosed in a matter of three or four days.
It enclosed her clammy hands and twined around her ankles.

Synset Examples

Fog enveloped the house

Related Terms

  • Synonym: envelop - to wrap up, cover, or surround completely
  • Antonym: expose - to leave uncovered or unprotected

Etymology

From Old French enclore, from Latin includere meaning 'to shut in, close in'.

3. VERB

/ ɪnˈkloʊz / (AmE), / ɪnˈkləʊz / (BrE)
To surround something completely, especially with a fence, wall, or other barrier.

Examples

He splashed on, mud sucking at his feet with each step, until he reached the end of the drainage ditch and the beginning of the fence that enclosed the farm.

Synset Examples

Darkness enclosed him
They closed in the porch with a fence

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surround - to be all around something or someone

Etymology

From Old French *enclos*, past participle of *enclore*, from Latin *includere* 'to shut in'.
