soak

1. VERB

/ soʊk / (AmE), / səʊk / (BrE)
to make something thoroughly wet by immersing it in liquid or covering it with liquid; to saturate with moisture.

Examples

She was personally sloppy, and when she had colds would blow her nose in the same handkerchief all day and keep it, soaking wet, dangling from her waist, and when she gardened she would eat dinner with dirt on her calves.

Synset Examples

souse water on his hot face

Related Terms

  • Synonym: drench - to make something completely wet; saturate
  • Synonym: saturate - to cause something to become thoroughly soaked with liquid
  • Antonym: dry - to remove moisture from; to make not wet

Etymology

From Old English socian (to soak, steep), from Proto-Germanic *sukōną.

2. VERB

/ soʊk / (AmE), / səʊk / (BrE)
To immerse something or oneself fully in liquid for a period of time, typically to absorb moisture, remove dirt, or relax.

Examples

I showed her the shower and tub, and she said, smiling, "If you really do n't mind, I think I 'll get clean in the shower, then soak for a few minutes in your tub.
"Soak as long as you want, Joyce.
I 'll be soaking for at least half an hour ".

Synset Examples

I soaked in the hot tub for an hour

Related Terms

  • Synonym: immerse - to submerge completely in a liquid

Etymology

Probably from Middle English soken, from Old English socian (to moisten, steep).

3. VERB

/ soʊk / (AmE), / səʊk / (BrE)
(informal) To overcharge someone or extract an excessive amount of money, often used in the context of taxation or prices.

Examples

The savage barbarian hordes of red Russian Communism descended on the Athens that was mighty Metronome, sacking and despoiling with their Bolshevistic battle cry of "Soak the rich'!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overcharge - to charge someone too much for something
  • Synonym: gouge - to overcharge or swindle; extort money
