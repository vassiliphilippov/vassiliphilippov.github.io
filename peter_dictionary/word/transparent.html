transparent

1. ADJ

/ trænˈspɛrənt / (AmE), / trænˈspærənt / (BrE)
allowing light to pass through so that objects behind can be seen clearly; able to be seen through with clarity

Examples

The film consists of a series of still, transparent photographs, or "frames ", 35 - mm. - wide.
Piddington and Minnett explained their observations by pointing out that rocklike materials which are likely to make up the surface of the moon would be partially transparent to radio waves, although opaque to infrared radiation.
Many cells, bacteria, and other microorganisms are transparent to visible light and must be stained for microscopic investigation.
Many of the cells and microorganisms which are transparent to visible light, absorb or reflect the much shorter wavelengths of the ultraviolet spectrum.
And the cast shell of a locust, straw colored and transparent, weighing nothing, fragile but entire, with eyes like bubbles and a gaping slit down its back.

Synset Examples

the cold crystalline water of melted snow
crystal clear skies
could see the sand on the bottom of the limpid pool
lucid air
a pellucid brook
transparent crystal

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: translucent - allowing light, but not detailed images, to pass through; semitransparent
  • Antonym: opaque - not able to be seen through; not transmitting light

Etymology

From Medieval Latin transparent-, transparens, present participle of Latin transparēre (to show through), from trans- (across) + parēre (to appear).

2. ADJ

/ trænˈspɛrənt / (AmE), / trænˈspærənt / (BrE)
(of a material or fabric) so thin or fine as to allow light to pass through; easily seen through.

Examples

And girls could n't have dressed like that - it is n't transparent enough ".

Synset Examples

a hat with a diaphanous veil
filmy wings of a moth
gauzy clouds of dandelion down
gossamer cobwebs
sheer silk stockings
transparent chiffon
vaporous silks

Related Terms

  • Antonym: opaque - not able to be seen through; not transparent or translucent
  • Degree Lower: translucent - allowing light to pass through diffusely

Etymology

From Latin 'transparent-' (shining through), from 'trans-' (through) + 'parere' (to appear).
