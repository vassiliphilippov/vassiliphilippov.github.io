patronize

1. VERB

/ ˈpeɪ.trəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpæ.trə.naɪz / (BrE)
to support or sponsor someone, a group, or an establishment by being a customer or benefactor

Examples

They buy some supplies from our colored grocers and they are patronized by some of the best white gentlemen in town ".
She patronized Greenwich Village artists for awhile, then put some money into a Broadway show which was successful (terrible, but successful).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sponsor - to support or finance an activity or organization
  • Antonym: boycott - refuse to support, deal with, or buy from as a protest

Etymology

From Latin patronus (patron), via Old French patroniser.

2. VERB

/ ˈpætrəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpatrənaɪz / (BrE)
To treat someone with apparent kindness or helpfulness but in a way that betrays a feeling of superiority.

Examples

Both these youths, who greatly admired Henrietta, were somewhat younger than she, as were also the neighboring Friedenwald boys, who were then studying medicine; and bright though they all were, they could not possibly compete for her interest with Papa, whose mind - although he never tried to dazzle or patronize lesser lights with it - naturally eclipsed theirs and made them seem to her even younger than they were.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: condescend - to show feelings of superiority; to look down on someone

Etymology

From French 'patroniser', from 'patron' (protector, master).

3. VERB

/ ˈpeɪtrəˌnaɪz / (AmE), / ˈpætrənaɪz / (BrE)
to regularly use the services of a business, shop, restaurant, etc.; to be a customer of.

Examples

During the summer, Negroes began quietly patronizing previously segregated restaurants and lunch counters in downtown retail establishments.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: support - to give assistance to; back up or help a business or organization

Etymology

From Latin 'patronus' (protector, patron) + '-ize', meaning to act as a patron.
