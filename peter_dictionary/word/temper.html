temper

1. NOUN

/ ˈtɛmpɚ / (AmE), / ˈtɛmpə / (BrE)
A sudden outburst of anger or irritation; a state of being easily annoyed or provoked.

Examples

As the civic temper rises, the more naive citizens begin to play soldier - but the guns are real.

Synset Examples

his temper sparked like damp firewood

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: rage - violent, uncontrollable anger
  • Antonym: calm - a state of being free from anger or agitation

Etymology

From Latin 'temperare' (to mix, moderate); via Old French 'tempre'.

2. NOUN

/ ˈtɛmpər / (AmE), / ˈtɛmpə / (BrE)
a tendency to become easily angry or lose self-control; a habitual state of irritability or anger

Examples

But both were high-spirited and vivacious, both had tempers to control, both loved languages, especially English and German, both were good teachers and wrote for publication.
Her temper sparked like charcoal when it first lights up.

Synset Examples

his temper was well known to all his employees

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disposition - a person's innate qualities of mind and character
  • Synonym: anger - a strong feeling of displeasure or hostility

Etymology

From Latin 'temperare' (to mix, moderate).

3. NOUN

/ ˈtɛmpər / (AmE), / ˈtɛmpə / (BrE)
A person's state of mind or mood, especially as it relates to feeling angry or calm.

Examples

Don't lose that famous temper of yours, Sonny.

Synset Examples

whether he praised or cursed me depended on his temper at the time
he was in a bad humor

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mood - a temporary state of mind or feeling
  • Synonym: humor - a mood or state of mind, especially a temporary one
  • Antonym: patience - the capacity to accept or tolerate delay, trouble, or suffering without getting angry or upset

Etymology

From Latin 'temperare' (to mix, regulate), via Old French 'tempere'.
