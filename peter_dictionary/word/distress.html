distress

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈstrɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈstrɛs / (BrE)
A feeling of extreme anxiety, sorrow, or pain; great emotional suffering or discomfort.

Examples

A second bullet ricocheted off Max's belt buckle, leaving him unhurt but in some distress.
"Yes, for they deal with distress.
At a party an English intellectual - so-called - asked me why I write always about distress.
One does not have to look for distress.

Synset Examples

the death of his wife caused him great distress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anguish - severe mental or physical pain
  • Synonym: misery - great unhappiness or suffering
  • Antonym: comfort - a state of physical or psychological ease

Etymology

From Old French 'destresce', from Latin 'distringere' meaning 'to stretch apart'.

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈstrɛs / (AmE), / dɪˈstrɛs / (BrE)
A state of great difficulty, danger, or need, often caused by adversity or misfortune.

Examples

The dweller at p is last to hear about a new cure, the slowest to announce to his neighbors his urgent distresses, the one who goes the farthest to trade, and the one with the greatest difficulty of all in putting over an idea or getting people to join him in a cooperative effort.

Synset Examples

a ship in distress
she was the classic maiden in distress

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hardship - Severe suffering or deprivation; adversity
  • Synonym: trouble - A state of difficulty or distress
  • Antonym: ease - Freedom from difficulty or distress
  • Antonym: prosperity - The state of being successful or thriving

Etymology

From Old French *destresse*, from Latin *districtia* (restraint, affliction).
