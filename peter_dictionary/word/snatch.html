snatch

1. VERB

/ snætʃ / (AmE), / snætʃ / (BrE)
to quickly take or grasp something in a sudden, eager, or forceful way

Examples

If he snatches a toy, she says, "Caroline wants her own truck just as you do ".
His broad flat fingers, petrified into massive sausages by years of bricklaying, snatched the bills clumsily.
Watson snatched a deep breath.
His hands snatched at an imaginary bucket, swooping down hard to grab it and coming away with equal snap like a ball that's been bounced hard.
Also, when she had bitten off half of the small radish, she found the suspense unbearable; and she would snatch the finger held half of the radish out to where she could inspect it.

Synset Examples

Before I could stop him the dog snatched the ham bone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grab - to take hold of suddenly or roughly
  • Synonym: seize - to take hold of something quickly and firmly

Etymology

Probably from Middle English snacchen, of uncertain origin, related to Dutch snakken (to pant, gasp).

2. VERB

/ snætʃ / (AmE), / snætʃ / (BrE)
to make a sudden or abrupt attempt to seize something, often by reaching or grasping quickly

Examples

He snatched at it savagely.

Synset Examples

the cat snatched at the butterflies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grab - to take hold of something in a rough or hurried way
  • Synonym: clutch - to grasp or seize something tightly or eagerly

Etymology

Probably of imitative origin; similar forms appeared in Middle English.
