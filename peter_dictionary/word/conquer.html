conquer

1. VERB

/ ˈkɑːŋkər / (AmE), / ˈkɒŋkə / (BrE)
To overcome or bring under control by force or authority; to defeat, especially in a way that ends resistance.

Examples

But her personality was such that each affair lasted only until that lover, too, had been conquered and reduced to passivity.
Hughes said, "This is not a plan to conquer space - but to conserve it ", pointing out the state population has increased 125000 each year since 1950.
And that's another reason why it is imperative for us these days to conquer our fears, to develop the poise that promotes peace.
And there is only one way for a man to conquer his fears in such a world.
And God had conquered that one by His grace!

Synset Examples

suppress a nascent uprising
stamp down on littering
conquer one's desires

Related Terms

  • Synonym: suppress - to put an end to forcibly; subdue
  • Synonym: overcome - to successfully deal with or gain control over something difficult
  • Antonym: yield - to give way under pressure, opposite of resist or conquer

Etymology

From Latin 'conquirere' (to seek, procure), from 'con-' (together) + 'quaerere' (to seek).

2. VERB

/ ˈkɑŋkər / (AmE), / ˈkɒŋkə / (BrE)
To take control of a place or people by military force.

Examples

After the Turks conquered the city in 1453 they converted it to a mosque, adding the stubby minarets.

Synset Examples

the invaders seized the land and property of the inhabitants
The army seized the town
The militia captured the castle

Related Terms

  • Synonym: capture - to take someone or something by force
  • Antonym: surrender - to give up control to another, especially in war

Etymology

From Latin 'conquirere' (to seek, procure, win), from 'con-' (together) + 'quaerere' (to seek).
