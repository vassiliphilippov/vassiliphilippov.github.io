throat

1. NOUN

/ θroʊt / (AmE), / θrəʊt / (BrE)
the opening in the vamp of a shoe at the instep

Examples

In the tintable group are high and little heels, squared and oval throats, and shantung like textures.
In a lacy open weave shoes have a luster finish, braided collar and bow highlight on the squared throat.

2. NOUN

/ θroʊt / (AmE), / θrəʊt / (BrE)
A narrow passage or opening in an object or structure that resembles a throat in shape or function.

Examples

Flats have a scalloped throat.
She had surprised Hans like she had surprised me when she said she 'd go, and then she surprised him again when she came back so quick like she must have, because when I came in with the snow she was there with a bottle with three white feathers on its label and Hans was holding it angrily by the throat.

Synset Examples

the throat of the vase
the throat of a chimney

Etymology

Old English 'prote', related to Old French 'gorge' (throat), ultimately from Latin 'gurgulio'.

3. NOUN

/ θroʊt / (AmE), / θrəʊt / (BrE)
The front part of the neck, containing the passage to the stomach and lungs below the chin and above the collarbone.

Examples

When East Germans fled to the West by the thousands, paeans of joy rose from the throats of Western publicists.
One, more horrible than the rest, lunged, growling deep in his throat, his hair bristling.
Light sticks of fingers, the tips gummy with dark earth, patted at Watson's throat.
Mrs. Horowitz was in charge of diseases of the nose and throat.
I did n't say anything, trying to get my throat clear, but I watched him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: neck - the part of the body connecting the head to the shoulders

Etymology

Old English 'throte', related to Old High German 'drozza' (throat).
