bribe

1. NOUN

/ braɪb / (AmE), / braɪb / (BrE)
A sum of money or other inducement given to someone in a position of trust to persuade them to act dishonestly.

Examples

On one of his 1921 ventures he was actually come upon by a Detective Sergeant John J. Ryan down on his knees with a tool embedded in a labour office safe in the Postal Telegraph Building; the jury wanted better evidence than that and he was acquitted, at a cost of $ 30000 in bribes, it was estimated.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: kickback - an illegal payment made to someone for facilitating a transaction

Etymology

From Middle English 'brybe' (piece of bread, beggar’s reward), later meaning 'something given to corrupt'; of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ braɪb / (AmE), / braɪb / (BrE)
to give money or other inducements to someone, especially a public official, in order to persuade them to act dishonestly or illegally in one’s favor

Examples

Apparently he bribed one of the guards.

Synset Examples

This judge can be bought

Related Terms

  • Synonym: buy off - to pay someone to act in one's favor illegally

Etymology

From Middle English *bribe* (something stolen; a bribe), from Old French *briber* (to beg, cheat).
