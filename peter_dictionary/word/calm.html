calm

1. ADJ

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
not showing or feeling nervousness, anger, or other strong emotions; peaceful and composed

Examples

He felt tired and full and calm.
He was calm, drugged, and lazy.
"Stay here in the parlor where it's cool ", she said, trying to be calm.
Jubal would have bet his life that if Anne were to witness the Second Coming, she would memorize date, time, personae, events, and barometric pressure without batting her calm blue eyes.
Once again, he shook his head, kept his face expressionless and his voice very calm, and had a strongly supported alibi ready.

Synset Examples

spoke in a calm voice
remained calm throughout the uproar
he remained serene in the midst of turbulence
a serene expression on her face
she became more tranquil
tranquil life in the country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: serene - calm, peaceful, and untroubled
  • Synonym: tranquil - free from disturbance; calm
  • Antonym: agitated - feeling or appearing troubled or nervous
  • Degree Lower: relaxed - free from tension or anxiety
  • Degree Higher: serene - even more peaceful and untroubled; very calm

Etymology

From Latin 'calmus' via Old French 'calme' (peaceful, still weather).

2. VERB

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
to make (someone or something) less agitated, anxious, or disturbed; to soothe or quiet

Examples

The monk who opened the door immediately calmed his worries about his reception: "I speak English ", the old man said, "but I do not hear it very well ".
It was Giselle, the fille de chambre, come to clean the room, and while she stood before him with ears pricked up and regard all curiosity, explaining her errand, Alex could see from the corner of his eye the doctor doing all he could to calm the displeased bird.
Its thick, substantial outline calmed me.
(The last doorman that saw me do that should calm himself.
It took him about fifteen minutes to calm himself; then he realized he was hungry.

Synset Examples

quiet the dragons of worry and fear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soothe - to gently calm or relieve anxiety
  • Antonym: agitate - to make someone troubled or nervous

Etymology

From Old French calme, from Latin cauma (heat of the day), later shifting to 'rest, quiet.'

3. NOUN

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
A state of mental steadiness and self-control, especially under stress or in difficult situations.

Examples

Lawrence listened with the practiced, deceptive calm of the lawyer, but his face was in the shadow.
A third idea is that artistic literature serves to reduce emotional conflicts, giving a sense of serenity and calm to individuals.
In the calm which follows the reading of a poem, for example, is the effect produced by the enforced quiet, by the musical quality of words and rhythm, by the sentiments or sense of the poem, by the associations with earlier readings, if it is familiar, by the boost to the self-esteem for the semi-literate, by the diversion of attention, by the sense of security in a legitimized withdrawal, by a kind license for some variety of fantasy life regarded as forbidden, or by half conscious ideas about the magical power of words?

Synset Examples

he accepted their problems with composure and she with equanimity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: composure - mental calmness and self-control
  • Synonym: equanimity - mental calmness and evenness of temper, especially in a difficult situation
  • Antonym: agitation - a state of anxiety or nervous excitement

Etymology

From Latin 'calmare', from 'calmus' (calm, quiet).

4. VERB

/ kɑm / (AmE), / kɑːm / (BrE)
to make something less disturbed, steady, or tranquil; to make steady

Examples

Once, after the Discovery lay for a week in rough weather, Hudson ordered the anchor raised before the sea had calmed.

Synset Examples

steady yourself

Related Terms

  • Synonym: steady - to make or become firmly fixed or supported; to make steady
  • Antonym: disturb - to interrupt the normal state or quiet of; to unsettle

Etymology

From Latin 'calmare' (to make calm), from 'calmus' (calm).
