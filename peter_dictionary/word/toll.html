toll

1. NOUN

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
A fee that must be paid to use a road, bridge, or similar public facility, usually to fund its maintenance.

Examples

That's why the FDA, the American Medical Association (AMA), and the National Better Business Bureau (BBB) have estimated the toll of mechanical quackery to be a substantial portion of the $ 610 million or so paid to medical charlatans annually.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: charge - an amount of money asked for goods or services
  • Synonym: fare - the money paid for a journey
  • Synonym: tax - a compulsory contribution to government revenue

Etymology

From Old English *toll* (tax, toll), from Late Latin *tollum*, probably of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
A cost or suffering incurred as a result of an action, event, or process, especially one that is damaging or demanding.

Examples

The economic toll that the device quack extracts is important, of course.

Synset Examples

the cost in human life was enormous
the price of success is hard work
what price glory?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cost - amount paid or lost in order to achieve something
  • Synonym: price - amount given or paid to acquire something

Etymology

From Old English *toll* (tax, fee), ultimately from Proto-Germanic *tullōną*.

3. VERB

/ toʊl / (AmE), / təʊl / (BrE)
(of a bell) to ring slowly, especially as a signal or to mark an occasion such as a death or funeral.

Examples

In many Northern towns and cities meetings were held and church bells were tolled.
[CHURCH BELL TOLLING] Quick!

Synset Examples

For whom the bell tolls

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chime - to ring (a bell) melodiously or repeatedly
  • Degree Higher: peal - to ring loudly (as bells in succession)

Etymology

From Old English *tollian* (to summon), related to Middle Dutch *tollen* (to lift up, ring a bell).
