heap

1. VERB

/ hip / (AmE), / hiːp / (BrE)
To give or direct something in large quantities, often with emphasis or intensity.

Examples

Upon receiving the news, Northern writers, editors, and clergymen heaped accusations of murder on the Southern states, particularly Virginia.

Synset Examples

He heaped him with work
She heaped scorn upon him

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pile - to accumulate or lay things on top of each other in a heap

Etymology

Old English *hēap* (a pile, mass), later used as a verb meaning to pile up.

2. VERB

/ hip / (AmE), / hiːp / (BrE)
to arrange or pile things in a heap or stack

Examples

At the end of the room there was a desk heaped with papers, and she began to riffle these, making sighs and and noises of girlish exasperation.

Synset Examples

heap firewood around the fireplace
stack your books up on the shelves

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stack - to arrange things in a neat pile, typically in layers

Etymology

Old English *hēap* (a pile or mass).

3. NOUN

/ hip / (AmE), / hiːp / (BrE)
A collection of things lying in a messy pile, typically thrown together carelessly.

Examples

But after the doctor's return that night Alex could see, from the high window in his own room, the now familiar figure crouched on a truly impressive heap of towels, apparently giving its egg hatching powers one final chance before it was replaced in its office by a sure-enough hen.
"This coat looks like a rag heap ".

And still another witness, one who had crawled out from under a heap of corpses, had to tell how the victims had been forced to lay themselves head to foot one on top of the other before being shot.
It gave him a thrill to help a youngster crawl off the shit heap.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pile - a quantity of things stacked together in a mass
  • Synonym: stack - an orderly pile, especially of flat objects

Etymology

Old English *hēap* (a pile, mass), of Germanic origin.
