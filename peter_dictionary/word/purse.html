purse

1. NOUN

/ pɝːs / (AmE), / pɜːs / (BrE)
a small bag, often with a clasp, used for carrying money and other small personal items, especially by women

Examples

To Voltaire's surprise, however, their host gives them fresh clothes to put on, opens his purse to lend them money and sits them down before a good dinner.
For your daughter's bridal purse.

Etymology

From Old English 'purs', from Medieval Latin 'bursa' (bag, purse).

2. NOUN

/ pɝs / (AmE), / pɜːs / (BrE)
A small bag, often carried by women, used for holding money, cards, and other small personal items.

Examples

His chelas were required to assume the matsyendra posture dressed in hand-woven diapers while he read aloud from Rig-Veda and an assistant guru examined their purses in another room - nothing was stolen; the purpose was less immediate.
Then on Monday morning - or it might have to be Tuesday - get up and leave just the usual time, and last thing, put the money in an envelope under the old woman's purse there in the drawer.
Then, with a shrug of pretended indifference, she took a compact from her purse and went through the motions of fixing her make-up.
The keys were still in it, and I was miles away before I remembered that my clothes and purse and everything were still in the little cabana where I 'd changed ".
"I guess so ", she said taking a Kleenex from her purse.

Synset Examples

she reached into her bag and found a comb

Related Terms

  • Synonym: handbag - a larger bag used by women for carrying personal items, especially in British English
  • Antonym: wallet - a flat folding case for holding money and cards, mainly used by men

Etymology

From Old English *purs* and Old French *borse*, from Medieval Latin *bursa* (bag, purse).

3. NOUN

/ pɝs / (AmE), / pɜːs / (BrE)
a sum of money considered as financial resources, especially those shared or allocated for a purpose

Examples

The brothers continued to help each other during their studies, sharing a joint purse, lodging together in the dormitory and dining together at the home of their aunt.

Synset Examples

he made the contribution out of his own purse
he and his wife shared a common purse

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fund - an amount of money set aside for a specific purpose
  • Synonym: pool - a common fund of money or resources shared by a group

Etymology

From Old English 'purs' via Medieval Latin 'bursa' (a bag, purse).

4. VERB

/ pɝs / (AmE), / pɜːs / (BrE)
(of the lips) to form into a rounded or puckered shape, often as an expression of disapproval or thoughtfulness.

Examples

Lips pursed mournfully, he stared down at its crazily sagging left side.
She pursed her lips, then clamped them together so tightly that I thought she was angry with me.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pucker - to gather or contract into wrinkles or folds

Etymology

From Old French *boursa*, from Medieval Latin *bursa* (a bag, purse); the verbal sense comes from the resemblance of puckered lips to a drawn purse.
