adjourn

1. VERB

/ əˈdʒɝn / (AmE), / əˈdʒɜːn / (BrE)
to suspend or end a meeting, session, or court case, usually with the intention of resuming it later or officially closing it

Examples

The judge became ill just as the Colfax District Court convened, no substitute was brought in, no criminal cases heard, only 5 out of 122 cases docketed were tried, and court adjourned sine die after sitting a few days instead of the usual three weeks.
But court adjourned after he testified he introduced James White and Jeremiah Hope Pullings, two of the defendants, and also introduced Pullings to Jessy Maroy, a man mentioned in the indictment but not indicted.
A measure passed by Congress just before adjourning softened the ruling's impact, on prior-year returns still under review, for clay-mining companies that make brick and tile products.
The General Assembly, which adjourns today, has performed in an atmosphere of crisis and struggle from the day it convened.
This court is adjourned!

Synset Examples

The court adjourned

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recess - to take a short break in a meeting or session
  • Antonym: convene - to come together or bring together for a meeting
  • Synonym: close - to officially bring to an end a meeting or session

Etymology

From Old French *ajorner* (to appoint a day), from *a-* (to) + *jor* (day, from Latin *diurnum*).

2. VERB

/ əˈdʒɝn / (AmE), / əˈdʒɜːn / (BrE)
to stop a meeting or other official gathering for a period of time, often with the intention to resume later

Examples

All right, this committee is now adjourned.

Synset Examples

We adjourned for lunch
The men retired to the library

Related Terms

  • Synonym: recess - a period when proceedings are temporarily suspended

Etymology

From Old French 'ajourner' (to appoint a day), from 'a-' (to) + 'jour' (day).
