glow

1. NOUN

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
A feeling of warmth and satisfaction, often following accomplishment or excitement.

Examples

A compromise will leave both sides without the glow of triumph, but it will save Berlin.
Mickie had a pleasant glow as he said, "You see, both of them, I mean the President and Jeff Lawrence, are romantics.
When he was in the war, he was in Law or Supplies or something like that, and an old buddy of his told me he would come down on Sundays to the Pentagon and read the citations for medals - just like the one we sent in for Trig - and go away with a real glow.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: elation - a feeling of great happiness or excitement
  • Degree Higher: triumph - a great victory or sense of achievement
  • Degree Lower: contentment - a state of happiness and satisfaction

Etymology

From Old English *glōwan* (to shine, glow); the figurative sense developed from the literal sense of radiance.

2. VERB

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
to shine with a steady, strong light, especially as if from heat

Examples

Soon they were picking their way along the edge of the stream which glowed in the night.

Synset Examples

The coals were glowing in the dark
The candles were burning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burn - to be on fire; to give off light from heat
  • Degree Lower: gleam - to shine softly or with a subdued light

Etymology

From Middle English 'glowen', from Old English 'glōwan' (to glow, blaze).

3. NOUN

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
A soft, steady light, especially one produced by nonthermal sources.

Examples

Two gas lamps were no more than a misleading glow.
The red glow from the cove had died out of the sky.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gleam - a brief or faint light
  • Synonym: shine - to emit light or be bright
  • Antonym: glare - a strong, blinding light
  • Antonym: shadow - dark area produced by blocking light

4. VERB

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
(of a person's face or complexion) to appear noticeably radiant or flushed with color, often from health, excitement, or emotion.

Examples

He glowed with anticipation about what would happen to the culprits when they caught them.
Her tanned foot, whose arch swept high and white, pointed artfully toward tapering toes - toes like fingers, whose tips glowed white.

Synset Examples

Her face glowed when she came out of the sauna

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flush - to become red in the face from emotion, heat, or exertion
  • Antonym: pale - having less color in the face than usual

Etymology

From Old English *glowan* (to shine, glow), related to Old Norse *gló* (to glow).

5. VERB

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
to emit a steady, even light without burning flames

Examples

He devised a detonating fuse in which a short wire was caused to glow by an electric current.
During a 1933 tsunami in Japan the sea glowed brilliantly at night.
Lighted windows glowed jewel bright through the downpour.
Its windows glowed with lamplight.
You give it such a glow.

Synset Examples

The fireflies were glowing and flying about in the garden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shine - to emit light, to be bright with reflected light
  • Synonym: radiate - to send out light or heat, to emit energy

Etymology

From Middle English *glowen*, from Old English *glowan* (to glow, shine), from Proto-Germanic *glōaną* (to glow).

6. NOUN

/ ɡloʊ / (AmE), / ɡləʊ / (BrE)
the emission of visible light by a body as its temperature is increased, especially until it becomes incandescent

Examples

To get it out into the daylight's glow Is my life's aim both first and last, the whole.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: incandescence - emission of light from a hot object

Etymology

From Middle English *glowen*, from Old English *glōwan* (to glow, shine, burn).
