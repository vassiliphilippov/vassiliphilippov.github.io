remarkable

1. ADJ

/ rɪˈmɑrkəbəl / (AmE), / rɪˈmɑːkəbəl / (BrE)
Unusual or surprising and likely to be noticed; worthy of attention.

Examples

Both, of course, were remarkable feats and further embossed the fact that baseball rightfully is the national pastime.
Charlotte Fairchild was excellent as the loyal Marie, who became the second Mrs. LaGuardia, singing and acting with remarkable conviction.
The nuclei of these fibers, as is shown in Figures 3 and 4, showed remarkable proliferation and were closely approximated, forming a chain like structure at either the center or the periphery of the fiber.
The growing contacts between Aegean and Orient are also a phase which should be linked primarily to the remarkable broadening of Hellenic culture after 750.
At last he consented to meet her, and following that brief interview Claire wrote him a yet more remarkable proposal:

Synset Examples

a remarkable sight
such poise is singular in one so young

Related Terms

  • Synonym: extraordinary - very unusual or remarkable
  • Antonym: ordinary - not unusual; normal
  • Synonym: notable - worthy of attention or notice
  • Antonym: unremarkable - not special or unusual; ordinary

Etymology

From French remarquable, from remarquer (to notice, remark), from re- + marquer (to mark).

2. ADJ

/ rɪˈmɑːrkəbəl / (AmE), / rɪˈmɑːkəbl / (BrE)
Worthy of attention or notice; unusual or exceptional.

Examples

And it is clearly argued by Lord Percy of Newcastle, in his remarkable long essay, The Heresy of Democracy, and in a more general way by Voegelin, in his New Science of Politics, that this same Rousseauan idea, descending through European democracy, is the source of Marx's theory of the dictatorship of the proletariat.

Synset Examples

a noteworthy fact is that her students rarely complain
a remarkable achievement

Related Terms

  • Synonym: noteworthy - deserving notice or attention
  • Antonym: unremarkable - not particularly special or interesting

Etymology

From French remarquable, from remarquer (to note, notice).
