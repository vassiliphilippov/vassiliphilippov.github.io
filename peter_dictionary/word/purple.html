purple

1. ADJ

/ ˈpɝː.pəl / (AmE), / ˈpɜː.pəl / (BrE)
Of a color intermediate between red and blue, typically rich or deep in hue.

Examples

Whenever she found time, she went blackberry picking with him, and they would come home together, mouths purple, arms and faces scratched, tired enough to forget grief for another day.
He helped Kate and Juanita enlarge the flower garden in the side yard, where they sometimes sat in the still evenings watching the last fat bees working against the summer's purple dusk.
Dead fledgling birds, their squashed looking nakedness and the odor of decay that clung to the hand when they had been buried in our graveyard in front of the purple flags.
I love purple!

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: violet - a bluish-purple color, closer to blue on the spectrum
  • Degree Lower: lilac - a pale purple color, lighter and less dense than purple
  • Degree Lower: red - primary color adjacent to purple in the spectrum
  • Degree Higher: blue - primary color adjacent to purple in the spectrum

Etymology

From Old English 'purpul', from Latin 'purpura' (purple dye, purple cloth).

2. NOUN

/ ˈpɝːpəl / (AmE), / ˈpɜːpl / (BrE)
A color between red and blue in the spectrum, often associated with royalty or luxury.

Examples

Thus Burns's "My love is like a red, red rose "and Hopkins' "The thunder-purple sea beach, plumed purple of thunder "although clearly intelligible in content, hardly present ideas of the sort with which we are here concerned.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: violet - a bluish-purple color
  • Degree Lower: lavender - a pale purple color

Etymology

From Latin 'purpura', from Greek 'porphyra' (the purple fish, source of purple dye).
