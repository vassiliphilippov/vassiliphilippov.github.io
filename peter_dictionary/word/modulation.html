modulation

1. NOUN

/ ˌmɑdʒəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɒdjʊˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
(Music) The process or act of changing from one key to another within a musical composition.

Examples

And this occurs now, at the refrain of Jacoby's song - at the point, in fact, of the name "Lizzy "-; a modulation described as "almost a stroke of genius ".
It is this modulation which reveals to Jacoby his own frightful abjection and, simultaneously, his wife's infidelity.
Also, it should be noted that the polytonal freedom of his melodies and harmonic modulations, the brilliant orchestrations, the adroitness for evading the heaviness of figured bass, the skill in florid counterpoint were not lost in his mature output, even in the spectacular historical dramas of the stage and cinema, where a large, dramatic canvas of sound was required.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: key change - a shift in the tonal center in music; change of key

Etymology

From Latin modulatio (a measuring, rhythmical arrangement), from modulare (to measure, regulate, sing, play).

2. NOUN

/ ˌmɑːdʒəˈleɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌmɒdjʊˈleɪʃən / (BrE)
(Electronics) The process of varying the amplitude, frequency, or phase of a carrier wave in order to transmit a signal.

Examples

A modulation frequency of 40 cps with an amplitude as small as possible, commensurate with reasonably good signal-to-noise quality, was used.
Corrections were applied for modulation broadening, apparatus background, and field shift.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: demodulation - the process of extracting the original signal from a modulated carrier wave
  • Degree Lower: amplitude modulation - modulation by varying the amplitude of the carrier wave
  • Degree Higher: frequency modulation - modulation by varying the frequency of the carrier wave

Etymology

From Latin modulatio (measured movement, rhythm), from modulare (to regulate, measure).
