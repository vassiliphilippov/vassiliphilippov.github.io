handicap

1. VERB

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændɪkæp / (BrE)
to cause permanent injury or disability; to make someone unable to do certain things because of a lasting injury or condition

Examples

The electric gadget is most helpful when there are many crowned teeth and in individuals who are elderly, bedfast with a chronic disease, or are handicapped by disorders such as cerebral palsy or muscular dystrophy.

Synset Examples

He was disabled in a car accident

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disable - to make unable to perform or function
  • Synonym: impair - to weaken or damage something permanently

Etymology

Originally from hand-in-cap, an old game involving forfeit, later shifting in meaning to denote disadvantage or disability.

2. NOUN

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændikæp / (BrE)
a condition that limits a person's ability to perform certain tasks because of physical or mental unfitness; disability (now often considered dated or potentially offensive)

Examples

And while her Adonis did not suffer from inexperience, satiety might well be an equal handicap.

Synset Examples

reading disability
hearing impairment

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disability - a physical or mental condition that limits activity; neutral/modern term
  • Synonym: impairment - a loss of function or ability, especially in a body part or organ

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'hand-in-cap,' a 17th-century game, later used in horse racing for equalizing competition, and then adopted for disability sense.

3. NOUN

/ ˈhændiˌkæp / (AmE), / ˈhændikæp / (BrE)
An advantage or disadvantage given to a competitor in order to equalize chances of winning, especially in sports or contests.

Examples

Then he played nine imaginary holes of golf, choosing his handicap, his irons, his stance, his opponents, and his weather in detail, but the green of the links seemed faded in the light of his business worries.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: advantage - a condition giving a greater chance of success
  • Antonym: disadvantage - an unfavorable condition that reduces the chance of success

Etymology

Originally from the phrase 'hand in cap', a trading game in the 17th century where parties placed forfeit money in a cap.
