cube

1. VERB

/ kjuːb / (AmE), / kjuːb / (BrE)
(Mathematics) To raise a number or quantity to the third power by multiplying it by itself twice; to multiply by itself twice.

Examples

Therefore, n is inversely proportional to the radius cubed and in fair agreement with the inverse 7 2 power derived from 1958 Alpha and 1959 Eta data.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: square - to raise to the second power; multiply by itself
  • Antonym: cube root - the value that, when cubed, gives the original number

Etymology

Derived from Latin 'cubus', from Greek 'kubos' (cube), used in mathematics to signify the third power due to analogy with the volume of a cube.

2. NOUN

/ kjuːb / (AmE), / kjuːb / (BrE)
a solid shape with six equal square faces and all right angles; a regular hexahedron

Examples

This theorem is similar to the theorem of Kakutani that there exists a circumscribing cube around any closed, bounded convex set in **f.

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin 'cubus', from Ancient Greek 'kýbos' (cube, die, a six-sided figure).

3. NOUN

/ kjuːb / (AmE), / kjuːb / (BrE)
a solid three-dimensional shape with six equal square faces

Examples

A borderline schizophrenic young man told me that to him the various theoretical concepts about which he had been expounding, in a most articulate fashion, during session after session with me, were like great cubes of almost tangibly solid matter up in the air above him; as he spoke I was reminded of the great bales of cargo which are swung, high in the air, from a docked steamship.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: square - a plane figure with four equal straight sides and four right angles

Etymology

From Latin 'cubus', from Greek 'kybos' (a die, a cube).
