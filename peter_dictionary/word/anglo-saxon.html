Anglo-Saxon

1. NOUN

/ ˌæŋɡloʊˈsæksən / (AmE), / ˌæŋɡləʊˈsæksən / (BrE)
a native or inhabitant of England before the Norman Conquest (1066), or relating to the early English people of this period

Examples

And the great majority of these people are of Anglo-Saxon or Celtic descent.
Most of them sincerely believe that the Anglo-Saxon is the best race in the world and that it should remain pure.

Etymology

From Medieval Latin 'Anglo-Saxones', combining Latin 'Angli' (Angles) and 'Saxones' (Saxons), the two main tribes comprising early English people.

2. ADJ

/ ˌæŋɡloʊ ˈsæksən / (AmE), / ˌæŋɡləʊ ˈsæksən / (BrE)
Relating to the Anglo-Saxons or their language, especially as used in England from the 5th to the 11th centuries.

Examples

Thus at the same time that William Henry Harrison was preparing to pacify the aborigines of Indiana Territory and winning fame at the battle of Tippecanoe, Anglo-Saxon settlement made a great leap into the center of the North American continent to the west of the American agricultural frontier.

Synset Examples

Anglo-Saxon poetry
The Anglo-Saxon population of Scotland

Etymology

From Medieval Latin *Anglo-Saxones*, combining Latin *Angli* (Angles) and *Saxones* (Saxons), two Germanic tribes.
