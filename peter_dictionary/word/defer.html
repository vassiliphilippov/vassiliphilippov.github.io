defer

1. VERB

/ dɪˈfɝː / (AmE), / dɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to yield or submit to someone else's wishes, opinion, or judgment

Examples

But dominant in the sense that every other objective, no matter how worthy intrinsically, must defer to it.

Synset Examples

The government bowed to the military pressure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: submit - accept or yield to a superior force or authority
  • Antonym: resist - refuse to accept or yield to

Etymology

From Latin 'differre' (to yield, carry away, or put off), through Old French 'déférer'.

2. VERB

/ dɪˈfɜr / (AmE), / dɪˈfɜː / (BrE)
to postpone or delay something until a later time

Examples

The Commission may in its discretion enter an award with respect to one or more items deemed to have been clearly established in an individual claim while deferring consideration and action on other items of the same claim.
In the afternoon, defense attorneys began the presentation of their cases with opening statements, some of which had been deferred until after the government had called witnesses and presented its case.

Synset Examples

let's postpone the exam

Related Terms

  • Synonym: postpone - to delay something until a later time
  • Antonym: advance - to move something to an earlier time

Etymology

From Latin 'differre' (to delay, put off), via Old French and Middle English.
