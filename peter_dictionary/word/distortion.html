distortion

1. NOUN

/ dɪˈstɔrʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈstɔːʃən / (BrE)
A change in the normal appearance, form, or sound of something that results in it becoming worse or less clear.

Examples

Any alteration of one of these factors is distortion, although we generally use that word only for effects so pronounced that they can be stated quantitatively on the basis of standard tests.
Yet it is the accumulation of distortion, the fitting together of fractional bits until the total reaches the threshold of our awareness, that makes records sound like records.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: warping - the act of becoming bent or twisted out of shape
  • Synonym: corruption - the act of making something less pure or correct
  • Antonym: improvement - a beneficial change from a previous situation

Etymology

From Latin 'distortio' (a twisting aside, distortion), from 'distortus' (twisted apart).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈstɔrʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈstɔːʃən / (BrE)
a shape that has been altered or deformed from its original form, typically as a result of an external force or process

Examples

The rf power level was maintained small enough at all times to prevent obvious line shape distortions by saturation effects.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deformation - the process or result of changing shape by applying force
  • Synonym: warping - the process or result of being bent or twisted out of shape

Etymology

From Latin distortio, from distortus, past participle of distorquere, meaning 'to twist apart'.

3. NOUN

/ dɪˈstɔrʃən / (AmE), / dɪˈstɔːʃən / (BrE)
A flaw or irregularity in an image produced by a lens or mirror, where the image does not accurately represent the object due to optical imperfections.

Examples

I waved with discretion and moderation to the vague golden faces fading through rising dust and the distortions of the back window glass.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: aberration - a defect in a lens or optical system causing image distortion

Etymology

From Latin 'distortio' (a twisting aside), from 'distorquere' (to twist apart).
