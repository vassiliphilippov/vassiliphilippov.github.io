safeguard

1. NOUN

/ ˈseɪfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈseɪfˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
A measure taken to protect against possible danger, harm, or loss.

Examples

He is a trustee for the common good, however feeble the safeguards which the positive or municipal law of property provides against his misuse of that share of the common fund, wisely or unwisely, entrusted to his keeping.

Synset Examples

he put an ice pack on the injury as a precaution
an insurance policy is a good safeguard
we let our guard down

Related Terms

  • Synonym: precaution - a measure taken in advance to prevent harm or ensure good results
  • Antonym: risk - exposure to danger or harm

Etymology

From safe + guard, modeled after Middle French sauvegarde (protection).

2. VERB

/ ˈsæfˌɡɑrd / (AmE), / ˈsɑːfˌɡɑːd / (BrE)
to protect something or someone from harm or danger; to make safe

Examples

Recognizing that the Rule of Law is "a dynamic concept which should be employed not only to safeguard the civil and political rights of the individual in a free society ", the Congress asserted that it also included the responsibility "to establish social, economic, educational and cultural conditions under which his legitimate aspirations and dignity may be realized ".
The lives so many of them gave, to forestall what they believed would be a fatal encroachment by the Union on the powers reserved to their states have continued ever since to safeguard all Americans against freedom's other foe.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: protect - to keep safe from harm or injury
  • Antonym: endanger - to put someone or something at risk of harm, danger, or loss

Etymology

From safe + guard, originally from Middle English 'saufgard,' influenced by Old French 'sauvegarde.'
