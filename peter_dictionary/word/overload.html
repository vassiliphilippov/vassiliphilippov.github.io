overload

1. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈləʊd / (BrE)
(of a system, machine, or device) to become burdened with too much load or pressure, often to the point of malfunction.

Examples

Ice caused the aerator to overload, straining the drive belts.

Synset Examples

The aerator overloaded

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overburden - to give too much of something to someone or something

Etymology

From over- (above, excessively) + load (to put a load on).

2. VERB

/ ˌoʊvərˈloʊd / (AmE), / ˌəʊvəˈləʊd / (BrE)
to fill or supply with too much of something, so that normal function is impaired or prevented

Examples

In spite of the dreams of the host for oneness in the group, the * * f incoming messages for each guest overload his receiving system beyond comprehension if N exceeds about six.

Synset Examples

Fear clogged her mind
The story was clogged with too many details

Related Terms

  • Antonym: underload - to supply with too little, not enough for normal function

Etymology

From over- (too much) + load (burden or weight).

3. NOUN

/ ˈoʊvərˌloʊd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvələʊd / (BrE)
A load on an electrical system that exceeds the available power or safe capacity.

Examples

Over a temperature range from 25 to 200 * * f and at pressures up to 250 atm, an overload of 300 psi, applied for a period of one day, results in an uncertainty in the pressure of, at most, one millimeter of mercury.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overcurrent - an electrical current that exceeds the rated capacity of equipment or wiring
  • Synonym: short circuit - an abnormal connection of low resistance in an electrical circuit

Etymology

From over- (above) + load (burden, weight).
