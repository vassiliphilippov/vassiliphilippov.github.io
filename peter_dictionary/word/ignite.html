ignite

1. VERB

/ ɪɡˈnaɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈnaɪt / (BrE)
to arouse or excite strong feelings or passions in someone or among a group

Examples

We were there at a moment when the situation in Laos threatened to ignite another war among the world's giants.

Synset Examples

The ostentatious way of living of the rich ignites the hatred of the poor
The refugees' fate stirred up compassion around the world
Wake old feelings of hatred

Related Terms

  • Synonym: inflame - to make feelings of anger, excitement, or passion stronger
  • Synonym: incite - to encourage or stir up strong feelings or actions
  • Antonym: quell - to suppress or subdue feelings or disorder

Etymology

From Latin *ignīre* (to set on fire), from *ignis* (fire).

2. VERB

/ ɪɡˈnaɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈnaɪt / (BrE)
to make something start burning; to set fire to something

Examples

Assistant Fire Chief Chester Cornell said gas fumes apparently were ignited by a candle which one of the three Kowalski girls present held for her mother, because the flat lacked electricity.

Synset Examples

Great heat can ignite almost any dry matter
Light a cigarette

Related Terms

  • Synonym: light - to make something start burning
  • Antonym: extinguish - to cause a fire or light to stop burning

Etymology

From Latin 'ignīre', from 'ignis' (fire).

3. VERB

/ ɪɡˈnaɪt / (AmE), / ɪɡˈnaɪt / (BrE)
to catch fire or cause something to start burning suddenly; to burst into flames

Examples

His policies had resolved the conflicts that threatened to ignite the cold war and workable solutions were beginning to take shape.

Synset Examples

Marsh gases ignited suddenly
The oily rags combusted spontaneously

Related Terms

  • Synonym: combust - to catch fire and start burning
  • Antonym: extinguish - to put out a fire or cause something to stop burning

Etymology

From Latin ignire (to set on fire), from ignis (fire).
