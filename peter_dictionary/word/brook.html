brook

1. NOUN

/ brʊk / (AmE), / brʊk / (BrE)
A small natural stream of water, smaller than a river and often feeding into a larger body of water.

Examples

His first shot in the Open last year landed in a brook that flowed along the right side of the fairway.

Synset Examples

the creek dried up every summer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: creek - a small stream, especially in North America
  • Degree Higher: river - a large natural stream of water

Etymology

From Old English *brōc* (brook, stream), related to Old High German *bruoh*.

2. VERB

/ brʊk / (AmE), / brʊk / (BrE)
(usually with negatives) to tolerate or allow something, especially something unpleasant; to endure.

Examples

There were several fairly good minor portraits in the play, including William Hansen's impersonation of a stubborn, rather pathetic father, and Katherine Squire's vigorous characterization of a farm mother who brooked no hifalutin' nonsense from her daughter, or anyone else.

Synset Examples

I cannot bear his constant criticism
The new secretary had to endure a lot of unprofessional remarks
he learned to tolerate the heat
She stuck out two years in a miserable marriage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tolerate - to allow or endure something unpleasant
  • Synonym: endure - to put up with something difficult or unpleasant
  • Synonym: bear - to accept or endure something unpleasant

Etymology

From Old English *brūcan* (to use, enjoy, digest), related to German *brauchen*.
