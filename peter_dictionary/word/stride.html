stride

1. NOUN

/ straɪd / (AmE), / straɪd / (BrE)
The distance covered by a single step when walking or running.

Examples

But it has been during the last two centuries, during the scientific revolution, that our independence from the physical environment has made the most rapid strides.
But these countries have made big strides toward developing the necessary human and social overhead capital; they have established reasonably stable and effective governmental institutions at national and local levels; and they have begun to develop a capacity to deal realistically and simultaneously with all the major sectors of their economies.

Synset Examples

he stepped off ten paces from the old tree and began to dig

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pace - a single step taken when walking or running, also the distance covered by such a step

Etymology

Old English 'strīdan' (to stride), related to Old High German 'strītan'.

2. NOUN

/ straɪd / (AmE), / straɪd / (BrE)
an important or significant advance in progress or development; a major step forward, especially in the phrase 'make strides'.

Examples

Display merchandising, backed by pre selling through advertising and promotion, will continue to make strides in the sixties.

Synset Examples

make strides
they made big strides in productivity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: step forward - an advance or improvement; progress
  • Antonym: setback - a reversal or check in progress

Etymology

From Old English 'strīdan' (to stride). Extended metaphorically from the physical act of striding to signify major advances.

3. NOUN

/ straɪd / (AmE), / straɪd / (BrE)
a long or decisive step in walking or running

Examples

Ben Prime extended his high-stepped stride until he could lay his goad across the noses of the oxen.
A lovely beast like that running around... ...could put steam in a man's strides.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: step - the act of lifting and setting down the foot in walking

Etymology

Old English 'strīdan' (to stride), of Germanic origin.

4. VERB

/ straɪd / (AmE), / straɪd / (BrE)
to walk with long, decisive steps, especially in a confident or purposeful way

Examples

The next morning, right about the time Raquel was spilling her little secret, a man nobody ever laid eyes on before strode into the Maine National Bank.

Synset Examples

He strode confidently across the hall

Related Terms

  • Antonym: stroll - to walk in a leisurely way
  • Synonym: march - to walk in a steady, regular manner, often with group or purpose

Etymology

Old English strīdan (to stride), of Germanic origin.
