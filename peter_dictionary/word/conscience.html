conscience

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑn.ʃəns / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.ʃəns / (BrE)
A person's inner sense of what is morally right or wrong, which guides their thoughts and actions.

Examples

The conscience is non-existent in the 2 - year old.
During this precarious period of development the mother should continue to influence the growth of the child's conscience.
Indeed, of all that is his every man is by nature and reason and therefore by conscience obligated to regard himself as a custodian.
In contrast to this Stoic patristic view, Utopia implies that the nature of man is such that to rely on individual conscience to supply the deficiencies of municipal law is to embark on the bottomless sea of human sinfulness in a sieve.
The Utopians brace conscience with legal sanctions.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: morality - principles concerning the distinction between right and wrong
  • Synonym: scruples - feelings of doubt or hesitation about the morality of an action
  • Degree Higher: guilt - a feeling of having committed wrong or failed in an obligation

Etymology

From Old French *conscience*, from Latin *conscientia* (knowledge within, sense of right and wrong), from *conscire* (to be conscious).

2. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːn.ʃəns / (AmE), / ˈkɒn.ʃəns / (BrE)
A person's moral sense of right and wrong, acting as a guide to one's behavior, often causing feelings of guilt or shame when one does something wrong.

Examples

The New England conscience became desensitized.

Synset Examples

he has no conscience about his cruelty

Related Terms

  • Synonym: remorse - a deep regret or guilt for a wrong committed
  • Synonym: guilt - a feeling of responsibility or remorse for some offense
  • Antonym: shamelessness - lack of guilt, moral scruples, or sense of shame

Etymology

From Old French *conscience*, from Latin *conscientia* (knowledge within oneself, moral sense).
