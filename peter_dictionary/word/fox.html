fox

1. NOUN

/ fɑks / (AmE), / fɒks / (BrE)
A small to medium-sized, carnivorous mammal with a pointed muzzle, upright ears, and bushy tail, known for its cleverness; especially any of several species in the genus Vulpes.

Examples

They plan to become county people who know the proper way to terminate a fox's life on earth.
Swadesh, and therefore Hoijer, felt compelled to omit all terms denoting species or even genera (fox, vulture, salmon, yellow pine, manzanita); their classes of animal and plant terms are restricted to generalizations or recurrent parts (fish, bird, tree, grass, horn, tail, bark, root).
An elephant or a fox or a swan or a cocopalm or a banana possess in unusually high degree this quality of obvious, common-sense, indubitable identity, as do an eye or tooth or nail.

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: wolf - large wild carnivorous mammal of the dog family, especially Canis lupus
  • Degree Lower: dog - domesticated carnivorous mammal, Canis familiaris

Etymology

Old English *fox*, related to Dutch *vos* and German *Fuchs*.

2. NOUN

/ fɑks / (AmE), / fɒks / (BrE)
A clever and deceitful person, especially one who uses trickery to achieve their aims.

Examples

"Not this old fox ", chuckled Uncle Randolph.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trickster - a person who deceives or cheats people
  • Antonym: dupe - someone who is easily deceived or tricked

Etymology

From Old English *fox*, ultimately from Proto-Germanic *fuhsaz*; the use for a cunning person comes from the animal's reputation in folklore.
