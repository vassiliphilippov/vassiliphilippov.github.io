blur

1. VERB

/ blɝː / (AmE), / blɜː / (BrE)
to make something unclear or less distinct in form or meaning

Examples

The second step is to recognize the substantial agreement - frequently blurred by emotionalism and inaccurate newspaper reporting - already existing between Catholics and non-Catholics concerning the over-all objectives of family planning.

Synset Examples

Her remarks confused the debate
Their words obnubilate their intentions

Related Terms

  • Synonym: obscure - to make hard to see, understand, or distinguish
  • Antonym: clarify - to make clear or easy to understand

Etymology

Probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Norwegian 'blorra' to dim the sight.

2. VERB

/ blɝː / (AmE), / blɜː / (BrE)
(of vision or eyes) to become unfocused or glassy; to lose clear sight.

Examples

As it was, his vision blurred and for a moment he was unable to move.
His bifocals blur.

Synset Examples

Her eyes glazed over from lack of sleep

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clear - (of vision) to be sharp or easily seen

3. NOUN

/ blɝː / (AmE), / blɜː / (BrE)
a shape or image that is not clear or distinct, appearing hazy or out of focus

Examples

There was a blur just under my focus of vision, a crash; the car's far windshield panel turned into a silver web with a dark hole in the center.

Synset Examples

it happened so fast it was just a blur
he tried to clear his head of the whisky fuzz

Related Terms

  • Synonym: haze - a slight obscuration of the lower atmosphere, typically caused by fine suspended particles
  • Synonym: fuzz - a distorted or blurred image or sound
  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear, in particular the quality of coherence and intelligibility

Etymology

Probably from Middle English *bleren* (to make dim), of uncertain origin.
