occupation

1. NOUN

/ ˌɑːkjəˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
a person's regular job or profession; the principal activity in which someone is engaged to earn a living

Examples

The petition listed the mayor's occupation as "attorney "and his age as 71.
Because of his brain injury and the extreme damage suffered to his sight, the patient had to train himself for a new line of work, that of a portfolio maker, an occupation requiring a great deal of precision in the making of measurements and a fairly well developed sense of form and contour.
Without exception Hino's brothers turned to either one or both of their father's occupations, but Hino showed a talent for neither and instead spent most of his time on the beach where he repaired nets and proved immensely popular as a storyteller.
The "diversified occupations "program is a part-time trade preparatory program conducted over two school years on a cooperative basis between the school and local industrial and business employers.
Unemployed older workers who have no expectation of securing employment in the occupation in which they are skilled should be able to secure counseling and retraining in an occupation with a future.

Synset Examples

he's not in my line of business

Related Terms

  • Synonym: job - a paid position of regular employment
  • Synonym: profession - a paid occupation that requires formal training and qualification
  • Synonym: career - an occupation undertaken for a significant period of a person's life

Etymology

From Latin occupatio, from occupare (to take possession of).

2. NOUN

/ ˌɑːkjuˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
the act of occupying or taking possession of a building or space, especially by force or without permission

Examples

From the moment of the occupation Lublin became a focal point.

Synset Examples

occupation of a building without a certificate of occupancy is illegal

Related Terms

  • Synonym: takeover - the act of taking control of something, especially by force

Etymology

From Latin occupatio (a taking possession), from occupare (to seize).

3. NOUN

/ ˌɑːkjəˈpeɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən / (BrE)
The control and possession of a country by the military forces of a foreign power.

Examples

But I suspect that the old Roman was referring to change made under military occupation - the sort of change which Tacitus was talking about when he said, "They make a desert, and call it peace " ("Solitudinem faciunt, pacem appellant ".)

Related Terms

  • Synonym: invasion - an entering by force with intent to conquer
  • Antonym: liberation - the act of setting someone free from imprisonment, slavery, or oppression

Etymology

From Latin occupatio (a taking possession), from occupare (to seize).
