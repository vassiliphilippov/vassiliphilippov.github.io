joint

1. ADJ

/ dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
involving two or more people or groups working together; shared by two or more parties

Examples

As a contemporary bonus, the set includes Carl Sandburg's address at a joint session of Congress, delivered on Lincoln's birthday two years ago.
There are other good representations of peasants and people of the court by actors who are finely costumed and magnificently photographed in this last of the Russian films to reach this country in the program of joint cultural exchange.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Antonym: separate - not shared or united; distinct

Etymology

From Old French 'joint', past participle of 'joindre' (to join), from Latin 'jungere' (to join).

2. NOUN

/ dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
an informal or disreputable place of entertainment, such as a bar, nightclub, or gambling house

Examples

Also on the bill at the Fifty-fifth Street is a nice ten minute color film called "Sunday in Greenwich Village ", a tour of the haunts and joints.
The hotels here are bigger and swankier than any of the rug joints we put in Vegas.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dive - a disreputable or run-down bar or club
  • Synonym: speakeasy - an illicit bar or club, especially during Prohibition

Etymology

Originally American slang (late 19th century), meaning 'place, establishment', from the idea of a place where things are joined or connected.

3. NOUN

/ dʒɔɪnt / (AmE), / dʒɔɪnt / (BrE)
A point at which two bones or parts of the skeleton are connected, especially one that allows movement.

Examples

It is as follows: "The usual sensitivity tests showed that the specific qualities of skin-perceptiveness (pressure, pain, temperature), as well as the kinesthetic sensations (muscular feelings, feelings in the tendons and joints), were, as such, essentially intact, although they seemed, in comparison with normal reactions, to be somewhat diminished over the entire body.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: articulation - anatomical term for a joint

Etymology

From Old French 'joint', from Latin 'junctus', past participle of 'jungere' (to join).
