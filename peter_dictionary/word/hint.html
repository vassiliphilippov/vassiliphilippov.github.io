hint

1. NOUN

/ hɪnt / (AmE), / hɪnt / (BrE)
A slight or barely noticeable sign, indication, or trace of something.

Examples

He could think of nothing else to tell them: no assurances, no hopeful hints at great discoveries that day.
Not a hint.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: trace - a very small amount or sign of something
  • Synonym: vestige - a small remaining part or sign of something that once existed

Etymology

Of uncertain origin; possibly from Middle English 'hinten' or variant of 'hent' meaning 'to seize'.

2. VERB

/ hɪnt / (AmE), / hɪnt / (BrE)
to suggest or imply something indirectly

Examples

Another, more interesting explanation, is hinted at by Watson when he observes on several occasions that Holmes would have made a magnificent criminal.
Cool air moving slowly through the open or smashed out side windows hinted of blooming roadside vegetation, and occasionally a faint fragrance of perfume swirled from the back seat.
If W. W.'s retiring soon, as hinted, he ai n't talking - yet.
The fact that secrets of the Dreadnought, and thereby of the American undersea fleet, were involved in the spy case had been hinted at earlier.
Frankie was fairly glutted with ideas, as he had hinted upon his arrival: "It's really tremendous when you think Ella Fitzgerald is coming from Australia.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: suggest - to put forward an idea for consideration; to imply
  • Synonym: imply - to suggest something without stating it directly
  • Antonym: state - to say something clearly and openly

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'henten' (to seize), influenced by 'hint' as a noun (indirect suggestion).

3. NOUN

/ hɪnt / (AmE), / hɪnt / (BrE)
A slight or indirect suggestion, clue, or indication intended to guide or inform without stating explicitly.

Examples

He immediately rode on to Cheyenne, threw a ten day drinking spree and dropped some very strong hints among friends.

Synset Examples

not a breath of scandal ever touched her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: clue - a piece of evidence or information that helps to solve a problem or mystery

Etymology

Probably from Middle English 'hinten', perhaps related to German 'hindeuten' (to hint at).
