scatter

1. VERB

/ ˈskæt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
to throw or spread things over a wide area in an irregular way

Examples

What you were looking for (unless you make a hobby of collecting old tennis rackets and fly screens) eludes me, but to judge from phonograph records scattered about a fumed oak Victrola, you danced two tangos and a paso doble, which must have been fairly enervating in that milieu.
Bursting paper cartridges, he scattered powder beneath the nearest wagon and dumped the contents of the canister upon it.

Synset Examples

He scattered gun powder under the wagon

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to spread or distribute over a wide area

Etymology

From Middle English 'scateren', of uncertain origin, perhaps related to Middle Dutch 'schateren' (to scatter, shatter).

2. VERB

/ ˈskæt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
To sow seeds by throwing or spreading them over an area rather than planting them carefully in rows or holes.

Examples

Other flowers we might gather as we pleased: myrtle and white violets from beneath the lilacs; the lilacs themselves, that bloomed so prodigally but for the most part beyond our reach; snowballs; hollyhock blossoms that, turned upside down, make pink petticoated ladies; and the little, dark blue larkspur that scattered its seed everywhere.

Synset Examples

scatter seeds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sow - to plant seeds in the ground

Etymology

From Old English *scaterian*, from a Germanic base meaning 'to spread.'

3. VERB

/ ˈskætɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
(of people, animals, or things) to move quickly in different directions so as to avoid something or as a reaction to something; to separate and go in different ways suddenly.

Examples

Radiation scatters somewhat like light.
The guerrillas scattered for cover.

Synset Examples

The crowds dispersed
The children scattered in all directions when the teacher approached

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to go or move in different directions; to spread apart
  • Antonym: gather - to come together in a group

Etymology

From Middle English scateren, probably from Old Norse skata (to scatter, distribute).

4. VERB

/ ˈskætɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
to cause things or people to separate and move in different directions over a wide area

Examples

His thoughts were scattered by the sharp report of a rifle from the other side of the door.

Synset Examples

break up kidney stones
disperse particles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to spread or distribute over a wide area
  • Antonym: gather - to bring together into one place or group

Etymology

From Middle English 'scateren', probably of Scandinavian origin (compare Old Norse 'skathra', 'skutr').

5. VERB

/ ˈskæt̬ɚ / (AmE), / ˈskætə / (BrE)
to cause (things or people) to separate and move in different directions over a wide area

Examples

A few drops of rain just before midnight, when Sarah Vaughan was in the midst of her first number, scattered the more timid members of the audience briefly, but at this hour and with Sarah on the stand, most of the listeners did n't care whether they got wet.

Synset Examples

She waved her hand and scattered the crowds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to spread or distribute things or people over a wide area

Etymology

Old English *scaterian*, of Germanic origin, related to Dutch *schateren* (to burst out laughing, burst apart).
