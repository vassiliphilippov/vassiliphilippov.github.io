upset

1. ADJ

/ ˈʌpˌsɛt / (AmE), / ˈʌpˌsɛt / (BrE)
(of a victory or result) in which the favored team or person is unexpectedly defeated.

Examples

Rabb, the former Louisiana State field general, came off the bench for his debut with the Bills Sunday and directed his new team to a 22 - 12 upset victory over the Houston Oilers, defending league champions.

Synset Examples

the Bills' upset victory over the Houston Oilers

Related Terms

  • Synonym: surprise - an unexpected event or result, often in sports
  • Antonym: expected - likely to happen; anticipated; the opposite of an upset result

2. NOUN

/ ʌpˈsɛt / (AmE), / ʌpˈsɛt / (BrE)
an unexpected disruption or disturbance affecting a situation, process, or physical state

Examples

In an effort to fortify himself against the unforeseen upsets sure to arise in the future, Herbert A. Leggett, banker editor of the Phoenix "Arizona Progress ", reflects upon a few of the depressing experiences of the feverish fifties.

Synset Examples

his carelessness could have caused an ecological upset
she was unprepared for this sudden overthrow of their normal way of living

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disturbance - an interruption of a state of peace or order

3. VERB

/ ʌpˈsɛt / (AmE), / ʌpˈsɛt / (BrE)
to cause someone to become disturbed, troubled, or lose their composure; to make someone emotionally distressed or agitated

Examples

But the one that upset the financially wise was the professional dancer who related in a book how he parlayed his earnings into a $ 2000000 profit on the stock market.
But Sojourner was not easily excited or upset and said quite calmly: "Let's go and see what it's like ".
And I know that'’s upset you in the past.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: distress - to make someone very upset or anxious
  • Antonym: calm - to make someone quiet or less disturbed

Etymology

From Middle English *upsetten*, literally 'set up', from up + set.
