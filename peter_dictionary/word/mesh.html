mesh

1. NOUN

/ mɛʃ / (AmE), / mɛʃ / (BrE)
a measure of the fineness of a woven screen, indicating the number of openings per linear inch and used to specify the size of particles that can pass through

Examples

The DEAE-cellulose, containing 0.78 mEq of N / g, was prepared in our laboratory by the method of Peterson and Sober (7) from powdered cellulose, 100 - 230 mesh.

Synset Examples

a 100 mesh screen
100 mesh powdered cellulose

Etymology

From Middle French meshe, from Old French mesche (loop, mesh in net), of Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ mɛʃ / (AmE), / mɛʃ / (BrE)
(of parts or people) to engage or fit together closely and work effectively; (especially of gears or machinery) to become or make engaged so that movement is transferred.

Examples

They should be as straight as possible, as this will effect their ability to mesh properly when the walls are erected.

Synset Examples

engaged the gears

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interlock - to connect or fit together securely
  • Antonym: disengage - to separate or release from engagement

Etymology

From Middle French *mesche* (mesh, net), probably of Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ mɛʃ / (AmE), / mɛʃ / (BrE)
(of ideas, plans, systems, etc.) to fit or work together effectively, so that all parts function well as a whole.

Examples

Why does Wisman's ever-changing code always mesh with the fragments in possession of the button pushers?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: harmonize - to bring into agreement or harmony
  • Antonym: conflict - to be incompatible or at odds

Etymology

From Old French *meshe* (open net), ultimately of Germanic origin.
