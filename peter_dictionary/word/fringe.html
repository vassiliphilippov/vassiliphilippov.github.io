fringe

1. NOUN

/ frɪndʒ / (AmE), / frɪndʒ / (BrE)
the outer edge, border, or boundary of an area or object

Examples

A fringe of housing and gardens bearded the top of the heights, and behind it were sandy roads leading past farms and hayfields.
He had only a fringe of hair and he wore it cropped short.
Some plants go overboard on one type of fringe - say a liberal retirement plan - and find themselves vulnerable elsewhere.
M + R Dietetic Laboratories, Inc., Columbus, gives all its workers a facsimile checkbook - each check showing the amount the company spends on a particular fringe.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: center - the middle point or part of something

Etymology

From Middle English 'fringe', from Old French 'fringe', from Vulgar Latin *frimbia, from Late Latin 'fimbria' (border, hem).

2. NOUN

/ frɪndʒ / (AmE), / frɪndʒ / (BrE)
the outer edge or border of an area, especially the part of a city far from the center

Examples

He lived in an apartment house not over three or four years old, a reclaimed island of landscaped brick and glass on the fringe of the business district.

Synset Examples

they built a factory on the outskirts of the city

Related Terms

  • Synonym: outskirt - the outer parts of a city or town
  • Antonym: center - the central part of a city or area

Etymology

From Old French *fringe* (border, edge), from Vulgar Latin *frimbia*, ultimately from Latin *fimbriae* (threads, fibers, fringe).
