outrage

1. VERB

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
to cause someone to feel great anger or shock; to offend deeply

Examples

The kiss outraged our friends but it was done and meanwhile had released in me all the remote, exciting premonitions of lust, all the mysterious sensations that I had imagined a truly consummated kiss would convey to me.
It might be that Low has seen too many stupidities and that they do not outrage him now.

Synset Examples

The scandalous behavior of this married woman shocked her friends

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shock - to cause someone to feel surprised and upset
  • Synonym: offend - to cause displeasure or anger

Etymology

From Old French *outrager* (to do excessive harm), based on *outrage* (excess, insult), from Vulgar Latin *ultraticum*, from Latin *ultra* (beyond).

2. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
a wantonly cruel or violent act that shocks or offends people

Examples

As the first collective confrontation of the Nazi outrage, the Trial of Eichmann represents a recovery of the Jews from the shock of the death camps, a recovery that took fifteen years and which is still by no means complete (though let no one believe that it could be hastened by silence).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: atrocity - an extremely wicked or cruel act, especially one involving physical violence

Etymology

From Old French outrage (excess, insult), from outre (beyond), from Latin ultra (beyond).

3. NOUN

/ ˈaʊtˌreɪdʒ / (AmE), / ˈaʊt.reɪdʒ / (BrE)
A strong feeling of shock and anger caused by something perceived as unjust, offensive, or unfair.

Examples

Muller, nakedly exposed at the bright window like a deer pinned in a car's headlights, threw down the rifle and turned to jump from the table; his face wore a look of outrage.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: indignation - anger or annoyance provoked by perceived unfair treatment
  • Antonym: calm - a state of tranquility or peace, without anger

Etymology

From Old French *outrage* (excess, insolence), from Vulgar Latin *ultraticum*, from Latin *ultra* (beyond).
