obliterate

1. VERB

/ əˈblɪtəˌreɪt / (AmE), / əˈblɪtəreɪt / (BrE)
to remove all signs of something; to destroy or erase completely

Examples

The bad luck that can so often mar a well-played round of golf is simply overpowered and obliterated by the contemptuous boldness of these champions.

Synset Examples

kill these lines in the President's speech

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erase - to remove something completely, especially written or drawn marks
  • Synonym: delete - to remove, erase, or take out (especially digitally or from text)
  • Antonym: preserve - to keep something from being destroyed or erased

Etymology

From Latin oblitterare (to wipe out, cause to be forgotten), from ob- (over) + littera (letter).

2. VERB

/ əˈblɪtəˌreɪt / (AmE), / əˈblɪtəreɪt / (BrE)
to remove all traces of (something) from recognition or memory

Examples

The English never again developed a strong native music that could obliterate the traces of an earlier great age the way, say, the opera in Italy blotted out the Italian madrigal.

Synset Examples

efface the memory of the time in the camps

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erase - remove all traces of, especially written or recorded marks
  • Synonym: efface - wipe out or make indistinct, especially from memory or surface
  • Antonym: remember - to have (an event, thing, or person) in one's memory

Etymology

From Latin oblitterare, from ob- 'over' + littera 'letter'.
