derivation

1. NOUN

/ ˌdɛrəˈveɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛrɪˈveɪʃən / (BrE)
the origin or source from which something is derived

Examples

Such a dual derivation was strikingly demonstrated during the injection process where initial filling would be noted to occur in several isolated pleural vessels at once.

Synset Examples

he prefers shoes of Italian derivation
music of Turkish derivation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: origin - the point or place where something begins or is created

Etymology

From Latin derivatio (a drawing off, derivation), from derivare (to lead or draw off).

2. NOUN

/ ˌdɛrəˈveɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛrɪˈveɪʃən / (BrE)
an account of the historical origin and development of a word or phrase, especially as studied in historical linguistics

Examples

For example, when the film is only four minutes old, Neitzbohr refers to a small, Victorian piano stool as "Wilhelmina ", and we are thereupon subjected to a flashback that informs us that this very piano stool was once used by an epileptic governess whose name, of course, was Doris (the English equivalent, when passed through middle Gaelic derivations, of Wilhelmina).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: etymology - the study of the origin and history of words

Etymology

From Latin derivatio (drawing off, derivation), from derivare (to draw off)

3. NOUN

/ ˌdɛrəˈveɪʃən / (AmE), / ˌdɛrɪˈveɪʃən / (BrE)
a process of showing how a conclusion logically follows from accepted statements or propositions; a sequence of logical steps from premises to result

Examples

A chief characteristic of experience in the mode of causal efficacy is one of derivation from the past.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deduction - the inference of particular instances by reference to a general law or principle
  • Synonym: inference - a conclusion reached on the basis of evidence and reasoning
  • Antonym: induction - reasoning from specific cases to general principles

Etymology

From Latin derivatio (a drawing off, derivation), from derivare (to lead or draw off).
