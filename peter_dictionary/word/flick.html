flick

1. NOUN

/ flɪk / (AmE), / flɪk / (BrE)
a light, quick, and sharp movement, especially one that makes contact, as with a finger, whip, or brush

Examples

Angelina placed the tray on the table and with a flick of dark wrist drew off the cloth.
Finally come those little flicks of a rigger brush and the job is done.

Synset Examples

he gave it a flick with his finger
he felt the flick of a whip

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snap - a sudden, sharp movement or sound
  • Synonym: tap - a light touch or blow

Etymology

Originated in the mid-16th century, of imitative origin, possibly influenced by German 'flicken' (to strike lightly).

2. VERB

/ flɪk / (AmE), / flɪk / (BrE)
to make something move or to throw something with a sudden, quick motion, often using a sharp movement of part of the body

Examples

Greg's eyes flicked up from his instrument panel.

Synset Examples

flick a piece of paper across the table
jerk his head

Related Terms

  • Synonym: jerk - to move or cause to move with a sudden quick motion
  • Synonym: snap - to move or make something move suddenly and quickly

Etymology

Perhaps imitative of a sudden, light movement or noise; 19th-century origin.

3. VERB

/ flɪk / (AmE), / flɪk / (BrE)
To look through a book, pages, or items quickly, turning them over lightly or rapidly.

Examples

His mind flicked through the mental pictures he had from the hours of Aircraft Identification.

Synset Examples

He thumbed through the report
She leafed through the volume

Related Terms

  • Synonym: leaf through - to turn the pages of a book quickly, scanning the contents
  • Synonym: thumb through - to look quickly through the pages of a book or magazine

Etymology

Probably imitative of the quick, light motion implied by the word.

4. VERB

/ flɪk / (AmE), / flɪk / (BrE)
(of a light or signal) to flash or turn on and off intermittently.

Examples

And I had hardly finished my business in the toilet on the aforementioned occasion when the lights in that place, like the hall lights controlled from the switch in the office, flicked off and on impatiently.

Synset Examples

The lights flicked on and off

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flicker - to shine with a wavering or unsteady light

Etymology

Of imitative origin, possibly related to the sound or motion associated with quick movement.

5. VERB

/ flɪk / (AmE), / flɪk / (BrE)
to cause something to move with a sudden quick or light movement, usually by a brief motion of the finger, hand, or wrist

Examples

He nodded, clasping and unclasping his hands over his paunch, and flicked glances at me.

Synset Examples

he flicked his Bic

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flip - to turn over or move something quickly with a sudden movement

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound or light movement, first attested in the 19th century.
