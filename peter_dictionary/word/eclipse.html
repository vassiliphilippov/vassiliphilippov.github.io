eclipse

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
An occasion when one celestial body passes in front of another, partially or fully obscuring it from view.

Examples

The results of present observations of the thermal radio emission of the moon are consistent with the very low thermal conductivity of the surface layer which was derived from the variation in the infrared emission during eclipses (e.g., Garstung, 1958).
There had been an eclipse of the moon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: occultation - the blocking of one object in space by another between it and the observer
  • Synonym: transit - the passage of a celestial body across the disk of a larger one

Etymology

From Greek 'ekleipsis' (a leaving out, forsaking, eclipse), from 'ekleipō' (to leave out, fail to appear).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
to make someone or something seem less important or impressive by comparison; to overshadow

Examples

Both these youths, who greatly admired Henrietta, were somewhat younger than she, as were also the neighboring Friedenwald boys, who were then studying medicine; and bright though they all were, they could not possibly compete for her interest with Papa, whose mind - although he never tried to dazzle or patronize lesser lights with it - naturally eclipsed theirs and made them seem to her even younger than they were.

Synset Examples

the tragedy overshadowed the couple's happiness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: overshadow - to appear more important or impressive than someone or something else

Etymology

From Old French 'eclipse', from Latin 'eclipsis', from Greek 'ekleipsis' (abandonment, a failing, an eclipse).

3. VERB

/ ɪˈklɪps / (AmE), / ɪˈklɪps / (BrE)
to cause an eclipse of (a celestial body) by passing in front of it and obscuring its light or vision

Examples

It was a bold, dark castle of pine boughs that stood like a medieval fortress, eclipsing the sun and human time.

Synset Examples

The Sun eclipses the moon today
Planets and stars often are occulted by other celestial bodies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: occult - to hide or block (a celestial object) from view

Etymology

From Greek ekleipsis, from ekleipein 'to fail to appear, be eclipsed'.
