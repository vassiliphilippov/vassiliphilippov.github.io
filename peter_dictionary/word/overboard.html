overboard

1. ADV

/ ˈoʊvɚˌbɔrd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəbɔːd / (BrE)
from on or off a ship into the water

Examples

They often threw tidbits overboard.
The anchor cable would have been lost overboard, but Philip Staffe was on hand to sever it with his axe.
Then we hung overboard in the water.

Synset Examples

they dropped their garbage overboard

Etymology

From over + board (meaning the side of a ship).

2. ADV

/ ˈoʊvɚˌbɔrd / (AmE), / ˈəʊvəbɔːd / (BrE)
To an excessive or extreme degree; beyond reasonable limits.

Examples

Some plants go overboard on one type of fringe - say a liberal retirement plan - and find themselves vulnerable elsewhere.
Do n't go overboard on insurance that pays benefits only upon death.
But have we not gone overboard in stressing their significance?

Synset Examples

he went overboard to please his in-laws

Related Terms

  • Synonym: too far - beyond what is reasonable or acceptable
  • Synonym: go too far - to do something to an excessive or unacceptable extent
  • Antonym: moderately - to a reasonable or moderate degree

Etymology

Originally from nautical usage meaning 'over the side of a ship into the water'. Extended metaphorical sense of 'to excess' is from the idea of exceeding bounds.
