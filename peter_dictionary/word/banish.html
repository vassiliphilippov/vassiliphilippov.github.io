banish

1. VERB

/ ˈbæn.ɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbæn.ɪʃ / (BrE)
to send someone away from a place or group, usually as a punishment and with the order not to return

Examples

You know the law: if you lose the debate after accepting a challenge, you will be banished "!
"Monsieur Favre indicated that if I would co-operate, after you and William are banished, following the debate, I will be given a place of influence ".
"That I would rather be banished with two such Christians than be made the Chief Syndic "!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expel - to force someone to leave a place or organization
  • Synonym: exile - to send someone away and forbid them to return, typically as punishment

Etymology

From Old French *banir* (to proclaim, banish), from Frankish *banjan* (to order, command).

2. VERB

/ ˈbænɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbanɪʃ / (BrE)
To cause something unwanted, such as a thought, feeling, or condition, to go away or disappear.

Examples

I lived in this onward driving contest where each day overcame a new difficulty, gained a new truth, or banished a previous error ".

Synset Examples

banish bad thoughts
banish gloom

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dispel - to drive away a thought or feeling
  • Antonym: summon - to call forth or bring to mind

Etymology

From Old French *banir* (to proclaim, banish), of Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ ˈbænɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbænɪʃ / (BrE)
To expel someone from a place, especially as an official punishment, often with the intention of them not returning.

Examples

The Jews had been banished from England in 1290 and were not permitted to return before 1655, when Shakespeare had been dead for thirty-nine years.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exile - the state or act of being barred from one's native country
  • Synonym: expel - to force someone to leave a place or organization
  • Antonym: repatriate - to send someone back to their own country, the reverse action of banish

Etymology

From Middle English 'banyssen', from Old French 'banir' (to proclaim, banish), of Germanic origin.

4. VERB

/ ˈbænɪʃ / (AmE), / ˈbænɪʃ / (BrE)
to expel someone from a place, especially as an official punishment or decree

Examples

Refrigeration, automated processing and packaging conspire to defy season and banish spoilage.

Synset Examples

he was banished from his own country

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exile - to force someone to live away from their native country
  • Synonym: expel - to force someone to leave a place
  • Antonym: recall - to bring back someone who was banished

Etymology

From Middle English banysshen, from Old French banir (to proclaim, to banish), from Germanic origin.
