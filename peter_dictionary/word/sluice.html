sluice

1. VERB

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
To wash or irrigate with a stream of water from a sluice or similar device.

Examples

"So that's sculpture ", commented Argiento wryly, when he had sluiced down the floor for a week, "making mud pies ".

Synset Examples

sluice the earth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rinse - to wash lightly with water
  • Degree Higher: flood - to inundate with a large amount of water

Etymology

From Old French *escluse* (sluice), from Latin *excludere* (to exclude, shut out).

2. VERB

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
(of liquid) to pour or flow forcefully and in large amounts, as if released from a sluice.

Examples

And then came the water - not rain, but solid sheets that sluiced down like water slopping from a bucket.

Synset Examples

An aggressive tide sluiced across the barrier reef

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gush - to flow out suddenly and in large amounts
  • Antonym: trickle - to flow in a small, slow stream

Etymology

From Old French escluse, from Late Latin exclusa (sluice, dam).

3. NOUN

/ sluːs / (AmE), / sluːs / (BrE)
A channel or passage for water, fitted with a gate (sluicegate) for controlling the flow.

Examples

He let down the tailgate and was knocked over by the sluice of water.

Etymology

From Old French *escluse*, from Medieval Latin *exclusa* (shut in), from Latin *excludere* (to shut out).
