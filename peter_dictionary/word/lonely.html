lonely

1. ADJ

/ ˈloʊnli / (AmE), / ˈləʊnli / (BrE)
Unhappy because you are alone or do not have anyone to talk to; lacking companions or companionship.

Examples

Ann Catt was a lonely, devoted soul, never married, conducting a spotless home and devoted to her church, but a perpetual dissenter and born critic.
The thought of this lonely woman sitting at her window touched him, although he was even more touched by her plumpness.
The remarks she made about the sufferings of a lonely woman seemed so broad at first that he did n't know what to make of them, but after the sixth drink he put his arm around her and suggested that they go upstairs and look for her checkbook there.
He said he was the lonely type and working in a cellar you saw funny things coming out of the cracks in the wall if they was n't nobody with you.

Synset Examples

he was alone when we met him
she is alone much of the time
the lone skier on the mountain
a lonely fisherman stood on a tuft of gravel
a lonely soul
a solitary traveler

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alone - having no one else present; solitary
  • Synonym: solitary - done or existing alone
  • Antonym: crowded - full of people; having many companions

Etymology

From 'lone' (an aphetic variant of 'alone') + -ly, dating from the late 16th century.

2. ADJ

/ ˈloʊnli / (AmE), / ˈləʊnli / (BrE)
Unhappy because you are alone or do not have anyone to talk to.

Examples

"I was lonely always ".
"But now I am not lonely.
I grok I shall never be lonely again ".
After a short time, both George and Donald joined the class with me so they would n't feel lonely, and we used to hang a sign on the door of the Brush-off reading "out to work ".

Synset Examples

felt sad and lonely
the loneliest night of the week
lonesome when her husband is away
spent a lonesome hour in the bar

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lonesome - feeling sad because of being alone
  • Degree Lower: solitary - preferring or living alone; without companions
  • Degree Higher: desolate - feeling or showing misery, unhappiness, or loneliness to a greater degree

Etymology

From 'lone' + '-ly'; 'lone' is a back-formation from 'alone'.
