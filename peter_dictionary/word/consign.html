consign

1. VERB

/ kənˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kənˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to put someone or something into a place or situation, especially one considered undesirable, or to commit irrevocably to a particular fate or outcome

Examples

There was no church like Drew Church, no preacher like Papa, who was intimate with Him, and could consign sinners to hellfire.
Long ago they consigned the notions of Kyne and Dixon to the scrap heap.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: commit - to entrust or assign to a particular purpose or place
  • Synonym: entrust - to assign the responsibility for doing something to someone
  • Degree Higher: abandon - to leave someone or something completely, usually forever

Etymology

From Latin consignare (to seal, ratify), from con- (together) + signare (to sign).

2. VERB

/ kənˈsaɪn / (AmE), / kənˈsaɪn / (BrE)
to give or transfer something into the hands or control of another for care, safekeeping, or disposal

Examples

He would consign the cash box into the hands of Jed Hawksworth, then stand by while his employer checked the contents and the list of items sold.

Synset Examples

consign your baggage

Related Terms

  • Synonym: entrust - assign the responsibility for something to someone
  • Synonym: relinquish - to voluntarily give up or hand over

Etymology

From Latin 'consignare' (to seal, mark), via French 'consigner'.
