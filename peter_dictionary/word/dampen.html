dampen

1. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
To make slightly wet or moist.

Examples

Pick the flowers, keep the soil dampened, and each of the pegged-down branches will take root and become a little plant and go on blooming for the rest of the season.
To form spout, between two designs, dampen area slightly and gently push clay outward.

Synset Examples

The dew moistened the meadows

Related Terms

  • Synonym: moisten - to make or become slightly wet

Etymology

From damp + -en (verb-forming suffix).

2. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
To make something less strong or intense; to diminish or suppress (especially feelings, enthusiasm, or prospects).

Examples

The altercation in the coffee house had done little to dampen his spirits, but he was still a little wary around Rector for they had not yet discussed the incident.
Sadie, like Beth March, suffered ill health - got rheumatic fever and had to be careful of her heart - but that never dampened her spirits.
Initially the White House reaction was that the bitter exchanges with Moscow over Cuba and the conflict in Laos had dampened prospects for a meeting.

Synset Examples

Stifle your curiosity

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lessen - to make or become less; reduce
  • Antonym: intensify - to make or become more intense

Etymology

From Middle English dampen, from damp (moisture, vapor).

3. VERB

/ ˈdæmpən / (AmE), / ˈdæmpən / (BrE)
To make something less strong or effective; to lessen the force, intensity, or impact of something.

Examples

Won't this gang war dampen the city's 200th anniversary?

Synset Examples

soften a shock
break a fall

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soften - to make less severe or intense
  • Antonym: intensify - to make stronger or more intense

Etymology

From damp + -en, meaning to make or become damp, later extended to 'lessen' metaphorically.
