sheath

1. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
A close-fitting protective covering, especially for the blade of a knife or sword.

Examples

This energy transfer depends on the current, the temperature in the arc column, the anode material, and the conditions in the anode sheath.
Grabbing his Winchester from its sheath, Cook prepared to fight from behind the arroyo bank.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scabbard - protective case for a sword or dagger

Etymology

Old English sceath (covering, sheath), cognate with Old Norse skeið (sheath, scabbard).

2. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
A protective, enclosing layer or structure that surrounds an organ or part, especially in animals or plants.

Examples

In February, 1959, during the second admission to The New York Hospital, a biopsy specimen of the left gastrocnemius showed striking increase in the sarcolemmal sheath nuclei and shrunken muscle fibers in several sections.

Etymology

Old English *sceatha* (covering, sheath), related to German *Scheide*.

3. NOUN

/ ʃiθ / (AmE), / ʃiːθ / (BrE)
a woman's close-fitting dress, typically straight-cut and nipped at the waist without a waist seam, often suitable for formal occasions

Examples

Carrying it to the living room, she imagined the picture she made: tall and roundly slim, a bit sophisticated in her yellow sheath, with a graceful swingy walk that she had learned as a twirler with the school band.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shift - a simple, straight-cut dress without a defined waist

Etymology

From Old English *sceāth* (cover, sheath), originally referring to a covering, later extended to the clothing style.
