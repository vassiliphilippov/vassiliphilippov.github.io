fog

1. NOUN

/ fɑɡ / (AmE), / fɒɡ / (BrE)
A thick cloud of tiny water droplets suspended in the atmosphere at or near the earth's surface, which obscures or restricts visibility.

Examples

I dig it, rake it smooth, sow the seeds and wet them down with a fog spray.
There was a slight lapse condition, a moderate fog, and 100 per cent relative humidity.
There was a fog, which increased the darkness of the night.
Somewhere in the fog, the nigger boy was still yelling murder.
His mind closed on that prospect, as though fog had descended to blot out a valley.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: mist - a thin cloud of water droplets in the air, less dense than fog

Etymology

Origin uncertain; perhaps related to Old Norse 'fog', meaning 'mist, spray', or to the obsolete English word 'fog' (long grass).

2. NOUN

/ fɑɡ / (AmE), / fɒɡ / (BrE)
An atmosphere in which visibility is reduced because of a cloud or mist of some substance.

Examples

What is beyond this fog?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: mist - a thin fog with condensation near the ground
  • Synonym: haze - reduced visibility due to fine suspended particles in the air
  • Antonym: clarity - clearness or lucidity of the atmosphere

3. NOUN

/ fɑɡ / (AmE), / fɒɡ / (BrE)
A state of mental confusion or a lack of clarity in thought or expression.

Examples

Questions like this, framed in verbal fog, are perhaps the only kind that have ever stumped an experienced politician.
He knows that he is thinking about action in relation to a future which can be perceived but dimly through a merciful fog.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: haze - mental confusion or vagueness
  • Antonym: clarity - clearness of mind or expression

Etymology

From Old Norse 'fog', meaning mist or spray.
