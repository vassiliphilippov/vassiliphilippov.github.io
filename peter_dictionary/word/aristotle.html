Aristotle

1. PROPN

/ ˈærɪˌstɑːtəl / (AmE), / ˈærɪˌstɒtəl / (BrE)
(384–322 BC) A Greek philosopher, pupil of Plato and teacher of Alexander the Great, regarded as one of the greatest ancient thinkers and influential in many fields, including logic, metaphysics, ethics, and natural sciences.

Examples

The word "mimesis " ("imitation ") is usually associated with Plato and Aristotle.
Aristotle also tended to stratify all aspects of human nature and activity into levels of excellence and, like Plato, he put the pure and unimpassioned intellect on the top level.
But Aristotle kept the principle of levels and even augmented it by describing in the Poetics what kinds of character and action must be imitated if the play is to be a vehicle of serious and important human truths.
For both Plato and Aristotle artistic mimesis, in contrast to the power of dialectic, is relatively incapable of expressing the character of fundamental reality.

Etymology

From Greek Aristotélēs (Ἀριστοτέλης).
