artillery

1. NOUN

/ ɑrˈtɪlɚi / (AmE), / ɑːˈtɪl.ər.i / (BrE)
Large, heavy guns or cannons that are designed to launch projectiles over a long distance, typically used in warfare.

Examples

Then we pull out under our mortar and artillery cover, but nobody pulls out until I say so.
On Big Stone Lake near the headwaters of the Red River, Robert Dickson, Superintendent of the Western Indian Department of Canada, had a trading post and planned in 1818 to build a fort to be defended by twenty men and two small artillery pieces.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cannon - a large, heavy gun typically mounted on wheels
  • Synonym: howitzer - a type of artillery piece with a short barrel
  • Antonym: small arms - weapons such as rifles and pistols, contrasted with artillery

Etymology

From Old French 'artillerie', from 'artillier' (to equip), based on Latin 'ars' (art).

2. NOUN

/ ɑrˈtɪlɚˌi / (AmE), / ɑːˈtɪl.əri / (BrE)
A branch of an army or military unit equipped with large-caliber guns used in warfare.

Examples

Artillery and air strikes were called in to kill them by the hundreds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: infantry - branch of army that fights on foot

Etymology

From Old French 'artillerie', from 'artillier' (to equip), ultimately from Latin 'articulum' (small joint, mechanism).

3. NOUN

/ ɑrˈtɪlɚi / (AmE), / ɑːˈtɪləri / (BrE)
powerful arguments or methods used to persuade or influence, especially in a forceful or overwhelming way

Examples

First, the student trains on his hapless parents the heavy artillery of his newly acquired psychological and sociological insights.

Synset Examples

he used all his conversational weapons

Related Terms

  • Synonym: weapon - something used to achieve an aim or gain an advantage, especially in an argument

Etymology

From Old French *artillerie*, from *artiller* (to equip), ultimately from Latin *articulum* (joint, tool).
