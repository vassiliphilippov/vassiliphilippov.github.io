erupt

1. VERB

/ ɪˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ɪˈrʌpt / (BrE)
(of something, especially something unpleasant or violent) to begin or appear suddenly and forcefully.

Examples

A bevy of interlacing lagers, work camps, concentration camps erupted in the area.
The Kennedy administration moves haltingly toward a Geneva conference on Laos just as serious debate over its foreign policy erupts for the first time.
That was something of an understatement in a week when the underlying conflict between the West and Communism erupted on three fronts.
Presently the well-armed members of the Force Publique - many of them drawn from savage and even cannibalistic tribes, erupted in mutiny, rioting, raping and looting.
A truth-revealing crisis erupted in Katanga for a couple of days this month, to be quickly smothered by the high pressure verbal fog that is kept on tap for such emergencies.

Synset Examples

After 1989, peace broke out in the former East Bloc

Related Terms

  • Synonym: break out - to start suddenly (of war, fire, violence, etc.)
  • Antonym: subside - to become less intense or violent after an eruption or violence

Etymology

From Latin 'erumpere', meaning 'to burst forth' (ex- 'out' + rumpere 'to break').

2. VERB

/ ɪˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ɪˈrʌpt / (BrE)
(of a feeling, conflict, or situation) to begin suddenly and violently; to break out or intensify rapidly.

Examples

It has erupted on the national level in the matter of including birth-control information and material in foreign aid to underdeveloped countries.
Where it is not actually erupting, it rumbles and smolders in sullen resentment like a volcano, ready to explode at any moment.

Synset Examples

Unrest erupted in the country
Tempers flared at the meeting
The crowd irrupted into a burst of patriotism

Related Terms

  • Synonym: flare - to suddenly begin or intensify, especially in conflict or emotion
  • Antonym: subside - to become less intense, violent, or severe

Etymology

From Latin eruptus, past participle of erumpere, meaning 'to burst forth.'

3. VERB

/ ɪˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ɪˈrʌpt / (BrE)
(of a fire or flame) to start suddenly or burst into flames.

Examples

Luckily, she was not completely aflame and would go down before the gasoline could erupt.

Synset Examples

Marsh gases ignited suddenly
The oily rags combusted spontaneously

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ignite - to catch fire or cause to catch fire
  • Synonym: combust - to start to burn
  • Antonym: extinguish - to cause a fire or light to stop burning

Etymology

From Latin 'erumpere' (to burst forth, break out).

4. VERB

/ ɪˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ɪˈrʌpt / (BrE)
(of a tooth or similar structure) to break through the surface or skin, especially through the gums.

Examples

If baby teeth are retained too long, the incoming second teeth may be prevented from emerging at the normal time or may have to erupt in the wrong place.

Synset Examples

The tooth erupted and had to be extracted

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emerge - to come into view or become apparent

Etymology

From Latin 'erumpere' meaning 'to burst forth'.

5. VERB

/ ɪˈrʌpt / (AmE), / ɪˈrʌpt / (BrE)
(of a volcano or similar natural feature) to become active and send out lava, rocks, ash, or gas suddenly and violently.

Examples

One of the wagons erupted a massive pillar of flame.

Synset Examples

Vesuvius erupts once in a while

Related Terms

  • Synonym: explode - to burst or shatter violently
  • Antonym: subside - to become less intense or violent (of volcanoes, etc.)

Etymology

From Latin 'erumpere' (to burst forth).
