pain

1. NOUN

/ peɪn / (AmE), / peɪn / (BrE)
An unpleasant physical sensation caused by illness or injury.

Examples

When she submitted to his treatment with the capsules, Mrs. Shaefer felt intense pain.
Severe back pain in June, 1959, prompted a third hospital admission.
On July 4, 1959, the patient developed marked abdominal pain and distension, went into shock, and died.
There is perhaps no value statement on which people would more universally agree than the statement that intense pain is bad.
There are signs that it has struggled for days to escape and that in a frenzy of hunger, pain, and fear, it has all but eaten off its own leg.

Synset Examples

the patient developed severe pain and distension

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ache - a continuous or dull pain
  • Degree Lower: discomfort - slight pain or physical unease
  • Degree Higher: agony - extreme or intense pain

Etymology

From Old French *peine*, from Latin *poena* (penalty, punishment).

3. NOUN

/ peɪn / (AmE), / peɪn / (BrE)
A physical feeling of discomfort or suffering, often caused by injury or illness.

Examples

After his pains got worse, Tom decided to see a real doctor, from whom he learned he was suffering from cancer of the lung.
In a conditioning experiment, he demonstrated the antagonism between feeding and pain.
The pain became thus the symbol for food and elicited salivary secretion (conditioned reflex).
Even when the intensity of the shocks was increased gradually, it failed to evoke any signs of pain.
Since strong nociceptive stimuli produce an experimental neurosis during which the animals fail to eat in the experimental situation, Wolpe thought that he could utilize the feeding pain antagonism to inhibit the neurotic symptoms through feeding.

Synset Examples

as the intensity increased the sensation changed from tickle to pain

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ache - a continuous, dull pain
  • Synonym: soreness - painful or tender sensation, especially in muscles or joints
  • Antonym: comfort - a state of physical ease and freedom from pain

Etymology

From Old French *paine*, from Latin *poena* (penalty, punishment).

4. VERB

/ peɪn / (AmE), / peɪn / (BrE)
to cause bodily suffering or discomfort; to make someone feel physical pain or indisposed

Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hurt - to cause physical pain or injury

Etymology

From Old French *paine*, from Latin *poena* (punishment, penalty).

5. NOUN

/ peɪn / (AmE), / peɪn / (BrE)
A person or thing that causes irritation, trouble, or inconvenience; a nuisance.

Examples

I left it noisy, so it scares away any pain-in-the-ass innocent bystanders.

Synset Examples

washing dishes was a nuisance before we got a dish washer

Related Terms

  • Synonym: nuisance - a person, thing, or circumstance causing inconvenience or annoyance
  • Synonym: bother - a cause of minor trouble or irritation
  • Antonym: pleasure - a feeling of happy satisfaction and enjoyment; the opposite of a nuisance

Etymology

From Old French *peine*, from Latin *poena* (punishment, penalty, pain).
