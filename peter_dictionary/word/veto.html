veto

1. NOUN

/ ˈviːtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
The power or right to reject or prohibit a proposed decision, law, or act, especially by casting a vote that blocks its effect.

Examples

With the neutralists maintaining pressure for one of their own to succeed Mr. Hammarskjold, U Thant emerged as the only possible candidate unlikely to be waylaid by a veto.
Constitutional government, popular vote, trial by jury, public education, labor unions, cooperatives, communes, socialized ownership, world courts, and the veto power in world councils are but a few examples.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: approval - the action of officially agreeing to or accepting something

Etymology

From Latin *veto* (I forbid), from *vetare* (to forbid).

2. VERB

/ ˈviːtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
to refuse to accept or approve something, especially officially; to reject a decision or proposal by exercising a right or authority

Examples

He considered opening a can of beer but vetoed that idea too.

Synset Examples

The President vetoed the bill

Related Terms

  • Antonym: approve - to officially agree to or accept something
  • Synonym: reject - to refuse to accept, consider, or use something

Etymology

From Latin 'veto' (I forbid), from 'vetare' (to forbid).

3. VERB

/ ˈviːˌtoʊ / (AmE), / ˈviːtəʊ / (BrE)
to refuse to allow something, especially officially; to prohibit or reject a proposal or decision, particularly by exercising a right or authority

Examples

If any one of them has any power to veto the Secretary General's decisions the nature of the organization will have changed.

Synset Examples

I forbid you to call me late at night
Mother vetoed the trip to the chocolate store
Dad nixed our plans

Related Terms

  • Antonym: approve - to officially agree to or accept

Etymology

From Latin 'veto' (I forbid), from 'vetare' (to forbid).
