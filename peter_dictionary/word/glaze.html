glaze

1. VERB

/ ɡleɪz / (AmE), / ɡleɪz / (BrE)
to coat (an object or surface) with a smooth or shiny layer, often to give a glossy finish or seal it

Examples

McFeeley noted the immaculate lawn and gardens: each blade of grass cropped, bright and firm; each shrub glazed with good health.
Instructions for preparing clay, drying, glazing and firing are not given.
glazed with two coats of Creek-Turn white stoneware glaze (no glaze on sides or bottom).
Glaze with two coats of clear or transparent matt glaze.
Glaze with two coats of matt glazes in turquoise with touches of blossom pink on lid.

Synset Examples

the potter glazed the dishes
glaze the bread with eggwhite

Related Terms

  • Synonym: varnish - to apply a protective, glossy coating (often to wood)
  • Synonym: gloss - to make shiny or glossy by applying a gloss

Etymology

From Middle English glasen, from Old English glæs (glass); related to glass.

2. VERB

/ ɡleɪz / (AmE), / ɡleɪz / (BrE)
(of eyes) to become glassy or start to look shiny and unfocused, often as a result of emotion, boredom, or unconsciousness.

Examples

The creature in feathers looked around and David saw the mad eyes, glazed with an insane fear.

Synset Examples

Her eyes glaze over when she is bored

Related Terms

  • Antonym: cloud - (of eyes) to become less clear or blurred in appearance

Etymology

From Old French *glasier* (to glaze), from *glas* (glass), of Germanic origin.
