bark

1. NOUN

/ bɑrk / (AmE), / bɑːk / (BrE)
A sailing ship with three or more masts, typically with a particular rigging arrangement, used especially in the 18th and 19th centuries.

Examples

On April 17, 1610, the sturdy little three masted bark, Discovery, weighed anchor in St. Katherine's Pool, London, and floated down the Thames toward the sea.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: barque - alternative spelling for a type of three-masted sailing ship

Etymology

From Middle French *barque* (ship), from Latin *barca* (boat), likely of Celtic origin.

2. NOUN

/ bɑrk / (AmE), / bɑːk / (BrE)
The tough protective outer covering of the stems and roots of trees and other woody plants.

Examples

Most of the letters were written in the hubbub of camp, on stumps, pieces of bark, drum heads, or the knee.
Every morning early, in the summer, we searched the trunks of the trees as high as we could reach for the locust shells, carefully detached their hooked claws from the bark where they hung, and stabled them, a weird faery herd, in an angle between the high roots of the tulip tree, where no grass grew in the dense shade.
Swadesh, and therefore Hoijer, felt compelled to omit all terms denoting species or even genera (fox, vulture, salmon, yellow pine, manzanita); their classes of animal and plant terms are restricted to generalizations or recurrent parts (fish, bird, tree, grass, horn, tail, bark, root).

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rind - the tough outer skin of some fruits or vegetables; can refer to tree covering in rare contexts
  • Degree Lower: skin - the outer layer or covering of an organism or thing; broader meaning than 'bark'

Etymology

From Old Norse *bǫrkr* (bark of a tree), related to Middle Dutch *barc*.

3. VERB

/ bɑrk / (AmE), / bɑːk / (BrE)
to speak to someone in a sharp, unfriendly, or abrupt manner

Examples

He would pick up the ringing phone with studied negligence, then bark into it with gruff importance.

Synset Examples

She barked into the dictaphone

Related Terms

  • Synonym: snap - to speak sharply or suddenly, especially in anger
  • Synonym: growl - to speak in a low, angry, or unfriendly voice

Etymology

Figurative use from the literal sense of 'bark' meaning the sound made by a dog, Old English 'beorcan'.

4. VERB

/ bɑrk / (AmE), / bɑːk / (BrE)
(of a dog or other animal) to make a short, loud, sharp sound.

Examples

[SCREAMING] [DOGS BARKING] MAN: Over here!

Synset Examples

The dogs barked at the stranger

Related Terms

  • Synonym: woof - a similar sharp sound made by a dog; used especially in children's language
  • Degree Lower: growl - to make a low, threatening sound, especially by a dog
  • Degree Higher: howl - to make a long, loud, wailing sound, as some dogs do

Etymology

From Old English 'beorcian' (to bark), of imitative origin.
