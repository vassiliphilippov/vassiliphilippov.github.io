storm

1. NOUN

/ stɔrm / (AmE), / stɔːm / (BrE)
A situation of intense disturbance or commotion, often causing turmoil or upheaval.

Examples

The storms of the past had died away, and the great upheaval which was to mark the following century had not yet begun to disturb men's minds.
As construction progresses, the volume of storm drainage will be sharply reduced.
I just didn't expect the storm would last as long as it has.

Synset Examples

the storms that had characterized their relationship had died away
it was only a tempest in a teapot

Related Terms

  • Synonym: uproar - a state of noisy confusion or excitement
  • Antonym: calm - stillness or peace, especially following a storm

Etymology

From Old English storm, from Proto-Germanic *sturmaz (noise, tumult).

2. NOUN

/ stɔrm / (AmE), / stɔːm / (BrE)
a violent weather condition marked by strong winds, usually with rain, thunder, lightning, snow, or other precipitation

Examples

He was Papa's chosen; therefore, nothing but good could happen to him, even in God's wrathful storms.
After the storm, the sky cleared blue and cool, and fragrant air swept the hills.
They had for cover both darkness and a summer storm.
It was dark early, because of the storm.
Also because of the storm, the streets of Rockfork were deserted.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tempest - a violent windy storm, often with rain, hail, or snow

Etymology

From Old English *storm* (violent disturbance, tumult), of Germanic origin.

3. VERB

/ stɔrm / (AmE), / stɔːm / (BrE)
to move or behave with great anger or force, often in a sudden or dramatic way

Examples

With Ritter he was exposed to the fantastic profusion of ideas that stormed through his host's fertile but disorganized mind.
Each time Letch "went up "in his "lines ", I was the one to be patient, helpful and apologetic while he indulged in outbursts of temperament, profanity and abuse, blaming others, going into "sulks "and, on more occasions than I care to count, storming off the "set "for the rest of the day.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rage - to move or behave with great anger or violence
  • Antonym: calm - to behave or move without anger or agitation

Etymology

From Old English *storm* (violent disturbance of the atmosphere), later extended figuratively.

4. VERB

/ stɔrm / (AmE), / stɔːm / (BrE)
to attack and capture (a place) by sudden and forceful assault

Examples

It came to him as he wept there aboard the Somers that it was as foolish to strive for greatness as to seek to storm the gates of heaven.

Synset Examples

Storm the fort

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assault - make a physical attack on; attack violently
  • Synonym: besiege - surround and attack with armed forces

Etymology

From Old English 'storm' (violent disturbance), influenced by Old Norse 'stormr'.
