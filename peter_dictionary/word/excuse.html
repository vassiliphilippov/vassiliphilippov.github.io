excuse

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈskjuz / (AmE), / ɪkˈskjuːz / (BrE)
to attempt to justify or defend (an action, behavior, or person), often by providing reasons or explanations

Examples

1) Every age rewrites the events of its history in terms of what should have been, creating legends about itself that rationalize contemporary beliefs and excuse contemporary actions.

Synset Examples

rationalize the child's seemingly crazy behavior
he rationalized his lack of success

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justify - to show or prove to be right or reasonable
  • Antonym: condemn - to express strong disapproval of; to judge to be wrong

Etymology

From Latin excusare (to free from blame), from ex- (from) + causa (accusation).

2. NOUN

/ ɪkˈskjus / (AmE), / ɪkˈskjuːs / (BrE)
A reason or explanation given to justify a fault, failure, or offensive behavior, often to avoid blame or criticism.

Examples

"Ex-cuse me ", he said in Berlitz English, and got up and left them, to bathe and dress.
His arm had been giving him some trouble and Rector was not enough of a medical expert to determine whether it had healed improperly or whether Hino was simply rebelling against the tedious work in the print shop, using the stiffness in his arm as an excuse.
More often than not I have found easy excuse to leave my own work and stand at a respectable distance where I could watch this man transform raw nature into a composed, not imitative, painting.
There was excuse enough to keep him home, that young Mr. Arthur's still over there ".
He and the prime minister would be back from the window, seated at Mahzeer's desk; they would be going over papers Mahzeer had saved as excuse for just such a meeting.

Synset Examples

he kept finding excuses to stay
every day he had a new alibi for not getting a job
his transparent self-justification was unacceptable

Related Terms

  • Synonym: justification - a reason or explanation that makes something seem acceptable
  • Synonym: alibi - an excuse or reason for not being present or responsible
  • Antonym: accusation - a claim that someone has done something wrong

Etymology

From Old French *escuse*, from Latin *excusa* (justification, apology), from *excusare* (to excuse).

3. VERB

/ ɪkˈskjuz / (AmE), / ɪkˈskjuːz / (BrE)
to politely ask someone to forgive you for a minor fault or interruption; used when politely getting someone's attention or apologizing.

Examples

-' Scuse me, sir.
In one cartoon a family is shown outside a theater with the head of the family addressing the doorman: "Excuse me, but when we came out we found that we had left my daughter's handbag and my wife's behind ".
Excuse me.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pardon - to forgive; used in polite speech similarly to 'excuse'
  • Synonym: forgive - to stop feeling angry or resentful toward (someone for an offense)

Etymology

From Latin excusare (to free from blame).

4. VERB

/ ɪkˈskjuz / (AmE), / ɪkˈskjuːz / (BrE)
to serve as a reason or justification for something; to make an action appear less blameworthy by providing an explanation

Examples

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin excusare (to free from blame), from ex- (out) + causa (cause, reason).
