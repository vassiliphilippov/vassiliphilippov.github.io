interpose

1. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to introduce something or someone, especially so as to interfere or intervene between two parties or events

Examples

But He set a bound to his [ state of ] sin, by interposing death, and thus causing sin to cease, putting an end to it by the dissolution of the flesh, which should take place in the earth, so that man, ceasing at length to live in sin, and dying to it, might live to God ".

Synset Examples

God interposed death

Related Terms

  • Synonym: insert - to put something into something else
  • Synonym: intervene - to come or occur between events, people, or points in time

Etymology

From Latin interponere, from inter- ‘between’ + ponere ‘to put’.

2. VERB

/ ˌɪntərˈpoʊz / (AmE), / ˌɪntəˈpəʊz / (BrE)
to place or insert something or oneself between other things or people, especially to intervene or interrupt

Examples

Communist guerrillas recently have been reported increasing their activities and the great flood of the Mekong River has interposed a new crisis.

Synset Examples

She interjected clever remarks

Related Terms

  • Synonym: interject - to say or insert something abruptly, especially as an interruption

Etymology

From Latin 'interponere', from 'inter-' (between) + 'ponere' (to put).
