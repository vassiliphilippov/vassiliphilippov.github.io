gull

1. VERB

/ ɡʌl / (AmE), / ɡʌl / (BrE)
to trick or deceive someone; to make a fool of someone

Examples

Although the false glamour surrounding bourbon or other whisky commercials is possibly no more fatuous than the pseudo-sophistication with which TV soft-drinks are downed or toothpaste applied, there is a sad difference between enticing a viewer into sipping Oopsie-Cola and gulling him into downing bourbon.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dupe - to deceive or trick someone, especially by making them believe something untrue
  • Synonym: deceive - to cause someone to believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive or cheat someone

Etymology

Probably from Old Norse *gull* (fool), or related to 'gull' (a credulous person).

2. VERB

/ ɡʌl / (AmE), / ɡʌl / (BrE)
to deceive or cheat someone; to fool or trick

Examples

The principle is commendable but we suspect that in the practice somebody is going to get gulled.

Synset Examples

The immigrant was duped because he trusted everyone
You can't fool me!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dupe - to deceive or trick someone
  • Synonym: trick - to deceive or cheat by cunning
  • Synonym: deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Synonym: fool - to trick or mislead

Etymology

Perhaps from Old French *guille* (deceit, trickery), or related to 'gull' (dupe, fool), of uncertain origin.

3. NOUN

/ ɡʌl / (AmE), / ɡʌl / (BrE)
A mainly white aquatic bird with long pointed wings and short legs, commonly found near coasts and lakes.

Examples

[DOOR SLAMMING] [GULLS CAWING] [SINGING IN ITALIAN] [CHURCH ORGAN PLAYING] [PRIEST RECITING IN LATIN] [SPEAKING LATIN] [UPBEAT MUSIC PLAYING] MAN: Did you bring the car keys?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: seagull - an informal term for the gull, especially those found near the sea

Etymology

Probably related to Old Norse *gulr* (yellow) or perhaps from Welsh *gwylan* (seagull).
