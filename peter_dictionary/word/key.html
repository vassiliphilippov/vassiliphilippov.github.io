key

1. ADJ

/ kiː / (AmE), / kiː / (BrE)
Of crucial importance; essential or central to achieving a result or understanding something.

Examples

"He knocked down the interference and made key stops lots of times.
When that failed, he enlisted Branch Rickey's aid in the formation of a third major league, the Continental, with New York as the key franchise.
Here are some key areas to examine to make sure your pricing strategy will be on target:
I submit that this is the key problem of international relations, that it always has been, that it always will be.
The soldiers are fighting and the Americans are helping, he said, but in the fight against the Pathet Lao the key factor is the villager himself.

Synset Examples

a cardinal rule
the central cause of the problem
an example that was fundamental to the argument
computers are fundamental to modern industrial structure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: central - being in the center or of greatest importance
  • Synonym: fundamental - serving as a basic, essential component
  • Antonym: peripheral - of secondary or minor importance; marginal

Etymology

From Old English 'cǣg' (instrument for locking/unlocking), which led to figurative use for something providing an essential means or explanation.

2. VERB

/ ki / (AmE), / kiː / (BrE)
to identify (a plant, animal, etc.), especially by using a biological or taxonomic key

Examples

Let us not try to key them out at this stage of the game, and let us just call them Bombus; there must be several dozen species in the United States alone.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: identify - to recognize or determine what something is
  • Synonym: classify - to arrange in categories based on shared characteristics

3. NOUN

/ kiː / (AmE), / kiː / (BrE)
A small, shaped piece of metal used for operating the lock of a door, vehicle, etc., by fitting into it and turning.

Examples

A romantic is one who thinks the world is divinely inspired and all he has to do is find the right key, and then divine justice and altruism will appear.

Finding the key under his shoe, he started the engine, and while it warmed up he turned to her standing there in the dripping fog, and said, "Defrost the refrigerator ".
"Have you got our keys handy "?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: lock - a device that secures something and is operated by a key

Etymology

From Old English cǣg (key), origin uncertain.

4. NOUN

/ ki / (AmE), / kiː / (BrE)
a thing that provides an essential explanation or solution; the most important factor in achieving or understanding something

Examples

The key to effective marketing is wrapped up in defining your company's marketing problems realistically.
The key to the world of geology is change; nothing remains the same.
The key to Protestant development, therefore, is economic integration of the nucleus of the congregation.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solution - means of solving a problem or dealing with a difficult situation
  • Synonym: secret - something that gives a means of solving or understanding a paradox or mystery
  • Antonym: obstacle - something that makes achieving understanding more difficult

Etymology

From Old English 'cæg' (instrument for opening locks); figurative sense developed later.

5. NOUN

/ kiː / (AmE), / kiː / (BrE)
(Music) The group of notes based on a particular note that forms the tonal framework of a piece of music, such as the key of C major or G minor.

Examples

And I would further note that they all - with one exception again - sang in one key or another the same song.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: tonality - the character of a piece of music as determined by the key or scale used

Etymology

From Old French 'clef', from Latin 'clavis' (key), originally referring to a physical key, later transferred in music to signify the starting note or tonal center.
