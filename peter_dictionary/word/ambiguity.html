ambiguity

1. NOUN

/ ˌæmbɪˈɡjuəti / (AmE), / ˌambɪˈɡjuːɪti / (BrE)
The quality of being open to more than one interpretation; uncertainty or inexactness of meaning in language.

Examples

In fact, the recent warnings about the use of X-rays have introduced fears and ambiguities of action which now require more detailed understanding, and thus in this instance, science has momentarily aggravated our fears.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: equivocation - the use of ambiguous language to conceal the truth or to avoid committing oneself
  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand

Etymology

From Latin ambiguitas, from ambiguus (doubtful, ambiguous).

2. NOUN

/ ˌæm.bɪˈɡju.ə.ti / (AmE), / ˌæm.bɪˈɡjuː.ɪ.ti / (BrE)
a situation or expression in which the meaning is unclear or can be understood in more than one way

Examples

There are many types of ambiguity and many of them have been described by rhetoricians under such names as amphibology, parisology, and other ologies.
One species of ambiguity tries to baffle by interweaving repetition.
Ambiguity of a non repetitious kind describes the dilemma one girl found herself in.
Since brevity is the soul of ambiguity as well as wit, newspaper headlines continually provide us with amusing samples.
Apart from misplaced modifiers and headlinese, journalism contributes a wide variety of comic ambiguities in both editorial and advertising matter.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: clarity - the quality of being clear and easy to understand
  • Synonym: equivocation - the use of ambiguous language to conceal the truth or to avoid committing oneself

Etymology

From Latin ambiguus (uncertain, doubtful), from ambigere (to wander about, waver).
