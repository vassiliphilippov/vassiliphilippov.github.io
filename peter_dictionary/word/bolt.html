bolt

1. NOUN

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
A sudden flash of lightning accompanied by the sound of thunder.

Examples

A flash illumined the trees as a crooked bolt twigged in several directions.
If an unlucky accident should befall him, if he should be shot by a police officer, or if he should hang himself in his jail cell, or if he's struck by a bolt of lightning, then I'm going to blame some of the people in this room.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lightning - a sudden electrical discharge in the atmosphere
  • Synonym: thunderbolt - a flash of lightning and its accompanying thunder

2. VERB

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
to move or run away suddenly and quickly, often out of fear or surprise

Examples

One moment there was a man in the saddle; the next a headless horror on a horse that bolted through the redcoat ranks, and during the next second or two, we all of us fired into the suddenly disorganized column of soldiers.
The model quite plainly thought Michelangelo crazy; only the instructions from his rabbi kept him from bolting.

Synset Examples

She bolted from her seat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - to run somewhere quickly and suddenly
  • Synonym: flee - to run away from a place or danger
  • Antonym: linger - to stay in a place longer than necessary

Etymology

From Old English *bolt* (arrow, short shaft), related to the idea of sudden movement.

3. NOUN

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
A sudden swift movement, especially when running or escaping quickly.

Examples

He had gone into the Japanese navy, had been trained as an officer, had participated in one or two battles - he never went into detail regarding his military experience - and at the age of twenty-five, quite as a bolt out of the blue, he had walked into the mission as if he belonged here and had become a Christian.

Synset Examples

he made a dash for the door

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dash - a sudden and quick movement, especially towards something
  • Synonym: sprint - a short, fast run

Etymology

Old English *bolt* (projectile, arrow), of Germanic origin.

4. NOUN

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
the sliding metal bar in a breech-loading firearm that ejects an empty cartridge and loads a new one, closing the breech

Examples

The American firearms and ammunition manufacturers through diligent research and technical development have replaced the muzzle loader and slow firing single shot arms with modern fast firing autoloaders, extremely accurate bolt, lever, and slide action firearms.

5. NOUN

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
a metal bar that slides into a socket to lock a door or window, operated by a key or by hand

Examples

He reached out to pull the door shut and fasten it with a sliding bolt.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: latch - a fastening device for doors or gates, usually less secure than a bolt

Etymology

Old English *bolt* (projectile, bar, arrow), related to Old Norse *boltr* (round object).

6. VERB

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
To swallow food or drink quickly, often without chewing or savoring it.

Examples

Although they are forbidden to sit with the customers, the dancers are sometimes proffered drinks, and most of them can bolt one down in mid-shimmy.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: guzzle - eat or drink something quickly or greedily
  • Antonym: sip - drink something slowly in small amounts

Etymology

From Old English 'bolt' (arrow, missile), related to the rapid movement implied in both senses.

7. VERB

/ boʊlt / (AmE), / bəʊlt / (BrE)
to fasten or secure (something, especially a door or window) with a bolt

Examples

The only way to determine the final combustion chamber volume when such pistons are used is by measuring it with liquid while the cylinder head is bolted to the cylinder block and the piston is in top dead center position.
The drill press should be leveled and, depending on whether it is a bench or floor model, bolted securely to a sturdy bench or stand or screwed to the floor with lag or expansion screws.

Synset Examples

bolt the door

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unbolt - to open by removing or withdrawing the bolt

Etymology

From Old English *bolt* (projectile, arrow, bolt for fastening), of Germanic origin.
