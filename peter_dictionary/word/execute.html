execute

1. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkjuːt / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪkjuːt / (BrE)
to kill (someone) lawfully as a punishment for a crime, usually as ordered by a court of law

Examples

Two Americans and seven Cubans were executed by firing squads today as Castro military tribunals began decreeing the death penalty for captured invasion forces and suspected collaborators.
In February the murder of Patrice Lumumba, who had been kidnaped into Katanga and executed on order of Tshombe, again stirred the U. N. to action.

Synset Examples

In some states, criminals are executed

Related Terms

  • Antonym: pardon - to forgive or excuse someone from punishment
  • Antonym: reprieve - to postpone or cancel the punishment of execution

Etymology

From Latin *exsequi* ('carry out, accomplish, follow to the end'), from *ex-* ('out') + *sequi* ('to follow').

2. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkjuːt / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪˌkjuːt / (BrE)
to carry out or put into effect a plan, order, or course of action

Examples

The work is executed in England (by hand) and can be worked in any desired design and color.

Synset Examples

carry out a task
execute the decision of the people
He actioned the operation

Related Terms

  • Synonym: implement - to put a plan, system, or law into effect
  • Synonym: carry out - to perform or complete a task or plan

Etymology

From Latin 'exsequi' meaning 'to follow out, accomplish'.

3. VERB

/ ˈɛksɪˌkjut / (AmE), / ˈɛksɪkjuːt / (BrE)
to deliberately kill someone, especially as part of a planned act or organized crime

Examples

He showed us how he dealt with his enemies: he executed them before TV cameras.

Synset Examples

The Mafioso who collaborated with the police was executed

Related Terms

  • Synonym: assassinate - to murder someone important or prominent, often for political reasons
  • Synonym: murder - to unlawfully kill someone with intent

Etymology

From Latin exsequi (carry out, accomplish), via Old French executer.
