modest

1. ADJ

/ ˈmɑːdɪst / (AmE), / ˈmɒdɪst / (BrE)
Not talking about or making obvious your own abilities and achievements; having a humble attitude, or marked by simplicity and lack of luxury.

Examples

THE MODEST AND HAPPY Spahn waved off his new laurels as one of those good days.
If Nikita buys a small plot in some modest rural cemetery, everyone will understand.
He's too modest.

Synset Examples

a modest apartment
too modest to wear his medals

Related Terms

  • Synonym: humble - having or showing a modest or low estimate of one's importance
  • Antonym: arrogant - having or revealing an exaggerated sense of one's own importance or abilities

Etymology

From Latin modestus (keeping measure, moderate, modest).

2. ADJ

/ ˈmɑdɪst / (AmE), / ˈmɒdɪst / (BrE)
not large, elaborate, or showy; simple or unpretentious in style or appearance

Examples

They want to own a junior-grade castle, or a manor house, or some modest little place where Shakespeare might once have staged a pageant for Great Elizabeth and all her bearded courtiers.

Synset Examples

comfortable but modest cottages
a simple rectangular brick building
a simple man with simple tastes

Related Terms

  • Synonym: simple - plain; not elaborate or ornate
  • Antonym: grand - impressive and imposing in appearance or style

Etymology

From Latin 'modestus', meaning 'keeping due measure, moderate'.

3. ADJ

/ ˈmɑdɪst / (AmE), / ˈmɒdɪst / (BrE)
not large, extreme, or excessive in size or amount; moderate or limited, yet sufficient for its purpose

Examples

One of the reasons for the high percentage of Jewish teen-agers in college is that a great many urban Jews are enabled to attend local colleges at modest cost.
From actions aboard, it is easy to guess that Spencer's boast of twenty staunch followers was a modest estimate ".

Synset Examples

a modest salary
modest inflation
helped in my own small way

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: small - of size that is less than normal or usual
  • Degree Higher: large - of considerable or relatively great size or extent

Etymology

From Latin modestus (keeping measure), from modus (measure, manner).
