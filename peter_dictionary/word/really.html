really

1. ADV

/ ˈriːli / (AmE), / ˈrɪəli / (BrE)
In truth; truly; in accordance with fact or reality.

Examples

"Then we 'd really have someplace to go ".
He really crucified him.
In the fifth, Wally Post slashed a 2 - run homer off Bud Daley, but by that time the score was 11 - 5 and it really did n't matter.
It's a wonder, really, to how much mendacious trouble Larkin puts himself to sell the Jerebohms that preposterous manse.
It is really as though the Russians have seen in this character the oftentimes underlying vitality and courage of supposed buffoons.

Synset Examples

she was now truly American
a genuinely open society
they don't really listen to us

Related Terms

  • Synonym: truly - genuinely or in accordance with fact
  • Synonym: actually - used to emphasize the truth of a statement
  • Antonym: falsely - not in accordance with the truth or facts

Etymology

From real + -ly, formed in Middle English.

2. ADV

/ ˈrɪli / (AmE), / ˈriːli / (BrE)
in actual fact; truly; used to emphasize that something is true or exact, often in contrast to what is only apparent, assumed, or believed.

Examples

He does n't really need the immense sum of money (probably converted from American gold on the London Exchange) he makes them pay.
Bob Carroll may not bear quite as close a physical resemblance to LaGuardia as Tom Bosley does, but I was amazed at the way he became more and more Fiorello as the evening progressed, until one had to catch one's self up and remember that this was n't really LaGuardia come back among us again.
For we have said, in effect, that of the two alternatives to his position variously represented by the other participants in the demythologizing discussion, only one is really an alternative.
There were many letters of strong protest against the portrait of the Anglican clergyman, who was indeed portrayed as a man not particularly concerned with religious matters and without really very much to do as clergyman.
Henri has always had shapely legs from swimming and water skiing and really does n't have to work them very much.

Synset Examples

to be nominally but not actually independent
no one actually saw the shark
large meteorites actually come from the asteroid belt

Related Terms

  • Synonym: actually - in fact, as the truth or facts of a situation
  • Synonym: truly - in a truthful or genuine way; really
  • Antonym: apparently - as far as one knows or can see; seemingly

Etymology

From real + -ly, first used in the early 17th century to mean 'in reality'.

3. ADV

/ ˈɹiːli / (AmE), / ˈɹiːəli / (BrE)
Used to emphasize an adjective or adverb; to a great degree or extent.

Examples

How would you like it if I taught you how to catch the really big fish?

Synset Examples

she was very gifted
he played very well
a really enjoyable evening
I'm real sorry about it
a rattling good yarn

Related Terms

  • Synonym: very - used for emphasis, to a high degree
  • Degree Higher: extremely - to a very high degree
  • Degree Lower: quite - to some extent (less strong than 'really')

4. ADV

/ ˈrɪli / (AmE), / ˈriːəli / (BrE)
Used to emphasize the truth of a statement or the seriousness of what is being said; in fact or actually.

Examples

What really happened with Pentangeli, Michael?

Synset Examples

in truth, moral decay hastened the decline of the Roman Empire
really, you shouldn't have done it
a truly awful book

Related Terms

  • Synonym: truly - in a truthful way; used for emphasis
  • Synonym: actually - as the truth or facts of a situation; used for emphasis

Etymology

From real + -ly, formed in English.
