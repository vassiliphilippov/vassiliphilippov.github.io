radiate

1. VERB

/ ˈreɪdieɪt / (AmE), / ˈreɪdɪeɪt / (BrE)
to emit energy, especially light, heat, or radiation, in the form of rays or waves from a central source.

Examples

The use of this concept does not specify the origin of the radiation, and only if the planet really radiates as a black body, will the apparent black-body temperature correspond to the physical temperature of the emitting material.
It is sufficiently small compared with the surface temperature of the anode holder, to make the energy flux radiated from the environment toward the anode holder negligible within the accuracy of the present measurements.

Synset Examples

The sun radiates heat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emit - to send out (energy, light, heat, sound, etc.)
  • Antonym: absorb - to take in or soak up (energy, light, or liquid)

Etymology

From Latin radiatus, past participle of radiare 'to beam, shine', from radius 'ray, beam'.

2. VERB

/ ˈreɪdiˌeɪt / (AmE), / ˈreɪdɪeɪt / (BrE)
(of a person) to clearly express a particular quality or emotion so that it is easily noticed by others.

Examples

She radiated warmth and good fellowship.

Synset Examples

She radiates happiness

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emit - to send out (something such as light, heat, sound, etc.)
  • Synonym: exude - to display an emotion or quality strongly and openly

Etymology

From Latin radiatus, past participle of radiare 'to shine, beam', from radius 'ray, beam'.
