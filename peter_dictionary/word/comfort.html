comfort

1. NOUN

/ ˈkʌmfɚt / (AmE), / ˈkʌmfət / (BrE)
A feeling of ease and relief from worry, distress, or disappointment.

Examples

For added comfort some of the Italian designed sandals have foam padded cushioning.
Designed for summer comfort are the shoes illustrated.
The comfort was short-lived, yet she found herself returning to the assurance whenever her imagination forced images on her too awful to contemplate without the prop of illusion.
A blower may be installed to increase comfort.
We speculate that compulsives in the unstructured schools are under greater strain because of the lack of systemization in their school setting, but that their need to organize (for comfort) is so intense that they struggle to induce the phonic rules and achieve in spite of the lack of direction from the environment.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ease - absence of difficulty or effort; freedom from discomfort or worry
  • Antonym: distress - extreme anxiety or sorrow; opposite of comfort
  • Synonym: solace - comfort or consolation in a time of distress

Etymology

From Old French confort (strengthening), from Latin confortare (to strengthen greatly).

2. VERB

/ ˈkʌmfərt / (AmE), / ˈkʌmfət / (BrE)
to offer moral or emotional support to someone in distress or sorrow

Examples

Continuing glibly in this vein, he paused to comfort her:
The only reason for contacting Lissa was to comfort or to be comforted.
The only reason for contacting Lissa was to comfort or to be comforted.
Gavin's stallion was in the barn and he tightened the cinches over the saddle blanket, working by touch in the darkness, comforting the animal with easy words.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: console - to comfort someone at a time of grief or disappointment
  • Antonym: distress - to cause someone anxiety, sorrow, or pain

Etymology

From Old French *conforter* (to comfort, to strengthen), from Latin *confortare* (to strengthen greatly).

3. NOUN

/ ˈkʌmfərt / (AmE), / ˈkʌmfət / (BrE)
The act of offering consolation or relief to someone experiencing distress or trouble.

Examples

Poor where they had once been rich, humbled where they had been arrogant, having no longer any hope of sharing in the leadership of the nation, the rebels who would not surrender in spirit drew comfort from the sympathy they felt extended to them by the mother country.
It gives him aid, comfort, even solace, in meeting mundane life situations where his own unassisted practical knowledge and skill are felt by him to be inadequate.
She wanted to be with him, to give him the comfort and companionship she knew he needed.
Then he took off his wet boots and dropped down into the water to talk with the beasts, needing their comfort more than they needed his.

Synset Examples

his presence was a consolation to her

Related Terms

  • Synonym: consolation - comfort received by a person after loss or disappointment
  • Synonym: support - help or encouragement given to someone in need
  • Antonym: distress - extreme anxiety, sorrow, or pain

Etymology

From Old French 'conforter' (to comfort, strengthen), from Late Latin 'confortare' (to strengthen).

4. NOUN

/ ˈkʌmfərt / (AmE), / ˈkʌmfət / (BrE)
A state of physical ease and freedom from pain or constraint; a feeling of relaxation and well-being.

Examples

Everything was orderly and it seemed to be arranged for the workman's comfort, convenience and efficiency.

Synset Examples

he is a man who enjoys his comfort
she longed for the comfortableness of her armchair

Related Terms

  • Antonym: discomfort - lack of physical ease or comfort

Etymology

From Old French 'confort' (strength, support), from Late Latin 'confortare' (to strengthen).

5. VERB

/ ˈkʌmfərt / (AmE), / ˈkʌmfət / (BrE)
To make pain or discomfort less severe; to alleviate.

Examples

Parents have to find other ways of comforting him.

Synset Examples

ease the pain in your legs

Related Terms

  • Synonym: alleviate - to make pain or a problem less severe
  • Synonym: relieve - to reduce pain or distress
  • Antonym: exacerbate - to make a problem or pain worse

Etymology

From Old French conforter (to comfort, strengthen), from Latin confortare (to strengthen greatly).
