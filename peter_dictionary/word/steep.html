steep

1. ADJ

/ stip / (AmE), / stiːp / (BrE)
(of a slope, surface, or angle) rising or falling sharply; having a high degree of inclination.

Examples

Well, the grass was there, though in some places the ground was too steep for a cow to get to it.
There was brush, and stands of pine that no grass could grow under, and places so steep that cattle would n't stop to graze.
The first part of the road was steep, but it leveled off after the second bend and curled gradually into the valley.
The giant waves are more dangerous on flat shores than on steep ones.
On some of the islands' beaches the waves came in gently; they were steepest on the shores facing the direction of the seaquake from which the waves had come.

Synset Examples

the steep attic stairs
steep cliffs

Related Terms

  • Antonym: gentle - (of a slope) not steep or sharp; gradual

Etymology

From Old English *stēap* (high, lofty, deep), related to Dutch *stijf* (stiff) and German *stief* (stiff, rigid).

2. VERB

/ stiːp / (AmE), / stiːp / (BrE)
(usually be steeped in) To immerse or saturate thoroughly, especially with knowledge, culture, or a particular quality.

Examples

That is not to deny that he has been aware of traditions, of course, that he is steeped in them, in fact, or that he has dealt with them, in his books.
He is too deeply steeped in William Faulkner and Robert Penn Warren.

Synset Examples

He immersed himself into his studies

Related Terms

  • Synonym: immerse - to involve oneself deeply in an activity or interest
  • Synonym: engross - to absorb all the attention or interest of someone

Etymology

From Old English *stēpan* (to submerge), related to steep (to soak in liquid).
