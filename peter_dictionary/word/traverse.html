traverse

1. VERB

/ trəˈvɝs / (AmE), / trəˈvɜːs / (BrE)
to travel across or through an area, land, or obstacle; to pass over or through.

Examples

The travel time of sound in tissue is about 1500 meters per second thus it takes about 16 | msec. to traverse 25 mm. of tissue.
Then we drove on, until there was no more road and we traversed dry rice fields, bouncing across their squat earth walls.
He has traversed provinces and kingdoms, but he has not consolidated them behind him, nor does he control them.
A third, one of at least equal and perhaps even greater importance, is now being traversed: American immersion and involvement in world affairs.

Synset Examples

The caravan covered almost 100 miles each day

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cross - to go or extend across; pass from one side to the other

Etymology

From Old French 'traverser', from Late Latin 'traversare' (to cross), from Latin 'transversus' (turned across).

2. VERB

/ trəˈvɜrs / (AmE), / trəˈvɜːs / (BrE)
(Law) To formally deny an allegation of fact made by the opposing party in a legal action.

Examples

Hence the petitioner had full opportunity to traverse the only conclusions of the Department on file with the Board.

Etymology

From Old French 'traverser', from Late Latin 'traversare', meaning 'to cross'. Legal use refers to 'turning aside' or 'opposing'.

3. VERB

/ trəˈvɜrs / (AmE), / trəˈvɜːs / (BrE)
to extend or pass across, over, or through an area or period of time

Examples

These are traversed by another line of vaults, and thus rooms, arched on all four sides, are formed.

Synset Examples

Rivers traverse the valley floor
The parking lot spans 3 acres
The novel spans three centuries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: span - to extend across a period or area
  • Synonym: cross - to go or extend across

Etymology

From Old French *traverser* (to cross), from Latin *transversare* (to cross, thwart).
