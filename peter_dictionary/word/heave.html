heave

1. VERB

/ hiv / (AmE), / hiːv / (BrE)
(of a surface or mass) to rise and move in great waves or billows.

Examples

That keeps in the cold, retains moisture and prevents the heaving of alternate freezing and thawing.

Synset Examples

The army surged forward

Related Terms

  • Synonym: billow - to surge or swell like a wave
  • Synonym: surge - to move suddenly and powerfully forward or upward

Etymology

Old English *hebban* (to lift, raise), related to German *heben*.

2. VERB

/ hiv / (AmE), / hiːv / (BrE)
to throw or lift something with great effort or force

Examples

Somebody heaved a stone.
The first thing every morning when she washed her face she could see the scar on her chin where he 'd cut her with a boot cleat, and maybe she saw him heaving it again, the dirty sock popping out as it flew.
He got a knee up into Roberts' belly, used both hands and heaved him clear, then scrambled to his feet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toss - to throw lightly or carelessly
  • Synonym: hurl - to throw with great force
  • Synonym: fling - to throw suddenly or forcefully

Etymology

From Old English hebban (to raise, lift), related to Old High German heffan, Gothic hafjan.

3. VERB

/ hiv / (AmE), / hiːv / (BrE)
to produce a sound, such as a sigh or groan, with noticeable effort

Examples

Digby heaved a deep ethereal sigh.
Whenever New England liberalism is reminded of the dramatic confrontation of Parker and the fraternity on January 23, 1843 - while it may defend the privilege of Chandler Robbins to demand that Parker leave the Association, while it may plead that Dr. N. L. Frothingham had every warrant for stating, "The difference between Trinitarians and Unitarians is a difference in Christianity; the difference between Mr. Parker and the Association is a difference between no Christianity and Christianity "- despite these supposed conclusive assurances, the modern liberal heaves repeatedly a sigh of relief, of positive thanksgiving, that the Association never quite brought itself officially to expel Parker.

Synset Examples

She heaved a deep sigh when she saw the list of things to do

Related Terms

  • Synonym: sigh - to let out a long, deep audible breath expressing sadness, relief, or disappointment

Etymology

From Old English *hebban* (to lift, raise), related to the idea of effortful movement or exertion.

4. VERB

/ hiv / (AmE), / hiːv / (BrE)
to lift or haul something with great effort or force

Examples

He heaved the dead man onto the buckboard, yelled and lashed at the team and got out of there fast.
He started to struggle up, heaving desperately.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hoist - to raise or lift, especially with mechanical help
  • Synonym: lift - to raise to a higher position or level
  • Antonym: lower - to move or let something go down

Etymology

From Old English hebban (to lift, raise), of Germanic origin.

5. VERB

/ hiv / (AmE), / hiːv / (BrE)
(of a ship) to move or come into view, especially gradually or with effort; to appear or become visible, often as a result of movement.

Examples

Then the skiff hove into sight, just a dot of light at first against the roiling blackness and crimson streaks of the Coal Sack.

Synset Examples

The vessel hove into sight

Etymology

Old English *hebban* (to lift, raise), related to Dutch *heffen*, German *heben*.
