eject

1. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to force someone or something to leave a place or position; to expel

Examples

The transpiring gas ejected from the anode formed a jet directed axially towards the cathode below.
It also urged that the U. N. eject, and prevent the return of, all Belgian and other foreign military and political advisers; ordered an investigation of Lumumba's death; urged the reconvention of the Congolese Parliament and the reorganization of the army.

Synset Examples

The unruly student was excluded from the game

Related Terms

  • Synonym: expel - to force someone to leave a place or organization
  • Synonym: exclude - to prevent someone from entering or participating
  • Antonym: admit - to allow someone to enter a place

Etymology

From Latin *ejectus*, past participle of *eicere* (to throw out).

2. VERB

/ ɪˈdʒɛkt / (AmE), / ɪˈdʒɛkt / (BrE)
to force or expel (a substance) out of something, especially as part of a process

Examples

The Russian experimenters claim that only a small fraction of the impulse from the sensors is caused by the incident momentum with the remainder being momentum of ejected material from the sensor.

Synset Examples

combustion products are exhausted in the engine
the plant releases a gas

Related Terms

  • Synonym: emit - to send out (especially gas, heat, light, etc.)
  • Synonym: discharge - to release or let out (a substance)
  • Antonym: absorb - to take in or soak up (a substance)

Etymology

From Latin ejectus, past participle of ejicere (to throw out).
