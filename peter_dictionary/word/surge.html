surge

1. NOUN

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
a sudden and powerful increase or upward movement

Examples

Khrushchev himself is reported to be concerned by the surge of animosity he has aroused, yet our own nuclear statesmen seem intent on following compulsively in his footsteps.

Synset Examples

stimulated a surge of speculation
an upsurge of emotion
an upsurge in violent crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: upsurge - a sudden increase; similar meaning
  • Synonym: spike - a sharp increase in level or amount
  • Antonym: decline - a decrease or reduction; opposite meaning

Etymology

From Latin 'surgere' meaning 'to rise'.

2. NOUN

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
A sudden and powerful forward or upward movement, often of liquid, people, or emotion.

Examples

He laughed, suddenly, feeling a surge of power telling him of his hold over them, seeing himself before them, receiving utmost respect and attention.
The true surge had to be inside the marble itself.
At the end of the performance, Dave and Max came out into the brilliantly lit foyer among a surge of gowned and tuxedoed first nighters.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: rush - a sudden rapid move or increase
  • Antonym: ebb - the movement of the tide out to sea; a decrease or lessening

Etymology

From Latin 'surgere' (to rise), combining 'sub-' (up) and 'regere' (to direct).

3. VERB

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
(of a crowd, liquid, or feeling) to move suddenly and powerfully forward or upward; to rise or increase strongly and rapidly.

Examples

The music surged up, but it failed to check it.

Synset Examples

surging waves

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ebb - move away from the land; recede (opposite of surge, in relation to tide or emotion)

Etymology

From Latin surgere (to rise, spring up), from sub- (up) + regere (to direct).

4. VERB

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
(of a crowd, group, emotion, or physical force) to move suddenly and powerfully forward or upward, like a wave.

Examples

Beneath his clutch, a flat strip of muscle surged on the bone.
Because of this diversionary attack the main group that had been pinned down on the hill was able to surge forward again.
They found a large welcoming group - El Paso policemen, Border Patrol, sheriff's deputies, and FBI men, who surged around the plane with rifles and submarine guns.

Synset Examples

The army surged forward

Related Terms

  • Synonym: billow - to rise or roll in waves or surges

Etymology

From Latin surgere (to rise), from sub- (up) + regere (to direct, to keep straight).

5. VERB

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
to move or rise suddenly and powerfully, especially upward or forward, as under the influence of a natural force like a wave

Examples

They surged around him, fingers pointing, eyes prying.

Synset Examples

the boats surged

Related Terms

  • Antonym: ebb - (of water) to flow back or recede, opposite of surge

Etymology

From Latin 'surgere' meaning 'to rise'.

6. VERB

/ sɝdʒ / (AmE), / sɜːdʒ / (BrE)
to rise, move, or increase suddenly and powerfully

Examples

Fierce anger surged through Russ.

Synset Examples

the dollar soared against the yen

Related Terms

  • Synonym: soar - to rise or increase rapidly to a high level

Etymology

From Latin *surgere* (to rise).
