drill

1. VERB

/ drɪl / (AmE), / drɪl / (BrE)
to teach or learn something by repeating it many times; to train by means of repeated exercises or practice.

Examples

- Held out of Texas Tech's sweat suits drill Monday at Lubbock was tackle Richard Stafford, who is undergoing treatment for a leg injury suffered in the Raiders' 38 - 7 loss to Texas A+M.

Synset Examples

We drilled French verbs every day
Pianists practice scales

Related Terms

  • Synonym: practice - to perform an activity or exercise repeatedly in order to improve skill

Etymology

From Middle Dutch *drillen* (to turn, whirl, drill); later extended to repetitive practice.

2. VERB

/ drɪl / (AmE), / drɪl / (BrE)
To train soldiers in military exercises or the use of weapons through repeated practice.

Examples

The road seemed animated with a few more wagons than usual; a carriage raising up the choking June dust, and beyond, in a meadow, a local militia company drilling with muskets, Kentuck' rifles, every kind of horse pistol, old sword, or cutlass.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: train - to teach or instruct a person or animal in a particular skill
  • Synonym: practice - to perform an activity repeatedly to improve competence

Etymology

From Dutch *drillen* (to turn, to drill), originally relating to turning or spinning.

3. VERB

/ drɪl / (AmE), / drɪl / (BrE)
To make a hole in something using a pointed tool, especially a power or hand tool.

If you are considering a part-time farm where the water must be provided by a well, find out if there is a good well on the farm or the probable cost of having one drilled.

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bore - make a hole in something, especially with a tool

Etymology

Perhaps from Middle Dutch *drillen* (to bore, turn, spin).
