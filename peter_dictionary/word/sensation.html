sensation

1. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a person who is remarkably talented or skilled in a particular field and attracts widespread attention

Examples

The rangy, Albany (Cal.) native, a surprise slugging sensation for the Flock last year as well as a defensive whiz, set "improved fielding "as his 1961 goal.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: star - a famous and exceptionally talented person in a particular field
  • Synonym: prodigy - a person, especially a young one, endowed with exceptional abilities
  • Antonym: mediocrity - an ordinary person without special talent

Etymology

From Latin 'sēnsātiō' (perception, feeling), from 'sentiō' (to feel).

Related Terms

Etymology

From Latin 'sensus' (feeling, perception) via French 'sensation'.

3. NOUN

/ sɛnˈseɪʃən / (AmE), / sɛnˈseɪʃən / (BrE)
a general feeling of excitement or heightened interest, often accompanied by emotional or physical stimulation

Examples

The kiss outraged our friends but it was done and meanwhile had released in me all the remote, exciting premonitions of lust, all the mysterious sensations that I had imagined a truly consummated kiss would convey to me.

Synset Examples

anticipation produced in me a sensation somewhere between hope and fear

Related Terms

  • Synonym: thrill - a sudden feeling of excitement and pleasure
  • Synonym: excitement - a feeling of lively and cheerful joy
  • Antonym: boredom - the state of being bored; lack of excitement

Etymology

From Latin 'sensus' (feeling, perception) via French 'sensation'.
