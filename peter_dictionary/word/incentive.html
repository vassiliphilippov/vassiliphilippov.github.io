incentive

1. NOUN

/ ɪnˈsɛntɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɛntɪv / (BrE)
something that encourages a person to do something or to work harder

Examples

Our central goal should be to provide the greatest positive incentive for these societies to tackle boldly the tasks which they face.
By holding out prospects for external capital assistance, the United States can provide strong incentives to prepare for the concerted economic drive necessary to achieve self-sustaining growth.
It must be conceded that his native land provided Prokofieff with many of the necessary conditions for great creative incentive:
Thus, there is an added incentive to stay on the job.
"The commercials have just been for money, there has n't been any real incentive for me to do them, but in Underwater Western Eye I 'd have a chance to act.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: disincentive - something that discourages action or effort
  • Synonym: motivation - the reason or reasons for acting or behaving in a particular way
  • Synonym: inducement - a thing that persuades or influences someone to do something

Etymology

From Latin incentivum (something that sets the tune, incitement), from incendere (to set on fire).

2. NOUN

/ ɪnˈsɛntɪv / (AmE), / ɪnˈsɛntɪv / (BrE)
an additional payment or other reward offered to employees to encourage increased productivity or performance

Examples

Much of the important individual and social action has been owing to religious incentives.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: bonus - an extra payment to reward achievement or increase performance
  • Antonym: penalty - a punishment to deter undesired behavior, opposite of reward

Etymology

From Latin incentivum (something that sets the tune, incites), from incinere 'to strike up'.
