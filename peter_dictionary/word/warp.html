warp

1. VERB

/ wɔrp / (AmE), / wɔːp / (BrE)
(of a material) to bend or twist out of shape, especially due to heat, damp, or pressure.

Examples

She turned and walked stiffly into the parlor to the dainty legged escritoire, warped and cracked now from fifty years in an atmosphere of sea spray.

Synset Examples

The highway buckled during the heat wave

Related Terms

  • Synonym: twist - to form into a bent, curling, or distorted shape
  • Antonym: straighten - to make or become straight

Etymology

From Old English 'weorpan' (to throw, cast), later relating to distortion in weaving.

2. VERB

/ wɔrp / (AmE), / wɔːp / (BrE)
to distort or falsify (facts, information, or accounts), especially so as to mislead

Examples

In all his miscalculations, Stravinsky made the fatal historical blunder of presuming that he could transform other composers' inspirations - representing many peoples, time periods and styles - into his own music by warping the harmony, melody, or form, to verify his own experiments.
As Kennan shows, the judgment of the Allied governments about what was happening in Russia was warped by the obsession of defeating Germany.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: falsify - to make something false or incorrect, especially by altering documents or facts
  • Synonym: distort - to change something so that it is no longer true or accurate
  • Antonym: straighten - to make or become straight or true, opposite of warp

Etymology

Old English *weorpan* (to throw, distort), related to Old Norse *verpa* (to throw).
