bomb

1. NOUN

/ bɑm / (AmE), / bɒm / (BrE)
an explosive weapon designed to cause destruction or injury by means of a sudden release of energy when triggered

Examples

The nuclear war is already being fought, except that the bombs are not being dropped on enemy targets - not yet.
Some time ago, however, Mr. Khrushchev decided that when bigger bombs were made, the Soviet Union would make them.
The bombs are as harmless as an automobile in a garage.
Each plane carried two five-hundred pound bombs.
The dark brown bombs hanging under each wing looked large and powerful.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: explosive - a substance or device that causes an explosion
  • Degree Lower: grenade - a small explosive device thrown by hand or launched
  • Degree Higher: nuclear bomb - an extremely destructive bomb using nuclear energy

Etymology

From French 'bombe', from Italian 'bomba', from Latin 'bombus' (a booming, buzzing noise), ultimately from Greek 'bombos'.

2. NOUN

/ bɑm / (AmE), / bɒm / (BrE)
a strong, sealed metal vessel used in bomb calorimeters for measuring the heat of combustion of a substance

Examples

500 ml of 1 M aqueous f with 1 g f added are heated in a **bomb** at 170 ` C for 48 hours.

3. VERB

/ bɑm / (AmE), / bɒm / (BrE)
to attack (a place or target) by dropping bombs from aircraft or using other explosive devices.

Examples

He was marching up and down the locker room of the Grassy Brae Golf Club shouting, "Bomb Cuba!
Bomb Berlin!
"Bomb them "! he shouted.

Synset Examples

The Americans bombed Dresden

Related Terms

  • Synonym: shell - to attack with shells (explosives) fired from artillery
  • Synonym: bombard - to attack with bombs, shells, or missiles

Etymology

From French 'bombe', from Italian 'bomba', from Latin 'bombus' (a booming or buzzing sound), from Greek 'bombos'.
