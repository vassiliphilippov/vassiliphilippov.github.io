cheat

1. VERB

/ tʃit / (AmE), / tʃiːt / (BrE)
to act dishonestly or unfairly in order to gain an advantage, especially by deceiving others or breaking rules

Examples

We use terms from our personal experience with individuals such as "trust ", "cheat ", and "get tough ".

Synset Examples

Who's chiseling on the side?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: deceive - to make someone believe something that is not true
  • Antonym: honest - free of deceit; truthful and sincere

Etymology

From Old French 'escheat' (to escheat, fall to the lot of), from Latin 'excadere' (to fall out).

2. VERB

/ tʃit / (AmE), / tʃiːt / (BrE)
to defeat or gain an advantage over someone by trickery, deception, or dishonest means

Examples

It was n't like getting cheated at the fair.
Mr. Cunningham tries not to cheat the chance method; he adheres to its dictates as faithfully as he can.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defraud - to illegally obtain money from someone by deception
  • Synonym: swindle - to cheat someone out of money or assets
  • Antonym: honest - free of deceit; truthful and sincere

Etymology

Possibly from Old French 'eschier' (to escape), or from Latin 'escapare' (to escape).

3. VERB

/ tʃit / (AmE), / tʃiːt / (BrE)
To deprive someone of something by deceit or fraud.

Examples

When a husband is sexually selfish and heedless of his wife's desires, she is cheated of the fulfillment and pleasure nature intended for her.
They greeted the news angrily, as though they had been cheated of purpose.
Frankie, they feel cheated.

Synset Examples

The con-man beat me out of $50
This salesman ripped us off!
we were cheated by their clever-sounding scheme
They chiseled me out of my money

Related Terms

  • Synonym: defraud - to illegally obtain money from someone by deception
  • Synonym: swindle - to cheat someone out of money or assets
  • Antonym: honest - free of deceit and untruthfulness; sincere

Etymology

Probably from Old French *escheter* (to fall to by chance), from Late Latin *excadere* (to fall out).
