slice

1. NOUN

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
(in golf) a shot in which the ball curves to the right for a right-handed player, usually unintentionally.

Examples

The ball went off in a majestic arc, an out-of-bounds slice.

Synset Examples

he took lessons to cure his slicing

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hook - a golf shot that curves to the left for a right-handed golfer

2. VERB

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
to hit a ball so that it spins backwards or curves to one side, especially in sports like golf or tennis

Examples

He tried again and once more sliced out of bounds.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hook - to hit a ball so that it curves in the opposite direction to a slice

3. NOUN

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
a portion or share of something, especially of money, business, or life

Examples

The first superhighways - New York's Henry Hudson and Chicago's Lake Shore, San Francisco's Bay Bridge and its approaches, a good slice of the Pennsylvania Turnpike - were built as part of the federal works program which was going to cure the depression.
Leaving the theatre after the performance, I had a flash of intuition that life, after all (as Rilke said), is just a search for the nonexistent cup of hot coffee, and that this unpretentious, moving, clever, bitter slice of life was the greatest thing to happen to the American theatre since Brooks Atkinson retired.
When, as a diplomatic gesture of amity and in payment for the loan of gunmen in the April election, Torrio had given O' Banion a slice of Cicero, the profits from that district had been $ 20000 a month.

Synset Examples

a slice of the company's revenue

Related Terms

  • Synonym: portion - a part or share of a whole
  • Synonym: piece - a part of something larger

Etymology

From Old French *esclice* (a splinter, slice), from *esclicer* (to split), based on Latin *exclīdere* (to cut out).

4. VERB

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
to cut through something smoothly and cleanly, often using a sharp blade

Examples

"They 'll get them by God and let them bring them down here to me, just let them, God I 'll slice their balls right off ".
Then he noticed the clouds racing upon them - heavy, ominous, leaden clouds that formed even as they sliced over the crests of the surrounding hills.

Synset Examples

slit her throat

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cut - to penetrate or divide with a sharp-edged instrument
  • Synonym: slit - to make a long, narrow cut or opening

Etymology

From Old French *esclicer* (to split, slice), from Late Latin *exclidere* (to cut out).

5. NOUN

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
A thin, flat piece that has been cut from a larger portion of food or another material.

Examples

"Slice o' mutton, bhoy "? she had queried in her soft guttural.
"Slice o' mutton "?

Synset Examples

a piece of pie
a slice of bread

Related Terms

  • Synonym: piece - a portion of an object or material, produced by cutting, tearing, or breaking the whole

Etymology

From Old French *esclice* (splinter, chip), from *esclicier* (to split), ultimately from Frankish or a Germanic root meaning 'to split'; related to English 'slit'.

6. VERB

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
(in sports, especially golf or tennis) to hit a ball so that it spins and curves to one side rather than going straight.

Examples

The latter involved hitting a full four-wood out to the first fairway and toward the clubhouse, hoping to slice it back to the deeply bunkered 9 th green.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: hook - hit a ball so that it curves in the opposite direction (in golf or tennis)

7. VERB

/ slaɪs / (AmE), / slaɪs / (BrE)
to cut something into thin, flat pieces

Examples

Peel, core, and slice across enough apples to make a dome in the pie tin, and set aside.

Synset Examples

Slice the salami, please

Related Terms

  • Synonym: chop - to cut something into pieces with repeated sharp blows
  • Synonym: carve - to cut meat into slices

Etymology

From Old French *esclicer* or *escliceir* (to split), from *es-* (out) + *clice* (splinter).
