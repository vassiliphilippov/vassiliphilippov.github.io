Stoicism

1. NOUN

/ ˈstoʊɪˌsɪzəm / (AmE), / ˈstəʊɪˌsɪzəm / (BrE)
An ancient Greek school of philosophy founded by Zeno of Citium, teaching that virtue, the highest good, is based on knowledge, and that the wise live in harmony with the divine Reason governing nature, indifferent to pleasure, pain, and external circumstances.

Examples

Accordingly we may speak of the Platonism peculiar to Shelley's poems or the type of Stoicism present in Henley's "Invictus ", and we may find that describing such Platonism or such Stoicism and contrasting each with other expressions of the same attitude or mode of thought is a difficult and challenging enterprise.

Related Terms

Etymology

From Greek *Stoïkos* (of the Stoa, referring to the Stoa Poikile where Zeno taught) + -ism.
