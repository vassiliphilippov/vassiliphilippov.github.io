crossroads

1. NOUN

/ ˈkrɔsˌroʊdz / (AmE), / ˈkrɒsˌrəʊdz / (BrE)
A point at which a critical decision must be made, often one that affects future outcomes.

Examples

The foregoing remarks imply that the hypothalamic balance plays a crucial role at the crossroads between physiological and pathological forms of emotion.

Synset Examples

Freud's work stands at the crossroads between psychology and neurology

Related Terms

  • Synonym: turning point - a time at which a decisive change in a situation occurs
  • Synonym: crisis - a time of intense difficulty or important decision

Etymology

From cross + roads, originally referring to a place where two or more roads intersect.

2. NOUN

/ ˈkrɔsˌroʊdz / (AmE), / ˈkrɒsˌrəʊdz / (BrE)
A point at which a crucial decision must be made, typically one that will have far-reaching consequences.

Examples

Burial had taken place at night in the ground at the public crossroads under the gibbet, so that his enemies could not find his body and have it dug up and burned.

Synset Examples

at that juncture he had no idea what to do
he must be made to realize that the company stands at a critical point

Related Terms

  • Synonym: turning point - a time at which a decisive change takes place
  • Synonym: crisis - a time of intense difficulty or danger, requiring a decision

Etymology

From 'crossroad', itself from 'cross' (intersect) + 'road', originally denoting a place where two roads cross, later used figuratively.

3. NOUN

/ ˈkrɔsˌroʊdz / (AmE), / ˈkrɒsˌrəʊdz / (BrE)
a small rural community located at the intersection of two or more roads, often smaller than a village

Examples

They held the funeral the next morning from the crossroads church and buried the little box in the quiet family plot.
He claimed to be visiting the waterfront saloon at the crossroads to play cards and drink with his cronies, but Kate had not smelled brandy on him since Mrs. Lattimer's funeral.
He patted the eye Joel had had blackened in a fight over being Rebel at the crossroads some days back.
No one went much to the crossroads now except Uncle Randolph.

Etymology

From cross + roads, originally referring to a place where roads cross and by extension to settlements at such intersections.
