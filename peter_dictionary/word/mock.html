mock

1. ADJ

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
Not real; made to look or seem like something else, especially as an imitation or for practice.

Examples

"Husky young man ", he said with mock distaste.
He regaled Claire with an account of the mock mass performed by the cassocked bloods, which he had had at firsthand from old Bud Dodington, one of the leaders of the so-called "Order ".

Synset Examples

boys in mock battle

Related Terms

  • Antonym: real - actually existing or occurring, not imitation
  • Antonym: genuine - truly what something is said to be; authentic

Etymology

From Middle French 'moquer' (to mock), ultimately of uncertain origin.

2. VERB

/ mɑk / (AmE), / mɒk / (BrE)
to treat someone or something with ridicule, contempt, or derision; to make fun of

Examples

Believing that God is the Author of this law and of all laws of nature, Roman Catholics believe that they are obliged to obey those laws, not frustrate or mock them.
Can God be mocked, ever, in the long run?
That would mock me too much!
It seemed to Barton that the green eyes mocked him, the thin-lipped smile held insolence, but he had no time to waste now.

Synset Examples

The new constitution mocks all democratic principles

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ridicule - to make fun of or deride someone
  • Antonym: praise - to express approval or admiration

Etymology

From Middle French moquer (to mock), of uncertain origin.
