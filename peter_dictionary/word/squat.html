squat

1. NOUN

/ skwɑt / (AmE), / skwɒt / (BrE)
A strength exercise in which a person repeatedly lowers and raises their body by bending and straightening the knees while keeping the back straight, typically to build leg muscles.

Examples

It improves over-all balance and control for the bodybuilder, and helps to make Squats more easily and more correctly performed.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: knee bend - exercise involving repeated bending and straightening of the knees

2. VERB

/ skwɑt / (AmE), / skwɒt / (BrE)
To sit with your knees bent and your heels close to or touching your buttocks or the back of your thighs, balancing on your feet rather than sitting fully.

Examples

He saw Mose squatting by the hearth, breaking up hardtack into a pan.
Squatting on our haunches beside the flat stone we broke them on, we were safe behind the high closed gates at the end of the drive: safe from interruption and the observation and possible amusement of the passers-by.
So you sit in the car and listen to the air run out and listen to the rain and see the mud in front of the headlights, waiting for you, for your new spectator pumps, waiting for you to squat by yourself out there in your tight skirt, crying and afraid and trying to get that damned son-of-a-bitch tire off, because that is being an old maid too, if you happen to drive a car, it is changing the tire yourself in the night, and in the mud and the rain, hating to get out in it but afraid to stay and afraid to try to walk out for help.
She remained squatting on her heels all the time we were there; like the man, she was entirely naked.
They squatted on their heels with their heads bent far forward, their eyes only a few inches from the ground.

Synset Examples

In some cultures, the women give birth while squatting
The children hunkered down to protect themselves from the sandstorm

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crouch - to lower the body close to the ground by bending the knees and leaning forward, often to avoid detection or as a protective movement

Etymology

Probably from Old French esquatir, related to Old Norse squata (to squat or crush).

3. ADJ

/ skwɑt / (AmE), / skwɒt / (BrE)
short and thick in form; having a broad, stocky shape, often with short legs or height

Examples

There was the squat loose jowled man, smiling lopsidedly up at him.
Cause of all this commotion: squat, pug-nosed, balding, hopelessly ugly Jean-Pierre Bravado, a Bogartian figure, who plays a sadistic, amoral, philosophic Tasti-Freeze salesman in old New-Waver Fredrico de Mille Rossilini's endlessly provocative film, A Sour Sponge.
Then we drove on, until there was no more road and we traversed dry rice fields, bouncing across their squat earth walls.

Synset Examples

some people seem born to be square and chunky
a dumpy little dumpling of a woman
dachshunds are long lowset dogs with drooping ears
a little church with a squat tower
a squatty red smokestack
a stumpy ungainly figure

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dumpy - short and thick or heavyset in appearance
  • Synonym: chunky - having a short, thick, and solid form
  • Antonym: slender - thin in an attractive or elegant way
  • Antonym: lanky - tall and thin

Etymology

Ultimately from Old French esquatir, related to Latin exquadrare (to make square).

4. VERB

/ skwɑt / (AmE), / skwɒt / (BrE)
(of a building or object) to sit low or close to the ground, often appearing broad or wide in relation to its height.

Examples

It squatted low and square upon the sidewalk with a heavy iron grating supporting a glass facade.

Synset Examples

The building squatted low

Related Terms

  • Synonym: crouch - to bend down low with the limbs close to the body, especially to avoid detection

Etymology

From Old French esquatir (to crouch, make squat), from Old Provençal esquatar, of Germanic origin.

5. ADJ

/ skwɑt / (AmE), / skwɒt / (BrE)
(of a building or object) short and thickset, with a low center of gravity or built close to the ground.

Examples

They followed him into the rain and across to the squat stone building fifty feet to the rear.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: tall - having considerable height, especially in relation to width or depth

Etymology

From Middle English 'squat', possibly of Scandinavian origin, related to Old Norse 'skvatta' (to squat).
