syntax

1. NOUN

/ ˈsɪn.tæks / (AmE), / ˈsɪn.tæks / (BrE)
A systematic and orderly arrangement or organization of elements.

Examples

Ripe Geometric potters continued to employ the old syntax of ornaments and shapes and made use of the well-defined though limited range of motifs which they had inherited.

Etymology

From Greek 'syntaxis', meaning 'arrangement', from 'syn-' (together) + 'tassein' (to arrange).

2. NOUN

/ ˈsɪn.tæks / (AmE), / ˈsɪn.taks / (BrE)
the arrangement of words and phrases to create well-formed sentences in a language

Examples

Its citizens spoke all of the world's surviving tongues, plus a new one called Lingo, a pidgin whose vocabulary was derived from the other six and whose syntax was so simple it could be contained on half a sheet of paper.
The description of the Siddo syntax was very restricted.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grammar - the rules governing the structure of language; broader than syntax
  • Antonym: semantics - the meaning of words and sentences; contrasted with syntax

Etymology

From Greek *syntaxis* (arrangement), from *syn-* ‘together’ + *taxis* ‘arrangement’.
