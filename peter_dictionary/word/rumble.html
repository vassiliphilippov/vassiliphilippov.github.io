rumble

1. VERB

/ ˈrʌmbəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌmbəl / (BrE)
(of a machine, thunder, or similar things) to make a continuous deep, resonant, or echoing sound.

Examples

For an instant John longed for the sound of the bells of Noyon-la-Sainte, the touch of his mother's hand, the lilt of Charles's voice in the square raftered rooms, his father's bass tones rumbling to the canons, and the sight of the beloved bishop.
Greg rumbled down the rough metal taxi strip, and one by one the seven members of his flight fell in behind him.
The plane rumbled and slowed.

Synset Examples

rumbling thunder

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: roar - to make a loud, deep noise
  • Degree Lower: murmur - to make a low, soft, indistinct sound

Etymology

Probably from Middle Dutch *rommelen*, meaning ‘to rumble or make noise’.

2. VERB

/ ˈrʌmbəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌmbl̩ / (BrE)
to make a continuous, low, deep, resonant sound; to emit or utter such a sound.

Examples

Where it is not actually erupting, it rumbles and smolders in sullen resentment like a volcano, ready to explode at any moment.
[THUNDER RUMBLING] Michael, why are the drapes open?

Synset Examples

he grumbled a rude response
Stones grumbled down the cliff

Related Terms

  • Synonym: grumble - to make a low, indistinct grumbling noise

Etymology

Imitative in origin, resembling the sound itself; related to Middle Dutch rommelen (to rumble).

3. NOUN

/ ˈrʌm.bəl / (AmE), / ˈrʌm.bəl / (BrE)
a loud, low, continuous sound, often one that is not immediately identifiable or is distant

Examples

Later, they 'd heard the rumble of thunder and then, just outside Rockfork, they ran into rain.

Synset Examples

they heard the rumbling of thunder

Related Terms

  • Synonym: roar - a loud, deep, continuous sound
  • Degree Lower: murmur - a soft, low, continuous sound; quieter than a rumble

Etymology

Imitative origin, echoing the sound itself. Early recorded in 14th century English.
