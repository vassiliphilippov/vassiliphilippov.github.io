dissolve

1. VERB

/ dɪˈzɑlv / (AmE), / dɪˈzɒlv / (BrE)
to cause (a solid, gas, etc.) to become incorporated into a liquid so as to form a solution

Examples

As a substitute for this, organic fertilizer dissolved in water to half the strength in the directions, may be used.
A sample of Fraction * * f from group O plasma was dissolved in starting buffer, dialyzed against this buffer and subjected to chromatography using the gradient shown in Fig. 2.
Now, driving the horse and sulky borrowed from Mynheer Schuyler, he felt as if every bone was topped by burning oil and that every muscle was ready to dissolve into jelly and leave his big body helpless and unable to move.

Synset Examples

The recipe says that we should dissolve a cup of sugar in two cups of water

Related Terms

  • Synonym: melt - to change from solid to liquid by heat

Etymology

From Latin 'dissolvere', from 'dis-' (apart) and 'solvere' (to loosen, to release).

2. NOUN

/ dɪˈzɑlv / (AmE), / dɪˈzɒlv / (BrE)
(in film and video) a gradual transition from one scene to another, in which the first scene fades out as the next fades in.

Examples

One of these is the "dissolve ", which makes possible a visually smooth transition from scene to scene.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fade - a gradual darkening or lightening of the image at the beginning or end of a scene

Etymology

From Latin dissolvere, meaning 'to loosen apart'.

3. VERB

/ dɪˈzɑlv / (AmE), / dɪˈzɒlv / (BrE)
(of an organization or assembly) to officially end or disband, so that its members no longer function as a unit.

Examples

The precipitate was washed twice with an 80 % saturated solution of * * f, dissolved in a small quantity of 0.1 M neutral phosphate buffer, dialyzed against cold distilled water till free from ammonium ions, and lyophilized using liquid nitrogen.

Synset Examples

The political wing of the party dissolved after much internal fighting

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disband - to break up an organization or group
  • Antonym: form - to bring together into a group, organization or unit

Etymology

From Latin 'dissolvere' (to loosen, break up), from 'dis-' (apart) + 'solvere' (to loosen).

4. VERB

/ dɪˈzɑlv / (AmE), / dɪˈzɒlv / (BrE)
(of a relationship, organization, or agreement) to end or be brought to an end; to cease to exist.

Examples

Mary was free to marry again, not knowing that the Sturch had secretly given her a divorce, thinking that death had dissolved her marriage.
The central concern of Erich Auerbach's impressive volume called Mimesis is to describe the shift from a classic theory of imitation (based upon a recognition of levels of truth) to a Christian theory of imitation in which the levels are dissolved.

Synset Examples

Their marriage dissolved
The tobacco monopoly broke up

Related Terms

  • Synonym: end - to come or bring to a final point; finish
  • Synonym: terminate - to bring to an end or halt
  • Antonym: begin - to start; to come into being

Etymology

From Latin *dissolvere* (separate, break apart), from dis- (apart) + solvere (to loosen).

5. VERB

/ dɪˈzɑlv / (AmE), / dɪˈzɒlv / (BrE)
(of something perceived or experienced) to gradually become weaker or less distinct until it disappears; to fade away or vanish.

Examples

The image of the world tends to reflect the hostility and indifference of man or else to dissolve into empty spaces and overwhelming mystery.
As the tooth moves, bone cells on the pressure side of it will dissolve, and new ones will form on the side from which the tooth has moved.
Then at last the darkness began to dissolve.

Synset Examples

The sound faded out

Related Terms

  • Synonym: fade - to gradually become weaker or disappear
  • Synonym: disappear - to cease to be visible or present

Etymology

From Latin dissolvere (to loosen, break up), from dis- (apart) + solvere (to loosen).

to cause something to gradually disappear or fade away, especially so that it is no longer visible or distinct

Related Terms

From Latin dissolvere (to loosen, break up), from dis- (apart) + solvere (to loosen).
