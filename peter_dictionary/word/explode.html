explode

1. VERB

/ ɪkˈsploʊd / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊd / (BrE)
to cause something, especially a bomb or device, to burst or break apart suddenly and violently, releasing energy, noise, or force

Examples

The biggest nuclear device the United States has exploded measured some 15 megatons, although our B-52s are said to be carrying two 20 - megaton bombs apiece.
He seems to have at least a few 30 - and 50 - megaton bombs on hand, since we cannot assume that he has exploded his entire stock.
He has gained only one thing - he has exploded a 50 - megaton bomb and he probably has rockets with sufficient thrust to lob it over the shorter intercontinental ranges.
They set bouncing betties to jump and explode at testicle level while we more mercifully had them go off at the head.
Though Bob Carroll seemed to have had his head practically blown off by the exploding grenade, he lived.

Synset Examples

We exploded the nuclear bomb

Related Terms

  • Synonym: detonate - to explode or cause to explode with sudden violence
  • Antonym: implode - to collapse or burst inward violently

Etymology

From Latin 'explodere' (drive out by clapping), later used in reference to going off with a bang.

2. VERB

/ ɪkˈsploʊd / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊd / (BrE)
to burst outward violently with noise, often as a result of internal pressure or a reaction

Examples

Miss Stuart is as intensely vibrant as one could wish, almost an icy shriek threatening to explode at any moment.
Where it is not actually erupting, it rumbles and smolders in sullen resentment like a volcano, ready to explode at any moment.
The element is inserted in the discharge circuit in place of the exploding wire, and the calorimetric heating of the element is measured with high accuracy.
I found a trooper once the Apache had spread-eagled on an ant hill, and another time we ran across some teamsters they 'd caught, tied upside down on their own wagon wheels over little fires until their brains was exploded right out o' their skulls.
Mike, off balance, managed to bat the muzzle away a moment before it exploded.

Synset Examples

The champagne bottle exploded

Related Terms

  • Synonym: burst - break open or apart suddenly, especially as a result of pressure

Etymology

From Latin 'explodere' (to drive out by clapping, to burst out), from 'ex-' (out) + 'plaudere' (to clap).

3. VERB

/ ɪkˈsploʊd / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊd / (BrE)
to suddenly and violently express strong emotion, especially anger

Examples

"Just let me meet up with that damned bushwhackin' coward face-to-face "! he exploded.

Synset Examples

The boss exploded when he heard of the resignation of the secretary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: blow up - to suddenly lose one's temper; to erupt in anger
  • Synonym: erupt - to suddenly emit strong feelings, especially anger

Etymology

From Latin 'explodere' (to drive out by clapping), originally in the sense of driving an actor off the stage with noise.

4. VERB

/ ɪkˈsploʊd / (AmE), / ɪkˈspləʊd / (BrE)
(of an emotion or force) to suddenly and violently emerge or be expressed, often with great intensity.

Examples

He will not curb his instinctual desires but release the energy within him that makes him feel truly and fully alive, even if it is only for this brief moment before the apocalypse of annihilation explodes on earth.

Synset Examples

His anger exploded

Related Terms

  • Synonym: erupt - to burst out suddenly, especially with force or noise
  • Antonym: subside - to become less intense, violent, or severe

Etymology

From Latin 'explodere', meaning to drive out by clapping; originally theatrical, later referring to bursts.
