milk

1. NOUN

/ mɪlk / (AmE), / mɪlk / (BrE)
A white, nutritious liquid produced by mammals to feed their young, commonly used by humans as food and in various products.

Examples

If it's true that contented cows give more milk, why should n't happy ball players produce more base hits?

Related Terms

  • Degree Higher: cream - the thick fatty part of milk that rises to the top
  • Degree Lower: skim milk - milk from which the cream has been removed

Etymology

Old English *meolc*, from Proto-Germanic *meluks*.

2. VERB

/ mɪlk / (AmE), / mɪlk / (BrE)
to exploit or extract as much as possible from a situation, resource, or opportunity, often in a way that is considered excessive or unfair

Examples

He milks the latest scientific advances, translating them into his own special Buck Rogers vocabulary to huckster his fake machines as a cure-all for everything from hay fever to sexual impotence and cancer.

Synset Examples

I am milking this for all it's worth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: exploit - to make full use of and derive benefit from (a resource)

Etymology

Figurative use from the literal sense of extracting milk; compare the action of drawing out all possible value as one would milk from a cow.

3. NOUN

/ mɪlk / (AmE), / mɪlk / (BrE)
The white liquid produced by the mammary glands of female mammals to nourish their young.

Examples

Juanita and Mrs. Tussle kept Kate in bed a week until her milk dried.

Etymology

Old English meolc, of Germanic origin; related to Dutch melk and German Milch.

4. VERB

/ mɪlk / (AmE), / mɪlk / (BrE)
to draw milk from the udder of a female mammal, especially cows or goats

Examples

Starr Jones gets up every morning at five o' clock, milks his family cow, attends to farm chores, and then takes a two-hour train trip to New York.

Synset Examples

Cows need to be milked every morning

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lactate - to secrete milk (used of female mammals)

Etymology

Old English *meolcian*, from *meolc* (milk).
