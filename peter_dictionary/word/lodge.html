lodge

1. VERB

/ lɑdʒ / (AmE), / lɒdʒ / (BrE)
to stay somewhere temporarily, especially as a paying guest or tenant

Examples

Who, then, is of sufficient stature to lodge with Lenin?
The brothers continued to help each other during their studies, sharing a joint purse, lodging together in the dormitory and dining together at the home of their aunt.
Employees deduct expenses of travel, meals and lodging while away from home in connection with the performance of their services as employees.

Synset Examples

Where are you lodging in Paris?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: stay - remain in a place as a visitor or guest
  • Synonym: reside - live in a particular place

Etymology

From Old French *logier* (to shelter), from *loge* (hut, cabin), of Frankish or Germanic origin.

2. VERB

/ lɑdʒ / (AmE), / lɒdʒ / (BrE)
to formally submit or present (a complaint, charge, claim, etc.) to the proper authority

Examples

After the recommendation of the Department is forwarded to the appeal board, that is the appropriate place for a registrant to lodge his denial.

Synset Examples

The suspect was charged with murdering his wife

Related Terms

  • Synonym: file - to formally submit (a document, charge, etc.) to an authority

Etymology

From Old French *logier* (to dwell, stay), from *loge* (hut, compartment), originally referencing placing something somewhere.

3. VERB

/ lɑdʒ / (AmE), / lɒdʒ / (BrE)
to become stuck, fixed, or embedded in a place or object; or to cause something to become so

Examples

But the seven-iron shot he used to approach the green strayed into a bunker and lodged in a slight depression.

Synset Examples

lodge a bullet in the table
stick your thumb in the crack

Related Terms

  • Synonym: embed - to fix firmly and deeply in a surrounding mass
  • Antonym: dislodge - to remove from a position where it is stuck or fixed

Etymology

From Old French 'logier' (to shelter, to stay), from 'loge' (hut, cabin), from Frankish 'laubja' (shelter, hut).
