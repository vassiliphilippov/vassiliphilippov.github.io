double-cross

1. VERB

/ ˈdʌbəl ˌkrɑs / (AmE), / ˈdʌbəl ˌkrɒs / (BrE)
to deceive or betray someone, especially after previously appearing to act as their ally

Examples

An only child, he had done all the things that young men do who have been born to money and social position until his father double-crossed him by dying broke.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: betray - to be disloyal or unfaithful to someone

Etymology

Perhaps from the literal sense of crossing someone's intention twice; popularized in early 20th-century underworld slang.

2. VERB

/ ˈdʌbəl krɑs / (AmE), / ˈdʌbəl krɒs / (BrE)
to betray someone, especially by acting against a person or group that one has previously agreed to support or work with

Examples

He thought you double-crossed him.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: betray - to be disloyal or reveal secrets, harming someone who trusted you

Etymology

From 'double' + 'cross', originally from the slang of betting and criminal circles in the early 20th century.
