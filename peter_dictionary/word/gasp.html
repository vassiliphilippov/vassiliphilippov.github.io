gasp

1. VERB

/ ɡæsp / (AmE), / ɡɑːsp / (BrE)
to breathe suddenly and noisily, often as a result of surprise, pain, or difficulty in breathing

Examples

Southern California gasped and blinked under an autumn hot spell, drier, more enervating, more laden with man's contrived impurities than the worst days of the summer past.
The figure halted, and Watson gasped.
Hal gasped, and he said, "A divorce "?
Then, as he doubled, gasping, vomiting the breakfast he had so lately eaten, Lord straightened him with an uppercut.
Powell gasped.

Synset Examples

The runners reached the finish line, panting heavily

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pant - to breathe quickly and heavily, often after exertion

Etymology

Probably imitative of the sound of gasping; recorded in English since the 14th century.

2. NOUN

/ ɡæsp / (AmE), / ɡɑːsp / (BrE)
A sudden, short intake of breath, often due to surprise, shock, or difficulty in breathing.

Examples

Beth was very still and her breath came in small jerking gasps.
He sensed rather than heard the gasp that swept across the audience.
He was shaking with anger, his breath coming in long, painful gasps.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pant - to breathe quickly and with difficulty

Etymology

From Middle English *gaspen*, probably of Scandinavian origin; compare Old Norse *gasp* (to yawn).
