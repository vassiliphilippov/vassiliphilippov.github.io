sigh

1. VERB

/ saɪ / (AmE), / saɪ / (BrE)
to emit a long, deep, audible breath, typically as an expression of sadness, relief, tiredness, or similar emotion

Examples

And one of the Milwaukee rookies sighed and remarked, "Wish I was 40, and a top grade big leaguer.
John sighed.
"It always comes in threes ", she sighed heavily.
He sighed.
The secretary sighed with relief and then the telephone clicked in Rector's hand.

Synset Examples

She sighed sadly

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groan - make a low sound expressing pain or emotion
  • Degree Higher: sob - cry noisily, making loud, convulsive gasps

Etymology

From Old French *sighier*, of imitative origin.

2. NOUN

/ saɪ / (AmE), / saɪ / (BrE)
A long, deep audible breath expressing sorrow, relief, tiredness, or similar emotion.

Examples

And with each sigh, like a whip in the hand of an expert, the grass stripped something from Warren.
The things left unsaid they both felt deeply, and with a sigh they fell back on the well stuffed pillows.
At the end of the room there was a desk heaped with papers, and she began to riffle these, making sighs and and noises of girlish exasperation.
Digby heaved a deep ethereal sigh.
One could hear a very faint, ladylike sigh of relief.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: groan - a low sound of pain or discomfort
  • Synonym: moan - a long, low sound made in pain or despair

Etymology

From Old French *soupir*, from Latin *suspirare* (to sigh, to breathe deeply).

3. VERB

/ saɪ / (AmE), / saɪ / (BrE)
to say or express (something) with a sigh, often indicating sadness, weariness, or relief

Examples

"That's Johnny ", sighed the bank president, "the best hearted boy in the world, but absent-minded ".
All but the most rabid of Confederate flag wavers admit that the Old Southern tradition is defunct in actuality and sigh that its passing was accompanied by the disappearance of many genteel and aristocratic traditions of the reputedly languid ante-bellum way of life.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: murmur - to say something in a low or soft voice

Etymology

From Old French *sighier*, from Vulgar Latin *suspirare* (to draw a deep breath); related to Latin *suspirare* (to sigh).
