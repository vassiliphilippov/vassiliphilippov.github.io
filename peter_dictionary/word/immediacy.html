immediacy

1. NOUN

/ ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (AmE), / ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (BrE)
the quality of bringing one into direct and instant involvement or experience, without anything intervening

Examples

For the illusion of depth created by the contrast between the affixed material and everything else gives way immediately to an illusion of forms in bas-relief, which gives way in turn, and with equal immediacy, to an illusion that seems to contain both - or neither.
Yet the violent immediacy of the wallpaper strips pasted to the paper, and the only lesser immediacy of block capitals that simulate window lettering, manage somehow to push the grape cluster back into place on the picture plane so that it does not "jump ".

Synset Examples

Related Terms

  • Synonym: directness - the quality of being straightforward or immediate
  • Antonym: mediation - the process of intervening or acting as a mediator

Etymology

From immediate + -cy, originally from Latin immediatus (without anything between).

2. NOUN

/ ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (AmE), / ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (BrE)
The quality of happening or being done quickly and without delay.

Examples

The market was not far and, once there, the doctor's sense of immediacy left him and he fell into a state of harmony with the birds around him.

Synset Examples

the immediacy of their response
the instancy of modern communication

Related Terms

  • Synonym: promptness - the quality of acting quickly without delay
  • Antonym: slowness - the quality of not being immediate or prompt

Etymology

From immediate + -cy, modeled after similar abstract nouns.

3. NOUN

/ ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (AmE), / ɪˈmiːdiəsi / (BrE)
the state or quality of being directly and intuitively perceived or experienced, without mediation or reflective thought

Examples

The term idea refers to our more reflective or thoughtful consciousness as opposed to the immediacies of sensuous or emotional experience.

Etymology

From immediate + -cy, modelled on Latin immediatus ('without anything between').
