swallow

1. VERB

/ ˈswɑloʊ / (AmE), / ˈswɒləʊ / (BrE)
(of a thing) to enclose, engulf, or envelop completely, as if by swallowing.

Examples

Mark asked, and Abel lifted his eyes from the double lines in the middle of the road, the twin white ribbons which the car swallowed rapidly as it ascended the crest of the hill and came down.
The drug went after the pain and swallowed it.

Synset Examples

The huge waves swallowed the small boat and it sank shortly thereafter

Related Terms

  • Synonym: engulf - to sweep over and cover completely
  • Synonym: consume - to completely use up or destroy

2. NOUN

/ ˈswɑːloʊ / (AmE), / ˈswɒləʊ / (BrE)
An amount of liquid or food that is swallowed at one time; the act of swallowing.

Examples

I took a deep breath and an even deeper swallow of my drink, and said, "I admit that going back to Ralph Waldo Emerson for humor is like going to a modern musical comedy for music and comedy ".

Synset Examples

one swallow of the liquid was enough
he took a drink of his beer and smacked his lips

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gulp - an act of swallowing quickly or in large amounts
  • Degree Lower: sip - a small mouthful of liquid

3. VERB

/ ˈswɑloʊ / (AmE), / ˈswɒləʊ / (BrE)
to cause food, drink, or another substance to pass down the throat from the mouth into the stomach

Examples

Several years ago headlines were made by a small radio transmitter capsule which could be swallowed by the patient and which would then radio internal pressure data to external receivers.
Japanese fishermen have sometimes observed that sardines hauled up in their nets during a tsunami have enormously swollen stomachs; the fish have swallowed vast numbers of bottom living diatoms, raised to the surface by the disturbance.
A traditional Lao explanation is that the moon was being swallowed by a toad, and the remedy was to make all possible noise, ideally with firearms.
Sarah swallowed past another kind of constriction in her throat.
"I 've been thinking ", she said, swallowing the toast.

Synset Examples

Swallow the raw fish--it won't kill you!

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gulp - to swallow quickly or in large amounts
  • Antonym: sip - to drink (a liquid) by taking small mouthfuls

Etymology

Old English *swelgan* (to swallow, devour), of Germanic origin.

4. VERB

/ ˈswɑloʊ / (AmE), / ˈswɒləʊ / (BrE)
To take in or engulf completely, especially so as to destroy or absorb; to absorb or incorporate something or someone, often with the result of total loss of identity.

Examples

As a creative enterprise, its abilities are primarily in "swallowing "creative enterprises developed outside its own organization (an ability made possible by us, and almost mandatory).

Synset Examples

The Nazis swallowed the Baltic countries

Related Terms

  • Synonym: engulf - to sweep over and cover completely; to overwhelm
  • Synonym: devour - to consume or destroy eagerly or completely
  • Synonym: subsume - to absorb or include within something else
  • Antonym: release - to set free or allow to dissolve; the opposite of engulf

Etymology

Old English 'swelgan'; cognate with Dutch 'zwelgen', German 'schwelgen'.

5. NOUN

/ ˈswɑːloʊ / (AmE), / ˈswɒləʊ / (BrE)
an amount of liquid that you swallow at one time

Examples

He took several large swallows, recollected that Docherty had gone up another flight, and decided he would be wise to cover himself by finding him.
Gracias ", I hollered, my first long swallow filling me with confidence and immediately doubling the size of my Spanish vocabulary.

Synset Examples

a sup of ale

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: sip - a small mouthful of liquid, taken in sips
  • Degree Higher: gulp - a large mouthful of liquid swallowed quickly
