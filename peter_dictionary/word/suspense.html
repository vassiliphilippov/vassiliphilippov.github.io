suspense

1. NOUN

/ səˈspɛns / (AmE), / səˈspɛns / (BrE)
a feeling or state of uncertainty and excitement, often involving anxious anticipation about what may happen next

Examples

General manager Pels even suggested that it might be wise to keep the Mexicans in suspense rather than accept their offers to sell out and move away, and try to have a few punished.
She experienced none of the suspense of some poor stranger selling encyclopedias.

Synset Examples

the matter remained in suspense for several years

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anticipation - expectation or looking forward to something
  • Antonym: certainty - the state of being sure or confident

Etymology

From Old French 'suspens' (delayed, uncertain), from Latin 'suspensus' (suspended, uncertain), past participle of 'suspendere'.

2. NOUN

/ səˈspɛns / (AmE), / səˈspɛns / (BrE)
A feeling of excitement or anxiety about what will happen next, especially in a story or situation where the outcome is uncertain.

Examples

Also, when she had bitten off half of the small radish, she found the suspense unbearable; and she would snatch the finger held half of the radish out to where she could inspect it.
Once he had abandoned himself to the very worst, once he had quieted all the dragons of worry and suspense, there would n't be very much for Mae to do.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: anticipation - expectation or looking forward to something
  • Synonym: anxiety - a feeling of worry or unease about what may happen
  • Antonym: certainty - the state of being sure about what will happen

Etymology

From Old French 'suspens' (delay, doubt), from Latin 'suspensus', past participle of 'suspendere' (to suspend).
