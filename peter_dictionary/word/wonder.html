wonder

1. VERB

/ ˈwʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
to feel uncertain or to be curious about something; to ask oneself a question or express doubt or speculation

Examples

Every man who dabbles in the market to make a little easy money on the side and suffers losses could at the time hardly face his wife who was wondering how her husband could be so dumb.
(Several times recently I have wondered whether shows were being staged for the sake of the script or just to entertain the audience with the spectacle of scenery being shifted right in front of their eyes.
"When you pile your "guy "on the bonfire tomorrow night, I wonder how much of the true story of Guy Fawkes you will remember?
One wonders about its applicability to people.
One might also wonder if monkeys are capable of developing bronchiolitis as we know it in man or the horse.

Synset Examples

I wonder whether this was the right thing to do
she wondered whether it would snow tonight

Related Terms

  • Synonym: doubt - to feel uncertain about something
  • Synonym: question - to express uncertainty or seek information
  • Antonym: know - to be certain or aware of something

Etymology

From Old English wundrian (to marvel, be surprised), from wundor (a wonder); later sense extended to questioning or expressing doubt.

2. NOUN

/ ˈwʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
A person, thing, or event that causes admiration or amazement.

Examples

It's a wonder, really, to how much mendacious trouble Larkin puts himself to sell the Jerebohms that preposterous manse.
With these gadgets - impressive to the gullible because of their flashing light bulbs, ticks, and buzzes - he then carries out a vicious medical con game, capitalizing on people's respect for the electrical and atomic wonders of our scientific age.
(Small wonder a Spokane jury awarded the husband $ 35823 for his wife's death.)
Small wonder, then, that we fear.

Synset Examples

the wonders of modern science

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marvel - a thing that causes great wonder; synonym for 'wonder' in this sense

Etymology

From Old English wundor (miracle, wonder); related to Old High German wuntar.

3. VERB

/ ˈwʌndər / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
to desire to know or be curious about something; to feel a wish to learn or find out

Examples

There is a pause in the merriment as your friends gaze at you, wondering why you are staring, open-mouthed in amazement.
Rudyard Kipling's scorn for the "jargon "of psychical research was altered somewhat when he wondered "how, or why, had I been shown an unreleased roll of my life film "?
He wondered what expression, as he made that gesture, was on his face.
He wondered if it wore the old anxiety, or the old, taut stoicism.
How was she to behave, Claire wondered.

Synset Examples

He wondered who had built this beautiful church

Related Terms

  • Synonym: ponder - to think about something carefully, especially before making a decision
  • Synonym: speculate - to form a theory or conjecture without firm evidence

Etymology

From Old English wundrian (to be astonished), influenced by 'wonder' (noun).

4. VERB

/ ˈwʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
To be filled with amazement or admiration at something remarkable or extraordinary.

Examples

Skeptics may deny the more startling phenomena of dreams as things they have never personally observed, but failure to wonder at their basic mystery is outright avoidance of routine evidence.

Synset Examples

We marvelled at the child's linguistic abilities

Related Terms

  • Synonym: marvel - to be filled with wonder or astonishment
  • Synonym: admire - to regard with respect or warm approval
  • Antonym: disregard - to pay no attention to; to ignore

Etymology

From Old English *wundrian* (to be astonished), related to *wundor* (wonder, miracle).

5. NOUN

/ ˈwʌndɚ / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
a feeling or state of curiosity and desire to know more about something

Examples

Upon consideration of the variety of soils and fabrics normally encountered in the washing process, it is little wonder that the use of a number of detergent constituents having "synergistic "properties has gained widespread acceptance.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curiosity - a strong desire to know or learn something; inquisitiveness

Etymology

Old English wundor (marvel, miracle).

6. NOUN

/ ˈwʌndər / (AmE), / ˈwʌndə / (BrE)
A feeling of surprise and admiration caused by something beautiful, unexpected, or unfamiliar.

Examples

And no wonder, for Vientiane, the old City of Sandalwood, had become the City of Bullet Holes.
"Sportin "'! he echoed again in soft wonder.
And Carla, watching in wonder, turned to Sam.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: amazement - a feeling of great surprise or astonishment
  • Synonym: awe - a feeling of reverential respect mixed with fear or wonder
  • Antonym: indifference - lack of interest or concern

Etymology

From Old English wundor (marvel, wonder); related to Old High German wuntar (miracle).
