oleander

1. NOUN

/ ˌoʊliˈændɚ / (AmE), / ˌəʊliˈændə / (BrE)
an evergreen ornamental shrub with long narrow leaves and clusters of fragrant white, pink, or red flowers, native to the East Indies but widely grown in warm regions; the plant is toxic if ingested

Examples

My great grandmother, I have been told, made her garden her great pride; she cherished rare and delicate plants like oleanders in tubs and wall-flowers and lemon verbenas in pots that had to be wintered in the cellar; she filled the waste spots of the yard with common things like the garden heliotrope in a corner by the woodshed, and the plantain lilies along the west side of the house.

Etymology

From Latin 'oleander', alteration of 'variant of 'olea' (olive) and 'andros' (man), possibly because the leaves resemble those of the olive tree.
