shame

1. NOUN

/ ʃeɪm / (AmE), / ʃeɪm / (BrE)
a painful feeling caused by the awareness of having done something wrong, foolish, or improper

Examples

I know that I myself felt that it was a mortal shame for a man to be torn open by a British musket ball, as Isaac had been, yet I also felt relieved and lucky that it had been him and not myself.
For many nights afterward, the idea of her having been so close to me in that imagined bed would return and fill me with obscure and painful desires, would cause me to lie awake in shame, tossing with irresolution, longing to fall into a deep sleep.
And Alexander sobbed like a girl for the dreams he had had, and he felt no shame.

Related Terms

  • Degree Lower: embarrassment - a feeling of awkwardness or discomfort in social situations
  • Degree Higher: remorse - deep regret for a wrong committed

Etymology

From Old English *scamu* (shame, disgrace), of Germanic origin.

2. NOUN

/ ʃeɪm / (AmE), / ʃeɪm / (BrE)
A state of disgrace or loss of honor, caused by a wrongful or improper act or association.

Examples

In defiance, a chinless reprobate, Jake Camaret, marched down the aisle in St. Peter's one Sunday morning, followed by one of the women from the Bordel, whose dress and walk plainly showed the lack of any shame.

Synset Examples

one mistake brought shame to all his family
suffered the ignominy of being sent to prison

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disgrace - loss of reputation or honor
  • Antonym: honor - a state of respect or esteem

Etymology

From Old English *scamu* (shame, disgrace), related to Old High German *skama* (sense of disgrace).

3. NOUN

/ ʃeɪm / (AmE), / ʃeɪm / (BrE)
A circumstance or event that causes regret or disappointment; an unfortunate or regrettable situation.

Examples

Once in a while they said what a shame it was, with Granny dying, but they all agreed she would n't have wanted it any other way.
It would be a shame, Mr. Chairman, if we allowed a few rotten apples to give a bad name to the whole barrel.

Synset Examples

it's a pity he couldn't do it

Related Terms

  • Synonym: pity - an unfortunate event or development causing regret
  • Degree Higher: tragedy - a very sad or unfortunate event
  • Degree Lower: inconvenience - a minor problem or difficulty

Etymology

From Old English *sceamu* (a feeling of guilt or disgrace); sense of 'unfortunate event' developed later.

4. VERB

/ ʃeɪm / (AmE), / ʃeɪm / (BrE)
to bring disgrace or dishonor upon someone, or cause someone to feel ashamed

Examples

Above all, disabuse yourselves of any thought that I propose to vent moral indignation at your rifling my residence, to whimper over the loss of a few objets d'art, or to shame you into rectitude.

Synset Examples

he dishonored his family by committing a serious crime

Related Terms

  • Synonym: dishonor - bring disgrace or loss of respect to someone/something
  • Synonym: disgrace - cause someone to lose respect or to be in a state of shame
  • Antonym: honor - to show respect or bring distinction (opposite meaning)

Etymology

From Old English *scamu* (disgrace, shame), of Germanic origin.
