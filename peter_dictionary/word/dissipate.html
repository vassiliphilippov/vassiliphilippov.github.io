dissipate

1. VERB

/ ˈdɪsɪˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪsɪpeɪt / (BrE)
(of a group or substance) to scatter or disperse and gradually disappear.

Examples

It is one of the ironic quirks of history that the viability and usefulness of nationalism and the territorial state are rapidly dissipating at precisely the time that the nation-state attained its highest number (approximately 100).

Synset Examples

The crowds dispersed
The children scattered in all directions when the teacher approached

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to spread out or scatter in different directions
  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or spread things over a wide area

Etymology

From Latin dissipare, dissipat- ‘to scatter, squander’.

2. VERB

/ ˈdɪsɪˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪsɪpeɪt / (BrE)
to cause something to disperse or scatter in different directions, or to disappear gradually

Examples

Direct confrontation and acceptance of Mrs. B.'s anger against the second baby soon dissipated her fears of annihilation.

Synset Examples

She waved her hand and scattered the crowds

Related Terms

  • Synonym: disperse - to distribute or spread over a wide area
  • Synonym: scatter - to throw or spread things over a wide area
  • Antonym: collect - to bring or gather things together

Etymology

From Latin dissipare (to scatter, disperse).

3. VERB

/ ˈdɪsəˌpeɪt / (AmE), / ˈdɪsɪpeɪt / (BrE)
to spend money, resources, or energy wastefully or in a reckless manner

Examples

As for his private monies, they were rapidly dissipated in drinking, gaming and carousing.

Synset Examples

Fritter away one's inheritance

Related Terms

  • Synonym: squander - to spend money, resources, or opportunities wastefully
  • Antonym: conserve - to protect and use resources carefully

Etymology

From Latin dissipatus, past participle of dissipare (to scatter, squander).
