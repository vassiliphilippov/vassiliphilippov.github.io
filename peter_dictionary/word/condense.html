condense

1. VERB

/ kənˈdɛns / (AmE), / kənˈdɛns / (BrE)
(of a gas or vapor) to change into a liquid, especially when cooled or compressed.

Examples

Matheson highest purity tank chlorine was passed through a tube of resublimed * * f into an evacuated Pyrex system where it was condensed with liquid air.
The desired amounts of inactive chlorine and radioactive chlorine were likewise condensed in these cells on the vacuum line following which they were frozen down and the manifold as a whole was sealed off.

Synset Examples

water condenses
The acid distills at a specific temperature

Related Terms

  • Antonym: evaporate - to change from a liquid to a gas

Etymology

From Latin condensare, meaning 'to make thick or dense'.

2. VERB

/ kənˈdɛns / (AmE), / kənˈdɛns / (BrE)
To make (something) more concise or compact by shortening or reducing its content without losing its meaning.

Examples

These errors were then collected and written on a blackboard, condensing similar ideas.

Synset Examples

condense the contents of a book into a summary

Related Terms

  • Synonym: abridge - to shorten a text without losing the sense
  • Antonym: expand - to make something longer or more extensive

Etymology

From Latin 'condensare', from 'com-' (together) + 'densare' (to make dense).
