closet

1. NOUN

/ ˈklɑːzət / (AmE), / ˈklɒzɪt / (BrE)
A small room, cupboard, or recess used for storage, especially for clothes; a private storage space built into a house or room.

Examples

Aggie might fly into a closet, shut the door and bury her head in the clothes; he dared to wait for the lightning.
Winston took the clothesbrush out of the closet and went to work.
The velvet smoking jackets, pearl gray, wine, and blue, which Miss Ada had bought him hung brushed and unworn in the closet.
He got his hat out of the closet.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: cupboard - A piece of furniture or a small recess with shelves for storing utensils, food, or other items

Etymology

From Middle English closet, from Old French closet (small enclosure, closet), diminutive of clos (enclosure), from Latin clausum, neuter of clausus (closed).

2. NOUN

/ ˈklɑzət / (AmE), / ˈklɒzɪt / (BrE)
A tall piece of furniture with a door, used for storing clothes; typically equipped with rails or hooks for hanging garments.

Examples

Without comment he opened the closet and from its shelves constructed a highboard around the egg case which he had placed on the floor inside.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: wardrobe - a tall piece of furniture for storing clothes
  • Synonym: cupboard - a piece of furniture with shelves for storing items, often with doors

Etymology

From Old French *closet* (small enclosure), diminutive of *clos* (enclosure), from Latin *clausum* (closed space).

3. NOUN

/ ˈklɑzət / (AmE), / ˈklɒzɪt / (BrE)
(historical, chiefly British) A small room with a toilet; a water closet.

Examples

She could not count the times Herman had rapped on the door, just a couple of bangs that shook the whole damned closet and might, someday, break away the pipe connections from the wall.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: toilet - sanitary fixture for defecation and urination

Etymology

From Middle English *closet* (private room), from Old French *clos* (enclosure).
