drape

1. VERB

/ dreɪp / (AmE), / dreɪp / (BrE)
To arrange (fabric or other material) loosely or decoratively over or around something.

Examples

The figure leapt from the box, almost lost its balance, the flag draped there tore in the air, the figure landed on its left leg, fell on its hands, and pressed itself up.
Here and there on work table or pedestal stood a shape with a sheet or a tarpaulin draped over it.
It was draped with snowy napkins that kept hot a platter of oyster salt roast and a mound of corn fritters.

Synset Examples

drape a cloth

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hang - to suspend or be suspended from above

Etymology

From Middle English 'drape', from Old French 'drap' (cloth, piece of cloth), from Late Latin 'drappus' (cloth).

2. VERB

/ dreɪp / (AmE), / dreɪp / (BrE)
to place or arrange something loosely or casually over or around something

Examples

He then draped him over the rough stand, explained that he was supposed to be recently dead, and was being held on his mother's lap.

Synset Examples

The cat draped herself on the sofa

Related Terms

  • Synonym: hang - to suspend or be suspended without support from below

Etymology

From Middle English drapen, from Old French draper (to make cloth), from drap (cloth).

3. NOUN

/ dreɪp / (AmE), / dreɪp / (BrE)
A piece of cloth hung as a blind or curtain, especially for a window.

Examples

On the third floor one of the two windows was lighted; it was framed in maroon drapes, and no faces were visible.
[THUNDER RUMBLING] Michael, why are the drapes open?

Related Terms

  • Synonym: curtain - a piece of cloth that hangs down to cover a window, stage, etc.

Etymology

From French *drap* (cloth), ultimately from Latin *drappus* (cloth).
