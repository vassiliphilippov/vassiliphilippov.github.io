log

1. VERB

/ lɑg / (AmE), / lɒg / (BrE)
to cut down trees in a forest for timber

Examples

An area may have been partially logged and requires removal of stumps or clean up.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: lumber - to cut and prepare timber for use

2. NOUN

/ lɔg / (AmE), / lɒg / (BrE)
a section of the trunk or a large branch of a tree, after it has been cut and stripped of branches

Examples

He was sitting on top of a log which was spinning round and around in the water.
The log was spinning.
Why should I be spinning just because the goddamn log is spinning?
No doubt it had to do with the log.
Out of long experience I have found that incidental figures and other objects like trees, logs, and bushes can be traced from the original sketch and moved about in the major areas on the final sheet until they occupy the right position, which I call' clicking'.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: timber - wood prepared for use, especially building material
  • Degree Lower: branch - a limb off the main trunk of a tree
  • Degree Higher: tree trunk - the main stem of a tree

Etymology

From Old English 'logg' or Scandinavian origin, possibly Norwegian 'låg' (tree trunk).

3. VERB

/ lɑɡ / (AmE), / lɒɡ / (BrE)
to record or enter details, events, or quantities in an official logbook or system, especially as part of regular monitoring or tracking

Examples

Pursuing it, he has logged 500000 miles, suffered indescribable digestive indignities, and meticulously collected physiological data on the health and eating habits of 10000 individuals, from Bantu tribesmen to Italian contadini.
"I 'm just logging ", the cowboy explained.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: record - to set down in writing or some other permanent form for later reference

Etymology

From Old Norse 'lag' (a felled tree). The recording sense developed from the practice of recording ship speeds with the help of a log-line.
