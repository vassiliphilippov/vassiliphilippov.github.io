Attorney General

1. NOUN

/ əˌtɝni dʒəˈnɝəl / (AmE), / əˌtɜːni dʒəˈnɜːrəl / (BrE)
the principal legal officer who represents a country or state in legal proceedings and gives legal advice to the government; in the United States, the head of the Justice Department and chief law enforcement officer.

Examples

Attorney General Palmer made a series of raids that sent more than 4000 so-called radicals to the jails, in direct violation of their constitutional rights.

Synset Examples

the post of Attorney General was created in 1789

Etymology

From Latin 'attornatus' (appointed) and 'general' (of the whole, relating to all), indicating an official appointed for general legal matters.

2. NOUN

/ əˌtɝni dʒəˈnɚəl / (AmE), / əˌtɜːni dʒəˈnɛrəl / (BrE)
The principal legal officer who represents a country or state in legal proceedings and gives legal advice to the government, often serving as the head of the Justice Department.

Examples

The Attorney General shall assign such officers and employees of the Department of Justice as may be necessary to represent the United States as to any claims of the Government of the United States with respect to which the Commission has jurisdiction under this title.

Synset Examples

Edmund Randolph was the first Attorney General, appointed by President Washington

Related Terms

  • Synonym: solicitor general - senior law officer who assists the Attorney General and may represent the government in court

Etymology

From 'attorney', ultimately from Old French 'atorné' (appointed), and 'general', from Latin 'generalis'. Title originated in English law.
