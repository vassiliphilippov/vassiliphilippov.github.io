grandfather clause

1. NOUN

/ ˈɡræn(d)ˌfɑðɚ ˌklɔz / (AmE), / ˈɡrɑːnfɑːðə ˌklɔːz / (BrE)
A provision in a new law or regulation that exempts certain people or entities from its requirements if they met specific conditions before the law was enacted, often to maintain existing rights or status.

Examples

That is in the continuance of the "grandfather clauses "in their membership requirements.
The reason and the day of "grandfather clauses "has long since passed.

Etymology

Originally from U.S. legal usage after the Civil War; referring to clauses exempting those whose ancestors had voting rights before the Civil War, primarily to disenfranchise Black voters.

2. NOUN

/ ˈɡræn(d)ˌfɑðɚ klɔz / (AmE), / ˈɡrɑːn(d)ˌfɑːðə klɑːz / (BrE)
A provision in a new law or regulation that exempts certain people or entities from its requirements if they were involved in an activity before the law took effect, originally used in the Southern United States to allow illiterate whites to vote after the Civil War.

Examples

The licenses were grandfathered in, so there was no problem with the Gaming Commission.

Etymology

Named for clauses in late 19th-century U.S. Southern constitutions referencing one's grandfather, used to circumvent voting restrictions.
