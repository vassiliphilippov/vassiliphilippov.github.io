common law

1. NOUN

/ ˈkɑːmən lɔ / (AmE), / ˈkɒmən lɔː / (BrE)
The part of a legal system based on customs and judicial precedents rather than statutes, especially as developed in England.

Examples

First was the period of codification of existing law: the Code Napoleon in France and the peculiar codification that, in fact, resulted from Austin's restatement and ordering of the Common Law in England.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: statutory law - law established by legislative enactment

Etymology

From Middle English 'commune lawe', referring to a body of law common to a country or community, as opposed to local or special law.
