direct sum

1. NOUN

/ dəˈrɛkt sʌm, daɪˈrɛkt sʌm / (AmE), / dəˈrɛkt sʌm, daɪˈrɛkt sʌm / (BrE)
The combination of two or more disjoint sets or mathematical structures, such that each element of the resulting set is formed by combining exactly one element from each of the original sets, commonly used in mathematics and algebra.

Examples

If the direct sum decomposition (a) is valid, how can we get hold of the projections * * f associated with the decomposition?

Related Terms

  • Antonym: direct product - mathematical construction pairing elements from component sets, often contrasted with direct sum

Etymology

Formed from the mathematical sense of 'direct' (straightforward, without intermediaries) and 'sum' (result of addition), used since the 19th century in algebra.
