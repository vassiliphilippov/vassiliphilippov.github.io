conditioned reflex

1. NOUN

/ kənˈdɪʃənd rɪˈflɛks / (AmE), / kənˈdɪʃənd rɪˈflɛks / (BrE)
A reflexive response that is learned through repeated association with a particular stimulus, rather than being innate.

Examples

In other studies, loss of differentiation in previously established conditioned reflexes resulted from repeated convulsive (metrazol) treatments, suggesting a fundamental disturbance in the balance between excitatory and inhibitory cerebral processes.
The pain became thus the symbol for food and elicited salivary secretion (conditioned reflex).

Related Terms

  • Antonym: unconditioned reflex - an automatic, innate reflex not acquired by learning

Etymology

Based on 'condition' from Latin 'conditio' (agreement, condition) and 'reflex' from Latin 'reflexus' (bent back). Popularized in psychology after work by Ivan Pavlov (early 20th century).
