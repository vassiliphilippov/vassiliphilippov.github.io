subordinating conjunction

1. NOUN

/ səˌbɔrdɪˈneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (AmE), / səˌbɔːdɪˈneɪtɪŋ kənˈdʒʌŋkʃən / (BrE)
a word or phrase (such as ‘because’, ‘although’, or ‘that’) used to introduce a subordinate clause and connect it to a main clause

Examples

MICHAEL: I thought you were going to tell me that you were moving your family to Vegas and that you'’d been offered the, uh, Vice Presidency of the Houstan hotels there.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: coordinating conjunction - a conjunction that joins words or clauses of equal importance

Etymology

From 'subordinate' (from Latin subordinare, 'to arrange under') and 'conjunction' (from Latin coniungere, 'to join together').
