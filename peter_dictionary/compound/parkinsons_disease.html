Parkinson's disease

1. NOUN

/ ˈpɑrkɪnsənz dɪˌziz / (AmE), / ˈpɑːkɪnsənz dɪˌziːz / (BrE)
A progressive degenerative disorder of the central nervous system, especially marked by tremor, muscular rigidity, and impaired movement.

Examples

Max, in a fit of despair, takes Alicia and runs off for two marvelous weeks in Burbank (Fink calls it "the most wonderful and lovely fourteen days in my whole life "), at the end of which Alicia tragically contracts Parkinson's disease and dies.

Etymology

Named after James Parkinson (1755–1824), the English surgeon who first described the disease in 1817.
