Fifth Amendment

1. NOUN

/ fɪfθ əˈmɛndmənt / (AmE), / fɪfθ əˈmɛndmənt / (BrE)
The amendment to the United States Constitution that protects individuals against self-incrimination, double jeopardy, and requires due process of law and just compensation for property taken for public use.

Examples

A ten-to-one shot he would take the Fifth, and I'’d lose.

Etymology

Refers to the fifth article added (amendment) to the U.S. Constitution in 1791.
