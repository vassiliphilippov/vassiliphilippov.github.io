corpuscular radiation

1. NOUN

/ kɔːrˈpʌskjələr ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (AmE), / kɔːˈpʌskjʊlə ˌreɪdiˈeɪʃən / (BrE)
Radiation consisting of a stream of atomic or subatomic particles, such as alpha particles, beta particles, or neutrons, which may be positively charged, negatively charged, or neutral.

Examples

Whipple (1955) extends the effects to include the solar corpuscular radiation pressure, which increases both the minimum particle size and the drag.
Further, the corpuscular radiation, i.e., the solar wind protons, must sputter away the surface atoms of the dust and cause a slow diminution in size, with a resultant increase in both the Poynting-Robertson effect and the ratio of the repulsive force to the gravitational force.

Related Terms

  • Antonym: electromagnetic radiation - Radiation consisting of waves (such as light, radio waves, or X-rays), not particles

Etymology

From Latin 'corpusculum' (diminutive of 'corpus', meaning 'body'), referring to a small particle, combined with 'radiation'.
