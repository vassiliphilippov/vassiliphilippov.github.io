Doppler effect

1. NOUN

/ ˈdɑplɚ ɪˌfɛkt / (AmE), / ˈdɒplə rɪˌfɛkt / (BrE)
A change in the apparent frequency or wavelength of a wave in relation to an observer moving relative to the wave source.

Examples

The Doppler effect, using ultrasonic signals, can be employed to measure the flow of blood without cutting into the blood vessel.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: Doppler shift - the change in frequency or wavelength of a wave for an observer moving relative to its source

Etymology

Named after Austrian physicist Christian Doppler (1803–1853), who first described the phenomenon.
