plaster of Paris

1. NOUN

/ ˌplæstɚ əv ˈpærɪs / (AmE), / ˌplɑːstə əv ˈpærɪs / (BrE)
a white powder made from gypsum that hardens when mixed with water, used for making molds, sculptures, and medical casts

Examples

I would not want to be one of those writers who begin each morning by exclaiming, "O Gogol, O Chekhov, O Thackeray and Dickens, what would you have made of a bomb shelter ornamented with four plaster-of-Paris ducks, a birdbath, and three composition gnomes with long beards and red mobcaps "?

Etymology

Named after Paris, France, where the material was extensively produced from local gypsum deposits.

2. NOUN

/ ˈplæs.tɚ əv ˈpær.ɪs / (AmE), / ˈplɑː.stər əv ˈpær.ɪs / (BrE)
a white powder made from gypsum (calcium sulfate), which forms a paste when mixed with water and hardens into a solid; used in making molds, sculptures, and casts for broken limbs

Examples

After a few tortuous moments of wondering who "he "is, the camera pans across the room to the plaster statue, and we realize that Neitzbohr is trying to redeem himself in the eyes of a mute piece of sculpture.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: gypsum - a soft sulfate mineral; major component of plaster of Paris

Etymology

Named after Paris, where gypsum deposits were historically prominent.
