order of magnitude

1. NOUN

/ ˈɔrdɚ əv ˈmæɡnəˌtud / (AmE), / ˈɔːdə əv ˈmæɡnɪtjuːd / (BrE)
A class, usually in powers of ten, expressing the scale or size of a quantity relative to another; two quantities are of the same order of magnitude if one is less than ten times larger or smaller than the other.

Examples

This negative pressure is not explained by the velocity head * * f since this is not sufficient to explain the readings by several magnitudes.

Related Terms

  • Synonym: scale - Relative size or extent of something, especially as represented by a ratio or measurement

Etymology

From Latin 'ordo' (order, rank) and 'magnitudo' (greatness, size).

2. NOUN

/ ˈɔrdɚ əv ˈmæg.nɪ.tud / (AmE), / ˈɔːdə əv ˈmæɡ.nɪ.tjuːd / (BrE)
a level in a scale of size or quantity, especially in powers of ten; a difference by a factor of ten

Note that the mass scale is one to two orders of magnitude greater than some previously used; for example, Jacchia (1948) derived a scale of 0.15 g for a * * f, zero magnitude meteorite.
These extrapolated fluxes are about an order of magnitude less than the values from the satellite data and the figures in Whipple's table.
