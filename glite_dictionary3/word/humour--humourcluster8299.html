humour

noun
(archaic) body fluid, esp. in ancient physiology
Pronunciation: / ˈhjuːmər / (AmE), / ˈhjuːmə / (BrE)
(historical/archaic) any of the bodily fluids once believed to affect health and temperament in ancient and medieval medicine

Examples

body humours
According to medieval medicine, an excess of any humour could cause disease.
The ancient Greeks believed that the balance of bodily humours affected a person's health and temperament.
The eye contains two main humours: the aqueous humour and the vitreous humour.
Bloodletting was once used to restore the body's humours to their proper proportions.
In medieval times, doctors believed that a person's health depended on the balance of the four humours.
The theory of humours influenced medical practice well into the Renaissance.
