to subside

verb
to sink or settle into something soft
Pronunciation: / səbˈsaɪd / (AmE), / səbˈsaɪd /; / səbˈsʌɪd / (BrE)
to sink, settle, or go down to a lower level, often after movement or disturbance

Examples

to subside into a chair
After a long day, she subsided into the armchair and closed her eyes.
As soon as the meeting ended, he subsided into his seat, exhausted.
He subsided into bed, too tired to even pull up the covers.
After the earthquake, part of the mountain began to subside and created a deep valley.
Old mine tunnels can cause land above them to subside unexpectedly.
The ground may subside when water is drained from beneath the surface.
After the flood, the water in the fields began to subside slowly over several days.
It took hours for the stirred-up sediment in the pond to subside and allow the water to clear.
The house started to crack as the ground beneath it began to subside unexpectedly.

Related Terms

  • antonym – rise: to move upward or go up to a higher level
  • synonym – settle: to sink or subside to a lower level
  • synonym – sink: to move downward, especially below the surface of a liquid or soil
