to solarise

verb
reverse tones in a photograph by brief light exposure
Pronunciation: / ˈsoʊləˌraɪz / (AmE), / ˈsəʊləˌraɪz /; / ˈsəʊləraɪz / (BrE)
in photography, to expose film or an image to too much light, causing the image to change or become altered (such as reversing tones or losing detail)

Examples

to solarise a photo
If you solarise a negative, you'll see dramatic contrasts in the image.
She experimented with different ways to solarise her black-and-white photographs.
The photographer decided to solarise the print for a surreal effect.
If you solarise the film, you'll ruin the picture quality.
Photographers must avoid solarising their negatives during development.
She accidentally solarised the image by leaving it in direct sunlight too long.
Be careful with exposure time, or the image will solarise and lose its detail.
Due to a mistake in development, several negatives started to solarise unexpectedly.
If you leave the old film in the sun, it might solarise and spoil the pictures.
