Antoine Laurent Lavoisier

proper noun
French chemist, father of modern chemistry
Pronunciation: / ænˈtwɑn loʊˈrɑn lɑːvwɑːˈzjeɪ / (AmE), / ˈæntwæn loˈrɒ̃ ləvwɑːˈzjeɪ / (BrE)
(1743–1794) A French chemist regarded as the father of modern chemistry, known for discovering oxygen and disproving the phlogiston theory.

Examples

Antoine Laurent Lavoisier discovered oxygen
Antoine Laurent Lavoisier is often credited with naming both oxygen and hydrogen.
Students in chemistry classes frequently learn about Antoine Laurent Lavoisier and his role in debunking the phlogiston theory.
The experiments of Antoine Laurent Lavoisier fundamentally changed the field of chemistry in the 18th century.
