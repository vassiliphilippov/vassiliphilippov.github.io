to drive

verb
cause to enter a state or action
Pronunciation: / draɪv / (AmE), / draɪv / (BrE)
to urge, compel, or motivate someone to act or to enter a particular state, either physically or metaphorically

Examples

to drive someone crazy
Lack of sleep might drive you crazy after a few days.
Stress can drive people to extreme behavior.
The coach's constant criticism drove him to quit the team.
His passion drove him to succeed despite the challenges.
She has been driving herself hard to finish the project on time.
They are driving relentlessly toward their goal of expanding the business.
He was driven to make a drastic decision by mounting financial troubles.
Her persistent criticism drove him to quit the project entirely.
The pressure of deadlines can drive people to work long hours without rest.

  • antonym – advance: to move forward or make progress, opposite of retreat
  • synonym – coerce: to persuade or force someone to do something by using threats or pressure
  • synonym – compel: to drive or urge forcefully or irresistibly
  • antonym – discourage: to cause someone not to want to do something
  • synonym – force: to make someone do something against their will
  • synonym – motivate: to provide someone with a reason for doing something
  • antonym – persuade: to cause someone to do something by reasoning or argument
  • synonym – push: to make strong effort to achieve something
  • synonym – push back: to force an enemy or opponent to move backwards
  • synonym – repel: to force something to move away or keep at a distance
  • synonym – strive: to make great efforts to achieve or obtain something
