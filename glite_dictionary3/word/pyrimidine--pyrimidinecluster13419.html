pyrimidine

noun
heterocyclic organic compound with two nitrogen atoms
Pronunciation: / pɪˈrɪməˌdin /; / pɪˈrɪmɪˌdin / (AmE), / pɪˈrɪmɪˌdiːn /; / pɪˈrɪmɪdiːn / (BrE)
a chemical compound with a six-membered ring containing two nitrogen atoms, important as a building block of DNA and RNA

Examples

pyrimidine base in DNA
Pyrimidine is one of the building blocks of DNA and RNA.
Chemists often study pyrimidine derivatives because of their pharmaceutical applications.
The structure of pyrimidine features two nitrogen atoms in a six-membered ring.
The structure of a pyrimidine ring is similar to that of benzene, except for the nitrogen atoms.
Thymine and cytosine are two common pyrimidines found in DNA.
Uracil is a pyrimidine base found in RNA.
