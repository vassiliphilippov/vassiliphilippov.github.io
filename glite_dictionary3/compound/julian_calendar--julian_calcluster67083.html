Julian calendar

noun
calendar introduced by Julius Caesar
Pronunciation: / ˈdʒuːliən ˈkæləndɚ / (AmE), / ˈdʒuːliən ˈkælɪndə / (BrE)
the calendar introduced in 46 b.c. by Julius Caesar, featuring a 12-month year of 365 days and a leap year every fourth year, used in Europe before the Gregorian calendar

Examples

the Julian calendar date
Historians often convert ancient dates from the Julian calendar to the modern Gregorian calendar for consistency.
Many Eastern Orthodox churches still use the Julian calendar to determine religious holidays.
The Julian calendar was gradually replaced in Europe by a more accurate system in the 16th century.

Related Terms

  • antonym – Gregorian calendar: the calendar system that replaced the Julian calendar in most countries
