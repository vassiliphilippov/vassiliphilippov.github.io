angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor

noun
blood pressure–lowering drug class
Pronunciation: / ˌændʒioʊˌtɛnsɪn kənˈvɜrtɪŋ ɛnˌzaɪm ɪnˈhɪbɪtər / (AmE), / ˌandʒɪəʊˌtɛnsɪn kənˈvɜːtɪŋ ˈɛnzaɪm ɪnˈhɪbɪtə / (BrE)
a drug that lowers blood pressure by inhibiting the activity of angiotensin-converting enzyme, thereby relaxing arteries and promoting salt and water excretion; used to treat hypertension and heart failure

Examples

take an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor daily
Many patients with heart failure benefit from taking an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor daily.
Research shows that an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor can reduce the risk of kidney problems in diabetics.
The doctor prescribed an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor to help control her high blood pressure.
