trial by ordeal

noun
historical practice of judging guilt by dangerous test
Pronunciation: / ˈtraɪ.əl baɪ ɔrˈdil / (AmE), / ˈtraɪ.əl baɪ ɔːˈdiːl / (BrE)
a method from early legal systems in which an accused person's guilt or innocence was determined by subjecting them to dangerous or painful tests, with survival or escape seen as a sign of innocence

Examples

trial by ordeal with fire
Historians study trial by ordeal to understand the evolution of legal systems.
In medieval Europe, trial by ordeal was sometimes used to settle accusations of witchcraft.
The accused was subjected to a trial by ordeal, such as holding a red-hot iron to prove innocence.
