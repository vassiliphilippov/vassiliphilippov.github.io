Markoff process

noun
stochastic process with memoryless property
Pronunciation: / ˈmɑrkɔf ˌproʊsɛs / (AmE), / ˈmɑːkɒf ˌprəʊsɛs / (BrE)
a type of stochastic process where the probability of each future state depends only on the current state and not on the sequence of events that preceded it

Examples

Markoff process in probability theory
In the study of queues, analysts frequently use the properties of a Markoff process.
The basic assumption of the Markoff process is that history does not affect future states beyond the current one.
The random movement of particles in a fluid can often be modeled as a Markoff process.
