long castling

noun
chess: castling on the queenside
Pronunciation: / lɔŋ ˈkæsəlɪŋ / (AmE), / lɒŋ ˈkɑːslɪŋ / (BrE)
In chess, the move in which the king and the queenside rook are moved simultaneously as part of castling, with the king moving two squares towards the rook on the queenside.

Examples

long castling in chess
After long castling, White's rook is immediately active on the d-file.
One risk of long castling is that the a-file can become weak.
The player chose long castling to launch an attack on the kingside.
