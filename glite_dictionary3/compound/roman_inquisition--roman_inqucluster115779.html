Roman Inquisition

noun
Catholic tribunal established in 1542 to combat heresy
Pronunciation: / ˌroʊ.mən ɪnˌkwɪˈzɪʃ.ən / (AmE), / ˌrəʊ.mən ɪnˌkwɪˈzɪʃ.ən / (BrE)
the tribunal established by the Roman Catholic Church in 1542 in Italy to suppress Protestantism and enforce religious orthodoxy, notably responsible for the trial of Galileo.

Examples

the Roman Inquisition tried Galileo
Galileo was famously tried by the Roman Inquisition for his support of heliocentrism.
Historians have studied the archives of the Roman Inquisition to understand its procedures and influence.
The Roman Inquisition played a major role in policing religious beliefs during the Counter-Reformation.
